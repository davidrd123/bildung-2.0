## Promoted claims

### 1. The maturity statuses converge structurally with fallibilist discipline

The strongest architecture-level contact point is the repo's maturity language:

- `seed`
- `tentative`
- `stable`
- `open`

The important point is not that the project has "implemented Peirce" but that it has independently converged on a structurally similar discipline:

- beliefs or renderings are usable before they are final
- `open` is a positive status, not a deficiency
- resistance from the material is treated as information rather than embarrassment

This is a real observation about the repo's own structure. It does not require superlatives about being the "best" implementation of anything.

### 2. Evidence chains behave like interpretant chains

The evidence-handle structure supports a second real convergence.

Each new exercised passage:

- does not merely confirm a term
- changes the active sense of that term
- leaves the handle stable while the meaning under that handle drifts and sharpens

That is close enough to semiosis to be worth banking as a project-side observation. The important claim is modest: the evidence chain behaves like an interpretant chain in practice. It need not be promoted into a total Peircean framework.

### 3. `Secondness` is useful vocabulary for the project's recurring experience of resistance

The opening Peirce mapping is strongest where it names something the project was already discovering:

- perception or participatory feel is one pressure
- formalization or explicit structure is another
- but a third thing keeps appearing: the source pushes back, the glossary fails, the rendering is resisted, the framework has to bend

Calling this pressure `Secondness` is useful.

The safer formulation is enrichment rather than dissolution: Peirce gives a name to a third pressure the project had already begun to encounter. He does not yet warrant rewriting the whole Goethe / Leibniz split in Peircean terms.
