This run takes the bundle's second scale:

- Jünger `§51 + §54`
- Dick `Folder 83 memory-work + Folder 1 splintered truth`

It is narrower:

- what happens when names remain active but stop compelling
- what sort of holding-operation appears before a new stable naming becomes possible

## Collision question

If these bounded surfaces are read together without forcing a common doctrine, what becomes most adjacent?

More narrowly:

- is the main contact still threshold-language
- or is the stronger contact what each archive does when naming has started to lag the object

## What becomes adjacent

The strongest contact is not a shared image of threshold.

It is a shared pressure around **holding contact when names have become unreliable**.

On the Jünger side:

- future things can already be seen
- the old historical vocabulary still acts
- but it no longer reaches compellingly what is arriving
- therefore the honest move is typology, symptom-reading, and guarded delay

On the Dick side:

- something real happened and matters too much to lose
- but the archive cannot yet close around one total name for it
- therefore the honest move is repetition, fragment-preservation, and recollective partiality

The contact is methodological before it is thematic.

Both texts teach a discipline of retention under naming pressure:

> when inherited names still act but no longer bind correctly, preserve the fragments and symptoms before forcing a final name.

Or more compactly:

- Jünger: **typology under dangerous naming**
- Dick: **memory-work under splintered truth**

## Surprise

The surprise is that `§51-54` lands less as a dramatic threshold image than as a method problem.

The sharper adjacency is not `Zeitmauer` paired with a Dickian symbol.

It is:

- Jünger's claim that naming future things is dangerous because the old words still conjure while misfiring
- Dick's claim that the archive must keep circling because the truth is exploded and cannot yet be gathered into one stable vocabulary

So the contact zone is less "both stand at a threshold" than:

> both preserve contact with what exceeds the current naming regime by refusing to let either fact or fragment be prematurely closed.

That is a better middle-band result than a new threshold slogan would have been.

## What resists direct pairing

1. Jünger still points beyond delay toward a new order of naming.

His line in `§54` is not archival modesty for its own sake. The old order's compartments fail, but the pressure points toward system-formation and a new `Hochgrad`.

Dick is not doing that here.

2. Dick's archive is not public-historical vocabulary under pressure.

His problem is not primarily that words like `war`, `freedom`, or `state` have gone deceptive. It is that the event outstrips the archive's own theological and metaphysical names.

3. Repetition and typology are not the same move.

They become adjacent here because both slow closure. But Jünger's typology is a stance toward emerging public reality, while Dick's repetition is a retention device for a disclosure that keeps being misranked.

## Provisional result

This bundle run was useful.

Not because it yielded a shared ontology of threshold.

Not because it replaced the tighter temporal packet sequence.

It was useful because it surfaced a middle-band adjacency that the earlier time-crisis work did not center:

> under lagging names, both projects rely on holding operations that preserve contact without pretending to final naming.

Jünger's version is typological and symptomatic.

Dick's version is archival and recollective.

The contact sits there, at the level of how thought behaves when the object is ahead of the available names.
