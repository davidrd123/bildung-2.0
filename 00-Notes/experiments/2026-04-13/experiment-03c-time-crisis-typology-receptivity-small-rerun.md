# Experiment 03C: Time Crisis / Typology-Receptivity Small Rerun

1. Result statement: The adjacency survives at smaller scale, but only as a shared discipline against forcing deceptive appearances into premature explanation; it does not survive as a common method or a shared doctrine of access.

2. Bridge sentence: Jünger's turn from polemical illumination toward typology and Dick's turn from paranoid projection toward receptivity meet in one narrow methodological demand: when apparent change misleads, the reader must stop imposing the old frame and learn how to receive what is actually there.

3. Refusal sentence: Do not turn Jünger's typology into Dick's inward de-occlusion of perception, and do not turn Dick's receptivity into Jünger's public typology of world-processes, because Jünger stays with historical-civilizational misfit while Dick stays with damaged reception on the subject side.

4. What carries the bridge on the Jünger side: `§47` says that more and more figures and events no longer fit the historical frame, that the old words of order are becoming deceptive, and that historiography slides into borrowed polemics; the closing methodological correction is that the one who wants to know what is happening should care more for a typology of the world and its processes than for polemical illumination. `§48` sharpens that correction by adding that where the future outlines itself beyond the `Zeitmauer`, naming itself is dangerous. The bridge therefore sits in disciplined restraint before a failing inherited vocabulary.

5. What carries the bridge on the Dick side: Folder 21 says that change is largely counterfeit permutation, that false time passes by look-differents and fake indices of change, and that the right stance toward pattern is receptive rather than paranoid projection. Folder 90 then states the decisive correction: the occlusion is not lifted from the world but from us, and salvation is not a change in world but a change in perception. The bridge therefore sits in disciplined correction of reception before deceptive apparent change.

6. Provisional judgment: The adjacency holds, but narrowly. It works if the claim is that both writers reject projective over-reading when temporal appearances have become untrustworthy. It fails cleanly if the claim is that they now give the same advice, share the same method, or describe the same route to truth.

7. What the result does not license: It does not license a shared cosmology, a shared ontology, or a shared method; it does not justify turning `typology` and `receptivity` into standing cross-project vocabulary from one packet; it does not reopen the wider Dick/Jünger synthesis; and it does not permit reading Jünger as if his problem were a prison of private perception or Dick as if his problem were chiefly the public breakdown of historical language.
