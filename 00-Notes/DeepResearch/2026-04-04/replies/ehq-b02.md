## Question Restated At Full Strength

The hard question is not whether medieval Latin vocabulary lost prestige. It is whether the live problem-clusters organized by scholastic philosophy—universals, individuation/haecceity, real possibility, and irreducible relations—were actually solved by later analytic philosophy, or whether the older synthesis collapsed while the problems themselves were redistributed into new vocabularies and subfields. A second layer asks whether today’s powers metaphysics and Zalamea’s Peircean mathematics are genuine continuations, loose family resemblances, or retrospective overreadings.

## Best Current Answer

The best current answer is: **neither “solved” nor “buried” works as a blanket verdict**. There really was a rupture in the self-conscious scholastic tradition; Pasnau is not exaggerating when he describes scholastic philosophy as a tradition that crashed down with unusual speed. But the SEP article on medieval universals says just as clearly that the *specifically medieval* problem ended only by giving way to recognizably similar modern variants, and Marenbon’s reviewer reports a mixed picture: some medieval issues connect strongly to contemporary analytic philosophy, some only indirectly, and some no longer map cleanly because the problems were reformulated. So the strongest verdict is **uneven reformulation plus institutional marginalization**, not clean solution and not total disappearance. ([Spot Colorado][1])

**Universals** were not buried in the simple sense. Contemporary analytic metaphysics still frames nominalism partly as rejection of universals, the metaphysics of properties remains an active and controversial field, and Armstrong’s universals-based account of laws is a canonical modern option. The strongest “analytic philosophy did address this” case is therefore very strong. The strongest “it didn’t” case is narrower: the medieval dispute was not just about whether properties exist, but about the relation among language, mind, singulars, universals, and, in many settings, theology. Marenbon’s reviewer even notes that the realism/nominalism dispute was partly a meta-philosophical fight about the relation of philosophy to theology. So the modern debate is a real continuation, but it is not the whole scholastic package in new dress. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])

**Haecceity / individuation** were also not simply dropped. There is an active contemporary literature on haecceitism, including Lewis-style and other metaphysical treatments of non-qualitative thisness, and the issue resurfaces in philosophy of physics in debates over individuality and distinguishability in quantum theory. That is the strongest case that analytic philosophy did address the problem. But the strongest counterpoint is that the analytic version is often a *modal* recasting—questions about transworld identity, non-qualitative properties, and qualitative supervenience—whereas the medieval problem was a broader question about what individuates substances in the first place. So again: not solved, not forgotten, but transformed. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][3])

**Real possibility / real modality** is where the original “buried” story becomes weakest. Analytic philosophy unquestionably made major advances here: possible-worlds semantics gave modal logic a rigorous formal treatment, and the modal literature after Kripke is a genuine achievement, not a dodge. So any claim that analytic philosophy simply failed to address modality is false. But the older question was not only semantic. It was also ontological: what in reality makes possibilities real? That question remains open, and the contemporary anti-Humean literature on dispositional essentialism, laws, and powers shows that the ontological grounding of modality is still contested. Historical work by Peter King and Calvin Normore makes the continuity explicit by tracing current powers/modality debates back to medieval discussions, especially Scotus and Ockham. So the right answer is: formal progress, no final settlement. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][4])

**Triadic relations** is the place where the original chat most clearly overcalled its case. If the claim is that modern analytic logic forgot how to handle three-place relations, that is just wrong. Modern predicate logic explicitly handles polyadic predicates; the logic of relations developed by Peirce continued into later mathematical logic, including Tarski; and current metaphysics of relations uses arity distinctions as basic furniture. The stronger, more defensible claim is different: analytic philosophy did not make **Peirce’s broader thesis of irreducibly triadic sign-relations and mediations** central to its mainstream self-understanding. Even there, caution is needed: the SEP article on Peirce notes that the general rationale for his triadism is still not especially clear. So “triadic relations were buried” is false at the level of formal logic and only partly true at the level of Peirce’s larger metaphysics and semeiotic. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][5])

The **“reinventing Scotus”** line about the powers revival is, on current evidence, **overcalled if taken literally**. There are real convergences: Bird/Ellis-style dispositional essentialism, Molnar’s anti-Humean powers ontology, and Heil/Mumford’s real-powers approaches all reject Humean passivism, treat modal structure as real, and allow powers to exist whether or not they are manifested. Those are genuine thematic overlaps with scholastic and especially Scotist concerns about powers and modality. But Scotus’s package also includes common natures, haecceity, formal distinctions, synchronic contingency, and a theological setting that contemporary powers metaphysics usually does not reproduce. The safer judgment is **family resemblance or selective convergence**, not straightforward rediscovery. Ironically, the clearest explicit bridge to Scotus comes less from Bird or Heil than from historians like King and Normore, who directly connect medieval powers debates to current ones. ([PhilArchive][6])

On **Zalamea**, the evidence supports something more substantial than analogy but less than field-wide consensus. His chapter in the *Handbook of Cognitive Mathematics* explicitly proposes a merger of Peirce’s pragmaticist maxim and topos theory, and a major review by Longo presents the program as a mathematically articulated Peircean framework involving structures, models, and functorial transits. Zalamea’s 2022 volume on Peircean mathematics is described as offering formal developments in existential graphs, continuum theory, category theory, HoTT, and modal logic. That is enough to say the work is mathematically serious and programmatic, not just poetic comparison. But the reception I found is specialized—reviews and a Peirce-centered journal ecology—not evidence that this is a mainstream consensus in category theory or philosophy of mathematics. So the defensible claim is: **Zalamea is one of the clearest mathematically serious Peircean continuators, but “the strongest living continuation” is a ranking claim the evidence here does not justify.** ([IDEAS/RePEc][7])

Serious historians do support this more qualified picture. Pasnau supports the strongest “burial” claim at the level of tradition and curriculum. Marenbon’s reviewer supports a differentiated model: some medieval topics remain very live for analytic philosophers, some only loosely so, and some have changed too much for simple continuity claims. The medieval-logic revival is explicit about contemporary relevance, and work on the liar paradox openly frames itself as revival of medieval solutions in modern formal settings. A separate, non-analytic afterlife appears in the continued treatment of Heidegger’s early Scotus study as formative for his concept of facticity. Put together, that looks much more like **dispersal, rerouting, and selective recovery** than like either total severance or full solution. ([Spot Colorado][1])

## Where The Thinking Needs More Pressure

The first pressure is to keep **problem, formulation, and tradition** separate. A problem can survive even when its medieval formulation dies, and a tradition can collapse even while its questions migrate elsewhere. The medieval-universals literature and Marenbon’s framing both push toward exactly that distinction. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][8])

The second pressure is to distinguish **formal treatment from metaphysical treatment**. Analytic philosophy often did solve or greatly advance the formal side of a problem—modal semantics, polyadic logic, relation theory—without thereby settling the older ontological question about what possibilities or relations *are*. The modality and triadic-relations claims become much clearer once that split is enforced. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][4])

The third pressure is to distinguish **explicit lineage from structural convergence**. King and Normore give explicit medieval-to-current bridges on powers and modality; Bird, Molnar, Mumford, and Heil mainly give contemporary positions that resemble parts of scholastic debates. Those are not the same kind of continuity. ([OUP Academic][9])

The fourth pressure is scope. “Analytic philosophy,” “twentieth-century thought,” “modern logic,” and “institutional curricula” are not interchangeable. A claim can be true of mainstream Anglophone curricula and false of philosophy of physics, medieval scholarship, formal logic, or Heidegger’s reception of Scotus. ([Spot Colorado][1])

## Where A Softer Model Would Probably Go Wrong

It would tell a beautifully coherent **loss narrative** and ignore how much real work contemporary metaphysics and logic have already done on universals, modality, haecceity, and relations. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])

It would treat every anti-Humean or powers-based view as a hidden return of Scotus, when the safer description is usually partial overlap rather than doctrinal recovery. ([PhilArchive][6])

It would use the triadic-relations point sloppily, as if modern logic were trapped in dyads, even though polyadic logic and relation theory are standard. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][5])

It would either dismiss Zalamea as metaphor or canonize him as decisive. The evidence supports neither extreme: the program is mathematically serious, but specialized. ([IDEAS/RePEc][7])

## What Would Actually Move This Forward

The next real step is not another global narrative. It is a **controlled comparison on one problem-cluster at a time**.

Start with either **universals** or **real modality**, because those have the clearest medieval and contemporary bodies of work. For each one, ask four narrow questions: what was the medieval formulation, what is the contemporary analytic formulation, what was genuinely gained, and what was lost when the problem moved. Use one medieval/historical source, one contemporary analytic source, and one historian of continuity/discontinuity. On the evidence here, Pasnau plus Marenbon for the historical side, then SEP plus a contemporary modality/powers source for the analytic side, would already tighten the issue considerably. Until that is done, the most defensible live conclusion is this: **the questions were not solved away, but neither were they simply buried; they were decomposed, formalized, and rerouted, while the scholastic synthesis that once held them together was genuinely marginalized.** ([Spot Colorado][1])
