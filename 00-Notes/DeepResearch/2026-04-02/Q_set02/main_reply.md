No single prior work really covers the exact same spread. The closest answer is a **cluster** of precedents, each matching a different part of what `bildung-2.0` is doing.

**Closest overall precedents**

**Aby Warburg, especially the *Mnemosyne Atlas* and the Warburg library project.** This is probably the nearest ancestor if you mean breadth of material plus cross-domain method. Warburg was not just interpreting individual texts. He was tracing long-range survivals and reactivations of forms across art, cosmology, ritual, and historical memory, and the Atlas was explicitly an unfinished synthetic apparatus rather than a normal monograph. The Warburg Institute itself describes the *Mnemosyne Atlas* as a summa of Warburg’s life’s work and an attempt to map the afterlife of antiquity across times and media. That is very close to your repo’s combination of source work, dossiering, and synthetic thread-building. ([The Warburg Institute][1])

**Ernst Cassirer, above all *The Problem of Knowledge* and *The Philosophy of Symbolic Forms*.** Cassirer is the closest precedent for the project’s effort to move across philosophy, science, history, myth, language, and form without treating any one domain as fully sovereign. Yale’s overview of *The Problem of Knowledge* emphasizes exactly that large-scale history of scientific and intellectual concepts, and your own repo is already right to see Cassirer as a hinge figure rather than just another source author. He is less archival and less apparatus-heavy than your project, but closer than almost anyone in range of material. ([Yale University Press][2])

**Ernst Robert Curtius, *European Literature and the Latin Middle Ages*.** Curtius is a major precedent for civilizational continuity work through philology. Princeton and JSTOR both summarize the book as an argument for continuity across a very large historical span, with the Latin Middle Ages functioning as the connective tissue of European literature. That resembles your interest in broken or obscured continuities and in recovering buried connective tissue, though Curtius is much more canonically literary and much less recursive or experimental in method. ([Google Books][3])

**Erich Auerbach, *Mimesis*.** Auerbach is a strong precedent for a one-person project that traverses centuries of material through close reading, excerpt-centered comparison, and historically ambitious synthesis. The Princeton materials and standard summaries emphasize its chapter-by-chapter movement across Western literature through selected scenes rather than exhaustive coverage. That is relevant to your method of using bounded passages as high-pressure sites for larger claims. But Auerbach is more linear, more polished, and less infrastructural than what you are building. ([Wikipedia][4])

**Walter Benjamin, especially *The Arcades Project*.** Benjamin is a close precedent for the archive-like, dossier-like, not-fully-closed form of the work. Harvard describes *The Arcades Project* as an attempt to represent and critique nineteenth-century bourgeois historical experience, and the work is famously assembled through notes, quotations, cross-references, and unfinished constellations rather than a finished system. That makes it one of the nearest precedents for your Exegesis-atlas side and for the sense that the apparatus itself is part of the thought. ([Harvard University Press][5])

**Frances Yates, especially *Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition*.** Yates is a good precedent for the recovery of under-read or misframed intellectual lineages that cut across philosophy, religion, magic, science, and Renaissance culture. Chicago’s summary stresses exactly that interplay. She is narrower than your project in format, but important as a model of how hidden traditions can be made historically legible without reducing them to one discipline. ([University of Chicago Press][6])

**Hans Blumenberg, especially *The Legitimacy of the Modern Age*.** Blumenberg is a strong precedent for the scale of diagnosis more than for your method. MIT Press summarizes the book as a large argument against the simple “modernity is secularized theology” thesis. That makes him relevant wherever your project is making civilizational claims and trying to redescribe the long history of modern concepts. He is not doing the same kind of source-layer apparatus work, but he is one of the nearest precedents for the size of the historical-philosophical move. ([MIT Press][7])

**If you mean “who spans the most similar *range*,” not just style or method, my ranking would be:**

1. **Warburg**
2. **Cassirer**
3. **Benjamin**
4. **Curtius**
5. **Auerbach**
6. **Yates**
7. **Blumenberg** ([The Warburg Institute][1])

**Why none of them is actually the same thing**

Your project combines at least five elements that usually live apart:

* source translation and glossarial pressure
* recursive archive mapping
* cross-project conceptual dossiers
* modern concept encounters treated like source encounters
* an explicit attempt to turn all that into a reusable apparatus

Warburg comes closest on atlas-like breadth. Cassirer comes closest on intellectual range. Benjamin comes closest on unfinished dossier form. Curtius and Auerbach come closest on philological seriousness across long spans. But none of them combines all five in quite this way. That is why the right answer is probably not a single predecessor, but **Warburg + Cassirer + Benjamin, with Curtius and Auerbach as the philological stabilizers**. ([The Warburg Institute][1])

The one caution I would add is that once you ask “what prior work spans a similar range of material,” the temptation is to look only for glamorous humanities ancestors. But some of the closest structural cousins are not famous books at all. They are research institutes, archival editorial enterprises, and note-apparatus projects. Warburg’s library becoming an institute, and Benjamin’s dossier-archive form, both point in that direction more than toward the solitary masterpiece model. ([The Warburg Institute][8])
