Below is the map I would use if the question is not “who wrote about these authors?” but “which strong minds metabolized them into new work?” I’m treating “major” fairly strictly. In the thin cases, I would rather give you a shorter honest line than pad it.

## 1) Jakob von Uexküll

This is one of the thickest cases. Uexküll has real descendant traditions in phenomenology, philosophical anthropology, biosemiotics, and medicine. ([dokumen.pub][1])

**Major Auseinandersetzungen**

* **Heidegger — *Die Grundbegriffe der Metaphysik* (German lecture course, 1929–30) — Confrontation.** This is the canonical case: Heidegger takes Uexküll’s *Umwelt* seriously enough to make it a hinge in his own account of animality and worldhood, but the result is a transformation of Uexküll into Heidegger’s ontology of “world,” not a faithful reconstruction of Uexküll’s biology. ([Beyng][2])

* **Plessner — *Die Stufen des Organischen und der Mensch* (German book, 1928) — Confrontation / Extension.** Plessner absorbs the *Umwelt/Umgebung* distinction into philosophical anthropology. If you want the strongest anthropological confrontation after Heidegger, Plessner is the one to read before Gehlen. ([Springer][3])

* **Cassirer — late writings around organism, symbol, and objectivity (German essays/books) — Extension.** Yes: Cassirer did engage Uexküll directly. The evidence suggests he likely knew *Theoretische Biologie* and later referred to earlier and later Uexküll texts; the uptake seems more deep appropriation than polemical struggle, but it is philosophically substantial. ([Frederik Stjernfelt][4])

* **Merleau-Ponty — *La nature* / *Nature* lectures (French lecture course, 1956–60) — Extension.** Uexküll becomes a resource for organism–milieu reciprocity and for a non-mechanistic account of embodied sense. This is one of the most fertile philosophical continuations of Uexküll. ([Dialnet][5])

* **Canguilhem — “Le vivant et son milieu” in *La connaissance de la vie* (French essay/chapter) — Confrontation / Extension.** Canguilhem reconstructs Uexküll’s distinctions and then reorients them toward biological normativity: the organism does not merely sit in a milieu, it actively institutes one. ([ResearchGate][6])

**Significant appropriations**

* **Scheler — philosophical anthropology (German) — Appropriation.** Scheler uses Uexküll as part of his own ranking of life and spirit; important, but less structurally decisive than Plessner. ([Taylor & Francis][7])

* **Deleuze & Guattari — *Mille plateaux* / *A Thousand Plateaus* (French book) — Appropriation.** The tick becomes a portable conceptual machine for assemblage and becoming-animal. Powerful, but fragmentary and opportunistic rather than continuous. ([Penn English Department][8])

* **Agamben — *L’aperto* / *The Open* (Italian book) — Appropriation / Confrontation.** Agamben reads Uexküll through Heidegger and uses him to reopen the human/animal threshold. ([Ewa][9])

* **Blumenberg — essays/chapters in German — Transformation.** A later German line turns Uexküll’s bioepistemology into phenomenological reflection. This is less famous than Heidegger or Merleau-Ponty, but intellectually strong. ([University of Luebeck][10])

* **Adolf Portmann; Viktor von Weizsäcker; Thure von Uexküll (German biology/medicine) — Extension.** If you want biological uptake rather than purely philosophical uptake, this is the surprising line: organismal expressivity, the *Gestaltkreis*, and psychosomatic medicine extend Uexküll more fruitfully than cybernetics did. ([PhilPapers][11])

**Intellectual genealogy**

* **Biosemiotics** is the clearest living descendant line: Sebeok, Hoffmeyer, Kull, Magnus, Tønnessen, and related work in *Biosemiotics* and *Sign Systems Studies*. ([ZBI][12])

* **Psychosomatic / clinical theory** via Thure von Uexküll is a genuine applied descendant tradition, not a footnote. ([dokumen.pub][1])

* **Japanese / mesological reception** is real, but the strongest line I could verify runs less through canonical “Japanese biosemiotics” than through *fūdo* / mesology: Watsuji and Imanishi are explicitly paired with Uexküll, and Berque later turns *Umwelt* into a theory of milieu and trajection. ([OJS UTlib][13])

**Surprising connections / thin lines**

* **Cassirer:** yes, directly.
* **Plessner:** emphatically yes.
* **Gehlen:** present, but thinner than Plessner or Scheler. ([Springer][14])
* **Cybernetics / systems theory:** thinner than often claimed. Bertalanffy appears as an appreciative neighbor, but a really thick direct Bateson-style confrontation is hard to document. ([Springer][15])

## 2) Ernst Jünger

Jünger’s afterlife is intense but narrow. *Der Arbeiter* and the nihilism/technology nexus drew heavyweight readers; *An der Zeitmauer* has fewer major successors than one might expect. ([Suhrkamp Verlag][16])

**Major Auseinandersetzungen**

* **Heidegger — *Zur Seinsfrage* plus the 1949–1975 correspondence (German essay + correspondence) — Confrontation.** This is the central philosophical duel. Heidegger treats Jünger as a privileged diagnostician of the age, but only to relocate him inside his own history of being; the exchange is most concrete around *Über die Linie*. ([Internet Archive][17])

* **Carl Schmitt — *Briefe 1930–1983* (German correspondence) — Confrontation / parallel dialogue.** Not a monograph, but decades of sustained contact around state, war, nihilism, and historical form. It is one of the real Jünger archives of thought-on-thought. ([JSTOR][18])

* **Hans Blumenberg — *Der Mann vom Mond. Über Ernst Jünger* (German essays/glosses) — Confrontation.** This is stronger than many people realize: the volume gathers largely unpublished Jünger essays from Blumenberg’s estate and combines admiration for Jünger’s stylistic precision with relentless criticism of his political and theoretical attitudes. ([Suhrkamp Verlag][16])

* **Maurice Blanchot — sections of *L’Entretien infini* / essays on epoch-change (French essays) — Confrontation.** For *An der Zeitmauer* specifically, Blanchot is the strongest French philosophical engagement I could verify. He wrestles with Jünger’s attempt to think a civilizational threshold and the possibility of crossing a “wall of time.” 

* **Julius Evola — essay on *Der Arbeiter* plus correspondence (Italian / German) — Extension / Appropriation.** Controversial, yes, but intellectually substantive: Evola metabolizes Jünger’s figure of the Worker into his own anti-bourgeois and traditionalist project. ([Jünger Translation Project][19])

**Significant appropriations**

* **Alain de Benoist — *Ernst Jünger: Between the Gods & the Titans* (French book) — Appropriation.** De Benoist organizes Jünger around the Soldier, Worker, Rebel, and Anarch and makes him usable for contemporary metapolitics. ([Middle Europe Books][20])

* **Botho Strauß — essays/prose (German) — Appropriation.** Real affinity, but more literary and atmospheric than argumentative. ([Brill][21])

* **Hermann Hesse — review of *An der Zeitmauer* (German review, 1960) — Reception worth noting.** Not a whole genealogy, but a serious contemporary acknowledgment of late Jünger as a thinker of epochs, not only of war. ([De Gruyter Brill][22])

* **Recent Anthropocene / geological readings of *An der Zeitmauer* — Appropriation.** These use Jünger’s deep-time imagination for contemporary questions of planetary history rather than continuing his whole metaphysical project. ([ZfL Berlin][23])

**Intellectual genealogy**

* There is **no broad Jünger school** in the way there is a Uexküll or Rosen line. What persists is a split afterlife: conservative-revolutionary / metapolitical appropriation on one side, scattered philosophy-of-technology and geological/Anthropocene reuse on the other. ([Middle Europe Books][20])

**Surprising connections / thin lines**

* **Blumenberg:** definitely yes.
* **French reception:** yes, but the heavyweight node is Blanchot rather than a large French Jünger movement. ([Suhrkamp Verlag][16])
* **Natural-science reception of the late nature philosophy:** I could not verify a major sustained confrontation by entomologists, paleontologists, or historians of science at the level you want.

## 3) Ernst Cassirer — *Das Erkenntnisproblem*

The decisive afterlife of *Das Erkenntnisproblem* is not primarily Davos or symbolic forms. It is the historiography of early modern science and modernity. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][24])

**Major Auseinandersetzungen**

* **Alexandre Koyré — studies on Galileo and the scientific revolution (French books/essays) — Extension / Confrontation.** Cassirer’s “mathematization of nature” becomes, in Koyré, a more sharply metaphysical history of scientific revolution. SEP explicitly names Koyré as one of the seminal intellectual historians who acknowledged and developed Cassirer’s insight. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][24])

* **E. A. Burtt — *The Metaphysical Foundations of Modern Physical Science* (English book) — Extension.** Burtt is part of the first major English-language uptake of the Cassirer problem-field. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][24])

* **E. J. Dijksterhuis — *The Mechanization of the World Picture* (Dutch book) — Extension.** Another major history-of-science continuation of Cassirer’s framing. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][24])

* **Thomas Kuhn — early historiography and *Structure* (English) — Transformation.** Friedman argues that “virtually all” the figures on Kuhn’s list of influences stand, one way or another, in lines reacting to Cassirer’s *Erkenntnisproblem*; Kuhn’s immediate conduit is Koyré, and Kuhn places himself squarely in that historiographical tradition. 

* **Hans Blumenberg / Hans-Georg Gadamer on Cusanus and modernity (German books/essays) — Confrontation / Extension.** Cassirer’s reading of Cusanus as a harbinger of modernity becomes a live issue for later German philosophy of modernity. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment][25])

**Significant appropriations**

* **Carnap / Reichenbach line — neo-Kantian philosophy of science (German) — Extension.** This is more systematic philosophy of science than direct historical confrontation with *Erkenntnisproblem*, but Friedman explicitly notes Cassirer’s imprint on Carnap’s way of representing knowledge through serial formal structures, and the Reichenbach/Carnap route matters for the later Kuhn story. 

* **Arthur Pap — *The A Priori in Physical Theory* (English book) — Extension.** If you want an analytic descendant who really took something from Cassirer, Pap is a better bet than Quine or Goodman. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][24])

* **Renaissance historians/philosophers working on Cusanus/Bruno — Appropriation / contestation.** Cassirer’s readings helped define the field they later had to accept, revise, or resist. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment][25])

**Intellectual genealogy**

* The living descendants are strongest in **historical epistemology** and in integrated **history-and-philosophy of science**, not in mainstream analytic metaphysics. Cassirer’s line to Kuhn through Koyré is the key bridge. ([Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy][26])

**Surprising connections / thin lines**

* For this specific work, **Davos is not the main sequel**.
* **Kuhn:** yes, but mediated.
* **Lakatos, Feyerabend, Goodman, Quine:** I could not verify comparably strong direct confrontations with *Das Erkenntnisproblem* itself.

## 4) Alexander Bogdanov — *Tektologiya*

This is the clearest case of **retroactive foundationality plus historical discontinuity**. Bogdanov looks foundational in hindsight, but the direct line is broken. ([Social History Portal][27])

**Major Auseinandersetzungen**

* **Ludwig von Bertalanffy — possible link to General System Theory (German) — plausible Extension, but contested.** Some scholars argue Bertalanffy likely knew Bogdanov because a German translation existed before his early systems work; others say the consensus is that he did not. I would treat this as a possible but not secure line. ([PhilArchive][28])

* **Vadim Sadovsky, Vladimir Kelle, I. Blauberg, E. Yudin, and related Soviet systems philosophers (Russian essays, forewords, encyclopedia work) — Recovery / Extension.** This is the real posthumous reactivation of Bogdanov: they explicitly reinstall *Tektology* as a precursor to systems science inside late Soviet thought. ([Academia][29])

* **Leonid Abalkin and post-Soviet Russian system thinkers (Russian essays/edited volumes) — Extension.** Abalkin’s “Bogdanov’s Tektology: Towards a New Paradigm” is not just memorialization; it tries to reactivate Bogdanov for contemporary systems questions. ([Social History Portal][27])

* **Gerald Midgley, Peter Jackson, and recent systems thinkers (English articles/editorials) — Recovery / Extension.** The contemporary systems revival treats Bogdanov as an ancestor of systems unity and critical systems thinking, but this is a recovery project, not a continuous school. ([ResearchGate][30])

**Significant appropriations**

* **Stafford Beer — mostly retrospective comparison, not direct engagement.** Jackson explicitly says he found no evidence Beer knew Bogdanov. The similarity is striking, but it is not a documented intellectual encounter. ([ResearchGate][31])

* **Prigogine, Jantsch, Maturana, complexity theory — analogical / retrospective appropriation.** Scholars often say Bogdanov anticipated them; that may be illuminating, but it is usually a comparison made after the fact rather than a traceable confrontation. ([PhilArchive][28])

* **Vernadsky — also mostly retrospective.** There is a real comparative literature linking *Tektology* and biosphere thought, but I did not verify a strong direct encounter. ([Social History Portal][27])

**Intellectual genealogy**

* The real descendants are **late Soviet and post-Soviet systems philosophy**, and now a **21st-century systems-recovery literature**. That is a living research agenda, but not a lineage of uninterrupted first-rank successors. ([Academia][29])

**Surprising connections / thin lines**

* I could not verify strong direct engagements by **Ashby, Forrester, or Luhmann**.
* The strongest honest description is: **Bogdanov mattered more as a rediscovered precursor than as a continuously read master.** ([Social History Portal][27])

## 5) Nicolás Gómez Dávila

Here the line is genuinely thin. I can defend **one clear heavyweight confrontation**, several mediators, and a stronger literary than philosophical afterlife. ([Academia][32])

**Major Auseinandersetzungen**

* **Vittorio Hösle — 2003 essay and 2022 *Im Dialog mit Gómez Dávila* (German book) — Confrontation.** This is the strongest case by far. Hösle literally answers Gómez Dávila with “counter-aphorisms,” variations, and corollaries: not commentary on the *Escolios*, but a dialectical duel in the same compressed form. ([VitalSource][33])

* **Carlos B. Gutiérrez — essays on democracy and Nietzsche/Gómez Dávila (Spanish) — Confrontation / contextualization.** Serious Latin American wrestling, especially around aristocratic anti-democracy and Colombian context, though not at Hösle’s scale. ([PhilPapers][34])

* **Miguel Saralegui — “Gómez Dávila as Critic of Hispanic Culture” (Spanish article) — Extension / Confrontation.** This is a genuine attempt to position him as a civilizational critic rather than a cult aphorist. ([Investigadores Universidad San Sebastián][35])

**Significant appropriations**

* **Franco Volpi — essays/mediation in Italian and Spanish/German reception — Mediation / Appropriation.** Volpi is less a combatant than the decisive European transmitter; he made Gómez Dávila legible beyond Colombia. ([Revistas Javeriana][36])

* **Reinhart Maurer and parts of the German conservative-philosophical reception (German) — Appropriation.** Real, but small-scale. ([Academia][32])

* **Botho Strauß / Martin Mosebach / literary right-intellectual admiration (German) — Appropriation.** Important for reception, but this is more elective affinity than philosophical Auseinandersetzung. ([Ediciones Atalanta][37])

**Intellectual genealogy**

* The living line is mainly **small, German-Colombian-Latin American**, with editors/interpreters such as Alfredo Abad, Gutiérrez, and Saralegui. It exists, but it is not a broad school. ([Academia][38])

**Surprising connections / thin lines**

* The strongest European confrontation is **Hösle**, not Volpi.
* I could not verify substantial direct engagements by **Ratzinger, de Lubac, MacIntyre, or Böckenförde** at the level you want.

## 6) Robert Rosen

Rosen has one of the strongest living research afterlives in your list. Here the line is not just reception but **ongoing technical continuation and correction**. ([A. H. Louie][39])

**Major Auseinandersetzungen**

* **Howard Pattee — essays such as “Laws, Constraints, and the Modeling Relation” (English) — Confrontation / Extension.** This is Rosen’s deepest peer relation. Pattee says their approaches were “complementary”: Rosen emphasized the formal relational conditions of life; Pattee emphasized the physical realization of those relations and the symbolic control that makes them evolvable. ([ResearchGate][40])

* **A. H. Louie — *More Than Life Itself: A Synthetic Continuation in Relational Biology* (English book) — Extension.** Louie is the most explicit internal continuer of Rosen’s project, restating Rosen on a more rigorous mathematical footing. ([A. H. Louie][39])

* **J. H. S. Hofmeyr — *(F,A)-system* work (English articles) — Correction / Extension.** Hofmeyr’s fabrication-assembly model is presented as an alternative to Rosen’s *(M,R)* because Rosen’s scheme has been notoriously difficult to realize biochemically. This is exactly the kind of live internal correction you asked about. ([ScienceDirect][41])

* **Cornish-Bowden / Cárdenas / Letelier / Soto-Andrade (English and Spanish-influenced technical literature) — Correction / Extension.** This line keeps Rosen’s question alive while pressing hard on computability, closure to efficient causation, and biochemical realizability. It is continuation under severe technical pressure, not mere homage. ([ScienceDirect][41])

* **Ehresmann & Vanbremeersch — “Memory Evolutive Systems as a Model of Rosen’s Organisms” (English article) — Extension.** This is one of the clearest category-theoretic continuations of Rosen’s organism concept. ([Springer][42])

**Significant appropriations**

* **Autopoiesis bridge literature — Appropriation / synthesis.** There is real work linking Rosen and Maturana–Varela rather than simply juxtaposing them; the key papers ask whether autopoietic systems and *(M,R)* systems can be synthesized. ([ScienceDirect][43])

* **Relational-biology descendants in anticipatory systems and systems biology — Extension.** Rosen remains a live technical ancestor rather than a historical curiosity. ([A. H. Louie][39])

**Intellectual genealogy**

* The living line runs through **Louie, Hofmeyr, Letelier/Cárdenas/Cornish-Bowden, Ehresmann/Vanbremeersch**, and parts of the autonomy/autopoiesis literature. ([A. H. Louie][39])

**Surprising connections / thin lines**

* **Rosen–Pattee:** agreement on the irreducibility of life; disagreement on where the explanatory weight lies — formal closure for Rosen, symbol–matter realization for Pattee. ([ResearchGate][40])
* **Category theory:** Rosen learned category theory from the Eilenberg–Mac Lane milieu, but I did not verify strong reciprocal engagement by Lawvere, Mac Lane, or Eilenberg with Rosen’s biological use of it.

## 7) Philip K. Dick — *The Exegesis*

This line is real, but mostly outside canonical philosophy and dogmatic theology. The strongest readers are in religion, esotericism, media theory, and adjacent philosophical writing. ([Bloomsbury Publishing][44])

**Major Auseinandersetzungen**

* **Richard Doyle — afterword “A Stairway to Eleusis” and later essays (English) — Appropriation / Extension.** Doyle treats Dick not just as an author to decode but as a source for a broader speculative/perennial philosophy. ([Scribd][45])

* **Erik Davis — annotations/editing and essays on gnosis (English) — Appropriation.** Davis is one of the main figures who made the *Exegesis* thinkable as a serious gnostic/intellectual object rather than fan debris. ([Scribd][45])

* **Gabriel McKee — theological work on Dick and chapter on the *Exegesis* (English) — Extension.** McKee explicitly treats the theological content of the *Exegesis* as a live research topic, not just a literary appendix. ([Bloomsbury Publishing][44])

* **Jeffrey J. Kripal — religion/esotericism (English) — Appropriation / Extension.** Kripal visibly places *The Exegesis* inside his own larger orbit of gnosis, consciousness, and the paranormal. ([Kripal Center][46])

**Significant appropriations**

* **Simon Critchley — annotations plus philosophical essays on Dick (English) — Appropriation.** This is real, but lighter and more essayistic than Doyle or Kripal. ([Scribd][45])

* **The 2026 Bloomsbury volume *The Esoteric Theology of Philip K. Dick* — emerging theology field.** The important point is not only the book itself but what it shows: a genuine attempt to read Dick’s 1974 material as Christian, gnostic, mystical, and theological in a sustained way. ([Bloomsbury Publishing][44])

**Intellectual genealogy**

* The living line is a **religion/esotericism/media-theory network**, not a mainstream theological school: Doyle, Davis, McKee, Kripal, the Zebrapedia ecosystem, and now the “esoteric theology” volume. ([Bloomsbury Publishing][44])

**Surprising connections / thin lines**

* I could not verify a heavyweight **Nag Hammadi specialist** or **process theologian** who has made the *Exegesis* central to a major independent project.
* The strongest present line is **subcultural but serious**, not canonical.

## 8) Howard Pattee

Pattee’s line is alive but dispersed. He is less a school-founder with one camp than a nodal figure between theoretical biology, biosemiotics, hierarchy theory, and constraint-based thought. ([Casci Binghamton][47])

**Major Auseinandersetzungen**

* **Robert Rosen — peer interlocutor (English essays/books) — Confrontation / Extension.** Already discussed above; this is the central peer dialogue. Pattee himself describes the two approaches as complementary but different in emphasis. ([ResearchGate][40])

* **Peter Cariani — “Symbols and dynamics in the brain” (English article) — Extension.** Cariani is one of the clearest direct continuers of Pattee’s symbols/dynamics problem, extending it into neuroscience and epistemic systems. ([PETER CARIANI][48])

* **Jesper Hoffmeyer / biosemiotics line (English) — Extension with partial critique.** Pattee became a major source for biosemiotics, but not a simple hero: Hoffmeyer accepted the importance of enabling constraints while resisting Pattee’s stronger epistemic-cut language. ([Springer][49])

* **Terrence Deacon — “How Molecules Became Signs” and related work (English) — Extension.** Deacon explicitly credits Pattee’s separation of symbolic and dynamical constraints as an important precursor to his own work on sign processes and biological information. ([Springer][50])

* **Hierarchy theory and constraint-based thinkers (Salthe, Allen, Starr, Juarrero, Conrad, etc.) — Extension.** Pattee’s enabling-constraint picture radiates outward here more than in mainstream philosophy of physics. 

**Significant appropriations**

* **John von Neumann — precursor rather than descendant, but indispensable.** Pattee explicitly says the deepest historical influence was von Neumann: self-reproducing automata and the arbitrariness/necessity of the cut become Pattee’s own symbol–matter and measurement/control problematic. ([Casci Binghamton][47])

* **Biosemiotics more broadly — Appropriation / Extension.** Pattee did not originally set out to do “biosemiotics,” but the field adopted him as one of its central ancestors. ([Springer][49])

**Intellectual genealogy**

* The clearest descendants are **Cariani, Deacon, Hoffmeyer/Kull-style biosemiotics, and hierarchy/constraint theorists**. This is a real living research line, but it is distributed rather than school-like. ([PETER CARIANI][48])

**Surprising connections / thin lines**

* **Uexküll:** mostly indirect. Pattee does appear in Uexküll-honoring biosemiotic contexts, but I did not find a central direct Uexküll–Pattee line of the same importance as the von Neumann line. ([Binghamton University][51])
* **Philosophy of physics:** the epistemic cut clearly grows from measurement and subject/object problems, but I did not verify a large mainstream philosophy-of-physics uptake. ([Casci Binghamton][47])
* **Peirce:** the bridge is mainly mediated through biosemiotics, not through Pattee alone. 

---

## Bottom line

The **thickest living genealogies** in your set are Uexküll, Rosen, Pattee, and — in a different key — Cassirer. Uexküll ramifies into phenomenology, anthropology, biosemiotics, and medicine; Rosen and Pattee continue as active technical research programs; Cassirer’s *Erkenntnisproblem* survives most powerfully in the history/philosophy of science line running through Koyré and into Kuhn. ([ZBI][12])

The **thin or broken lines** are Bogdanov, late Jünger, Gómez Dávila, and *The Exegesis* — but for different reasons. Bogdanov is broken by historical discontinuity; Jünger is intense but narrow; Gómez Dávila has admirers and one major dialectical opponent but no large school; *The Exegesis* has a serious afterlife, but mostly in religion/esotericism/media theory rather than canonical philosophy or theology. ([Social History Portal][27])

The most surprising connections, to my eye, are these: Uexküll’s strongest biological descendants may be **medicine and biosemiotics** more than cybernetics; Jünger’s strongest late philosophical interlocutor for *An der Zeitmauer* may be **Blanchot** more than “philosophers of history”; Cassirer’s deepest specific descendant for *Erkenntnisproblem* is **Kuhn-via-Koyré**; and Pattee’s most durable legacy may run through **constraint theory and biosemiotics** rather than orthodox philosophy of physics. ([dokumen.pub][1])
