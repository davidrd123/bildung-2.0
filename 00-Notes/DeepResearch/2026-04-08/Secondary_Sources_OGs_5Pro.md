I did the follow-up directly in the older journals, series, and collections. Your diagnosis was right: the older Cassirer layer is real, but it is not a continuous “Cassirer studies” tradition. It is dispersed across direct reviews, geometry/axiomatics/relativity problem-books, Warburg-series publications, and then two big reconcentration points in 1936 and 1949. The densest direct old material is around *Substanzbegriff und Funktionsbegriff* and *Zur Einsteinschen Relativitätstheorie*; for *Das Erkenntnisproblem* the directly indexed German reception is much thinner, with Uebinger’s 1909 review the clearest contemporaneous hit I found. ([JSTOR][1])

Here is the older stratum I would actually target.

### 1. Direct Cassirer-facing journal pieces

* **Richard Hönigswald, “Substanzbegriff und Funktionsbegriff. Kritische Betrachtungen zu Ernst Cassirers gleichnamigen Werk,” *Deutsche Literaturzeitung* 33 (1912), no. 45, cols. 2821–2843; no. 46, cols. 2885–2902.** This looks like the single most important old-style direct critique of *Substanzbegriff* from inside German Erkenntniskritik. ([JSTOR][1])

* **Gerard Heymans, “Zur Cassirerschen Reform der Begriffslehre,” *Kant-Studien* 33 (1928), 109–128.** A later but still contemporaneous assessment, and one of the few older pieces modern Cassirer scholarship still points back to when reconstructing early reception of *Substanzbegriff*. ([De Gruyter Brill][2])

* **Uebinger, “Cassirer, E., *Das Erkenntnisproblem in der Philosophie und Wissenschaft der neueren Zeit*,” *Philosophisches Jahrbuch* 22 (1909), 80–82.** This is the clearest early German review I found for *Erkenntnisproblem*. The field around that book looks much thinner than around *Substanzbegriff*. ([PhilPapers][3])

* **Moritz Schlick, “Kritizistische oder empiristische Deutung der neuen Physik? Bemerkungen zu Ernst Cassirers Buch: *Zur Einsteinschen Relativitätstheorie*,” *Kant-Studien* 26 (1921), 96–111.** This is the essential direct controversy piece on the Einstein book: Schlick taking Cassirer on, in the flagship journal, at the moment the relativity debate is still open. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][4])

### 2. Same-problem interlocutors: not “secondary” in the narrow sense, but exactly the studious pressure sources

* **Richard Hönigswald, *Zum Streit über die Grundlagen der Mathematik: eine erkenntnistheoretische Studie* (Heidelberg: C. Winter, 1912)**, together with **Bruno Bauch’s review** in *Kant-Studien* 18 (1913), p. 517, and **Hans Hahn’s review** in *Monatshefte für Mathematik und Physik* 27 (1916), p. A24. This is one of the best routes into the Hilbert/axiomatics battlefield surrounding Cassirer’s shift from substance to relation/function. ([HathiTrust][5])

* **Hans Reichenbach, *Relativitätstheorie und Erkenntnis apriori* (1920).** This is not a review of Cassirer, but it is exactly the kind of tranche-local interlocutor you said was missing. Reichenbach explicitly says Cassirer’s just-appeared Einstein book is the first attempt by a major neo-Kantian to engage general relativity, and he links Cassirer’s earlier dissolution of physical concepts—especially the substance concept—to relativity. The same book also contains the Hilbert/implicit-definition material that gives the science-method pressure you were after. ([Project Gutenberg][6])

* **Ewald Sellien, *Die erkenntnistheoretische Bedeutung der Relativitätstheorie* (Berlin: Reuther & Reichard, 1919), *Kant-Studien*, Ergänzungsheft 48.** A very good pre-Cassirer neo-Kantian relativity benchmark: Sellien argues that Kant’s account of space and time concerns intuitive space and so is untouched by Einstein’s measurable spaces and times. ([Internet Archive][7])

* **Ilse (Schneider) Lengyel, *Das Raum-Zeit-Problem bei Kant und Einstein* (Berlin: Springer, 1921).** Same-year neo-Kantian relativity book; one of the clearest contemporaneous comparanda for Cassirer’s Einstein book. ([Internet Archive][8])

* **Rudolf Carnap, *Der Raum* (1922).** This is more adjacent than directly polemical, but it belongs in the same shelf. Modern reconstructions of Carnap’s early work repeatedly note its proximity to Cassirer on constitutive principles and the relativized synthetic a priori. ([Springer][9])

* **Alfred C. Elsbach, *Kant und Einstein: Untersuchungen über das Verhältnis der modernen Erkenntnistheorie zur Relativitätstheorie* (1924)**, plus **Einstein’s 1924 review** in *Deutsche Literaturzeitung* 45, 1685–1692, and **Edgar Wind’s 1927 review** in *The Journal of Philosophy* 24, 65–71. This triad is useful because it shows the wider neo-Kantian/relativity battlefield and unexpectedly connects that battlefield back into the Warburg orbit through Wind. ([De Gruyter Brill][10])

### 3. Warburg/Hamburg-adjacent material: Cassirer as participant in a field

This part of your hunch was exactly right. From 1921 onward Saxl organized the *Vorträge* and *Studien der Bibliothek Warburg* with Cassirer’s support, and Cassirer later called the Warburg library the “archimedean point” of his work. ([West 86th Street][11])

* ***Vorträge der Bibliothek Warburg* 1921–1922**: contains Cassirer’s **“Der Begriff der symbolischen Form im Aufbau der Geisteswissenschaften,”** pp. 11–39. This is one of the best places to watch him appear not as an object of commentary but as an active participant in a Hamburg field of inquiry. ([Heidelberger historische Bestände][12])

* ***Vorträge der Bibliothek Warburg* 1922–1923 / I. Teil**: contains Cassirer’s **“Eidos und Eidolon. Das Problem des Schönen und der Kunst in Platons Dialogen,”** pp. 1–27. This is especially relevant if your Cassirer work opens out from *Erkenntnisproblem* into Plato/Renaissance/Warburg continuities. ([Springer][13])

* ***Studien der Bibliothek Warburg*, Band 1**: Cassirer, ****Die Begriffsform im mythischen Denken** (1922)**; and **Band 10**: Cassirer, ****Individuum und Kosmos in der Philosophie der Renaissance** (1927)**. The latter is the key older Warburg-side item for your Renaissance-natural-philosophy line. ([Heidelberger historische Bestände][14])

### 4. Old-style collected volumes: the two places where the dispersed field reconcentrates

* **Raymond Klibansky and H. J. Paton (eds.), *Philosophy and History: Essays Presented to Ernst Cassirer* (Oxford: Clarendon, 1936).** This is probably the single best old-style volume for seeing Cassirer inside a living field rather than as the object of a later industry. It was edited with the support of the Warburg Institute. Two especially useful essays for your purposes are **Johan Huizinga, “A Definition of the Concept of History”** (pp. 1–10) and **Edgar Wind, “Some Points of Contact between History and the Natural Sciences”** (pp. 255–264). It also contains the early bibliography of Cassirer’s writings by **Raymond Klibansky and Walter M. Solmitz**. ([Canadian Centre for Architecture][15])

* **Paul Arthur Schilpp (ed.), *The Philosophy of Ernst Cassirer* (1949).** This is the other major concentration point, and it is much better on the science-method side than people sometimes remember. The table of contents includes **Felix Kaufmann, “Cassirer’s Theory of Scientific Knowledge,”** **Dimitry Gawronsky, “Cassirer’s Contribution to the Epistemology of Physics,”** **Harold R. Smart, “Cassirer’s Theory of Mathematical Concepts,”** **Kurt Lewin, “Cassirer’s Philosophy of Science and the Social Sciences,”** **Walter M. Solmitz, “Cassirer on Galileo,”** and **John H. Randall Jr., “Cassirer’s Theory of History as Illustrated in his Treatment of Renaissance Thought.”** The volume also includes a bibliography of Cassirer’s writings to 1946 compiled by **Carl H. Hamburg and Walter M. Solmitz**. ([Library of Living Philosophers][16])

### 5. Retrieval tools for the dispersed reception

* **Yale’s guide to the Cassirer papers** is more useful than it looks. It explicitly lists folders for **articles/clippings on Cassirer, 1945–53**, **book reviews/clippings, 1945–50**, and an offprint of **Robert Nadeau’s “Bibliographie des Textes sur Ernst Cassirer” (1974)**. That is exactly the sort of mop-up instrument you need once the main old pieces are in hand. ([Yale EAD PDFs][17])

* **The older bibliographies matter.** The 1936 Festschrift carries the Klibansky/Solmitz bibliography of Cassirer’s writings, and Bayer’s survey also points to Nadeau’s 1974 bibliography of critical texts. Those are the best bridges from the canonical items to the scattered reviews and minor notices. ([Filozófia Tanszék][18])

### Bottom line

The “studious OG” layer you want is real, but it is uneven. It is strongest for ****Substanzbegriff und Funktionsbegriff**** and the **relativity book**; it is much thinner for ****Das Erkenntnisproblem**** taken narrowly as a single target of older German commentary. The best acquisition order, for your purposes, would be: **Hönigswald 1912 review; Schlick 1921; Reichenbach 1920; Sellien 1919; Schneider 1921; the Warburg *Vorträge* volumes; *Philosophy and History* (1936); and Schilpp’s *The Philosophy of Ernst Cassirer* (1949).** Riehl matters more as background position and via the Kant/Einstein space-time line than as a major direct Cassirer reviewer in the material I found; Carnap belongs here too, but as a same-problem interlocutor rather than a direct Cassirer polemicist. ([JSTOR][1])

The best next move would be for me to turn this into a **dated acquisition checklist** split into “must get scans now,” “likely library copies,” and “archival follow-up.”
