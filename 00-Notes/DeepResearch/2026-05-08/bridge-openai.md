## Mechanistic interpretability as an empirical instrument

## Empirical computational cognitive linguistics

### Conceptual metaphor theory is now experimentally accessible

The clearest recent bridge from cognitive linguistics to LLMs is the conceptual metaphor literature. “Science is Exploration: Computational Frontiers for Conceptual Metaphor Theory” argues that LLMs can identify and explain conceptual metaphors using annotation-guideline-based prompting, which makes them plausible instruments for large-scale metaphor studies rather than mere text generators. The newer metaphor-identification work extends this by showing that LLMs can partially automate full-text metaphor annotation, and the NLI study on metaphorical inputs demonstrates a crucial methodological check: models may detect a metaphor while still mishandling the inferential consequences of that metaphor downstream. That is exactly the kind of result your project should prize, because it distinguishes superficial labeling from conceptually substantive behavior. citeturn23search2turn23search5turn23search0

### Construction grammar gives you sharper probes than generic capability tests

Construction grammar is emerging as one of the best theory-driven ways to test whether LLMs grasp form-meaning pairings rather than merely memorizing local patterns. “Assessing Language Comprehension in Large Language Models Using Construction Grammar” builds targeted evaluations designed to avoid contamination by pretraining familiarity, and finds that even strong models struggle with abstract constructional meanings when examples are distributionally unfamiliar. “Construction Grammar Evidence for How LLMs Use Context-Directed Extrapolation to Solve Tasks” is relevant for a different reason: it offers a linguistically grounded middle position between hype and dismissal by arguing that model behavior is best understood as extrapolation from priors, guided by prompt context. The cautionary counterpoint is “LLMs Learn Constructions That Humans Do Not Know,” which shows that models can hallucinate construction-like regularities that linguistic theory would reject. citeturn22search1turn22search2turn22search16turn22search15

### Frame semantics is unusually well aligned with interpretability-style questions

Frame semantics is a strong fit for your project because it already treats meaning as structured, relational, context-sensitive, and partially decomposable. “Do LLMs Encode Frame Semantics?” suggests that models can identify frames from context even without explicit task supervision, while “Can LLMs Extract Frame-Semantic Arguments?” shows more mixed results when the task becomes structured argument extraction rather than broad semantic recognition. The reason this matters for mechanistic interpretability is that frame-semantic behavior naturally invites feature localization questions: Which features or circuits track frame evocation, role expectations, or frame competition? The main gap is that the current literature mostly evaluates behavior at the prompt-response level. It rarely follows the question into the internal representation space where your project wants to work. citeturn20search2turn22search7turn20search6

### Image schemas look promising; Talmy-style motion work is still thin

Image-schema research is beginning to provide the kind of embodied, structured probe set that could productively meet mechanistic interpretability. “When Cognition Meets Data” tests whether LLMs encode representations consistent with the CONTAINER and SOURCE-PATH-GOAL schemas, and “Sensorimotor Regularities as Alignment between Humans and Large Language Models” turns image schemas into an evaluation framework for human-model alignment. This is promising because image schemas are much closer to the deep organizational claims in cognitive linguistics than ordinary benchmark tasks. By contrast, my targeted search for Talmy-style motion-event work surfaced mostly classical typology and crosslinguistic background literature rather than comparable LLM-instrument studies. So for motion events the theoretical foundation is strong, but the computational bridge still looks underbuilt. citeturn24search0turn24search2turn21search13turn25search2turn25search5

The most useful classical precedent here is entity["people","Bernard Williams","British moral philosopher"]’s critique of utilitarianism: the target is not mathematics as such, but ethical systems that flatten the texture of agency, integrity, and situated judgment into a decision procedure that appears universal because it has abstracted away what mattered. entity["people","G. ### Fairness literature already has a name for one major failure mode

A good contemporary anchor is Selbst et al.’s “Fairness and Abstraction in Sociotechnical Systems.” Its core claim is that technical fairness interventions often fail because the abstractions inherited from computer science do not preserve the social, legal, and institutional structure of the problem they claim to solve. That is very close to your “constraint versus decoration” distinction. The problem is not that formal models are always bad. The problem is that they can become precise on the wrong object. In an AI interpretability project that wants philosophical seriousness, this paper is a powerful warning against translating concepts like symbol, form, or semiosis into metrics before you know what would count as success, failure, distortion, or omission. citeturn41search1turn41search9

### The formal-rationality critique is directly on point

Nishant and colleagues’ work on the formal rationality of AI-based algorithms is one of the cleanest contemporary statements of the relevant problem. By drawing on Weber’s distinction between formal and substantive rationality, it argues that bias is not only a matter of bad data or bad engineers; it can arise because the optimization machinery itself filters out contextually embedded norms and values. That is directly relevant to your project’s anti-apologetic discipline. A formal apparatus can be genuinely informative when it helps identify where optimization destroys meaning or context, but it becomes ideological when the loss of meaning is hidden by the elegance of the model. This is a more useful frame than generic “ethics of AI” writing because it targets the epistemic mechanics of formalism itself. citeturn41search3turn41search11

### Computational ethics is useful best when it advertises its incompleteness

The expert-interview paper on “Formalizing ethical principles within AI systems” is valuable precisely because it does not assume that codifying ethics is straightforward. It documents cross-disciplinary concern about whether ethical principles can be translated into machine-executable form without distortion, and how such systems should be bounded if they are attempted at all. The nearby “Legal Alignment for Safe and Ethical AI” agenda is relevant as a counterexample to blanket anti-formalism: sometimes formalization is necessary because one needs enforceable constraints, auditable procedures, and institutional accountability. Your project can borrow the right distinction from this literature. Use formalization when it binds action, records assumptions, and exposes failure. Reject it when it merely converts normative ambition into symbolic prestige. citeturn38search0turn38search4turn38search19

## Recursive and structured uses of language models

### urlDSPyturn39search1 is the cleanest “method over promptcraft” framework

Among structured LLM-use frameworks, urlDSPyturn39search1 is the strongest fit for your methodological aims because it treats LM systems as declarative programs and optimization targets rather than as prompt strings to be hand-tuned by intuition. That matters for your project because it provides a disciplined way to build retrieval, translation, comparison, and hypothesis-generation pipelines whose components can be swapped, tested, and evaluated. In other words, it is closer to instrument design than to agent theater. citeturn39search11turn39search1

### Recursive Language Models are the clearest current statement of LM-calling-LM

If you specifically want language-models-calling-language-models, “Recursive Language Models” is the most direct recent paper in the retrieved set. Its core move is to treat long prompts as an external environment that the model can recursively inspect, decompose, and query through subcalls, rather than as a passive context blob. That is relevant well beyond long-context QA. For humanities research, it suggests a way to traverse long archival texts, build staged subquestions, and preserve provenance for intermediate judgments. The methodological attraction is that recursion makes the decomposition explicit. The open problem is control: recursive systems are only as credible as their traceability, stopping criteria, and evaluation regime. citeturn39search0turn39search7turn39search3

### ReAct and least-to-most prompting remain useful because they externalize decomposition

ReAct and Least-to-Most Prompting are older than the rest of this section, but they still matter because they establish two crucial design norms. ReAct interleaves reasoning and action, showing that the model can update its own intermediate state by consulting external tools or corpora. Least-to-Most shows that difficult tasks can often be made tractable by explicitly decomposing them into easier subproblems whose outputs condition later steps. For your project, these are not just capability tricks. They are methodological commitments to exposing intermediate structure. That makes them valuable even if you ultimately implement the same logic inside a more formal stack like DSPy rather than as raw prompts. citeturn40search0turn40search3

### Program of Thoughts and Self-Refine are useful because they make intermediate representations inspectable

Program of Thoughts is valuable because it separates reasoning from computation and forces part of the intermediate state into executable structure. Self-Refine is valuable because it turns revision into a first-class step rather than a hidden hope. Both techniques support the kind of disciplined research workflow you want: retrieval can produce candidate passages, one model call can formulate a structured comparison or translation schema, another can critique it, and a final pass can synthesize a claim that is still inspectable against its intermediate artifacts. The right way to use these methods is not as generic “agents,” but as scaffolds whose internal states can be audited and whose outputs can be ablated, re-run, or falsified. citeturn40search2turn40search1turn39search17
