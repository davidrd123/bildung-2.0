### How to read this paper

It's dense but the architecture is simple. Three passes will get you most of the way:

**Pass 1 — Skeleton.** Skip ahead to §11 and read the list of five categories. Then read §1-2 for the basic move. Now you know the starting point (manifold of sense), the destination (unity in a proposition), and the five waypoints. Don't worry about the rest yet.

**Pass 2 — Vocabulary.** Build a glossary in your own words (use what's below). Don't try to follow the argument yet — just learn the technical terms.

**Pass 3 — Derivation.** Now §3 through §10 become tractable. Peirce is deriving each category by asking "what conception is next, working from being back toward substance?" The test he keeps applying is *prescision*: can I think A without B? If A presupposes B but not vice versa, B is more fundamental.

Optional Pass 4 — §13-§15 apply the categories to logic and signs (Likeness/Index/Symbol). This is where the payoff lives.

### Glossary

**Manifold.** Just "the many" — Kant's term *Mannigfaltigkeit*. The diverse, unorganized stream of impressions before the mind organizes them. Not mathematical.

**Reducing the manifold to unity.** Straight from Kant. The mind's job is taking that chaos and pulling it into one coherent thought or judgment. Conceptions are the tools for doing this. A conception earns its keep only if it's *needed* to achieve unity — otherwise it's a "superfluous fiction" (Peirce's test for whether a category is real).

**Substance.** Not Aristotle's *ousia* and not "stuff." For Peirce it's the bare "it" — the pre-predicate presence of whatever you're attending to. "This stove" *before* you've said anything about it. He says substance "cannot itself be made a predicate" — it can never go on the right side of "is." It's the pure pointing-at, the most immediate thing.

**Being.** Not Heidegger, not capital-B metaphysical Being. For Peirce, "being" is just what the copula "is" expresses — the bare form of joining a predicate to a subject. It has no content of its own; it just licenses the connection. Substance and Being are the two endpoints: substance most immediate (closest to raw experience), being most mediate (most abstract).

**Predicate.** What is said *of* the subject in a proposition.

- "The dog *is barking*"

- "Sugar *is sweet*"

- "Caesar *crossed the Rubicon*"

- "Two *is even*"

The predicate ascribes some character, activity, or relation to the subject.

**Immediate vs. mediate.** A conception is *immediate* if it sits close to raw experience — "this stove" is immediate, you just attend to it. A conception is *mediate* if it requires abstraction — "blackness as such" is mediate, because to apply it to the stove you first have to peel it off as an abstract quality, then refer the stove to it. The whole paper traces a path from substance (most immediate) to being (most mediate), with each intermediate category one step further from sense.

**Prescision (and its cousins).** A very specific kind of mental separation. He distinguishes three, in increasing strength:

- *Discrimination*: just telling two meanings apart. You can discriminate red from color.

- *Prescision*: actively attending to one while ignoring the other. You can prescind space from color (imagine empty uncolored space) but not color from space (a color that's nowhere makes no sense).

- *Dissociation*: imagining one with the other totally absent. You can dissociate red from blue, but not red from color.

Prescision is one-way, and that asymmetry is Peirce's main tool for ordering the categories.

**Ground.** A pure abstraction — a quality considered in itself, not as belonging to anything. "Blackness" as such, not "this black thing." When you say two things "agree in color," *color* is the ground of the agreement — the respect in which they agree. To predicate a quality of a substance, you first abstract the quality into a pure ground, then refer the substance to it. So in Peirce's system, **Quality = reference to a ground.**

**Correlate.** The other thing in a relation. If A is the murderer, the murdered person is the correlate. If *homme* is the relate (the entry being looked up), *man* is its correlate. Comparison always involves a thing-being-compared and a thing-it's-compared-to. **Relation = reference to a correlate.**

**Interpretant.** The mediating representation — the third thing that connects relate and correlate. Comparing "p" and "b" requires imagining one rotated and overlaid on the other; that mediating image is the interpretant. A weathercock represents wind direction *to a mind that knows the convention*; that understanding mind (or the mediating concept in it) is the interpretant. The interpretant is whatever does the interpretive work — what says "this stands for that." **Representation = reference to an interpretant.**

### Where this paper sits in Peirce

It's the keystone of his philosophy. Three reasons it matters:

1. **The three categories.** Quality, Relation, Representation become his famous **Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness** — the trichotomy that runs through all his later metaphysics, phenomenology, and logic. This paper is their first appearance.

2. **The sign theory.** §15 introduces Likeness/Index/Symbol — which become **Icon/Index/Symbol**, the most influential typology in modern semiotics, now standard across linguistics and philosophy of mind.

3. **The interpretant.** Introduced here for the first time. This concept is the engine of his entire semiotic and the seed of pragmatism — meaning is bound up with the interpretive consequence of a sign, not just its referent. The 1878 pragmatism papers, the later phenomenology, the elaborate sign-classifications — all trace back to moves made here.

### One reading tip

Anchor everything to the "stove is black" example. Whenever you get lost, return to it:

- *Stove* — the substance, the bare it

- *Blackness* — the ground (the pure quality, abstracted)

- *Black* — the predicate (the ground applied)

- *Is* — being, the joining

- The whole proposition — unity achieved

Not confusion on his part — it's a substantive thesis. For Peirce, logic *is* a branch of semiotic, and eventually he just identifies them outright: logic, in his mature view, is simply another name for the formal doctrine of signs. The reasoning is direct: all thought happens in signs (you can't think without some representation), so the science of how thought attains truth has to be the science of how signs function correctly.

In this very paper he lays out the trivium that follows from his three categories:

- **Formal grammar** — what makes something a sign with meaning at all (reference to ground)

- **Logic proper** — what makes a sign true (reference to object)

- **Formal rhetoric** (later: *speculative rhetoric* or *methodeutic*) — what makes a sign produce further thought (reference to interpretant)

Modern logic is essentially just that middle branch. Peirce's logic is much broader.

The split you're describing happened for a few reasons:

1. **Mathematical logic took over.** Frege (1879), Russell-Whitehead (1910s), Tarski (1930s). Formal logic became a syntactic-semantic calculus that didn't need a general theory of signs to ground it. Hugely successful, hugely narrowing.

2. **Semiotics developed independently.** Saussure's *Cours* (1916) launched a separate *semiology* rooted in linguistics and the humanities, with no link to logic. Two parallel traditions, barely speaking to each other.

3. **Morris (1938) institutionalized the division.** Charles Morris took Peirce's trichotomy and rebranded it as syntax/semantics/pragmatics — but then logic kept syntax and semantics while pragmatics drifted off to linguistics and philosophy of language. The original Peircean unity got broken into sub-disciplines.

4. **Peirce's late work stayed unpublished.** His mature semiotic sat in manuscripts until decades after his death in 1914. By the time scholars reconstructed it, the field had moved on.

There's actually been some reconvergence — Wittgenstein's later work, speech-act theory, Brandom's inferentialism, recent pragmatist philosophy of language — all of which start to look more Peircean. But mainstream logic and semiotics are still mostly separate disciplines, and most logicians today have no professional reason to read Peirce.

So he didn't mix them up. He had a unified theory that the tradition split apart, partly for principled reasons (the formal calculus really does work on its own) and partly for accidents of which texts got read when.

Examined disciplinary reunification through research bridges and theoretical synthesis

Partially, yes — and several research programs are actively doing it. But "back together" is probably the wrong frame. What you'll get isn't Peirce's system reconstituted; it's selective recovery of his insights using tools he didn't have, while some of the separation turns out to have been justified.

The most explicit bridge-building:

- **Inferentialism** (Brandom, *Making It Explicit*, 1994). Meaning is constituted by inferential role. The interpretant becomes the consequences a sign licenses. Connects formal logic to a pragmatist theory of meaning, with explicit Peircean and Sellarsian debts.

- **Proof-theoretic semantics** (Prawitz, Dummett). Meaning of logical constants given by introduction/elimination rules, not Tarskian models. A formal-logic version of "meaning is in the use."

- **Cognitive semiotics** and **biosemiotics**. Extending triadic sign theory into cognitive science and biology. Peirce is foundational. Hoffmeyer, Deacon, Zlatev, Kull.

- **Categorical logic**. Categories as the natural setting where syntax and semantics live together. Some category theorists explicitly notice the triadic structure.

- **Pragmatism revival in philosophy of language**. Misak, Price, late Putnam. Truth and meaning re-pragmatized.

What blocks full reunification is partly disciplinary, partly principled. Disciplinary: model theory and Saussurean structuralism aren't translations of each other; they're different formalisms with different success criteria. Mathematical logic measures itself by theorems and decidability; semiotics by interpretive yield. Forcing them together risks losing what each does well.

The principled question is whether Peirce was *right* that they belong together. The mainstream analytic view is that formal semantics has done fine without a general theory of signs, and that adding one mostly buys obscurity. The pragmatist counter is that formal semantics has been quietly importing Peircean ideas — use, inference, interpretive role — without crediting them, and would be more honest if it acknowledged the debt. Brandom is essentially making that argument at length.

So the realistic picture: the **vocabulary** of triadic semiotics (icon/index/symbol, sign/object/interpretant) travels well and is now standard across linguistics, cognitive science, and parts of philosophy. The **theoretical core** — interpretant, three categories — survives in inferentialism and proof-theoretic semantics. But Peirce's specific architecture, with logic flanked by formal grammar and formal rhetoric as a single tripartite science, is unlikely to be rebuilt as such. What you'll see instead is a network of partial bridges, each capturing some of what he saw, none reproducing the whole.

### Pass 3: the derivation in §3–§10

The whole architecture works by pinning down two endpoints and asking what must lie between them. Substance is most immediate (the bare *it*), being is most mediate (the empty form of unity in a proposition). Both lack content — substance can never be a predicate, being adds nothing to the subject. They're the two limits. Then the method (§5–§6): walk from being toward substance using the prescision-test, asking at each step "what conception cannot be prescinded from the one below it, while the one below can be prescinded from this one?"

**Step 1 — Quality (§7).** A proposition asserts something *of* a substance: "this stove is black." For *black* to be applied, blackness must first be considered as a pure abstraction — blackness as such. Peirce calls that abstraction the *ground*. Quality is reference to a ground. Test: you can have being without quality (the empty copula, no predicate yet), but no quality without being (a quality is a quality only insofar as it can be predicated). Quality presupposes being but not vice versa.

**Step 2 — Relation (§8).** A quality is only knowable through contrast or similarity with another. The other thing is the *correlate*. Relation is reference to a correlate. Test: you can have a ground without a correlate (a pure quality entertained on its own), but no correlate without a ground (a correlate is correlate-with-respect-to some quality). Relation presupposes quality.

**Step 3 — Representation (§9).** Comparison itself requires a third thing. Comparing *p* and *b* requires imagining one rotated and overlaid; that mediating image is a third element. Looking up *homme* requires the dictionary entry that mediates between *homme* and *man*. The mediating thing is the *interpretant*. Representation is reference to an interpretant. Test: you can have a correlate without an interpretant (mere correspondence in fact), but no interpretant without a correlate. Representation presupposes relation.

**Why the list closes (§10).** This is the move that finishes the system. Reference to an interpretant doesn't add another concept *to* substance — it arises directly from the diversity of impressions. Many impressions held together as ours just *is* their unity in an interpretant. There's no further unification to do. So representation is the last category before you arrive back at substance through the manifold itself.

The five (§11): Being, Quality, Relation, Representation, Substance.

**Bonus — §14, the sign trichotomy.** Here the architecture pays off. He applies the categories to themselves. A relate's reference to ground can be either prescindible from reference to interpretant or unprescindible — same for reference to correlate. Working through the cases yields exactly three kinds of representation: likenesses (related to object by shared quality), indices (related by correspondence in fact), symbols (related by imputed character, requiring an interpretant). The icon/index/symbol distinction isn't stipulated. It falls out of the categorical structure recursing on itself.

### Sidebar: underexplored Peirce

You're right about the existential graphs — there's active work mapping them to string diagrams in monoidal categories, with parallels in the ZX-calculus for quantum computation and in proof nets. Bob Coecke's categorical quantum mechanics has a recognizably Peircean spirit even when it doesn't cite him.

Other areas with live but unmined ore:

**Synechism and the continuum.** Peirce had a deep critique of the Cantor-Dedekind real line. He thought a genuine continuum is stronger than a set of points — points should have no separate existence in true continuity. Connections to smooth infinitesimal analysis (J.L. Bell), synthetic differential geometry, and constructive topology are all natural and underexploited.

**Tychism and evolving laws.** His 1890s metaphysical papers argued that the laws of nature themselves evolved from an initial state of pure chance. Most philosophers found this embarrassing for a long time. Lee Smolin's cosmological natural selection and the broader "evolving laws" literature in physics now look surprisingly Peircean, but the explicit connection has barely been made.

**Theorematic vs. corollarial reasoning.** His distinction between two kinds of mathematical inference — one drawn by inspection of the premises, the other requiring you to *introduce an auxiliary construction not given in the premises*. This is the cleanest articulation of why mathematical proof is creative. The philosophy-of-mathematical-practice movement has barely touched it.

**Logic of vagueness.** He had an explicit logic of vagueness (where contradiction can fail) distinct from his logic of generality (where excluded middle can fail). This anticipated a lot of modern non-classical logic but doesn't get its due in the fuzzy-logic and paraconsistent-logic literatures.

**Speculative rhetoric / methodeutic.** The third branch of his trivium — how signs lead to further signs, the productive dynamics of inquiry. He never finished it. Argumentation theory, philosophy of science as social practice, and even some questions in how reasoning processes propagate touch this territory without connecting back.

**Phaneroscopy.** His 1903 phenomenology, deriving the same three categories by direct inspection of the phaneron rather than by logical argument. Distinct from Husserl, never absorbed by either analytic or continental phenomenology. The bridges have barely been built.

If I had to bet on which directions will pay off most in the next decade, I'd say the existential-graph / categorical-logic work and the synechism / continuum work are most likely to produce real breakthroughs. The evolving-laws cosmology and the methodeutic are wilder bets, but with bigger upside if they hit.

A few major threads still worth surfacing:

**The anti-psychologism (§6).** Easy to miss, but this sentence is crucial: "throughout this process, introspection is not resorted to." Peirce is explicitly refusing to ground his categories in inner experience — anti-psychologism a quarter-century before Frege and Husserl made it famous. His categories aren't inferred from how minds happen to work; they're derived from what's logically required for any thought (any sign-use) to function. This is why the theory survives changes in psychology, and why it can claim to be logic rather than empirical generalization.

**The unity is propositional (§4).** "The unity to which the understanding reduces impressions is the unity of a proposition." This is the deepest break with Kant. Kant said the understanding unifies through *concepts* (the categories themselves). Peirce says no — concepts are abstractions; the actual unit of thought is the *proposition*, predicate joined to subject. Being is just what the copula does. This propositional priority is everything downstream: it's why his logic is a logic of relations, why predication drives the categories, why the interpretant matters (an interpretant always interprets a proposition as standing for another proposition).

**The three argument forms (§15).** This is where the essay's biggest external payoff lives. Peirce identifies three:

- *Deduction* — premises form a general sign (symbol) under which the conclusion is contained.

- *Hypothesis (abduction)* — premises form a likeness (icon) of the conclusion. Schema: *M has features P', P'', P'''; S has those features; therefore S is M.* This is inference to best explanation, in modern jargon.

- *Induction* — premises form an index of the conclusion. Schema: *S', S'', S''' are samples of M; they have property P; therefore all M has P.*

Peirce was the first to identify abduction as a distinct logical form, and he's hooking it up here to the sign-trichotomy. Argument-types and sign-types share one structure. Half of post-1950 philosophy of science is downstream of this move.

**Extension, comprehension, information (end of §15).** Traditional logic had two dimensions of a term — extension (objects denoted) and comprehension (qualities connoted) — and held them in inverse relation. Peirce adds a third: *information*, the synthetic propositions you know about the term. Extension and comprehension are inversely related *only when information is held fixed*. Increasing information lets you increase both. Small move, big consequence: meaning is reorganized around inquiry. Meaning grows with what you learn. This is the seed of his later doctrine that inference itself increases information.

**The realist footnote (§7).** "This agrees with the author of *De Generibus et Speciebus*." Easy to skim, but it's a flag — Peirce is signaling his medieval realism. He thinks blackness (the ground) is *real*, not a mere name. This realism-vs.-nominalism commitment runs through all his work and is the lens through which he reads almost every philosophical opponent. If you're tracking his deep commitments, this footnote is where the realism shows up first.

**Imputed character (§14).** Subtle but load-bearing. Three kinds of qualities arise from how prescision works:

- *Internal* (prescindible from correlate and interpretant) — agreement in a shared character; grounds icons.

- *Relative* (unprescindible from correlate) — opposition / correspondence in fact; grounds indices.

- *Imputed* (unprescindible from interpretant) — the character exists only because the interpretive context imposes it; grounds symbols.

That third notion is where convention and meaning enter the system. A word's meaning is an imputed character — it has the meaning it does only because there's an interpretive practice that imputes it. Symbols are constituted by their interpretants in a way icons and indices aren't.

**The "this it" of substance (§3).** Does more work than it looks like. The "it" is what attention picks out before any predication is possible — not a pronoun, a category. It cannot be made a predicate; only attributes can. This anti-descriptivist treatment of bare reference predates Russell's theory of descriptions by decades and points in a different direction. For Peirce, reference is irreducibly indexical — the "it" is something pointed at, not described. The icon/index/symbol trichotomy at the end of the essay is, in part, a vindication of the indexicality already at work in the very first category.

If I had to rank these by how much they unlock for the rest of his system: anti-psychologism and propositional unity are the methodological keys; the three argument forms are the biggest external payoff; imputed character and realism are the deep philosophical commitments.

It's a recurring failure mode of disciplinary academic philosophy and mathematics — the field's evaluation procedures are tuned to recognize problem-solvers, and they systematically miss the figures whose contribution is the architecture rather than the answer.

Synthesized Peirce's polymathic training, methods, and conditions

Several clusters. None alone explains it, but together they make the synthesis legible.

**Training.** His father Benjamin Peirce was the leading American mathematician of the mid-nineteenth century, a Harvard professor, and he drilled Charles in logic and mathematics from very young. Brent's biography documents the intensity. Young Peirce was reading Kant's first *Critique* by his teens (he claimed to have gone through it cover-to-cover eight times), competent in Greek and German philosophy, and trained to think architectonically. Most philosophers come to philosophy from outside; Peirce came pre-equipped, by a father who treated rigorous thinking as a daily exercise.

**Profession.** He worked thirty years at the U.S. Coast and Geodetic Survey doing precise pendulum measurements of gravity. Hard empirical science, not theory. His fallibilism wasn't a doctrine; it was a working scientist's discipline of confronting measurement error. His abduction was earned at the eyepiece, where you have to generate plausible hypotheses and then test them. Plus chemistry at Harvard's Lawrence Scientific School, and the brief Johns Hopkins logic professorship (1879–84). He had professional-grade competence in multiple sciences and in philosophy simultaneously, which almost no contemporary had.

**Reading.** He read deeply across traditions in their original languages — scholastic Latin (Duns Scotus especially), German (Kant, Hegel), French (Comte), British (Boole, De Morgan, Whately). Most philosophers know one tradition; Peirce knew several. His scholastic realism (the Abelard footnote in 1867) wasn't antiquarian; it was earned by long reading of medieval logic. This gave him resources to draw on whenever a new problem emerged — he was rarely starting from scratch.

**Method.** From early in his career he was building an *architectonic* — a unified system. The 1867 paper isn't an essay on categories; it's an attempt to derive THE categories. The architectonic ambition organized his attention: every new domain he engaged (sign theory, logic of relations, evolution, religion, mathematics) got placed in the same triadic frame. The categories were also self-applying — once he had them, he could recurse them on any new material, the way §14 generates the icon/index/symbol trichotomy by reapplying the categories to themselves. This is a method that scales across interests rather than fragmenting them.

**Cognition.** He thought in diagrams. The existential graphs aren't a late side project; they're how he reasoned. Diagrammatic cognition lets you track relational structure across domains that symbolic notation would compartmentalize. Combined with a deliberate habit of generalizing — every result asked what abstract form it had and where else the form applied — diagrammatic thinking produced the cross-domain transfers that made his work synthetic rather than just broad.

**Conditions.** He was forced out of Johns Hopkins in 1884 (divorce scandal plus difficult-colleague reputation) and never held another academic position. From the 1890s on he lived in poverty with his second wife at Arisbe, his Pennsylvania farm. The cost was severe — no students, no salary, no institutional platform. The benefit was decades of unstructured time. He wrote thousands of pages of manuscript without anyone telling him to specialize or finish. Most academics don't get that kind of time; the ones who do usually misuse it. Peirce produced.

**Pathology.** Brent documents probable trigeminal neuralgia (severe chronic facial pain), opium and probably cocaine use for the pain, manic episodes, and a generally difficult temperament. The pathology cost him careers but plausibly contributed to manic productivity. This is the unromantic side of the synthesizer profile, and many wave-thinkers share something like it.

The combination is what's distinctive. Plenty of people had the training. Plenty had architectonic ambition. Plenty had unstructured time. What Peirce had was all of them at once, with a recursive method that could propagate insights across the domains his cross-training had opened up. The cost was a wrecked career, an unfinished and scattered corpus, and a hundred years of intermittent recovery work.

A useful lens: he was an *unfair* synthesizer, in the sense that almost no one else had the same combination of preconditions. The synthesis isn't easily replicable. It's a singular conjunction of training, profession, method, cognition, and conditions, none of which on its own would have produced it. The closest contemporary analogue might be Leibniz two centuries earlier — same family-trained polymathy, same architectonic drive, same scattered corpus, same multi-wave recovery.

### Reading Chapter IV with the question: does Langer bring anything on top of Peirce?

Short answer: yes, substantially. The chapter develops a positive theory of *presentational symbolism* as a parallel mode of cognition with definite logical properties, defends the cognitive status of art and feeling against logical positivism, and integrates Gestalt psychology in ways Peirce couldn't have. But the gains come with losses — she gives up the unified categorical apparatus that lets Peirce place discursive and non-discursive cognition in one framework, and her account of *how* presentational symbols mean is shallower than Peirce's interpretant doctrine. Six places where the question gets adjudicated.

**1. The discursive/presentational distinction itself (p. 78–79).** Langer's central technical move. Discursive symbolism has vocabulary, syntax, discrete elements with independent meanings, translatability, and primarily general reference. Presentational symbolism has none of these — its elements have no meaning in isolation, no vocabulary or syntax in the strict sense, no translatability, simultaneous rather than successive grasp, and primarily individual rather than general reference.

This is genuinely beyond 1867 Peirce. The 1867 likeness (icon) is a kind of sign within one trichotomy, distinguished from index and symbol by mode of correspondence. Langer's presentational symbolism is a *parallel mode of articulation*, with formal properties distinguishing it from discursive thought as a whole. She's not classifying signs by their object-relation; she's classifying *symbol-systems* by their structural logic. Mature Peirce gets closer with his hypoicon trichotomy (image / diagram / metaphor), which divides icons by structural complexity, but even that doesn't develop the full parallel-mode theory Langer articulates here. *(Hold/verify: hypoicon characterization is from training; should be checked against the 1903 Syllabus and Speculative Grammar manuscripts.)*

**2. The defense of non-discursive cognition against logical positivism (p. 67–71).** This is the polemic Peirce couldn't have made — he died before the *Tractatus*. Langer is taking on Carnap, Wittgenstein, and Russell explicitly: their position is that anything non-discursive is mere expression of emotion, on the level of cries or laughter. She's clearing space for art, music, and metaphysics as genuinely *symbolic* — carrying cognitive content rather than venting feeling. The Carnap citation comparing lyrical poetry to laughing is what she's pushing back against. Peirce's apparatus could be turned to this defense, but he didn't have to make it. The positive thesis (presentational symbolism as articulate) is the alternative she's offering.

**3. The cognitive theory of art and feeling (p. 81–82).** Langer's central positive claim: art, especially music, is a presentational symbolism for *feeling*. The forms of feeling — their dynamic patterns, tensions, resolutions — can be presented through music in a way language cannot articulate. Music doesn't express the composer's emotion; it presents a conception of how feeling moves. This is the project that becomes *Feeling and Form* (1953).

This is genuinely beyond Peirce. He had the iconic apparatus to handle this kind of structural mapping, but he didn't develop the theory of art-as-cognition or the application to musical form. The cognitive status of feeling is also new. Peirce had Firstness (in mature work) as the category of pure quality / feeling, but didn't develop a theory of how feelings are articulable through symbolic forms. Langer is making feeling cognitively respectable. *(Hold/verify: Peirce's lack of developed art-theory; should be checked against the Lowell Lectures and aesthetic material in the Collected Papers.)*

**4. The integration with Gestalt psychology (p. 73–74).** She draws on Wertheimer, Köhler, Koffka to argue that the eye and ear themselves make symbolic abstractions — perception is already presentational symbolism at work. This brings twentieth-century empirical psychology into a theory of meaning in a way Peirce couldn't have, and gives her a naturalistic ground for non-discursive cognition. Peirce had a theory of perceptual judgment as a kind of abductive inference, and his mature account of icon/diagram/metaphor was sophisticated, but the explicit fusion with Gestalt empirical work is Langer's.

**5. The four modes (p. 82–83).** She closes by setting up the rest of the book around four symbol-modes: *language, ritual, myth, music*. Each is to be studied as a distinct mode of presentational or discursive symbolism. This is Cassirer-derived — the *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms* studies myth, language, knowledge as distinct modes — but applied with sharper formal tools. Peirce doesn't do this. His architectonic places everything in one triadic frame and traces the categories through different domains, but he doesn't develop a theory of symbol-modes-as-cultural-formations. The Cassirerian dimension is genuinely additive.

**6. What Langer gives up.** The discursive/presentational distinction is sharp but it cuts. Once you've made it, you have two parallel kinds of symbolism with no common formal theory — Langer doesn't develop a unified account of how both modes participate in semiosis. Peirce's apparatus does that: icon, index, symbol are three modes of a single relation (sign-object), not two opposing classes. The triadic frame keeps continuity.

The interpretant problem returns here. Langer's presentational symbolism produces understanding, but she doesn't have a developed theory of the interpretive consequences of a presentational symbol — what Peirce calls the interpretant chain. *How* does a musical phrase mean? Langer says it presents a conception of feeling, but she doesn't articulate the structure of the resulting interpretive process. Peirce's chain — immediate, dynamic, final interpretant — provides exactly the structure she lacks.

She also loses continuity of semiosis. Her sharp human/animal distinction (signs for animals, symbols for humans, from Chapter III) gets reinforced by the discursive/presentational split. Both cuts together produce a categorical break that Peirce's triadic apparatus refuses. Whether that's a feature or a bug depends on whether you take biosemiotics seriously.

### Honest assessment

Langer brings real things on top of Peirce: the parallel-mode theory of presentational symbolism, the defense of non-discursive cognition against logical positivism, the cognitive status of art and feeling, the integration with Gestalt, and the Cassirerian symbol-modes architecture. Several of these required twentieth-century resources Peirce didn't have. They aren't just Peirce-in-different-vocabulary.

But the gains come with losses: unified categorical apparatus, interpretant chain, continuity of semiosis. The right reading isn't "Langer corrects Peirce" or "Peirce already did Langer." It's that they're solving different problems with overlapping but non-identical resources, and a strong theoretical synthesis would draw from both — Peirce's triadic continuity for the formal apparatus, Langer's discursive/presentational distinction for the parallel modes of cognition, with the interpretant chain doing the work of explaining how presentational symbolism actually means.

That synthesis hasn't been done well, to my knowledge. ### Cassirer's Einleitung I, pages 1–7: bounded reading

This is one of the densest stretches in the symbolic-forms project. The seven pages compress the whole post-Kantian backstory into a programmatic argument. Walking it.

**The historical narrative (pp. 1–3).** Cassirer opens with *Sein* (Being) as the starting point of philosophy — the moment when consciousness recognizes a unity of being against the multiplicity of beings. But pre-Socratic philosophy stays trapped: it picks out one privileged being (water, atom, number) and derives all others from it. The single grounding being is a "*methodisches Zwitterwesen*" — a methodological hybrid stuck between physical and ideal. Plato is the breakthrough. With Plato, *Sein* itself becomes the problem rather than something taken as given. From Plato on, thought has its own form, and that form determines the form of being.

This historical opening is doing real work. It's establishing that the Cassirer move — making the apparatus of cognition itself the problem — has a deep philosophical pedigree. It's not modernist invention.

**The post-naive turn in science (pp. 3–5).** The same dialectical pattern repeats in modern science. What looks at one stage like ultimate axioms becomes the next stage's problem. Being itself goes "in flux" — *only as goal, not as starting point*. The decisive consequence: the basic concepts of every science cease to be passive copies of given being and become *selbstgeschaffene intellektuelle Symbole* — self-created intellectual symbols. This is the load-bearing phrase of the whole introduction.

Hertz's *Principles of Mechanics* is the witness. We make "inner *Scheinbilder oder Symbole*" of external objects. The agreement between symbol and thing isn't pictorial similarity but a complex logical-relational expression. Helmholtz's theory of signs gets cited similarly — the language of "image" stays, but the image-concept itself has transformed. *(The Hertz citation is direct and substantial in Cassirer, p. 4.)*

**The plurality problem and the new unity (pp. 5–6).** Each science has its own mode of questioning and forming. The physical object isn't identical with the chemical or biological. So the demand for unity-of-being seems to dissolve into plurality. Cassirer reframes: unity can no longer come from a common simple object — instead, it must come from a *system* whose members in their necessary difference mutually condition and require each other. The crucial move is the substitution of *funktionelle Einheit* (functional unity) for unity of substrate / unity of origin.

This is the post-Kantian critique of substance metaphysics in compressed form, and it's the deep thread that links everything else in the chapter.

**Beyond cognition (pp. 6–7).** Then the move that makes this distinctively Cassirer's project. Knowledge — even taken as broadly as possible — is *only one kind of Formgebung* (formation, form-giving) within the totality of spirit's grasping of being. Cognition subsumes the particular under universal law. But there are *other* modes of objectivation that reach universally-valid content on a different path: not through logical concept and law.

The list: *Kunst, Sprache, Mythos, Erkenntnis* (art, language, myth, knowledge). And — explicitly — religion. Each has an "*ursprünglich-bildende, nicht bloß eine nachbildende Kraft*" — an originally-creating, not merely re-creating force. Each lives in its own *Bildwelten* (image-worlds), which it produces according to its own independent principle. None reduces to another. Each constitutes its own side of the "real." They are not different ways a thing-in-itself reveals itself; they are the paths spirit follows in its self-revelation.

That's the program announced.

### Peirce 1867 parallels

The structural parallels with the 1867 paper are striking, especially given that Cassirer doesn't cite Peirce here.

**Being and substance as limit concepts.** Cassirer opens with *Sein* as the starting point that becomes the problem; Peirce 1867 §3–4 has Substance and Being as the two limit conceptions, both contentless. Both reject taking them as given essences.

**Anti-essence move.** Cassirer's pre-Socratic critique (don't take some privileged being as ground) is structurally identical to Peirce's anti-nominalism in 1867 (don't take qualities as mere names; don't take any individual being as bottom). Both refuse essence-hunting.

**Substance metaphysics → relational/functional theory.** Cassirer's *funktionelle Einheit* replacing unity-of-substrate is doing the same work as Peirce's reference-to-ground / reference-to-correlate / reference-to-interpretant. Both shift from substance to relational structure.

**Symbol-as-self-created.** Cassirer's *selbstgeschaffene intellektuelle Symbole* (the basic concepts of science as self-created symbols, not passive copies) is structurally identical to Peirce's interpretant doctrine — meaning is constituted by the interpretive function, not given by a pre-existing real. Both are post-Kantian.

**Manifold to unity.** Cassirer's "rule governing the manifold of cognitive functions" is exactly Peirce's "function of conceptions is to reduce the manifold of sensuous impressions to unity" (1867 §1). Same Kantian inheritance, same problem framing.

The parallels are deep enough to suggest these are two independent realizations of the same post-Kantian program, with very different stylistic and substantive emphases. *(Hold/verify: claim that Cassirer doesn't cite Peirce here; need to check the volume's index and footnotes more broadly.)*

### Langer parallels — and a reframing

Once you've read Cassirer's Einleitung, much of what looked distinctively Langer in Chapter IV resolves into Cassirer-with-Anglophone-tools.

**Selbstgeschaffene intellektuelle Symbole → "meaning is a function of a term."** Langer's anti-essentialist move is Cassirer's, transposed into Russell-Whitehead-Wittgenstein vocabulary.

**Funktionelle Einheit → presentational symbolism.** Langer's discursive/presentational binary is one cut through Cassirer's multi-form architecture. Cassirer has *Kunst, Sprache, Mythos, Erkenntnis* as parallel modes, each with its own *Bildwelt*. Langer narrows this to a binary (discursive vs. presentational) for analytic clarity but loses the multi-form differentiation.

**Bildwelten → presentational forms.** Cassirer's "image-worlds" is what Langer calls presentational symbolism. The cognitive status of art, myth, music in Langer is direct Cassirer inheritance.

**Geisteswissenschaften framing.** Cassirer ends the Einleitung by announcing a "general philosophy of the *Geisteswissenschaften*." Langer's four modes (language, ritual, myth, music) are exactly that program in English-speaking dress.

### Cassirer's Einleitung I, pages 8–16: bounded reading

Picking up from where 1–7 left off. This section is doing structural work — it's setting up the *need* for a theory of symbolic forms by showing what philosophy would otherwise be stuck with. Several major moves.

**Kant's Copernican turn, generalized (pp. 8–9).** Cassirer reads Kant as having flipped the cognition/object relation: start from the law of cognition, not from the object. But the object Kant got is purely logically determined — the form of objectivity that fits mathematical physics. Even Kant himself, in moving to the three Critiques, had to broaden the program. The kingdom of freedom (second Critique), the kingdom of art (aesthetic judgment), the kingdom of organic nature (teleological judgment) — each opens a new side of *Wirklichkeit*. The total spirit doesn't get given in a single formula; it unfolds in the work of critique itself.

The decisive generalization: the Copernican turn applies "*mit gleichem Grund und Recht*" to every direction and principle of spiritual formation. The question that organizes all the domains is the same: do we understand *Funktion* from *Gebilde* or *Gebilde* from *Funktion*? The principle of the "*Primat der Funktion vor dem Gegenstand*" — primacy of function over object — has to be reground in each special domain: pure cognition, linguistic thought, mythic-religious thought, artistic intuition.

**Critique of Reason becomes Critique of Culture (pp. 9–10).** This is one of the famous lines: "*Die Kritik der Vernunft wird damit zur Kritik der Kultur*." Philosophy seeks to understand how all content of culture, insofar as it's grounded in a general form-principle, presupposes an originary act of spirit. The shift is from *Weltbegriff* to *Kulturbegriff* — from world-concept to culture-concept. And under the culture-concept, content is inseparable from the fundamental directions of spiritual production: *Sein ist hier nirgends anders als im Tun erfaßbar* — being is here nowhere graspable except in doing. The aesthetic object exists only insofar as there's a specific direction of aesthetic intuition. The religious object only through religious form-giving. And so on.

The unity-question gets reframed accordingly. The center of philosophy can lie not in any given being but only in a common task. Language, science, myth, art, religion become members of a single great problem-context — all of them attempts to transform the passive world of mere impressions into a world of pure spiritual expression.

**The "inner form" generalized (pp. 10–11).** Modern philosophy of language posited an *innere Sprachform* (Humboldt's term, picked up by Steinthal and the German linguistics tradition). Cassirer extends: an analogous "inner form" must be sought for religion, myth, art, scientific cognition. This isn't the sum of phenomena in each domain but the *conditioning law* of their construction. *(Hold/verify: the Humboldt-to-Steinthal lineage and Cassirer's specific use of "inner form" should be checked against the Humboldt section in 0115 and against secondary literature on Cassirer's neo-Kantian sources.)*

**The conflicts of culture (pp. 11–12).** A diagnostic move that's distinctively Cassirer's. Each fundamental form of spirit, when it emerges, tries to give itself not as a part but as a whole. Each claims absolute validity. This is what produces the *conflicts of culture* and the *antinomies of the culture-concept*. Science emerges using language as material but must necessarily go beyond it; a new *Logos* governed by a different principle than linguistic thought arises and develops, and against this Logos the formations of language come to look like mere obstructions and limits. Same pattern in the other domains. Religion separates from art (Xenophanes against anthropomorphic gods). Each domain achieves its identity through *Kampf* with the others.

This is a historical-philosophical claim that has no real Peircean counterpart and goes beyond Langer.

**Critique of dogmatic metaphysics (pp. 12–13).** The dogmatic systems of metaphysics fail to resolve these conflicts because they themselves stand *within* the conflicts rather than above them. They are *metaphysische Hypostasen* of one principle (logical, aesthetic, or religious). The more they enclose themselves in abstract universality, the more they close themselves off from the concrete totality of cultural forms.

What's needed is a standpoint above all forms but not *simply beyond* them — that lets us see the whole with one glance, making visible the immanent relations among forms rather than their relation to some transcendent being. Each form would get its meaning purely through its position in the system, by the richness of its relations with other spiritual energies.

**Descartes to Hegel: the reduction trap (pp. 13–14).** Cassirer surveys the modern philosophical tradition: Descartes' *ingenii limites definire* (to define the limits of mind) becomes the watchword of modern philosophy. But Descartes makes spirit collapse into pure thinking (*cogitatio*). The whole rationalist tradition reduces the systematics of spirit to the systematics of thought. Hegel completes the arc — the *Phenomenology of Spirit* gives the manifold of spiritual forms but ultimately runs to a single logical apex, the absolute concept. So even in Hegel, only the form of the logical, the form of the concept, gets genuine autonomy.

**The dilemma (pp. 14–15).** Hold to logical unity and you erase the specificity of each domain. Submerge in the individuality and you lose unity, get only atomistic history (history of language, of religion, of art, separately). Either way, philosophy of cultural forms collapses.

### Cassirer's Chapter I §V: Wilhelm von Humboldt

This is structural ground for the whole project. Cassirer reads Humboldt as solving — already in the early 19th century — the philosophical problem the Einleitung set up. Humboldt mediates three Kantian oppositions through language, and several of his moves turn out to be load-bearing for the Peirce-Langer-Cassirer triangle.

**The three oppositions (pp. 99–100).** Cassirer organizes the Humboldt section around three principled oppositions Humboldt seeks to reconcile through linguistic study:

1. Individual vs. objective spirit

2. Subject vs. object (subjectivity vs. objectivity in cognition)

3. Matter vs. form (*Stoff vs. Form*)

The third one is where the Peirce material lives.

**First opposition: individual / objective spirit (pp. 100–101).** Each individual speaks their own language but, in using it freely, becomes aware of an inner spiritual binding. Language is mediator — between infinite and finite nature, between one individual and another. Humboldt's striking metaphysical claim (which Cassirer quotes at length): I and Thou are not merely reciprocally requiring concepts but, traced back to the point of separation, *truly identical*. Language is the proof that humanity has no purely separated individuality. Each nation is a spiritual form of humanity, individualized with respect to ideal totality through a particular language.

This is more metaphysical than anything in 1867 Peirce or in Langer Chapter IV. It's where Cassirer's neo-Kantian project shows its Hegelian inheritance.

**Second opposition: subject / object — the transcendental correlation (pp. 101–104).** The Kantian core. Cassirer reads Humboldt as standing on the ground of the critical analysis of cognitive faculties, in Kant's own *fruchtbare[r] Bathos der Erfahrung* (fruitful bathos of experience). The decisive move: the metaphysical opposition of subjectivity and objectivity is replaced by their *pure transcendental correlation*. Just as for Kant the object as appearance isn't external to cognition but is constituted through cognition's own categories, so language for Humboldt isn't a mere barrier between us and objective being but is a *means of forming*, of *Objektivierung* of sensory impressions.

The famous Humboldt thesis follows: languages are not means to represent already-known truth but means to discover yet-unknown truth. Their difference is not in sounds and signs but in *Weltansichten* themselves.

**Leibnizian inflection (pp. 102–103).** Cassirer reads Humboldt as returning, via Kant and Herder, from Leibniz's narrowly logical view of language to a deeper universal-idealist conception grounded in Leibniz's own principles. As the universe is given only in the monads' perspectives, and as the totality of these perspectives plus their harmony constitute objectivity, so each language is an individual *Weltansicht*, and only the totality of all linguistic Weltansichten constitutes our reachable concept of objectivity. Humboldt: the subjectivity of all humanity becomes itself objective.

This is a real philosophical move beyond what Peirce 1867 reaches. Peirce's apparatus doesn't engage the diversity of natural languages; he abstracts directly to logical structure. Cassirer through Humboldt gets a theory in which different formal-linguistic structures constitute different objectivities. *(Hold/verify: the comparison with Peirce here is interpretive; mature Peirce on linguistic relativism / typology is a different question I'm not in a position to settle.)*

**The genetic method and Energeia (pp. 104–106).** This is the famous formulation. Language is *kein Werk (Ergon), sondern eine Tätigkeit (Energeia)* — not a work but an activity. Definition can therefore only be genetic. Words don't transfer ready-made content like substances; they *stimulate* (anregen) the hearer to form the corresponding concept with independent power. Humboldt's metaphor (which Cassirer quotes): humans understand each other not by giving each other actual signs of things, nor by determining each other to produce identical concepts, but by mutually touching the same link in the chain of sensory representations and inner concept-productions — striking the same key of their spiritual instrument, on which similar but not identical concepts spring forth.

This is one of the deep roots of the entire functional/pragmatic tradition that runs through Peirce-Cassirer-Langer. The Energeia/Ergon move is upstream of:

- Cassirer's *Sein nur im Tun*

- Langer's "meaning is a function of a term"

- Mature Peirce's pragmatic maxim

- Brandom's inferentialism

All of them inherit Humboldt's *Energeia*. *(Hold/verify: lineage claim is from training synthesis; should be checked against secondary literature on Humboldt's influence on Peirce specifically — Peirce did read German philosophy seriously and would have known Humboldt at least secondhand through neo-Kantian sources.)*

**The Satz as bearer of meaning (p. 106).** This is the move that connects directly to Peirce 1867 §4. Cassirer's Humboldt: it is fundamentally never the individual word but the *Satz* that is the true bearer of linguistic sense, because in the Satz the *original power of synthesis* reveals itself, on which all speaking and understanding ultimately rests.

This is the same claim Peirce 1867 §4 makes — the unity to which the understanding reduces impressions is the unity of a proposition. Same destination, independent realization, both reading Kant.

**Third opposition: matter and form, and the work of the copula (pp. 106–108).** The most directly Peirce-relevant material. Cassirer's reading: for Kant, form is a relation-expression (*Verhältnisausdruck*), but precisely because all our knowledge of appearances ultimately resolves into knowledge of temporal-spatial relations, form constitutes the *objectifying principle* of cognition. Unity of form, as unity of connection, grounds unity of object. The connection of a manifold can never come through the senses; it's always an act of spontaneity of the representation-power. To characterize this transcendental-subject-grounded yet objective form of connection, Kant relies on the unity of judgment, hence indirectly on the unity of the *Satz*.

Linguistically, this unity expresses itself *in der Kopula des Urteils, in dem Verhältniswörtchen "ist"* — in the copula of judgment, in the little relation-word "is." Through this "is," a fixed and unsublatable holding-together of the judgment is posited; what's expressed is that the matter is a belonging-together of representations, not their merely accidental psychological co-occurrence.

This is essentially what Peirce 1867 §4 says about the copula. Both Peirce and Cassirer-via-Humboldt are reading Kant's transcendental deduction and locating the work of objectivity in the copula's act of joining. Different vocabulary, same site.

**Humboldt extends Kant's copula-claim over all language (p. 108).** In every developed language, a special work and formal determination must be added to the bare designation of a concept by material features — by which the concept is placed in a category of thinking (substance, attribute, activity). This placement-in-category is "a new act of linguistic self-consciousness" through which the individual word is referred to the totality of possible cases.

This is Humboldt's generalization of the Kantian copula-doctrine: not just the copula "is" but every grammatical category-marker does this work. Form has *priority* over matter, but it's a priority of validity, not of empirical-temporal existence — both are co-present in any actual language.

### What the Humboldt section adds to the triangle

**The Satz / copula site is shared.** Both Peirce 1867 §4 and Cassirer-via-Humboldt locate the unity of meaning in the Satz / proposition, and both identify the copula as the site where transcendental-Kantian objectivity is constituted. This is a remarkable convergence — two independent readings of Kant's transcendental analytic that locate the same load-bearing point. The 1867 paper and the Humboldt section could be read as parallel chapters in the same post-Kantian project.

The difference: Peirce abstracts directly to formal categories (substance, being, quality, relation, representation). Humboldt-Cassirer stays at the level of the Satz and its grammatical realizations across natural languages. Peirce gives you a logic of categories; Humboldt-Cassirer gives you a comparative philosophy of grammar.

**The Energeia move is the deep root.** The substance-to-function shift that runs through Cassirer-Peirce-Langer is, traced back, a Humboldt move. Language as Energeia (activity), not Ergon (work). Meaning as activity, not substance. Definition as genetic, not analytical. This formulation, which Cassirer treats as Humboldt's signature insight, is the underlying form of the move that:

- Cassirer makes when he says *Sein nur im Tun*

- Langer makes when she says meaning is a function of a term

- Peirce makes (more rigorously, through the interpretant) in the move from substance to relational categories

- Mature Peirce makes again in the pragmatic maxim — meaning of a conception is the conceivable practical effects

**Humboldt's I/Thou identity adds something none of the others have.** Humboldt's metaphysical claim that language demonstrates the original identity of I and Thou — that individuality is constituted *through* the linguistic medium that also unifies — is something Peirce, Langer, and even Cassirer (in his more analytical moments) don't quite articulate. It's the social-relational ground of meaning, and it's something that the actual-questions file's biosemiotics question (4) would benefit from.

**Different ambitions become legible.** Peirce wants a unified categorical apparatus that handles all semiosis from biology to logic. Humboldt-Cassirer want a comparative philosophy of cultural-linguistic Weltansichten. Langer wants an Anglophone analytic theory of symbol modes. The Humboldt section makes these differences sharper because it shows the common Kantian root and where each diverges.

### Cassirer's Chapter V: the copula meets Peirce

This is the keystone reading. Three normalized surfaces, but a single argumentative arc: the copula and predicative synthesis as the site where pure relational being emerges from substantial being, traced both philosophically (back to Parmenides, forward through Kant and Fichte) and linguistically (across language families). #### 0151: Sentence over word, relation over substance (pp. 280–293)

**The Primat of the Satz (pp. 280–281).** Cassirer opens with the same claim that Humboldt made and that Peirce 1867 §4 makes in different vocabulary: not the word but the *Satz* is the original element of all linguistic formation. Speech isn't composed from prior words; words emerge out of the whole of speech. Cassirer cites Humboldt directly. He then notes the convergent witness from empirical-psychological analysis, language history, and the Aristotelian principle that the whole is prior to the parts. Language meets us, however far back we trace it, *immer schon als geformte Einheit* — already as formed unity.

**Even isolating languages aren't formless (pp. 282–284).** A digression on Chinese, which long served as the supposed example of "formless" languages where words simply stand next to each other. Cassirer dismantles this: Chinese isn't formless; *Wortstellung* (word order) carries the grammatical relations with extreme precision. Logically considered, word-order may even be the *most* adequate means of expressing pure grammatical relations — relations that, as relations, no longer need their own representational substrate. Humboldt himself said Chinese's apparent absence of grammar heightens the inner sense of formal connection. *Forma dat esse rei* — the old scholastic principle — finds new application: matter without form is only a limit-concept of method, with no actual independent existence in language. *(Hold/verify: forma-dat-esse-rei attribution; Cassirer doesn't name a specific source and the phrase has multiple medieval lineages.)*

**Suffixes: from substance to relation (pp. 285–287).** The empirical heart of the argument. In flexional languages, suffixes that now express pure categorical determinations (quality, attribute, manner) etymologically derive from concrete substantives. The substantial origin gets gradually forgotten — sometimes through actual linguistic "misunderstandings" (his example: German *-keit* arising from a mishearing in *êwic-heit*). But this *Vergessen* (forgetting) is itself a positive linguistic achievement: the *Übergang vom substantialen Ausdruck zum reinen Beziehungsausdruck* — the transition from substantial expression to pure relation-expression. The psychological darkening of the substantial becomes the logical means by which the relational expression is built.

This is one of the most striking moves in the chapter. It's the same shift Peirce 1867 traces logically — substance (most immediate) to being (most mediate), with the intermediate categories as relational structures — but Cassirer is showing it as an empirical-historical process in actual languages.

**Polysynthetic vs. flexional vs. parataxis-hypotaxis (pp. 288–293).** Cassirer surveys: polysynthetic languages over-prioritize synthesis (the whole sense compressed into a single Satzwort) at the cost of generality; flexional languages achieve a "labile equilibrium" between content-expression and relation-expression. Then a parallel survey of the parataxis-to-hypotaxis development. Early languages and child language run on simple coordination — Satz next to Satz, "asyndetic" connections. The hypotactic period-style is a late achievement, missing in early Greek (Homeric vs. Attic prose), missing in Sanskrit's relative-clause-poor structure, missing in many language families. The relative pronoun is everywhere a late and rare formation. Languages reach the pure category of *Relation* only haltingly, by detour through substance and attribute.

This material gives Cassirer something Peirce 1867 doesn't have: empirical evidence that the substance-to-relation movement is a *real historical-linguistic process*, not just a logical reconstruction. Languages do this themselves, slowly, over centuries.

#### 0152: The copula and predicative synthesis (pp. 293–295)

This is where the Peirce connection becomes nearly explicit.

**The copula as the form of pure synthesis (p. 293).** Cassirer's framing sentence: the form whose fundamental meaning separates principally from all thingly-substantial expression to serve only the expression of synthesis as such, of pure connection — this is the *Kopula*. Only in the use of the copula does the logical synthesis performed in the *Urteil* gain its adequate linguistic designation.

**The Kant quotation (pp. 293–294).** Cassirer then quotes at length from Kant's transcendental deduction (B141–142). The judgment, says Kant, is nothing other than the way of bringing given cognitions to the *objective unity of apperception*. The little relation-word "*ist*" (the *Verhältniswörtchen ist*) targets exactly this: distinguishing the objective unity of given representations from the merely subjective. The example: "*Der Körper ist schwer*" doesn't mean that body and weight always co-occur in subjective perception, but that they are joined *im Objekt*, in the object itself.

This is the same Kant passage that grounds Peirce 1867 §4. Both Peirce and Cassirer are reading the transcendental deduction's claim that objective unity is constituted in the act of judgment, with the copula as the linguistic site of that act. They take it in different directions — Peirce derives the categories formally, Cassirer traces it through linguistic history — but they're working from the same Kantian root.

**The linguistic backwardness of the copula (pp. 294–295).** Then Cassirer's distinctive contribution: language reaches the abstraction of pure being expressed in the copula only very gradually. Many languages — Negerlanguages, American native languages, Ural-Altaic — have no copula in our logico-grammatical sense. They use simple juxtaposition: "the city big" for "the city is big," "I man" for "I am a man." Even where something copula-like exists, it often carries concrete locative or temporal coloring — different "copulas" for standing vs. sitting vs. lying subjects, awake vs. sleeping. Pure relational being is a late linguistic achievement.

#### 0153: Being and language (pp. 295–300)

**The philosophical problem behind the linguistic one (pp. 295–296).** Cassirer makes the move that ties everything together. Even where language has unified all the locative/temporal/modal expressions of existence into a general being-expression, a gap remains between any expression of mere *Dasein* and "Sein" as the expression of pure predicative synthesis. This isn't just a linguistic problem; it's a philosophical problem stretching back to the Eleatics. Cassirer surveys: Parmenides trying to break free of language to think pure being and failing because his own *ἔστι τὸ εἶναι* mixes the verbal-predicative and substantive-absolute senses. Plato in the *Sophist* finally articulating the logical nature of pure relation-concepts. Medieval essence/existence debates. The ontological argument as the sharpest form. Kant's critique. **The empirical etymology of "is" (pp. 297–298).** Cassirer cites Brugmann on the descent of substantial verbs into the copula. The copula's formal-relational meaning emerges as the substantial-verbal meaning fades — *Herabsinken des Verbums zur Kopula*, the verb's descent into copula. **2. Talmy's open-class / closed-class distinction = Cassirer's content/relation distinction.** Talmy (*Toward a Cognitive Semantics*, 2000) distinguishes:

- Open-class elements (nouns, verbs — lexical content, indefinitely extensible)

- Closed-class elements (grammatical morphemes — structural meaning, fixed inventory)

Closed-class elements specify the *schematic structure* of the conceived event. Different languages package this structure differently — Talmy's typology of motion event encoding (verb-framed vs. satellite-framed) is the showpiece. His **force dynamics** (in the same volume) handles causation, permission, hindrance, agonist/antagonist relations as schematic structures encoded in verbs and modals. This is one of the most concrete versions of "Beziehungsausdruck" anywhere.

### Hoffmeyer's "On Biosemiotics" — bounded reading

Chapter 1 of Hoffmeyer's *Biosemiotics* (2008). This is the programmatic framing chapter — strong claims, but the real case is deferred to later chapters. The substance is in three moves: a definition, a genealogy, and a defense against four objections. Reading it through our triangle question.

#### What biosemiotics is

Hoffmeyer's core claim: life is fundamentally grounded in semiotic processes. Cultural sign processes are special instances of a broader biosemiosis that pervades the biosphere, not a separate kind. The defining concept is the **semiosphere** — a sphere of sign processes and elements of meaning that constitutes the frame within which biology must work. Hoffmeyer distinguishes his use from Lotman's narrower cultural usage; for him, the semiosphere is genuinely biological, with sign exchange happening at every scale from molecule to organism to ecosystem.

The strong methodological claim: biosemiotics doesn't contradict molecular biology, but argues that molecular processes *cannot be exhaustively described in chemical terms*, because their participation in life's organization makes them carriers of semiotic relationships that chemistry alone can't track.

#### The genealogy

Hoffmeyer is explicit about predecessors: **C.S. Peirce, Jakob von Uexküll, Gregory Bateson**. None of them used "biosemiotics" — the term comes from Rothschild (1962), gained traction in Russian semiotic literature in the 1970s (Stepanov), and proliferated only in the 1990s (Sebeok). Hoffmeyer claims biosemiotics is *anchored in the evolutionary philosophy of Peirce* rather than in Whitehead's process philosophy, even though both are process-philosophical.

The Peirce specific to Hoffmeyer is the late metaphysical Peirce — the cosmology, the "tendency of nature to take form habits," the evolutionary cosmism. This is *not* the 1867 Peirce we've been working with. It's the same author at a different moment, and the difference matters: 1867 derives categories logically; the 1890s Peirce builds an evolutionary metaphysics where habits, laws, and forms emerge through cosmic semiotic processes. Hoffmeyer's biosemiotics is downstream of that move, not of the 1867 derivation.

#### The four defenses

The chapter is mostly structured as defense against four objections. Each is interesting for what it reveals about Hoffmeyer's positioning.

**Against reductionism (humanists' worry).** No, biosemiotics is reductive only if you define signs narrowly as linguistic symbols. Hoffmeyer cites *Terence Deacon's Symbolic Species* (1997) and the icon/index/symbol distinction: animals share iconic and indexical reference with humans; symbolic reference is what's distinctive to humans. So humanity's uniqueness is preserved as a special form of sign-usage rather than being denied. *(This is exactly the move that addresses your actual-questions file's question 7 — Deacon as the bridge between Hoffmeyer's biological semiosis and Langer's human-symbolic discontinuity.)*

**Against vitalism (biologists' worry).** No — biosemiotics has no special life-force. But Hoffmeyer dwells extensively on the Driesch/Roux controversy in embryology to show that the *original problem* vitalism was responding to (form-generation, the harmonic equipotential system) was not actually solved by mechanicist explanations. Driesch's solution was wrong (entelechy as energy-direction), but the puzzle was real. Hoffmeyer's diagnosis: "the smart idea about genes as information has all too often paralyzed the critical nerves of biologists." Shannon-information is not the same kind of thing as biologically-meaningful information. The key to form-generation lies neither in mechanicism nor in vital forces but in **a semiotics of ontogenesis**.

**Against specious metaphysics (experimental biologists' worry).** Hoffmeyer connects biosemiotics to a broader 19th-century vitalist-organicist lineage that includes Bernard, Pasteur, Bohr, Mayr, Gould — figures who are not embarrassed by self-determination as a partial explanatory category. Then he opens a sharper diagnostic: Darwin's premise of overproduction. *Where does fertility come from?* Natural selection requires proliferation, but selection cannot explain proliferation. Selection theory is "a surface structure built on a deeper theory of evolution that explains how life relates to the processes fundamental to physical development of the universe." This is a structural critique of neo-Darwinism that's independent of vitalism.

**Against spontaneous semiotics being just sloppy language.** Modern biology already uses semiotic vocabulary throughout — lymphocytes "present" peptide fragments, "high fidelity" replication, etc. Hoffmeyer (citing Emmeche) calls this *spontaneous semiotics* and argues it isn't careless language but reflects something about the subject matter. ### Hoffmeyer's "Sign and Cause" — bounded reading

This is the keystone chapter for testing whether the switch to biosemiotics was generative. The chapter does substantive philosophical work that goes beyond what Peirce 1867 or Cassirer's Chapter V could produce alone. Walking the argument.

#### The setup: voluntarism vs determinism (pp. 39–40)

Hoffmeyer opens with a real tension. Western culture holds two beliefs: humans are free, nature is unfree. These coexist comfortably under theistic separate-creation, but evolution makes the contradiction acute — if humans come from nature, how does free emerge from unfree? Peirce's solution is described as a third option: natural laws are themselves products of evolution, and human cognition is of the same kind as all other processes in nature. This isn't anthropomorphism; it's de-anthropomorphizing the concept of mind.

The Cartesian dualism, Hoffmeyer argues, lacks a concept of purpose that doesn't collapse into human intention. Peirce's distinction between *purpose* (the human-conscious variant) and *final cause* (the general form) is the conceptual key. Final cause: the mode of bringing facts about whereby a general result comes about regardless of which particular path it takes (CP 1:121). The acorn doesn't intend to grow into an oak; the oak-result is the final cause of the developmental trajectory.

#### The second law as final cause (pp. 42–47)

A genuinely original move. Hoffmeyer reads the entropy law as a final cause in Peirce's sense: it determines that entropy will increase but not how. Christiansen's argument complicates this: defining microstates of any macroscopic system requires preexisting boundary conditions, themselves provided by experimenters who exist in non-equilibrium contexts. The famous example: superfluid He3 only exists where there are physicists to study it.

Schrödinger's *What Is Life?* (1944) provides the bridge: organisms consume low-entropy energy and export high-entropy thermal radiation. They sidestep the entropy law while creating organized structures. The thermodynamic conditions of life are themselves a kind of final causation that physicists aren't equipped to recognize because their framework allows only efficient causation.

The Bickhard-Campbell argument about quantum field theory carries this further: there's no bottoming-out level. It's organization of process all the way down. So there's no rationale for treating larger-scale patterns (cells, organisms, minds) as merely epiphenomenal — every level is equally a pattern of process.

This is genuinely new philosophical territory. Neither 1867 Peirce nor Cassirer's Chapter V engages thermodynamics or quantum field theory.

#### The bladderwort and ontology of relations (pp. 47–52)

The example is *Utricularia floridiana*, a carnivorous water plant whose feeding strategy involves an autocatalytic cycle: bladderwort grows periphyton (algae and bacteria film) which is grazed by zooplankton which is captured by the bladderwort's traps. Indirect mutualism — every species benefits.

Ulanowicz's analysis: such autocatalytic systems show *centripetality*. They actively create their own domain of influence. They have an identity that persists even as components are replaced. The Popper line cited: we are not things, but flames.

Then the philosophically critical move via John Deely. The bladderwort system has *intersubjective* existence — its reality is in the relations among species, not in any single species. This is what nominalism denies: that relations have independent ontological status. Hoffmeyer makes the realism-of-relations explicit: biosemiotics requires it. *(This connects directly to the realist footnote on Abelard in Peirce 1867 §7, and to the forma dat esse rei* moment in Cassirer Chapter V — but Hoffmeyer makes it a load-bearing ontological claim, where Peirce and Cassirer left it implicit.)

#### Aristotelian causation, with caveats (pp. 52–54)

Hoffmeyer warns against importing Aristotle wholesale. The four causes (material, efficient, formal, final) are useful, but Aristotle's matter-form dualism brings a metaphysics where matter is the passive recipient of form-giving principles. Bruno's critique is invoked — matter is potency, like a pregnant woman's potential offspring, not nothingness. And Aristotle's final causes are guided by the metaphysical assumption of harmony and "the good," whereas Peircean final causation is built on randomness and irreversibility. *Peirce's final causes are not Aristotle's.* This is an important methodological clarification that the actual-questions file's verification queue would benefit from.

#### The functionalist debates (pp. 54–57)

Karen Neander's etiological theory: a trait's function is what selection rewarded. Cummins's causal-role theory. Millikan's response: purpose is linked to historical becoming, not just present causation. Millikan's mouse example is striking — the mouse's escape behavior is "functional" only relative to the historical conditions in which mice evolved; under sufficiently abnormal conditions, the function evaporates.

Hoffmeyer's reading: Millikan opens the door to nonpsychological final causation in nature. The "free human / unfree animal" barrier collapses. Purpose isn't planted in genes or brains in any naive sense; it's an aspect of the historical process in which any individual organism is a participant.

This is the most philosophically rigorous engagement with the teleology debate I've seen in our reading. It directly addresses the live debate in philosophy of biology and gives biosemiotics specific philosophical resources.

#### The information critique (pp. 57–63)

This is where Hoffmeyer makes his strongest case for biosemiotics earning its keep. Crick's Central Dogma rests on a notion of information-as-specification that:

- Differs from Shannon information (purely probabilistic, content-free)

- Cannot escape context-dependence (a mutation can change what "AAA" specifies)

- Treats information as a thing/parcel that travels — but this metaphor breaks down if information is meaningful

- Compares to phlogiston as a placeholder concept obstructing genuine progress

The Sterelny-Griffiths doorbell argument is the philosophical key: signal/noise distinction is *a fact about us*, not about the world. Information requires an interpreting system to *be* information at all. Information is something that "must be understood — and therefore can be misunderstood." Jablonka tries to save the information concept through semantic definition but still wants to preserve the transport metaphor; Hoffmeyer says the transport metaphor has to go.

This argument is genuinely generative. It doesn't translate the Peirce-Cassirer apparatus; it does new philosophical work on a contested empirical-conceptual question (what is genetic information?) using Peircean apparatus as resource. The Sterelny-Griffiths argument, if it's right, has consequences for how we read the gene/information literature your actual-questions file's question 5 flags.

#### The Peirce categories in full (pp. 65–68)

This is the explicit bridge to the categorical apparatus we worked in 1867. Hoffmeyer cites Peirce's mature categorical statement (CP 6:32 / Dinesen-Stjernfelt 1994):

- **Firstness** — origin of things considered in itself; pure quality, potentiality, possibility, simple and uncompounded; psychological feeling; biological "arbitrary sporting"; chance

- **Secondness** — end of things; separateness, rupture, opposition, manifestation, existence, quantity; psychological sense of reaction; biological heredity; law as brute fact

- **Thirdness** — process mediating between First and Second; general conception, mediation; cultural, linguistic, biological habits; the tendency to form habits; evolution

The crucial methodological move: efficient causality operates at Secondness; final causation (semiotic causality) operates at Thirdness. Modern science systematically reduces Thirdness to Secondness. This reduction is what biosemiotics rejects.

The connection to 1867: the three middle categories of "On a New List of Categories" (Quality / Relation / Representation, derived as references to ground / correlate / interpretant) become Firstness / Secondness / Thirdness in the mature work. Hoffmeyer is using the descendants of our 1867 categories. The mature categorical vocabulary doesn't preserve the post-Kantian derivation from the proposition; it's a phenomenological-cosmological re-derivation. But the triadic structure is the same.

#### Semiotic causality as new metaphysics (pp. 64–68)

This is the chapter's deepest move. Hoffmeyer proposes "semiotic causality" as a non-reducible kind of causation: bringing about under guidance of interpretation in a local context. Semiotic causality cannot be reduced to efficient causality, but it depends on it: semiotic causality gives *direction* to efficient causality, while efficient causality gives *power* to semiotic causality.

The Peirce quote that anchors this: "Final causation without efficient causation is helpless, but efficient without final is worse than helpless… by far, it is mere chaos; and chaos is not even so much as chaos, without final causation; it is blank nothing" (CP 1:200, 1:213).

This is genuine new philosophical work. Neither 1867 Peirce nor Cassirer's Chapter V articulates a non-reducible semiotic causality. The 1867 paper is about the structure of the proposition; this is about how *causation itself* needs a triadic structure. The connection: both are post-Kantian moves to make functional/relational structure primary, but Hoffmeyer pushes the move into causation — which is empirically tractable in a way the 1867 categorical apparatus isn't.

This is biosemiotics earning its keep — using Peircean apparatus to do work that mainstream biology can't easily do in its own vocabulary.

Our copula convergence work showed that Peirce 1867 and Cassirer Chapter V are reading the same Kantian site (the copula's act of synthesis as constituting objectivity). ### Hoffmeyer's "Code-Duality" — bounded reading

Code-duality is the central biosemiotic mechanism Hoffmeyer claims is constitutive of life itself. The chapter weaves several threads together: the Weismann doctrine, niche construction, gene selectionism, the digital/analog distinction, Pattee's epistemic cut, and the replicator/interactor debate. The argument's spine is that life requires *two* codes — one digital, one analog — and that this duality, not natural selection alone, is what distinguishes life from nonlife.

#### The Weismann setup (pp. 71–75)

August Weismann's 1890s doctrine: somatic and germ cells lead separate lives; acquired characteristics aren't heritable; Lamarck refuted. Hoffmeyer's twist: Weismann's doctrine rests on a semiotic mechanism Weismann himself couldn't have understood — a duality between analog and digital representations. The doctrine's "inner core" is confirmed by molecular biology, but the aggressive transmission-genetic reductionism that Weismannism seemed to license isn't.

The niche construction critique (Lewontin 1983, Odling-Smee 1988) sits inside this. Organisms don't just adapt to environments — they shape them. The classic Darwinian equations dO/dT = f(O,E) and dE/dT = g(E) become coupled: dO/dT = f(O,E) and dE/dT = g(O,E). Species don't climb mountains; they jump trampolines. The cactus-spine-using Galápagos finch (*Cactospiza pallida*) is the example — there's no "thorn-digging gene"; the bird uses its general capacity for learning to construct a niche identical to the one woodpeckers occupy elsewhere.

#### Gene selectionism and the reification problem (pp. 75–80)

Dawkins's *Selfish Gene* (1976): organisms are survival machines for genes. Hoffmeyer's response is sharper than the usual critique. The historical detail matters: Wilhelm Johannsen, who coined the term *gene* in 1909, did *not* think genes were material things — for Johannsen the gene was a calculational tool, like Binet's IQ. The Drosophila Group (Morgan and others) reified Johannsen's calculational concept into a material structure. Dawkins inherits this reification and pushes it further: genes become agents.

The philosophical objection: Dawkins treats symbols as if they were things, and worse, as things with purposes. The term *replicator* implies agency, but agency cannot reasonably be a property of a DNA molecule. Hull's distinction between *replicators* (units that reliably make more units like themselves, transferring information across generations) and *interactors* (units that interact spontaneously with their surroundings during their own lifetimes) is more honest. But even Hull's reframing, Hoffmeyer argues, ends up restricted to a "rachitic" understanding of causality.

The connection to the previous chapter's causation analysis: replicators exert *formal* causality (they specify structural relations), interactors exert *efficient* causality (they act in here-and-now), and Peircean *final* causality uses both tools and is connected to life's code-dual structure. So the replicator/interactor distinction is real but neither side is dominant — both are needed, and both are subordinate to the semiotic causality of the whole.

#### Code-duality core argument (pp. 80–85)

The argument runs through Pattee. A living system must contain the distinctions necessary for its own identification — self-reference is the most basal requirement of life. Self-reference requires *re-description*: the system must construct a description of itself in order to perpetuate itself. This description must stay inactive or protected from the life processes, lest it change with them and ultimately die with the system.

In all known living systems, this description is DNA. But the description alone isn't enough — it must be translated into the physical reality of an actual living organism. This translation (developmental process) requires the egg cell to decipher the coded instructions and follow them. The egg's "tacit knowledge" (Polanyi 1958, Pattee 1977) must be presupposed by, not built into, the DNA description. Digital re-description alone is far from a total description of the organism.

So life requires:

- A **digital code** for memory: discrete, conventional, protected from life processes (DNA)

- An **analog code** for action: continuous, context-bound, expressing the message in physical reality (organism, behavior, ecological interaction)

Neither alone is sufficient. The chicken and the egg, in Hoffmeyer's image, encode each other.

#### Three advantages of digital codes (pp. 85–88)

This is the chapter's sharpest philosophical work. Digital codes have three properties analog codes lack:

1. **Freedom to be impossible.** Digital codes don't have to obey natural laws. Meryl Streep can have lunch with Socrates in a novel; a megaton cod can ravage Oslo. Genetic codes share this: a fertilized egg can produce a nonviable individual. This freedom-to-be-impossible explains the combinatorial creativity of evolution — incessant testing of the limits of possible combinations, including non-viable ones.

2. **Time independence and consensual objectivity.** Digital codes are ideal for memory. We have Plato's dialogues because they were written down; if they had been mimed, they wouldn't survive intact for more than a few generations. Wilden's distinction: a digital code is *outside* the sender and receiver and mediates their relationship; an analog code *is* the relationship. The DNA code's secluded existence in protected isolation from cellular metabolism is what allows it to conserve experiences from past survival outcomes.

3. **Capacity for abstraction — including negation.** Digital codes can construct meta-messages: messages about how other messages should be understood. Bateson's example: young monkeys snapping at one another in play. The snap signifies "this is not a bite" — but the negative message can't be communicated directly in analog; it must be announced as a positive (the snap stands in for the bite that isn't happening). Real abstraction needs digital codes' arbitrary, detached conventions.

This third advantage is the philosophically richest. The claim that *only digital codes can do negation* — that the analog can refuse but cannot deny — is exactly Langer's discursive/presentational distinction in different vocabulary. Langer's claim that music can express feeling but not propositional content closely tracks Hoffmeyer's claim that analog codes carry semantics but lack syntactics. The parallel goes deep enough to suggest a genuine convergence between Langer 1942 and Hoffmeyer 2008. *(Hold/verify: Hoffmeyer doesn't cite Langer; this is interpretive synthesis on my side. Langer's claim is at the level of human cognition, Hoffmeyer's at the level of biological coding — but the structure is the same.)*

#### Analog codes (pp. 88–91)

Analog codes are based on similarity, spatio-temporal continuity, or part-whole and cause-effect relations. They're "pregnant with meaning" but "barren" in syntax. Bateson on the asymmetry: when someone says "I love you," the listener pays more attention to kinesics and paralinguistics than to the words. We become uncomfortable when relationship-messages are translated into words; we prefer them to remain "analogic, unconscious, and involuntary."

Uexküll's *contrapuntal duets* are the biological exemplars: butterfly wings with eye-spots that mimic small predators and chase off small birds; flies and spiders; the iconic relations Uexküll documented. These are analog codings in nature — pre-linguistic, embodied, contextual.

The asymmetry is exact: what the analog gains in semantics it loses in syntactics, and what the digital gains in syntactics it loses in semantics. Each code does what the other can't.

#### Pattee's epistemic cut and the biosemiotic solution (pp. 92–95)

Pattee's epistemic cut: separating knower from known, observer from observed, semantic from syntactic. Pattee insists this is descriptive dualism, not Cartesian dualism. The cut emerges from measurement: any measuring device must obey the laws of physics, but the *function* of measurement cannot be derived from the physics — the observer must choose what to ignore and invent what must be heeded. Pattee leaves open the question of how a dynamic functional mode could become a semiotic functional mode.

Hoffmeyer's solution: both modes are semiotic. The difference is the *kind* of semiotics. The dynamic functional mode (cytoplasm, protein world) operates with iconic and indexical sign processes — analog. The symbolic functional mode (DNA function) operates with symbolic sign processes — digital. To claim only the digital twin is semiotic, while the analog twin remains in classical dynamics, is to block the only possibility for transcending the epistemic cut.

This is the deepest move in the chapter. It dissolves a Kantian-style cut not by collapsing one side into the other but by extending semiotic reality across both. The cut between knower and known, between sign and dynamics, between symbolic and material, is reframed: both sides are sign processes of different kinds. *(This connects to the substance-to-function shift we tracked through Cassirer Chapter V — the transcendental "site" where objectivity is constituted gets reread as the locus where two semiotic modes interact, not where dynamics meets symbols.)*

#### Organisms as superorganisms (pp. 97–101)

The standard biological organism concept is vertebrate-shaped: well-integrated, coherent, genetically uniform, with single-cell origins and well-defined life cycles. Most life isn't like this. Plants and fungi don't have vertebrate-style individual identity. Many invertebrates undergo metamorphic body shifts. Corals swim as larvae and end as colonies.

Dawkins's *extended phenotype* dissolves the supposed unity of organism and genotype. Parasites are the extreme case: *Entomophthora muscae* fungus infecting house flies; *Rhizocephala* parasitic barnacles taking over crab brood-care behavior. But mutualism does the same thing more constructively: lichens (fungi + algae as a single functional unit), mycorrhiza (fungi + plants linked through mycelial networks that "nurse" seedlings), ant-fungus mutualism (over 200 documented species pairs), the squid-*Vibrio fischeri* light organ symbiosis with quorum sensing.

The Buss critique (1987, *The Evolution of Individuality*): Weismann's separation of germ and soma is empirically wrong for most taxa. Most organisms exhibit *somatic embryogenesis* — the same cell line participates in somatic functions and conserves the competence to form germ cells throughout ontogenesis. Plants, fungi, and many invertebrates work this way. The Modern Synthesis canonized Weismann anyway because embryologists were absent from the discussions.

#### Semiotic fitness (pp. 107–108)

If genotype and "envirotype" reciprocally constitute the context in which fitness is measured, then fitness should be relational, not gene-centric. *Semiotic fitness*: the semiotic competence or success of natural systems in managing the genotype-envirotype translation processes. Optimization of semiotic fitness yields continuing growth and depth of interpretive patterns.

This is the chapter's positive program. Replace genetic fitness with semiotic fitness. Replace replicator-vs-interactor with code-duality. Replace gene-as-information with gene-as-digital-sign-system embedded in a code-dual semiotic dynamic.

#### What this brings to our triangle

**The negation / abstraction asymmetry as biological-semiotic claim.** Hoffmeyer's claim that only digital codes can do negation parallels Langer's discursive/presentational distinction so closely that it's worth flagging as a substantive convergence. Langer (1942) argued that music can present conceptions of feeling but cannot do propositional logic. Hoffmeyer (2008) argues that analog codes can refuse but not deny. The structure is the same; the domain is different. Either Langer was articulating a structural feature of semiotic systems that Hoffmeyer rediscovered for biology, or they're both tracking a real asymmetry between continuous and discrete signaling that has consequences across human-cognitive and biological domains. Either way it's worth naming.

**The code-duality move is genuinely beyond Peirce 1867 and Cassirer Chapter V.** Neither has the digital/analog distinction. Peirce's mature work has discrete signs and continuous quality (Firstness as quality, Secondness as discrete fact, Thirdness as mediation), but the digital/analog cut as a structural feature of life isn't there. The substance-to-function shift Cassirer did is being pushed further: not just from substance to function, but from substance to *two simultaneous functional modes that mutually require each other*.

Hoffmeyer's solution — both sides are semiotic — is a post-Kantian move that goes beyond what Peirce-Cassirer-Langer give us. Langer's symbolic threshold becomes harder to hold. The constellation question (#7) gets more complex: it isn't just about where humans differ, but about whether "organism" itself holds up as a unit.
