Looks like your "Also" got cut off — go ahead and finish that thought, but in the meantime let me give you a quick anchor on the sign/symbol thing, because that's the load-bearing distinction for everything coming in the next two chapters.

**Langer's distinction (which she'll fully formalize in Ch. III but is already operating in Ch. II):**

- A *sign* announces the presence/existence of something. Smoke for fire, the bell for dinner, a fever for infection. Logical structure is roughly 1-to-1: the sign and what it signifies belong to a single situation. Animals use signs fluently. The reaction is *to the thing signified* — you run from the smoke.

- A *symbol* does **not** announce its object's presence. It's "a vehicle for the conception of an object." The structure is triadic: subject — symbol — conception → object. A symbol lets you *think about* something in its absence, hold it, recombine it, deny it, imagine it. The word "fire" is a symbol; the smell of smoke is a sign.

- Slogan version: **signs make us act, symbols make us conceive.** Symbolic transformation (Ch. II's argument) is that the mind doesn't just process signals — it *spontaneously remakes experience into symbols*, even when no practical demand requires it. That's why dreams, ritual, art, and idle talk exist. It's a primary need, not a luxury.

**Peirce uses the same words differently, which is where it gets confusing.** For Peirce, "sign" is the *umbrella* category, and he subdivides it three ways: **icon** (signifies by resemblance — a portrait), **index** (signifies by causal/existential connection — smoke, a weather vane, a pointing finger), **symbol** (signifies by convention/habit — a word, a number). So Langer's "sign" is roughly Peirce's *index*, and Langer's "symbol" is broader than Peirce's symbol — it absorbs icons too, and presentational forms that aren't conventional at all. The two vocabularies are skew. When you toggle between them just remember: Langer's pair = practical-vs-conceptual; Peirce's trichotomy = how-the-sign-stands-for-its-object.

**The Whitehead anchor you already have:** PiNK is dedicated to Whitehead, and Langer is essentially extending his *Symbolism: Its Meaning and Effect* (1927). Whitehead's "symbolic reference" is the automatic fusion of two prior modes — causal efficacy (the dim, deep felt-inheritance from the body and past) and presentational immediacy (the clear sensa of the specious present). Langer takes this on board: symbolic activity isn't a late add-on to a rational animal, it's the constitutive operation of mind. That's why Ch. II reads almost biologically — symbolic transformation as a *function*, like digestion. Hoffmeyer's biosemiotics is downstream of the same intuition through Peirce; you're triangulating well.

Good question, and the answer cuts close to why Langer feels so weirdly contemporary even though she's writing in 1942.

**The mapping, quickly.** Hoffmeyer's biosemiotics is built on Peirce, not Langer, so the vocabulary doesn't line up cleanly — but the substantive overlap is large. Hoffmeyer's master claim is that *semiosis is coextensive with life*: every cell, every immune response, every gradient an organism follows is a sign-process. A bacterium reading a sugar gradient is doing semiosis. The triadic structure (something stands for something else *to some interpretant*) is happening at the cellular level, not just in nervous systems. So when Langer says "animals use signs," Hoffmeyer would say: yes, and so do their gut microbes, and so do the cells in their gut microbes. The whole biosphere is a "semiosphere."

**Where they diverge — and where you should hold them together.** Almost all of biological semiosis is what Peirce called *indexical* (causal/existential connection: this hormone level *means* low blood sugar; this scent trail *means* prey passed here recently). That's exactly Langer's "sign." Symbol-use in Peirce's strict sense — convention, generality, the capacity to think of an absent or non-existent object — is rare in nature, and Langer treats it as the human differentia. Hoffmeyer wouldn't disagree that human symbolic life is qualitatively distinctive; he'd just embed it in a much longer evolutionary continuum, where iconic and indexical semiosis prepare the ground for it. So you can hold the two together like this: Hoffmeyer gives you the *biological depth* of sign-use (it goes all the way down), and Langer gives you the *anthropological discontinuity* of symbol-use (it goes somewhere new). Each protects against an error in the other — Hoffmeyer against Langer's slight tendency to treat animals as mere signal-machines; Langer against any flattening of the human into "just more semiosis."

**One bit of friction worth noticing.** Langer's "sign" leans behaviorist in flavor — sign as trigger, response as output. Hoffmeyer (via Peirce) would say even the animal's sign-response is already triadic: there is always an *interpretant*, a way the organism takes the sign as meaning *this rather than that*. So if Ch. II at moments seems to flatten animals more than your Hoffmeyer-reading instincts want, trust the instinct — it's a real strain in Langer that later semioticians corrected. The Whitehead-via-Stengers move would also push you here: causal efficacy already involves felt inheritance and selection, not bare mechanism.

**Chunking proposal for Ch. II.** It's about 1150 lines, three natural movements:

**Chunk 1 — The new epistemology and the sign/symbol cut.** Opening: why a theory of knowledge centered on *symbols* (Cassirer, Whitehead, gestalt psychology, etc. as her chorus). Then the careful animal-vs-human comparison: signal-use, the Hobie example, why human language isn't just a richer signal repertoire. This is where she earns the distinction.

**Chunk 2 — The "useless" mind: symbolic transformation as a primary need.** The pivot question: *why does the mind make symbols that don't direct action?* Beauty, idle thought, the brain reframed not as a switchboard but as a continuous ideation engine. This is the chapter's positive thesis and the place where she's closest to Whitehead.

**Chunk 3 — Ritual, dream, sacrament: the spontaneity of symbolization.** Concrete illustrations of symbolic transformation as constitutive, not decorative: ritual, the sacred, Freud and *Totem and Taboo*, the claim that symbol-making is as primary as digestion. Sets up Ch. III's formal logic of signs and symbols.

Good call on Deacon — he's the most direct heir of the question Langer is raising in Ch. II, and I'll weave him in. ### Chunk 1 — The Epistemic Reframe and the Hobie Baker Dilemma (~lines 1–500)

#### 1. The opening reframe: from sensation to symbol

The chapter opens with a quiet but decisive move. The old empiricist picture (the British tradition through to the early Vienna Circle) treated the mind as a recording-and-combining device: senses bring data in, memory and association arrange it, intelligence is just the quality of that processing. On that picture, what makes humans special is *more* — more bandwidth, longer memory, faster association.

Langer says: no. The differentia is *symbol-using*, not sensitivity. Helen Keller, with two channels gone, lives in a richer world than any ape with all its senses intact. So the action of mind isn't *acquiring* experience but *transforming* it — handling it via something that stands for it. This is the "new key" of the title; the rest of the chapter argues that this isn't a small adjustment to the old picture but a different picture entirely.

The chorus she names — Cassirer, Whitehead, Ritchie, Lorimer, De Laguna, Kohler, Koffka, Carnap, Russell, Dewey, Piaget, Head, Delacroix — is a deliberate move to show this isn't her idiosyncrasy. It's a genuine inflection across philosophy, psychology, neurology, and anthropology around 1940. **Cassirer is the load-bearing one for you** given your *Erkenntnisproblem* reading: "animal symbolicum" is exactly Cassirer's thesis, and Langer is the one who carries it into Anglophone philosophy and into aesthetics.

#### 2. The continuity story she'll then dismantle

Before she breaks it, she sets up the standard genetic-evolutionary account fairly. Goes like this: the conditioned reflex is the birth of mentality — a sound becomes a *sign* of food when it has accompanied food often enough; the dog now salivates at the bell. Sign-using is an instrument of biological adaptation: self-preservation, growth, procreation. Animal mind = wider or narrower repertoire of signs and responses, that's the only variable. The mole's world is small, the eagle's wide, but they're doing the same kind of thing.

On this view, human language is just signs-extended: bigger switchboard, more wires, more complicated reflex-arcs. She quotes Lorimer's account of the apes' "ape-ways" gradually evolving into "folk-ways" via "vocal chatter" accumulating intensity until specific vocables become focal in social adjustment. It's the smooth-continuity story — no *saltus naturae*, no leap of nature, just gradient. The Latin tag she uses, "Nihil est in homine quod non prius in amoeba erat" (nothing is in man that was not first in the amoeba), captures it perfectly. **This is the position she's about to undermine.**

#### 3. The sign/symbol distinction — and where Peirce sits sideways

Here's the cut that will do all the work in the rest of the book. A *sign* in Langer's strict sense is a *signal*: it announces or replaces the presence of something the organism should act on. The bell signals dinner; the dog acts. A *symbol* is different — it's used to *talk about* a thing, not to direct attention or behavior toward it. Symbols are "substitute signs," "reminders," vehicles for thinking-of-something-in-its-absence. **Symbols are not symptoms of things.** That's the line to underline.

Peirce mapping (worth holding alongside): Langer's "sign" is roughly Peirce's *index* — the conditioned-reflex bell is a perfect Peircean indexical sign, connected to dinner by causal/temporal contiguity. Langer's "symbol" overlaps Peirce's *symbol* (conventional, general) but spreads wider, since for Langer a dream-image or a piece of music can also be symbolic — Peirce would call those iconic. So when Langer says "sign vs. symbol," translate roughly to "index vs. (icon + symbol)," or more loosely: "thing-that-points" vs. "thing-that-stands-in-the-place-of."

#### 4. The Hobie Baker pivot — where the chapter turns

Stuart Chase's cat is the lever. Chase's argument (which Langer quotes at length) is: Hobie is a "realist." He reads signs accurately, acts adaptively, never suffers superstitions or hallucinations because he has no language to *generate* them. Humans, equipped with symbols, are *worse* off in many ways — credulous, deluded, ritualistic.

Langer pounces. If the genetic-continuity story is right — if symbol-using is just a refined, more adaptive form of sign-using — then it should make us *more* fit, not less. But look at how humans actually use symbols: word-magic, burning in effigy, ritual sacrifice, religious dance around mountains, art that consumes lifetimes for no practical return. **No rat in a maze would persist for a thousand years in a behavior that doesn't open the door.** The symbol-user is, by adaptive standards, often a fool. Therefore the symbol-using capacity *cannot* have evolved primarily as a better signal-system. Whatever it's for, it's not for adaptation in the obvious sense. The continuity story has a hole in it.

That's the dilemma she leaves you with at the close of this chunk: either symbol-using is a malfunction of an over-developed switchboard (in which case its universality and persistence are inexplicable), or it serves *some other primary need* the genetic-biological picture hasn't named. Chunk 2 will name it.

#### Cross-connections

**Deacon (*The Symbolic Species*, 1997)** is the modern attempt to answer Langer's challenge inside biology rather than against it. Deacon agrees there's a real discontinuity at the symbolic — what he calls the "symbolic threshold" — but argues it's not a leap of metaphysical kind, it's a leap of *learning kind*. Indexical reference is learned one association at a time (bell → food). Symbolic reference can only be learned by reorganizing a whole system of indexical associations into a new logical structure where signs refer to each other as much as to the world. This is hard — most animals can't cross it — and it co-evolved with the human brain over a very long time. So Deacon vindicates Langer's intuition that something genuinely new happens with symbols, while keeping the evolutionary story intact. Where Langer says *the genetic-continuity picture has a hole*, Deacon says *yes, and here's the hole's shape: it's a phase transition in how reference works.* If you want to triangulate Langer with biology that doesn't flatten her, Deacon is the bridge.

**Hoffmeyer/biosemiotics** would push back on the Hobie Baker scene from the other side: Hobie isn't a switchboard, he's an interpreter — there's already a triadic interpretant in his sign-use, already proto-intentionality, already a self for whom signs *matter*. Stuart Chase's "realist cat" is too clean a foil. So Langer's argument lands, but its sharpness depends on a slightly behaviorist picture of the animal that biosemiotics has since softened. Hold this as a corrective: the gap between sign and symbol is real, but the bottom of the gap (the animal side) is richer than Langer paints it.

**Whitehead/Stengers.** Whitehead's *Symbolism* (1927) is the direct precursor — "symbolic reference" as the automatic fusion of causal efficacy (the felt, inherited, dim) with presentational immediacy (the clear, sensa-bright). For Whitehead, symbolic activity isn't bolted on top of perception, it constitutes the perceptual world we actually live in. Langer is taking that and running it into anthropology, art, language. The Stengers move you'll already feel: Langer is good company because she resists the reductionist continuity story without falling into miraculous discontinuity. There's *novelty* — but novelty is something nature does, not something added to it.

**Cassirer/Erkenntnisproblem.** The Cusanus material you're sitting with is the early scaffolding of what becomes the *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms*. Cusanus's *coincidentia oppositorum* and his picture of the human mind as a *measuring* power that doesn't passively reflect but actively constitutes its world is precisely the move Langer is now porting from German neo-Kantianism into mid-century Anglophone discourse. When she says "symbol-using makes man's world," she is, whether she names it or not, being Cassirerian.

Anchor for going in: the question you should be holding through Ch. II is **"why does the human mind do something so apparently useless, so persistently, so universally, that no adaptive story can explain it?"** Chunk 2 is her positive answer. Ready when you are.

Sharp instinct to ask. The answer is **yes, but mostly as foil rather than ally** — and the timing makes it especially interesting.

**The chronology is uncanny.** PiNK is 1942. The McCulloch-Pitts paper ("A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity") is 1943 — the *next year*. The Macy Conferences run 1946–1953. Wiener's *Cybernetics* is 1948. Shannon's *Mathematical Theory of Communication* is 1948. So Langer is writing *just before* the picture she's criticizing — mind as switchboard, message-relay, signal-and-feedback — becomes the dominant scientific paradigm of the postwar era. Her "telephone-exchange" critique is essentially preemptive: she's dismissing the model right before it gets formalized into the foundation of cognitive science. That's part of why she gets eclipsed in the 50s/60s — the field swings hard toward exactly the picture she said couldn't account for what mind actually does.

**Why it's mostly a foil.** The Macy/cybernetic project is, in its dominant strand, a sophisticated extension of the genetic-adaptive story Langer dismantles in Ch. II. Mind = information processing + feedback loops + control. Shannon's information is famously *content-free* (a measure of unexpectedness in a channel, not of meaning); cybernetic "communication" is signal-relay with regulation. From Langer's angle this is just the switchboard with better wiring. The whole point of her Hobie Baker move — that symbolic activity is *not primarily adaptive* and not primarily about transmitting messages efficiently — is precisely what cybernetics has no room for. The cyberneticists ask "how is the system regulated?"; Langer asks "why does the system *make myths*?" Different questions, and the second isn't reducible to the first. **This is exactly the lineage Hoffmeyer's biosemiotics later defines itself against** — Peircean triadic semiosis (with an interpretant, with meaning) against cybernetic dyadic information transfer.

**But there's a heterodox strand inside Macy that's friendlier.** Bateson is the obvious bridge. His later formulation — "information is a difference that makes a difference" — smuggles semantics back in: the *making a difference* requires a frame, an interpretant, a "for whom." By *Steps to an Ecology of Mind* (1972) Bateson is doing something much closer to Langer's symbolic transformation than to Shannon. Margaret Mead carries anthropological sensibility into the room (Langer cites the same anthropological literature). Lawrence Kubie is a psychoanalyst — directly interested in the symbolic. Heinz von Foerster's second-order cybernetics — the observer is part of the system — pushes the framework toward the place where Langer, Whitehead, and biosemiotics already are. So you can read the Macy decade as a quiet civil war between the *Shannon-Wiener mainline* (mind as computable information processing) and a *Bateson-Mead-von-Foerster minor key* (mind as pattern, meaning, observer-relative, symbolic). The mainline won at the time. The minor key is what biosemiotics, second-order systems theory, and now the new theories of biological agency are recovering.

**Where it fits in your campaign specifically.** Three useful uses:

First, it sharpens what's at stake in PiNK. Langer's critique reads as a period-piece flourish until you realize she's calling, in 1942, the exact failure mode of the picture that's about to dominate cognitive science for fifty years. *That* gives Ch. II a lot more weight.

Second, it's the connective tissue between your Stengers/Whitehead reading and your Hoffmeyer reading. Stengers reads Whitehead partly *against* the cybernetic-informational flattening of process and experience; Hoffmeyer reads Peirce *against* the same flattening on the biology side. Langer is the early Anglophone voice doing it on the philosophy-of-mind side. They're three fronts of the same long argument.

Third, Deacon is interesting here because he engages cybernetics/information theory directly and tries to get *out the other side*: his work on "absentials" and constraints (especially in *Incomplete Nature*, 2012) is an attempt to give a naturalistic account of why information can be *about* something without reducing it to physical signal. That's a direct technical answer to a question Langer poses philosophically and Hoffmeyer poses biosemiotically. So if you ever want to bring the campaign into the present, the line **Langer → Bateson → Hoffmeyer → Deacon** is a coherent through-line on "what's wrong with the cybernetic-computational picture of mind, and what should replace it."

Short version of the answer: Macy fits as the *historical hinge* that PiNK is trying to head off, and that biosemiotics is later trying to undo. Worth keeping in your peripheral vision but probably not worth a full campaign chunk unless you want to do Bateson on his own terms — and if you do, *Mind and Nature* (1979) or the "Form, Substance, and Difference" essay would be the entry points.

### Chunk 2 — The Heresy: Symbolization as Primary Need (lines 500–920)

This is the chapter's positive turn. Chunk 1 dismantled the continuity story by pointing to the persistent uselessness of the symbol-using mind. Chunk 2 builds the alternative — and the move is more careful and more structurally interesting than the headline "symbolization is a primary need" makes it sound. Watch how she earns it.

#### The inventory of useless mind

She gathers three witnesses against the utilitarian theory and lets them testify in series: *art, ritual/magic, dream.*

The art testimony is short but barbed. Artists "as a class are so ready to sacrifice wealth and comfort and even health to their trade, that a lean and hollow look has become an indispensable feature in the popular conception of genius." We condemn the destruction of the Parthenon more bitterly than the destruction of the homes that surrounded it. *"Why should the world wail over the loss of a play product, and look with its old callousness on the destruction of so much that dire labor has produced?"* Art is play, on the genetic-psychology theory. But play is "the abundance of strength after needs are satisfied" — and artists *starve* for play. The category breaks.

The dream testimony is the most lethal, and it's where the switchboard metaphor turns against the people who use it. *"Why does the central switchboard not rest when there is no need of making connections? Why should the plugs be popped in and out, and set the whole system wildly ringing, only to end with a universal 'Excuse it, please'?"* The whole point of a switchboard is that it stops ringing when no one is calling. The brain doesn't. It "goes right on manufacturing ideas — streams and deluges of ideas — that the sleeper is not using to think with about anything." Either the brain is a wildly malfunctioning switchboard *every single night* of every life, in which case the metaphor cannot be its account, or it isn't a switchboard at all. She lets the second conclusion settle.

That's the full witness-list: art's seriousness, ritual's persistence, dream's prodigality. *"The love of magic, the high development of ritual, the seriousness of art, and the characteristic activity of dreams, are rather large factors to leave out of account in constructing a theory of mind."* The understatement is doing the work — these are not edge cases, they are most of human life.

#### The methodological pivot — and what it costs to make it

Now the structural move. She does *not* abandon the naturalist register. Watch the exact phrasing:

> "I propose, therefore, to try a new general principle: to conceive the mind, still as an organ in the service of primary needs, but of *characteristically human* needs."

The category "primary need" is preserved. The category "organ" is preserved. What changes is the *content* of the inventory of needs. She refuses the slide that would have made things easier — refuses to say that human mind has a *higher* faculty serving a different kind of need. The need is still primary, still biological in register, still organic. It's just that one of the primary needs of *this* organism, the one with this cortex, is not the same as the primary needs of a cat.

The shape of this move is worth slowing on. It's the same shape as Whitehead's refusal, in *Science and the Modern World*, to import the criterion of "experience" or "feeling" from above and apply it to nature. Whitehead expands what counts as feeling rather than restricting feeling to humans; Langer expands what counts as biological need rather than placing symbol-making *above* biology. The retorsive move in both: don't accept the criterion the opposing position offers and then negotiate the differentia within it. **Reset the criterion at the point where the differentia first becomes visible.**

She insulates the move with a deliberate naturalism declaration: *"That man is an animal I certainly believe; and also, that he has no supernatural essence, 'soul' or 'entelechy' or 'mind-stuff,' enclosed in his skin. He is an organism, his substance is chemical, and what he does, suffers, or knows, is just what this sort of chemical structure may do, suffer, or know."* And then, immediately: *"Now this is a mere declaration of faith, preliminary to a confession of heresy."* The structure is precise. The naturalism is *unconditional*; the heresy is *internal to it.* She is not giving us soul-stuff. She is enlarging the inventory of what chemical structures, organized this way, can need.

#### The thesis stated — and the Ritchie inversion

> "This basic need, which certainly is obvious only in man, is the need of symbolization. The symbol-making function is one of man's primary activities, like eating, looking, or moving about. It is the fundamental process of his mind, and goes on all the time."

*Like eating, looking, or moving about.* Hold that comparison. She is not analogizing symbolization with a higher cultural function. She is analogizing it with metabolism, perception, locomotion. The register is physiological. Symbolization happens *while you sleep*, the way digestion does. It happens as a kind of constant brain-activity that has its own products whether or not anything practical needs producing.

Then she comes back to a passage she had already quoted in Chunk 1's territory — Ritchie's "The essential act of thought is symbolization" — and she *inverts* it. This is the kind of move the Erkenntnisproblem ledger has been tracking in Cusanus, the *same word taken up, registers shifted*:

> "It is not the essential act of thought that is symbolization, but an act essential to thought, and prior to it. Symbolization is the essential act of *mind*; and mind takes in more than what is commonly called thought."

What was Ritchie's *property of thought* becomes Langer's *operation prior to thought.* What thought *does* becomes the act *out of which* thought arises as one possible product. Mind is widened past thought; symbolization is moved upstream. *"Only certain products of the symbol-making brain can be used according to the canons of discursive reasoning. In every mind there is an enormous store of other symbolic material, which is put to different uses or perhaps even to no use at all — a mere result of spontaneous brain activity, a reserve fund of conceptions, a surplus of mental wealth."*

Mark the phrase **surplus of mental wealth**. The genetic-utilitarian theory has no place for surplus; everything has to pay its adaptive way. Langer's mind *generates surplus by constitution.* The brain is a surplus-producing organ.

#### Switchboard → transformer

The metaphor she has been waiting for arrives:

> "For the brain is not merely a great transmitter, a super-switchboard; it is better likened to a great transformer. The current of experience that passes through it undergoes a change of character, not through the agency of the sense by which the perception entered, but by virtue of a primary use which is made of it immediately: it is sucked into the stream of symbols which constitutes a human mind."

Two-thirds of the way through the chapter, the chapter title finally lands. *Symbolic Transformation*. The brain is a *transformer* — not in the sense of a mechanism that converts X into Y by following a rule, but in the sense that experience entering the brain *changes character* by being taken up into a stream of symbols. The transformation is not added to perception after the fact. It is the immediate *primary use* the brain makes of incoming experience. Sense-data don't sit there waiting to be interpreted; they are pulled into symbolic transformation as they arrive.

Then the precise neologism:

> "Symbolization is pre-rationative, but not pre-rational. It is the starting point of all intellection in the human sense, and is more general than thinking, fancying, or taking action."

*Pre-rationative, not pre-rational.* The distinction is doing exact work. Symbolization is prior to *acts of reasoning* — it is not itself a reasoning. But it is not below or outside reason in the sense of being mere drive or animal stuff; it is the operation *out of which* reason becomes possible. Rationality is one mode of using the symbols that symbolic transformation continually supplies. Reason is downstream.

The chapter epigraph from D. G. James now lands its meaning: *"The vitality and energies of the imagination do not operate at will; they are fountains, not machinery."* The brain is *"a veritable fountain of more or less spontaneous ideas."* Fountains, not switchboards. The shift of metaphor is the shift of theory. Sources, not channels.

#### Speech and ritual as parallel termini

The last move of the chunk is structural and sets up Ch. III directly:

> "Speech is the natural outcome of only one kind of symbolic process. There are transformations of experience in the human mind that have quite different overt endings. They end in acts that are neither practical nor communicative, though they may be both effective and communal; I mean the actions we call ritual."

Speech and ritual are not species-and-ancestor, not refinement-and-residue. They are *parallel termini* of symbolic transformation. The brain is constantly transforming experience into symbols; symbols press toward overt expression; the expression takes different forms depending on the kind of symbol involved. Discursive symbols terminate in speech. Other symbols terminate in ritual, art, myth, dream. The taxonomy of symbolic forms — which Cassirer has spent thousands of pages elaborating, and which Ch. III will start formalizing — corresponds to a taxonomy of *terminations* of one underlying transformation.

Notice the precision of: *"It is born in the cortex, not in the 'old brain'; but it is born of an elementary need of that organ, once the organ has grown to human estate."* Both halves matter. **Cortical** — not residue of limbic instinct, not survival of an older drive. **Elementary need** — not derived, not refined, not added on top. New organ, primary need. The cortex is not just a control layer above the old brain; it has its own elementary requirements, and one of them is symbolization.

The child-talk passage drives the same point developmentally. Children chatter for no purpose. Mr. Chase admits, even on his utilitarian theory, that *"children practice [words] with as much gusto as Hobie stalks a mouse."* Langer presses: stalking is a *present* drive in the cat, not preparation for future hunting; talking must be a *present* drive in the child, not preparation for future communication. **The impulse must be motivated by a present need, not a prospective one.** Symbolic transformation seeks its overt termination *now*, not because it will be useful later. The need is current and constitutive.

#### Cross-resonances (light)

**Cassirer.** The name-drop on line ~740 — *"as Cassirer, for instance, has done"* — is the chapter's quiet acknowledgment that Langer is doing in English what the *Philosophie der symbolischen Formen* did in German. The trajectory from your Cusanus reading goes: *docta ignorantia* establishing finite mind as constitutively measuring rather than reflecting → *conjectura* as positive participation in truth in alterity → Cassirer's mature claim that mind is constitutively *symbolic-form-producing*, with each form (myth, language, science, art) a distinct mode of the same constitutive activity → Langer porting "constitutive symbolic activity" into Anglophone philosophy of mind, with Whiteheadian inflection. The Ritchie inversion you just watched is the same structural move as the Cusan inversion of *conjectura* — a term taken up, register shifted, what was a property becomes the constitutive act.

**Whitehead.** Symbolic transformation as the *immediate* primary use the brain makes of experience is the philosophy-of-mind translation of *symbolic reference* — the automatic fusion, in Whitehead's account, of presentational immediacy with causal efficacy. The "transformer" brain is what an organism does when its perceptual operation is already interpretive at the moment of arrival. The "fountain not machinery" frame is exactly the kind of resistance to mechanism you've been working with in Stengers.

**Hoffmeyer.** Langer's claim — *symbol-making is a constitutive biological function, not a derivative of more basic functions* — is the move biosemiotics generalizes downward. Hoffmeyer would say: yes, and it's not unique to humans; semiosis is constitutive of all life, with symbolic semiosis being a late, rare, distinctively human (and possibly proto-emergent in some animals) elaboration. The argument-shape is the same; the scope differs.

**Deacon.** "The symbol-making function is one of man's primary activities, like eating" is the philosophical precursor to Deacon's evolutionary claim that the *need* for symbolic communication was the selection pressure that produced the brain capable of it. Deacon gives Langer the missing causal-evolutionary mechanism: the symbolic threshold is hard to cross, but once a population starts crossing it, brain and language co-evolve toward greater capacity. Langer says it's a primary need; Deacon says how a primary need of this kind could have been selected into a primate.

What the pigeon develops is *indexical* — its behavior comes to index the arrival of food through associative contiguity, exactly like the dog learning the dinner bell. Indexical pseudo-ritual is widespread in nature. What humans do in the mountain dance is *symbolic* — the dance stands for an opening-of-the-earth in a system of conventional meanings, embedded in a cosmology, repeatable in many forms, generative of variation. Deacon's "symbolic threshold" is precisely this distinction: indexical association can build surprisingly elaborate behavioral patterns (Skinner's pigeons, stereotypies, possibly the rain-dance), but those patterns are *trapped in indexicality* — they cannot generate new content, refer to absent objects, or be re-narrated as different things in different contexts. Symbolic ritual can. So Langer's underlying claim survives, but the way she should have stated it is: *animals exhibit non-functional repetitive behavior under conditions of stress and random reinforcement, but those behaviors are indexical-adventitious, not symbolic-generative; only the latter sustains itself across millennia and binds itself into cosmologies.*

### Chunk 3 — Ritual, Magic, Freud: The Spontaneous Termination (lines 920–1149)

This is the chapter's last movement, and it does something more careful than the case-material framing suggests. #### The first escape-route: ritual as social glue

She lets De Laguna make the most sympathetic version of the salvage move. *Maybe* ritual isn't directly adaptive, but it serves *social solidarity*; group cohesion is biological, so we've recovered the utilitarian story at one remove. The pivot from individual adaptation to group adaptation was a respectable position in 1942 anthropology — Durkheim's whole framework runs on it — and it's still the default in much contemporary cognitive science of religion (Boyer, Atran, the costly-signaling theorists). Langer hears it out and then turns the screw with a question that's still painful for that program:

> "Why should this social purpose not be served by a sensible dogma which the members of the society could reasonably be called on to believe, instead of 'elaborate and monstrous' creeds issuing in all sorts of cruel rites, mutilations, and even human sacrifices, such as Baal or the Aztec gods demanded?"

Then the *killing* example:

> "Why did the Cults of Reason set up in post-Revolutionary France and in early Soviet Russia not serve the purpose of social solidarity every bit as well as the 'Christian hocus-pocus' they displaced...?"

That sentence is doing real philosophical work. Robespierre's *Culte de la Raison* and the early Soviet anti-religious campaigns were *deliberately engineered* social-solidarity religions — designed by intelligent reformers who had read their Voltaire, who knew exactly what religion did sociologically and tried to install a more reasonable substitute. They failed. *They didn't fail at being implemented; they failed at being inhabited.* The "rational" cult evaporated; the "monstrous" rites persisted for millennia. **If ritual were really for social solidarity, the rational version should outcompete the irrational version, and the historical record is the opposite.** The functional theory predicts the wrong outcome at planetary scale across the entire historical record. So either the function isn't social solidarity, or the function isn't what's selecting for the form. She lets the inference settle without belaboring it. She invokes Plato — who saw the same problem and couldn't solve it — to mark that this is an old problem, not a new one.

The conclusion she draws is precise:

> "The answer is, of course, that ritual is not prescribed for a practical purpose, not even that of social solidarity. Such solidarity may be one of its effects, and sophisticated warlords may realize this fact and capitalize on it... but neither myth nor ritual arose originally for this purpose."

Mark the distinction: *effects* are not *purposes* and *capitalizations* are not *origins*. Bismarck used national religion; he didn't create religion. The state can leverage what's already there; it can't invent the form. **The form arose for a different reason, and only because it arose for that other reason is it now available for political capture.**

#### The second escape-route: ritual as bad reasoning

The other available salvage is to read magic as a *cognitive error* — primitive people had wrong theories of causality, did wrong-thing-on-the-basis-of-wrong-theory, and ritual is the survival of cognitive failure. Lévy-Bruhl's *primitive mentality* in its crude reading, and the Tylor-Frazer "intellectualist" school more broadly, ran on this. Langer's response is observational and devastating:

> "A savage who beats a tom-tom to drive off his brother's malaria would never make such a practical mistake as to shoot his arrow blunt end forward or bait his fishline with flowers. It is not ignorance of causal relations, but the supervention of an interest stronger than his practical interest, that holds him to magical rites."

This is *sharp* fieldwork-sense. The same person who beats the drum knows perfectly well which end of an arrow is sharp. He is *not confused* about practical causation. The "category mistake" reading requires that magical action and practical action be confused with each other; ethnographic observation shows they aren't. The drumming is not a *failed* attempt at medicine; it is a *successful* instance of something else. The interest is different, the operation is different, the criteria of correctness are different. **She has reframed magic from being a wrong answer to a different question into being a right answer to a different question.**

Then the formulation that pivots the whole chunk:

> "Magic, then, is not a method, but a language; it is part and parcel of that greater phenomenon, ritual, which is the language of religion."

Hold the precision. Not metaphor — *language*. Not "like a language" but a language proper, with its own grammar, its own rules of well-formedness, its own distinction between competent and incompetent performance. The drumming is *articulate*. It says something. The community can read it. It can be done well or badly. It admits of variation and innovation within constraint. *That* is what magic is. Calling it false science is the category mistake.

#### The biological framing of ritual — and its sharpest formulation

Now she takes the cortex/elementary-need point from Chunk 2 and pushes it into ritual specifically:

> "Ritual is a symbolic transformation of experiences that no other medium can adequately express. Because it springs from a primary human need, it is a spontaneous activity — that is to say, it arises without intention, without adaptation to a conscious purpose; its growth is undesigned, its pattern purely natural, however intricate it may be."

The simile that follows is the crucial one:

> "It was never 'imposed' on people; they acted thus quite of themselves, exactly as bees swarmed and birds built nests, squirrels hoarded food, and cats washed their faces. No one made up ritual, any more than anyone made up Hebrew or Sanskrit or Latin."

Watch what's happening. She has *paired* ritual with hive-behavior and language. The first pairing is biological: ritual is a species-typical behavioral form, like a hive or a nest, that emerges *automatically* under the right conditions because the organism has the cortex it has. The second pairing is linguistic: nobody designed Hebrew either, but Hebrew has structure, has constraints, has correctness conditions, transmits, evolves — *and* nobody designed it. **The "spontaneous" in "spontaneous activity" doesn't mean random; it means emergent without conscious authorship while still being highly structured.** This is one of the deepest moves in the chapter. Ritual is *biologically* spontaneous and *formally* articulate at the same time, and there's no contradiction because language is the same kind of thing.

This is also where she's closest to Cassirer. Symbolic forms are *not* artifacts of conscious design and *not* arbitrary either; they are species-typical structured emergences from the symbolic capacity of mind. The Cusan thread you've been working with — finite mind as constitutively measure-making, *conjectura* as positive participation in truth in alterity — runs through here transposed: the constitutively symbolizing mind doesn't *design* its symbolic forms, it *grows* them, the way an organism grows organs that have function it didn't intend.

#### The Boas precursor and Freud's contribution

Boas — solid early-anthropology authority — gets a careful mention because he saw two things: ritual *resembles language* in unconscious development of forms, and ritual has *symbolistic functions*. Langer treats him as foreshadowing without doing the metaphysical work. He noticed; he didn't theorize.

Then Freud. Watch how she handles this — it's tactical:

> "It was Freud who recognized that ritual acts are not genuine instrumental acts, but are motivated primarily *a tergo*, and carry with them, consequently, a feeling not of purpose, but of compulsion."

*A tergo* — from behind, pushed rather than pulled, motivated by what is rather than by what is sought. The ritual act doesn't aim at an outcome; it discharges a pressure. *Totem and Taboo* gets cited not because she endorses Freud's specific psychogenetic story (the primal-horde myth) but because Freud independently arrived at the structural point: religious rites have the *phenomenology* of compulsion-discharge, not goal-directed action. They feel like things you have to do, not things you do in order to.

Then the formulation that *answers what we've been talking about together* in the last few turns:

> "The great contribution of Freud to the philosophy of mind has been the realization that human behavior is not only a food-getting strategy, but is also a language; that every move is at the same time a gesture. Symbolization is both an end and an instrument."

*Every move is at the same time a gesture.* This is the line. Action is *double*: it does the thing it does *and* it expresses a meaning. The same lifting of a glass is hydration *and* a toast. The same kneeling is fatigue-relief *and* reverence. Behavior runs on two channels simultaneously, the practical and the symbolic, and most of human conduct is happening on both at once. **Symbolization is both an end and an instrument.** When it's an instrument, it's serving other purposes (communication, planning, reasoning). When it's an end, it's terminating the brain's primary symbolic-transformation activity in overt action. Both are happening in any given gesture; the proportions vary.

This is also where the connection to your numinous-mode discussion lives. **The classical contemplative repertoire, briefly inventoried, with what each does mechanically:**

*Lectio divina* — slow repeated reading of charged text, letting a single phrase work on you over days. Mechanically: it gives the symbolic-transformation activity a high-quality, traditionally-tested symbol-cluster to digest, which then propagates outward and reshapes the felt-quality of ordinary experience. The Benedictine version is monastic, but the form is portable. You can do it with Scripture, with a poem, with a passage of Whitehead, with anything sufficiently dense.

*Ignatian imaginative prayer* — placing yourself imaginatively inside a charged scene (the Gospel narratives in Ignatius's case) and letting your felt response to the scene reveal something. Mechanically: it deliberately recruits the symbolic-image-making capacity for self-knowledge, surfacing material the discursive mind hasn't accessed, and submitting it to a tradition's discernment criteria. Ignatius's *Spiritual Exercises* are essentially a four-week protocol for systematically restructuring the symbolic substrate of a person's life. *They were engineered for exactly this purpose.*

*The examen* — daily structured review of the day's affective movements, noticing when you felt drawn toward life and when away, asking what was happening then. Mechanically: it builds a habit of metacognitive attention to your own symbolic-affective channel, which is the precondition for any deliberate work on it. You can't reshape what you can't notice.

*Sustained silence and solitude* — the desert tradition's most distinctive technology. Mechanically: it withdraws the input stream long enough that the existing symbolic content has to surface and be metabolized rather than continuously refreshed. This is unpleasant. The desert fathers were extremely clear that the early phase of solitude is dominated by the surfacing of one's own *logismoi* — intrusive symbolic-affective material — and that learning to sit with this without acting on it is the work. **What contemporary attention culture has done is make this kind of sitting nearly impossible by ensuring constant input.** Recovering even small amounts of unmediated time is itself a contemplative practice now.

*Memorization* — long-form internalization of poetry, scripture, prayer. Mechanically: it embeds high-quality symbol-clusters in long-term memory where they become available in idle and stressed moments to crowd out lower-quality content the symbolic-transformation activity would otherwise grasp at. Auden, Brodsky, the medieval monasteries, the Quranic *hafiz* tradition — all knew this. *Your symbolic-transformation activity will work with what you give it; if you give it nothing, it will work with what's at hand.*

*Liturgy and embodied ritual* — kneeling, prostration, fixed-hour prayer, fasting, the bodily forms of religious practice. Mechanically: it engages the symbolic-affective channel through *the body*, which is harder for the discursive ego to short-circuit than purely mental practice. The Eastern Orthodox tradition is especially clear that the body has to be involved or the work doesn't go deep. This is why simply reading about contemplative practice doesn't do what doing it does.

*Spiritual direction* — long-form one-on-one work with someone trained in discernment. Mechanically: it provides the external constraint surface — someone who can tell when your numinous experience is genuinely formative and when it's flattering you, when your darkness is the real work and when it's depressive rumination dressed in religious clothes, when intensification is right and when it's a warning sign. **This is the discernment-apparatus piece you cannot supply yourself**, and its absence is one of the reasons contemporary self-directed contemplative practice goes off the rails so often.

*Sacrament* — for those with access to it, the deliberate engagement with traditionally-charged ritual acts (Eucharist most centrally) where the form is both ancient and corporately maintained. Mechanically: it offers a symbolic operation whose form has been refined across millennia and whose effects on practitioners have been observed in detail by the tradition. You're not designing the operation; you're entering one that's already been engineered carefully by people who took it seriously across many lifetimes.

*Curating environment*. *The book's deepest contribution, the thing it's actually known for, is the discursive/presentational distinction in Chapter IV.* This is the move that art theory, music theory, and philosophy of mind have been catching up to for eighty years. The intuition in plain form: there are *two* fundamentally different kinds of symbolic operation. *Discursive* symbolism (language, mathematics, formal logic) works by combining discrete elements according to rules, producing meanings that can be analyzed, paraphrased, translated, decomposed. *Presentational* symbolism (visual art, music, dance, ritual, dream-imagery) works by total simultaneous patterning where the meaning is in the configuration as a whole and cannot be paraphrased without destroying it. **A poem can be paraphrased; a melody cannot.** A scientific paper can be summarized; a Rothko painting cannot. ### Chunk 1 — Meaning as Function, and the Logic of Signs (lines 1–320)

The chapter's opening sentence is one of those quiet sentences that hides what's actually happening. *"So much work has already been done on the logic of meaning that it is not necessary to present long arguments in support of the theory here employed; let it suffice to outline the facts, or if you will, the assumptions, on which my further considerations are to rest."* She is ducking into the philosophy of language as it stood in 1942 — Frege, Russell, the early Wittgenstein, Carnap, Peirce — and refusing to argue for her positions from scratch. But she is *not* simply adopting any one of those programs. The modesty is a sleight of hand. She is going to make her own move inside that territory, and the move is going to be different from any of theirs in a way that matters.

To see what she's doing, you have to feel the field she's working in. By 1942 the philosophy of language was dominated by two kinds of project. The Frege-Russell-Carnap project: meaning is to be analyzed by formal logic, ideally by translating natural language into a logically perspicuous notation in which sense and reference are precisely fixed and ambiguity disappears. The Peircean project: meaning is to be analyzed semiotically, by an exhaustive taxonomy of *kinds* of sign-relations, each finely distinguished from its neighbors. Both projects assumed that the right approach was *clarification* — replace muddled folk-meanings with sharper technical ones, then build outward. Langer accepts the spirit of both projects but does not trust either of their methods to deliver what she needs, and the first ten paragraphs of this chapter are her quietly explaining why.

#### The first move — function, not quality

> *"There is in fact no quality of meaning; its essence lies in the realm of logic, where one does not deal with qualities, but only with relations. It is not fair to say: 'Meaning is a relation,' for that suggests too simple a business. Most people think of a relation as a two-termed affair — 'A-in-relation-to-B'; but meaning involves several terms, and different types of meaning consist of different types and degrees of relationship. It is better, perhaps, to say: 'Meaning is not a quality, but a function of a term.'"*

Slow down on this. The sentence is doing structural work that determines the shape of everything that follows.

*Quality* means something a thing has, or does not have, on its own. *Property*. Inhering. Substantival. To say meaning is a quality is to say that words *have* meanings the way apples have redness — somewhere in or attached to the word, available for inspection, the *thing* you would point at if asked "what is meaning?" The whole metaphysical tradition from Plato through the medieval *logica terminorum* through Locke's "ideas in the mind" and Mill's connotation-as-attribute *runs on this picture*. Meanings are entities. They live somewhere. The job of philosophy is to find them and characterize them.

*Relation* is what holds between things. A two-place relation is a holding-between of two terms: A loves B, A is taller than B, A is a sign of B. The Peirce-influenced semiotics of the early twentieth century was *already* a relational picture — meaning isn't *in* the sign, it *holds between* sign and object. But, Langer says, this is still too simple. *Meaning involves several terms.* Not two. The two-term picture (sign-object) leaves out the *interpretant*, the subject for whom the relation holds. And once you've got the third term, you have to ask: how do all three relate? Not just pairwise, but *as a structured whole*. So even the relational picture, in its standard form, doesn't yet give you what meaning actually is.

*Function of a term*: this is the move. A *function* in her sense — and it is essentially a mathematical sense — is *a structure of relations viewed with one term in privileged position*. The chord example is the demonstration:

> *"A musical chord may be treated as a function of one note, known as the 'written bass,' by writing this one note and indicating its relation to all the other notes that are to go above it... The chord is treated as a pattern surrounding and including A. It is expressed as a function of A."*

Then the hinge: *"Any term in a pattern may be taken as a key-term to which the others are related."* The same chord can be expressed as a function of A, or as the second-inversion-seventh-on-the-dominant-of-G (a function of D), and *the chord doesn't change*. What changes is which term you've taken as the *key* through which to view the whole. The pattern is one. The descriptions are many. Each description has its own legitimate use depending on what you're trying to do — analyze the harmony, write organ figuring, identify the chord by its bass note.

This is what she is going to say about meaning. Meaning isn't a thing inside a word, and isn't even a relation between word and world. It is a *structure of several terms* (subject, symbol, conception, object — that fourth term is coming) and *the meaning-as-experienced is a function of which term you take as the key.* When the symbol is the key, you get *logical* meaning — what the word means objectively. When the subject is the key, you get *psychological* meaning — what the speaker means by it. When the object is the key, you get *epistemic content* — knowledge-of-what-is. These are not three different things; they are three legitimate descriptions of one structured whole, viewed from different terms.

This is, philosophically, an enormously consequential move. It is the move that lets her *contain* the warring schools of meaning-theory without having to choose among them. Frege's *Sinn-Bedeutung* distinction (sense vs. reference) is a real distinction, but it's a distinction between *terms in the meaning-pattern*, not between two metaphysically separate kinds of stuff. The Russellian project of replacing ordinary language with a logically perfect notation is legitimate as one operation within the meaning-structure (the symbol-as-key view) but mistaken as a totalizing program because it cannot capture what the structure looks like when other terms are taken as key. The Peircean taxonomic program is right that there are many distinguishable types of meaning-relation but wrong that they are *empirically* distinguishable — they are distinguishable *structurally*, as different patterns within the four-term function. **Each of the standing programs has hold of a piece of the structure and mistakes its piece for the whole.** Langer's functional-structural framing lets her absorb the legitimate work of each without inheriting the totalizing mistake of any.

You should feel here the structural similarity to what you've been working with elsewhere. Cassirer's argument throughout the *Erkenntnisproblem* and the *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms* runs on exactly this kind of move: don't take any one symbolic form (mathematical, mythical, linguistic) as the standard against which the others are measured; treat each as a *legitimate articulation* of a structured cognitive achievement, and study the *forms of articulation* themselves. Whitehead does the same thing in *Process and Reality* with prehensions: don't take any one mode of perception (presentational immediacy, causal efficacy) as the standard; treat them as legitimate operations within a structured whole. **The function-not-quality move is a consistent twentieth-century gesture across several traditions, and Langer is doing it for meaning.**

The Peirce footnote (line 51, with the *59,049 types boiled down to 66*) is doing the same work as the Stengers approach to Whitehead's apparatus we noticed earlier: not refuting Peirce, but *naming where his approach dead-ends*. The proliferation isn't a defect of Peirce's diligence; it's the symptom of treating meaning as something that admits of *empirical* taxonomy when its differences are actually *structural*. You will not find the species of meaning by looking harder at meaning-events; you will find them by analyzing the relational patterns that organize meaning-events.

#### The sign-analysis, slowly

Now she lays out signs proper. Note the structural carefulness — every term in the analysis is precisely placed.

> *"A sign indicates the existence — past, present, or future — of a thing, event, or condition. Wet streets are a sign that it has rained."*

The list of examples (wet streets, patter on roof, falling barometer, smell of smoke, scar, dawn, sleekness) is not random. It runs across temporal direction: signs of past (scar), present (smell of smoke), and future (falling barometer); across causal relation: part-whole (smoke as part of fire-event), correlation (sleekness with food), prediction (dawn with sunrise). She is establishing that signhood does not depend on any *one* kind of objective relation; it depends on the *one-to-one correlation* that the subject can use.

> *"The logical relation between a sign and its object is a very simple one: they are associated, somehow, to form a pair; that is to say, they stand in a one-to-one correlation."*

Hold the precision here. The relation is *simple* — pair-formation, no more — and the *somehow* is doing important work. She does not need to specify *how* sign and object got correlated (causal contiguity, conventional pairing, statistical association); the analysis is indifferent to mechanism. What matters is that there is *some* correlation, that it produces a pair, and that the pair stands in the meaning-structure.

The key step is the asymmetry argument:

> *"Two terms merely associated as a pair, like two socks, two balances of a scale, two ends of a stick, etc., could be interchanged without any harm. The difference is, that the subject for which they constitute a pair must find one more interesting than the other, and the latter more easily available than the former... It is only where one is perceptible and the other (harder or impossible to perceive) is interesting, that we actually have a case of signification belonging to a term."*

This is doing real philosophical work. **Signification is not in the world; it is in the asymmetric interest of the subject.** Lightning and thunder are *correlated*, full stop, as objective natural events. *Which one signifies which* depends entirely on what the subject can perceive and what they care about. To a person hiding from the storm in a cellar, thunder signifies that lightning has just struck; to a person watching the sky, lightning signifies that thunder is about to come. The objective correlation is symmetric; the sign-relation is asymmetric, and the asymmetry is *imposed by the subject's interest*. **The interpretant doesn't add a third term to a pre-existing two-term relation; it constitutes the sign-relation as a sign-relation.**

This is, by the way, a *deeply* Whiteheadian move. In *Symbolism: Its Meaning and Effect* (which footnote 5 cites directly), Whitehead argues that perception itself involves the constitutive activity of the percipient — what registers as a sign of what is determined by the *concrescing* subject's interests, not by the world alone. Langer is taking this and making it formal: the sign-relation is not in the world but is *constituted* by the subject's interest in coupling perceptible-but-uninteresting with imperceptible-but-interesting. The reason this matters: it forecloses the realist fantasy that signs are objective features of the world that the mind merely *reads off*. Even at this lowest level of meaning, mind is constitutive.

#### Why this matters for the chapter's larger argument

Slow down on what she has now positioned herself to do. By treating meaning as a function-of-a-term (not a quality, not a simple relation), she has a structural framework that can absorb signs and symbols *as different patterns within the same kind of analysis*. By analyzing signs as one-to-one correlations subject-relative, she has placed signs as *the simplest case* of a more general meaning-structure. When she pivots to symbols in the next chunk, she will not be introducing a different metaphysical kind; she will be introducing a more elaborate *structural pattern* — the four-term denotation function — within the same framework. **The continuity of method conceals the radical difference of pattern.**

This is why Chapter II's anthropological-biological argument needed Chapter III's formal-logical apparatus. In Chapter II she could *assert* that signs and symbols are different in kind because they do different work (signs guide action, symbols guide thought). But the assertion looks like a stipulation if you can't show *what* the structural difference is. Chapter III shows it: signs are three-term functions (subject-sign-object), symbols are four-term functions (subject-symbol-conception-object), and the extra term — the conception — is what makes possible thinking-of-things-in-their-absence, abstraction, communication of universals, all the things that distinguish symbol-using from sign-using. The biological-anthropological claim and the formal-logical claim are the same claim seen from two angles. **Chapter II shows you what symbol-use does in a life; Chapter III shows you the formal structure that makes that doing possible.**

#### Cross-resonances at this depth

**Frege.** The opening move *is* recognizably the Fregean refusal of psychologism — meaning is not a mental quality but a public-logical relation. But Langer is doing something Frege didn't: she is allowing the psychological *into* the structure as one term (the subject) without letting it dominate. Frege's worry that admitting psychology would destroy the objectivity of meaning is answered structurally: the subject is *part of* the function, but the function as a whole is structurally invariant — the same chord regardless of which note you express it from. **You can have the subject in your meaning-theory without losing the public objectivity of meaning, because the publicity lies in the structural pattern, not in any one term being privileged.** This is genuinely a contribution.

**Wittgenstein.** The chord analogy and the function-of-a-term framing are uncannily close to the *Tractatus*'s picture theory at the structural level — *the proposition's meaning is a function of its elements taken as standing in a definite relation*. Langer is unlikely to have read the *Tractatus* deeply, but she's in the same conceptual neighborhood, and her version is in some ways more capacious because the picture-relation is just one of several possible functional relations within the broader meaning-structure she's building. The Tractatus is the *limit case* of Langer's analysis where the symbol-pattern *is* a picture of the fact-pattern; Langer will return to this in the picture-analysis section of Chunk 3.

**Peirce.** The footnoted dismissal of Peirce's 59,049 categories is unfair to the *spirit* of Peirce's work even if accurate about its methodological limits. Peirce was reaching for what Langer is now formalizing — the structural-relational shape of sign-functions — but did so by trying to *enumerate* rather than *generalize*. Where Peirce gives you icon, index, symbol, then qualisign-sinsign-legisign, then rheme-dicent-argument, and tries to combine them combinatorially, Langer says: *one structure, varied by which term you take as key, varied by how many terms the structure includes*. **The information Peirce was tracking is preserved in Langer's apparatus, but reorganized.** A Peircean *index* is a sign in Langer's strict sense (causal-existential pairing); a Peircean *symbol* is what Langer is calling a symbol (four-term denotation function); a Peircean *icon* will be partly handled in the picture-analysis (formal-analogy resemblance). Hoffmeyer's biosemiotics, working out of Peirce, gives you the *biological depth* of the indexical-sign use that Langer treats as "animal intelligence" in this chapter; the two analyses are complementary rather than rivals.

**Stengers/Whitehead.** The methodological gesture you've been tracking — refusing to inherit the opponent's question, reframing at the structural level rather than negotiating within their categories — is what Langer is doing here. The whole "is meaning a quality or a relation?" debate is dissolved by *function-not-quality*, and the dissolution is not a deflationary one (she is not saying the question is meaningless); it is a *reframing* in which the legitimate work of the prior positions is preserved while the totalizing claim of each is foreclosed.

**Cassirer.** Symbolic forms in Cassirer are exactly what Langer is now formalizing — different *patterns* of symbolic articulation, none reducible to any other, each with its own structural integrity. Langer is bringing the formal-logical tools to a project Cassirer was carrying in a more historical-cultural register. The Cusan thread you've been working on lives here too: *finite mind as constitutively measure-making rather than passively reflective* is the deep philosophical pedigree of *the subject as constitutive term in the meaning-function*. Langer doesn't cite Cusanus, but she is doing what the Cusanus-Cassirer trajectory has been doing for five hundred years.

### Chunk 2 — Symbols, Helen Keller, and the Three Meanings of "Meaning" (lines 320–700)

This is the chapter's structural heart. Slow down. The whole edifice she's building rests on what she does here.

#### The Mr. Smith passage and the four-term structure

The pivot from sign-analysis to symbol-analysis comes through a deliberately mundane example:

> *"A term which is used symbolically and not signally does not evoke action appropriate to the presence of its object. If I say: 'Napoleon,' you do not bow to the conqueror of Europe as though I had introduced him, but merely think of him. If I mention a Mr. Smith of our common acquaintance, you may be led to tell me something about him 'behind his back,' which is just what you would not do in his presence."*

The Mr. Smith case is the precise illustration. The *same name* — "Mr. Smith" — can function in two utterly different ways depending on the structural pattern in which it appears. As a *sign* of Mr. Smith's presence ("Mr. Smith!"), it would direct your action toward Mr. Smith *in person* — you'd stop your story, turn around, greet him. As a *symbol* of Mr. Smith ("did you hear what Smith did?"), it directs your thought *toward a conception* of Mr. Smith — and you can perfectly well do this when Mr. Smith is absent, indeed *only* when he's absent if you're going to say anything candid. The *behavior the term evokes is structurally different in the two cases*, and the difference isn't about Mr. Smith's actual location; it's about *which structural pattern the term is operating in.*

Then the formulation that does the work:

> *"Symbols are not proxy for their objects, but are vehicles for the conception of objects. To conceive a thing or a situation is not the same thing as to 'react toward it' overtly, or to be aware of its presence. In talking about things we have conceptions of them, not the things themselves; and it is the conceptions, not the things, that symbols directly 'mean.'"*

Hold the words: **vehicles for the conception of objects.** The vocabulary is deliberately Fregean — Frege's *Sinn* (sense) is approximately what Langer is calling *conception* — but Langer is making the structural point in her own terms. A symbol is not a substitute or proxy for its object (which would still be a three-term relation, just with the symbol replacing the object). A symbol is a *vehicle* — a carrier — for *a conception of* the object, which is a separate term from both symbol and object. The conception is *what the subject thinks* when the symbol is invoked. The object is *what the conception fits.* Four terms, structurally distinct.

> *"In an ordinary sign-function, there are three essential terms: subject, sign, and object. In denotation, which is the commonest kind of symbol-function, there have to be four: subject, symbol, conception, and object. The radical difference between sign-meaning and symbol-meaning can therefore be logically exhibited, for it rests on a difference of pattern, it is strictly a different function."*

This is the formal payoff of the whole chapter so far. **The difference between sign and symbol is not a difference of degree, of refinement, of complexity. It is a structural difference: a four-term function vs. a three-term function.** And once you have the four-term function, you have something that is qualitatively new: the conception is *separable* from both the symbol (which carries it) and the object (which it fits). You can have the conception *without* the object being present (think about the absent Mr. Smith). You can have the conception *embodied in different symbols* (Mr. Smith, James Smith, that fellow with the moustache). You can compare different subjects' conceptions of the same object and find them to differ. **The conception is the *operation* that symbols make possible and signs do not.** Animals lack symbols not because their signs are crude but because they are stuck in a three-term structure where conception cannot appear as an independent term.

This is also where Deacon's "symbolic threshold" lives in formal terms. To cross from indexical to symbolic semiosis is to cross from the three-term to the four-term structure. The conception, once available as an independent term, can be combined with other conceptions in ways that the bare object-sign correlation cannot — and that combinatorial freedom is what produces language, abstract thought, displacement, generality, all the things Deacon (and Langer) say constitute the symbolic.

#### The Helen Keller passage as "affidavit"

She quotes Keller's own description of the breakthrough at the well-house, then says:

> *"This passage is the best affidavit we could hope to find for the genuine difference between sign and symbol. The sign is something to act upon, or a means to command action; the symbol is an instrument of thought."*

The Keller passage is doing a specific kind of work, and you should feel why she calls it an *affidavit* — a witness statement to a structural fact. Keller had been using finger-motions for months. Her teacher had spelled water into her hand many times before that day. The *finger-motion* "w-a-t-e-r" was perfectly available to her; she had it as a *sign* — a stimulus to which she had been conditioned to respond. *"She had learned many finger acts, but they were as yet a meaningless play."*

What happens at the well is not the acquisition of a new finger-motion. It is the recognition that *this finger-motion stands for that thing in a way that allows me to think about that thing.* The motion shifted from being a stimulus to being a *name*. The four-term structure suddenly became available where only the three-term structure had been. Hold the precision of Keller's own phrase: *"This thought, if a wordless sensation may be called a thought."* She is *reflecting back* on her pre-symbolic state and recognizing that what she had then was *not yet thought* in the full sense. Not because she was unintelligent — she was intensely intelligent — but because thought *requires* the four-term structure, requires the separable conception, and that wasn't available to her until the symbol-function clicked into place.

> *"Real thinking is possible only in the light of genuine language, no matter how limited, how primitive; in her case, it became possible with the discovery that 'w-a-t-e-r' was not necessarily a sign that water was wanted or expected, but was the name of this substance, by which it could be mentioned, conceived, remembered."*

*Mentioned, conceived, remembered* — the three operations the four-term structure makes possible that the three-term structure does not. *Mentioning*: invoking the conception in the absence of the object. *Conceiving*: holding the conception independently of any particular sign-encounter. *Remembering*: re-invoking the conception across time, available for combination with present conceptions. None of these are doable in the three-term structure. All of them open at once when the fourth term appears.

The Keller passage is so important to Langer's argument because it is one of the rare cases where the *transition* between the two structures is *witnessed and reported*. Most humans cross into symbolic life so early in development that no memory of the pre-symbolic state survives. Keller crossed late and articulately, and her testimony gives Langer a clean phenomenological case that confirms her structural analysis. The chapter pivots on this passage because the formal point would otherwise be vulnerable to a charge of mere stipulation; with Keller, it is anchored in a witnessed crossing.

#### The three meanings of "meaning"

The structural payoff in formal terms:

> *"Here, then, are the three most familiar meanings of the one word, 'meaning': signification, denotation, and connotation. All three are equally and perfectly legitimate, but in no possible way interchangeable."*

Slow down. These three are doing a lot of work and they need to be properly held apart.

**Signification** is the three-term sign-relation: a sign signifies its object by being correlated with it (wet streets signify rain). The relation is between sign and object, with the subject as interpretant. Sign-meaning.

**Denotation** is the four-term symbol-to-object relation: a symbol denotes its object by being associated, *for a subject*, with a *conception* that fits the object. "James" denotes the actual person James through the conception we have of James, which the actual James satisfies. Symbol-meaning *as it reaches the world*.

**Connotation** is the four-term symbol-to-conception relation: a symbol connotes the conception it conveys. *"The connotation of a word is the conception it conveys. Because the connotation remains with the symbol when the object of its denotation is neither present nor looked for, we are able to think about the object without reacting to it overtly at all."* Symbol-meaning *as it lives in mind*.

This is approximately Frege's *Sinn-Bedeutung* distinction, but Langer's framing is structurally cleaner because she's locating both within the four-term function rather than treating them as two metaphysically distinct kinds. *Connotation* and *denotation* are not two things a word *has*; they are *two relations within the same four-term structure*, viewed from different keying terms. Connotation = the symbol-to-conception edge of the structure. Denotation = the symbol-to-object edge (mediated by the conception). Take the chord analogy: same pattern, different functional descriptions.

The pedagogical point worth pausing on: this is *why* she insists meaning is a function-not-a-quality. If meaning were a quality, you'd have to choose between connotation and denotation as the *real* meaning, and philosophers have been quarreling fruitlessly over that choice for centuries. Once meaning is a *function*, both are real, both are legitimate, both are operations within one structure, and you can analyze each precisely without needing to make either the master of the other. **The dispute dissolves; the analytical clarity sharpens.**

#### Error in symbol-use

She closes the section with a careful analysis of error:

> *"In every analysis of sign-using or symbol-using, we must be able to account not only for the genesis of knowledge, but also of that most human characteristic, error."*

Sign-error has been handled: misinterpretation, the wet-streets-from-the-sprinkler-wagon case. *Practical disappointment* as the criterion. Symbol-error is more complicated because the four-term structure has more places where things can break.

Two distinct symbol-errors get distinguished:

> *"Willy had mistaken one object for another; he misapplied a term of which he knew the connotation well enough."*

This is the Willy-and-the-H₂SO₄ case: Willy had the *right* connotation for "water" but applied the term to the wrong substance. The connotation was correctly held but the object didn't fit it. *Misapplication*.

> *"We may know that the symbol 'James' applies to our next-door neighbor, and quite mistakenly suppose it connotes a man with all sorts of virtues or frailties. This time we are not mistaking James for someone else, but we are mistaken about James."*

This is the *misconception* case: we know what the symbol denotes but we have a wrong conception attached to it. The denotation is right; the connotation is wrong. *Error about*, rather than *error in*.

The four-term structure makes both kinds of error analytically describable and distinguishable, and *neither* reduces to sign-error. Sign-error is one-dimensional (correlation broken). Symbol-error is structurally richer because the four-term function has more relations that can fail. **This is part of why symbolic life is *cognitively* harder than sign-life and why human thought has the kinds of failure-modes it has.** Misapplications, misconceptions, equivocations, conflations — all live inside the symbol-structure, none would even be possible in pure sign-relations.

#### The proper-name oddity

There's an interesting passage on the peculiarity of proper names:

> *"It is a peculiarity of proper names that they have a different connotation for every denotation. Because their connotation is not fixed, they can be arbitrarily applied... A word that functions as a proper noun is excused from the usual rules of application."*

This is worth noting because it's a place where Langer is doing analytical work that contemporary philosophy of language would later formalize at much greater length (Kripke's *Naming and Necessity*, the rigid-designator debates of the 1970s). Langer's casual observation — that proper names work differently because their connotation isn't fixed *in the way common nouns' connotations are* — is the seed of an enormous later literature. She doesn't develop it; she just notes it and moves on. **One of the reasons philosophy specialists tend to underrate her is that she keeps making moves like this — sketching in a sentence what someone else will later turn into a career — and not pausing to flag that she's done so.**

The passing observation about primitive societies (names changed when their accepted connotations don't fit the bearer — "Lightfoot," "Hawkeye," "Whizzing Death") is anthropologically dated in framing but the structural point is real: in some symbol-systems, proper names *do* carry connotations that the bearer must satisfy. The connotation/denotation independence of proper names is a feature of certain modern symbol-systems, not a logical necessity. **Symbol-structures can be more or less constrained depending on the cultural form they take.** This is a small foreshadowing of the discursive/presentational distinction that Chapter IV will develop.

#### Closing the section: the metaphysic-to-logic transition

The section ends with a methodological flag:

> *"So much, then, for the venerable 'logic of terms.' It appears a little more complicated than in the medieval books, since we must add to the long-recognized functions, connotation and denotation, a third one, signification, which is fundamentally different from the other two; and since, moreover, in discussing the semantic functions of terms we have made the rare discovery that they really are functions, not powers or mysterious properties or what-not, and have treated them accordingly. The traditional 'logic of terms' is really a metaphysic of meaning; the new philosophy of meaning is first of all a logic of terms — of signs and symbols — an analysis of the relational patterns in which 'meaning' may be sought."*

Hold the precision: *"The traditional 'logic of terms' is really a metaphysic of meaning; the new philosophy of meaning is first of all a logic of terms."* The medieval *logica terminorum* (Ockham, Buridan, the supposition theorists) treated meaning as something words *had* — a quality, a power, a property — and tried to analyze the kinds of meaning by analyzing the kinds of properties. Langer is saying: *that whole approach was metaphysics dressed as logic*. The genuinely logical approach is *structural*: not what is the word, but what is the pattern in which the word participates. *Meaning is what a word does within a structured function*, not what a word has by virtue of being a word. **This is one of the cleanest articulations of the linguistic-turn move in mid-century philosophy of language, and Langer makes it in passing.**

#### Cross-resonances at this depth

**Frege.** Connotation ≈ *Sinn*, denotation ≈ *Bedeutung*. But Langer's framework has the advantage of holding both within one structure, with connotation and denotation as *positions in the four-term function*. Frege treated *Sinn* and *Bedeutung* as somewhat separable; Langer's structure ties them together. Symbol → conception → object is *one structured chain*, and connotation and denotation are different *cuts* through it. The unity is preserved; the distinction is sharpened.

**Whitehead.** The four-term structure echoes Whitehead's prehensive structure — the concrescing subject, the prehended datum, the subjective form (how the datum is felt), and the resulting actual occasion. Both pictures insist on an *intermediate term* between the subject and the world: for Whitehead, the subjective form; for Langer, the conception. **Both are saying: don't analyze cognition as a two-term subject-object relation; the intermediate term is doing constitutive work and you'll miss the structure if you skip it.**

**Cassirer.** Connotation/denotation differentiates the *idealizing* pole of symbolic activity (the conception, the public concept, the *form*) from the *referential* pole (the world the conception fits). Cassirer's "symbolic forms" are precisely the historical-cultural articulations of how the connotation-pole has been organized in different domains — myth-form, language-form, science-form. Langer is making the formal-structural cut that Cassirer's whole project assumes.

**Hoffmeyer/Peirce.** The three-term sign-relation (subject, sign, object) maps onto Peirce's basic semiotic triad (interpretant, sign, object), and the indexical sign-relations Hoffmeyer says constitute biosemiosis live exactly here. The four-term symbol-relation is a *new structure* that emerges only with conception-as-separable-term. Hoffmeyer would say: this new structure is *built on top of* the biosemiotic substrate, not separate from it. Langer doesn't quite have this developmental story, but the formal analysis is compatible with it.

**Helen Keller as case-data.** The Keller passage is doing for Langer's argument what Whitehead's Bohr-atom example does for the introduction of the organism in *Science and the Modern World* (which we worked through in the Stengers campaign): a particular case where a structural shift *appears at the level of phenomenon*, witnessed and reported with precision. #### The Helen Keller passage as "affidavit"

She quotes Keller's own description of the breakthrough at the well-house, then says:

> *"This passage is the best affidavit we could hope to find for the genuine difference between sign and symbol. The sign is something to act upon, or a means to command action; the symbol is an instrument of thought."*

The Keller passage is doing a specific kind of work, and you should feel why she calls it an *affidavit* — a witness statement to a structural fact. Keller had been using finger-motions for months. Her teacher had spelled water into her hand many times before that day. The *finger-motion* "w-a-t-e-r" was perfectly available to her; she had it as a *sign* — a stimulus to which she had been conditioned to respond. *"She had learned many finger acts, but they were as yet a meaningless play."*

What happens at the well is not the acquisition of a new finger-motion. It is the recognition that *this finger-motion stands for that thing in a way that allows me to think about that thing.* The motion shifted from being a stimulus to being a *name*. The four-term structure suddenly became available where only the three-term structure had been. Hold the precision of Keller's own phrase: *"This thought, if a wordless sensation may be called a thought."* She is *reflecting back* on her pre-symbolic state and recognizing that what she had then was *not yet thought* in the full sense. Not because she was unintelligent — she was intensely intelligent — but because thought *requires* the four-term structure, requires the separable conception, and that wasn't available to her until the symbol-function clicked into place.

> *"Real thinking is possible only in the light of genuine language, no matter how limited, how primitive; in her case, it became possible with the discovery that 'w-a-t-e-r' was not necessarily a sign that water was wanted or expected, but was the name of this substance, by which it could be mentioned, conceived, remembered."*

*Mentioned, conceived, remembered* — the three operations the four-term structure makes possible that the three-term structure does not. *Mentioning*: invoking the conception in the absence of the object. *Conceiving*: holding the conception independently of any particular sign-encounter. *Remembering*: re-invoking the conception across time, available for combination with present conceptions. None of these are doable in the three-term structure. All of them open at once when the fourth term appears.

The Keller passage is so important to Langer's argument because it is one of the rare cases where the *transition* between the two structures is *witnessed and reported*. Most humans cross into symbolic life so early in development that no memory of the pre-symbolic state survives. Keller crossed late and articulately, and her testimony gives Langer a clean phenomenological case that confirms her structural analysis. The chapter pivots on this passage because the formal point would otherwise be vulnerable to a charge of mere stipulation; with Keller, it is anchored in a witnessed crossing.

#### The three meanings of "meaning"

The structural payoff in formal terms:

> *"Here, then, are the three most familiar meanings of the one word, 'meaning': signification, denotation, and connotation. All three are equally and perfectly legitimate, but in no possible way interchangeable."*

Slow down. These three are doing a lot of work and they need to be properly held apart.

**Signification** is the three-term sign-relation: a sign signifies its object by being correlated with it (wet streets signify rain). The relation is between sign and object, with the subject as interpretant. Sign-meaning.

**Denotation** is the four-term symbol-to-object relation: a symbol denotes its object by being associated, *for a subject*, with a *conception* that fits the object. "James" denotes the actual person James through the conception we have of James, which the actual James satisfies. Symbol-meaning *as it reaches the world*.

**Connotation** is the four-term symbol-to-conception relation: a symbol connotes the conception it conveys. *"The connotation of a word is the conception it conveys. Because the connotation remains with the symbol when the object of its denotation is neither present nor looked for, we are able to think about the object without reacting to it overtly at all."* Symbol-meaning *as it lives in mind*.

This is approximately Frege's *Sinn-Bedeutung* distinction, but Langer's framing is structurally cleaner because she's locating both within the four-term function rather than treating them as two metaphysically distinct kinds. *Connotation* and *denotation* are not two things a word *has*; they are *two relations within the same four-term structure*, viewed from different keying terms. Connotation = the symbol-to-conception edge of the structure. Denotation = the symbol-to-object edge (mediated by the conception). Take the chord analogy: same pattern, different functional descriptions.

The pedagogical point worth pausing on: this is *why* she insists meaning is a function-not-a-quality. If meaning were a quality, you'd have to choose between connotation and denotation as the *real* meaning, and philosophers have been quarreling fruitlessly over that choice for centuries. Once meaning is a *function*, both are real, both are legitimate, both are operations within one structure, and you can analyze each precisely without needing to make either the master of the other. **The dispute dissolves; the analytical clarity sharpens.**

#### Error in symbol-use

She closes the section with a careful analysis of error:

> *"In every analysis of sign-using or symbol-using, we must be able to account not only for the genesis of knowledge, but also of that most human characteristic, error."*

Sign-error has been handled: misinterpretation, the wet-streets-from-the-sprinkler-wagon case. *Practical disappointment* as the criterion. Symbol-error is more complicated because the four-term structure has more places where things can break.

Two distinct symbol-errors get distinguished:

> *"Willy had mistaken one object for another; he misapplied a term of which he knew the connotation well enough."*

This is the Willy-and-the-H₂SO₄ case: Willy had the *right* connotation for "water" but applied the term to the wrong substance. The connotation was correctly held but the object didn't fit it. *Misapplication*.

> *"We may know that the symbol 'James' applies to our next-door neighbor, and quite mistakenly suppose it connotes a man with all sorts of virtues or frailties. This time we are not mistaking James for someone else, but we are mistaken about James."*

This is the *misconception* case: we know what the symbol denotes but we have a wrong conception attached to it. The denotation is right; the connotation is wrong. *Error about*, rather than *error in*.

The four-term structure makes both kinds of error analytically describable and distinguishable, and *neither* reduces to sign-error. Sign-error is one-dimensional (correlation broken). Symbol-error is structurally richer because the four-term function has more relations that can fail. **This is part of why symbolic life is *cognitively* harder than sign-life and why human thought has the kinds of failure-modes it has.** Misapplications, misconceptions, equivocations, conflations — all live inside the symbol-structure, none would even be possible in pure sign-relations.

#### The proper-name oddity

There's an interesting passage on the peculiarity of proper names:

> *"It is a peculiarity of proper names that they have a different connotation for every denotation. Because their connotation is not fixed, they can be arbitrarily applied... A word that functions as a proper noun is excused from the usual rules of application."*

This is worth noting because it's a place where Langer is doing analytical work that contemporary philosophy of language would later formalize at much greater length (Kripke's *Naming and Necessity*, the rigid-designator debates of the 1970s). Langer's casual observation — that proper names work differently because their connotation isn't fixed *in the way common nouns' connotations are* — is the seed of an enormous later literature. She doesn't develop it; she just notes it and moves on. **One of the reasons philosophy specialists tend to underrate her is that she keeps making moves like this — sketching in a sentence what someone else will later turn into a career — and not pausing to flag that she's done so.**

The passing observation about primitive societies (names changed when their accepted connotations don't fit the bearer — "Lightfoot," "Hawkeye," "Whizzing Death") is anthropologically dated in framing but the structural point is real: in some symbol-systems, proper names *do* carry connotations that the bearer must satisfy. The connotation/denotation independence of proper names is a feature of certain modern symbol-systems, not a logical necessity. **Symbol-structures can be more or less constrained depending on the cultural form they take.** This is a small foreshadowing of the discursive/presentational distinction that Chapter IV will develop.

#### Closing the section: the metaphysic-to-logic transition

The section ends with a methodological flag:

> *"So much, then, for the venerable 'logic of terms.' It appears a little more complicated than in the medieval books, since we must add to the long-recognized functions, connotation and denotation, a third one, signification, which is fundamentally different from the other two; and since, moreover, in discussing the semantic functions of terms we have made the rare discovery that they really are functions, not powers or mysterious properties or what-not, and have treated them accordingly. The traditional 'logic of terms' is really a metaphysic of meaning; the new philosophy of meaning is first of all a logic of terms — of signs and symbols — an analysis of the relational patterns in which 'meaning' may be sought."*

Hold the precision: *"The traditional 'logic of terms' is really a metaphysic of meaning; the new philosophy of meaning is first of all a logic of terms."* The medieval *logica terminorum* (Ockham, Buridan, the supposition theorists) treated meaning as something words *had* — a quality, a power, a property — and tried to analyze the kinds of meaning by analyzing the kinds of properties. Langer is saying: *that whole approach was metaphysics dressed as logic*. The genuinely logical approach is *structural*: not what is the word, but what is the pattern in which the word participates. *Meaning is what a word does within a structured function*, not what a word has by virtue of being a word. **This is one of the cleanest articulations of the linguistic-turn move in mid-century philosophy of language, and Langer makes it in passing.**

#### Cross-resonances at this depth

**Frege.** Connotation ≈ *Sinn*, denotation ≈ *Bedeutung*. But Langer's framework has the advantage of holding both within one structure, with connotation and denotation as *positions in the four-term function*. Frege treated *Sinn* and *Bedeutung* as somewhat separable; Langer's structure ties them together. Symbol → conception → object is *one structured chain*, and connotation and denotation are different *cuts* through it. The unity is preserved; the distinction is sharpened.

**Whitehead.** The four-term structure echoes Whitehead's prehensive structure — the concrescing subject, the prehended datum, the subjective form (how the datum is felt), and the resulting actual occasion. Both pictures insist on an *intermediate term* between the subject and the world: for Whitehead, the subjective form; for Langer, the conception. **Both are saying: don't analyze cognition as a two-term subject-object relation; the intermediate term is doing constitutive work and you'll miss the structure if you skip it.**

**Cassirer.** Connotation/denotation differentiates the *idealizing* pole of symbolic activity (the conception, the public concept, the *form*) from the *referential* pole (the world the conception fits). Cassirer's "symbolic forms" are precisely the historical-cultural articulations of how the connotation-pole has been organized in different domains — myth-form, language-form, science-form. Langer is making the formal-structural cut that Cassirer's whole project assumes.

**Hoffmeyer/Peirce.** The three-term sign-relation (subject, sign, object) maps onto Peirce's basic semiotic triad (interpretant, sign, object), and the indexical sign-relations Hoffmeyer says constitute biosemiosis live exactly here. The four-term symbol-relation is a *new structure* that emerges only with conception-as-separable-term. Hoffmeyer would say: this new structure is *built on top of* the biosemiotic substrate, not separate from it. Langer doesn't quite have this developmental story, but the formal analysis is compatible with it.

**Helen Keller as case-data.** The Keller passage is doing for Langer's argument what Whitehead's Bohr-atom example does for the introduction of the organism in *Science and the Modern World* (which we worked through in the Stengers campaign): a particular case where a structural shift *appears at the level of phenomenon*, witnessed and reported with precision. The case isn't decorative; it's the point at which the formal claim is anchored in observation. Without the Keller passage, the four-term structure looks like a stipulation. With it, it has a witness.

### Chunk 3 — Pictures, Concept vs. Conception, Language as Supreme Symbolism (lines 700–1033)

This is where the chapter's formal apparatus gets stretched into its most consequential application. Two moves dominate. First: pictures are symbols, not duplicates, and what makes them symbols is *form-sharing*, not resemblance. Second: language is the supreme symbolism not because it has any unique semantic capacity but because of certain *physical and economic properties* of vocal sound that make it uniquely combinatorially powerful. The two moves together set up the seam that opens onto Chapter IV — because if language is supreme *for these reasons*, then there must be other forms of symbolism that *do other things* that language can't, which is the discursive/presentational distinction waiting in the wings.

#### Pictures, propositions, and the structural turn

The pivot from terms to discourse is announced at the boundary between Chunk 2 and Chunk 3:

> *"But a semantic of separate symbols is only a rudimentary foundation for a more interesting aspect of meaning. Everything is mere propaedeutic until we come to discourse. It is in discursive thinking that truth and falsehood are born. Before terms are built into propositions, they assert nothing, preclude nothing; in fact, although they may name things, and convey ideas of such things, they say nothing."*

Hold the precision. *Names denote, but they do not say.* "Cat" denotes a kind of animal; it doesn't claim anything about cats. To say something — to make an assertion that can be true or false — you need a *combination* of symbols in a structure. You need *propositional form*. The sentence "the cat is on the mat" can be true or false; the word "cat" cannot. **Truth is a property of structures, not of terms.** This is the linguistic-turn insight in compressed form.

Then the move that sets up everything that follows:

> *"It has become apparent that a proposition fits a fact not only because it contains names for the things and actions involved in the fact, but also because it combines them in a pattern analogous, somehow, to the pattern in which the named objects are 'in fact' combined. A proposition is a picture of a structure — the structure of a state of affairs. The unity of a proposition is the same sort of unity that belongs to a picture, which presents one scene, no matter how many items may be distinguishable within it."*

This is Wittgenstein's picture theory from the *Tractatus*, named explicitly. Langer is taking it, treating it as broadly correct, and *extending* it past where Wittgenstein left it. Wittgenstein used the picture analogy and then quickly moved to logical form; Langer is going to take the picture analogy *seriously as a picture analogy* and ask what makes a picture a picture of something. **That asking is going to crack open a question Wittgenstein didn't ask, and the answer is going to take her toward Chapter IV.**

#### The rabbit-and-cat figures: form, not resemblance

The next several pages are a careful analysis of what makes a picture function as a symbol. The childish outline drawing of a rabbit is the lever:

> *"The childish outline drawing... is immediately recognized as a rabbit, yet it really looks so unlike one that even a person nearly blind could not for a moment be made to think that he saw a rabbit sitting on the open page of his book. All it shares with the 'reality' is a certain proportion of parts — the position and relative length of 'ears,' the dot where an 'eye' belongs, the 'head' and 'body' in relation to each other... Beside it is exactly the same figure with different ears and tail; any child will accept it as a cat."*

The analytical claim: a picture functions as a symbol *by virtue of formal analogy* — it shares with its object certain *structural relations among parts* (the proportions, the positions, the relations of head-to-body, of ear-to-head) — and *not* by virtue of visual resemblance. The rabbit-line-drawing doesn't *look like* a rabbit; it *has the form of* a rabbit-relation-pattern, and that's what lets it function as a symbol of rabbits. Same line drawing with different ears and tail is now a cat, because the structural pattern has shifted at the relevant points. *The form is doing all the symbolic work; the visual fidelity is incidental.*

Then the strengthening of the claim through the architect's elevation drawing and the diagram:

> *"One step removed from the 'styled' picture is the diagram. Here any attempt at imitating the parts of an object has been given up. The parts are merely indicated by conventional symbols, such as dots, circles, crosses, or what-not. The only thing that is 'pictured' is the relation of the parts to each other. A diagram is a 'picture' only of a form."*

The progression — photograph, painting, sketch, elevation drawing, diagram — is a progression toward *purer form-representation*. The photograph carries lots of incidental information (color, texture, lighting). The diagram carries *only* the form, the relation-pattern. **All five can represent the same house** because all five preserve the relevant structural pattern. The pattern is what's being represented; everything else is, semiotically speaking, incidental decoration.

This is the move that brings her to the deepest claim of the chapter:

> *"Because each one of the very different images expresses the same relation of parts, which you have fastened on in formulating your conception of the house... That which all adequate conceptions of an object must have in common, is the concept of the object. The same concept is embodied in a multitude of conceptions. It is a form that appears in all versions of thought or imagery that can connote the object in question, a form clothed in different integuments of sensation for every different mind."*

#### Concept vs. conception — the deepest distinction in the chapter

This is the move you should slow down on most. It is the formal heart of everything Langer does for the rest of the book.

A *conception* is what an individual subject has in mind when they grasp a symbol. It is *clothed* — in personal images, sense-memories, affective tones, idiosyncratic associations. My conception of *house* is colored by the houses I've lived in; yours by yours. They are *different* conceptions even if they pick out the same kind of object.

A *concept* is the *form that all adequate conceptions share*. It is what makes my conception and yours both conceptions *of the same thing*. It is *abstract* — not in the sense of vague, but in the sense of *form-without-individual-clothing*, the pattern of relations that any adequate conception must instantiate. The concept of *house* is the structural pattern (some kind of dwelling-form, with parts in certain relations, etc.) that my house-conception and your house-conception both embody, despite their personal differences.

> *"A concept is all that a symbol really conveys. But just as quickly as the concept is symbolized to us, our own imagination dresses it up in a private, personal conception, which we can distinguish from the communicable public concept only by a process of abstraction. Whenever we deal with a concept we must have some particular presentation of it, through which we grasp it. What we actually have 'in mind' is always universalium in re. When we express this universalium we use another symbol to exhibit it, and still another res will embody it for the mind that sees through our symbol and apprehends the concept in its own way."*

The medieval Latin tag *universalium in re* — the universal in the thing — is doing precise work. Langer is taking a position in the ancient universals debate without quite saying so. Universals (concepts, forms) are not separate Platonic entities (*ante rem*, before the thing); not mere mental constructions (*post rem*, after the thing); but *in the thing* (*in re*) — the form realized in particulars, including in the particular conceptions we have. **This is structurally Aristotelian-Thomist territory, and the affinity is not accidental — Langer's mentor Whitehead was deeply read in scholastic philosophy, and the *in re* position is the metaphysically stable one for thinkers who want to take both form and embodiment seriously without choosing.**

The communicative payoff:

> *"Probably no two people see anything just alike. Their sense organs differ, their attention and imagery and feelings differ so that they cannot be supposed to have identical impressions. But if their respective conceptions of a thing... embody the same concept, they will understand each other."*

**This is the answer to the problem of intersubjective communication.** Two minds can never share conceptions — conceptions are private, clothed, personal. But two minds can share *concepts* — the formal structural patterns that organize their respective conceptions. Communication doesn't require my picture in your head; it requires that your picture and my picture both embody the same form. *Communication is form-sharing through symbol-mediation, not content-transfer.* This is a profoundly Cassirerean claim — symbolic forms are public-formal patterns that organize the private-experiential — and Langer is giving it a clean formal articulation.

The follow-up sentence is among the most important in the chapter:

> *"The power of understanding symbols, i.e. of regarding everything about a sense-datum as irrelevant except a certain form that it embodies, is the most characteristic mental trait of mankind. It issues in an unconscious, spontaneous process of abstraction, which goes on all the time in the human mind: a process of recognizing the concept in any configuration given to experience, and forming a conception accordingly. That is the real sense of Aristotle's definition of man as 'the rational animal.' Abstractive seeing is the foundation of our rationality, and is its definite guarantee long before the dawn of any conscious generalization or syllogism."*

Hold the precision: *abstractive seeing*. Reason is not, fundamentally, syllogistic operation on previously-acquired propositions. Reason is, fundamentally, *the capacity to see the form in the configuration*. To pick up the rabbit-pattern from the line drawing. To recognize the same house in photograph and elevation drawing. To grasp that "Mr. Smith" said by you and "Mr. Smith" said by me are about the same person despite our different inner pictures of him. *That perceptual-cognitive operation is the ground of rationality; the syllogism is a late and specialized application of it.* **Aristotle's definition is being reread: man is rational not because man can do logic but because man can see-as, can apprehend form, can extract the universal-in-the-thing.** This is an enormous claim and she makes it in two sentences.

You should feel the resonance with what Cassirer is doing in the *Erkenntnisproblem* trajectory and with what Whitehead is doing in his theory of perception. Cassirer: cognition is not reception of data but constitutive form-giving, *Bildungsprozess* of the symbolic. Whitehead: perception involves the *eternal objects* (forms) ingressing into the prehensive activity of the actual occasion — perception just *is* form-recognition in the flux. Langer: abstractive seeing as the ground of rationality, prior to and constitutive of syllogistic reason. **Three thinkers, three idioms, one position: form-apprehension is constitutive of cognition, not a late operation upon already-given content.**

#### The double picture of language

Now the chapter turns to language proper. Two questions get answered: *how does language work as a symbolism?* and *why has language become the dominant symbolism for human cognition?* The first answer is structural; the second is, surprisingly, almost mechanical.

The structural answer:

> *"How are relations expressed in language? For the most part, they are not symbolized by other relations, as in pictures, but are named, just like substantives. We name two items, and place the name of a relation between; this means that the relation holds the two items together. 'Brutus killed Caesar' indicates that 'killing' holds between Brutus and Caesar."*

This is doing precise work and you should feel the structural difference from pictures. In a picture, the *arrangement of the picture-elements* shows the arrangement of the corresponding object-elements. The rabbit-drawing has its ears in the right place because the rabbit has its ears in that place. The relations are *shown* by being *enacted* in the picture's own structure. **In language, the relations are *named*.** "Brutus killed Caesar" doesn't enact killing through any arrangement of letters; it *names* the relation ("killed") and uses word-order and grammar to indicate its direction. *Pictures show; language names.* This is going to be the seed of the discursive/presentational distinction — pictures and language do their semantic work through structurally different operations.

Then the supremacy argument, and this is where it gets interesting because it's *not* what you'd expect:

> *"The trick of naming relations instead of illustrating them gives language a tremendous scope; one word can thus take care of a situation that would require a whole sheet of drawings to depict it."*

Language wins on *combinatorial scope*. A picture has to *enact* every relation it represents; a sentence can name a relation in one word and combine it with other relations in a complex sentence. *"Your chance of winning is one among a thousand of losing"* — try to draw that. The picture-symbolism is overwhelmed; the language-symbolism handles it easily. So far so good.

But then the mechanical-economic argument:

> *"Bertrand Russell has put it, 'It is of course largely a matter of convenience that we do not use words of other kinds (than vocal). There is the deaf-and-dumb language; a Frenchman's shrug of the shoulders is a word; in fact, any kind of externally perceptible bodily movement may become a word, if social usage so ordains. But the convention which has given the supremacy to speaking is one which has a good ground, since there is no other way of producing a number of perceptively different bodily movements so quickly or with so little muscular effort.'"*

Hold the surprise of this. The supremacy of speech is *not* a metaphysical claim about the privileged relation between sound and meaning. It is a *practical-physical* claim about the bandwidth and cost of vocal articulation as opposed to other bodily movements. We talk because we can produce many distinguishable noises *fast* and *cheap*. **Language is supreme because it is the most efficient combinatorial symbolism the human body can produce.** That's it. There's no deeper reason than that.

Then the second and subtler property:

> *"Another recommendation for words is that they have no value except as symbols (or signs); in themselves they are completely trivial. This is a greater advantage than philosophers of language generally realize. A symbol which interests us also as an object is distracting. It does not convey its meaning without obstruction. For instance, if the word 'plenty' were replaced by a succulent, ripe, real peach, few people could attend entirely to the mere concept of quite enough when confronted with such a symbol... Peaches are too good to act as words; we are too much interested in peaches themselves. But little noises are ideal conveyors of concepts, for they give us nothing but their meaning."*

This is *brilliant* and slightly scandalous. The supremacy of words is partly that *words are interesting only as words*. They have no intrinsic interest as objects. You can use them transparently because there's nothing to hold the attention except the meaning they carry. **The transparency of language is a function of the worthlessness of its physical substrate.** A peach-as-symbol would distract you with peachness; a sound-pattern as symbol gives you only itself, which is nothing, leaving you free to attend to the meaning.

This passage is doing more work than it looks like. It is the *negative* image of what art-symbolism is going to be in Chapter VIII. *Music is interesting as itself.* You cannot use music transparently the way you use language transparently. The substrate of music is *not* worthless; it carries semantic load *through its very objecthood*. So the supremacy of language as transparent communicator is *not* a supremacy of language as symbolism *period*. It is a supremacy of language for the kind of work that requires transparent combinatorial encoding. Other kinds of symbolic work — work that art and music do — *requires* the substrate to be interesting in itself, to carry meaning through its felt-quality and not despite it. **The chapter is quietly preparing you to feel that language's virtues are also its limits; what makes it good for some kinds of cognitive work makes it useless for others.**

#### Combinatorial productivity and the verb

The third virtue of words:

> *"But the greatest virtue of verbal symbols is, probably, their tremendous readiness to enter into combinations. There is practically no limit to the selections and arrangements we can make of them... This makes it possible for us to grasp whole groups of meanings at a time, and make a new, total, complex concept out of the separate connotations of rapidly passing words."*

Combinatorial productivity is the third virtue (after speed/cheapness and transparency). Words can be combined in indefinitely many ways, producing new complex meanings out of the connotations of the parts. **This is the structural foundation of generativity in language** — the property Chomsky later names as the discrete infinity of language, the capacity to produce indefinitely many sentences from finite means.

The verb-analysis at the end is technical but worth attending to:

> *"When a word is given an arbitrary denotation (which may be a simple thing, or a complex affair), it is simply a name... A name may be awkward or convenient, ugly or pretty, but in itself it is never true or false. But if it already has a connotation, then it cannot be given an arbitrary denotation, nor vice versa... The application of a word with its connotation is the equivalent of a statement: 'This is a such-and-such.'"*

This is where she folds the verb-as-assertion analysis from her own 1927 *Journal of Philosophy* paper into the picture. Verbs do double work: they *express a relation* (as part of the propositional pattern) and they *assert that the relation holds* (mark the proposition as something put forward as true). *"A verb has the force of 'assert φ()'."* The assertion-sign of formal logic, which Frege and Russell had been making explicit by writing the *Urteilstrich* in front of propositions, is *built into* natural-language verbs. **Every verb is a covert assertion-marker.** This is why "Brutus killed Caesar" can be true or false in a way that the bare conjunction "Brutus, killing, Caesar" cannot — the verb-form itself is doing the truth-claim work.

#### The closing summary

The chapter closes with a brisk recapitulation:

> *"There are many refinements of logic that give rise to special symbol-situations, to ambiguities and odd mathematical devices, and to the legion of distinctions which Charles Peirce was able to make. But the main lines of logical structure in all meaning-relations are those I have just discussed; the correlation of signs with their meanings by a selective mental process; the correlation of symbols with concepts and concepts with things, which gives rise to a 'short-cut' relation between names and things, known as denotation; and the assignment of elaborately patterned symbols to certain analogues in experience, the basis of all interpretation and thought. These are, essentially, the relationships we use in weaving the intricate web of meaning which is the real fabric of human life."*

Three operations, then: sign-correlation, symbol-denotation, and structural-pattern-correspondence. **The third one is the new arrival of this chapter.** The first two were spelled out earlier. The third — *the assignment of elaborately patterned symbols to certain analogues in experience* — is what pictures do, what propositions do, and what (it will turn out in Chapter IV) art and music do in their own way. The *form-sharing* between symbol and what-is-symbolized is a third basic operation, and it is the one that does the heaviest lifting in cognition: *the basis of all interpretation and thought.* **Sign-correlation gets you animal intelligence. Symbol-denotation gets you naming. Form-sharing gets you thought.**

The closing sentence — *"the intricate web of meaning which is the real fabric of human life"* — is one of those quiet declarations that does framing work. The web of meaning isn't a *layer added to* human life by sophisticated cognition; it *is* human life. Take it away and what remains is animal sign-using, not stripped-down rationality. **Human life is symbolic life all the way down**, and the point of the chapter has been to give that claim a precise structural articulation.

#### What just happened, structurally

Step back from the details for a moment. Chapter III has now done four things, in order:

It established that meaning is *function-not-quality*, a structural pattern viewable from any of its several terms. This dissolves the standing fights about "what meaning really is" and reframes them as misplaced choices among legitimate keying-perspectives.

It analyzed signs as *three-term* functions (subject, sign, object) with one-to-one correlation as the structural relation. Sign-meaning is what animal intelligence runs on; sign-error is misinterpretation, with practical disappointment as criterion.

It analyzed symbols as *four-term* functions (subject, symbol, conception, object), with the *conception* as the new and crucial term. The Helen Keller case witnessed the structural transition. The four-term structure makes possible all the operations that distinguish symbolic from signal cognition: mention, conception, memory, abstraction, generality. Three meanings of "meaning" — signification, denotation, connotation — emerged as different keying-views of the four-term structure.

It extended the analysis to *propositions and pictures*, where the symbol-pattern matches a state-of-affairs-pattern through formal analogy. The *concept* (universal form) was distinguished from the *conception* (private clothed instance), with abstractive form-recognition emerging as the ground of rationality. Language was identified as the supreme symbolism for *certain kinds of cognitive work*, with the supremacy explained by speed, transparency, and combinatorial productivity rather than by metaphysical privilege.

#### Where this leaves the book

The seam onto Chapter IV is now visible. If language is supreme for combinatorial transparent encoding *because* its substrate is worthless-as-object, then there is a structural niche language *cannot* occupy: the niche where the substrate's objecthood *carries* the meaning. Music's sound is not a transparent vehicle for separately specifiable meaning; the sound *is* the meaning in some non-paraphrasable way. Visual art's color and form are not transparent vehicles for content that could be said in words; the painting *is* the meaning. **There must be a different mode of symbolism that does this work, and it must operate by different structural principles than discursive language.** Chapter IV is going to formalize this as the *discursive vs. presentational* distinction. Discursive symbolism (language, mathematics, formal logic) operates by combining discrete elements according to rules, with transparent substrate, with paraphrasable content. Presentational symbolism (visual art, music, dance, ritual, gesture) operates by total simultaneous patterning, with the substrate carrying meaning *through* its objecthood, with the meaning non-paraphrasable. **Both are fully symbolic. Both are fully cognitive. Neither reduces to the other.**

This is the move the entire book has been building toward. Chapters II and III are foundation; Chapter IV is the move; Chapters V–VIII are the application of the move to myth, ritual, and music. **What you've been reading is the philosophical ground-clearing that lets her, in Chapter IV, say: art and music are not lower or higher or earlier than language; they are a *different kind* of symbolic operation, with their own logic, their own truth-conditions, their own form of cognitive achievement.** That move is what made her famous and is what gives the book its long half-life. It is also what gives you, for your *Bildung* project, the technical apparatus to argue that listening to Bach is not less cognitive than reading Whitehead and to say *what kind* of cognition each is.

#### Cross-resonances at depth

**Wittgenstein.** The *Tractatus* picture theory is named explicitly and treated as broadly correct. But Langer extends it past Wittgenstein in two directions. First, by treating the picture analogy *seriously as a picture analogy* and analyzing what makes pictures work — formal-analogy form-sharing — she reaches a conclusion Wittgenstein flirted with but didn't develop: that *form-sharing* is a general structural operation that propositions, pictures, diagrams, and (later) music all perform. Second, by distinguishing *concept* (form) from *conception* (clothed instance), she gives a structural account of how form-sharing produces shared cognition across different minds, which the *Tractatus*'s solipsistic monad cannot do.

**Frege.** *Concept* (Begriff) in Frege has a related but technically different sense — for Frege, a concept is a function from objects to truth-values, an *unsaturated* logical entity. Langer's *concept* is closer to a Platonic-Aristotelian form: the universal pattern that conceptions instantiate. They're both doing the work of public-formal universals against psychologistic privacy, but Langer's framing is more capacious because it applies to art and music as well as discursive thought.

**Cassirer.** The concept-vs-conception distinction maps cleanly onto Cassirer's distinction between symbolic *form* (the public articulation, mythical, linguistic, scientific) and symbolic *content* (the private experiential clothing). The form is what makes cognition shareable; the content is what makes it lived. Langer is giving a formal version of what Cassirer carried in a historical-cultural register.

**Aristotle.** The *universalium in re* position is structurally Aristotelian, and the rereading of "rational animal" as "abstractive-seeing animal" is doing serious work in the long Aristotelian tradition. Aquinas's *species intelligibilis* — the form abstracted by the active intellect from the phantasm — is approximately what Langer is calling the concept extracted by abstractive seeing from the conception. **The Thomistic intellectual tradition has been saying versions of this for eight centuries; Langer is bringing it into a 1942 analytic-philosophical idiom.** This is not coincidental: Whitehead's late metaphysics is openly indebted to scholastic philosophy, and Langer inherits some of that pedigree.

**Whitehead.** *Symbolism: Its Meaning and Effect* is the proximate source for the form-sharing analysis. Whitehead argues that symbolic reference fuses presentational immediacy (sensory data) with causal efficacy (felt inheritance), and the fusion happens *through formal analogy* — the present sensum *resembles in form* the dimly-felt past it represents. Langer is operationalizing this. Pictures share form with their objects; propositions share form with their states of affairs; conceptions share form (the concept) with each other across different minds. **The form-sharing operation is the same operation Whitehead names as symbolic reference, given a formal-logical articulation.**

**Hoffmeyer/Peirce.** The form-sharing operation maps onto Peirce's *iconic* sign-relation — a sign that signifies by formal resemblance to its object. Peirce saw this as one of three sign-types (icon, index, symbol). Langer is making *iconic relation* central to the structure of thought rather than peripheral, by showing that *abstractive form-recognition* is the basis of conceptual cognition. Hoffmeyer's biosemiotics has a substantial literature on iconic semiosis in biology — pattern-recognition, mimicry, formal-analogy across organic systems. **The form-sharing operation Langer is naming as the basis of human thought is, on the biosemiotic view, a refined human use of an iconic capacity that runs throughout life.** This is one of the places where Langer's analysis can be productively extended downward into biology while preserving the human-distinctiveness she insists on.
