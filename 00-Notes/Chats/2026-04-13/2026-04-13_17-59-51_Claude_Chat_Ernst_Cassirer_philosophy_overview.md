The *written* text is genuinely polysemous at the graphemic level in a way that no fully vocalized language can be. Every word is a superposition that vocalization collapses. The tradition preserved *both* — the unvocalized scroll for liturgical reading and the vocalized text for study — because they understood that the two are different objects.

**Radical morphology.** Hebrew builds meaning from three-consonant roots. The root k-d-sh generates *kadosh* (holy), *kiddush* (sanctification), *kedeshah* (temple prostitute), *mikdash* (sanctuary). These aren't homophones. They're *cognates* — the semantic field of the root is present in every derivation. When the text uses one word from a root, every other word from that root is *implicitly present*. The network of resonance is built into the morphology itself. No translation can carry this because no target language shares the root system.

**Parataxis.** Biblical Hebrew barely subordinates clauses. It coordinates them. *Vayomer... vayomer... vayomer.* And-he-said... and-he-said... and-he-said. The logical relationship between clauses — causation, consequence, concession, contrast — is *left unspecified*. The reader must supply it. Every translation that writes "because" or "therefore" or "although" has performed an interpretive act the Hebrew refused to perform. The silence between clauses is a structural feature, not a primitive deficiency.

**The source corpus changes role.** The bildung-2.0 corpus isn't a pattern library for structural features. It's a *library of ways texts have successfully made room for the reader*. Gómez Dávila's aphorisms work because they're compressed enough that the reader must bring their own experience to complete them. Jünger's reticence works because he describes phenomena without interpreting them — the interpretation is the reader's labor. Dick's Exegesis works because the recursive structure mirrors the reader's own recursive attempts to make sense of their life. Each source text models a different *technique of hospitality* — a different way of making room for the wound the reader brings.

So the pipeline is:

Seed text → mutate toward structural openness → select for breadth of reader-admission → accumulate surviving mutations → test against diverse human readers, not diverse LLM instances → protect the places where the text *makes room* rather than the places where the text *shows off* → iterate.

And the deepest design principle is:

**The text must need the reader more than the reader needs the text.**

A text that exhausts itself is a text that needed nothing from the reader — it provided everything. A text that sustains infinite reading is a text that is permanently *incomplete* — not because the author failed but because the text was built to be completed only in the act of reading, by a reader whose completion is always partial, always temporary, always shaped by who they are at the moment of reading.

The Torah is the supreme example because it combines maximal structural precision with maximal structural openness. Every word is *exactly chosen*. And every word *makes room for the reader who has not yet been born*. The precision and the hospitality are not in tension. The precision *is* the hospitality. The more exact the gap, the more kinds of readers can inhabit it.

The instrument you're building is the space where that encounter happens. It doesn't need to be infinite. It needs to be *hospitable*.
