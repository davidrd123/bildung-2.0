# Review of a Book That Does Not Exist

## *The Content of the Golden Age: Mathematics, Optics, and Medicine as Intellectual Achievement in Abbasid Baghdad*

**A review by [unnamed], of a work by [no one]**

---

The book under review does not exist. Its non-existence is the subject of this review.

What follows is an account of the book that should have been written — what it would contain, why it cannot be produced, and what its absence reveals about the civilization that studies the civilization that produced the achievements in question.

---

## I. What the Book Would Contain

The book would do for al-Khwarizmi's algebra, Ibn al-Haytham's optics, and Ibn Sina's medicine what Salomon Bochner's *The Role of Mathematics in the Rise of Science* did for Newton's *Principia* — engage the content as living intellectual achievement, tracing its internal logic, its debts to its sources, and the points where it exceeds those sources.

Bochner showed that the *Principia* is simultaneously backward-oriented in its mathematical idiom (Archimedes, Apollonius) and forward-oriented in its content, full of incipiencies that only become legible through later developments. The unwritten book would demonstrate the same for the Abbasid corpus: that al-Khwarizmi's *al-Jabr* fuses Babylonian, Indian, and Greek techniques into something none of them contained; that Ibn al-Haytham's *Kitab al-Manazir* is not a commentary on Ptolemy but a refutation grounded in experimental method; that the Arabic mathematicians working on conic sections, infinitesimal methods, and number theory between the 9th and 11th centuries were not preserving Greek mathematics but extending it into territory the Greeks never entered.

The book would work from original Arabic texts — not translations of translations — and would require its reader to follow the mathematics. It would treat the golden age thinkers as colleagues working on real problems, not as historical figures to be contextualized.

It would contain, at minimum:

**Part One: Translation as Research.** How the act of translating Euclid, Apollonius, Archimedes, and Diophantus into Arabic was itself a mathematical act — translators were often mathematicians, and their "translations" were redactions that restructured the material for new purposes. The relationship between Hunayn ibn Ishaq's philological method (collating manuscripts, identifying spurious texts, choosing between reader-oriented and text-oriented strategies) and the intellectual culture that made original work possible. How vocabulary-creation in Arabic mathematical terminology was itself a form of conceptualization.

**Part Two: Algebra as Invention.** Al-Khwarizmi's *al-Jabr wa'l-Muqabala* not as a "contribution to mathematics" (the condescending formulation) but as the founding of a new discipline. What it meant to systematize the solution of equations as a general method rather than as a collection of tricks. The geometric justifications and their relationship to Greek geometric algebra. The trajectory from al-Khwarizmi through Abu Kamil, al-Karaji, and al-Samaw'al to something recognizable as polynomial algebra — a development with no Greek precedent.

**Part Three: Optics as Experimental Science.** Ibn al-Haytham's overturning of the Greek emission theory of vision. His construction of the camera obscura as experimental apparatus. His synthesis of mathematical, physical, and physiological approaches to vision — a methodological integration that would not be attempted again until Kepler. The *Kitab al-Manazir* as arguably the first work to practice what we would now call hypothetico-deductive method.

**Part Four: Infinitesimal Mathematics.** The Banu Musa, Thabit ibn Qurra, Ibn al-Haytham, and others working on areas, volumes, and approximation methods that go beyond Archimedean techniques. The relationship between these methods and what would later become integral calculus — not as a "precursor" narrative but as an independent mathematical tradition with its own internal logic.

**Part Five: Medicine Beyond Galen.** Ibn Sina's *Canon* not as a summary of Greek medicine but as a systematic reconstruction that incorporated clinical observation, pharmacology, and organizational principles the Greeks lacked. Al-Razi's clinical case studies and his explicit willingness to contradict Galen based on his own observations. The hospital (*bimaristan*) as an institution of medical practice and training without Greek or Roman precedent.

**Part Six: The Cessation.** Why it stopped. Not the Mongol-invasion story (too simple) but the structural transformation: the narrowing of the madrasa system toward legal-theological specialization, the fragmentation of patronage, the loss of the institutional conditions that supported polymathic work. The relationship between the end of the golden age and the institutional conditions that currently prevent this book from being written.

---

## II. Why the Book Does Not Exist

The book requires its author to possess: fluent Classical Arabic; Greek sufficient to compare translations with originals; mathematics at the level of a working researcher; history of science training; philosophical sensibility adequate to the material.

This combination requires approximately fifteen years of preparation. The tenure clock is six years. The person who could write this book cannot be produced by any existing doctoral program, hired by any existing department, or funded by any existing grant structure.

The mathematics department will not tenure a scholar who publishes on 9th-century Arabic algebra. The history department will not tenure a scholar whose papers contain equations. The Near Eastern Studies department might house such a person but cannot evaluate the mathematical content. History of science is a small field with few positions and, since the Skinnerian turn, a methodological commitment to context over content that would make this book unprofessional by construction.

Roshdi Rashed, the Egyptian-French historian of mathematics, has produced much of the technical material that would underlie this book — critical editions of Arabic mathematical texts with modern mathematical commentary, published across five volumes and dozens of articles over fifty years. He has done so from a permanent research position at CNRS in Paris, publishing primarily in French and Arabic. The Anglophone academy has not absorbed his work. No student of his has replicated his configuration of skills, because the institutional niche that sheltered him — a permanent research directorship with no teaching load and no publication pressure — has been closing for decades.

The book does not exist because the system that studies the golden age is optimized to prevent the emergence of the person who could write it.

---

## III. What the Absence Reveals

The dominant Anglophone treatment of the Abbasid translation movement and the golden age it enabled follows a pattern established by Quentin Skinner's 1969 essay "Meaning and Understanding in the History of Ideas": understand past thought in its own terms, do not evaluate it by present standards, contextualize rather than engage. This methodological commitment, whatever its merits for political thought, produces a specific deformation when applied to mathematics and science: it makes it illegitimate to say "al-Khwarizmi invented algebra and here is why it works."

Dimitri Gutas's *Greek Thought, Arabic Culture* (1998) — the major Anglophone monograph on the translation movement — exemplifies the pattern. It is brilliant on the social, political, and ideological conditions that generated the translations. It says almost nothing about what was done with them intellectually. The algebra, the optics, the medicine become symptoms of Abbasid imperial ideology rather than achievements of minds working at full capacity on real problems. The book explains the context so thoroughly that the content evaporates.

This is not Gutas's failure. It is the method's failure. The contextualizing move allows a scholar to produce a complete account of the golden age without understanding a single proof, a single experiment, a single clinical observation that the golden age produced. It is a method of analysis that is structurally identical to discussing a symphony by describing the economics of the orchestra, the sociology of the audience, and the labor conditions of the musicians — without ever mentioning what the music sounds like.

The golden age thinkers themselves practiced the opposite method. Hunayn ibn Ishaq did not "contextualize" Galen. He corrected him. Ibn al-Haytham did not "understand Ptolemy in his own terms." He proved Ptolemy wrong. Al-Khwarizmi did not write a sensitive historical account of Babylonian and Indian mathematical traditions. He took what worked, fused it, and built something none of his sources contained.

The modern scholarly apparatus, in the name of respecting the golden age on its own terms, adopts a posture that is the exact opposite of what the golden age thinkers did. The thing that made them great was their willingness to appropriate, judge, and surpass. The thing the modern scholar does is refuse to appropriate, judge, or surpass — and calls this respect.

It is not respect. It is abdication dressed as humility. The civilization that produced the achievements was structured for whole knowing. The civilization that studies those achievements is structured against it. The missing book is the measure of the distance between them.

---

## IV. Coda

There is a tradition in Arabic literature of the *fihrist* — the catalog. Ibn al-Nadim, a 10th-century Baghdad bookseller, compiled the *Kitab al-Fihrist*, an index of approximately ten thousand books in Arabic on every subject from scripture to alchemy. He had seen most of them. He noted their lengths so that buyers would not be cheated by copyists selling shorter versions.

This review is a *fihrist* entry for a book that was never copied because it was never written. Its length is zero. Its price is the distance between what we can study and what we can understand. The copyist cannot cheat the buyer because there is nothing to sell.

Ibn al-Nadim's father was a bookseller. The son made a catalog for the shop. The catalog became an encyclopedia of civilization. The instrument exceeded the intention. Perhaps this review will exceed its own intention. Perhaps not.

The book remains unwritten. The mathematics remains valid. The gap between them is the subject.

# The Book of Optics (Second Copy)

### A note on the work of Dr. Ahmad Shawqi al-Riyadi

In 2019, the Cairene mathematician Ahmad Shawqi al-Riyadi reportedly completed a manuscript of some four hundred pages entitled *The Content of the Golden Age*, which proposed to do for al-Khwarizmi, Ibn al-Haytham, and Ibn Sina what Bochner had done for Newton: engage their work not as historical artifact but as living mathematics, living optics, living medicine. Al-Riyadi, who held dual appointments in mathematics at the Université Paris-Cité and in Arabic studies at the University of Mansoura — an arrangement that puzzled both faculties — had spent seventeen years preparing the work. He read Greek. He read Syriac. He could follow a proof.

The manuscript was never published. Al-Riyadi could not find a press. The mathematics editors noted the extensive Arabic. The Arabic studies editors noted the extensive mathematics. The history of science editors observed that the work failed to adequately situate its subjects within their sociopolitical context, and that its treatment of al-Khwarizmi's algebra as *algebra* — rather than as an expression of Abbasid court ideology — reflected a methodological naïveté incompatible with contemporary standards.

I have not read al-Riyadi's manuscript. I have read his description of it, in a letter to a colleague that circulated briefly in the winter of 2020 before the pandemic drew attention elsewhere. The letter is in Arabic. In it, al-Riyadi describes six chapters. I summarize them here from memory, which is to say, inaccurately.

The first chapter concerned Hunayn ibn Ishaq, whom al-Riyadi called "the last man who could be trusted with a text." Hunayn's method was to collect every available Greek manuscript of a work, collate them, and produce a composite from which to translate. Al-Riyadi noted that this method was identical to modern textual criticism — and that Hunayn practiced it nine centuries before Lachmann. He also noted that Hunayn, upon losing his entire library, reconstructed his catalog of Galen translations from memory. Al-Riyadi called this "the opposite of a bibliography: a library that exists only in the mind of its librarian." I am told the chapter contained a detailed comparison of Hunayn's Arabic Galen with the surviving Greek, demonstrating fourteen points where the Arabic preserves readings superior to any extant Greek manuscript.

The second chapter was on algebra. Al-Riyadi argued that al-Khwarizmi did not "invent" algebra — a verb that belongs to patent offices — but rather *disclosed* it: revealed a structure latent in the problems that Babylonians, Indians, and Greeks had been solving piecemeal for centuries. The geometric proofs in the *al-Jabr* were not illustrations but *demonstrations of existence* — they showed that the algebraic solutions correspond to something real. Al-Riyadi traced the line from al-Khwarizmi through al-Karaji to al-Samaw'al with the attention of a man who had worked through every proof himself, and he ended the chapter with the claim that the Arabic algebraists had effectively constructed polynomial algebra five centuries before Viète. He anticipated the objection that this was anachronistic. His response, which I paraphrase: "It is not anachronistic to say that a man who has climbed a mountain has climbed it, merely because the map was drawn later."

The third chapter, on optics, was apparently the longest. Al-Riyadi presented Ibn al-Haytham's refutation of Ptolemaic visual theory not as a "contribution to the history of optics" but as what it was: a man proving that his predecessors were wrong by designing experiments to demonstrate it. The camera obscura passages were reproduced in full. Al-Riyadi observed that Ibn al-Haytham's methodology — hypothesis, experimental test, mathematical analysis — was not "proto-scientific" but simply scientific, and that the prefix *proto-* is an instrument by which Western historiography denies coevalness to civilizations it has decided must be antecedent.

Of the fourth chapter I know almost nothing, except that it concerned infinitesimal methods and that al-Riyadi believed Thabit ibn Qurra's work on the areas bounded by conic sections to be, in his words, "more elegant than the corresponding pages of the *Principia*."

The fifth chapter, on medicine, argued that Ibn Sina's *Canon* was not a synthesis but a *system* — a distinction al-Riyadi considered fundamental. A synthesis assembles. A system generates. The *Canon* could produce diagnoses that Galen's writings could not, because it was organized by principles rather than by accumulated cases. Whether this claim is correct I am not qualified to judge, not being a physician or a philosopher of systems. Al-Riyadi was both.

The sixth chapter was titled "The Cessation," and it concerned the end of the golden age and the conditions that prevent the book you are now reading from existing. Al-Riyadi reportedly argued that the golden age ended not because of the Mongol invasion — "a favorite explanation of those who prefer catastrophe to structure" — but because the institutional conditions shifted: the madrasa system narrowed toward legal specialization, patronage fragmented, and the polymathic scholar ceased to be a type the civilization could produce. He then noted that the modern university has undergone an identical narrowing, and that his own manuscript was the proof. A civilization that cannot produce a book about the achievements of a prior civilization is in no position to claim superiority over it.

Al-Riyadi's letter ends with a line I have seen quoted in two different forms. In one version: "The book exists. It is merely unread." In the other: "The book does not exist. It has merely been written."

I do not know which version is authentic. I am not certain that al-Riyadi is real. A colleague in Cairo tells me the name is unfamiliar. A colleague in Paris says she may have attended a lecture by someone of that description in 2017, on the subject of Apollonius's lost eighth book, but cannot be sure. The manuscript has not surfaced.

It is possible that al-Riyadi is a fiction — that someone, perhaps the author of the letter itself, invented him as a device to describe a book that ought to exist by pretending that it does. This would be in keeping with a tradition. Ibn al-Nadim, the 10th-century Baghdad bookseller who cataloged ten thousand books in his *Fihrist*, is known to have included works he had not personally verified. Some entries in the *Fihrist* may refer to books that never existed, or to books that existed only as titles, awaiting authors. The boundary between a catalog entry and a commission is thin. To name a book is halfway to writing it. To review it is the other half.

The mathematics, in any case, is not in dispute. Al-Khwarizmi's algebra works. Ibn al-Haytham's optics are correct. The proofs do not require the existence of al-Riyadi, or of this review, or of the civilization that cannot produce either.

They require only a reader.

# A Survey of the Book

It is well known that the Arabs translated Aristotle. It is less well known that they improved him, though this fact can be verified by anyone who takes the trouble to read both, which is to say, by no one. The historian regards the translation as a cultural event; the philosopher regards it as a footnote; the mathematician, who alone could judge whether the improvement was genuine, regards the whole matter as someone else's business. The result is that a civilization's greatest intellectual achievements are perpetually described by people who do not understand them.

I owe the discovery of this situation to the conjunction of a mirror and an encyclopedia — or rather, to the conjunction of a grant application and a rejection letter, which in the academic world serve the same ontological function.

In 1987, a mathematician at the University of Alexandria named Farouk Wael Nassim submitted to a London press a manuscript of some two hundred pages on the optics of Ibn al-Haytham. Nassim, who was unusual in being able to read both the Arabic and the mathematics, had demonstrated that Ibn al-Haytham's proof that vision occurs by intromission — light entering the eye, rather than fire leaving it — employed a method of experimental verification that would not be formalized in Europe for another six centuries. He had also shown that the standard English translation of the relevant passage was wrong, having been produced by a scholar who understood the Arabic but not the optics. The press replied that the work, while intriguing, was not suitable for their History of Science series, as it did not adequately engage with recent scholarship on the social construction of optical knowledge in medieval Islamic courts.

Nassim, who had spent four years on the manuscript, is said to have observed that light is not socially constructed, though courts may be. The press did not respond.

I learned of Nassim from a footnote in a paper by R—, the French-Egyptian historian of mathematics who, for the last fifty years, has been patiently editing Arabic mathematical texts in a language — French — that the Anglophone world does not read, from an institution — the CNRS — that the Anglophone world does not understand, in a field — the actual content of Arabic mathematics — that the Anglophone world does not recognize as a field. R— mentions Nassim's manuscript in passing, in a parenthetical, in the third volume of a five-volume series that has been translated into Japanese but not, as of this writing, adequately reviewed in English. This is fitting. Ibn al-Haytham, too, was better known in Latin translation than in the original.

The curious thing about the Islamic Golden Age — the thing that would strike any thoughtful person as requiring explanation, if any thoughtful person were permitted by the relevant institutions to attempt one — is not that the Arabs translated the Greeks. Many civilizations have translated their predecessors. The curious thing is that they *surpassed* them, and that the manner of the surpassing has been made invisible by the very discipline charged with studying it.

Consider: al-Khwarizmi did not translate algebra from any prior language, because algebra did not exist in any prior language. The word is Arabic. The discipline is Arabic. The Greeks had no general method for solving equations; they had specific geometric tricks for specific geometric problems, and a profound allergy to the concept of number as an independent entity. Al-Khwarizmi took the practical equation-solving techniques of Babylonians and Indians, detached them from any particular problem, and asked: what is the general structure? The answer was algebra. This is not translation. It is — I dislike the word, but accuracy compels it — creation.

A modern history of science will describe this event in a chapter on "knowledge transfer in the Abbasid court," sandwiched between a discussion of the Barmakid patronage network and an analysis of the political uses of astrology. The algebra itself — the actual technique, the actual proof that it works, the actual reason it was new — will be absent. This is because the historian does not know algebra. He is not expected to. His discipline has declared that knowing the content of the things one studies is not only unnecessary but methodologically suspect, a kind of intellectual trespass called "presentism," which consists of the sin of understanding things.

I am aware that this characterization is unfair. I am also aware that it is accurate. These two facts are not, as the dialecticians would say, in contradiction; unfairness and accuracy cohabit frequently, particularly in reviews of non-existent books.

The book I am reviewing does not exist. It would be a volume of perhaps three hundred pages describing, in language accessible to an educated reader, what the major figures of the Islamic Golden Age actually *did* — not why they did it, not who paid for it, not what it meant for Abbasid legitimation strategies, but what they did: what problems they solved, what methods they invented, what errors they corrected, and where their solutions still obtain. Such a book would require its author to read Arabic, to know mathematics, and to be willing to say, in print, that a 9th-century Iraqi scholar produced work of permanent intellectual value. This last requirement is the most difficult, as it violates the convention, now several decades old, that intellectual value is a social construction and permanence is a myth.

The convention is not wrong. It is merely useless, in the same way that it is not wrong to observe that a bridge is a social construction — someone decided to build it, someone paid for it, someone embedded it in a political context — but this observation is useless to the engineer who must calculate whether it will bear weight. The bridge, like the algebra, either works or it does not. The context in which it was built is interesting. The question of whether it works is essential.

No one has written this book. The nearest approach is the work of R—, who has produced the critical editions on which it would depend, but who has not — perhaps wisely — attempted the synthesis. R— is ninety years old. He occupies a position that no longer exists in an institution that no longer creates such positions. His students, such as they are, work in a field that cannot hire them. The Anglophone world, which would be the natural market for such a synthesis, has spent the last forty years developing an elaborate theoretical framework for explaining why it need not be written.

I think sometimes of Ibn al-Nadim, the bookseller of Baghdad, who in 987 compiled a catalog of ten thousand books. He had seen most of them. He noted their lengths so that buyers would not be cheated by copyists who shortened them. Among the entries are titles for which no other record survives — books known only because a bookseller thought someone might want to buy them. It is possible that some of these books never existed; that Ibn al-Nadim, in the optimism of his trade, cataloged not only what had been written but what ought to have been written, on the reasonable theory that if a book ought to exist, a customer would eventually require it, and a copyist could be found.

The book I am reviewing is of this type. It ought to exist. A customer requires it. The copyist has not been found.

It is conventional to end a review with a recommendation. I recommend the book without reservation. I also recommend the reader learn Arabic, as the original is superior to any translation, including this one, which is a translation of nothing into English, a language in which — as Nassim may or may not have observed — light is not socially constructed, but the study of light occasionally is.
