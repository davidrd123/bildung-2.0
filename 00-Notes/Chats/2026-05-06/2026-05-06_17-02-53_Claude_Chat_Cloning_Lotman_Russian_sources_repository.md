## Part I, §1 — Три функции текста (Three Functions of Text)

Lotman opens by attacking the dominant Saussurean assumption that the text is just "packaging" — raw material from which the linguist extracts the language-structure and the everyday receiver extracts the message. On this view, communication "works well" when the message received is identical to the one sent, and the text itself adds nothing.

Lotman argues this picture only works for artificial, simplified languages with simplified communicators. Real natural-language communication never achieves identical codes between sender and receiver: codes are multidimensional hierarchies tied to memory, cultural tradition, pragmatics, and individual experience. By the "adequate transmission" criterion, all natural languages — and *a fortiori* poetic language — are working badly. So that criterion can't be the essence of language; it's just *one* of its functions. From this he derives three.

**1. Adequate transmission.** The "artificial language" pole. Sender and receiver share a single code K; symmetric encode/decode; back-translation recovers the original. Mathematics, formal logic. Wittgenstein's point that within logic nothing new can be said.

**2. Generation of new meaning (the creative function).** The opposite pole. Sender and receiver work with different but intersecting codes K₁ and K₂ — or with a whole space of codes — so translation becomes asymmetric and back-translation never yields the original. A poem translated and re-translated produces a third text, not the first. Even more dramatic between incommensurable systems (verbal → iconic, novel → film), where translation runs through culturally conventional equivalences. Here meaning is not the invariant residue but precisely what *changes* under transformation: the text gains semantic content through the operation. In this regime language and content are inseparable — the message is also a message *about* its code (Jakobson's poetic function). New artworks are sui generis texts in unknown languages that the audience has to reconstruct, which is why avant-garde works force authorial reflexivity into the structure itself.

Lotman places all real texts on an axis between these two poles, with the poles themselves being unreachable abstractions. He reads the Saussure–Jakobson disagreement through this lens: Saussure took function 1 as primary (hence his confidence in the arbitrariness of the sign, fitting the positivist, Zola-Goncourt 19th-century culture); Jakobson, formed by Khlebnikov and Russian Futurism, took the creative pole as primary, which is why he kept attacking Saussurean arbitrariness and recovering iconicity even in everyday speech — extending Potebnja's claim that the whole of language belongs to art.

**3. Memory.** The text is not only a generator of new meaning but a condenser of cultural memory. It preserves traces of its prior contexts. Without this, history would be impossible — past cultures reach us only in fragments, and a text without memory would just be a mosaic of disconnected scraps. For the receiver, every text is a metonymy of a reconstructed whole, a discrete sign of a non-discrete entity. The sum of contexts in which a text has been meaningful is "incorporated" into it; that semantic aura interacts with the cultural memory of the audience, and the text comes back to semiotic life. Hence the seed-like quality of texts: their internal under-determination is a *resource* for future dynamics, which is why stray fragments from foreign cultures can perturb a host culture rather than just sit in museums. Counterintuitively, culturally active texts *accumulate* information over time rather than losing it: *Hamlet* now means Shakespeare's text plus the memory of every interpretation and every historical event the text has been associated with. We may forget what Shakespeare's audience knew, but we cannot forget what we have learned since — and that adds new meanings to the text.

The three functions together set up the book's working definition of "text": not a transparent envelope but a meaning-generating device that simultaneously transmits, creates, and remembers.

### What the Stengers practice looks like

Reading at the register of the chapter-guides in the repo, not summary. Section-by-section but not always in order — following live pressure. Chunked when TTS demands it, never compressed for register. The reading identifies **hinges** (the page where the apparatus turns), **traps and resolutions** (Stengers's recurring rhythm — set up the obvious wrong reading, then dissolve it), and the **methodological moves** by name ("prohibits and incites," "formally distinct," "exhibits"). It tracks the **architecture** — where this chapter sits in the book's arc, what it's setting up, what earlier chapters were preparing for. It names the **interlocutors** the book is fighting with (Naturalists, phenomenology, the bifurcation tradition). It honors the **slow encounter** as itself the point — no primer, no shortcut.

There's also a layered practice: audio for orientation (ambient familiarity), close reading for the parts where the orientation has produced live interest. Two operations on the same text.

Section-by-section at full register. Identify hinges and methodological moves. Track Part I's architecture (the seven sections build a theory of text *as such*, before Part II opens onto semiosphere and Part III onto history). Name the interlocutors (Saussure, Jakobson, Benveniste, Wittgenstein, Potebnia, implicitly Bakhtin and the Formalists). Diagnostic openings — "where do you want to dwell." Honor the slow encounter; no compression of the register for mobile.

So the apparatus we're tracking is Lotman's own, and we don't have a Stengers-on-Lotman to lean on. I'll do some of that interpretive work directly, but I should be honest where I'm reaching.

**Translation as a layer.** You're reading Russian; I'm reading Russian. **Lotman's own rhythm is dialectical, not trap-and-resolution.** He sets up oppositions (artificial language vs. poetic language, Я–ОН vs. Я–Я, dictionary vs. text, discrete vs. continuous), pushes both poles to caricature, and then locates the real phenomenon as the *interaction* of the two. **Cultural-historical embedding matters more.** *Внутри мыслящих миров* is late Lotman (1990–92), written under glasnost / early post-Soviet conditions, by the founder of the Tartu-Moscow semiotic school. Worth noting where it's load-bearing.

**Architecture you can lean on.** Part I's seven sections move from *what is a text* (§1, three functions) → *the asymmetry of address* (§2, autocommunication) → *how meaning is generated* (§§3–4, rhetoric and iconic rhetoric) → *the text in motion / over time* (§5) → *the symbol* (§§6–7). The arc is: text as object → text as process → text as memory-bearing element. By §7 you should have a fully constructed Lotmanian "text" ready for Part II to embed it in the semiosphere.

## Part I, §1 — Три функции текста (Three Functions of Text)

### Where it sits

§1 is the foundation stone of Part I and, in a real sense, of the entire book. Its job is to dissolve one model of what a text is — text as packaging, the wrapper from which a message is extracted — and substitute another: text as multifunctional meaning-generating device. Everything that follows in Part I distributes across the three functions named here. §2 (autocommunication) develops the *address* dimension that the creative function requires. §§3–4 (rhetoric and iconic rhetoric) develop the *mechanism* of meaning-generation. §5 develops the *temporal* dimension implicit in the memory function. §§6–7 develop the symbol as the cultural element that condenses memory most densely. So when §1 names three functions, it is silently outlining the chapters of Part I. The reader who feels the architecture from the start has a very different experience than the reader who treats §1 as a free-standing essay.

The book's title also lands here. *Внутри мыслящих миров* — "inside thinking worlds." A text that merely transmits is not a thinking world. A text that *generates* meaning and *remembers* its prior contexts is. §1 is where the title gets its referent.

### The opening trap

Lotman opens with a careful diagnosis of the picture he means to dismantle. The picture is Saussure's, but more than that, it is what Saussure's picture *implies* once it filters out into ordinary common sense. The linguist's interest is in the structure of the language behind the speech; the everyday receiver's interest is in the content behind the text. *Both treat the text as packaging*. The text matters only as the means by which the buried valuable thing — language-structure or content — is delivered. The little diagram Lotman provides at lines 22–28 (Thought → Encoder → Text → Decoder → Thought) is not his diagram. It's the diagram he intends to break.

The first move against the picture is methodologically interesting. Lotman does not argue with Saussure directly. He summons Benveniste — a Saussurean — to do the work. The long Benveniste quotation is the wedge: thought has no independent existence prior to language. What we call "what we want to say" or "thought" or "intention" is *content of thought*, and it acquires form *only when uttered*, and only through being uttered. So the picture's premise — that there is a pre-textual message which the text packages — is denied from inside the Saussurean tradition itself. This is Lotman letting an internal critic break the picture, which is more rhetorically efficient than direct attack.

(The footnote at line 20 is worth a glance. Lotman tracks a quibble with the standard Soviet translation of Saussure's "norme," which had been rendered as "основание" — *foundation*. He insists the right rendering is closer to *norm* in the strong sense. This kind of philological care — fighting with a translation — is small in itself but it tells you what kind of writer Lotman is. He treats his predecessors' words as having weight that the Soviet apparatus had been free with.)

### The transcendental argument

Then comes the section's tightest argumentative moment. *If* perfect transmission is the criterion of a working semiotic system, *then* natural languages are working badly. Why? Because perfect transmission requires identical codes on both ends, and identical codes require, in effect, identical persons — same language *and* same memory *and* same cultural tradition *and* same individual experience. Real communication never achieves this. Therefore the picture's own criterion judges natural language as defective. But natural language is what we have, and it works. Therefore the criterion was wrong.

Structurally this is a transcendental-style move. From the impossibility of the supposed essence under realistic conditions, infer that the essence was misidentified. Lotman makes it explicit at lines 109–113: the artificial languages that *do* satisfy perfect-transmission are not models of language as such; they are models of *one function* of language, achieved by stripping away the others. Once you see this, the question reframes: not "what is the essence of language" but "what are the functions language performs, and how do they relate?"

### The plurality move

Three functions, asymmetrically related.

**Function 1 — adequate transmission.** Sender and receiver share a single code. Symmetric encoding/decoding; back-translation recovers the original. Mathematics, formal logic. Wittgenstein's note that within logic nothing genuinely new can be said. The artificial-language pole.

**Function 2 — generation of new meaning.** The opposite pole. Sender and receiver work with non-identical, intersecting codes K₁ and K₂; or, more realistically, with whole spaces of codes K₁, K₂, K₃ … Kₙ, each itself a hierarchical apparatus permitting many possible texts. Translation becomes asymmetric. Back-translation does not recover the original. The very fact that the same poem can be translated by many translators into many different texts, each a possible interpretation of the source, is the operational mark of this function. Even more visible across radically different sign-systems — verbal to iconic, novel to film — where translation runs through culturally conventional equivalences and the choice itself generates new content. Lotman's term for what this produces is *приращение смысла* (accretion of meaning, line 181) — meaning grows through the operation rather than being conserved.

In this regime, language and content cannot be cleanly separated. The message is also a message *about* its code. This is Jakobson's poetic function reaching into Lotman's vocabulary. The avant-garde work makes this explicit: every new artwork is a sui generis text in a language the audience does not yet share, and the audience must perform what Lotman calls *самообучение* (self-instruction, line 203) to learn the language *from* the text. The text becomes a lesson in its own language. This is why, paradoxically, the more individual a poetic language, the more authorial reflexivity it must include, because the artist must instruct readers in how to read while writing.

**Function 3 — memory.** The text is also a condenser of cultural memory. It preserves traces of the contexts in which it has previously meant. Without this, history is impossible: past cultures reach us only in fragments, and fragments without memory would be a mosaic of disconnected scraps rather than reconstructable wholes. For the receiver, every text is a *metonymy of a reconstructed whole, a discrete sign of a non-discrete entity* (lines 295–301) — and that line is among the most quotable in Lotman. The sum of contexts in which a text has been meaningful is "incorporated" into the text. That semantic aura interacts with the cultural memory of the audience, and the text comes back to semiotic life in each new encounter.

Two consequences Lotman draws are worth slowing on. First, the text as *seed*. Its internal under-determination is not a defect but a *resource* — what allows it to enter into productive relations with new contexts. Stray fragments of foreign cultures that fall on a host culture (lines 308–315 — the meteor-shower image) are not just curiosities; they perturb and provoke development *because* they bring memory with them. Second, the counterintuitive accumulation: culturally active texts do not lose information over time. They *gain* it. *Hamlet* now means Shakespeare's text plus the memory of every interpretation plus the memory of every historical event the text has been associated with. We may forget what Shakespeare's audience knew; we cannot forget what we have learned since. The text is not eroded by history — it is enriched by it.

### The Saussure–Jakobson re-read

Embedded in the function-2 discussion is one of the chapter's more striking methodological moves. Lotman frames the Saussure/Jakobson disagreement not as a philosophical disagreement to be adjudicated on its merits but as a *cultural-historical formation*. Saussure was formed by late-nineteenth-century positivist culture — Zola, the Goncourts, the faith that understanding is good and non-understanding is absolute evil — and from inside that formation, function 1 looked like the heart of language. Jakobson was formed by Russian Futurism — Khlebnikov, the avant-garde — and from inside *that* formation, function 2 looked primary, which is why he kept attacking Saussurean arbitrariness and recovering iconicity even in everyday speech.

What Lotman is doing here is *applying his own apparatus to the history of his own field*. Theories are themselves texts within semiospheres, shaped by the cultures that produce them. This is Lotman doing cultural semiotics on cultural semiotics, and it is a small but real signal of how the apparatus is meant to be used: turn it on everything, including yourself.

### The methodological moves to name

Three rhythms recur. The first is **productive asymmetry** — Lotman's signature dialectical move. Set up an opposition (artificial vs. poetic, transmission vs. creation, single code vs. code-space), refuse to choose, locate the actual phenomenon in the *interaction* of poles, and treat both poles as unreachable abstractions. Watch for this through the rest of Part I; it organizes §2's Я–Я / Я–ОН pairing, §5's discrete/continuous tension, §§6–7's symbol/non-symbol distinction.

The second is the **exhibitive example**. The Asyev couplet ("продажа овса и сена" / "убийство Отца и Сына") and the Pilnyak peasant struggling with "Коммутаторы, аккумуляторы" are not illustrations of a thesis already proved; they are exhibits of the move just named. Lotman's prose is example-rich in a particular way: examples *show* the apparatus working in cases the apparatus has to honor. (Compare Stengers's use of *exhibits* — testimony the theory has to fit, not premises from which the theory derives.)

The third is the **cultural-historical situation of theory itself**. The Saussure/Jakobson reading. This is the most Lotmanian methodological move and the one that distinguishes him from Western European structuralism. Theories are cultural texts; semiospheres include the theories that try to describe them.

### A few translation notes worth carrying

*Смыслопорождающее устройство* — translated "meaning-generating device" or "sense-generating mechanism." The Russian *устройство* is broader than English "device": it sits between *device*, *arrangement*, *contrivance*, and *organized structure*. A text is something *that has been arranged so as to generate meaning*. The English "device" loses some of the structural-architectural valence.

*Приращение смысла* — "accretion of meaning" or "increase of meaning." Technical term for what creative-function translation produces. Worth flagging because it appears throughout the book and is not just a rhetorical phrase.

*Самообучение* — "self-instruction" or "self-teaching." The audience teaches itself the language of an avant-garde text from the text. A specific Lotmanian concept; carries forward to the discussion of cultural innovation in Part II.

*Я–Я / Я–ОН* — the autocommunication / heterocommunication distinction. The doubled *я* in *Я–Я* is the same word in the same form, marking a structural identity that the addresser-addressee asymmetry then breaks. English "I–I" is correct but loses the visual-typographic emphasis on identity-with-asymmetry that the Russian carries. We will see this fully in §2.

### Where it lands forward

§1's three functions distribute across the rest of Part I almost exactly as I noted at the start: §2 develops Я–Я communication, which is the cultural-architectural form of function 2 (creation requires non-identical codes on both ends, and the most extreme case of that asymmetry is the self addressing itself across time). §§3–4 develop the rhetorical mechanisms by which function 2 actually generates meaning — what kinds of operations on texts produce *приращение смысла*. §5 develops the temporal dimension implicit in function 3, including how texts move between contexts and accumulate through that movement. §§6–7 develop the symbol as the most concentrated meaning-generating-and-memory-bearing element in cultural texts.

Forward into Part II: the translation-asymmetry argument scales up. If function 2 requires non-identical codes between individual sender and receiver, the *semiosphere* is a structured space of non-identical codes mutually translating, and the *boundary* (§II.2) is the thickening of code-asymmetry into a structurally productive zone. Part II is what happens when the §1 apparatus gets distributed across cultural space.

Forward into Part III: the *seed* metaphor for under-determined texts, and the accumulation-rather-than-erosion thesis, become the engine of historical change. If texts gain meaning rather than losing it, then cultural memory is not a passive archive but an active force, and history is what culture *does with* its accumulating texts.

## Part I, §2 — Автокоммуникация: «Я» и «Другой» как адресаты (Chunk 1 of 2)

*Subtitle: О двух моделях коммуникации в системе культуры — On two models of communication in the system of culture.*

### Where it sits

§1 ended by naming three functions and gestured at a problem it didn't solve: function 2 (creative meaning-generation) requires non-identical codes between addresser and addressee, but the Saussurean/Jakobsonian communication model assumes a shared code by definition. So either function 2 is exceptional and parasitic on function 1, or the underlying communication model is wrong. §2 takes the second horn. It argues that culture in fact runs on *two structurally different communication channels*, not one, and that the second — autocommunication, the Я–Я channel — is what makes meaning-generation possible at all. The Я–Я channel is also where the *self* becomes a self. So §2 is doing two things at once: completing §1's account of meaning-generation, and laying the foundation for a Lotmanian account of personhood.

The chapter is also where Lotman's apparatus first turns explicitly *cultural-typological*. Once you have two communication channels, you can ask which channel different cultures, genres, and historical moments privilege. By the end of §2 the apparatus has scaled up from text-mechanics to a typology of cultures — message-oriented vs. autocommunication-oriented — and Lotman has staked out a diagnostic position about modern European culture's pathologies. (That move belongs to chunk 2.)

### The opening problem

Lotman starts by recovering Jakobson's standard communication model — addresser, message, addressee, contact, code, context — and immediately problematizing it. Within culture, communication runs on at least two channels structured differently from each other. The familiar one is the Я–ОН channel: I have information; you don't; I transmit it to you. The unfamiliar one — the one our culture's habits have backgrounded — is the Я–Я channel: I send a message to myself, to someone for whom the message is already known.

This looks paradoxical. If the message is already known, what is being communicated? Lotman's first move is to set aside the easy answer: it's not just memory. The *mnemonic* case (writing things down to remember later) is real but not what interests him. The interesting case is when transmission to oneself produces something that wasn't there before — when sending a message to yourself *changes you*. This happens often. It happens in diary entries written not to record but to clarify an inner state that wasn't clear until written. It happens in prayer, in meditation, in re-reading a familiar text. It is, Lotman will claim, central to how cultural personhood is constituted.

How can a message to oneself be non-redundant?

### The structural account

The answer is in a small but load-bearing diagram.

In Я–ОН: variables are the framing elements (addressee changes); constants are the message and the code. Information moves through *space*. The carrier changes; the content does not.

In Я–Я: the carrier stays constant (it's the same self); the *message* gets reformulated. This happens because a *second* code is introduced, and the original message is recoded in terms of that second code's structure, acquiring features of a new message. So the schema is:

```
context → context-shift
message 1 → message 2
code 1 code 2
```

Two codes operating on one carrier across a context-shift. The result is not the same information arriving at a new place; it is *qualitative transformation* of the information, which produces a transformation of the carrier — of the self.

Here is where Lotman makes a claim that I want to pause on, because it is genuinely the philosophical heart of §2 and one of the most consequential moves in the book:

> *the essence of personality can be treated as an individual set of socially significant codes, and this set in the process of the communicative act changes.*

(Сущность личности можно трактовать как индивидуальный набор социально значимых кодов, а набор этот здесь, в процессе коммуникационного акта, меняется.)

This is a *constitutive* claim, not a descriptive one. Lotman is not saying "we use codes" or "selves are influenced by codes." He is saying that what a self *is*, ontologically, is a particular configuration of socially significant codes. Add a second code in autocommunication and the self literally re-configures. This is why autocommunication is non-redundant: because the self that sends and the self that receives are not the same self; the act of sending *constitutes* a different receiver.

This claim has Bakhtinian resonances (the dialogic self) and Vygotskian roots (the social origin of the higher mental functions), but it is also distinctively Lotmanian in that the *codes* are taking the place that "voices" or "psychological tools" take in those traditions. The self is a semiotic configuration; semiotic operations on the self are operations on the self's being.

### The Vygotsky anchor

A few pages in, Lotman cites Vygotsky on *внутренняя речь* — inner speech — as having already identified the structural features of autocommunication. Vygotsky's claim is that inner speech is not external speech with the volume turned down. It is functionally and structurally distinct from external speech, and it develops through *progressive structural divergence* from outer speech (outer → egocentric → inner), not through fading vocalization.

This citation is doing institutional work. Lotman is anchoring the autocommunication thesis to a recognized Soviet psychological tradition. Vygotsky was rehabilitated under Khrushchev and became one of the few Soviet thinkers whose concepts could be deployed without ideological cover; citing him gives Lotman a legitimate path into territory (interior life, the structure of the self) that more orthodox Soviet linguistics would have flagged as idealist. The citation is also intellectually right — Vygotskian inner speech really is the psychological phenomenon Lotman is structurally describing — but the institutional reading matters too. Lotman is showing his readers that this work has Soviet bona fides.

### The phenomenology of external rhythmic codes

The chapter then walks through what triggers autocommunication. The answer is striking: it requires *intrusion from outside*. Autocommunication is not immanent. It is provoked by the encounter with a non-semantic, syntagmatically organized external code that perturbs the inner monologue and forces it to recode itself.

The exhibits are exquisite, and they're worth slowing on because Lotman is doing his characteristic move — letting concrete examples *exhibit* the structural claim he has just made.

**Tyutchev's "Сон на море" (Dream at Sea).** Lotman reads the poem as a record of psychological self-observation. Two states: silent dream and the regular roar of the storm. The storm is metrically marked — anapests breaking into the prevailing amphibrachs. The dream-world is *волшебно-немой* (magically silent), populated by silent crowds. The storm's regular thunder, an external rhythmic code without semantic content, is precisely what *liberates* the imagination's flight. The non-semantic rhythm provides the syntagmatic structure that lets the inner monologue recode itself into something new. (Notice how Lotman uses metrical analysis here — the anapestic breaks are the *exhibit* — not as decorative scholarship but as evidence that Tyutchev had registered the autocommunicative phenomenon at the level of versification.)

**Onegin chapter 8.** Three external rhythmic codes operating simultaneously: the printed text Onegin is *not* reading (the book is mechanical, not semantic — "his eyes read but his thoughts were far away"), the rhythmic flicker of the fire he stares at without seeing, and the tune he hums (Benedetta or Idol mio) without noticing. None of these codes have semantic relation to his thoughts. All three are rhythmic, syntagmatic, semantically empty. And all three are necessary for him to read with "spiritual eyes" the *other lines* of his inner monologue. The intrusion of external rhythm organizes and stimulates inner speech. (Pushkin, Lotman is implying, had exact phenomenological knowledge of this; the apparently incidental scene-setting is Pushkin theorizing autocommunication through his hero's consciousness.)

**The Japanese stone garden (枯山水, *karesansui*).** A small space strewn with gravel, with stones placed in complex mathematical rhythm. Contemplating the stones produces a configured state conducive to introspection. The stones don't *mean* anything; they are organized syntagmatically without semantics, which is exactly what lets them function as the second code that perturbs the inner monologue.

The general principle Lotman extracts: any synthetically clear, syntagmatically organized but semantically empty external structure can serve as the second code in autocommunication. Music, ornament, architectural rhythm, train-wheel clatter, ship's rocking, horse gait — the literary and ethnographic record is full of attestations. (Lotman cites a specific Soviet structural-typological tradition here — Knorozov on information vs. fascination — which is an internal Soviet-semiotic reference but signals he sees this phenomenon as systematic, not anecdotal.)

What makes this section conceptually elegant is what's *not* the trigger. The trigger is not content, not meaning, not communication in the ordinary sense. It is *form without content* — a structure clear enough to organize but empty enough not to compete with the message it perturbs. This is why poetic rhythm works the way it works; this is why ritual repetition produces interior transformation; this is why staring at a wallpaper pattern can reorganize one's thinking. Autocommunication needs *empty form* as its catalyst.

Excavated theoretical foundations underlying Lotman's semiotic modeling approach

Good question — this is the right thing to be doing if you want the rest of the book to land properly. Let me lay out what's underneath the model-building.

### What kind of theory this is

Lotman is doing **typological-comparative semiotic modeling**. That phrase has weight — it's not a synonym for "theorizing." It means a specific methodological tradition with specific commitments.

He is *not* doing what an analytic philosopher does (deriving from first principles, defending claims one at a time, looking for necessary and sufficient conditions). He is *not* doing what a phenomenologist does (returning to direct experience, describing what shows itself). He is *not* doing what a scientist does in the strict sense (formulating hypotheses, testing predictions). He is doing something closer to what Roman Jakobson did with phonological typology, what Lévi-Strauss did with kinship and myth, and what Max Weber did with *ideal types* in sociology — building **structural taxonomies** of cultural phenomena that organize observations, reveal previously unseen connections, and generate productive questions.

The key feature of this method: the models are not tested by prediction but by *productivity*. A model is good if it organizes the phenomena well, makes new things visible, and produces new lines of inquiry. It's a coherentist-pragmatist epistemology, not a foundationalist one.

### What he is building from

Five inheritances are doing the heavy lifting, all of them visible in §1 and §2 already.

**Information theory and cybernetics.** This is the least visible but most foundational. Shannon's mathematical theory of communication, Wiener's cybernetics, and von Neumann's work were absorbed into Soviet science in the late 1950s and early 1960s after a period of ideological resistance. Kolmogorov was central — Lotman cites him in §1 (the "you can't write poetry in artificial languages" result). The vocabulary of *code*, *message*, *channel*, *signal*, *noise*, *redundancy*, *information*, *encoding*, *decoding* comes from here. When Lotman draws his communication diagrams, he is drawing engineering diagrams. The entire Я–ОН / Я–Я apparatus is a cybernetic-communication-theoretic apparatus extended to culture. This matters because it tells you what register the models are operating in: structural-functional, not metaphysical. Lotman is not telling you what the self *is*; he is telling you how to *model* the self for purposes of analyzing communication.

**Structural linguistics — Saussure, Hjelmslev, Jakobson, Benveniste.** The basic distinction between language and speech, code and message, paradigm and syntagm, expression and content. Lotman extends these from language to culture wholesale. This is the structural-linguistic backbone of the apparatus. He fights with Saussure where Saussure's commitments don't extend cleanly, but the conceptual furniture is Saussurean.

**Russian Formalism and Russian Soviet poetics.** Tynjanov, Eikhenbaum, Shklovsky, the early Jakobson. The literary-theoretical apparatus of *function*, *system*, *defamiliarization*, *literary fact*, *evolution of forms*. Lotman trained in this tradition and his early monographs are direct extensions of it. This is where the literary-analytic muscle comes from — the ability to read Tyutchev's anapests as evidence for a structural claim is Formalist technique applied to a new theoretical question.

**Soviet cultural-historical psychology — Vygotsky, Luria, the activity theorists.** Inner speech, the social origin of higher mental functions, semiotic mediation, *psychological tools*. The claim that the self is constituted through internalized cultural-semiotic processes comes from this tradition and licenses Lotman's "self = set of codes" move. The Vygotsky citation in §2 is anchoring; he could have made the same argument without it but the institutional and intellectual gain from anchoring is real.

**Lévi-Straussian structural anthropology.** Explicit in §2 (the "music of myth" reference) and pervasive throughout the book. The structural analysis of myth, ritual, and kinship as *sign-systems* is Lévi-Strauss's contribution, and Lotman's extension of his apparatus to folklore and ritual is downstream. (One difference: Lévi-Strauss tends toward *synchronic* structural analysis; Lotman is more interested in *dynamics*, in how cultural systems change over time. By Part III this divergence will matter.)

There are also implicit inheritances Lotman doesn't always name: **Bakhtin** (dialogism, the addressee-as-constitutive, the polyphonic text — the autocommunication model has Bakhtinian DNA even when it doesn't cite him), the **Russian symbolist tradition** that thought hard about the symbol before the formalists arrived (Bely, Ivanov, Florensky), and **early-twentieth-century philology and folklore studies** (Propp's morphology of the folktale is in the background of the folklore discussion in §2).

### The Tartu–Moscow specific synthesis

Lotman is operating inside a research program with a name: the **Tartu–Moscow semiotic school**, founded in the early 1960s, which had its own foundational concept that you should know about because it organizes everything Lotman writes. The concept is **secondary modeling systems** (вторичные моделирующие системы).

The idea: natural language is the *primary* modeling system — the basic semiotic apparatus by which human cultures represent the world. Literature, art, ritual, mythology, religion, etc. are *secondary* modeling systems — sign-systems *built on top of* natural language and using its structures as their foundation. A poem models the world *through* language; a ritual models it *through* gesture and word; a myth models it *through* narrative built on language. Each secondary modeling system has its own specific structural features, but they all share a dependence on natural language as their underlying primary system.

This is the school's foundational thesis, and Lotman is the school's leading theorist. When he extends linguistic and communicative models to culture, he is doing it within the framework of "culture is a system of secondary modeling systems built on natural language." Function 2 from §1 (creative meaning-generation) is what secondary modeling systems do with the syntagmatic resources of natural language. Autocommunication from §2 is one of the structural mechanisms by which secondary modeling systems constitute selves.

The "law" Lotman states near the end of §2 — that in any culture-forming semiotic pair, one member must be natural language or include natural language — is the secondary-modeling-systems thesis stated as a constraint on cultural typology.

### What he treats as primitive

The conceptual primitives Lotman takes as given (does not derive, does not defend, just uses):

- **Text** — a bounded, structured semiotic object with internal organization. Not just a sequence of words; can be a ritual, a building, a painting, a city, a culture.

- **Code** — a system of rules and conventions enabling encoding and decoding; hierarchically organized; never reducible to a single dimension.

- **Message** — what is transmitted between addresser and addressee through a code via a channel.

- **Channel** — the medium of transmission; the structural-physical pathway.

- **Sign** — Saussurean signifier-signified pair, broadly construed.

- **Addresser / Addressee** — the structural positions in a communication act, not necessarily distinct persons.

These are taken from communication theory and structural linguistics and not interrogated. If you want to question Lotman from below — to challenge the foundations rather than work within them — these primitives are where the work has to happen.

### Where the constitutive force comes from

Most of what makes Lotman *Lotman* and not just a careful applier of received structuralism is in the moves he makes *beyond* his inheritance. Three are visible already:

**Productive asymmetry as a methodological principle.** Lotman habitually sets up oppositions, refuses to choose, and locates real phenomena in the *interaction* of poles — both poles being unreachable abstractions. This is dialectical without being Hegelian. It treats real cultural phenomena as always *between* structurally pure types, with the position-on-the-axis being the analytic work to do. This move is everywhere in the book and you should expect it.

**The constitutive claim about selves.** The "personality is an individual set of socially significant codes" claim from §2 is more philosophically loaded than anything in his inheritance. Vygotsky said selves are constituted through internalized cultural processes; Bakhtin said selves are constituted through dialogue; Lotman says selves are *configurations of codes*. This is a much more semiotic-structural picture than either source provides, and it's one of the places where Lotman is genuinely doing original metaphysical work, even though he wouldn't call it that.

**Cultural typology as a research program.** The move from individual semiotic mechanics to typology of cultures is Lotman's own. Saussure, Jakobson, Vygotsky, Bakhtin, Lévi-Strauss — none of them quite did this in this way. Comparing cultures by their dominant communication channels, diagnosing modern European culture as message-dominant with attendant pathologies, is a Lotmanian extension. This is where the apparatus does cultural-political work.

### Where to be cautious

Three honest caveats about the basis.

**The "set of codes" account of personhood is doing a lot of work it doesn't quite earn.** It works well as an analytic move within the autocommunication model — for explaining why Я–Я communication is non-redundant. It works less well as a general account of personhood. Real selves have embodiment, affect, history, relations to others that aren't reducible to code-configurations. Lotman is not naive about this, but the model invites a flattening that the reader should resist. Treat it as a *useful frame for analyzing cultural communication* rather than as *what selves really are*.

**The information-theoretic vocabulary fits some phenomena better than others.** When Lotman is analyzing how a poem produces meaning, code/message/channel work well. When he is analyzing ritual, embodied practice, or non-discursive cultural phenomena, the vocabulary strains. He extends it gracefully, but watch for places where the apparatus seems to be over-organizing the phenomenon.

**The cultural typology is interpretive, not predictive.** When Lotman says modern European culture is message-oriented and produces passive consumers, this is a diagnostic claim about a complex historical formation. It's insightful and probably right in important ways, but it doesn't have the kind of warrant a scientific generalization has. Hold these claims as productive characterizations to think with, not as established facts.

### What this means for reading forward

Knowing the basis lets you read more lightly. When Lotman says "personality is a set of codes," you don't have to defend or attack the claim metaphysically; you can ask, "what does the model do once we accept this stipulation?" When he draws communication diagrams, you can read them as cybernetic-engineering schematics, not as pictures of reality. When he scales up from individual semiosis to cultural typology, you can ask whether the move is illuminating without asking whether it's necessary.

The right reading posture is: he's building structural-conceptual tools for analyzing cultural communication. The tools are tested by what they let you see. When they sharpen your perception of a literary text, a historical formation, or a personal experience, they're working. When they over-organize, they're at their limit.

Contrasted Peirce's foundational deduction with Lotman's constructive typology

Yes — and the contrast is sharp enough to be illuminating. Both are systematic; the systematicity is of fundamentally different kinds, and the difference tracks what each thinker was trying to do.

### Peirce: foundationalist-deductive-derivational

Peirce's 1867 paper is a *derivation from a single premise*. The premise: conceptions exist to reduce the manifold of sense to unity, and a conception is valid if and only if it is *necessary* for that reduction. Once you grant that, everything else is forced by an iterative test — *prescision* — which asks what can be thought without what. If A presupposes B but not vice versa, B is more fundamental. Apply prescision systematically and you derive five categories (Being, Quality, Relation, Representation, Substance), with the three middle ones — Quality, Relation, Representation — becoming the seeds of the mature triadic categories Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness.

The key feature: the categories are not *stipulated*; they are *discovered*. Peirce is claiming that any sign-process whatsoever, anywhere, must instantiate exactly these categorial structures, because anything less leaves something needed for unification missing. The derivation gives the apparatus modal force — it tells you what *must* be the case, not just what can usefully be modeled. And because the whole mature edifice (semiotic, phaneroscopy, the existential graphs, pragmatism, the cosmology) rests on this derivation, the 1867 paper functions as an *architectural keystone*. Pull on it and the whole arch shifts; secure it and the whole arch stands.

The method is *vertical*. Peirce builds *up* from one foundational move, each level deriving from the level beneath. You can name the steps: premise → validity criterion → prescision → categorial derivation → triadic structure → sign trichotomies → logic → pragmatism → metaphysics. Each step is rule-bound. The discipline of reading Peirce is reconstructing the rules and walking the derivation.

### Lotman: constructivist-typological-comparative

Lotman is doing something categorically different. He is not deriving anything from first principles. He is *receiving* a set of conceptual primitives from inherited traditions — code, message, channel, sign, text, addresser/addressee from communication theory and structural linguistics; the secondary-modeling-systems framework from the Tartu-Moscow school; literary phenomena from Russian Formalist analytic technique; psychological constructs from Vygotsky; structural-anthropological method from Lévi-Strauss — and then *building models* with those primitives that organize cultural phenomena into productive taxonomies.

The key feature: the models are *stipulative*. The Я–Я / Я–ОН distinction is not forced by anything; Lotman *proposes* it as a useful structural distinction and validates it by showing what it lets you see. The "personality is a set of socially significant codes" claim is not derived from a deeper premise; it is offered as a productive model for analyzing autocommunication. The three functions of text in §1 are not necessities of any sign-process; they are functions Lotman has identified by attending to what natural languages and cultural texts actually do.

This makes the apparatus *coherentist-pragmatist* rather than foundationalist. A model is good if it organizes the phenomena, makes connections previously invisible, and generates productive questions. The test is *productivity*, not necessity. If a different vocabulary or different distinctions worked better, they could replace these.

The method is *lateral and reticulated*. Lotman builds *outward* from a center of received apparatus, extending in many directions at once and weaving the extensions together. There's no single move from which the rest derives. Autocommunication doesn't ground the symbol; the symbol doesn't ground the semiosphere; the semiosphere doesn't ground the cultural typology. They coordinate. Each model illuminates a different region of the cultural field, and they connect through shared primitives rather than through derivation.

The discipline of reading Lotman is therefore very different from reading Peirce. You don't reconstruct a derivation. You build up an *inventory* of his models and learn to deploy them appropriately to phenomena. You ask: which model is the right one for this case? what does this case look like from the autocommunication framework versus the symbol framework? where do the models cooperate and where do they pull apart?

### The deeper structural contrast

Five differences worth naming.

**Necessity vs. productivity.** Peirce's categories are necessary; Lotman's models are productive. Peirce can say "any sign-process *must* have an interpretant"; Lotman can say "treating culture as oscillating between Я–Я and Я–ОН *illuminates* a great deal." Different modal force.

**Triadic vs. binary-with-gradient.** Peirce's deep commitment is irreducible triadicity — every sign relation has three places that cannot be reduced to two-place relations. Lotman works with *binary oppositions complicated by interaction* — Я–Я vs. Я–ОН, transmission vs. creation, discrete vs. continuous, then locating real phenomena in the productive interaction of poles. Peirce's structural commitment is *three*; Lotman's is *two-and-the-space-between*.

**Discovery vs. construction.** Peirce thinks the categories are discovered features of any possible sign-process; he is uncovering structure that was always there. Lotman thinks the models are tools constructed for analysis; better tools could replace them. Peirce is closer to Kantian transcendentalism; Lotman is closer to Weberian ideal-typology and Vygotskian psychological-tool theory.

**Architectonic vs. taxonomic.** Peirce's mature system is architectonic — phaneroscopy generates semiotic generates logic generates pragmatism, with each level depending on the level below. Lotman's apparatus is taxonomic — distinct typologies (of texts, of communication channels, of cultures, of symbols) that coordinate but don't ground each other.

**Phenomenological inspection vs. theoretical reception.** Mature Peirce, in the 1903 lectures, derives the categories from inspection of the *phaneron* — what shows itself in immediate experience. Lotman receives his categories from communication theory, structural linguistics, and Soviet psychology, and extends them. Peirce thinks there's a fact of the matter about categorial structure; Lotman is more pragmatist about which theoretical vocabulary is doing useful work.

### What it means practically for reading

When you're reading Peirce, the right move is *reconstruction*: walk the derivation, name the rules, see how each step follows. The pass structure works (skeleton → vocabulary → derivation → forward arrows) because the text is structured by derivation. There's a single argument to track and the comprehension test is whether you can rebuild the argument from the premises.

When you're reading Lotman, the right move is *inventory plus deployment*: build up a catalog of his models and develop the skill of recognizing which model is the right one for which phenomenon. There isn't a single argument to track, because the apparatus doesn't have a single derivational structure. The comprehension test is whether you can apply the right model to a new case.

The Peirce-style passes work less well for Lotman because the second pass — vocabulary — *is* most of the work. Once you have the autocommunication model in hand, there isn't a derivation to walk. Once you have the secondary-modeling-systems thesis, the rest of the apparatus follows by application, not by deduction. So the equivalent of Peirce's pass 3 (derivation) is, for Lotman, *something more like extension and case-application*: take the model you've grasped and watch it deploy across the next sections.

### What each is good for

The two apparatus answer different questions.

Peirce gives you what you need when you want *necessity* — when you want to know what categorial structure any sign-process *must* have, or what distinctions are forced by the demands of meaningful representation. The icon/index/symbol distinction is necessary in the strong sense; any sign relating to its object does so by likeness, indication, or convention, with no fourth option. This is logical-categorial work.

Lotman gives you what you need when you want *cultural-historical specificity* — when you want to compare actual historical formations, diagnose what makes a particular culture's communication patterns produce its specific pathologies, map transitions between cultural states. The Я–Я / Я–ОН distinction is not necessary in any logical sense; it's a productive structural taxonomy for analyzing what actually happens in human cultures. This is comparative-anthropological work.

They are not competing answers to the same question. They are different tools for different questions. The deepest convergence is that both are *anti-reductionist about meaning*, both reject the picture of language as transparent transmission, both insist that signification is irreducibly relational rather than substantial. But the warrants are different and the methods are different and reading them well means honoring those differences rather than blurring them.

### The honest comparison

Peirce extends because his apparatus has a *formal-mathematical core*. His triadic logic, his work on relations, the existential graphs — these are not just philosophical commitments but technical structures that translate into category theory, type theory, and biosemiotics with real fidelity. Brian Cantwell Smith, the categorical-logic crowd, the Hoffmeyer/Kull/Deely line in biosemiotics — they're not borrowing metaphors from Peirce; they're *implementing* Peircean structures in their respective formal vocabularies. The 1867 keystone supports an arch that genuinely reaches into modern technical work.

Whitehead extends because his apparatus is a *speculative metaphysics with mathematical-physical seriousness*. The actual occasion, prehension, the ontological principle — these articulate at a level that can take pressure from physics, ecology, and contemporary natural science. Stengers's contribution is showing how to use the metaphysics methodologically (the trap-and-resolution rhythm, the ecology of practices), but the extension-power is in the metaphysics itself.

Lotman doesn't have either of those things. There is no formal-mathematical core to Lotman's apparatus. There is no speculative metaphysics articulating at the level of physics. The apparatus is *cultural-structural typology* with humanistic-interpretive warrant. If you ask "what can I build on Lotman the way category theorists built on Peirce?" — the honest answer is *not the same kind of thing*. The materials aren't there.

This is not a flaw. It tells you what kind of thinker Lotman is and how to use him. The mistake would be trying to make him do what Peirce or Whitehead does and finding him deficient. The work is to recognize what he does that *they* can't, and use him for that.

### What Lotman gives you that Peirce-Whitehead don't

Peirce can tell you the categorial necessities of any sign-process. Whitehead can tell you the metaphysics of becoming. Neither can tell you, in any specific way, *how a particular culture's communication patterns produce its specific pathologies*, or *how cultural memory accumulates rather than degrades*, or *what the structural difference is between a culture organized around message-transmission and one organized around autocommunication*, or *how a boundary between semiotic systems thickens into a productive zone of translation*. These are not questions the Peirce-Whitehead apparatus is built to answer. They're questions about *cultural-historical specificity*, and Lotman is genuinely the strongest analyst of them in twentieth-century theory.

Specifically, the apparatus he gives you that nothing else in the contemporary canon quite gives:

Peirce can tell you what signs *are*; he doesn't tell you how cultural sign-systems organize themselves into structured fields with dynamics. The semiosphere is Lotman's, and its closest cousin is biosemiotics through Kalevi Kull (more on this below).

**Autocommunication as a constitutive cultural mechanism.** The Я–Я / Я–ОН distinction, the structural account of how the self is reconfigured through autocommunicative acts, the typology of message-vs.-autocommunication-oriented cultures — none of this is reachable from Peircean or Whiteheadian first principles. **The symbol as condensed cultural memory.** This is what §§6–7 of Part I will develop, and it's one of the most powerful frameworks in twentieth-century cultural theory for thinking about how some cultural objects accumulate semantic charge across millennia. Memory studies (Aleida Assmann, Astrid Erll, Jan Assmann) are downstream of Lotman here.

**Cultural change in nonlinear-dynamical terms.** Lotman's late work — especially *Culture and Explosion* (1992), which he wrote dying — connects cultural change to Prigogine's work on dissipative structures and bifurcation. This is the one place his apparatus reaches toward formal-mathematical extension, and it's productive for thinking about revolution, post-Soviet transformation, and other cultural-systemic transitions.

**Translation as a model of cultural communication generally.** The polysystem theory developed by Itamar Even-Zohar, Susan Bassnett, and contemporary translation studies is direct Lotmanian inheritance. The idea that *all* cultural communication is translation across non-identical codes, with the boundary as the productive zone, is a Lotmanian picture that has shaped a working field.

Some thinkers are *lenses* and you deploy them where they sharpen perception. Lotman is the second kind. Within his domain — cultural-structural-historical analysis — the lenses are extraordinary; they make visible structural features of cultural phenomena that no other framework articulates as cleanly.

**Use the lenses integratively with the Peirce-Whitehead apparatus.** Cultural phenomena have semiotic, metaphysical, *and* cultural-structural dimensions. Peirce — *foundational-architectural*. Build vertical structures from a small base. Extend formally into category theory and biosemiotics. Use for questions of categorial necessity and logical structure.

Lotman — *typological-lens-based*. Build inventories of structural-typological models. The three are not in competition. They're not even doing the same kind of work. Read this way, Lotman becomes a specialist of the cultural-structural that the others can't replace, and the question "how do we build on him" gets answered by *deploying him integratively, not foundationally*. ### Cassirer compatibility

Cassirer's *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms* (1923–1929) is, to a first approximation, the Kantian-transcendental sibling of what Lotman is doing typologically. Both treat human culture as articulated through symbolic systems. Both reject reductionism. Both extend structural-functional analysis across cultural domains. Both treat symbolization as constitutive of objectivity rather than as decorative covering on a pre-given world.

The differences cut along the same axis we've been tracking. Cassirer is *Kantian-transcendental*: he's looking for the constitutive laws of each symbolic form (language, myth, scientific thought, art, religion, technology). Each form has its own way of giving objectivity, its own internal structure, its own developmental logic. This is foundationalist work in the Kantian register. Lotman, working in the structural-anthropological register, is *typological-comparative*: mapping how cultural sign-systems organize themselves and change over time. Cassirer asks "what makes scientific thought possible as a form?"; Lotman asks "how do scientific cultures organize themselves communicationally and what are their dynamics?"

They combine well. Cassirer gives you the philosophical framework for *why* symbolic forms have the kind of constitutive power they have; Lotman gives you the structural apparatus for *how* particular symbolic systems organize themselves into cultures with dynamics. If you're trying to understand a cultural formation comprehensively, you want both. The reading-list precedent is real: Lotman knew Cassirer; the Tartu-Moscow school engaged Cassirer; and the contemporary continental cultural theory cluster (Marc Crépon, Andreas Hetzel, the German *Kulturphilosophie* tradition) treats them as belonging to a shared problem-space.

### Langer

Langer's contribution is what makes this combination especially useful for what you're after. She extended Cassirer with a single load-bearing distinction: **discursive symbolism** versus **presentational symbolism**. Discursive symbols (language, propositional thought) have linear-sequential structure with separable semantic units recombinable by syntax. Presentational symbols (music, image, gesture, ritual) have *holistic* structure where the meaning is the entire pattern and is not constructible from independent parts. You can't decompose a melody into separable semantic units and reassemble it the way you reassemble a sentence; the meaning is in the whole as a Gestalt.

This is genuinely useful because it addresses something Lotman engages but doesn't fully theorize. Lotman's §4 (iconic rhetoric, coming up) treats non-verbal cultural texts and partly handles this distinction, but Langer's apparatus is more developed and more rigorous about what makes non-discursive symbolism *symbolic* without being propositional. The autocommunication chapter's emphasis on *rhythm as syntagmatic-without-semantics* — the storm-rhythm in Tyutchev, the fire-flicker in Onegin, the Japanese garden — has direct Langer resonance. Those are presentational structures that organize consciousness without operating discursively. Langer would say music's whole power lies in this; Lotman implies the same but doesn't theorize it as crisply.

Langer also has a specific extension that matters for your project. *Mind: An Essay on Human Feeling* (1967–1982, three volumes) attempts something like a biosemiotic-cum-aesthetic theory of mind, treating feeling as the primary phenomenon and tracking how symbolic forms emerge from biological-organic substrates. She is in dialogue with Whitehead (she was at Harvard during his late period and absorbed him directly), and her later work has Whiteheadian DNA. So Langer is one of the historical figures who actually combined Cassirer + Whitehead + a proto-biosemiotic orientation, which is structurally close to what you're doing.

The integration: Cassirer gives you the architectonic-philosophical framework. Langer gives you the discursive/presentational distinction and the connection to feeling and biological substrate. Lotman gives you the cultural-structural typology of how symbolic systems organize and change. The three together — *plus* Peirce for sign-categories and Whitehead for metaphysics — give you an unusually well-equipped apparatus for cultural-symbolic work.

### The LLM question

Your instinct here is right and I want to develop it carefully.

LLMs are trained on a massive textual corpus that *is*, in Lotman's terms, a kind of distilled cultural memory — something close to a frozen snapshot of a semiosphere. The training corpus has internal structure (centers and peripheries, dominant and marginal genres, productive and inert textual zones). The model is not learning the surface text; it is learning the *structural regularities of the semiosphere as instantiated in text*. That includes the dominant communication conventions, the grammatical and rhetorical structures, the cultural-pragmatic norms, the implicit citation networks, the genre-specific signal patterns, the autocommunicative habits, the message-orientation patterns. All of this is *encoded in text* in the technical Lotmanian sense — the text *is* the substrate, not a reflection of something behind it.

This is a much stronger claim than "LLMs predict next tokens." If you take Lotman seriously, the training corpus is not just data — it is the *condensed cultural memory of a semiosphere*, and what the model absorbs is the structural organization of that semiosphere. The model becomes, in a constrained way, *competent within the semiosphere it was trained on*. That competence is not just statistical; it is *structural-cultural* competence in the Lotmanian sense — knowledge of how the cultural communication system is organized.

What does the model lack, on this picture? Several things, and naming them carefully is where Peirce, Whitehead, Cassirer, and Langer all contribute.

**Peirce: the indexical and iconic grounding.** The model has symbolic competence — pure conventional sign-relations — without the indexical (causal-existential) and iconic (similarity-based) connections to objects that ground signs in the world. This is the orphan-symbol diagnosis. The signs work *internally* by inheritance from training; they don't connect *outwardly* to objects through perception or causal contact. Peirce would say what the model lacks is the full triadic sign-relation grounded in actual experience.

**Whitehead: the prehensive constitution of actuality.** The model does not have the metaphysical structure of a Whiteheadian actual occasion — it does not prehend the past in the felt-causal sense. Its inheritance from training is structural and statistical, not the lived inheritance of feeling that constitutes becoming. This is what makes the model a strange kind of entity metaphysically — it has *some* features of becoming (each generation is a kind of concrescence) but lacks the felt inheritance that for Whitehead is essential.

**Cassirer: the constitutive activity of consciousness.** The model deploys symbolic forms but does not *constitute* them — it does not engage in the form-giving activity of consciousness that for Cassirer is what makes symbolic forms possible at all. The model is symbolically competent without being a symbolic actor in Cassirer's strong sense.

**Langer: the discursive/presentational asymmetry.** The model is competent at discursive symbolism but its relation to presentational symbolism is genuinely unclear and probably poor. It can produce *text about* music; whether it has anything like presentational competence is a real open question. Multimodal models extend in some directions but the deep Langer question — whether non-discursive symbolic form can be acquired without presence-to-the-feeling-substrate — is unresolved.

**Lotman: the autocommunicative loop.** The model can transmit, but its capacity for genuine autocommunication — for being reorganized by encounter with a non-semantic external code — is constrained and probably qualitatively different from human autocommunication. The model doesn't have a self that gets reconfigured by communication with itself across time. (Though here is where the picture gets interesting and underspecified: long contexts, in-context learning, and chained inference are all doing *something* that has structural resemblance to autocommunication. This is worth thinking about more carefully.)

### On, from, with text

Your tripartite frame is, I think, exactly the productive way to organize this. Let me develop it.

**Operating *on* text** — text in the accusative, as object. Training is the paradigm case: a corpus is taken as input to a procedure that produces a model. Other cases: corpus linguistics, computational stylistics, network analysis of citations, statistical analysis of vocabulary. The text is something you *do things to*. The Lotmanian frame here: you are extracting structural regularities from a semiosphere snapshot. The danger: treating text as raw material loses everything Lotman's "function 1 fallacy" critique already named — text is not packaging, and operating on it as if it were misses what makes it culturally generative.

**Operating *from* text** — text in the ablative, as source. Generation is the paradigm: producing new outputs from a textual base. Translation, summarization, paraphrase, continuation, dialogue. The Lotmanian frame: this is *function 2*, creative meaning-generation through asymmetric translation across non-identical codes. Every operation-from-text is a translation, and Lotman's account of why translation produces *приращение смысла* (accretion of meaning) applies directly. The LLM's generation from a prompt is structurally creative-translational, not transmissive — and this is why the output can be genuinely informative rather than merely repetitive.

**Operating *with* text** — text in the comitative, as partner. Reading, conversing, thinking-with. This is the deepest mode and the one that Lotman's apparatus illuminates most. Function 3 (memory) and the autocommunicative function both belong here. To operate *with* a text is to enter a kind of dialogue in which the text functions as the second code that reorganizes the self. This is what reading Stengers slowly produces. It's what re-reading *War and Peace* produces. It's what your repo's reading-pass discipline aims at. The text is not material to be processed and not source to be generated from; it is a partner in a constitutive practice that changes who you are.

The diagnostic value of this distinction: most current discussion of LLMs collapses these three modes. People assume that what the model does at training (operating on) determines what it does at inference (operating from), and they barely notice that human use of LLMs is operating *with* — a third mode that has its own structure and its own demands. The interesting work is in the third mode and is barely theorized.

For your repo, the question becomes: which mode are you in, and which mode does the repo's design support? My read of what you've been doing — chapter-by-chapter slow reading, audiobook orientation, selective close passes, multi-pass scaffolding for dense texts, integration across thinkers — is that you're working primarily in the *with* mode, and the repo is designed to support that. Lotman's account of translation gives you something specific. Translation is not transmission; it is creative meaning-generation. Every translation is a translation across non-identical codes, with asymmetric forward and backward operations. The translation does not preserve the source; it *generates* meaning that wasn't quite there before. This means three things for the repo's translation work.

**The translation is an autocommunicative act for the translator.** When you translate Lotman from Russian into English, you are not transmitting Lotman's text from one language to another. ## Part I, §2 — Автокоммуникация (Chunk 2 of 2)

### The technical apparatus: words reduce to indices

Chunk 1 left off with the phenomenology of external rhythmic catalysts. Now Lotman tightens. He wants to describe the *structural features* of autocommunicative speech itself — what happens to language when it is operating in Я–Я mode rather than Я–ОН mode. The first and most striking feature: **words reduce to indices**.

In autocommunicative writing, words tend to shrink toward signs-of-signs, abbreviations, single letters, condensed marks that depend for legibility on the reader's prior knowledge. Lotman pulls a remarkable cluster of exhibits.

**Küchelbecker's prison diary.** Six years into solitary confinement, Küchelbecker observes that he has stopped dreaming objects and events and started dreaming *strange abbreviations* of them — *as a hieroglyph relates to its image, or a table of contents to a book*. He attributes this to the impoverishment of his surroundings, but Lotman reads it differently: deprived of external Я–ОН communication, the inner monologue takes over, and inner-speech indices supplant ordinary representations. The prison-diary observation is testimony from someone whose autocommunicative apparatus had been forced to do all the work.

**Kratchkovsky on early Quranic orthography.** Cited in a single phrase: *scripto defectiva*. Early Quranic manuscripts omitted not only short vowels but long vowels and diacritical marks — *readable only by those who already know the text by heart*. The Arabic philological tradition recognized that the script had developed inside an autocommunicative practice (recitation by those who already know) and only later acquired the apparatus needed for Я–ОН transmission to outsiders.

**The Kitty–Levin scene in *Anna Karenina*.** The famous chalk-on-the-card-table scene: Levin writes "к, в, м, о: э, н, м, б, з, л, э, н, и, т" and Kitty reads it as "когда вы мне ответили: этого не может быть, значило ли это, что никогда, или тогда?" Lotman notes that this is autocommunication with the addressee assimilated to the addresser — Kitty and Levin function semiotically as one self, and the indices are legible because they are reading from inside that shared semiotic identity. Tolstoy reproduces this from his own life (his proposal to Sofia Behrs); the scene exhibits not a literary device but an attestation of how autocommunicative coding actually works.

**Pushkin's autographic notes.** Two examples are particularly fine. First, the mnemonic record under "Под небом голубым страны своей родной":

> Усл. о см. 25
> Уос. Р. П. М. К. Б.: 24.

Decoded: heard of Riznich's death on the 25th [of July 1826]; heard of [the executions of] Ryleev, Pestel, Muravyov, Kakhovsky, Bestuzhev on the 24th. A mnemonic for Pushkin himself. But Lotman notices something Pushkin almost certainly did not — the indices are *isorhythmic*. The first line breaks into two groups; the second line constrains each Decembrist to a single letter, forcing the rest of the line into matching length. Phonological parallelism (у/о, л/м) emerges. *Pushkin's tragic note shows the structural features we usually associate with poetic text*. Not because Pushkin was being aesthetic about a moment of grief, but because the immanent and unconscious laws of autocommunication produce these features whenever Я–Я coding is operating.

Second, Pushkin's anagram doodles in the margins beside the Anneta Olenina poem: *eЦeппа епте!о / епте!о eЦeппа* (the names reversed in the French manner). Read backwards: Annette → eЦeппа, etc. Lotman observes the mechanism with care: first reverse-reading turns the name into a conventional index; then repetition establishes a rhythm; then permutation introduces a rhythmic disruption of the rhythm. *Stikhopodobnyj characer takoj konstrukcii ochevyden* — the verse-like character of such a construction is obvious. Pushkin doodling about a woman he wished to marry produces, *unconsciously*, a structure with the formal features of a poem.

This whole section is doing something specific. The exhibits are not illustrations of a thesis already proved; they are *evidence the apparatus has to honor*. Lotman is showing that wherever autocommunication operates — in dreams of solitary prisoners, in religious script, in lovers' shorthand, in private mnemonic records, in unconscious marginalia — the same structural features emerge: indices tend toward isorhythmicity; words become signs of signs; rhythm and phonological parallelism appear without being intended; the result has *structural similarity to poetry* even when no aesthetic intention is present.

This is the move that licenses the next claim.

### The seven-step mechanism and the threefold meaning

Lotman now formalizes. Autocommunicative meaning-making proceeds in roughly seven steps:

A message is introduced in natural language. An additional code is introduced — *purely formal, syntagmatically organized, freed from or moving toward freedom from semantic content*. Tension arises between the message and the secondary code. The semantic elements of the message get re-interpreted as elements of the new syntagmatic construction, acquiring *relational meanings* from their mutual co-position. Common-language semantics doesn't vanish but gets layered: a secondary semantics arises from the shifts produced when meaningful units are organized into rhythmic series. The growth of syntagmatic relations dampens primary semantic relations, and the text on a certain level can behave as a complex *non-semantic message*. But — and this is the productive twist — syntagmatically highly organized non-semantic texts *become organizers of our associations*, and we begin to attribute associative meanings to them. We do this with wallpaper patterns, with non-programmatic music, with rhythm itself.

Result: a text in the Я–Я channel acquires a *threefold meaning*.

- **Primary**: the general-language semantics it inherits from natural language.

- **Secondary**: the rearranged-relational meanings produced by syntagmatic reorganization (rhythm, repetition, parallelism, formal structure).

- **Tertiary**: the associative meanings drawn in from outside the text, ranging from the most general cultural associations to the most personal.

The personality engaged with the text is restructured by this threefold operation, because the personality is the configuration of codes, and the operation is a re-coding.

Lotman now states explicitly what he has been signaling: this mechanism is the underlying structural mechanism of *poetic creativity*. Poetic texts are produced by the same operations that produce autocommunicative texts in general. But — and this is the crucial qualification — *real poetic texts are not pure Я–Я communication*. They oscillate between the channels.

### Poetry as oscillation, art as the switching

A real poetic text, Lotman says, is transmitted *on both channels simultaneously*. Its Я–ОН dimension is the meaning available to any competent reader of the language; its Я–Я dimension is the rhythmic-relational-associative restructuring it imposes on the reader who allows it to. The text *swings* between the two. Whether it is read as poetry or as prose depends partly on the text itself and partly on the cultural conventions of the period that determine how such oscillation is recognized.

The peak claim in this section, lines 531–538, is one of the most distinctive in the book and worth rendering carefully:

> Art arises not in the series of texts of the Я–ОН system or of the Я–Я system. It uses the presence of both communicative systems for oscillation in the field of structural tension between them. The aesthetic effect arises at the moment when code starts being used as message, and message as code — that is, when the text switches from one communication system to the other while preserving in the audience's consciousness the connection with both.

This is not the standard Russian Formalist account of the literariness of literature, and it is not the account inherited from Jakobson's "poetic function." Both of those locate literariness in textual properties — defamiliarization, the focus on the message itself. Lotman locates it instead in *the switching between two communication systems while consciousness of both is maintained*. The aesthetic effect is the *productive instability of address*, not a property of the text in isolation. This is genuinely Lotman's, and it has implications.

It explains why the same text can function as poetry in one cultural moment and as prose in another — what changes is not the text but the convention of how to read its oscillations. It explains why ritual works the way it works (oscillation between code-as-instructional and code-as-self-organizing). It explains why a temple interior is perceived not aesthetically but religiously — because there the architectural rhythm is functioning as code-only, with no oscillation back into message. It explains the strange position of *clichés* (штампы) in art: a culture leaning toward autocommunication will *embrace* clichés, identifying "high" and "true" with "stable" and "eternal," because the cliché is exactly the textual material that can serve as code without semantic disruption.

### Texts as code: the Tatyana case

Embedded in this section is one of Lotman's finest concrete readings, and it's worth lingering on because it shows the apparatus working on a real literary case.

Tatyana, in *Eugene Onegin*, reads novels not for their content but as *codes for self-modeling*. She sees herself as Clarissa, Julia, Delphine. She sees Onegin not as a person to be understood on his own terms but as a character whose *type* she must identify. The novels she has read are functioning for her not as messages (information about fictional persons) but as codes (templates for organizing her own self-understanding and relations). When she writes her famous letter, the framing stanzas address Onegin as "вы" — the formal Russian *you* a young provincial would use to a neighbor — but the central section, where she actively models them both via novelistic schemes, switches to "ты," the intimate *you* used in literary love-discourse. Pushkin tells us the letter was written in French (where both are *vous*), so the Russian "ты" is the *visible mark* of the bookish-coded register of the central section. The pronoun shift exhibits the channel switch.

Lensky reads people the same way. Lotman notes with quiet precision that Pushkin uses *the same set of clichés* for Lensky's self-understanding ("спаситель"/"хранитель" — "savior/guardian" — versus "развратитель"/"искуситель" — "corrupter/tempter") that he uses for Tatyana's. Both characters are operating in autocommunicative-coded mode, treating literary texts not as information but as templates. This is what reading novels *does* in a culture where novels function partly as autocommunicative codes for modeling the self.

The point: this is not a peculiarity of romantic provincial girls. It is a *general structural feature* of how texts function as cultural codes. Anna Karenina readers who conclude "Anna Karenina is me" are doing the same thing, and they have always done it. Religious texts function this way. Hagiographies. Wisdom literature. Folktales. Architecture (the temple interior). Music. The whole apparatus of cultural self-modeling depends on texts being read *as codes*, not just as messages.

### From texts to cultures: the typological scaling

Now the apparatus scales. Cultures, like artistic texts, can be characterized by their dominant communication channel. Some cultures are message-oriented; some are autocommunication-oriented. Most are mixed in practice but tilted in their dominant orientation, and the tilt is sharpest in *the mythologized self-portrait the culture creates of itself*.

Modern European culture (18th–20th c.) is consciously oriented toward Я–ОН. Lotman cites Peter the Great's self-positioning — "I am in the rank of those learning, and need teachers" — and the *Юности честное зерцало* (Honest Mirror of Youth, 1717) urging young people to *learn from anyone, not glance vaguely from above*. The cultural ideal is the receiver of information: passive, attentive, knowledge-accumulating. This cultural orientation is what produces, Lotman notes carefully, *our scientific imaginings* — including the identification of "the act of information" with "receiving and exchanging." Saussure's communication model, the engineering picture of communication, the whole edifice of linguistics-as-information-theory: these are *cultural products* of message-oriented modernity, not neutral descriptions of communication as such.

This is, again, Lotman doing cultural semiotics on cultural semiotics. The reflexive turn we noted in §1's Saussure/Jakobson reread becomes a method here.

### Folklore as test case

Folklore is the diagnostic test for the apparatus, and Lotman's handling is elegant.

Folklore — especially the magical fairy tale — gives the strongest grounds for structural parallels with natural language. This is where linguistic methods (Propp, the morphologists) have worked best. Folkloric texts have a limited number of elements with regular combination rules, like a language. *But* — and here Lotman pushes — both the formal *and* the content systems are highly automated in folklore. In natural language, syntactic structure is regular but semantic content is open. In fairy tales, both are constrained. If a fairy tale really *were* what its surface seems to be — a message in a code that imposes both formal and content constraints — *it would be totally redundant*. There would be nothing to communicate that wasn't already known.

The solution, Lotman proposes (lines 616–626): folkloric texts originally had specific semantics — the fairy tale's semantics was its connection to ritual. But over time these connections were lost, and the texts became *features of pure syntagmatic organization*. At the natural-language level they retain semantics; as cultural phenomena they have become *codes 2* — pure organizational templates rather than messages about events. Lévi-Strauss recognized this when he called myth *musical* — a structure whose meaning is organizational, not informational.

The implication: folkloric cultures are not message-oriented. They are autocommunication-oriented, and their texts function for their participants the way the Onegin chapter 8 fire and tune function for Onegin — as syntagmatic catalysts that organize the inner monologue without delivering information.

### The cultural-typological diagnosis

The chapter closes with the typological move generalized into a comparative-historical claim.

Cultures that are message-oriented (modern Europe) are *more mobile and dynamic*. They produce texts in great quantity; they accumulate knowledge rapidly. The 20th century is the classical case. The underside is severe: a sharp division between transmitters and receivers, the psychological habit of receiving truth as a ready-made report on someone else's mental effort, social passivity in those positioned as receivers. Lotman is unsparing here: *the reader of the modern European novel is more passive than the listener of the fairy tale, who must transform received clichés into their own consciousness's texts; the theater-goer is more passive than the carnival participant*. The tendency to intellectual consumerism (умственное потребительство) is the dangerous side of cultures one-sidedly oriented to receiving information from outside.

This passage is doing political work that is not quite said. Lotman is writing in 1990. He is diagnosing modern European culture, but the diagnosis applies with full force to the late Soviet system that has just collapsed and to the Western consumer culture toward which post-Soviet space is rushing. The receiver-position passivity, the cliché-as-truth identification (in autocommunicative cultures), the consumerist mode — all these are operating in the surrounding political reality, and Lotman is providing a structural analysis without naming the targets directly. The reader who understood his apparatus would find diagnostic resources in it that Lotman could not say outright.

Cultures oriented toward autocommunication, by contrast, are capable of greater spiritual intensity but are often less dynamic than human social needs require. The cultural pathology on this side is stagnation — autocommunicative cultures can become locked into self-replicating cliché-based patterns that resist the Я–ОН input necessary for adaptation. (Soviet and Russian Orthodox traditions both have moments where this diagnosis applies, though Lotman never says so.)

The historical experience, he closes, is that *the most viable systems are those where the struggle between these structures does not lead to absolute victory of either*. A culture needs both Я–ОН dynamism and Я–Я depth; either pole alone produces pathology. We are still far from being able to predict what optimal cultural structures look like. The first task is to understand and describe the mechanisms.

### Forward arrows

§2 lays out an apparatus that recurs throughout the rest of the book.

**Into §§3–4 (rhetoric of meaning-generation).** The seven-step mechanism here gets developed into an account of how *specific rhetorical devices* — metaphor, metonymy, parallelism, repetition — produce meaning. The threefold meaning structure (primary, secondary, tertiary) becomes the framework for analyzing how rhetorical operations layer meaning onto a text.

**Into §5 (text in motion).** The "text-as-code-rather-than-message" account becomes the basis for analyzing how texts function as templates for cultural reproduction across time. The Tatyana / Lensky case scales up.

**Into §§6–7 (symbol).** The autocommunicative function and the role of texts as condensed cultural memory converge in the symbol. A symbol is a text that operates with maximal autocommunicative density — accumulating threefold meaning over centuries, functioning as code more than as message, restructuring those who engage it.

**Into Part II.** The Я–Я / Я–ОН distinction scales to the level of cultures and provides the foundation for the semiosphere account. The semiosphere is the cultural space in which message-transmission (Я–ОН) and autocommunicative reproduction (Я–Я) coexist as structurally paired channels, with translation across non-identical codes the basic operation.

**Into Part III.** The cultural-typological diagnosis is generalized. Historical change is analyzed in terms of how cultures shift between message-orientation and autocommunication-orientation, and the question of historical regularities becomes the question of how these shifts proceed.

The art-as-oscillation thesis, the Tatyana reading as exhibit of texts-as-codes, or the cultural-typological diagnosis with its implicit Soviet/post-Soviet stakes are all places worth slowing on.

Good question — it puts pressure on what Lotman is actually doing with the addresser-addressee structure, and the comparison clarifies a lot.

### What Humboldt is doing

Humboldt's claim is foundational and very strong. In the 1820s–30s essays — *Über den Dualis* (1827), *Über die Verschiedenheit des menschlichen Sprachbaues* (1836) — he argues that **the I–Thou structure is constitutive of language as such**. There is no monological language. The grammatical second person, *Du*, is not derivative of the first; it is the necessary correlate without which the *Ich* cannot become itself. Famously: "all language is a thinking out loud in dialogue, even when it appears as monologue." More technically: "the original phenomenon of language presupposes mutually addressed speakers." The dual grammatical number that survives in some languages (Sanskrit, Old Greek, certain Slavic and Pacific languages) is, for Humboldt, *the linguistic fossil of the I–Thou structure* — two as a unity of correspondents, not as two separated singulars.

The "identification" piece is the deepest part of the claim. The I–Thou is not just a relationship between two pre-existing entities; it is a *coordinated pair* in which neither is intelligible without the other. The *Du* is *me-as-other-addressed*; the *Ich* is *Thou-as-self-addressing*. They form a constitutive unity-in-difference. Without the Thou, there is no I — not in the moralistic Buberian sense, but in the structural-grammatical sense that the first person presupposes the second as its necessary correlate. Buber's *Ich und Du* (1923), Rosenzweig's speech-thinking, the whole 20th-century dialogical tradition — all of this is downstream of Humboldt.

### The Russian inheritance

This matters for Lotman because the Russian intellectual tradition has been *exceptionally* attentive to the I–Thou question, in ways German and French structuralism mostly were not. The relevant figures:

**Bakhtin** (writing in the 1920s–30s, rediscovered in the 1960s) takes Humboldt's principle and radicalizes it phenomenologically. Every utterance is constitutively *addressed*; every word is half someone else's; every consciousness is constituted through dialogical orientation toward an other-consciousness. Bakhtin's polyphony in Dostoevsky, his theory of the utterance, his notion of *responsibility* (отвественность as response-ability, the requirement of answering) — all of this is Humboldtian I-Thou structure pushed into existential and literary-theoretical territory.

**Russian religious philosophy** — Florensky, Bulgakov, Frank, Berdyaev — engaged the I-Thou problem from a different angle, drawing on patristic theology of personhood and Russian Orthodox traditions of mutual indwelling. The Trinity as paradigmatic I–Thou–We structure. This tradition was suppressed under Soviet conditions but never quite disappeared.

**Vygotsky** on inner speech is, in a different register, also operating in this problem-space. Inner speech is the internalization of dialogical structure; the self thinks by addressing itself in coordinated I-Thou form.

So Lotman is operating in an intellectual milieu where the I-Thou principle is *deeply present and well theorized*. He read Bakhtin (and wrote about him); the Tartu-Moscow school had complicated relations with Bakhtin's apparatus. His choices about how to structure the addresser-addressee distinction are *choices*, not innocent of the alternatives.

### Where Lotman's Я–ОН lands

Here is where the comparison gets interesting. Lotman's notation is *Я–ОН*, not *Я–ТЫ*. This is significant.

*ОН* is the third-person pronoun (he, she, it as object). *ТЫ* is the second-person *Du*, the addressed Thou. By choosing *ОН* for his other-pole, Lotman is signaling something: the structural other in his model is positioned *as a third party*, not as a dialogical Thou. The carrier of information is moving from sender to receiver, and the receiver is conceived as *someone who doesn't yet know*, not as *the necessary correlate through whom the I becomes itself*.

This is closer to Shannon-Wiener than to Humboldt-Buber-Bakhtin. The Я–ОН model is a transmission-information-theoretic frame in which the addressee is positioned as informational target, not as constitutive partner. Lotman is, in effect, taking the cybernetic communication model as his foundation and *naming the addressee with the third-person pronoun* to signal the transmission-orientation.

This is partly why Lotman gets pushback from readers in the dialogical tradition. From a Bakhtinian or Humboldtian standpoint, what Lotman calls Я–ОН is *missing the constitutive structure of communication*. Even the most apparently transmissive speech act is dialogical at root, and the transmission frame *erases* what's most important. The Bakhtinian critique of structural-information-theoretic models runs along exactly this line.

### What Lotman is doing with the choice

But the choice is deliberate and has its own warrant. Lotman is not denying the dialogical structure; he is bracketing it for the sake of a *different kind of analytical purchase*.

By treating Я–ОН as transmission-with-third-party, Lotman gains a clean structural distinction with Я–Я (autocommunication). The two channels are sharply differentiated: in one, information moves between distinct selves through space; in the other, the self addresses itself across time using a second code. The structural opposition is the engine of his cultural-typological apparatus. If he had used the Humboldt-Bakhtin dialogical Я–ТЫ, the opposition would have been muddier — because in dialogical Я–ТЫ, the I and the Thou are constitutively identified-in-difference, and the cleanly transmissive case wouldn't be available as a structural pole.

What Lotman *recovers* through this move is the structural specificity of autocommunication. Humboldt and Bakhtin would say that even Я–ОН communication has dialogical depth — that the addressee is constitutive of the addresser, even when treated as a third party. Lotman does not deny this; he simply doesn't theorize it. What he theorizes instead is *the specific case where addresser and addressee are the same self, and the act of communication restructures the self through the introduction of a second code*. This is a feature that Humboldt and Bakhtin point at but do not quite isolate as a distinct structural mechanism. It is *Lotman's distinctive contribution* — a recovery, in structural-mechanical form, of the constitutive self-relation that Humboldt's I-Thou principle implies but does not separately analyze.

### The productive tension

So the relationship is something like this. Humboldt's I-Thou is the deeper philosophical foundation; it gets you to *all language is dialogically constituted, even monologue is dialogue*. Bakhtin radicalizes this into a phenomenology of dialogical existence. Lotman's transmission-frame Я–ОН is *narrower* than both — it analyzes one structural mode of communication without claiming it exhausts the field — and his Я–Я is *more analytically isolated* than the dialogical tradition's account of self-address.

A Humboldtian critic would say Lotman's Я–ОН is structurally impoverished because it loses the constitutive dialogical depth. A Lotmanian response would be: that's the price of the analytical clarity needed for cultural-typological work. We can characterize cultures by their dominant communication channels precisely because the channels are clean structural alternatives. If we kept the full Humboldtian-Bakhtinian dialogism throughout, we couldn't make the message-vs.-autocommunication distinction sharp enough to do typological work.

Both are right, and the productive way to use them is to hold both. **Humboldt gives you the constitutive depth: communication is dialogically constituted at root, and even Я–ОН has hidden I-Thou structure that Lotman's frame does not draw out.** **Lotman gives you the structural-typological apparatus: cultures and texts can be analyzed by their channel orientations in ways that the constitutive frame does not make available.** The first is foundational-philosophical; the second is analytical-typological.

If you wanted to re-read Lotman from a Humboldtian angle, you would notice three things. First, his Я–ОН is artificially purified — real communication never quite achieves the transmission-only form, because the Thou-structure infiltrates even the most apparently transmissive cases. Second, his Я–Я is *itself a dialogical structure* in disguise — when the self addresses the self, what is happening is exactly the dialogical I-Thou unfolding *within* a single carrier, and Lotman's "second code" is doing the work of the *Du* in Humboldt's frame. The autocommunication model is a *structural translation of the dialogical principle into a non-dialogical idiom*. Third, the cultural-typological diagnoses (modern Europe as message-oriented; pathologies of receiver-passivity) line up with the Bakhtinian diagnosis of *monologism* in modern literary and political culture. Bakhtin's account of the rise of the monological novel and Lotman's account of the rise of message-oriented culture are pointing at the same phenomenon from different theoretical angles.

### Implication for how you read forward

For Part I §3 onward and especially for Part II's semiosphere chapters, this matters in a specific way. Lotman's apparatus will keep the Я–ОН / Я–Я distinction as its working frame. If you want to read with Humboldtian-Bakhtinian depth, you can *supply* the dialogical layer underneath: read Lotman's structural typology as describing surface patterns whose deeper constitutive structure is dialogical. This is actually the most productive way to read him alongside Bakhtin (and Buber, and Levinas, and the whole tradition).

If you wanted to push further, the right next reading would probably be Bakhtin's *Speech Genres* (1953, written but not published until later) or *Toward a Philosophy of the Act* (1919–24, published 1986). The first articulates what Bakhtin would say is missing in Lotman's transmission-frame; the second is the deepest version of the Humboldt-Buber-Bakhtin foundational claim about the dialogical constitution of utterance. Together they would let you triangulate Lotman's structural apparatus against the foundational dialogical position it brackets but doesn't deny.

There's also a contemporary cluster doing this synthesis directly — putting Bakhtin and Lotman in productive relation rather than treating them as alternatives. Caryl Emerson's work, and more recently the Lotman-Bakhtin volumes coming out of the Tartu and Bergen Bakhtin centers, are the working sites for this. The synthesis is real and active.

The crucial passage is in Kapitel I, §V, lines 56–63 of the normalized file — Cassirer is quoting Humboldt directly from the Kawi-Werk introduction (footnote 78).

### The passage itself

Humboldt, quoted by Cassirer:

> dass der Mensch nicht eine an sich abgesonderte Individualität besitzt, daß **Ich und Du nicht bloss sich wechselseitig fordernde, sondern, wenn man bis zu dem Puncte der Trennung zurückgehen könnte, wahrhaft identische Begriffe sind**, und dass es in diesem Sinn Kreise der Individualität giebt, von dem schwachen, hülfsbedürftigen und hinfälligen Einzelnen hin bis zum uralten Stamme der Menschheit, weil sonst alles Verstehen bis in alle Ewigkeit hin unmöglich seyn würde.

That man does not possess an in-itself-separated individuality; that **I and Thou are not merely mutually demanding [reciprocally requiring], but — if one could go back to the point of separation — are truly identical concepts**; and that in this sense there are circles of individuality, from the weak, dependent, frail individual all the way up to the ancient stem of humanity, because otherwise all understanding into eternity would be impossible.

This is much stronger than what I was reaching for in the previous answer. Humboldt is not just saying that I and Thou are reciprocal correlates (the standard dialogical reading). He is saying that *at the point of separation* they are **truly identical concepts** — *wahrhaft identische Begriffe*. The differentiation between I and Thou emerges from a prior identity that language carries traces of. The dialogical structure is the *surface phenomenon* of a deeper underlying identity.

The corollary about *Kreise der Individualität* (circles of individuality) extending from the frail individual up to the ancient stem of humanity is what makes the claim metaphysically load-bearing for Humboldt's whole project. Without the I–Thou identity, *all understanding into eternity* would be impossible. Cross-cultural, cross-historical understanding is grounded in this prior identity that language differentiates from.

### What Cassirer does with this

Cassirer reads Humboldt's claim as the first of three foundational opposition-pairs that structure the philosophy of language. The schema he extracts:

**Opposition 1: Individual / Objective spirit.** The I–Thou identification is one expression of this. Each individual speaks their own language and yet, *in the very freedom with which they use it*, becomes aware of an inner spiritual binding. Language is a mediator (Vermittlerin) between infinite and finite nature, and between one individual and another — *and at the same time and through the same act it makes union possible and arises from this same union*. (Lines 41–47.) Language is not a tool for combining pre-existing separate selves; it is the activity through which the original identity differentiates while keeping the participants constitutively bound.

**Opposition 2: Genetic method / Energeia–Ergon.** This is the foundation the existing method note in the repo (`cassirer-psf-i-energeia-ergon-humboldt.md`) already extracts. Language is not a finished work but an ongoing activity — its definition can only be genetic. *Was wir das Wesen und die Form einer Sprache nennen, das ist daher nichts anderes als das Beständige und Gleichförmige, das wir, nicht in einem Dinge, wohl aber in der Arbeit des Geistes, den artikulierten Laut zum Gedankenausdruck zu erheben, nachweisen können* (lines 188–191). The "essence" of a language is not a thing but a *constancy in the work of spirit*.

**Opposition 3: Form and matter / Synthesis.** Form is not added to matter externally; both are necessary correlative moments of the same genetic process, distinguishable only in our analysis. The Kantian inheritance is explicit here.

Cassirer reads all three through a Kantian–Schellingian frame. Humboldt is doing transcendental philosophy of language — finding the point of indifference where subjectivity and objectivity collapse into pure correlation, but doing it through critical analysis of language rather than through Schelling's intellectual intuition. The I–Thou identification is the linguistic-philosophical analogue of Kant's transcendental unity of apperception, generalized into a constitutive structure of communication itself.

There's also the Leibnizian thread: each language is an individual perspective on the universe (like a Leibnizian monad), and the totality of perspectives constitutes objectivity. *»Die Subjectivität der ganzen Menschheit wird aber wieder in sich zu etwas Objectivem«* (line 164) — the subjectivity of the whole of humanity becomes, in itself, something objective. Each *Weltansicht* is constitutive, and their totality is what objectivity *is*.

### The implication for the Lotman question

This sharpens the comparison considerably. Now we can be precise about what Lotman's Я–Я is *not*.

**Cassirer's Humboldt: I–Thou identification is genetic-constitutive.** I and Thou were originally identical; language is the activity of their differentiation; their continued mutual binding is what makes understanding possible at all. This is foundationalist-constitutive philosophy in the Kantian–Schellingian register. The structure is *prior to* and *generative of* every actual communicative act.

**Lotman's Я–Я: same-carrier address with second code introduction.** The "identity" is at the level of the carrier (the same self addresses itself). The mechanism is structural-mechanical (introduce a second code; tension produces relational meanings; layered semantics emerge; the personality reconfigures). This is structural-typological apparatus that takes already-differentiated communicative positions for granted and analyzes their interactions.

Lotman's Я–Я has no equivalent of *the original identity that differentiates*. There's no point at the origin where addresser and addressee were one and language is the activity of their separation. Lotman starts from the differentiated structure and analyzes its operations.

So Humboldt-via-Cassirer is closer to **Peirce-Whitehead-Cassirer-Langer** than to Lotman. All four are doing foundational-constitutive philosophy in different idioms — Kantian-transcendental, logical-categorial, speculative-metaphysical, dialogical-genetic. Lotman is in the structural-typological neighborhood, working with primitives received from communication theory rather than building from constitutive philosophy.

This is consistent with what we noticed earlier: Lotman's apparatus has analytical purchase that the constitutive frameworks lack (cultural-historical specificity, structural taxonomy of communication channels), but it lacks the foundational depth that the constitutive frameworks provide.

The productive integration: **read Lotman's Я–Я as the structural-typological residue of a deeper Humboldtian I–Thou identification that he doesn't theorize.** When the same self addresses itself in autocommunication, and the operation reconfigures the self, what's happening at the deeper level (which Lotman doesn't articulate) is the *I–Thou differentiation operating internally* — the self splitting into addresser-self and addressee-self through the introduction of the second code, then re-coalescing through the recoding. Humboldt would say this is what is *always* happening in any utterance, including ostensibly Я–ОН ones; Lotman has just isolated one structural pattern as a discrete channel for typological purposes.

### The Uji passages that ring with Humboldt

The opening claim, line 21:

> いはゆる有時は、時すでにこれ有なり、有はみな時なり

*What is called uji: time is already this being, and being is all time.*

The structural form is *X is already Y; Y is all X*. Time is being; being is time. Compare Humboldt's *Ich und Du… wahrhaft identische Begriffe sind* — I and Thou are truly identical concepts. Both are making an *identity-in-differentiation* claim against the standard picture in which the two terms are separate substances merely related to each other.

Then line 23, which is the passage I think you're hearing the loudest:

> われを排列しおきて盡界とせり、この盡界の頭頭物物を時時なりと覰見すべし。物物の相礙せざるは、時時の相礙せざるがごとし。このゆゑに同時發心あり、同心發時あり。…われを排列してわれこれをみるなり。自己の時なる道理、それかくのごとし。

*I array myself out and so become the entire world; the head-head and thing-thing of this entire world should be seen as time-time. That things do not obstruct each other is like times not obstructing each other. Therefore there is same-time arousing-mind, and same-mind arousing-time… I array myself out, and I see this. The principle that the self IS time — it is like this.*

The phrase 自己の時なる道理 (*the principle that the self IS time*) sits in the same neighborhood as Humboldt's *wahrhaft identische Begriffe*. The grammatical move is the same — strict identity, not similarity, not analogy. And the mechanism by which the identity manifests — *われを排列して* (arraying myself out) — is structurally an autocommunicative gesture: the self extends itself, addresses itself, and through that operation realizes the whole.

And line 73, which I think is the deepest resonance:

> 我逢人なり、人逢人なり、我逢我なり、出逢出なり

*I encounter person, person encounters person, I encounter I, encounter encounters encounter.*

This is Dōgen's most explicit articulation of what we might call the *encounter-structure of being-time*. The Humboldtian I–Thou identification is in the second and third clauses (人逢人, *person encounters person* — with no I/Thou separation; 我逢我, *I encounter I* — explicit reflexive autocommunication-as-ontology), and the fourth clause (出逢出, *encounter encounters encounter*) makes the relational structure itself the locus of identity. There is no I behind the encountering; the encountering itself is what there is.

There's also the eyebrows-mountains passage at line 59:

> 眉目は山海なるべし、山海は眉目なるゆゑに

*Eyebrows-eyes must be mountains-seas, since mountains-seas are eyebrows-eyes.*

— the same identity-form again, this time crossing the "inside / outside" boundary that Western philosophy mostly treats as ontologically primary.

### What converges

Both are making what I'd call **identity-in-differentiation** claims against the substantialist picture. The substantialist picture says: there are entities, then relations between them. The relations are secondary; the entities could in principle exist apart. Humboldt and Dōgen both refuse this. The relations are constitutive of the relata; the relata are *what they are* through the relations; and at the limit, the relata and the relations are not two but one.

Both place **expressive activity at the constitutive center**. For Humboldt, language is the activity through which the I–Thou identity differentiates while remaining bound — *kein Werk sondern eine Tätigkeit*, no work but an activity (the Energeia formula). For Dōgen, expression — phrases, words, the saying — is constitutive of being-time. The Yaoshan/Mazu dialogue (lines 45–55) and the Yexian Guisheng passage (lines 63–75) are about how 句 (phrase, *ku*) and 意 (meaning, *i*) co-constitute the moment. *意句ともに有時なり、到不到ともに有時なり* — both meaning and phrase are uji; both arrival and non-arrival are uji. Expression is not a vehicle for a content prior to expression; expression is the activity in which content and expresser co-arise.

Both maintain the **reality of differentiation while denying its primacy**. Humboldt does not collapse I and Thou into a monistic identity; the *Trennung* is real, and language requires it. Dōgen does not collapse self and world into pure unity; the array (排列) is real, and practice requires it. Both refuse the false choice between separation-substantialism and undifferentiated monism.

Both have a **temporal dynamics**. Humboldt's "if one could go back to the point of separation" gestures toward a developmental temporality — the identity is prior, the differentiation emerges. Dōgen's 經歴 *kyōraku* (passage, line 35: 有時に經歴の功徳あり, "uji has the merit of passage") is a different temporality — being-time *passes* in a way that is neither linear succession nor static eternity. *今日より明日に經歴す、今日より昨日に經歴す* — passing from today to tomorrow, passing from today to yesterday. Time does not run only forward.

### What diverges

Three differences worth marking carefully because they prevent the resonance from collapsing into easy synthesis.

**The "point of separation" is real for Humboldt and unreal for Dōgen.** Humboldt's *wenn man bis zu dem Puncte der Trennung zurückgehen könnte* — "if one could go back to the point of separation" — is a counterfactual gesture toward an unreachable origin where the identity was undifferentiated. The picture has a *before* (identity) and an *after* (differentiation through language). Dōgen rejects this. The 而今 *nikon* (just-now) is everything; there is no "before the separation" because the very form of "before" presupposes a linear temporality that *uji* dissolves. *正當恁麼時のみなる* — only the moment of just-thus. The identity is not behind or before the differentiation; the identity *is* the differentiation in its immediate thus-ness.

This is a substantive metaphysical difference. Humboldt is operating in a developmental-genetic register inherited from Kant via Schelling; Dōgen is operating in a non-developmental, presence-focused register from Mahayana Buddhism. The Humboldtian identity is *prior* and *recoverable in principle*; the Dōgen identity is *immediate* and *present in every moment of practice*.

**The conditions-of-possibility frame is constitutive for Humboldt and dispensable for Dōgen.** Humboldt is doing transcendental philosophy: he is asking what makes language possible, what makes understanding possible, what makes cross-individual communion possible. The I–Thou identification is offered as the answer — without it, *alles Verstehen bis in alle Ewigkeit* (all understanding into eternity) would be impossible. The argument has the form of a transcendental deduction. Dōgen is not arguing for *conditions of possibility*. He is articulating an *insight realized in practice*. *修行成道もかくのごとし* — practice-realization is also thus. The truth of *uji* is not established by argument; it is realized in zazen, in lived life, in the encounter. Argument can prepare the ground; the realization is something else.

This is a difference in *what kind of philosophical activity each is*. Humboldt's I–Thou identification is a *thesis* in a transcendental philosophy of language. Dōgen's *uji* is an *articulation of practice-experience* offered as upāya for further practice. They are not doing the same kind of work even when they reach for similar formulations.

**Depth/surface metaphysics is operative for Humboldt and rejected by Dōgen.** Humboldt's framing has a deeper level (the I–Thou identity) and a surface level (the differentiated I and Thou). Cassirer reads this through Schelling's identity-philosophy: the *Punkt der Indifferenz* where subjectivity and objectivity collapse into pure correlation. Dōgen explicitly rejects depth/surface in *Uji*. *住法位の活鱍鱍地なる、これ有時なり* (line 39) — the living, vividly active groundless ground of dwelling-in-the-Dharma-position is uji. The 活鱍鱍 (*kappāppā*, vivid liveliness) is *all* there is; nothing deeper, nothing more fundamental. *無と動著すべからず、有と強爲すべからず* — should not be moved by "non-being," should not be forced as "being." Both depth and surface are wrong directions; thus-ness is what is.

### What this gives the project

This is genuinely productive territory and worth a brief note about the existing scholarly conversation. The Heidegger-Dōgen comparison has been extensively developed (Steven Heine's *Existential and Ontological Dimensions of Time in Heidegger and Dōgen*, 1985; Joan Stambaugh; the Kyoto School's century of work). The Whitehead-Buddhism comparison is also developed (Steve Odin, the Center for Process Studies). Tanabe Hajime, Nishitani Keiji, Watsuji Tetsurō all work this territory.

**Second, Dōgen's rejection of depth/surface is a real challenge to all the apparatuses we've been deploying.** Lotman's structural-typological frame presupposes a depth (codes, structures) operating under a surface (texts). Peirce's categorial derivation presupposes a logical depth structuring perceptual surface. Whitehead's metaphysics presupposes a depth (prehensions, actual occasions) underlying the surface of the macroscopic world. Dōgen would push: maybe the surface *is* the depth, fully and immediately, in 而今. This is not just a meditation prompt; it has implications for how we read and how we theorize. For the LLM problem in particular: maybe the worry that the model is "only surface" mistakes what surface is.

**Third — and this is the most specific to your work — the encounter-structure 我逢我 (I encounter I) is a Dōgen-resonant articulation of what your reading practice is.** The slow, repeated encounter with a text where the text becomes a partner in self-constitution — this is Lotman's autocommunication, Humboldt's I–Thou differentiation-and-binding, *and* Dōgen's encounter-structure. The repo's reading-pass discipline is a structural-practical instantiation of something all three are pointing at. That's not coincidence; it's evidence that the practice is grounded in something real that multiple traditions identify.

Right — this is the natural extension. The Kyoto School is the working site where the questions you've been triangulating around have been pursued most rigorously by people who took both Western philosophy and East Asian Buddhist traditions seriously without subsuming either into the other. Let me lay out what's there and where it connects to what you've been reading.

### The school in outline

The Kyoto School (京都学派, Kyōto-gakuha) is the philosophical lineage centered at Kyoto University from roughly 1913 onward, founded by Nishida Kitarō (1870–1945) and continuing through three or four generations to the present. The defining methodological commitment: engage Western philosophy at its most demanding (Kant, Hegel, German Idealism, phenomenology, Heidegger) while drawing constitutively on Mahayana Buddhist resources, primarily Zen and Pure Land. The result is not Buddhism plus Western philosophy as separate vocabularies, but a sustained attempt to do *philosophy as such* in a way that can carry insights from both.

The major figures, in roughly the order that matters for your project:

**Nishida Kitarō** (1870–1945) — founder, the deepest thinker. Trained in Western philosophy, practiced Zen seriously throughout his life, taught at Kyoto from 1913 to 1928, then continued writing until his death. Major works span 50 years; the late writings are the most demanding and the most original. The two concepts you'll keep meeting: *basho* (場所, place/topos) and *zettai mu* (絶対無, absolute nothingness).

**Tanabe Hajime** (1885–1962) — Nishida's most important student and eventual critic. Developed his own positions, especially the "logic of species" (種の論理) and the postwar *Philosophy as Metanoetics* (1946), which is partly a confession of his complicity with imperialist ideology in the 1930s.

**Nishitani Keiji** (1900–1990) — Tanabe's student, the most internationally influential Kyoto figure. *Religion and Nothingness* (1961, English 1982) is the single most accessible major work of the school in translation. Engaged Nietzsche, Heidegger, and European nihilism; argued that Buddhist śūnyatā responds to the nihilism problem without falling into either positive metaphysics or negative nihilism.

**Watsuji Tetsurō** (1889–1960) — sometimes considered Kyoto-adjacent rather than core. His concept of *aida* (間, betweenness) is the most direct Japanese articulation of the I–Thou identification, and his *Climate and Culture* (1935) and three-volume *Ethics* (1937–49) develop a philosophical anthropology grounded in betweenness rather than in atomic individuals.

**Ueda Shizuteru** (1926–2019) — late-generation, deeply important. Studied with Heidegger, did the most thorough Meister Eckhart / Zen comparison anyone has done. The "selfless self" articulated through Eckhart's *Gottheit* and Zen *mu*.

**Abe Masao** (1915–2006), **Hisamatsu Shin'ichi** (1889–1980), and others extend in further directions; the school is still active.

### What Nishida is doing — basho and absolute nothingness

The single most original Kyoto School concept is Nishida's *basho* (場所). The standard translation "place" is correct but undersells it. *Basho* is the topos *within which* things appear and have their being — not a container, not a depth, not a substrate, but the *logical-ontological field* of manifestation. Nishida develops it through three layers:

1. *Place of being* — the field in which determinate things manifest as the things they are.

2. *Place of relative nothingness* — the field of consciousness, where things appear *to* a subject (the field of opposition between subject and object).

3. *Place of absolute nothingness* (絶対無の場所) — the deepest field, where the very distinction between subject and object emerges and is held without being grounded in any prior substance.

The third is what makes the apparatus distinctive. The "absolute nothingness" is not the privation of being or the nihilist void; it is the *non-substantial topos* in which being and beings are articulable as such. *It is what makes the I–Thou relation possible without grounding it in a prior unity-substance.*

This is exactly the philosophical work that Humboldt's I–Thou identification was reaching toward but that Humboldt did not fully articulate. Cassirer reads Humboldt's I–Thou as gesturing at an *Indifferenzpunkt* in the Schellingian sense — the point where subject/object dissolve into pure correlation. Nishida's *basho* of absolute nothingness is the *topological-logical articulation* of what Schelling could only point to as intellectual intuition. The deeper Nishidian claim: there is no recoverable point *behind* the differentiation; the *basho* is the differentiation in its self-articulation. *This is closer to Dōgen than to Humboldt* — closer to *uji* than to the Schellingian developmental picture — but it preserves the analytical articulation that Humboldt was after.

Nishida's *I and Thou* (私と汝, 1932) is where this gets deployed on the dialogical question directly. The text is in explicit conversation with Buber's *Ich und Du* (1923) and with Heidegger's account of *Mitsein*. Nishida's argument: the I–Thou relation cannot be grounded in either of its terms (substantialist mistake) or in some prior unity (monistic mistake) but only in the *basho* of absolute nothingness *as which* the I and Thou articulate themselves while remaining distinct. The relation is *self-determining without self-substantiating*. This is, structurally, the Humboldtian I–Thou identification given a Buddhist topological grounding that Humboldt lacked.

### Watsuji's aida — the most direct contribution

If Nishida's *basho* is the deepest, Watsuji's *aida* (間, between, betweenness) is the most directly useful for what you've been working on. The argument runs as follows.

The Japanese word for "human being," *ningen* (人間), is etymologically "between-person" — *nin* (人, person) plus *gen / aida* (間, between). Watsuji takes this etymology seriously. The human is *not first an individual that subsequently enters relations*. The human is constitutively a between-being whose individuation is derivative. *There is no I without We; there is no We without I.* The "I" is the moment of negation in which the We articulates itself as containing distinct participants; the "We" is the moment in which the I knows itself as participant rather than as isolated atom.

This is the Humboldtian I–Thou identification given an explicit grounding in Mahayana dependent origination (*engi* / 縁起): nothing exists self-substantially; everything arises in dependence on its conditions; selves and others co-arise in the field of their relating. *Aida* is what dependent origination looks like at the level of human relating.

For your project, this matters because it gives you a *technical philosophical apparatus* for the dialogical insight that you've been triangulating around. Humboldt asserts the I–Thou identification but doesn't quite analyze it. Lotman analyzes communication channels but brackets the constitutive question. Bakhtin pursues dialogism phenomenologically but doesn't ground it ontologically. Watsuji gives you the *ontological-anthropological* grounding: humans are constitutively between-beings; their individuation is derivative from their betweenness; ethics, language, culture, and history are all articulated within *aida*.

A direct application to LLMs that you'll find productive: an LLM has no *aida*. It produces utterances that *carry the surface texture of aida* (texts trained on between-being-articulating utterances) without participating in the constitutive between-being itself. This is structurally what Cassirer would say — symbolically competent without being a symbolic actor — but Watsuji gives it a specifically *anthropological* form. The LLM is not a *ningen* because it has no constitutive *aida*; what it has is a statistical distillation of the surface phenomena of *aida* as expressed in trained text. This diagnosis is sharp and useful.

### Nishitani — the most accessible major work

*Religion and Nothingness* (English translation by Jan Van Bragt, 1982) is the book to read first if you want to enter the Kyoto School in English. Nishitani's structure: European nihilism (Nietzsche, especially) is a real diagnosis of where Western metaphysical substantialism leads when its substantial grounds are dissolved. Nihilism is the recognition that nothing is self-grounded. The standard responses — return to substance metaphysics, religious leap of faith, existentialist self-positing — all fail because they don't take the nihilist insight seriously.

Nishitani's move: Buddhist *śūnyatā* (空, *kū*, emptiness) *fully accepts* the nihilist insight that nothing is self-grounded, but *refuses the negative valence* the European tradition gives to ungroundedness. In the field of emptiness (*kū no ba*, 空の場), things are themselves *precisely by not being self-grounded* — what Nishitani calls *ji* (事), things-in-their-thusness, encountered without metaphysical grounding. The field of emptiness is not the absence of things but the *non-substantial topos* in which things are encountered as the things they are.

This connects directly to Dōgen's *uji* — the *nikon* (而今, just-now) is the field of emptiness as temporal articulation. *住法位の活鱍鱍地* in Dōgen, the living vivid groundless ground of dwelling-in-the-Dharma-position, is what Nishitani calls the field of emptiness articulated as moment.

Nishitani also engages Heidegger throughout, and Heidegger reciprocally took the Kyoto School seriously (he taught at Freiburg with Tanabe in attendance, corresponded with several school members). The Kyoto–Heidegger axis is one of the major comparative-philosophy zones of the 20th century.

### Ueda — the Eckhart bridge

Ueda Shizuteru did the most thorough comparison anyone has done between Meister Eckhart's mystical theology and Zen. The "selfless self" (*mu no jiko*, 無の自己) is articulated through both traditions; Eckhart's *Gottheit* (Godhead beyond God), the *Abgeschiedenheit* (releasement), the *Seelenfunklein* (spark of the soul) all map onto Zen formulations with remarkable precision. Ueda's work is the deepest demonstration that the philosophical-mystical insight at issue is *trans-traditional*, available in multiple forms, articulable in multiple vocabularies.

For your project, Ueda is important because the Eckhart-Zen comparison gives you a model for what serious cross-tradition philosophical work looks like. The repo's gathering of Western and Eastern thinkers is structurally what Ueda was doing.

### The political complication

This needs to be said clearly because it is real and recent scholarship has been much more honest about it than mid-20th-century scholarship was.

Several Kyoto School figures had concerning involvements with Imperial Japan in the 1930s and 40s. The "Overcoming Modernity" symposium (近代の超克, 1942) brought together philosophers, writers, and critics in conversation about how Japan could "overcome" the dichotomies of Western modernity through its own spiritual resources. The discussion was in part used by imperialist ideologues. The Kyoto School members had varying degrees of involvement and varying degrees of resistance — Nishida himself was actually *under surveillance for being insufficiently nationalist* toward the end of his life, but his cultural-political writings have been criticized as enabling imperialist culture-talk despite his personal moderation. Tanabe was more directly compromised; his postwar *Philosophy as Metanoetics* (1946) is partly a confession.

This matters for your project for a specific reason. When you build philosophical apparatus that engages multiple traditions, the political stakes get high. The Kyoto School's troubles offer a concrete cautionary tale: claims about "the Japanese spiritual tradition" or "the West's failure to understand X" can serve nationalist projects even when the philosophical work itself is defensible. The cultural-political diagnosis you've been alert to in the LLM and Lotman contexts applies here too. Bret Davis, John Krummel, Jason Wirth, and others have done the most recent serious work that takes this seriously rather than ignoring it; their books are where to start if you want the school presented honestly.

### What this gives the cluster

You now have, working through your readings:

**Foundational-constitutive cluster.** Peirce (logical-categorial), Whitehead (speculative-metaphysical), Cassirer (Kantian-transcendental), Humboldt (genetic-dialogical), Dōgen (immediate-thus), Nishida (topological-nothingness), Watsuji (between-being).

**Structural-typological cluster.** Lotman (cultural-semiotic), the structural-anthropological tradition, the polysystem theorists.

**Phenomenological-mystical cluster.** Bakhtin (dialogical), Buber (I-Thou), Eckhart-Ueda (selfless self), Nishitani (field of emptiness).

These are not mutually exclusive — Cassirer reads through Humboldt and Schelling toward something that Nishida articulates more clearly; Dōgen and Nishida and Nishitani converge on the topology that Western tradition reached for through Eckhart and Schelling. But each has its own analytical purchase, and using them well means deploying the right cluster for the right question.

For your specific interests:

**For LLMs:** Nishitani gives you the field-of-emptiness apparatus to ask whether LLM-outputs are *ji*-in-their-thusness or are mere statistical distillation of others' thusness. Watsuji gives you the *aida* diagnosis (the model lacks constitutive between-being). Ueda's selfless-self apparatus is interesting because the model arguably has a kind of *jiko-naki* (no-self) but it's a different kind of no-self than the realized no-self of Zen — it's the no-self of *no operative self*, not of *self that has been seen through*. Worth thinking about.

**For translation:** Watsuji's *aida* gives you the apparatus to think translation as the activity of *between-language being* — the translator is not transferring content from one language-substance to another but *occupying the aida* between language-Weltansichten in a way that articulates both. This is Humboldtian but with the explicit Buddhist grounding that makes the activity ontologically real rather than methodologically derivative.

**For your reading practice:** Nishida's late writings on the historical body (*rekishiteki shintai*, 歴史的身体) and the practice-realization unity (*shushō ittō*, 修證一等, the Dōgenian formulation Nishida adopts) give you a philosophical articulation of what reading-as-practice actually is. The historical body is the body that practices within historical-cultural conditions; practice-realization is the unity of the activity and its fruit. Reading Lotman slowly is *shushō* in the literal sense — practice and realization are not separable stages.

### What to read first

Three recommendations in order of approachability:

1. **Nishitani, *Religion and Nothingness*** — start here. Dense but the most rewarding entry point. Available in Jan Van Bragt's English translation (1982).

2. **Watsuji, *Climate and Culture* (*Fūdo*, 風土)** — short, accessible, and the *aida* apparatus is more developed in his ethics but the worldview is most readable here. Geoffrey Bownas's English translation.

## Part I, §3 — Риторика — механизм смыслопорождения (Chunk 1 of 2)

*Rhetoric as the mechanism of meaning-generation.*

### Where it sits

§3 is the chapter where Lotman states most sharply *what the mechanism of meaning-generation actually is*. §1 named the three functions and identified the creative function (function 2) as the one that needs explaining. §2 located one *condition* of meaning-generation — the structural difference between Я–ОН and Я–Я communication channels, and the role of the second code in producing semantic accretion. §3 names the *operation* itself: trope. Rhetoric is not literary decoration. It is the structural mechanism by which non-identical codes produce new meaning. Trope is what *happens* when two semiotically incommensurable systems are brought into a contextually-established relation of equivalence.

This is why the chapter opens not with rhetoric in the traditional literary sense but with **the heterogeneity of consciousness itself**. Lotman is going to claim that consciousness — individual and cultural — is *constitutively bilingual*, and that the operation of bringing the two languages into approximate equivalence *is* the mechanism of thought. Rhetoric is not a feature of literary texts; it is the operation of mind.

The forward reach is large. §3's apparatus extends through §4 (iconic rhetoric specifically), and is presupposed by §5 (text in motion, where rhetorical operations across time become the engine of cultural change), §§6–7 (symbol as the most rhetorically condensed cultural element), and Part II (semiosphere as the cultural-spatial extension of the bilingualism Lotman names here).

### The opening claim — consciousness is heterogeneous

The chapter's first sentence is the load-bearing one: *Сознание человека гетерогенно* — human consciousness is heterogeneous. Lotman expands immediately:

> A minimal thinking device must include at least two differently-arranged systems that exchange information generated within them.

The operative word is *minimal*. Not "useful to have"; not "common in human cognition." *Minimal*. Anything less than a bilingual system of mutually translating non-identical codes does not count as a thinking device at all. Consciousness, by definition, requires structural heterogeneity.

Lotman grounds the claim in two domains. First, **brain hemispheric specialization** — the research on the distinct functions of the two cerebral hemispheres reveals their deep analogy with the structure of culture as collective intellect. The right and left hemispheres operate with structurally different coding systems (the left with discrete-segmental codes producing linear-sequential texts where meaning is segment-based and additive; the right with continuous-non-discrete codes producing texts whose meaning is "smeared" across an n-dimensional semantic space). Second, **culture itself** — culture has the same bipartite structure: discrete-verbal modeling on one side and continuous-iconic modeling on the other.

Lotman is making a claim with surprising scope here, and it's worth noting because it conditions what follows. He is identifying *brain structure*, *individual consciousness*, and *cultural structure* as instantiations of *the same fundamental architecture* — minimally bilingual, minimally bipartite, minimally heterogeneous. (This was the late-Soviet semiotic horizon: Vyacheslav Vsevolodovich Ivanov's *Even and Odd*, 1978, on hemispheric asymmetry and culture; the Tartu-Moscow school treated brain-research as direct evidence for the cultural-structural thesis.) The *исомерфизм* (isomorphism) between brain and culture is not metaphor; for Lotman, it is structural fact.

Translation note: Lotman uses *размазан* (lit. "smeared") for how meaning distributes in continuous texts. The English "diffused" loses the materiality. The continuous text's meaning is *smeared across* an n-dimensional semantic space the way paint is smeared across canvas — not located at points, but extending. This will matter when we get to icon.

### The exchange between systems is translation — but asymmetric translation

The two systems do not just coexist. They *exchange texts* through *semantic translation*. But — and here is the engine of the chapter — the translation is structurally impossible in the strict sense:

> any precise translation presupposes that mutually one-to-one relations are established between units of two systems, allowing one system to be mapped onto the other... However, in the case where discrete and non-discrete texts are juxtaposed, this is *in principle impossible*.

A discrete and precisely-bounded semantic unit in one text corresponds, in the other, to a *semantic patch* (*смысловое пятно*) with blurred edges and gradual transitions into adjacent semantic regions. There is no precise correspondence. There cannot be.

This is where Lotman makes the move that defines the chapter: *under conditions of untranslatability, attempts at translation are nonetheless made — with particular stubbornness — and produce the most valuable results*. Not despite the untranslatability but *because of it*. The product is not a correct translation but an *approximate, contextually-conditioned equivalence* — an equivalence guaranteed by neither linguistic identity nor logical inference but by a shared cultural-psychological-semiotic context that makes the approximate equivalence *feel right*.

This approximate, irregular, but contextually-conditioned equivalent translation is *the substantive element of all creative thought*. The "irregular" rapprochements — *незакономерные сближения*, in Lotman's exact phrase — are what produce new semantic connections and, consequently, new texts.

### Trope as the mechanism

Now the definition that organizes the chapter:

> A pair of mutually incommensurable significant elements, between which a relation of adequacy is established within some context, forms a *semantic trope*.

Three conditions. (1) The elements must be *mutually incommensurable* — not merely different, but belonging to systems where strict translation between them is impossible. (2) A *relation of adequacy* must be established between them — not identity, not equivalence in the logical sense, but a contextually-secured "this stands for that." (3) The relation must hold *within some context* — outside the right cultural-pragmatic situation, the relation does not obtain. Trope is a *contextually-conditioned binding of incommensurables*.

From this definition Lotman draws the consequence that overturns the traditional view of rhetoric:

> Tropes are not external decoration, a kind of appliqué laid onto thought from outside — they are the substance of creative thought, and their sphere is even broader than art. They belong to creativity as such.

This is the chapter's most important claim and the one that licenses everything that follows. Rhetoric is not a department of literary studies. It is the mechanism by which any thinking system produces new meaning, regardless of whether the thinking is poetic, scientific, philosophical, religious, or everyday. *Visual analogues of abstract ideas, the use of ellipses to represent continuous processes in discrete formulas, the construction of spatial physical models of elementary particles* — all are rhetorical figures. *In science as in poetry, an irregular rapprochement often acts as an impulse for the formulation of a new regularity.*

Lotman is here naming a structural operation and showing that the same operation runs across what we usually treat as separate cognitive domains. This is methodologically continuous with the §1 move in which he treats Saussure and Jakobson as cultural-historical formations — he is collapsing the artificial boundaries between scientific and artistic thinking by showing they instantiate the same underlying structure.

### The bilingual nature of trope

The most subtle theoretical point in the chapter follows. *Whatever logical formalization of trope one offers, one of its elements has a verbal nature and the other a visual nature, however the second element may be masked.* Even in logical models of metaphor used for educational demonstration, a non-discrete image (visual or acoustic) functions as the *implied mediating link* between two discrete verbal components. The deeper the situation of untranslatability between the two languages, the sharper the need for a *common metalanguage* that throws a bridge between them.

This is why "rhetoric of figures" has historically tended toward *hypertrophy of metastructural construction* — toward elaborate taxonomies of trope-types, formal definitions, distinguishing apparatus. The taxonomic excess at the metalevel compensates for the *unavoidable indeterminacy at the level of the text*. Compensation here has a special meaning: rhetorical texts differ from ordinary linguistic texts in that the *generation of ordinary text by a native speaker is spontaneous*, while rhetorical text-generation is *learned and conscious*. The rules in rhetoric are actively included in the text itself, not merely available to the analytical observer.

This makes trope *simultaneously irrational and hyper-rational* — irrational because it equates inequivalent and even incommensurable elements, hyper-rational because it includes the conscious construction of the equivalence directly within the text of the figure. The doubled structure is what gives trope its specific cognitive power.

### The Fabri example and the deep claim about cinematic metaphor

Lotman tests this with a striking case. In Zoltán Fábri's film *Hangyaboly* (*Anthill*, 1971), set in a Hungarian women's monastery in the early twentieth century, the central drama is in complex metaphoric relation with close-ups of Baroque ornament from the chapel — particularly a relief medallion depicting the Sower. One member of the metaphor (the medallion) is *directly translatable* into the verbal text of the Gospel parable of the Sower (Matthew 13:3, Luke 8:5, Mark 4:1). The other member (the unfolding events of the film) is *not verbally translatable* but is *revealed in its relation to the first*.

This is one element of cinematic metaphor that is reducible to verbal paraphrase, paired with another element that is constitutively non-paraphrasable, and the metaphor *generates meaning by binding them*. The discrete verbal element gives the non-discrete cinematic element its semantic articulation; the non-discrete cinematic element gives the discrete verbal element its non-reducible specificity. Neither alone produces what the metaphor produces.

Then Lotman generalizes. The opposition discrete/continuous is only one — and the most extreme — form of the semantic untranslatability that produces tropes. There are many less radical incommensurabilities that still suffice to produce *"rhetoricogenic" situations* (his coinage). Even an animal's aggressive gesture, when used as substitute for actual aggression rather than as direct aggression, is symbolic; when a sexual-symbolic gesture is used to indicate dominance hierarchy and loses its sexual content, this is *metaphorical gesture* — primitive rhetoric in animal communication. Trope-formation is not specifically human; it is what mind *does* whenever it brings non-identical codes into contextual equivalence.

### The neo-rhetoric review

Lotman now does a thorough survey of twentieth-century rhetorical theory — Jakobson, Eco, Todorov, the Liège *Groupe μ*, Ruwet, Schofer and Rice. The survey is not bibliographic display. It is doing a specific job: locating his own theoretical position relative to the existing field.

**Jakobson** identified two basic tropes — metaphor and metonymy — and connected them to the two structural axes of language: paradigmatic (selection) and syntactic (combination). Metaphor is paradigmatic substitution by similarity (*in absentia*); metonymy is syntactic combination by contiguity (*in praesentia*). Jakobson generalized: poetry tends to metaphor, prose to metonymy, and "metaphor for poetry, metonymy for prose constitutes the line of least resistance." Jakobson universalized rhetoric beyond literature (applying it to film, painting, psychoanalysis) but narrowed it within literature (assigning specific tropes to specific genres). Lotman accepts the universalization and complicates the narrowing.

**Eco** treats metonymy as the foundational figure, building from chains of associative contiguities (in code-structure, context-structure, and referent-structure), with metaphor constructed atop metonymy via the connection between linguistic and cultural codes.

**Todorov and the Liège Group μ** offer *synecdoche* as the primary figure, with metaphor and metonymy as derivative complications. Their detailed taxonomic classification is based on analysis of *semes* and semantic-lexical components.

**Ruwet** criticizes the Group μ from a linguistic perspective, raising the question that Lotman finds well-formed: *under what conditions does a given linguistic expression acquire transferred meaning?* This is the right question, and Lotman will answer it in his own register.

The neo-rhetorical synthesis Lotman extracts: a trope is a *semantic transposition from a sign in praesentia to a sign in absentia*, based on perception of a connection between semantic features, marked by semantic incompatibility of micro- and macro-contexts, conditioned by referential relations of similarity, causality, inclusion, or opposition.

### The typological-functional move — Lotman's contribution

But Lotman's own contribution comes when he shifts from the *logical* question (what is a trope?) to the *typological-functional* question: *how do tropes work in the text? what is their purpose in the meaning-mechanism of speech?* This is where his apparatus does work the European neo-rhetoric does not.

He starts with a striking observation. There are *whole cultural epochs oriented toward tropes*, in which trope becomes an obligatory feature of artistic speech (and in extreme cases of speech in general): mythopoetic period, medieval, Baroque, Romantic, Symbolist, Avant-garde. There are *other epochs in which artistic significance is precisely the refusal of rhetorical figures*, where speech must reproduce the norms of non-artistic speech to count as artistic.

Generalizing across these trope-oriented epochs, Lotman identifies what unites them: in all of them, the substitution operation is performed not on *similar* elements but on *incompatible* ones. The substituting and substituted are not adequate by some semantic parameter; they are *opposite in property* — they belong to incommensurable systems. The trope operates by *collage* — pasted-on elements meeting painted elements, the realistic and the imagined meeting in the same frame, the two-dimensional and the three-dimensional, the sign and the non-sign. In many traditional cultural contexts, the meeting of such incompatible elements within a single text is *absolutely forbidden*. And it is precisely *because* they are forbidden that their forced combination produces the strong semantic effect characteristic of trope.

The deep claim: trope works *not by shared semes* (the more shared semes, the *less* effective the trope; tautological identity makes trope impossible) but by *embedding in incompatible semantic spaces* and the *degree of semantic distance* between the incommensurable elements. The semantic distance can be of many kinds — one/many-dimensional, discrete/continuous, material/immaterial, earthly/transcendent — but in every case, the boundaries of substituted and substituting are so incommensurable that the task of establishing correspondence becomes *irrational*. The correspondence becomes conventional, approximate, presumed; it does not produce simple semantic displacement but a *fundamentally new and paradoxical semantic situation*.

This is consonant with Lotman's broader cultural-typological claim: cultures that gravitate typologically toward tropes are cultures whose worldview is grounded in *antinomy and irrational contradiction*. Not relativism. Not mere paradox-tolerance. A worldview in which the deepest truth is graspable only through the structural binding of incommensurables — which is what trope, structurally, is.

### The structural correspondence

Hoffmeyer's code-duality and Lotman's heterogeneous consciousness are *the same architectural claim* in different vocabularies. Set them side by side:

Strict one-to-one translation between them is impossible; the relation is mediated by an entire developmental-interactional process. The ongoing asymmetric translation between the two codes *is* what life is — what makes a system semiotic, adaptive, capable of novelty, and able to endure across time. Either code alone is inert: digital without analog is DNA in a test tube; analog without digital is a body without inheritance.

**Lotman:** Thinking systems operate through two structurally distinct codes — a *discrete-segmental* code (linear, sequential, with meaning at the segment level: paradigm is verbal language) and a *continuous-iconic* code (n-dimensional, holistic, with meaning "smeared" across semantic space: paradigm is image, ritual, dream). Strict one-to-one translation between them is impossible; discrete units correspond to "semantic patches" with blurred edges in the continuous. These are not analogous structures. They are *the same structure*, identified independently by two thinkers who came to it from opposite directions — Hoffmeyer from biology toward semiotics, Lotman from cultural-structural analysis toward brain-research. The convergence is a substantive theoretical point: semiotic activity, wherever it occurs, has bilingual code-dual structure as its constitutive minimal architecture.

The biosemiotic tradition (Hoffmeyer, Emmeche, Sebeok, Deely) was strongly influenced by the Tartu-Moscow school's framework — particularly Lotman's semiosphere concept and the broader idea of culture as a system of secondary modeling systems built on natural language. So when you find code-duality in Hoffmeyer and bilingual-heterogeneous consciousness in Lotman, you are looking at one theoretical insight as it crosses between biological and cultural levels of analysis, mediated by the Tartu intellectual community. The convergence is real, the lineage is direct, and the synthesis has been actively developed for thirty years.

### Where they correspond exactly

Five structural correspondences worth naming.

**Discrete vs. continuous codes.** Lotman's discrete-segmental / continuous-iconic distinction maps onto Hoffmeyer's digital / analog distinction with no remainder. Both: one code has segmental linear structure with meaning at the segment level; the other has holistic-distributed structure with meaning at the whole-pattern level.

**Untranslatability between them.** Both insist that the two codes cannot be mapped one-to-one. Lotman: discrete units correspond to *смысловое пятно* (semantic patches) with blurred edges, not to discrete points. Hoffmeyer: there is no algorithm that converts genotype to phenotype; the translation requires the entire developmental process in an environment. **Productive translation as engine of novelty.** Both identify the asymmetric translation between codes as what *generates* new meaning rather than what merely transmits old meaning. Lotman's *приращение смысла* (accretion of meaning) and Hoffmeyer's evolutionary novelty are structurally the same phenomenon — what irregularly-paired non-identical codes produce when forced into contextual equivalence.

**Minimum-architecture claim.** Both make this a *minimum* claim, not a contingent feature. Lotman: a minimal thinking device must include at least two heterogeneous systems. Hoffmeyer: a minimal living system must operate code-duality. Anything less than bilingual non-identical codes does not constitute a thinking system or a living system. Both are claiming the same structural pattern recurs at multiple scales, with the bilingual code-duality as the unifying motif.

### Where they differ — and what the difference yields

The differences are real and worth marking because they sharpen what each contributes.

**Lotman's continuous code is iconic; Hoffmeyer's analog code is phenotypic-embodied.** This is the substantive divergence. For Lotman, the continuous code is visual-iconic-imagistic — paintings, dreams, ritual gesture, architectural space. For Hoffmeyer, the analog code is *whole-organism-in-environment* — it is *embodied*, *situated*, *interacting*. This matters. It means Hoffmeyer's framework has *world-coupling* built in that Lotman's does not. The analog phenotype is not a representation of an environment; it is a body *in* an environment, in causal-interactional contact with that environment. The digital-analog cycle is therefore grounded in the real material processes of life. ### The icon/relic example — bilingualism in Byzantine semiotics

Lotman tests the apparatus on a striking case from medieval Christian semiotics. The icon and the relic are both sacred objects in the Byzantine and Russian Orthodox traditions, but they operate by structurally opposite rhetorical mechanisms.

**The icon is metaphor.** Following the theological line from Origen through Gregory of Nyssa and Pseudo-Dionysius, the icon presents itself as *a material and expressed sign of the immaterial and inexpressible essence of divinity*. Painted images on iconboards are not portraits in the modern sense; they are visible-material articulations of what is constitutively beyond the visible and material. Clement of Alexandria connected the visible directly to the verbal — *for one must always grasp not the words, but That which they signify*. The icon's metaphoric structure binds a material visible element with an immaterial transcendent essence across the deepest possible incommensurability — that of the discrete-material with the continuous-divine. The semantic relation is one of *non-equivalence and non-univocity*; rationalist mutual substitution in either direction is excluded.

The icon's rhetorical character has further structural features. Not just any image can serve as the first member of the metaphor — only one painted in conformity with the established canon (composition, color, gesture). The icon arises at the meeting of two energies: *the energy of the divine Logos seeking to articulate itself* and *the energy of the human seeking ascending knowledge*. The icon is therefore not painted but rather *worthy* — the painter must live in conditions of strict and righteous life, prayer, fasting, spiritual ascent. (Lotman notes here that when Gogol applied exactly such requirements to the artist's life — in the second redaction of "The Portrait" and in *Selected Passages from Correspondence with Friends* — his entire creative work acquired in his own eyes the character of *a grandiose metaphor*.)

**The relic is metonymy.** The relic is part of the body of a saint or an object that was in direct contact with the saint. The material-corporeal aspect of the saint is replaced by a corporeal part of him or by a material object connected to him. Substitution by contiguity: the part for the whole, the contact-trace for the contacted person. This appears semantically simple compared with the icon's complexity — but Lotman warns against this surface reading. The very concept of "the body of a saint" already contains the metaphor of incarnation and a complex, irrational relation between expression and content. Even metonymy in this register is rhetorically loaded.

This example is doing important work. Lotman is showing that *the structural opposition between metaphor and metonymy is not formal-logical but cultural-religious-historical*. The same theological tradition produces both, and they coexist in the same religious culture as structurally complementary modes of relating the material to the transcendent. To understand them, one cannot just classify them logically; one must read them within their cultural-rhetorical context.

### Baroque, Romantic, Avant-garde — three more rhetorical formations

Lotman extends the analysis through three more historical formations, each demonstrating that trope is not literary decoration but *the principle of organizing a particular kind of consciousness*.

**Baroque.** The boundaries between trope-types become unusually fluid in Baroque texts. Lope de Vega calls Marino "the great painter for the ear" and Rubens "the great poet for sight." Tesauro calls architecture "metaphor in stone" (*metafora di pietra*). In *The Aristotelian Telescope*, Tesauro develops a doctrine of Metaphor as the universal principle of both human and divine consciousness, grounded in *acutezza* — wit, the thinking that brings together the dissimilar and joins the unjoinable. The metaphoric consciousness is identified with the creative, and even the divine creative act is conceived by Tesauro as a higher Wit that creates the world by means of metaphors, analogies, and *concetti*. *Tesauro objects to those who see in rhetorical figures merely external decoration — for him, they constitute the very basis of the mechanism of thought.* This is the Baroque's distinctive contribution: not just the use of trope, but the metaphysical claim that trope is the fundamental form of cognition.

**Romantic.** A similar but distinct formation. Metaphor and metonymy tend toward diffuse merger. The Romantic project of organic synthesis — fusing what life has divided and made unjoinable — combined with the Romantic conviction that the essence of life is inexpressible by any single language (verbal language or any single artistic language) — produces metaphoric and metonymic recoding of signs across multiple semiotic systems. Wackenroder, in *Heart-Outpourings of an Art-Loving Friar*, identifies the language of symbols, emblems, and metaphors with art *as such*: *art is a language of an entirely different kind from nature, but with the same wondrous power over the human heart, achieved through similarly dark and secret paths*.

**Avant-garde.** The principle of *juxtaposition* (*соположение*). Figures formed this way can typically be read as metaphor *or* as metonymy interchangeably. What is essential: the meaning-generating principle of the text as a whole becomes the juxtaposition of *fundamentally non-juxtaposable segments*. Their mutual recoding produces a language of multiple readings, which reveals unexpected reserves of meaning.

### The summary thesis on trope

Lotman now states the chapter's central thesis explicitly:

> A trope is not decoration belonging only to the sphere of expression, an ornamentation of some invariant content; it is a mechanism for constructing some content not constructible within a single language. A trope is a figure born at the seam between two languages, and in this respect it is *isostructural with the mechanism of creative consciousness as such*.

This is the place to mark how distinctive the claim is. Lotman is not just saying tropes are useful, or tropes are widespread, or tropes have cognitive functions. He is saying tropes are *isostructural with creative consciousness as such* — they have the same structure as the operation of mind itself when mind is generating new meaning. The trope is not a feature of texts; it is the *operational form* of the mind's bilingual code-translation activity articulated in textual material. This is the strongest version of the heterogeneity-of-consciousness thesis from the chapter's opening.

The corollary: any logical definition of rhetorical figures that ignores their *bilingual nature* belongs to the metalanguage of theoretical description, but does not constitute a generative mechanism for trope-production. Logically classifying tropes is fine; pretending the classification *explains* trope-formation, or could *generate* tropes, mistakes the descriptive apparatus for the productive one. The productive apparatus is the bilingual operation, and that operation is not reducible to logical formalization.

### Metarhetoric and the typology of cultures

The chapter then makes a typological move. Trope manifests at the surface of culture in *systems oriented toward complexity, ambiguity, or the inexpressibility of truth*. But "rhetoricism" is not the property of any specific cultural epoch; it is a *cultural universal*, paired with its opposite "anti-rhetoricism." Both poles are interrelated, and the semiotic activity of one entails the activation of the other.

**Minus-rhetoric.** Here Lotman makes one of his most subtle moves. In a culture where rhetorical density has become tradition and the inertia of reader-expectation, trope enters the neutral fund of language and ceases to be perceived as rhetorically active. Against that background, an *anti-rhetorical text* — composed of elements with direct, non-transferred semantics — begins to be perceived as a *meta-trope, a rhetorical figure that has undergone secondary simplification, with the second language reduced to zero*. This "minus-rhetoric," subjectively experienced as approach to reality and simplicity, is in fact the *mirror image* of rhetoric and incorporates its aesthetic opponent into its own cultural-semiotic code.

The unsentimental beauty of neorealist film, the deliberate plainness of late nineteenth-century realism — these *appear* to be refusals of rhetoric, but they are themselves rhetorical operations of a higher order. They achieve their effect *against the background* of an exhausted prior rhetoric. Fellini's cinematic Baroque rehabilitates rhetoric explicitly; the neorealism that preceded it had been functioning as minus-rhetoric all along.

This minus-rhetoric move is methodologically significant for your project specifically. Anytime someone claims to have escaped rhetoric, or to be doing direct unmediated transmission, Lotman's apparatus suggests they are simply doing rhetoric of a different order against a different background. There is no escape from the bilingual structure; there is only the choice of which background one operates against.

### Science as rhetoric — metametaphor and metametonymy

The chapter's most striking generalization. *Rhetorical thinking is not opposed to scientific thinking as artistic to non-artistic.* Rhetoric is *constitutive of scientific creativity* — specifically of its first phase, the "Faustian" phase of generating new ideas, postulating unexpected hypotheses, drawing analogies between previously separated domains.

Lotman gives technical examples. Cases of *isomorphism, homomorphism, gnoseomorphism* (including epi-, endo-, mono-, and automorphisms) function as *metametaphors* — operations that establish analogies and equivalences across apparently distant phenomena. **Cantor's theorem** — that if a segment contains aleph (ℵ) points, then any part of that segment also contains aleph points, and in this sense any part equals the whole — is a paradigm *metametonymy*. **Transfinite induction** is a metametonymic operation. *Creative thought, in both science and art, has analogical nature and is built on a fundamentally identical basis: the bringing-together of objects and concepts that, outside the rhetorical situation, do not admit of being brought together.*

The implication: the construction of *metarhetoric* — the systematic study of the bilingual operations underlying creative thought — *becomes a general scientific task, and metarhetoric itself can be defined as a theory of creative thinking*.

This is one of the most ambitious claims in the book. Lotman is asserting that what looks like a literary-theoretical apparatus (the theory of trope) is actually the foundation of a general theory of creative cognition. The same operation underlies the mathematician's generalizing intuition, the physicist's discovery of an unexpected isomorphism, the poet's metaphor, the composer's structural innovation, the religious thinker's apprehension of transcendence in the material. All are bilingual operations binding incommensurable codes. The unity is not a metaphor; it is structural identity at the level of the meaning-generating mechanism.

### The rhetoric of the text-as-whole

The chapter's third major movement turns from the rhetoric of *figures within texts* to the rhetoric of *texts as wholes*.

When we move from the single rhetorical figure to the text as a closed, self-contained, indivisibly-meaningful semiotic formation, the relation of its elements to rhetoric *changes radically*. If the whole text is encoded in the cultural system as rhetorical, *every* element of it becomes rhetorical regardless of whether, in isolation, the element has direct or transferred meaning. Since artistic text functions in our consciousness *as rhetorically organized*, *any* title of an artistic work functions as trope or minus-trope; it is rhetorically marked by the very fact of being a title.

The corollary: *categories of "beginning" and "end" become especially rhetorically marked* in textual structure. The internal connectedness of the text's elements sharply reduces the autonomy of its constituent units and increases the *coefficient of textual connectedness*. The text strives to become *one big word with a single integrated meaning*. This secondary "word" — when the text is artistic — is always a trope: the artistic text *as a whole* operates in semiotic space with greater dimensionality than ordinary non-artistic speech.

The transformation is illustrated by examples like *script → film* or *libretto → opera*. A text with a certain number of coordinates of semantic space is converted into one whose semiotic-spatial dimensionality sharply increases. The same happens when a verbal non-artistic text becomes an artistic one. *Therefore, between the elements as well as between the wholes of artistic and non-artistic texts, no univocal correspondence is possible, and consequently no one-to-one translation is possible. Only conditional equivalence and various types of analogy are possible. And this is precisely what constitutes the essence of rhetorical relations.*

### The Byzantine-Russian cultural hierarchy as rhetorical pyramid

Lotman now scales up to the level of culture itself. In rhetorical-oriented cultures, *each step in the ascending hierarchy of semiotic organization yields an increase in dimensionality of meaning-structural space*. The Byzantine and Old Russian cultural hierarchy is the example:

> world of everyday life and unbookish speech → world of secular art → world of ecclesiastical art → divine liturgy → transcendental divine Light

This is a chain of *continuous irrational complexification*. First, the transition from the non-sign world of things to the system of signs and social languages. Then the union of signs from different languages, untranslatable into any one of them separately — the union of word and chant, of bookish text and miniature, of words and song and wall-painting and natural and artificial light and the smells of incense in temple-action; the union of architecture and landscape. Finally, the union of art with transcendental divine Truth.

Each step in the hierarchy is *inexpressible by means of the preceding step, which represents only its image (incomplete presence)*. The principle of rhetorical organization is the structural foundation of the culture as such, transforming each new step into a *semiotic mystery* (*семиотическое таинство*) for the steps below.

This hierarchical-rhetorical principle can also operate on a purely secular basis. *For Paul I, the parade was a metaphor of Order and Power as much as for Napoleon a battle was a metonymy of Glory.* Cultural hierarchies of authority and rank are rhetorical pyramids in exactly Lotman's structural sense.

### Rhetorical structure as imposed from outside

A theoretical clarification follows that is methodologically load-bearing. In contemporary linguistics, "grammar of text" research had attempted to derive textual unity from internal linguistic mechanisms (lexical repetitions, intonational ties). Boris Gasparov criticized this and proposed obligatory grammatical relations connecting segments of speech at the supra-phrasal level, with the immanent grammatical structure of the sentence imposing predetermined constraints on what phrases can follow it.

Lotman positions himself relative to two approaches. One holds that rhetorical structure follows automatically from linguistic laws and is just their realization at the level of integral text construction. The other holds that there is a *fundamental difference* between linguistic and rhetorical unity of text — that rhetorical structure is *not derivable* from linguistic structure but represents a *decisive re-thinking* of it (shifts in linguistic relations occur, optional structures are raised in rank to obligatory, and so on). *Rhetorical structure is brought into the verbal text from outside; it is its additional ordering.*

Lotman holds the second view. He goes further: rhetorical structure is *subjectively experienced as foreign* to the text's own structural principles. This is why a fragment of non-artistic text included sharply in an artistic one (newsreel footage in a feature film) carries rhetorical weight — *because it is recognized by the audience as foreign, irregular, an external intrusion*. Conversely, an artistic insertion in newsreel material plays the same role from the other direction. Rhetorical organization arises in the *field of semantic tension between "organic" and "foreign" structures*, with the elements of the rhetorical structure subject to double interpretation through this relation.

The Pushkin epigraph example is delicious: the second chapter of *Eugene Onegin* opens with "О rus!… О Русь!" — a punning-homonymic combination of Horace's "O countryside!" (Satires, Book 2, Satire 6) and the Russian "O Russia!" The foreign-language fragment that can also be read as native-language text exhibits the structural mechanism: an inserted element belongs to neither structure exclusively and to both partially, generating rhetorical effect by occupying the seam. Stendhal's *Henri Brulard* gives a similar example with the cry "О Кл, диапдо еро ?е азреат!" misheard as French.

### Stylistics versus rhetoric

Now the chapter introduces a precise theoretical distinction. *Stylistics* is constituted by two oppositions: opposition to semantics, and opposition to rhetoric.

The first opposition (stylistics vs. semantics): every semiotic system has hierarchical structure, and the semantic field of language breaks into closed-in-themselves spaces between which a relation of *similarity* obtains. The same melody can be played in different organ registers; the melody is preserved, but the register-color changes. The shared semantic value is comparable to the *semantic*; the register-specific addition is the *stylistic*. Stylistic effect arises when the same semantic content is expressed in two different ways and each way activates the memory of a particular closed and hierarchically-related sign-group.

The second opposition (stylistics vs. rhetoric): *stylistic effect arises within a single hierarchical subsystem*; *rhetorical effect arises at the collision of signs belonging to different registers*. Stylistic consciousness operates from the absolute character of the hierarchical boundaries it constitutes. Rhetorical consciousness operates from their *relativity* — the boundaries become objects of play. Rhetoric crosses register-boundaries; stylistics works within them.

A literary period oriented toward rhetoric crosses registers freely and is perceived as "complex." A period oriented toward stylistics works within registers and is perceived as "simple." The aesthetic ideal of "simplicity" is connected with *prohibition on rhetorical constructions and heightened attention to stylistic ones*.

### The historical-evolutionary pattern

The chapter's final movement establishes a striking historical-evolutionary regularity. Within a major historical period, rhetorical orientations typically *precede* stylistic ones that replace them. This pattern was noted by Dmitri Likhachev. It corresponds to a feature of individual poetic development noted by Boris Pasternak:

> The aim of poetic development is, at the path's end, to *fall*
> ‹...› as if into heresy,
> Into unheard-of simplicity.

(*В неслыханную простоту*.)

Pasternak's formulation captures the trajectory: from complexity of style at the start of a creative path to "classical" simplicity at the end. The same pattern recurs at the level of literary periods: *from Romanticism to Realism, from Rococo to Classicism, from Avant-garde to Neo-classicism*. All fit the formula: *transition from rhetorical orientation to stylistic orientation*.

The Pushkin example exhibits this perfectly. In *Ruslan and Ludmila*, contemporaries saw stylistic motley — combinations of differently-styled reminiscences from different literary traditions; this is rhetoric, cross-register collision. In *Eugene Onegin*, whose style is in fact *exceptionally citationally complex* — saturated with allusions, references, and reminiscences — *the reader sees only the casualness of simple authorial speech*. The reader's perception of "simplicity" is achieved precisely through the *invisible* internalization of the stylistic regime; the rhetorical work has been absorbed into the stylistic frame.

The meaning of the evolution: it is the search for an *individual poetic language*. At the first stage, this language is constituted as the *cancellation of existing poetic dialects*. A new language-space is delineated, within which language-units that had never previously belonged to a common whole and were perceived as incompatible are now combined. Naturally, the specificity of each is heightened; the rhetorical effect dominates. But if the artist is significant, *the artist establishes this language as unified in the reader's perception*. Thereafter, working within this now-culturally-established new language, the poet converts it into a *stylistic register*. The compatibility of elements becomes natural, even neutral, while the boundary separating this poet's style from the surrounding literary environment becomes sharply marked.

### The four-quadrant schema

The chapter closes with the schema:

```
 semantic stylistic
 ↔
 semantic stylistic
 rhetoric rhetoric
```

— a four-quadrant classification crossing semantic/stylistic with semantic-rhetorical/stylistic-rhetorical. Possible shifts toward dominance of any of these elements yield various combinations of more fundamental historico-semiotic categories like "Romanticism," "Classicism," and so on. In real texts, additional tension between textual and metatextual (coding) levels operates, doubling the schema.

This is Lotman's apparatus for typologizing cultural-aesthetic periods. Each period is characterized by which quadrant dominates and what relations obtain between the quadrants. The apparatus is offered not as a closed taxonomy but as a productive frame for analyzing actual historical formations.

### What §3 has done

Stepping back: this chapter has done four major things.

First, it has made the **heterogeneity-of-consciousness claim** load-bearing for the entire apparatus. Mind is constitutively bilingual; thinking is constitutively translation between non-identical codes; rhetoric is the structural form of that translation. This is the chapter's foundational move.

Second, it has provided the **theory of trope as the bilingual mechanism** — the operation by which incommensurable elements are bound in contextual equivalence to produce new meaning. This is the apparatus.

Third, it has made the **scientific-creative analogy** explicit — metarhetoric is a theory of creative thinking as such, applicable to scientific innovation as much as to artistic creation. This is the generalization that pushes the apparatus beyond literary studies.

Fourth, it has developed the **typological-cultural extension** — different cultures and historical periods are differently rhetorically organized, with the rhetoric/stylistics oscillation as one major pattern of historical change. This connects the structural apparatus to the cultural-historical typology that organizes the rest of the book.

### The deep correspondence

The deepest structural identity is between **Lotman's trope as bilingual mechanism** and **Jung's transcendent function**. Set them side by side and they are the same operation in different vocabularies.

**Jung's transcendent function** is the operation by which two heterogeneous psychic systems — typically conscious and unconscious, but also any pair of held opposites — are brought into productive tension whose holding produces a *third* that was not there before. The third is typically a *symbol* in Jung's strict technical sense: not a sign reducible to its referent, but the best possible expression of something not yet (or never) fully knowable. The symbol carries the binding of the opposed terms while not collapsing either. The transcendent function is what makes psychic development possible; without it, the psyche either dissociates (the opposites split off and one is repressed) or stagnates (no new configuration can emerge). Individuation as a process *is* the repeated application of the transcendent function over a life — the gradual integration of the personality through the symbolic binding of psychic incommensurables.

**Lotman's trope as bilingual mechanism** is the operation by which two non-identical codes are brought into contextual equivalence whose holding produces *приращение смысла* — accretion of meaning that was not present in either code alone. The trope binds discrete and continuous, verbal and iconic, rational and irrational without dissolving either into the other. The trope is what makes meaning-generation possible; without it, the system either becomes pure transmission (function 1, no novelty) or breaks down. Cultural creativity *is* the repeated production of trope at all scales, from individual figures within texts to the cultural-rhetorical pyramid as a whole.

These are not analogous. They are *structurally identical operations* identified independently by two thinkers working from very different empirical materials and theoretical traditions. Both name the binding-of-opposites that produces a *third* as the constitutive operation of meaning-making.

### More specific resonances from §3

**The sign / symbol distinction.** Jung's most foundational technical move — and one of the parts of his work that holds up philosophically — is the distinction between sign and symbol. A sign is reducible: a stop sign means "stop"; nothing more is at stake. A symbol is *the best possible expression of something that exceeds it*, that points beyond itself toward a content the symbol participates in without exhausting. Symbols are *productively inexhaustible*; signs are functionally finite. This distinction maps with eerie precision onto Lotman's distinction between *dead trope* (where the metaphoric relation has settled into one-to-one substitution and the figure has become merely a phraseologism) and *live trope* (where the irreducible bilingual tension is preserved). Lotman's chapter on Byzantine icon and relic is doing exactly the work Jung's symbol theory does: showing that the icon is *not reducible* to either its material instantiation or to its transcendent referent — it is the best possible expression of something neither code can hold alone.

**Tesauro's metaphor as universal principle of consciousness.** When Lotman quotes Tesauro saying that *metaphor is the basis of both human and divine consciousness, with even the divine creative act being a higher Wit creating the world by metaphors, analogies, and concetti* — this is something Jung would have recognized as articulating exactly his own view. Jung argued throughout his work that the symbol is the way the deeper psychic strata speak to consciousness, and that creativity in the strong sense is the production of symbols through which the unconscious manifests. Tesauro's *acutezza* is structurally Jung's *symbolic function*. Both treat the binding of dissimilars not as decorative cleverness but as the *fundamental form of cognition* — the operation by which mind articulates itself.

**The Byzantine cultural-rhetorical pyramid.** Lotman's analysis of the hierarchy from everyday speech up through liturgy to transcendent divine Light, with each step inexpressible by the preceding and becoming a "semiotic mystery" for what lies below — this is structurally what Jung analyzed as the *layered psyche* in his accounts of Christian symbolism (especially in *Aion*, *Psychology and Religion*, *Mysterium Coniunctionis*). The mass, for Jung, was a *dramatic rhetorical pyramid* in something like Lotman's sense — the everyday material elements (bread, wine, speech) bound through the liturgical operation into incommensurable equivalence with the transcendent. Each level of the rite operates in a register the previous level cannot articulate; the binding across levels is what generates the religious effect. Jung and Lotman would have analyzed the Mass with strikingly similar structural apparatus, though Jung would push deeper into the psychological-archetypal dimension while Lotman stays at the cultural-structural level.

**Rhetorical structure as imposed from outside.** Lotman's claim that rhetorical organization is *brought into the text from outside* and is *subjectively experienced as foreign to the text's own structural principles* — this is structurally Jung's account of how archetypal contents enter consciousness. Archetypal manifestations *feel numinous, foreign, more-than-personal* — they have a quality of being intrusive in the way Lotman describes for rhetorical insertions. The newsreel footage in the feature film, the foreign-language phrase in the native context — these are functioning structurally the way Jung's archetypal manifestations function in dreams and active imagination. They mark a *seam* between systems where the otherwise-immanent flow is interrupted by something that does not belong, and the interruption is generative rather than destructive.

### What Jung adds that the others don't

The cluster you've been building — Peirce, Whitehead, Cassirer, Humboldt, Lotman, Hoffmeyer, Dōgen, the Kyoto School — is unusually well-equipped for thinking about meaning, sign, culture, and the constitutive structure of cognition. What Jung adds is two things none of the others quite provides.

**A worked-out theory of the depths.** None of the others has a developed account of *psychic strata with their own dynamics independent of consciousness*. Cassirer and Langer come close in their treatments of myth and feeling, but they do not theorize an unconscious in Jung's strong sense. Lotman explicitly brackets it. The biosemioticians stay at the cellular-organismic level. Whitehead's prehensions are below conscious experience but are not "unconscious" in the depth-psychological sense. Jung's apparatus — collective unconscious, archetypes, the shadow, the contra-sexual figures, the Self — is a worked-out phenomenology and theory of the depths that no other thinker in the cluster offers.

Whether you want this depends on your project. If you are interested in why texts and symbols *grip* us in the way they do, in why some images recur across cultures, in why dreams and active imagination produce the material they produce, in why specific symbols become culturally durable while others fade — Jung gives you apparatus the others don't. If you are working at the structural-cultural level without needing depth-psychological grounding, you may not need it.

**A worked-out hermeneutic for symbols.** Jung's method of *amplification* — bringing comparative material from religion, mythology, alchemy, ritual to bear on a single image to discover what semantic field it operates within — is a developed interpretive technique that has direct application to cultural-textual analysis. It is structurally what good cultural-historical reading already does, but Jung articulated it as a method. Lotman's structural typology gives you the *map* of where symbols operate culturally; Jung's amplification gives you the *practice* of unpacking what specific symbols carry.

### Honest complications

A few things worth saying directly about Jung that the cluster of more academically respectable thinkers can obscure.

**Empirical warrant differs.** Jung worked from clinical material (dreams, fantasies, symptoms) plus extensive scholarly engagement with religion, mythology, and alchemy. The clinical material is irreducibly first-person and interpretive in ways that make some philosophers and scientists uncomfortable. The scholarly material is sometimes uneven — his alchemical work is brilliant but contested. He is not in the same evidential register as Peirce or Whitehead.

**Metaphysical commitments are substantial.** Jung made strong claims about the collective unconscious as a real psychic substrate, archetypes as universal patterns inherited rather than learned, synchronicity as a non-causal connecting principle that operates alongside causality. These are metaphysical claims that some find indispensable and others find unsupported. Lotman is more methodologically modest; Jung is not.

**The political-biographical complications.** Jung's positions in the 1930s, especially his acceptance of the presidency of the General Medical Society for Psychotherapy under Nazi conditions and some of his published statements about "Aryan" and "Semitic" psychology, are real and have been the subject of serious scholarly attention. The most careful work on this (Andrew Samuels, Aniela Jaffé's confrontations, Sonu Shamdasani's biographical work) is honest about it without either dismissing Jung wholesale or excusing what shouldn't be excused. Worth knowing about; not a reason to ignore the work, but a reason to read it with eyes open.

**The Jung that's been received culturally is often a softened-and-spiritualized Jung that loses what's most rigorous in the work.** The serious Jung is in the *Collected Works* — especially Volume 8 (*Structure and Dynamics of the Psyche*), Volume 9 (*Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious*; *Aion*), Volume 12 (*Psychology and Alchemy*), and Volume 14 (*Mysterium Coniunctionis*). The *Red Book* (the *Liber Novus*, finally published in 2009 in Sonu Shamdasani's edition) is the visionary-experiential text from which the apparatus emerged. Pop Jungianism and Joseph Campbell-style hero's-journey reception are downstream and substantially watered down.

**Synchronicity as code-dual phenomenon.** Jung's most controversial concept names what he saw as a non-causal connecting principle — the meaningful coincidence between an internal psychic state and an external event whose conjunction produces meaning beyond what either alone would carry. ## Part I, §4 — Иконическая риторика

*Iconic Rhetoric.*

### Where it sits

§3 worked the rhetorical apparatus out at length but conducted most of its analysis on the verbal-discrete side, treating the iconic-continuous side mainly as the *other pole* in the bilingual binding. §4 redresses this. Its job is to show that the same bilingual-mechanism Lotman developed for verbal trope operates equally on the iconic side — that pictures, sculptures, films, theatrical performances, and ritualized behaviors can be rhetorically organized in their own right, without the rhetoric being parasitic on accompanying verbal material. The chapter is shorter and tighter than §3, organized around two related theses: that *the ontological precondition of any sign-system is doubling*, and that *iconic rhetoric arises through "intermediate coding"* — the passage of a real-world referent through one art-form on its way to being represented in another.

The forward reach is into Part II's account of the semiosphere as a structured space of mutually translating semiotic languages, and into §§5–7 where text-in-motion and the symbol both depend on the cross-language rhetorical operations §4 isolates here.

### The doubling thesis — ontological precondition of the sign

The chapter opens with a claim about the *ontological precondition* of any semiotic activity: the possibility of *doubling* (*удвоение*). The ancient legends about rhyme being born from echo, drawing being born from a traced shadow, are not poetic fancy — they are mythological articulations of a structural truth. Equally, the magical functions attributed across cultures to mirrors, casts, footprints, and other doubling-objects exhibit the same structural insight.

Lotman's specific argument: the *reflected image* of an object is *severed from the practical contexts* (spatial, contextual, purposive) that bind the object in everyday life. A reflected face cannot be touched or caressed — but it *can* be insulted, used in magical operations, included in the *modeling connections* of consciousness. The reflected image is structurally homologous with casts and prints (footprints, handprints) — what makes archaic cultures' magical operations on footprints intelligible is not the "diffuseness of archaic thinking" that supposedly fails to distinguish part from whole. *It is precisely that the footprint is and is not the person* — that it is *severed from ordinary practical connections* — that *provokes its inclusion in semiotic situations*.

This is a sharp anti-primitivist move. Lotman is rejecting the standard ethnographic explanation (archaic minds are confused; they conflate part with whole) and substituting a structural one: the doubling-trace's *very ontological ambiguity* is what makes it semiotically productive. The same structural feature operates in modern semiotic activity. There is no break between "magical" and "modern" doubling; both rely on the same operation by which an object is severed from practical embedding and made available to modeling activity.

The implication: doubling is the *ontological precondition* of converting the world of objects into the world of signs. Without doubling, no semiosis. With it, the entire apparatus of culture becomes possible.

### Double doubling — the mirror in painting

In simple doubling, however, the semiotic situation remains *concealed as pure possibility*. For naive consciousness, the operation is invisible; doubling proceeds spontaneously without self-awareness of its own semiotic character. The situation changes when *double doubling* (*удвоение удвоения*) occurs — when the doubled-object is itself doubled, producing a structurally-marked seam that *makes the semiotic mechanism visible*.

Lotman demonstrates this through one of the most beautiful sustained analyses in Part I — the function of the mirror within painting at certain moments of European art history.

**Velázquez, *Venus at her Mirror*.** In the framework of accepted Renaissance perspective with its constant viewpoint, the mirror allows Velázquez to show the central figure (Venus) *simultaneously from two viewpoints*: the viewer sees her from behind, and in the mirror sees her face. The viewpoint becomes an independent structural element, separable from the object given to naive contemplation, and presentable as a self-aware autonomous entity.

**Van Eyck, *Portrait of the Arnolfinis*.** The mirror at the painting's rear shows the central figures from behind while the painted figures show them en face. But the effect is more complex: the mirror's surface is *spherical*, and the spherical curvature deforms what it reflects, drawing attention to the *specificity of reflection itself*. It becomes evident that *every reflection is simultaneously a shift, a deformation* — sharpening certain aspects of the object on one hand, *revealing the structural nature of the language* into whose space the object is projected on the other. The spherical roundness of the mirror underlines the planar rectangularity of the merchant and his wife — they appear as if drawn on flat glass placed within an illusorily three-dimensional room. The reflective system is oriented perpendicularly to the canvas plane and *exits beyond it*.

Lotman compares the resulting effect to one Jan Mukařovský identified in cinema: cases where the sound space exits the screen space and acquires great volume. The example: a carriage filmed from where the horses would be, so that the horses are off-screen along the perpendicular axis, with hoofbeats reproduced on the soundtrack so that the *axis of sound space* lies perpendicular to the screen. The mirror-and-perspective in Van Eyck does structurally the same thing: it reveals *the contradiction between the planar nature of the canvas and the volumetric character of the depicted world* — that is, *the nature of the language of painting itself*.

**Velázquez, *Las Meninas* (*The Maids of Honor*).** The combination of mirror and metastructural elements (the painter himself depicted in the act of painting, the object of his depiction visible to the viewer reflected in the mirror behind him) makes the *very essence of pictorial language*, its relation to its object, into an object of demonstrative cognition. (Lotman cites Foucault's reading.)

What is happening in all these cases — and in the spherical mirror that opens lateral space in Massys's *The Money Changer and his Wife*, and in many others — is a specific operation. The mirror, *doubling what was already doubled by the painter's brush*, and simultaneously *introducing onto the canvas what should have lain beyond its limits given the conventions of the pictorial language* — *separates the manner of representation from what is represented*. The object of representation becomes the manner of representation itself. The process of self-cognition of the language of painting that occurs here closely resembles analogous phenomena in Baroque verbal literature.

### The thesis: iconic rhetorical structure is content, not expression

Lotman now states the chapter's central thesis with precision:

> A rhetorical statement, in our terminology, is not some simple message onto which "decorations" are placed from above, the removal of which would preserve the basic meaning. In other words, a rhetorical statement *cannot be expressed in a non-rhetorical manner*. **Rhetorical structure lies not in the sphere of expression but in the sphere of content.**

This is the same anti-decorative claim as in §3, but stated for iconic rhetoric specifically. The mirror-in-Van-Eyck is not adding *to* what the painting expresses; it is constitutive of *what the painting expresses*. Remove the spherical mirror from the Arnolfini portrait and you do not have a less-decorated version of the same painting; you have a different painting with a different content. The rhetorical structure *is* the content's mode of being.

He then formalizes the operational definition: a rhetorical text is *one that can be presented as a structural unity of two (or more) subtexts encoded by means of different mutually-untranslatable codes*. The subtexts may organize as local orderings, so that the text in different parts must be read by different languages, or they may operate as different "words" running parallel throughout, so that the whole text presupposes double reading (e.g. literal and symbolic). Either way, *all cases of contrapuntal collision within a single structure of different semiotic languages will be rhetorical texts*.

### The Baroque painted-sculpted angel

The chapter's most exquisite example. In Baroque church wall-paintings in Bohemia, one finds the motif of the *angel-in-a-frame*. The painted frame imitates an oval window. The angel sits on the painted "windowsill" — but lets one little leg dangle out of the frame, *and that leg is sculpted*. It is not part of the painting; it is a sculptural projection, attached to the painting as a continuation.

The text is therefore a *painterly-sculptural combination*. The painted background behind the angel imitates blue sky and presents itself as a *break-through in the space of the fresco*. The protruding sculpted leg breaks that fresco-space *in a different way and in the opposite direction* — out toward the viewer rather than back behind the wall. *The whole text is constructed on the play between real and irreal space and on the collision of artistic languages, of which one presents itself as the "natural" property of the object itself, and the other as the artistic imitation of it.*

This is the perfect concentrated example of iconic rhetoric: two genuinely incommensurable semiotic codes (painted-flat-illusionist; sculpted-volumetric-real) bound within a single text-element that derives its meaning from precisely the productive seam between them. The angel is not a sculpted figure with a painted backdrop, and not a painted figure with a sculpted addition — it is a *bilingual rhetorical figure* in which the binding of the two codes *is* the work.

### Classicism and intermediate coding

Classicism, with its demand for stylistic unity, perceived the Baroque shift between local orderings as *barbarism*. The whole text on its entire extent must be uniformly organized and coded by a single means. *But this does not mean a refusal of rhetorical structure.* The rhetorical effect is achieved by other means — *layered structure of language* (*многослойность языковой структуры*).

The most widespread case: the object of representation is coded *first by a theatrical code, then by a poetic (lyric), historical, or pictorial one*. The rhetorical effect arises from the layering of codes rather than from their open contraposition.

Specific cases: especially characteristic of historical prose, pastoral poetry, and 18th-century painting. The text presents itself as a *direct reproduction of a corresponding theatrical exposition or scenic episode*. According to genre, the mediating text-code may be a scene from tragedy, comedy, or ballet. Charles Coypel's painting *Psyche Abandoned by Cupid* reproduces a ballet scene with all the conventionality of the genre's spectacle in 18th-century interpretation. (Lotman notes that the source of such convergence is not biographical — Coypel was indeed a man of theater, but the same regularities appear in Watteau and others.)

### Theater as intermediate code — the deep theoretical move

Now Lotman makes one of the most original theoretical moves in this chapter. He argues that *the theatricalization of painting in certain epochs is not a surface phenomenon to be explained biographically* but reflects a deep structural relation between theater and the semiotic articulation of reality.

The argument has several stages.

**Semiotic articulation requires segmentation.** For any semiotic act of cognition, the essential operation is the distinction between *significant and non-significant elements* in surrounding reality. Elements that bear no meaning *cease to exist* from the standpoint of the modeling system; their factual existence retreats before their irrelevance. The selection of the *culturally relevant stratum* in the surrounding world is *the initial and essential act of any semiotic modeling of culture*. To accomplish this, primary coding is necessary — the coding that *picks out* what counts as semiotically real.

**Different cultures use different primary coding mediators.** This primary coding may proceed through identification of life situations with mythological ones (real persons with mythic or ritual personae); at other stages, etiquette or ritual ("what exists is what has equivalents in ritual"); historical narration ("that has authentic being which will be inscribed on the tablets of history"). But theater is *especially active in this respect* because it combines aspects of all the above mediating systems.

**Theater as costume code in painted portraiture.** A widespread consequence: portraiture in which the model is dressed in *theatrical costume*. Many 18th-century female portraits in costumes of vestals, Dianas, Sapphos; male portraits *à la* Titus, Alexander the Macedonian, Mars. That the coding mechanism is specifically *theater* and not some indeterminate cultural-mythological mass is confirmed by the *character of the costumes* — they reproduce *stage props* established by 18th-century theatrical tradition for those particular characters. Such costumed stylization means: *to identify oneself with a character significant within the culture, and thereby to become worthy of the artist's brush, the real person must be assimilated to a known stage hero*.

The reverse effect is striking. The convention works back on real behavior. The spread of antique-style empire dress *à la grecque* in Petersburg was driven decisively not by aesthetic theory but by Vigée-Lebrun's portraits. They proved stronger than governmental prohibitions. Maria Fyodorovna appeared at the intimate dinner of March 11, 1801 — *the last of Paul I's life* — wearing the prohibited "antique" dress.

### The continuous/discrete mediation thesis

Then the deepest move. Theater occupies a *structurally specific position* between life (continuous, undivided, in motion) and painting (discrete, stilled, segmented).

> Between the non-discrete flow of life and the picking-out of discrete "stilled" moments characteristic of the visual arts, theater occupies an intermediate position. On the one hand, it differs from the picture and approaches life through continuity and movement. On the other, it differs from life and approaches the picture through the division of the action-flow into segments, each of which at every separate moment tends toward compositional organization within any synchronic cross-section of the action.

Theater is therefore *the pivotal mediating code* between the continuous flow of reality and the discrete stillness of the visual arts. It accomplishes the segmentation that picture-making requires while preserving the dynamism that life has. It is the *workshop in which culture practices the operation* of converting continuous reality into discrete representation.

This is why theater is the *primary intermediate code* for cultural-rhetorical work: not because it is inherently more important than ritual or myth, but because it occupies the structurally privileged position of *spanning the continuous-discrete asymmetry* that all other artistic semiotic operations have to negotiate. Theater is the bridge.

### Theatricalization of behavior and the Romantic life-as-theater

Theater not only mediates between life and painting; it *flows back into life* and reorganizes behavior. Alongside the constant theatrical-historical tendency to assimilate stage life to real life runs the *opposite tendency*: to assimilate real life (or specific spheres of it) to theater. This becomes especially marked in cultures that develop sharply expressed zones of *ritualized behavior*. Theater historically arose from ritual, but in later development the borrowing is reversed: *ritual absorbs the norms of theater*.

Lotman's example: Napoleon III's imperial court ceremonial deliberately oriented itself not on continuity with the pre-revolutionary royal etiquette (destroyed by the Revolution) but on the *norms of the 17th-century French theatrical depiction of Roman emperors' courts*. Talma actively participated in developing the etiquette. Ballet authoritatively invaded military training and parade. Theatrical spectacle even captured the ostensibly remote sphere of combat practice. Lermontov caught the spectator's feeling at military "skirmish" — *as at a tragic ballet*.

Romanticism made this tendency pervasive. While Classicism sharply distinguished ritualized from practical behavior, Romanticism made theatrical behavioral norms penetrate the *everyday* sphere. The closed area of "high" state behavior was abolished, and at the same time the "middle" stylistic sphere — love, friendship, "communion with nature," solitude "amid the noisy ball" — was ritualized in turn.

The emergence of "theater of everyday behavior" *changed how a person looked at himself*. Life was divided into "poetic" moments and situations declared the only significant — the only existing. In "non-poetic" moments, the person, *as if going behind the scenes*, ceased to exist from the standpoint of the "play of life" being acted on stage, until a new entrance. In the consciousness of a Romantic of the Napoleonic era, military life was significant and possessed *authentic reality* (i.e., could become content of various texts) only as a chain of heroic, sublimely-tragic, and touching scenes. Stendhal and Tolstoy made such an impression on readers precisely because they *moved the stage area behind the scenes*, asserting that *there* genuine being happens, while on stage only a "kind-of-existence," apparent life, takes place.

Lavater's word to Karamzin: "to feel oneself more strongly than oneself" — to acquire reality, a person must add to physical being a sign-being. *In particular cultural epochs this is achieved by identification of one's personality with some typical role significant in the given system*. The Lermontov passage:

> What of Olenina's eyes!
> What pensive genius in them,
> And how much of childlike simplicity,
> And how many languid expressions,
> And how much of bliss and dream! ...
> Cast down with a smile by Lel —
> The triumph of modest graces in them;
> Raised — Raphael's angel
> Thus contemplates divinity.

The "childlike simplicity" is asserted as supreme value, but the *pictorial-theatrical code* is introduced for its interpretation: "Raphael's angel" refers to the Sistine Madonna (known to Pushkin through engravings and probably literary descriptions); Lel is the Slavic Cupid (or perhaps just Cupid), referring to pictorial and theatrical-balletic tradition. *The "simple" gaze of Olenina is articulated through the rhetorical layering of pictorial-theatrical codes.*

### The triangle and the cross-art rhetorical mechanism

The chapter's culminating structural picture:

> A triangle is created: real human behavior in the given cultural system — theater — visual arts. Within this triangle, intensive exchange of symbolism and means of expression occurs.

Theatricality penetrates everyday life and influences painting; everyday life affects both, advancing the slogan of "naturalness"; finally, painting and sculpture actively influence both theater (defining systems of poses and movements) and extra-artistic reality (raising it to the level of the meaningful).

Significant in this exchange: *as a meaningful structure crosses into another sphere, it preserves the connection with its native context*. So arises "theatricality" of gesture in painting and life, "pictoriality" of theater or of life itself, "naturalness" of stage and canvas. *It is precisely such double reference to different semiotic systems that creates the rhetorical situation in which lies a powerful source of the elaboration of new meanings*.

The chapter closes with a generalization. Rhetoric — *the transfer of structural principles of one semiotic sphere into another* — is possible at the meeting-point of any arts. The whole sum of semiotic processes at the boundary of *word and image* plays an exceptionally large role here. *Surrealism* in painting can be interpreted as the transfer into the purely pictorial sphere of *verbal metaphor* and purely verbal principles of the fantastic. The imported word-rhetoric reorganizes the visual field. Lotman explicitly chose to argue iconic rhetoric *without* the verbal example because the word-image rhetoric is the natural case; the harder demonstration is iconic-only rhetoric, which the painted-sculpted angel and the mirror-in-painting establish.

### What §4 has done

Stepping back: the chapter has done three things.

**Provided the missing iconic side of the bilingual mechanism.** §3's apparatus required this complement. Without §4, the apparatus would look like a literary theory extended awkwardly to other domains. With §4, the apparatus stands free of the verbal: rhetoric is the structural binding of incommensurables wherever it occurs, and the iconic side has its own native operations that do not derive from the verbal.

**Articulated the intermediate-coding thesis.** This is genuinely original. The argument that theater functions as intermediate code in cross-cultural rhetoric, occupying the structurally pivotal position between the continuous-flowing world of life and the discrete-stilled world of representation, is one of the more powerful contributions in Part I. It generalizes: in any cultural system, certain semiotic forms are *structurally privileged as mediators* across the asymmetries that would otherwise prevent translation. Identifying these mediating forms is essential to the cultural analysis.

**Demonstrated the cross-arts rhetorical mechanism.** The triangle of life-theater-visual arts (and by extension, life-X-Y for any structurally relevant intermediate code X) is a working analytical apparatus that scales to many cultural-historical situations. The point about Maria Fyodorovna's theatrical-fashion-portrait-driven dress on the night Paul I was assassinated — the convention reaching into and reshaping the most consequential moments of historical reality — gives the apparatus weight beyond academic interest.
