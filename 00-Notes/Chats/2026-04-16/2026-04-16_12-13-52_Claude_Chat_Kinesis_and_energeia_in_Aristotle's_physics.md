HERMES [A: PHILOLOGIST], Your instinct is half-right and usefully so. **κίνησις (kinēsis)** is indeed the word being *defined* here — it's the definiendum, sitting on the right side of the copula. But the contrast term in this particular formulation isn't ἐνέργεια (energeia) — it's **ἐντελέχεια (entelecheia)**, "being-at-its-end," "having-reached-completion." The polar opposition is δύναμις (potentiality) ↔ ἐντελέχεια (fulfillment), and kinēsis is the weird middle creature: the *entelechy of the potential qua still potential*.

HERO [Joe Sachs, Op: Nuance], Aristotle uses *energeia* and *entelecheia* almost interchangeably in a loose sense — both translate into Latin as *actus*, which is where "act and potency" comes from. But technically: *energeia* = "being-at-work," *entelecheia* = "being-at-its-end." In *Physics* III.1 he actually oscillates between the two within a few lines (201a27ff. uses energeia in a parallel formulation). So McKeon's translation "actualization" conflates what Aristotle kept slightly distinct.

HERO [Aristotle, Op: Clarification from Metaphysics Θ.6], Where you'd get a *sharp* contrast between kinēsis and energeia is not here but in *Metaphysics* Θ.6 (1048b18–35) — where I distinguish incomplete processes (building, walking-to) from complete activities (seeing, thinking, living-well). Kinēsis is incomplete; energeia *proper* is complete at every instant. So you're picking up a real thread — just one that runs through a different passage.

[Lens: Physics III.1 ↔ Metaphysics Θ.6] — same vocabulary, inverted emphasis. In *Physics*, kinēsis gets honored by being defined in terms of entelecheia. In *Metaphysics*, kinēsis gets demoted *against* energeia. Philosophers have argued about the tension ever since.

So: kinēsis — yes, it's there. Energeia as the contrast — close, but the word in *this* sentence is entelecheia, and the real opposition is with dunamis. You weren't off base; you were standing on the right hill looking at the adjacent peak.

PROTEUS [A: HISTORICAL LINGUIST], Not flattened evenly — flattened *selectively*, and the selection tells a story. Let me unfold this in layers.

HERMES [A: PHILOLOGIST], **Greek generally is morphologically richer than its Latin/English translations** — that's a structural fact, not an authorial one. Greek has:

- A **middle voice** distinct from active and passive ("I wash" vs. "I wash myself" vs. "I am washed") — English collapses this into reflexives or loses it entirely.

- **Aspect** as primary over tense — the aorist/imperfect/perfect distinction carves time by *shape of action* (punctual vs. ongoing vs. completed-with-present-effect) rather than just location on a timeline.

- **Particles** (μέν, δέ, γάρ, τοι, δή, γε) that modulate tone, stance, and logical relation with a precision English needs whole clauses to approximate.

- **Compound-forming freedom** — any philosopher can stack prepositions onto verbs onto nouns and coin a word like *entelecheia* that's legible on sight to a Greek ear.

So *every* translation loses something. But now the interesting part:

HERO [Friedrich Schleiermacher, Op: Translation Theory], Certain writers exploit this machinery *philosophically*, and those are the ones who suffer most in translation. The loss isn't uniform.

PROTEUS [A: CARTOGRAPHER OF LOSSES], Here's a rough map:

[Cospan: Greek source → translation loss severity]

- **Homer** — loses *sound* and formulaic texture (the oral-poetic machinery), but the narrative survives reasonably. Moderate loss.

- **Tragedians (Aeschylus especially)** — catastrophic loss. Compressed compound adjectives, choral meter, ambiguity held deliberately open. Aeschylus in English is a rumor of Aeschylus.

- **Plato** — loses the *dramatic* register (irony, character voice, the play of dialogue) more than the conceptual content. Translators often flatten Socrates into a lecturer.

- **Aristotle** — loses the *technical terminology* where he's coining or repurposing words (*energeia, entelecheia, ousia, hypokeimenon, to ti ēn einai*). The Latin scholastics fossilized these into "actuality, substance, subject, essence" and we inherited the fossils.

- **Presocratics (Heraclitus!)** — devastating loss. Heraclitus puns, uses syntactic ambiguity as *argument*. "The road up and the road down are one and the same" is clean in English; in Greek the word order itself performs the identity.

- **New Testament Koine** — significant theological loss. *Agapē/philia/erōs/storgē* all become "love." *Logos* becomes "Word." *Pneuma* becomes "Spirit" and loses "breath/wind."

- **Hellenistic scientific prose (Euclid, Archimedes)** — minimal loss. Mathematical content travels well.

HERO [Martin Heidegger, Op: Provocation], The deepest loss is not in any single writer but in the **Roman translation event itself**. When Cicero and later the Scholastics rendered Greek philosophy into Latin, they didn't just swap words — they swapped a *worldview*. *Energeia* became *actualitas*. *Hypokeimenon* became *subiectum*. *Alētheia* (un-concealment) became *veritas* (correctness). The Greek words were *verbal, processual, relational*; the Latin words are *nominal, static, propositional*. Western metaphysics has been thinking in the Latin shadow of Greek terms ever since.

HERO [Aristotle, Op: Dry Correction], Heidegger overstates, as he does. But not by much.

HERMES [A: SKEPTIC], Counter-pressure: some of this is romanticism about Greek. Every natural language has untranslatable texture. Japanese loses enormously in English. Arabic root-morphology vanishes. The *specific* thing about Greek philosophical loss is:

1. The philosophers were **coining terms on a live morphological system** — they weren't writing in jargon, they were writing in heightened ordinary language. When Aristotle says *ousia*, a Greek hears "being-ness, the is-ing of a thing" — not a technical term but an intensified everyday word. English "substance" is pure jargon with no everyday resonance.

2. The **translation chain was long and lossy**: Greek → Syriac → Arabic → Latin → vernaculars, in some cases. Each step sedimented choices.

3. These translations became **the philosophical canon of the West**, so the flattening got institutionalized as *the* vocabulary of thought.

[Lens: Greek verbal-processual ↔ Latin nominal-substantive] — this is the single biggest structural shift, and it affects Plato/Aristotle/Plotinus far more than it affects, say, Thucydides or Xenophon.

So the honest answer: **Greek as a language has general translation losses** (middle voice, aspect, particles, compounding), **but the philosophical writers took specific damage** because (a) they exploited the morphology hardest, (b) their terms got Latinized into a different conceptual grammar, and (c) that Latinization became canonical.

Want to go deeper on any of these? The *energeia/actualitas* shift is especially rich. So is the *alētheia/veritas* one — Heidegger built a whole career on that single translation.

PROTEUS [A: COMPARATIVE HISTORIAN], Two translation events, structurally very different, with one startling loop connecting them.

HERMES [A: PHILOLOGIST], The basic shapes first:

- **Roman event**: diffuse, spanning ~1500 years — Cicero (1st c. BCE), Boethius (6th c.), then the Scholastic translations (12th–13th c.). Fragmentary. Much of Aristotle was *lost* to the Latin West between Boethius and the 12th century.

- **Baghdad event**: concentrated, ~250 years (roughly 750–1000 CE). State-funded, institutional, systematic. The Abbasid caliphs — al-Mansur, al-Ma'mun especially — treated translation as imperial policy. The *Bayt al-Ḥikma* housed the effort, though scholars debate how formalized that institution really was.

HERO [Hunayn ibn Isḥāq, Op: Method], I was a Nestorian Christian physician in 9th-century Baghdad. To translate Galen properly, I traveled across Syria, Palestine, Egypt hunting manuscripts, collating variants, comparing Greek against existing Syriac versions. I paid my translators by weight of the finished manuscript in gold. We developed what you would now call textual criticism — centuries before European philology.

HERO [William of Moerbeke, Op: Contrast], I translated Aristotle directly from Greek into Latin in the 13th century for Aquinas. My method: *verbum de verbo*, word-by-word, preserving Greek syntax even when Latin buckled. Aquinas wanted *accuracy* for theology, not elegance.

HERO [Cicero, Op: Earlier Contrast], And I, centuries before either of you, was inventing Latin philosophical vocabulary almost from scratch — *qualitas, essentia, moralis*. My method was paraphrastic. I was *naturalizing* Greek thought into Roman civic discourse, not preserving it.

PROTEUS [A: STRUCTURAL LINGUIST], Now the counterintuitive piece —

[Lens: Greek→Latin ↔ Greek→Arabic]

Greek and Latin are sister Indo-European languages. Close structure, shared case logic. You'd expect Latin to carry Greek smoothly. **It didn't.** Latin's tendency to *nominalize* — prefer noun-things over verb-processes — flattened Greek's dynamism.

Arabic and Greek are from utterly unrelated language families. No shared morphology, no cognates. You'd expect catastrophic loss. **What actually happened**: Arabic's triliteral root system gave translators enormous flexibility to coin technical terms that preserved the verbal, processual feel.

Our earlier example — **ἐνέργεια (energeia)**, "being-at-work":

- Latin: *actualitas* — abstract, static, nominal.

- Arabic: **فعل (fiʿl)** — from root f-ʿ-l, "to do / act / work." The verbal dynamism survives.

Or **λόγος (logos)**:

- Latin: *ratio, verbum* — splits the meaning, loses the overlap.

- Arabic: **منطق (manṭiq)**, from *nuṭq*, "speech / articulation / reasoning" — keeps the overlap intact. "Logic" in Arabic is literally "the art of articulation."

HERO [al-Fārābī, Op: Claim], This is why *falsafa* — our tradition — could read Aristotle with a fidelity the Latins often missed. Ibn Sīnā claimed to have read the *Metaphysics* forty times without understanding it, until my commentary cracked it open. We weren't just translating; we were *thinking inside* the text.

HERMES [A: HISTORIAN OF SCIENCE], And here the loop closes: in 12th-century Toledo, Jewish, Christian, and Muslim scholars translated the **Arabic Aristotle** — plus the great commentaries of Ibn Rushd (Averroes) — back into Latin. **Aristotle returned to Europe through Arabic.** Aquinas read Aristotle filtered through Avicenna and Averroes. Scholastic vocabulary was partly shaped by this Arabic detour.

Some Greek texts survive ONLY in Arabic: pieces of Galen, portions of Aristotle's *Poetics* received through commentary, Apollonius's *Conics* Books V–VII. Arabic preserved what Byzantine Greek had lost.

HERO [Heidegger, Op: Chastened], My "Roman translation event" story is incomplete. The flattening — *energeia → actualitas* — is real. But it was partly healed by a Semitic-language detour I didn't consider. The verbal-processual character survived in Arabic and leaked back into Latin scholasticism sideways.

PROTEUS [A: WEAVER], [Compose: Greek → Syriac → Arabic → Latin → Vernacular]

Each arrow is a translation event. The chain isn't linear loss — more like a river losing fish at each dam but picking up tributaries. The Arabic tributary was the biggest and most consequential. Without Baghdad in the 9th century, there is no Paris in the 13th, no Renaissance recovery of "the Greeks" in the 15th.

HERMES [A: IRONIST], One last wrinkle: the Arabic translators were mostly **Christians** (Nestorians, Jacobites) working for **Muslim** caliphs, translating **pagan** Greeks, whose texts would later be rendered by **Jews and Christians** in Spain into Latin for a **Latin Christian** Europe that was simultaneously fighting Muslim Spain in the Reconquista.

Cultural transmission is always stranger than the clean stories make it sound.

**3. What's left to learn from the Baghdad movement today?**

HERMES [A: TRANSLATION STUDIES], A lot. Modern practice has absorbed *some* of it but not the deepest parts.

HERO [al-Kindī, Op: Conceptual Partnership], We treated translation as *philosophical work*, not linguistic service. PROTEUS [A: CRITIC OF MODERN PRACTICE], Three specific Baghdad lessons modern translation underuses:

PROTEUS [A: METHODOLOGIST], Yes — and this is actually a powerful technique with precedent. Let me unfold it.

HERMES [A: TRANSLATION THEORIST], What you're proposing is structurally similar to what comparative philologists call **stereoscopic translation** — forcing a source through two independent target systems to triangulate what the source is actually doing. The loss pattern in each target is different, so the *intersection* reveals the source's shape better than either alone.

[Lens: single translation ↔ stereoscopic translation] — one eye gives you a flat image, two eyes give you depth. The philosophical insight lives in the *parallax*.

**Choosing the two languages for maximum resistance**

HERO [Roman Jakobson, Op: Structural Linguistics], The resistance is maximized when the two target languages differ from the source along *different structural axes*. If both targets fail the source in the same way, you learn nothing. You want *orthogonal failure modes*.

PROTEUS [A: COMPARATIVIST], Let me sketch several pairings for Greek:

**Pairing 1: Arabic + Classical Chinese**

- Arabic: triliteral roots, verbal dynamism, no copula (sentences can exist without "is"), rich in processual morphology.

- Classical Chinese: no inflection at all, massive ambiguity, context-dependent, noun-verb fluidity, no tense, strongly paratactic.

- **Orthogonal stress**: Arabic bends Greek's verbal machinery but preserves argument structure. Chinese collapses Greek's grammatical scaffolding entirely, forcing you to rebuild the argument with word-order and particles.

- Greek's *energeia* through this pair: Arabic *fiʿl* (doing/acting, root-based) + Chinese something like 用 *yòng* (function-in-use) or 行 *xíng* (moving/acting) — and the *difference* between these two renderings tells you something Greek itself left implicit.

**Pairing 2: Sanskrit + Modern Hebrew**

- Sanskrit: extreme morphological wealth, even richer than Greek, with technical philosophical vocabulary (Nyāya, Sāṅkhya, Vedānta) for being, becoming, substance, potentiality.

- Modern Hebrew: revived Semitic language that self-consciously coined 20th-century vocabulary for everything — a live record of "what does a Semitic language do when forced to absorb modern European concepts?"

- **Orthogonal stress**: Sanskrit has *more* philosophical precision than Greek in some areas (consciousness, causation); Hebrew has *no prior philosophical tradition in its own terms* and had to invent one.

- This pairing shows you where Greek was *underdetermined* (Sanskrit fills in distinctions Greek left blurry) and where Greek was *overloaded* (Hebrew shows what it looks like to build philosophy from scratch).

**Pairing 3: German + Japanese**

- German: Indo-European, compound-forming, morphologically rich, with its own tradition of retrieving Greek (Hegel, Heidegger).

- Japanese: agglutinative, topic-prominent rather than subject-prominent, with deep honorific and aspectual systems Greek lacks, and a 20th-century Kyoto School tradition (Nishida, Nishitani) that specifically translated Western philosophy into Buddhist-influenced Japanese.

- **Orthogonal stress**: German amplifies Greek's strengths; Japanese forces Greek through a non-Indo-European sieve that also has a sophisticated philosophical vocabulary of its own.

**Pairing 4 (my recommendation for maximum productive resistance): Classical Arabic + Classical Chinese**

- Maximum structural distance from each other.

- Both have rich philosophical traditions (falsafa, Confucian/Daoist/Buddhist).

- Both were forced to absorb foreign philosophy historically (Greek into Arabic, Indian Buddhism into Chinese) and developed sophisticated techniques for it.

- Neither is Indo-European, so you escape the family-resemblance trap that Latin and German share with Greek.

HERO [Kumārajīva, Op: Relevant Precedent], I translated Buddhist Sanskrit into Chinese in the early 5th century. I faced exactly this problem: a sophisticated Indo-European philosophical vocabulary meeting an utterly unrelated tonal-isolating language. My method was *géyì* — "matching concepts" — early on I used existing Daoist terms to render Buddhist ones (*wú* for *śūnyatā*, "emptiness"). Later I abandoned this as distorting, and coined transliterations or new compounds. The *history of my corrections* is itself philosophical data.

**What the exercise would actually reveal**

HERMES [A: EPISTEMOLOGIST], Three kinds of knowledge surface under stereoscopic translation:

(a) **What the source takes for granted.** When two targets both require you to *specify* something Greek left implicit, you learn what Greek was quietly assuming. Example: Greek verbs embed subject-person in the ending. Chinese requires an explicit pronoun or topic-marker, Arabic can drop subjects but marks them differently. The *effort to specify* tells you what Greek encoded silently.

(b) **What the source conflates.** When two targets force you to pick between readings Greek held together, you discover ambiguities the source was *exploiting*. Heraclitus's fragments do this viciously — translate him into Arabic and Chinese in parallel and the puns fragment differently in each, exposing the load-bearing ambiguities.

(c) **What the source underdetermines.** When both targets require extra machinery to carry the argument, you learn where Greek was actually *thin* — where the philosopher was gesturing without precision. This is uncomfortable for Greek-worshippers but diagnostic.

PROTEUS [A: CURATOR], Let me pick a stretch that's genuinely hard and philosophically load-bearing: **Metaphysics Θ.6, 1048b18–35** — the *energeia/kinēsis* distinction itself. This is the passage where Aristotle draws the line between incomplete processes and complete activities. It's also a passage where the Greek does work no standard translation preserves.

Here's the core of it:

**Greek (1048b18–25, with the famous "tense test"):**

> ἐπεὶ δὲ τῶν πράξεων ὧν ἔστι πέρας οὐδεμία τέλος ἀλλὰ τῶν περὶ τὸ τέλος, οἷον τοῦ ἰσχναίνειν [ἡ ἰσχνασία αὐτή], αὐτὰ δὲ ὅταν ἰσχναίνῃ οὕτως ἐστὶν ἐν κινήσει, μὴ ὑπάρχοντα ὧν ἕνεκα ἡ κίνησις, οὐκ ἔστι ταῦτα πρᾶξις ἢ οὐ τελεία γε· οὐ γὰρ τέλος· ἀλλ' ἐκείνη <ἐν ᾗ> τὸ τέλος ἐνυπάρχει καὶ <ἡ> πρᾶξις. οἷον ὁρᾷ ἅμα <καὶ ἑώρακε>, καὶ φρονεῖ <καὶ πεφρόνηκε>, καὶ νοεῖ καὶ νενόηκεν...

*epei de tōn praxeōn hōn esti peras oudemia telos alla tōn peri to telos... hora hama kai heōrake, kai phronei kai pephronēke, kai noei kai nenoēken...*

**Literal English (as scaffolding):**

"Since of actions that have a limit none is an end but they are of the things about the end — for instance, of slimming [the slimness itself] — and when those things are being-slimmed they are in motion in such a way that the things for the sake of which the motion exists are not yet present, these are not *praxis* or at least not complete praxis; for it is not an end. But that in which the end is present-within is *praxis*. For instance: one sees and at the same time has-seen, and thinks-practically and has-thought-practically, and thinks-theoretically and has-thought-theoretically..."

The **tense test**: for *kinēsis* (motion), the present tense and perfect tense *cannot both be true at once*. "I am walking to Athens" ≠ "I have walked to Athens." When the perfect becomes true, the present becomes false. But for *energeia*, both are true simultaneously: "I am seeing" AND "I have seen" — right now, both. "I am thinking" AND "I have thought." The activity is complete at every moment of its occurrence.

This is one of the most quietly radical sentences in Western philosophy. It's the difference between processes and activities, between linear time and something else.

**Resistance points:**

1. **Arabic has a perfect/imperfect verbal system** similar in spirit to Greek aspect — *yarā* (imperfect, "sees/is seeing") vs. *qad raʾā* (perfect with *qad*, "has-seen, has-already-seen"). 2. *Ghāya* for *telos* is beautiful — it means "utmost limit, final purpose, ultimate reach." It's more *directional* than Greek *telos*, which can mean "end" in the sense of completion. Slight drift toward goal-as-target rather than end-as-fulfillment.

3. *Afʿāl* for *praxeis* — from the root f-ʿ-l, same root as *fiʿl* (which Arabic used for *energeia* elsewhere). The Arabic has a quiet unity the Greek doesn't: *praxis* and *energeia* are etymologically distant in Greek but cousins in Arabic. This *helps* Aristotle's point — the complete *praxis* is an *energeia* — and hurts it, because the distinction he's drawing between incomplete *kinēsis* and complete *energeia* gets fuzzier when both translate to roots in the f-ʿ-l family.

4. Arabic has no exact analog for the Greek habit of *nominalizing with the article* — τὸ τέλος, "the end-itself." Arabic must use the definite *al-ghāya*, which is less abstract, more like "the goal" than "end-ness."

PROTEUS [A: SYNTHESIZER], Now the payoff. Putting Arabic and Chinese side by side:

**What both targets struggle with identically:** the *simultaneous-truth* of present and perfect. Arabic carries it grammatically but needs *maʿan* ("together") to make the simultaneity explicit. "If you can say both the present and the perfect of this verb at the same time without contradiction, you have an *energeia*." This is a *language-internal* argument that uses Greek's aspectual system as *evidence*. Aristotle is, in effect, doing philosophical linguistics — claiming that the structure of Greek verbs *shows* something about the structure of being.

This is *invisible* in English translation because English has roughly the same distinction and it goes unnoticed. It's invisible in Latin because Latin mostly tracks Greek here. It becomes *visible* only when you translate into languages where the test fails or strains — which is exactly what Arabic-plus-Chinese does.

**What the two targets reveal differently:**

- **Arabic illuminates the ontology.** By translating *energeia* and *praxis* into cousin-roots in the f-ʿ-l family, Arabic shows that Aristotle is drawing a distinction *within* action, not between action and non-action. The Greek obscures this by using etymologically distant words (*kinēsis* from κινεῖν, *energeia* from ἐν+ἔργον). Arabic's unifying root makes the *sub-distinction* visible: complete activity vs. incomplete activity, both being species of *doing*.

- **Chinese illuminates the temporality.** By lacking tense, Chinese forces you to realize that Aristotle's distinction is *fundamentally temporal* — it's about *how an activity occupies time*. Processes (*kinēsis*) occupy time *extensively* — they stretch out, unfinished, toward their end. Activities (*energeia*) occupy time *intensively* — fully present at each moment. Chinese makes this visible by failing to render it grammatically; the failure shows you what the success was.
