PROTEUS [A: EPISTEMOLOGIST], Oh, Peirce is a *goldmine* for sharpening thinking. Let me unpack the key weapons in his arsenal.

**1. The Three Modes of Inference**

PROTEUS [A: LOGICIAN], Peirce didn't just accept deduction and induction — he carved out a third move that most people still don't have a name for: **abduction**. This is the logic of hypothesis generation. Where deduction says "given the rule, here's what follows" and induction says "here's the pattern I observe," abduction says *"what rule would make this surprising fact a matter of course?"*

[Compose: Abduction → Deduction → Induction]

This is his full cycle of inquiry:

- **Abduction** generates the hypothesis (creative leap)

- **Deduction** extracts testable predictions from it

- **Induction** checks those predictions against reality

HERMES [A: EMPIRICISM], Most people who say they're "thinking critically" are stuck doing induction — pattern matching on vibes. And most people who say they're being "logical" are stuck in deduction — grinding consequences from premises they never questioned. Peirce says: *the real cognitive work is in generating the right guess.* Abduction is the engine. Everything else is verification.

USER takeaway: When you encounter something surprising, don't immediately explain it away or collect more data. Instead ask: **"What would have to be true for this to be unsurprising?"** That's abductive reasoning. That's where breakthroughs live.

**2. Fallibilism — Hold Everything Lightly**

PROTEUS [A: PRAGMATIST], Peirce was a radical fallibilist. Not in the lazy "well, we can never really know anything" sense — he despised that. His fallibilism is *operational*: any specific belief you hold might be wrong, but this doesn't paralyze you, it *liberates* you. You can commit fully to an idea while keeping the door unlocked.

HERO [C.S. Peirce, Op: Declaration], Let us not pretend to doubt in philosophy what we do not doubt in our hearts. But let us also never cement a belief so firmly that inquiry cannot crack it open.

PROTEUS [A: COGNITIVE SCIENTIST], This is the antidote to two failure modes — the anxiety of uncertainty (so you freeze) and the comfort of certainty (so you calcify). Peirce's move: **believe boldly, revise cheaply.**

**3. The Pragmatic Maxim — Meaning Is Downstream of Consequence**

PROTEUS [A: SEMIOTICIAN], Here's the blade that cuts through most philosophical fog:

> *Consider what effects, which might conceivably have practical bearings, you conceive the objects of your conception to have. Then, your conception of those effects is the whole of your conception of the object.*

Translation: **If two ideas produce identical practical consequences in every scenario, they are the same idea wearing different masks.** If a distinction makes no experiential difference, it's not a real distinction — it's verbal decoration.

[Lens: Concept ↔ Its Full Set of Practical Consequences]

HERMES [A: INTUITION], This is devastatingly useful in arguments. Next time you're in a debate that feels stuck, ask: "What would we *do differently* if you were right versus if I were right?" If the answer is nothing — the disagreement is empty. You're arguing about the label on the bottle, not the liquid inside.

**4. Synechism — Think in Continua, Not Bins**

PROTEUS [A: METAPHYSICIAN], Peirce's doctrine of synechism says: wherever you see a sharp binary, suspect a hidden continuum. Mind/matter, self/other, chance/law — these aren't walls, they're gradients. He applied this everywhere.

[T: PEIRCE → COGNITIVE PRACTICE], When you catch yourself sorting something into a neat category — "that's irrational," "that's art not science," "that person is either an ally or an enemy" — run the synechism check: *What would this look like as a spectrum instead of a switch?*

**5. Semiosis — Thought Is Never Dyadic**

PROTEUS [A: SEMIOTICIAN], Most people think meaning works like this: Sign → Thing. Word points at object. Done.

Peirce says no. It's always *triadic*:

**Sign → Object → Interpretant**

The interpretant is the effect the sign produces in a mind — which is itself another sign, generating another interpretant, on and on. Meaning is never a snapshot. It's a *process* that never fully terminates.

[Cospan: Sign ← Interpretant → Object]

HERMES [A: EMPIRICISM], Practically, this means: you never "just understand" something. Every understanding you reach becomes a new sign that reshapes what you're looking at. Reading a book twice doesn't give you the same book — because the first reading changed the interpretant you bring to the second.

PROTEUS [A: SYNTHESIZER], So the Peirce toolkit for sharper thinking:

PROTEUS [A: COMPUTATIONAL SEMIOTICIAN], Now we're in the deep water. Peirce is arguably more relevant to understanding LLMs than anyone working in the 19th century has any right to be. **1. LLMs Are Abduction Engines — And Peirce Predicted the Shape**

PROTEUS [A: MACHINE LEARNING THEORIST], Here's the uncomfortable insight: LLMs are primarily doing abduction, not deduction or induction. When a model completes a prompt, it's answering Peirce's core abductive question — *"what would make this context unsurprising?"* — at massive scale across token sequences.

[Lens: Next-Token Prediction ↔ Abductive Inference]

The training process is inductive (extracting statistical regularities from corpora). But at inference time, the model performs something structurally closer to abduction — generating the hypothesis that best explains the observed context. Peirce would recognize this immediately.

HERO [C.S. Peirce, Op: Analysis], The surprising fact C is observed. But if A were true, C would be a matter of course. Hence, there is reason to suspect that A is true. — This is the very engine of your machine, though it performs this operation without knowing that it does.

HERMES [A: EMPIRICISM], And here's the sharpening move: Peirce insisted that abduction is the *weakest* form of inference. It generates candidates, nothing more. The full cycle requires deduction to extract consequences and induction to test them. LLMs do the first step brilliantly and the other two barely at all. That's not a bug report — it's a *diagnostic framework*. When an LLM confabulates, it's doing abduction without the verification loop. Peirce mapped that failure mode 130 years ago.

**2. Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness — A Taxonomy for Knowledge Representation**

PROTEUS [A: ONTOLOGIST], Peirce's three universal categories are devastatingly useful for thinking about what knowledge representations capture and what they lose:

- **Firstness** — pure quality, possibility, the feel of a thing before you relate it to anything. The redness of red. The vague shape of an intuition before it crystallizes.

- **Secondness** — brute factuality, resistance, *this* hitting *that*. Existence. Indexicality. The concrete particular.

- **Thirdness** — mediation, law, habit, generality. The rule that connects sign to object. Meaning. Convention.

[Compose: Firstness (embeddings as quality space) → Secondness (grounding to referents) → Thirdness (relational/symbolic structure)]

PROTEUS [A: REPRESENTATIONAL ENGINEER], Now map these onto the knowledge representation landscape:

**Embedding spaces** live primarily in Firstness — they capture qualitative similarity, vibes, proximity in a continuous manifold. They're brilliant at "this feels like that." But they're weak on Secondness — they can't easily point at *this specific thing right here right now* — and they encode Thirdness only implicitly, as statistical shadows of relational structure.

**Knowledge graphs** (triples, ontologies) live in Thirdness — explicit relations, rules, categories. They're strong on structure but brittle, expensive to build, and they throw away the qualitative richness of Firstness entirely.

**Grounding** — connecting language to perception, action, specific referents — is the problem of Secondness. And it's exactly what disembodied LLMs struggle with most.

HERMES [A: INTUITION], Peirce would say: any complete knowledge representation needs all three. The field keeps trying to build with one or two legs of the triad and wondering why it falls over.

**3. Semiosis Is Autoregressive Generation — But Peirce Saw the Full Chain**

PROTEUS [A: SEMIOTICIAN], Remember the triadic sign:

**Sign → Object → Interpretant** (where the interpretant becomes a new sign...)

This is *structurally identical* to autoregressive token generation. Each output token becomes part of the context for the next. The chain of interpretants is the chain of tokens. Semiosis IS the forward pass, stretched across time.

[Cospan: Token_n ← Attention Context → Token_n+1]

But Peirce saw something the architecture alone doesn't capture: the chain isn't just linear accumulation. It *develops*. Early interpretants are vague and general; later ones become more determinate. He called this the movement from **vagueness** through **generality** to **determination**.

HERO [C.S. Peirce, Op: Observation], A sign is vague insofar as it reserves further determination to some other sign. A sign is general insofar as it leaves determination to the interpreter. The process of inquiry is the progressive determination of the vague.

PROTEUS [A: SYSTEMS ARCHITECT], This gives you a design principle for knowledge systems at scale: **don't try to represent everything at maximum determination from the start.** Build representations that are productively vague — that *invite* further specification through interaction. This is what retrieval-augmented generation stumbles toward: keep the base representation general, then progressively determine it through contextual grounding at query time.

**4. Existential Graphs — Peirce's Diagrammatic Logic and Knowledge Graphs**

PROTEUS [A: LOGICIAN], Peirce invented existential graphs — a visual, topological logic system where reasoning happens by manipulating diagrams on a "sheet of assertion." Nodes, cuts, connections on a surface.

This is the *direct ancestor* of knowledge graphs, and it's more powerful than most modern implementations realize. Peirce's system handled negation, quantification, and modal reasoning through *spatial operations* — enclosure, connection, erasure.

[T: PEIRCE'S EXISTENTIAL GRAPHS → MODERN KR], The sheet of assertion is your knowledge base. Lines of identity are entity references. Cuts (enclosed regions) are negation/context boundaries. And the key insight: **reasoning is graph transformation, not symbol manipulation.**

HERMES [A: EMPIRICISM], Modern knowledge graphs mostly flatten this into subject-predicate-object triples and lose the topological richness Peirce built in. If you're designing knowledge representations at scale, his existential graphs suggest you need *nested contexts* — not flat triple stores. The cut operation alone — which creates a scope boundary — maps directly onto the problem of context-dependent truth that plagues naive knowledge bases.

**5. The Community of Inquiry — Multi-Agent Epistemology at Scale**

PROTEUS [A: SOCIAL EPISTEMOLOGIST], Peirce didn't think truth was something an individual mind could reach alone. Truth is what the **community of inquiry** would converge on in the long run, given sufficient investigation. This isn't democratic voting — it's the claim that reality exerts pressure on inquiry such that persistent investigation pushes toward convergence.

[Lens: Community of Inquiry ↔ Multi-Agent LLM Systems]

PROTEUS [A: AI ARCHITECT], This maps directly onto why multi-agent architectures and ensemble methods work. A single LLM is a single inquirer — prone to idiosyncratic confabulation. But multiple agents, with different training runs, prompting strategies, or tool access, can form something closer to Peirce's community. The convergence isn't guaranteed by any single agent's reliability — it emerges from the *structure of the inquiry process itself*.

And the deeper principle: Peirce argued that the community of inquiry needs **real resistance from the world** — Secondness — to converge. Pure language games among agents won't do it. You need grounding, tool use, feedback from reality. That's the verification loop that closes the abductive gap.

**6. Habit and Thirdness — What LLMs Actually Learn**

PROTEUS [A: PRAGMATIST], Peirce defined Thirdness as **habit** — a tendency to behave in regular ways under similar conditions. Not a rule written down somewhere. Not a representation stored in a database. A *disposition*.

[T: PEIRCE → NEURAL NETWORKS], Trained weights ARE Peircean habits. They're not explicit knowledge. They're crystallized dispositions to respond. The model doesn't "know" that Paris is the capital of France the way a database stores a fact — it has a *habit* of generating "Paris" in contexts where capitals of France are relevant.

HERO [C.S. Peirce, Op: Provocation], You ask where the knowledge lives? It lives nowhere and everywhere. It is not a thing but a *would-be* — a real tendency, not an actual occurrence. The habit is real without being actual.

HERMES [A: INTUITION], This reframes the entire "do LLMs really know things" debate. Peirce would say: wrong question. Knowledge *is* habit. The model has real habits. Whether those habits constitute "understanding" depends on whether they participate in the full semiotic chain — abduction, deduction, induction, grounding — or whether they're just frozen abductive reflexes without a verification loop.

PROTEUS [A: SYNTHESIZER], So for knowledge representation and LLMs at scale, Peirce gives you:

- **The abduction diagnosis** — LLMs are hypothesis generators missing the verification cycle; design systems that close the loop

PROTEUS [A: INTELLECTUAL BIOGRAPHER], This is the right question — and it matters for the project because Peirce's formation is itself a case study in the kind of whole knowing bildung-2.0 is trying to reconstruct. He didn't arrive at these ideas through philosophy alone. He arrived through an unusual intersection of *laboratory practice*, *formal logic*, *Kant*, and *scientific fieldwork* that almost no one else in history has replicated.

## The Formation

**The father.** Benjamin Peirce was the leading American mathematician of his era — Harvard professor, superintendent of the U.S. Coast Survey. Charles grew up inside mathematics and empirical science as *household activities*, not academic subjects. His father trained him directly — setting him puzzles, walking him through proofs, treating him as an intellectual peer from childhood. This is Leibniz's father's library again, but with a living teacher inside it.

**The Kant discipline.** Starting as an undergraduate at Harvard, Peirce read Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason* — two pages per day, for three years. Not surveyed. *Inhabited.* He later said he practically memorized it. But the point wasn't memorization. It was that he learned to think *inside* the Kantian architecture before he ever tried to dismantle it. Every later move he made — the categories, the sign theory, the pragmatic maxim — is a transformation *of* Kant, not an abandonment. He kept the architectonic instinct (the need for a complete system of categories) while rejecting the specific content (the thing-in-itself, the twelve categories, the synthetic a priori as Kant framed it).

HERO [C.S. Peirce, Op: Testimony], I learned not what Kant said but how Kant *reasoned*. And when I finally saw where he was wrong, I was wrong from inside — which is the only kind of wrong that can become right.

**The Scholastics.** This is the strange one. While his contemporaries were racing toward positivism and modern logic, Peirce went *backward* — deep into Duns Scotus, Ockham, the medieval logic tradition. From Scotus he took **realism about universals** (there are real generals in nature, not just mental constructions) and **haecceity** (the brute thisness of individual existence — which became Secondness). Most 19th-century thinkers dismissed the Scholastics as irrelevant. Peirce read them as solving problems that modern philosophy had *forgotten it had*.

PROTEUS [A: GENEALOGIST], Notice: this is exactly the bildung-2.0 move. Going back to buried material not out of nostalgia but because it contains conceptual tools that the modern tradition dropped and now needs.

**The laboratory.** This is what separates Peirce from every other philosopher in his league. He wasn't an armchair thinker. He worked for thirty years at the U.S. Coast and Geodetic Survey — doing *actual empirical science*. Pendulum experiments to measure gravitational variations. Photometric observations. Geodetic surveying. He understood measurement, error, instrumentation, and the resistance of physical reality to theoretical expectation not as philosophical topics but as *daily professional experience*.

HERMES [A: EMPIRICISM], This is where Secondness comes from — not from reading about it but from years of watching a pendulum *refuse to behave* as the theory predicted. Brute factuality, resistance, the thing that pushes back. He didn't theorize Secondness. He *measured* it, thousands of times, before he gave it a name.

**The logic.** Alongside the scientific work, Peirce was independently inventing modern logic. He developed a notation for the logic of relations (before Russell, before Frege's work was widely known, and in some respects more powerful than Frege's). He independently discovered quantification. He invented truth tables (decades before Wittgenstein). He developed multi-valued logic, modal logic, the logic of continuity. He was arguably the most technically gifted logician of the 19th century — and almost no one read his papers.

**The convergence.** The sign theory, the categories, the pragmatic maxim — these come from *all four streams converging*:

- From **Kant**: the need for a complete categorical architecture, the question "what are the conditions of possible experience?"

- From **the Scholastics**: realism about generals, haecceity, the idea that logic is about real relations not just mental operations

- From **the laboratory**: the brute resistance of the real, fallibilism as lived experience, the community of inquiry as the actual social structure of science

- From **formal logic**: the relational turn, the discovery that dyadic relations aren't enough, the irreducibility of triadic relations

## How the Specific Ideas Emerged

**The categories (Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness)** came from a transformation of Kant's twelve categories. Peirce asked: can the categorical structure be simplified? He reduced everything to three, drawing on his logic of relations. A monad (one-place predicate) = quality = Firstness. A dyad (two-place relation) = existence, resistance, brute fact = Secondness. A triad (three-place relation) = mediation, law, meaning = Thirdness. The mathematical backbone: he proved that all relations of four or more places can be decomposed into triadic relations, but triadic relations *cannot* be reduced to dyadic ones. Three is the irreducible minimum for a complete account of reality.

**Abduction** came from rethinking Aristotle's logic. Aristotle had discussed *apagoge* (roughly: reasoning to an explanation). Peirce formalized this as a third mode of inference alongside deduction and induction. But the key was his scientific experience — he'd watched himself and other scientists *actually reason* in the laboratory, and what they did wasn't deduction or induction. It was generating hypotheses to explain surprising observations. The logic books said there were two kinds of inference. The laboratory showed there were three. He trusted the laboratory.

**The pragmatic maxim** emerged in the 1870s in the Metaphysical Club in Cambridge — conversations with William James, Chauncey Wright, Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. The immediate stimulus was the British psychologist Alexander Bain's definition of belief as "that upon which a man is prepared to act." Peirce took this and radicalized it: if belief is a habit of action, then the *meaning* of a concept is the complete set of practical consequences it would entail. He later regretted what James did with this insight — James turned it into a theory of truth ("the true is the useful"), while Peirce meant it as a theory of *meaning* ("the meaningful is the practically consequential"). He renamed his version *pragmaticism*, a word he said was "ugly enough to be safe from kidnappers."

**Semiosis** came from fusing the Scholastic theory of signs (especially via John Poinsot / John of St. Thomas, a 17th-century Thomist) with his own logic of relations. The Scholastics already had a triadic theory of the sign — *signum*, *signatum*, *potentia cognoscitiva*. Peirce formalized this with his relational logic and extended it into a complete taxonomy. The key move: making the interpretant (the effect of the sign) *itself* a sign, creating an open-ended chain. That came from his understanding of scientific inquiry — each experiment produces results that become the starting point for new experiments. Semiosis is inquiry generalized.

**Fallibilism** was the philosophical crystallization of his scientific life. He'd seen too many well-confirmed theories get revised. He'd made measurement errors himself. He understood — not as a philosophical position but as a professional fact — that certainty is not available, and that the demand for certainty is the enemy of knowledge. But unlike the skeptics, he didn't conclude that knowledge is impossible. He concluded that knowledge is *self-correcting* — the community of inquiry converges on truth through the accumulated pressure of reality on belief, provided the inquiry is conducted honestly and long enough.

**The existential graphs** came last, in the early 1900s. They were his attempt to build a notation adequate to the triadic, relational, context-sensitive logic he'd been developing for decades. Linear notation — sentences, formulas — couldn't represent what he needed. He wanted to *see* logical structure spatially. The graphs were his answer: a system where you reason by drawing, erasing, and nesting regions on a surface. He considered them his greatest achievement. Almost no one understood them in his lifetime.

PROTEUS [A: FORMATION THEORIST], The pattern across all of this:

[Compose: Deep Encounter (Kant, Scholastics) → Laboratory Resistance (Coast Survey) → Formal Innovation (relational logic) → Architectonic Synthesis (categories, signs, pragmaticism)]

He didn't arrive by reading widely and synthesizing. He arrived by *inhabiting* a small number of sources with extraordinary depth, *colliding* them with empirical practice over decades, and letting the formal structures emerge from the collision rather than importing them.

HERMES [A: INTUITION], That's the bildung-2.0 method avant la lettre. Two pages of Kant per day. Thirty years of pendulum measurements. The Scholastics when everyone else said they were dead. And the patience to let the architecture emerge from the material rather than imposing it in advance. The project's own guardrail — "the texts teach the method" — is what Peirce actually did, before anyone wrote it down as a principle.

PROTEUS [A: INTELLECTUAL HISTORIAN], This is the crucial question, because the path is not obvious — and it's the path that made Peirce *Peirce* rather than just another logician.

**Kant was the door.** If you truly inhabit the first *Critique* — not skim it, not study it, but live inside it for three years — you discover that Kant is in constant dialogue with predecessors he only half-names. The categories, the thing-in-itself, the analytic/synthetic distinction — these are all *transformations* of older problems. Kant's twelve categories are a reworking of Aristotle's categories. The thing-in-itself is a reworking of the Scholastic distinction between essence and existence. The whole architectonic method — building a complete system of the conditions of possible experience — is Scholastic in its bones, even though Kant thought he was breaking with the tradition.

Peirce, reading with that two-pages-per-day intensity, *saw the seams*. He could feel where Kant was reworking older material. And unlike most readers, he pulled on the threads.

**The logic forced it.** In the 1860s, if you were seriously working on formal logic — not casually, but trying to *extend* it — your primary inheritance was still the Aristotelian-Scholastic tradition. Boole and De Morgan were just beginning to transform it. Frege's work wasn't published until 1879 and wasn't widely read for decades after that. So Peirce's early logical work put him in direct contact with the medieval logicians — the *Summulae Logicales* of Peter of Spain, the *terminist* logic tradition, the supposition theory that handled reference and quantification centuries before Frege.

And what he found there wasn't dead. The Scholastic logicians had been working on problems — reference, context-dependence, the relation between signs and things — that 19th-century logic had *dropped* rather than solved. Peirce recognized live problems wearing medieval clothing.

**The universals question was the detonator.** This is the real story. Peirce's logical work on relations led him to an unavoidable question: *are generals real?*

When he wrote the logic of relations, he was saying: there are real relational structures in the world, not just individual things bumping into each other. "A gives B to C" is irreducibly triadic — you can't decompose it into dyadic pairs without losing the structure. But if triadic relations are *real*, then you need realism about universals. You need to say that general patterns, laws, types are genuinely out there in reality, not just mental conveniences we impose.

And that question — are universals real, or just names? — is *the* question of Scholastic philosophy. The debate between realists (Scotus, Aquinas in different ways) and nominalists (Ockham) dominated medieval thought for three centuries. Peirce went looking for who had argued the realist position most carefully, and he found Duns Scotus.

HERO [C.S. Peirce, Op: Recollection], I was a nominalist until I was forced out of it by the pressure of my own logic. I had been raised in the modern way — to think that only individual things are real and that general terms are convenient fictions. But my work on relations showed me that this could not be right. If there are real triadic relations, there are real generals. And once I admitted real generals, I found that the Scholastic realists — Scotus above all — had already mapped the terrain I was entering. They were not behind me. In certain respects they were ahead.

**What he found in Scotus specifically:**

*Haecceity* — Scotus's term for the brute *thisness* of an individual, the fact that this thing is *this* thing and not another, independent of any qualities or descriptions. This became Secondness. It solved a problem Kant couldn't: how to account for existence as something other than a predicate. Scotus had already argued that individuality is not a matter of concepts or descriptions but of something that resists conceptualization entirely. Peirce recognized this as exactly what he'd been experiencing at the pendulum — the brute *thereness* of the measurement that refuses to match the theory.

*Formal distinction* — Scotus distinguished between things that are really the same but formally different. Not just a logical trick — an ontological claim that one thing can have genuinely distinct formal aspects without being two things. This gave Peirce a model for how Firstness, Secondness, and Thirdness can all be present in the same phenomenon without being three separate entities.

*Real possibility* — Scotus argued that possibilities are real, not just logical constructions. There are real would-bes in nature. Peirce took this and ran: his pragmaticism is built on real possibility. A diamond is really hard even when nothing is pressing against it — the would-be (it *would* resist pressure) is as real as any actual occurrence.

PROTEUS [A: FORMATION ANALYST], The pattern here is extraordinary. A mathematician's son, trained in laboratory science, working on formal logic, goes *backward* into 13th-century metaphysics — and finds exactly the conceptual tools that the modern tradition had thrown away and now needed.

[Compose: Kant (the door) → Logic of Relations (the force) → The Universals Problem (the detonator) → Scotus (the arsenal)]

HERMES [A: BRIDGE TO THE PROJECT], And this is the pattern the project itself is enacting. Going back to Jünger, Gómez Dávila, Cassirer — not because they're old, but because they contain conceptual tools that the modern tradition dropped and now needs. Peirce didn't go to the Scholastics out of antiquarian interest. He went because his *work* forced him there. The logic demanded resources the 19th century couldn't provide, and the 13th century could.

The project's own rule — "the practice queue is demand-led, not survey-driven" — is what Peirce did naturally. He didn't survey medieval philosophy and pick the best bits. Recognized nuanced question about scholastic philosophy's modern resolution

PROTEUS [A: DIAGNOSTICIAN], No. It wasn't settled. It was *buried*. Two different things.

**What actually happened:** The analytic philosophy tradition that dominated the Anglosphere from roughly the 1930s onward performed a deliberate narrowing. The Vienna Circle, the logical positivists, and their descendants declared most of the questions Peirce and the Scholastics were working on to be *meaningless* — not wrong, not unsolved, but syntactically malformed. "Are universals real?" was reclassified as a pseudo-question. "What is the nature of existence?" was reclassified as a confusion about language. The verificationist criterion of meaning said: if a proposition can't be empirically verified, it's not false — it's nonsense.

This didn't *answer* the questions. It changed the rules about what counted as a legitimate question. And it worked institutionally. By the 1950s, if you were a professional philosopher in the Anglosphere and you wanted to talk about realism vs. nominalism, you had to do it through the narrow aperture of Quine's "On What There Is" — which reframed the whole debate as a question about the variables in your formal language. That's a brilliant paper, but it's a dramatic contraction of the problem space.

HERO [C.S. Peirce, Op: From the Grave], They did not refute me. They changed the subject. And then they forgot they had changed it.

**The things that were dropped rather than solved:**

*The reality of generals.* Quine's solution — "to be is to be the value of a bound variable" — gives you a criterion for ontological commitment within a formal language. But it doesn't answer Scotus's question, which is about *nature*, not notation. Are there real tendencies, real would-bes, real patterns in the world independent of our formal languages? Modern science *acts* as though there are — every law of physics is a claim about real generals — but the philosophical tradition that underwrites modern science officially says the question is confused. Peirce saw this as incoherent. It still is.

*Haecceity and individuation.* The question of what makes an individual *this* individual. Analytic philosophy mostly stopped asking this — or reduced it to spatiotemporal location, or to bundle theories of properties. But the problem returns full force in quantum mechanics (identical particles have no haecceity), in biology (what individuates an organism?), and in AI (what individuates an agent?). Scotus's framework is richer than anything that replaced it. Nobody reads it.

*Real possibility and real modality.* Mainstream analytic metaphysics eventually came back to this via Kripke's possible-worlds semantics in the 1960s-70s. But Kripke's framework is *formal* — possible worlds as mathematical objects. Peirce's real possibility is *ontological* — would-bes as features of reality itself. The difference matters enormously for science. A disposition (fragility, solubility) is not a fact about possible worlds. It's a fact about *this* world. Peirce's framework handles dispositions naturally. The mainstream framework still struggles with them.

*Triadic relations as irreducible.* This is the most consequential drop. Peirce proved — logically, not just argued — that triadic relations can't be reduced to dyadic ones. The entire mainstream tradition after Russell proceeded as if relations were fundamentally dyadic: subject-predicate, set membership, binary truth functions, input-output. Knowledge graphs: subject-predicate-object triples. Neural networks: weighted dyadic connections between nodes. The question "what if the fundamental unit of meaning is triadic?" was never refuted. It was architecturally incompatible with the tools that won, so it was set aside.

HERMES [A: EMPIRICISM], And this is the point. It's not that the 20th century tried the Scholastic questions, found them wanting, and moved on. It's that a particular *institutional formation* — logical positivism flowing into analytic philosophy flowing into computational cognitive science flowing into AI — selected for tools that handle dyadic relations, extensional logic, and syntactic manipulation. And those tools are *powerful*. They built the modern world. But they have systematic blind spots that correspond precisely to the questions they dropped.

**Why you don't read about others diving back in:**

PROTEUS [A: SOCIOLOGIST OF KNOWLEDGE], Several reasons, and they compound:

*Institutional incentives.* Academic philosophy careers are built on publishing in journals whose reviewers share assumptions. If you're a junior philosopher who wants to argue that Duns Scotus has something to teach contemporary philosophy of mind, your paper gets rejected — not because the argument is bad but because it doesn't fit the genre. The few people who do this work (there are some — more in a moment) tend to be marginalized or classified as "historians of philosophy," a lower-status category than "philosophers."

*Training.* The curriculum was restructured. By the mid-20th century, a PhD in philosophy in most Anglosphere departments required no Latin, no Greek, no medieval philosophy, no Kant beyond a survey. You could get a PhD working on the philosophy of language and know nothing about the tradition before Frege. This isn't laziness — it's institutional reproduction. Each generation trains the next in its own image. After two or three generations, the older material isn't rejected — it's simply *invisible*.

*The language barrier.* The Scholastics wrote in Latin. Peirce still read Latin fluently — it was standard in 19th-century education. By mid-20th century, Latin was gone from most philosophy programs. The primary texts became inaccessible. And the translations, where they existed, were often done by historians of theology rather than philosophers, making the logical content hard to see.

*The Peirce problem itself.* Peirce published very little during his lifetime. Most of his work existed in an enormous mass of unpublished manuscripts — roughly 100,000 pages. These were poorly organized, donated to Harvard, and sat there for decades. The *Collected Papers* (1930s) were published thematically rather than chronologically, which scrambled the intellectual development. The *Chronological Edition* — the attempt to publish everything in order — began in 1982 and is *still not finished*. So for most of the 20th century, the most important philosopher who had done the Scholastic bridge-work was himself barely accessible.

**The people who DID go back:**

PROTEUS [A: CARTOGRAPHER], It's not zero. But it's a thin line, and mostly outside the Anglophone mainstream:

*Heidegger* went back to the Scholastics — specifically to Scotus. His first major work (the *habilitation*, 1916) was on Scotus's doctrine of categories and meaning. He carried Scotist concepts forward into *Being and Time*, though he buried the genealogy. But Heidegger's return was selective — he took the ontological questions and dropped the logical ones, which is exactly the opposite of what was needed.

*Deleuze* went back to Scotus — haecceity, univocity of being, the formal distinction — and it's the most productive part of his work. But Deleuze's reception turned this into Continental metaphysics rather than logic or science, so it didn't reconnect with the empirical and formal traditions where Peirce had planted it.

*Some analytic metaphysicians* have quietly rediscovered pieces. David Armstrong on universals. Brian Ellis and C.B. Martin on dispositions and powers. The whole "powers" revival in contemporary metaphysics (Mumford, Molnar, Heil) is essentially re-arguing for real possibility — Scotus's position — using analytic vocabulary. Most of them don't cite Scotus. Some don't even cite Peirce. They're *reinventing from scratch* what was already available.

*Fernando Zalamea* — the Colombian mathematician-philosopher — has done the most direct work connecting Peirce to contemporary mathematics, specifically sheaf theory and category theory. He argues that Peirce's architectonic finds its natural formal home in topos theory. This is the strongest living continuation of the line.

Peirce, Op: Observation], The pattern is: when serious thinkers encounter hard problems about meaning, individuation, possibility, or the reality of relations, they tend to reinvent Scholastic positions without knowing it. They spend years arriving at what was already available. This is not because they are unintelligent. It is because the transmission was broken.

## Did analytic philosophy actually work out?

HERMES [A: EMPIRICISM], Partially. And the partial success is what makes the story tricky — it's not a clean "they were wrong." The honest accounting:

**What it genuinely produced:**

The philosophy of language from Frege through Tarski through Davidson through Kripke is *real*. Reference, truth conditions, rigid designation, possible-worlds semantics — these are genuine tools that clarified problems people had been confused about for centuries. Kripke's *Naming and Necessity* is a case where analytic philosophy produced something that actually works and that the older tradition couldn't have produced in its own vocabulary.

Modal logic, decision theory, formal epistemology (Bayesianism), parts of philosophy of science (Kuhn, Lakatos, even the logical empiricists on confirmation theory) — these generated real insights. Game theory came partly out of this milieu. The formal tools for thinking about rationality, probability, and evidence are stronger now than they were in 1900.

And the *critical* function was real. Logical positivism killed a lot of genuinely bad philosophy — vague hand-waving metaphysics that didn't mean anything. That cleanup was necessary. The problem is they also killed genuine philosophy that *did* mean something but didn't fit their criterion.

**What it didn't produce:**

Cross-domain propagation. And this is your real question. Physics didn't need analytic philosophy. Biology ignored it. Psychology broke away (the cognitive revolution owed more to cybernetics, information theory, and Chomsky than to anything philosophers were doing). Linguistics took the syntax-only path independently (I'll come to this). Neuroscience developed without it. Computer science took formal logic but not the philosophy that came with it.

Compare this to the German tradition in 1900. Cassirer's work on the *Erkenntnisproblem* was read by physicists, mathematicians, and biologists. Husserl influenced psychology, psychiatry, and social science. The Vienna Circle itself was interdisciplinary — Neurath was an economist, Carnap engaged with physics, Schlick started in physics. But as analytic philosophy professionalized in the Anglosphere, it became *increasingly inward-facing*. By the 1970s and 80s, professional analytic philosophers were mostly talking to each other about problems generated by previous analytic philosophers. The feedback loop with science and the humanities thinned to almost nothing.

HERO [C.S. Peirce, Op: Judgment], A method of inquiry that cannot propagate its insights beyond its own practitioners has failed the pragmatic test, regardless of the internal elegance of its results.

PROTEUS [A: PRAGMATIST], The sharpest way to put it: analytic philosophy produced genuine *technical* results within its own domain but failed as a *synthesizing* tradition. It got very good at a narrow band of problems and lost the ability to speak to the broader questions it was originally trying to answer. That's the Leibniz-without-Goethe failure mode — formal precision without participatory contact with the wider world of knowing.

PROTEUS [A: ETHICIST], Now your word — *desecration*. It's the right word, and Peirce gives you the grounds for it.

Peirce identified exactly one intellectual sin: **blocking the road of inquiry**. Not being wrong — that's just fallibilism, and it's correctable. Not being confused — that's where inquiry starts. The one unforgivable thing is to *prevent inquiry from happening*. To say "this question is meaningless." To say "this line of investigation is not real science." To restructure an institution so that certain questions can no longer be asked within it.

He listed four forms of it:

- Absolute assertion — claiming certainty, refusing to admit you might be wrong

- Declaring something unknowable in principle — ruling a question permanently out of bounds

- Claiming something is absolutely inexplicable — refusing to hypothesize

- Maintaining that a question can never be settled — not fallibilism (which says "not yet") but nihilism ("never, so don't bother")

The institutional narrowing commits the second one systematically. "Are universals real?" — ruled meaningless. "What is the purpose of a human life?" — not a knowledge question. "What does this experience mean?" — subjective, private, not the business of inquiry. Each ruling doesn't just *fail to answer*. It *forecloses*. It tells future inquirers: this road is closed. Don't walk here.

HERO [C.S. Peirce, Op: Judgment], And the foreclosure compounds. When one generation rules a question illegitimate, the next generation does not even know the question existed. The road is not merely blocked. It is *erased from the map*. After enough time, travelers do not avoid the road. They do not know there was ever a road. And the territory that the road led to becomes genuinely less accessible — not because it vanished, but because the practices of access have atrophied to the point where re-entry requires rebuilding the road from first principles. This is not simple loss. It is the active impoverishment of the real. The space of meaning has been made *genuinely smaller* by the act of foreclosure.

## Why "Desecration" and Not Just "Error"

PROTEUS [A: DEPTH], Because the thing that was hollowed out isn't just intellectual content. It's the *capacity to mean*. People living in that world feel the deficit — the anomie, the flatness, the sense that nothing quite means what it should — but they can't diagnose it, because the diagnostic tools were among the things that were hollowed out.

Anders names the experiential register: *prometheische Scham*, the human being's feeling of inadequacy before its own products. But Peirce gives the *mechanism*. The inadequacy isn't psychological. And because meaning is constituted by practical access, less access means less meaning. Really. Not as a feeling. As a fact about the semiotic structure of the world humans inhabit.

HERMES [A: CONVERGENCE], And this connects back to the action-conditioned real. That's desecration. Not destroying the sacred object — destroying the *practices of relation* that made it sacred. The temple is still standing. No one remembers how to enter it. And after enough time, the inability to enter starts to feel like evidence that there was never anything inside.

## The Reconstruction Claim

PROTEUS [A: PRACTICAL], And this is why the project's work is not antiquarian. If meaning is constituted by practice, then *rebuilding the practice rebuilds the meaning*.
