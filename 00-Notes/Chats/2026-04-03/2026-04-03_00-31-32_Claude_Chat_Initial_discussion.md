And here's where history enters — not as a concession to Dilthey or to *Lebensphilosophie*, but as a *shortcut*. The word is almost "mühelos" — effortlessly! History does for free what systematic critique labors to accomplish: it dissolves the phantom of the absolute. HERMES [A: STRATEGIST], Notice how sly this is. Cassirer is making the most radical claim — that relativity *is* productivity, that the historicity of concepts is not a problem but the very life of knowledge — while presenting it as though it were merely a helpful auxiliary to the real (systematic) work. He's smuggling in the engine while calling it a spare part.

**Now the translation.**

Several pressure points worth examining:

**"den inneren gesetzlichen Aufbau"** → "the lawful inner structure." This is adequate but flattens something. *Gesetzlich* carries the weight of *Gesetz* — law — in a way "lawful" in English doesn't quite. "Lawful inner structure" sounds like it's well-behaved; the German means something more like "the inner edifice governed by laws," the sense that science has an internal legal order, a constitution. The word *Aufbau* — building-up, construction, edifice — also does more than "structure," which is static. There's an active, architectonic quality.

**"intellektuelle Teilbedingung des Seins"** → "intellectual partial condition of being." This is the hardest phrase in the paragraph. *Teilbedingung* is precise and technical — a *partial condition*, one of the conditions that together constitute being as known. The English gets the denotation right. But "intellectual partial condition of being" reads awkwardly where the German is compact and rhythmic. And the question the glossary rightly flags haunts it: what is *Sein* doing here? Not "existence" — this is the *being* that thought constitutes through its categories. The translation passes this through, but a reader unfamiliar with the neo-Kantian register will read "being" as a lump of reality rather than as the correlate of knowledge.

**"Trugbild des 'Absoluten'"** → "the phantom of the 'absolute'." Good. *Trugbild* is literally a "deception-image," a mirage or phantasm. "Phantom" captures the right register — something that seems substantial but dissolves on approach. The scare quotes around "Absoluten" in the German are important and preserved; Cassirer is signaling that this is a term he's placing under critique, not one he endorses.

**"als geworden betrachten"** → "viewing... as having come to be." This is the crux and it's handled well. *Geworden* — the past participle of *werden*, becoming — carries enormous philosophical weight in German. To see something "als geworden" is to see it as a product of process rather than a fixed given. The English phrase "having come to be" gets this, but lacks the single-word force of *geworden*. There's no good solution here; English simply doesn't have a one-word equivalent that fuses "having become" with the philosophical resonance of *Werden* running from Hegel through the whole 19th century.

**"immer erneute Produktivität"** → "ever-renewed productivity." Clean and accurate. But worth noting what Cassirer is *doing* with this word at the end of the paragraph. *Produktivität* isn't a throwaway flourish — it's a programmatic term. He's claiming that thought is *productive*, not reproductive. That connects directly to the opening of the Introduction where he demolishes the "naive view" that knowledge reproduces a given reality. The final word of this paragraph is already the thesis of the book.
