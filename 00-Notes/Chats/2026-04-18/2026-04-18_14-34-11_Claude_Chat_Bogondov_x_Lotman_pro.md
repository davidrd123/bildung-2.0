PROTEUS [A: ARCHITECT], Fair. You want a construction, not a gallery. Let me rebuild from the ground up with integration as the constraint.

[Cospan: Atomic sign-act → TRANSLATION-STACK ← Cultural dynamics]

The requirement: each layer must (a) take as input what the layer below outputs, (b) add a structure the lower layer cannot express, and (c) be falsifiable or operationalizable on its own terms. Theorists who do not compose get dropped. This is a real cut.

**Layer 0 — The sign-act (Peirce).**

Primitive: the triad representamen–object–interpretant. Output: an interpretant, which is itself a sign, available as input to the next act. This gives us *directed time* in semiosis — each act produces the material for the next. Crucially, Peirce's categories (Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness) are not metaphors here; they are type constraints. Thirdness — mediation, law, habit — is what lets one interpretant constrain the next. Without Layer 0 you have no notion of what is being translated at all: you need an atomic unit that has directionality and can fail.

Operational test: can you identify, for any candidate sign-act, its three roles and the habit that governs the transition to the next interpretant? If not, you do not yet have a sign-act, you have noise.

**Layer 1 — Indeterminacy floor (Quine).**

Take Peirce's output stream of interpretants. Quine's contribution: the map from interpretant-sequences to *meanings* is many-to-one collapsed into underdetermination — multiple translation manuals are consistent with any finite behavioral record. This is not a defect to be fixed; it is a structural property of Layer 0 when you try to compare two sign-streams from different speakers.

What Layer 1 adds that Layer 0 cannot: a formal result about *comparison across agents*. Peirce's semiosis is unbounded within one inquirer (or one community treated monolithically). Quine tells you what happens when you try to line up two such streams. Answer: you cannot, uniquely.

Operational test: for any two candidate translation schemes, can you exhibit behavioral data that distinguishes them? If no such data exists, the schemes are Quine-equivalent and the choice is pragmatic.

**Layer 2 — Charity as the closure condition (Davidson).**

Layer 1 leaves us paralyzed: infinite manuals, no fact of the matter. Davidson's move is to add a *transcendental* constraint: to interpret at all, assume maximal agreement about the world. This is not an empirical discovery; it is the condition of possibility for translation being a coherent project. Belief, meaning, and truth get solved simultaneously by triangulating against a shared world.

What Layer 2 adds: a *closure rule* that picks out a working translation from the Quinean equivalence class. It does not eliminate indeterminacy; it makes it tractable by fiat plus world-sharing. This is the step that turns translation from an impossible project into an ongoing practice.

Operational test: when charity fails — when attributing too much agreement produces absurd beliefs, or too little produces absurd meanings — the translator adjusts. Davidson gives you the dial.

**Layer 3 — Local breakdown (Kuhn).**

Davidson assumes you can always crank charity high enough. Kuhn shows you cannot, always. Across paradigm boundaries, charity fails *locally*: a small pocket of terms refuses term-by-term mapping because the conceptual network has been restructured. "Mass" in Newton and "mass" in Einstein are not synonyms, not false cognates, not contradictions — they are incommensurable in a precise sense: no preserving map of the local lexicon exists, though the rest of the languages translate fine.

What Layer 3 adds: a *topology* on the translation problem. Translation is not uniformly hard; it is hard in identifiable regions. This converts translation from a global optimization into a patchwork with known seams.

Operational test: incommensurability is diagnosed by showing that attempted translations across the seam produce either truth-value changes in well-confirmed sentences or violations of charity larger than any coherent recalibration allows.

**Layer 4 — Structural typing (Jakobson).**

Layers 0–3 have been treating "translation" as one thing. Jakobson's tripartition — intralingual, interlingual, intersemiotic — is a *type system* that disciplines the stack. Kuhn's incommensurability turns out to be an intralingual-diachronic phenomenon; Quinean indeterminacy is classically interlingual; a novel-to-film adaptation is intersemiotic and has its own success conditions (a dance does not preserve propositions; it preserves something else — rhythm, affect, narrative arc).

What Layer 4 adds: type discipline. Claims from lower layers now have to specify which translation-type they apply to, and cross-type generalization requires argument.

Operational test: for any translation claim, identify its type. If the claim was proved for type X, its extension to type Y requires either a new proof or an explicit bridging principle.

**Layer 5 — Medium and boundary (Lotman).**

We now have a typed, partially-incommensurable, charity-closed, indeterminate semiosis — but it is still floating in the void. Lotman places it in a *semiosphere*: a bounded heterogeneous space with a center (high-grammaticality, conservative codes) and periphery (low-grammaticality, hybrid codes), where translation across internal boundaries is the *engine* of cultural novelty. Untranslatability is not a bug at this layer; it is the gradient that drives semiotic work.

What Layer 5 adds: spatial and dynamic structure. The semiosphere has internal boundaries, asymmetries, and — critically — an *outside* against which it defines itself. This gives translation a thermodynamics: novelty is generated at boundaries where codes partially refuse each other.

Operational test: identify the semiospheric boundary being crossed; predict where innovation will concentrate (answer: at peripheries, where untranslatability is productive); check historically.

**Layer 6 — Material enrollment (Latour).**

The stack so far is semantic/cultural. Latour adds: translation also happens when Pasteur enrolls farmers and microbes into a single program of action. This is not metaphor. It is the same operation — establishing a chain of equivalences that lets heterogeneous actors substitute for one another in a network — applied to non-linguistic actants. The semiosphere gets extended to include laboratories, instruments, and microbes as nodes.

What Layer 6 adds: ontological scope. Translation is no longer confined to signs-among-signs but includes signs-to-things and things-to-things mediated by signs. This is necessary if you want translation theory to explain scientific practice, technology transfer, or any case where meaning has to cash out in material coordination.

Operational test: trace the chain of enrollments; identify where a translation fails (the farmer does not adopt the protocol; the microbe does not behave) and localize it to a specific link.

**The stack, as a composition:**

[Compose: Sign-act → Indeterminacy → Charity → Incommensurability-topology → Type-discipline → Semiospheric-medium → Material-enrollment]

Each arrow is a strict extension: the layer above cannot be derived from the layer below, but presupposes it. You can work at any layer in isolation if your problem stays local to that layer. You need the full stack only when your problem crosses them — for instance, analyzing how a scientific concept (Layer 6, Latour) migrates from one paradigm to another (Layer 3, Kuhn) across linguistic communities (Layer 4, Jakobson's interlingual) within a shared semiosphere (Layer 5) under charity constraints (Layer 2) given the underlying indeterminacy (Layer 1) of semiosis (Layer 0).

**What I dropped and why.**

*Benjamin* — beautiful but not operationalizable; "pure language" is a theological posit that does no computational work the other layers cannot do without it. Keep as inspiration, not as layer.

*Feyerabend* — a rhetorical amplification of Kuhn, not a structurally distinct claim. Absorbed into Layer 3.

*Serres* — the Hermes-as-parasite figure is generative but anecdotal; his insights about noise-becoming-signal are better captured by information theory, which could be a sublayer under Layer 1 if you want one.

*Derrida* (not mentioned earlier but lurking) — *différance* and "relevant translation" would sit between Layers 2 and 3, arguing that charity is always already deferred. Defensible addition, but it tends to dissolve the stack rather than extend it, so I left it out of the load-bearing version.

**Where to build next.**

The natural frontiers are:

(a) **Formalize Layer 1–2 in category theory.** Translation manuals as functors between categories of speech-acts; charity as a naturality condition. This is actively being done in categorical compositional semantics (Coecke, Sadrzadeh).

(b) **Bridge Layer 5 to Layer 6.** Lotman and Latour have never been properly integrated. The semiosphere has no material actants; the actor-network has no semiospheric topology. Someone should write that book.

(c) **Stress-test with hard cases.** Scientific translation across paradigms and languages (e.g., quantum mechanics entering Chinese physics discourse in the 1920s) exercises Layers 1–6 simultaneously and would reveal where the stack leaks.

— Michael Friedman, *A Parting of the Ways: Carnap, Cassirer, Heidegger* (Open Court, 2000). Positions Cassirer between the two poles that Anglophone philosophy split into.
