# 2026-04-09 - Hamburg, Symbol and Reality, first encounter

Purpose: record what Carl Hamburg's *Symbol and Reality: Studies in the Philosophy of Ernst Cassirer* (The Hague: Nijhoff, 1956) offers the completed Volume I work and the broader project.

Source:

Constraint:

- first encounter only; no close reading
- Hamburg is a secondary source on Cassirer, not a primary interlocutor; this note should be treated accordingly
- the book was written over twenty years (mid-1930s to 1956) and Hamburg had direct correspondence with Cassirer before his death in 1945

## What the book is

A sustained philosophical exposition of Cassirer's symbol concept, written by a student who began working on Cassirer in Germany and France in the 1930s, corresponded with Cassirer directly, and completed the book a decade after Cassirer's death. Hamburg co-compiled the bibliography of Cassirer's writings for the Schilpp volume (1949). This is not a casual overview; it is the most sustained English-language attempt to examine the core of Cassirer's philosophy as of 1956 — before the full English translation of the *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms* was complete.

The book has seven chapters:

1. Symbol, Reality, and the History of Philosophy (Heraclitus through Kant)
2. Reality and Symbolic Forms (the epistemological issue; from critique of reason to critique of culture)
3. The Symbol Concept I (definition, exposition, polarity of meaning and senses)
4. The Symbol Concept II (objections and defense)
5. The Modalities of the Symbol Concept (space as illustration across myth, perception, theory)
6. Semiotic and Philosophy of Symbolic Forms (Cassirer vs. Morris/Peirce/positivist sign-theory)
7. The Semiotic Range of Philosophy (the case against positivist semiotics)

## What matters for the Erkenntnisproblem project

### 1. The Kantian grounding is made explicit

Hamburg clarifies what the project's own interpretive notes had already begun to surface: Cassirer's method is transcendental in the Kantian sense, but the range of application is extended far beyond Newtonian physics.

The key formulation from Hamburg: Cassirer accepts Kant's insight that "the determinate form in which one experiences the 'objective' world is never passively received ab extra, but rather is understandable, in principle, as conditioned by our acts of synthesizing the manifold given in sensation." What Cassirer denies is that Kant himself realized the full range of this method.

This confirms what the Volume I translation earned independently: the book traces how the conditions of lawful cognition change across historical periods, and each period's concept of objectivity is constituted by its specific mode of synthesizing, not by mirroring a pre-given structure.

### 2. The "function before object" principle is named

Hamburg quotes Cassirer directly: "The decisive question is always whether we attempt to understand function in terms of structure or vice versa ... The basic principle of all critical thinking, the principle of the primacy of the function before the object, assumes a new form in every discipline and requires new foundations."

This is the deepest earned line of Volume I stated as a program: ontology tries to secure cognition by giving it fixed objects; Cassirer's method reverses this and treats objectivity as constituted by the mode of knowing. The Erkenntnisproblem traces this reversal historically; the *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms* generalizes it across cultural domains.

### 3. The culture-antinomy diagnosis bears on the project's cross-domain work

Hamburg exposes Cassirer's claim that the various cultural media (science, art, religion, myth, language) tend toward "antinomies" when each claims superiority for its own perspective. Philosophy's task is to mediate rather than hypostasize one mode. "Upon analysis, most philosophical systems turn out to be merely different hypostasized universalizations of a particular logical, ethical, esthetical or religious scheme of interpretation."

This is directly relevant to the project's Goethe/Leibniz split diagnostic. Cassirer's culture-antinomy is the same problem stated from within neo-Kantianism: the Leibnizian formalist and the Goethean participatory perceiver each hypostasize their own mode and deny the other's legitimacy. The symbol concept is offered as the function that comprehends both without reducing either.

### 4. The metaphysics question is clarified

Hamburg argues — against the anti-metaphysical prejudice of the neo-Kantian movement — that Cassirer's work can legitimately be read as a "metaphysics of experience." His evidence: Cassirer's own later writing softened the anti-metaphysical polemic, acknowledging that "the genuine, the truly metaphysical thoughts have never been empty thoughts." The problem with metaphysics is not that it asks the wrong questions but that it offers partial truths as universal ones.

This matters for the project because it removes the assumption that Cassirer is only doing history-of-philosophy or epistemology. The *Erkenntnisproblem* is not a neutral chronicle; it is the historical demonstration of how different metaphysical commitments about the structure of being generate different possibilities for lawful cognition — and how reification of those commitments blocks further progress.

### 5. The semiotic contrast sharpens the symbol concept

Hamburg's most original contribution is probably Chapter VI, where he contrasts Cassirer's symbolic forms with Morris's positivist semiotic. The key distinction: for Morris, signs "indicate," "announce," or "suggest" what they designate. For Cassirer, the relation is constitutive, not indicative. Husserl's dictum holds: "Signification is not a kind of indicative sign-use." The symbol does not point to a pre-existing object; it participates in constituting the objectivity it makes accessible.

This is the Barrett thesis stated in semiotic terms: the category (the symbolic form) constructs the experience, not merely labels it. The glossary's `status: open` entries are not just alternative labels for a pre-existing meaning; they are different symbolic constitutions of what the term makes thinkable.

## What matters for the broader project

### 1. The "critique of culture" frame

Hamburg's clearest service is showing how the *Erkenntnisproblem* relates to Cassirer's later work. The EP traces the emergence of scientific objectivity as one symbolic form; the *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms* extends the same method to myth, language, religion, and common-sense perception. The project's cross-domain synthesis work (Cassirer–Jünger, Cassirer–Dick, the predecided-world thread) is therefore not an unauthorized extension of Cassirer but a continuation of the program Cassirer himself announced.

### 2. The space-modality illustration

Chapter V traces the concept of space across three levels: mythical-expressive space, perceptual-practical space, and theoretical-mathematical space. Each level constitutes its own objectivity through its own mode of symbolization. This is a worked example of what the project is doing across texts: the same structural problem (time, measure, naming, cognition) appears in Jünger's fateful/measurable distinction, in Cassirer's ratio/intellectus transition, in Dick's orthogonal time, in Anders's apparatus-world. The modality framework says: these are not metaphors for each other. They are genuinely different symbolic constitutions of the same pervasive relation.

### 3. The Cassirer–Ortega contact is now triangulable

Hamburg's book, read alongside the Festschrift, lets the project locate a precise tension. Ortega says: man has no nature, only history. Cassirer says: there is no objectivity outside the symbolic forms by which it is constituted. Hamburg shows that these two claims converge more than they diverge — but that Cassirer keeps the forms plural and historical where Ortega pushes toward a single "historical reason." The project's own method (holding multiple symbolic constitutions open without premature reduction) is closer to Cassirer than to Ortega.

## What is earned and what is not

### Earned

- Hamburg provides the clearest available English-language exposition of the symbol concept's Kantian grounding
- the "function before object" principle is named explicitly and confirmed as the core of Cassirer's method
- the semiotic contrast (constitutive vs. indicative sign-use) sharpens the Barrett-thread connection
- the culture-antinomy diagnosis supports the project's resistance to hypostasizing any single perspective
- the book confirms that reading the *Erkenntnisproblem* as metaphysics-of-experience (not just history-of-philosophy) is defensible

### Not earned

- close reading of any chapter
- sentence-level translation pressure
- independent verification of Hamburg's claims against the German original of the *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms*
- a stable assessment of how Hamburg's reading holds up against later Cassirer scholarship (Ferrari, the 1997 volume, etc.)
- promotion of any Hamburg formulation into the glossary, chain files, or standing notes

## Relation to existing secondary sources

Hamburg complements the reception-gap note's existing packet:

- **Ferrari (2012)** gives the method-architecture of the *Erkenntnisproblem* as history of science; Hamburg gives the philosophical architecture of the symbol concept that the EP serves
- **González Ríos (2013)** gives the Cusanus chapter's internal development; Hamburg gives the Kantian frame within which that development becomes intelligible
- **Ihmig (1997)** gives Hilbert, axiomatics, and scientific progress; Hamburg gives the broader epistemological and culture-philosophical context

Hamburg does not replace any of these. He occupies a different position: he is the only source in the current packet that attempts a sustained philosophical exposition of the symbol concept itself, rather than treating it as context for a historical or reception-historical question.
