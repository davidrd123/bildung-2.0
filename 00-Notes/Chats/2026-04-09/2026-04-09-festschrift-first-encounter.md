## What the volume is

The 1936 Festschrift was presented to Cassirer on his sixtieth birthday. It was produced through the Warburg Institute, edited by Klibansky (who was also Cassirer's student and later editor of the Cusanus critical edition) and H.J. Paton. Contributors include Huizinga, Alexander, Brunschvicg, Calogero, Webb, Gilson, Groethuysen, Stebbing, Gentile, Litt, Medicus, Bréhier, Hoffmann, Lévy-Bruhl, Saxl, Panofsky, Wind, Pos, Gundolf, Ortega y Gasset, and Klibansky.

The governing theme is the relation between philosophy and history. The preface says: the variety of approaches "may at first sight seem rather disconnected; but perhaps we may plead that they at least reflect the complexity of the subject itself."

The preface also quotes Nicholas of Cusa: *Una veritas in variis signis varie resplendet.* That is not accidental — it is a direct Cassirerian and Cusanian claim about how a single truth refracts through multiple symbolic forms.

The 1963 Torchbook reprint adds a note from Klibansky and Paton acknowledging that many contributors have died and some survivors have changed their views, but the volume was reprinted without changes.

## Priority map

### Read now

#### 1. Ortega y Gasset, "History as a System" (pp. 283–322)

Strongest single essay for the current project.

Why it matters:

- Ortega argues that man has no *nature* in the Eleatic sense — no fixed, identical, predetermined being. Man is "not a thing but a drama — his life."
- He traces the concept of *natura* from Aristotle through Boyle to Comte and declares: the whole line is Eleatic. Even positivism's "invariability of the laws of nature" is still identical being, still the projection of the concept's mode of being onto reality.
- He explicitly cites Cassirer's *Erkenntnisproblem* (Vol. II, p. 433) on Boyle's definition of nature as law-of-phenomena. That means Ortega is reading Cassirer's historical reconstruction and using it as evidence for his own counter-thesis.
- His proposed replacement — *historical reason* — "accepts nothing as a mere fact: it makes every fact fluid in the *fieri* whence it comes." This is a direct cousin of the action-conditioned-real thread.
- His critique of the *Geisteswissenschaften* is sharp: *Geist* is still *res*, still identity, still Eleatic, only dressed in incorporeal clothing. The protest against naturalism failed because it left the deeper ontological assumption intact. This matters for the project's own handling of Dilthey-adjacent vocabulary.
- His phrase: "we must learn to dis-intellectualize the real if we are to be faithful to it" — this is the anti-reification thesis stated at the level of ontology, not only of method.

Contact with Erkenntnisproblem Volume I:

- Cassirer's deepest earned line is: ontology repeatedly tries to secure cognition, method repeatedly loosens it, exact science appears when relation and law replace substance. Ortega takes this further: the entire ontological tradition — including the concept of *natura* that Cassirer reconstructs — must be left behind for human reality. Where Cassirer traces the *emergence* of lawful relation within an inherited ontological frame, Ortega declares the frame itself must go.
- This is a productive break, not a simple extension. Cassirer's historical method depends on continuity-within-rupture; Ortega is willing to break more sharply.
- The Bayle close of Volume I — critique that can dissolve but not build — is exactly what Ortega is trying to move beyond by replacing destructive reason with *historical* reason.

Contact with broader project:

- the Jünger time-crisis and the predecided-world thread converge here: Ortega's "life is a task" and "man is the novelist of himself" are cousins of Jünger's passage-figures who must undergo something, not merely describe it
- Ortega's attack on the Greek kalends resonates with the action-conditioned-real: don't defer truth to a future physics of morality; the knower must be adequate *now*

#### 2. Edgar Wind, "Some Points of Contact Between History and Natural Science" (pp. 255–264)

Strongest methodological essay for the project's own self-understanding.

Why it matters:

- Wind identifies three structural isomorphisms between historical and natural-scientific method:
  1. **Circularity of document and instrument.** Both participate in the structure they are meant to reveal. The historian judges documents by the very events he consults them about. The physicist measures with instruments subject to the laws he is testing. "Every instrument and every document participates in the structure which it is meant to reveal."
  2. **Intrusion of the observer.** The investigator disturbs what he investigates. The historian who drags a forgotten document from an archive disturbs the historical process. The physicist who splits a beam disturbs the nexus of nature. This is not metaphor.
  3. **Self-transformation of the knower.** Every successful disturbance modifies the investigator's own equipment. Wind quotes Eddington: "We have found a strange foot-print on the shores of the unknown... And lo! it is our own."
- Wind dismantles Dilthey's Natur/Geist division by showing that inner experience is not transparent and that the circular, participant, self-transforming character of inquiry is the same in both domains.
- He cites Peirce: "it is the belief men betray, and not that which they parade, which has to be studied." This is a sharper empiricism than Dilthey's appeal to lived experience.

Contact with Erkenntnisproblem Volume I:

- the circularity of document and instrument is structurally identical to Cusanus's `Zirkel im Geiste`: the mind's own forms participate in what they reveal about the world
- Wind's self-transformation of the knower maps directly onto the interpretive-notes finding about the Barrett connection: categories construct experience, and the translation method itself changes what the translator can perceive
- the journal's own discovery that the apparatus mutates under pressure from the material is a local enactment of Wind's thesis

Contact with broader project:

- Wind's three isomorphisms are the Warburg-school version of the action-conditioned-real thread
- the von Foerster thread ("if you desire to see, learn how to act") is the cybernetic descendant of exactly this argument
- Wind's essay is evidence that the Cassirer circle already understood what the project is rediscovering: that method and object co-constitute one another

#### 3. Klibansky, "The Philosophic Character of History" (pp. 323–337)

Best essay for the project's relation between time, science, and formation.

Why it matters:

- Klibansky argues that history works under the constitutive category of qualitative time: "the backward look into the past is most intimately bound up with the forward look into the future"
- He proposes that the concept of science cannot be static. It must be reconceived as *intentio* — in three senses: Stoic (germ of reason, force stirring consciousness), Kantian (regulative principle ordering phenomena), Platonic (normative pattern toward which empirical knowledge is directed)
- His closing contrast: physics transcends time by eliminating it (Minkowski's space-time union); history transcends time by deeper immersion in past destiny and "ever new condensation of its changing appearances into lasting pictures"
- His critique of the dogmatists: every finding of meaning implies an imparting of meaning, but this does not reduce history to subjectivism — it means objectivity is a *tendency of consciousness*, not a fixed confrontation

Contact with Erkenntnisproblem Volume I:

- Klibansky's dynamic concept of science as *intentio* is what the journal's own five-phase mutation enacts: the apparatus kept changing function because the concept of what the work was doing could not remain static
- the close-reading ledger's discipline of catching sentence-scale gains before they vanish is a local version of Klibansky's "condensation of changing appearances into lasting pictures"

Contact with broader project:

- the Jünger measurable/fateful time distinction is precisely Klibansky's contrast between physics-time (eliminated) and history-time (immersed)
- the Cassirer–Jünger method-break cross-project note already names this asymmetry: Cassirer traces the rise of measurable order, Jünger diagnoses what that order leaves under pressure. Klibansky gives the epistemological ground for why both are needed

### Keep nearby

#### 4. Panofsky, "Et in Arcadia Ego" (pp. 223–254)

Not directly about epistemology, but a masterpiece of Warburg-method iconography.

Why it matters for the project:

- tracks how a Latin formula changes meaning as it passes through different visual and literary contexts: from Death's threat ("Even in Arcadia, there am I") to human elegy ("I too lived in Arcadia") — through a *grammatical error* that Poussin's painting made aesthetically necessary
- this is a micro-instance of the Barrett thesis: the category (here, a grammatical parsing) constructs the experience; the "wrong" reading became the culturally generative one because it matched the image's emotional truth
- the transformation from *memento mori* to "serene resignation" and "metamorphosis" parallels the project's larger interest in how forms of knowing change the field of what is knowable

#### 5. Pos, "The Philosophical Significance of Comparative Semantics" (pp. 265–276)

Directly addresses Cassirer's *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms* and Humboldt's insight that conceptual worlds are irreducible.

Why it matters:

- argues that intellectual forms are not interchangeable and the system of possible meanings has never been drawn up in advance
- philosophical knowledge is "the product of the interpenetration, within certain actual limits, of alien categories and those proper to itself"
- this is the theoretical ground for the glossary method: `status: open` entries exist because alien categories cannot be prematurely reduced to the investigator's own

#### 6. Gundolf, "Historiography" (pp. 277–282)

An unfinished introduction to a book on German historians from Herder to Burckhardt.

Why it matters:

- traces the two fundamental impulses of historiography: the desire to preserve what actually was, and the desire to interpret events in the light of universal forces
- names the permanent tension between seeing/telling and viewing-in-perspective/interpreting as structural to the discipline, not a defect to be resolved
- this is a genealogical cousin of the project's Goethe/Leibniz split

## What is earned and what is not

### Earned from this first encounter

- The Festschrift is a genuine interlocutor for the completed Volume I, not only for later Cassirer work
- Ortega, Wind, and Klibansky each independently pressure the same threshold the project has been working: where method, knower, and object co-constitute one another and where fixed ontological assumptions must give way
- Ortega's direct citation of the *Erkenntnisproblem* confirms that the book was read by contemporaries as bearing on the question of *natura* and its Eleatic inheritance, not only as antiquarian history of philosophy
- Wind's three isomorphisms provide an independent derivation of the action-conditioned-real thesis from within the Warburg circle
- The volume as a whole is evidence of the intellectual milieu the project's higher-purpose distillation describes as shattered after 1933: Warburg Institute, Hamburg, Cassirer's network of interlocutors across countries and disciplines
