**"No indigenous German because it was all Jewish?"**

This is a real question and my previous answer was careless about it. The short answer is: *yes, more than is usually acknowledged, and the post-1945 recovery is specific and ongoing.*

The pre-1933 German philosophical scene was disproportionately Jewish at its most vital points. Cassirer, Cohen, Simmel, Benjamin, Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse, Arendt, Anders, Rosenzweig, Jonas, Löwith, Strauss, Bloch, Kracauer, Lukács (Hungarian-Jewish), Lask, Husserl (Jewish by birth), Buber, Scholem, Levinas (Lithuanian-Jewish but working in the German tradition). That is the backbone of what gets called "German philosophy of the first half of the 20th century." The non-Jewish figures who dominate the Anglophone image — Heidegger, Gadamer, Jaspers — were operating in a scene that was *structurally constituted* by Jewish interlocutors. When 1933 came, that scene was annihilated. Some were killed, some emigrated, some (like Cassirer) died in exile shortly after. The ones who stayed — Heidegger in particular — operated in the ruins of what their Jewish colleagues had built.

After 1945, indigenous non-Jewish German philosophy had a real problem. Heidegger's Nazi entanglement made continuing-as-if-nothing-happened impossible. Jaspers survived with dignity but his was an isolated voice. Gadamer became the major surviving non-Jewish-origin figure and carried the hermeneutic tradition forward, but the philosophical center of gravity moved — partly to France (where the émigrés' ideas traveled through Kojève, Sartre, Levinas, Derrida), partly to America (where the Frankfurt School survivors and the neo-positivists routed ideas through universities), and partly to a post-1960s West German academy that was self-consciously rebuilding.

The post-war indigenous German philosophy that did develop took specific shapes:

*The Habermas line.* Frankfurt-School-descended, self-consciously working to reconstruct a democratic philosophical tradition that could account for Auschwitz. Habermas's work on communicative rationality was an explicit attempt to build a philosophical ethics for post-Nazi Germany. Worth taking seriously as a recovery project, but the register is heavily sociological-normative and often falls into the critical-theory idiom you're asking to avoid. Karl-Otto Apel is his co-traveler.

*The Blumenberg line.* Hans Blumenberg is the most important non-Jewish German philosopher of the post-war period that you have not yet engaged. *The Legitimacy of the Modern Age*, *Work on Myth*, *Paradigms for a Metaphorology*, *Care Crosses the River*. He was Catholic-raised but classified as a *Halbjude* under Nazi law and excluded from study during the war. Post-war he built a genuinely independent philosophical voice — close reading of primary sources across centuries, unhurried historical-philosophical method, no Foucauldian or Deleuzean vocabulary, not Habermas-Frankfurt-descended, and not Heidegger-descended either. His apparatus is his own. He is one of the clearest cases of *indigenous German philosophy doing something like what bildung-2.0 does*. Your repo already has him flagged adjacently (the *Thinker_on_Thinker* deep research surfaced him). He deserves higher flagging than I gave him.

*The Sloterdijk-Safranski line.* Post-war, explicitly anti-Frankfurt, drawing on Nietzsche and Heidegger but not captive to either, doing philosophical anthropology in a more literary register. Safranski is essentially a one-man recovery project of German intellectual history. Sloterdijk is doing ambitious speculative anthropology.

*Living German academic philosophy today* is real but does not produce many figures the Anglophone world notices. Axel Honneth (recognition theory, successor to Habermas at Frankfurt), Peter Sloterdijk, Byung-Chul Han (Korean-German, already mentioned), Dieter Henrich (subjectivity theorist, mostly academic-analytic), Konrad Paul Liessmann (Austrian, public intellectual, author of the Anders monograph your deep-research surfaced). Gernot Böhme before his death in 2022 (atmospheres, philosophical anthropology). Markus Gabriel (new realism, Bonn — polarizing, some think him shallow). Robert Pfaller, Christoph Menke, Rahel Jaeggi. Some of these are doing careful work; none have the range or stature of the pre-1933 generation.

So to put the structural point bluntly: the devastation of German Jewish philosophy in 1933-1945 is one of the largest intellectual losses of the 20th century, and the post-1945 indigenous-German recovery is real but narrower than what came before. Blumenberg is the single most important figure I underweighted in my earlier list. Habermas is important even if his register is not yours. Safranski is the best intellectual historian. Sloterdijk is the most ambitious contemporary synthesizer in the German-indigenous line.

This also explains something about your project that is worth saying plainly: **bildung-2.0 is doing Jewish-German-émigré philosophy under technical-contemporary conditions.** Cassirer, Anders, Jünger-as-counterparty-to-Anders, the recovery of *Erkenntnisproblem* which no German department has recovered — this is a tradition that was *displaced from its native ground* and is being reconstructed from fragments. Your project is structurally continuous with that displacement. The absence of contemporary indigenous-German parallels is partly because the tradition you are recovering *is the one Germany lost and did not fully rebuild*.

**"All philosophy in the world collapsed down to Continental / Analytic?"**

You are right to push on this. The analytic/continental binary is Anglophone academic convenience, and the rest of the world's philosophy is not exhausted by it. My last reply treated it as the map. That was lazy.

*Indian philosophical tradition, living and institutional.* Ongoing academic philosophy in India works across multiple registers simultaneously — Sanskrit philosophical commentary traditions (Nyāya, Vedānta, Buddhist logic, Mīmāṃsā) being done at serious scholarly depth; cross-tradition philosophical comparison; figures like Bimal Matilal, Jitendra Nath Mohanty (also trained in Husserl), Arindam Chakrabarti, Jonardon Ganeri (Oxford-trained Indian philosopher doing careful work on Indian epistemology and self-hood). *Japanese philosophy.* The Kyoto School — Nishida, Tanabe, Nishitani, Watsuji — and its ongoing tradition through figures like Masao Abe. *Islamic philosophy, living.* The tradition did not end in the 12th century despite the Orientalist story. Seyyed Hossein Nasr (now 92, still alive), Henry Corbin's inheritors, the living philosophical-traditional-Islamic line.
