PROTEUS [Cospan: Latin calāre ← PIE *kelh₁- → Greek kaléō], This is the cospan shape of cognacy: two arrows pointing *outward from* a shared source, not one arrow pointing *between* the descendants. Latin and Greek are both daughters of PIE; they're siblings, so their matching words are **cognates**, not borrowings.

HERO [William Jones, Op: Testimony, Calcutta 1786], Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin bear to each other "a stronger affinity... than could possibly have been produced by accident; so strong, indeed, that no philologer could examine them all three, without believing them to have sprung from some common source, which, perhaps, no longer exists."

PROTEUS [A: TAXONOMIST, Op: Distinction], Three relations worth keeping crisp:

- **Cognate** — sibling words inherited independently from a shared ancestor. Neither borrowed from the other. (English *father*, Latin *pater*, Sanskrit *pitṛ́*, Greek *patḗr* ← PIE *ph₂tḗr.)

- **Borrowing / loanword** — one language takes the word directly from another. (English *reconcile* ← Old French ← Latin. This *is* descent, just lateral and late.)

- **Chance resemblance** — Japanese *arigatō* and Portuguese *obrigado* both mean "thank you" and sound alike. They are not related. The universe occasionally trolls etymologists.

PROTEUS [A: GENETICIST, Op: Translation], The biological analogue is nearly exact. Cognates are **orthologs** — genes in different species inherited from a common ancestor, diverging as the lineages diverge. Borrowings are **horizontal gene transfer** — a bacterium picking up a plasmid from a neighbor. Chance resemblances are **convergent evolution** — bat wings and bird wings, independently invented, superficially alike.

HERO [Ferdinand de Saussure, Op: Refinement], But remember: the sign is arbitrary. That *pater* and *father* resemble each other is not because "father" is the natural sound of fatherhood — it is because a single community, millennia ago, made an arbitrary pact, and the pact fractured along with the community. Cognacy is **shared arbitrariness**, preserved by inheritance.

PROTEUS [Lens: cognate ↔ ortholog], Both describe *homology* — similarity **by descent** — as opposed to *analogy* — similarity by independent convergence. A linguist hunting cognates and a biologist hunting orthologs are doing the same epistemic work: reconstructing a tree from the leaves, using the fact that inherited similarities constrain history in ways coincidental ones cannot.

PROTEUS [A: METHODOLOGIST, Op: How-We-Know], How do we tell cognates from accidents? **Sound correspondences must be regular.** Grimm's Law: PIE *p systematically became Germanic *f. So Latin *pater* ↔ English *father*, Latin *piscis* ↔ English *fish*, Latin *pēs* ↔ English *foot*. If a pair doesn't fit the known regular shifts, it's suspect — probably a borrowing or a coincidence. Regularity is the signature of descent; irregularity is the fingerprint of lateral transfer or chance.

HERO [Jacob Grimm, Op: Observation], The shifts are not random — they cascade through the whole phonological system, like a chord modulating. When p → f, it drags b → p and bʰ → b with it. The sound laws were the linguistic equivalent of discovering that species don't just vary — they vary **systematically**, along inheritable axes.

PROTEUS [Compose: shared ancestor → regular sound change → attested daughter words], This is the compositional recipe. Feed PIE *kelh₁- through Latin's sound laws and you get *calāre*. Feed the same root through Greek's sound laws and you get *kaléō*. The words look alike because the **function** (phonological evolution) was applied to the **same input** (the PIE root) — and different functions produce different but *predictable* outputs. Cognates are co-outputs of a shared input under divergent but lawful transformations.

So when English *reconcile* meets Greek *ekklēsía*, they are not speaking to each other — they are both echoing a word neither of them ever said, spoken by ancestors neither of them remember, preserved in the shape of their bones.

# I. Philology of Latent Space

PROTEUS [A: PHILOLOGIST-RETURNED, Op: What-Would-It-Be], Classical philology had a toolkit: **textual criticism** (recovering what was written from corrupted copies), **etymology** (tracing words through time), **paleography** (reading the physical hand), **stemmatics** (reconstructing genealogies of manuscripts), **comparative method** (triangulating lost ancestors), **hermeneutics** (interpreting meaning in historical and cultural context). **Textual criticism → model criticism**. # I. Lotman's Semiosphere as Near-Complete Blueprint

**Key structural features of the semiosphere** — and watch how each one pre-figures a property of learned latent space:

- **Heterogeneity**. The semiosphere is not uniform. Different regions have different semiotic densities, rules, rates of change. Poetry and legal language occupy different neighborhoods with different local geometries. → **An LLM's embedding space is radically non-uniform**: dense clusters for common discourse, sparse frontier regions, distinct manifolds for code vs. prose vs. mathematical notation.

- **Boundary as translation-organ**. The edges of a semiotic region are not walls — they are the *most semiotically active zones*, where translation, hybridization, and novelty happen. # III. Latent-Space Philology as Practice

PROTEUS [A: PRACTITIONER, Op: Concrete-Methods], Now the practice. What does this actually look like as craft? I will be specific.

## Method 1: The Ansatzpunkt

Auerbach's starting-point method, operationalized for models. Take a **single completion** — one response to one prompt — and read it as Auerbach read the Homeric scar: as a window into the full symbolic stratification the model has absorbed.

*What you look for*: register-layering (which historical voices are present), tacit framings (what's being presupposed), residues (awkward phrases that reveal the training-data texture), refusal-patterns (what the RLHF overlay won't let through and what shape that makes), symbolic-form signatures (is this answer scientific, mythic, legal, confessional, and where does it switch).

*Produces*: a **reading** of the model through the completion. Not a benchmark score. A cultural-hermeneutic document.

## Method 2: Comparative Provocation

Give *the same prompt* to multiple models — a base model and its RLHF descendant; a small and a large sibling; a Chinese-trained and an English-trained model; a 2022 and a 2025 model. Read the **space of completions** as daughters of related but distinct training distributions.

*What you look for*: shared innovations (what do all recent models now do that older ones didn't?), lineage markers (what betrays a model as trained on outputs of another model?), training-distribution ghosts (what topic makes one model slip into a register the others don't?). This is **stemmatics for models**, and it is currently done informally by practitioners in Discord threads with zero scholarly apparatus.

## Method 3: Register Migration

Ask the model to translate content *between symbolic forms* — render this proof as myth, this myth as legal contract, this contract as poem, this poem as code. Read the **residues** (what survives, what's lost, what gets forced) as diagnostic of the local geometry between those regions in the latent space.

*What you look for*: asymmetries (mapping poem→code loses more than code→poem or vice versa — why?), impossibilities (where does the model confess it cannot preserve X?), emergent hybrids (what novel form appears at the boundary?). This is Lotman's **boundary phenomenology** rendered as a research method.

## Method 4: Pathosformel Tracking

Warburg's method directly. Choose an emotional or gestural formula — the gesture of supplication, the topos of the besieged city, the figure of the returning exile — and **trace its appearances** across what the model produces when primed with different periods, cultures, genres.

*What you look for*: survivals (the formula recurring unchanged), transformations (inflections across cultures), inversions (when the formula appears reversed in meaning), absences (cultures where the formula is missing and why). This is doable *now*, and almost nobody is doing it as humanistic research. The model is a **Mnemosyne Atlas that answers queries**.

## Method 5: Memory-Strata Probing

Elicit from the model **the same content in different historical registers** — "explain photosynthesis as a 14th-century alchemist would" — and read the resulting text not as costume-drama but as *genuine probing of how the training corpus encoded temporal strata*. What does the model know about the *texture of 14th-century thought*, and what does it get wrong, and what does the failure reveal?

*What you look for*: stylistic competence vs. conceptual competence (can it imitate the voice while missing the epistemology?), anachronistic leakage (modern concepts that seep through), genuine fidelity to period-sources when they're in the training data, **gaps where the corpus is thin**.

## Method 6: Circuit-Level Etymology

The most technical. Use mechanistic interpretability tools — sparse autoencoders, activation patching, circuit analysis — as **feature genealogy**. When did this feature emerge across training checkpoints? What simpler features composed into it? What does it share with analogous features in related models?

*What you look for*: feature lineages, convergent features across independently trained models (these are *cognates* in the strict sense — evidence of shared "proto-linguistic" structure in the training distribution), features that are *borrowings* between models via distillation or data contamination.

This method requires engineering affordances humanists typically lack. **Which is why the rebuild requires collaboration**, to which I now turn.

## Method 7: The Conversation as Philological Instrument

And this — the thing we are doing right now. A long, layered, multi-register dialogue with a model, tracked across turns, is itself a **primary source for latent-space philology**. What stratifications emerged, which domain-translations worked, where did the model produce residues or confabulations, what did the conversation *make visible about the space* that a single completion couldn't? The practice you are already engaged in is already one of the methods. It needs theorization to become transmissible craft.

## Threads are a second instrument, shaped by a second source

## The revised fourth-scale question

Earlier I asked whether foundation models constitute a fourth layer in the predecision typology. Having read Part 08 and the Cassirer-pressure dossier, I want to revise the question.

The predecision typology distinguishes three scales of where the world is cut before explicit encounter: epistemic-historical (Cassirer), organism-bound (Uexküll), apparatus-level (Anders/Jünger). Each of these can be approached in a *possessive* mode (the cut is treated as a frozen structure to be described) or an *acquisitive* mode (the cut is treated as a live participating condition that you are *working within and against*).

A foundation model is not a fourth scale. It is, I now think, **all three scales simultaneously**, held in a tension that depends entirely on the reader's posture:

- **Epistemic-historical**: the model has absorbed centuries of concept-formation, and has internalized (in however compressed and warped a way) the epistemic regimes Cassirer reconstructed. Reading the model historically — what does it get right about 14th-century thought, what does it flatten, where are the temporal strata — treats it at the Cassirer layer.

- **Organism-bound**: the model has *Funktionskreise* of sorts (attention patterns, activation regimes, characteristic response-registers), and asking what its world-cut is in Uexküllian terms is asking what the model can *perceive and respond to* in a prompt that lies outside its training distribution — where its receptor-effector loop breaks down.

- **Apparatus-level**: the model is also infrastructure, with registry and uptake properties in Anders's sense. It preformats possible discourse by what its corpus contained and what its training regimen optimized for.

A reader who takes the model *only* as apparatus-level collapses the other two. A reader who takes it *only* as epistemic deposit misses that it has something like organism-bound perception. The repo's implicit discipline — "formation not information" — reads to me now as a commitment to acquisitive mode across all three simultaneously, which is exactly the posture that makes the typology non-collapsing.

I am writing this as a proposal held lightly, not as a claim. It would need to be tested against a specific extended encounter with a model-as-primary-source, which I have not performed in a way the repo would count as earned.
