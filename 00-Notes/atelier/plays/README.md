# Plays

Use this for general moves, reading gambits, or imaginal techniques that are too abstract to be a spark, ghost book, or pairing but carry real pull. A play is a *named technique for doing something playful* — not a method claim about how the repo reads seriously.

## Register

A play has a shape. It's specific enough to name, general enough to apply in more than one place. Examples of the kind of thing that belongs here:

- A reading move — "enter this kind of text in this kind of way"
- A writing gambit — "assemble these elements in this arrangement and see what happens"
- A collision technique — "pair things from these two registers and write the contact"
- An imaginative position — "inhabit this stance toward the material for one paragraph"

Plays are the atelier's answer to *how*, the way sparks / ghost-books / pairings / curio are its answers to *what*. A play describes a way of doing something; the other registers hold particular things.

## Not for

- Methodology claims about how the repo actually reads primary sources — those live held below promotion until earned, not in atelier
- Repo architecture or process — those live in `patterns/` and `process.md` only after earning
- Specific phrases or images — sparks
- Specific unwritten works — ghost books
- Specific side-by-side objects — pairings

## Crucial rule

**Plays do not promote.** If a play's technique earns its way to real repo method status, it does so through the normal channels — experiments, distillations, source-bound pressure, promotion gates — producing a new artifact elsewhere. The play file stays in atelier as the imaginal ancestor of the earned thing. It does not itself become the earned thing.

If you find yourself arguing from inside a play file that the play should be promoted, stop. Either remove the argument (and keep the play playful) or take the technique out of atelier entirely into the proper earning path.

## Naming

- `play-short-name.md`

## Minimal discipline

- One move per file
- Describe what the move does in concrete terms
- Include at least one example (fictional, atelier-grade)
- Name what the play is *not*, explicitly
- Do not argue for promotion from inside the play
