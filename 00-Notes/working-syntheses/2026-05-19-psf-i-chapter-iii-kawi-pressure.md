## Working Claim

Chapter III is where Cassirer's Humboldt becomes least generic. Kawi is not
being used merely as a name attached to Humboldt's general doctrine of
language-as-form. It supplies comparative material for Cassirer's account of
how language builds the intuitive world through concrete operations: deixis,
space, time, number, person, verbal action, and possession.

The Kawi opening matters because it frames the object as historically layered:
Kawi is not simply a present vernacular and not simply a dead sacred code. It
is introduced as the language of an extinct, Indian-inflected literature,
preserved in saga, poetry, religion, law, and theater. That makes the
philological question unavoidable: what kind of language is this, and what kind
of formation produced it?

The opening of §23 answers that question in a way that aligns strongly with
Cassirer's use of Humboldt. Humboldt treats Kawi as an older form of Javanese
Landessprache that incorporated Sanskrit words and poetic diction while
retaining Javanese grammatical structure. The decisive point is formal:
vocabulary can be borrowed, but grammatical form carries the character of the
language. Kawi is therefore not corrupted Sanskrit, not Pali, and not a merely
artificial priestly manufacture. It is a formed historical language surface in
which borrowing, poetic elevation, intelligibility, and grammatical continuity
all have to be held together.

## Pressure On Cassirer

This clarifies why Cassirer can use Kawi in chapter III as more than example
stock. His chapter is about `anschaulicher Ausdruck`: the way language first
organizes intuition before the fully abstract concept is available. The Kawi
material gives him philological evidence that the categories we treat as
settled grammar are themselves products of formation.

- In spatial deixis, pointing is already an act of spiritual ordering, not only
  a natural sound.
- In space-verbs, relation begins to move from static substance toward action.
- In nominal time-expression, temporality is not already identical with the
  modern finite verb.
- In number and plural formation, plurality is still group-like, personal, or
  action-bound before it becomes homogeneous mathematical number.
- In the noun/verb boundary, the verbal does not appear as a detachable thing;
  it becomes actual with person, time, and determinate situation.

The §23 excerpt gives the deeper methodological reason for this: language-form
is not reducible to the lexicon. Even where Sanskrit supplies the learned
concept-word, the receiving language gives the grammatical means by which that
concept enters speech.

The first exact Cassirer-facing Kawi locus now confirms the stronger version of
the space-verb point. Humboldt says the use of verbs as prepositions shows a
finer linguistic sense than the use of substantives such as back or face,
because the verb is taken according to its direction in space. On the Tagalog
side, `sa` only looks like a preposition: in the cited case the place concept
is made into a verb by the movement-indicating form. Cassirer's chapter III
claim is therefore not only "some languages use spatial words for relation";
it is that relation becomes graspable as directed act.

## What This Does Not License

This does not make Kawi a privileged origin point for Cassirer's theory. The
chapter III Kawi citations remain distributed across many pages of the Kawi
volume, and most are still raw. It also does not license a claim that Cassirer
is simply repeating Humboldt's own conclusion from §23. Cassirer is using
Humboldt's comparative material inside his own problem of symbolic formation.

The safer formula is:

Kawi helps Cassirer show language-form in formation, especially where
intuition, person, number, and action have not yet been separated into the
clean grammatical categories assumed by later theory.
