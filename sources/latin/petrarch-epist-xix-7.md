# Petrarch, Epistulae rerum familiarium XIX.7

**Triggered by:** Escolios epigraph (p. 10 of the original edition)

## The OCR Text

```
Et miraris quod paucis placeo, cui cum
paucis convenit, cui omnia fere aliter
videntur ac vulgo a quo semper quod longissime
abest id penitus rectum iter
censeo.
```

## Cleaned Latin

```
Et miraris quod paucis placeo, cui cum paucis convenit,
cui omnia fere aliter videntur ac vulgo,
a quo semper quod longissime abest
id penitus rectum iter censeo.
```

## Attempt

> And you are surprised that I please few, I who agree with few, to whom nearly everything appears otherwise than to the crowd, and who always considers the path that lies furthest from the crowd to be altogether the right one.

## Notes

- *paucis placeo... cum paucis convenit* — "I please few... I agree with few." The repetition of *paucis* is the argument: the few who are pleased and the few who are agreed with are the same few.
- *vulgo* — the crowd, the common people. Ablative: "from/than the crowd." The same word Gómez Dávila uses in Spanish (*el vulgo*, §77, §88).
- *quod longissime abest* — "what lies furthest away." Superlative: not merely far but *furthest*. The further from the crowd, the more correct.
- *rectum iter* — "the right/straight path." *Rectum* carries both moral (right) and geometrical (straight) force. *Iter*: road, path, journey.
- *penitus* — thoroughly, altogether, deeply. Intensifier: the judgment is total, not hedged.

## Why Gómez Dávila Chose This Epigraph

Petrarch is the first modern reactionary — a man who lived in the 14th century and preferred the 1st. He wrote in Latin by choice when Italian was available, looked to Cicero and Seneca as contemporaries, and measured his distance from the crowd as a virtue. Gómez Dávila places this epigraph alongside Nietzsche's complaint about being misread and Cervantes's Sancho Panza defending his misunderstood proverbs. The message: I know I will please few. I consider that a credential.

## What Latin Does Here

The sentence structure — nested relative clauses (*cui... cui... a quo...*) building toward the final judgment (*rectum iter censeo*) — enacts the intellectual independence it describes. You have to follow the syntax through several turns before you reach the verdict. Latin's ability to suspend the main verb until the end of a complex clause creates a different *rhythm of thought* than English: the judgment arrives after all the qualifications, not before them. English front-loads; Latin defers. The deferral is itself a mark of the *rectum iter* — the path isn't obvious, you have to follow it.

## Connections

- The *paucis* theme connects directly to Escolios §5 (limiting your audience limits your capitulations) and §107 (being right is one more reason you won't succeed).
- *vulgo* recurs throughout the Escolios in Spanish as *el vulgo* — always with the same Petrarchan contempt.
- Petrarch's self-conscious distance from his own century is the prototype for Gómez Dávila's reactionary stance: not nostalgia for a specific past but permanent dissent from the present.
