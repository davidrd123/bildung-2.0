## Terms To Watch

- `cognitio secreta / abdita` - appears immediately in Liber XIV Caput I as the soul's hidden self-knowledge.
- `spiritus tenuis, calidus, lucidus` - the spirit-body account of soul, deliberately not Aristotelian substantial form.
- `forma idealis` / `formalitas idealis` - the unity added over the many dissimilar bodily forms.
- `habitator in domo` / `nauta in navi` - soul's relation to body; must be compared to earlier arguments against Aristotle.
- `sensus rerum` - Part III Caput II explicitly references the separate book and the Part II proof that even corpses and non-animals retain some sensing.
- `si Entia, & non respectus sunt obiecta sensuum` - reopens the page-order trial's entity/relation/apparition cluster, now through animal sensory superiority.
- `magia` - appears inside the soul-excellence proof before Liber XVI; track natural, demonic/angelic, and divine registers rather than flattening.
- `proles infiniti Entis` - Caput II's proof from infinite intellect/appetite; likely important for immortality in Caput III and beyond.
- `carere contrario et subiecto` - Liber XIV Caput IV's core immortality criterion; superiority and subsistence alone do not prove immortality.
- `intelligere est intus legere` - Caput III's anti-Peripatetic claim that intellect is inward reading by the same sensing spirit, not a separate agent intellect.
- `spoliat` / `vestit` - Campanella reverses the abstraction model: cognition does not strip matter from the phantasm so much as clothe a partial affection with the unseen whole.
- `unitas decomposita` - the animal is not a simple form/matter unity but a natural decomposite unity; compare clock, mill, loom.
- `instinctus` vs. `libertas` - Caput V grants animal speech, sociality, and sensitive discourse but distinguishes object-induced instinct from inwardly elicited free reasoning.
- `religio` - by leaf 0860, religion is both hidden divine art in the mind and the path from law/virtue/prophecy into grace.
- `patria` / `extra propriam regionem` - Liber XVI opens religion as return from the mind's wrong region, linked to self-ignorance and shame.
- `gratia gratum faciens` - strengthens the power-part, illuminates the knowing-part, sanctifies the willing-part; likely important for the rest of Liber XVI.

## Sequential Tranche Notes, part-iii-003 through part-iii-027

- Parts 003-008 move from Platonic and Aristotelian proofs into Campanella's own metaphysical proof. The key correction is that human excellence proves divinity or superiority, not immortality by itself. Immortality requires no contrary, no corrupting subject, and constitution from uncontracted primalities.
- Parts 005-007 are a dense anti-Peripatetic psychology: universals arise from weak/distant sensing, memory, and comparison. The dog knowing foxes, the distant sight of "human" before "Peter," and the `intus legere` etymology all keep cognition inside a widened sensing power.
- Parts 009-011 complete the positive proof through subsistence, lack of contrary, infinite intellect/appetite, ideas, religion, martyrdom, shame, and divine self-disclosure. The proof is not Cartesian inward certainty; it is a bundle of metaphysical, theological, bodily, and public signs.
- Parts 012-017 are a strong animal-objection block. Campanella grants animal medicine, politics, speech, weather-prophecy, and sensitive reason, but denies that these are ordered to post-mortem religion or divine life. Transmigration fails because broad sensation would push immortal soul down into insects, plants, stones, and metals.
- Parts 018-020 answer human weakness by distinguishing mind from the corporeal spirit through which it normally operates. Ordinary acquired science can be lost when spirit fails; hidden and supernal knowledge remain. Created-soul and preexistence questions are held probabilistically rather than forced.
- Parts 021-024 handle why an immortal mind is joined to body and why it is not generated by parent-soul, angels, or world-soul. Parent produces body and sensing spirit; world-soul/angels administer formation; God gives mind. Liber XIV closes by classifying lives as bestial, human, celestial, angelic, and divine.
- Parts 025-027 open Liber XVI by recasting religion as return to the mind's homeland. Human shame and failure to do the known good reveal a fall from order; law and virtue teach but do not execute; prophet, religion, and grace are needed for actual return to God.
