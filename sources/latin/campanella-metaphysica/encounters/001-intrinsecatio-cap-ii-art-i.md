# 001 — Intrinsecatio and the Modality of Beatitude

## Trigger

Cassirer, *Das Erkenntnisproblem*, Vol. 2, Part 018 (Spinoza, Renaissance background):

> Die Schau der Seele — so beschreibt Campanellas Metaphysik den Akt der höchsten Erkenntnis — ist mit dem Schauen des Auges nicht einerlei: denn während das Auge die Dinge durch Bilder erkennt, die ihm von außen zugeführt werden, so erblickt die Seele ihren Gegenstand, indem sie sich in ihn und ihn in sich innerlich verwandelt. Das intuitive Erkennen ist somit das innerliche Einswerden, durch welches das eine zum andern wird (intrinsecatio, per quam unum fit aliud).

Cassirer cites Campanella to establish a pre-Spinozistic precedent for cognition as ontological participation — knowing as becoming-one-with — before turning to Spinoza's third kind of knowledge.

## Local Context

Liber XVII Cap. II is on beatitude — its argument is set out in the italic banner-summary at the head of the chapter:

> *Beatitudinem, quae sit conservatio Entitatis humanae, in summo Ente consistere, in unione secundum potentiam, sapientiam, & amorem: nec consummari, nisi in fruitione, quae praesupponit habere, & sapere, & qualiter, & qualia.*

That is: beatitude — which is the preservation of human being in the supreme Being — consists in union according to potency, wisdom, and love; and is not consummated except in fruition, which presupposes both having, and knowing, and the manner and the qualities of what is had and known.

The intrinsecatio passage sits inside Articulus I of this chapter, where Campanella distinguishes the soul's mode of cognition from the eye's, to argue that what beatitude requires is the soul's mode (intuitive, intrinsecating) rather than the eye's (mediated by species).

## Cassirer Passage and Its Surround

The lines Cassirer quotes (cleaned from the ABBYY/long-s OCR, verified against direct JP2 crops of the left and right columns):

> Sed intuitus animae non est, sicut intuitus oculi, qui videt res per species delatas a luce tincta rebus: sed videt per intrinsecationem rei in se, & sui in illam, sicut per essentiam videt lucem, quantam potest tolerare: & per lucem rebus tinctam videt res.
>
> [...]
>
> Ergo intelligere intuitivum est intrinsecatio, per quam unum fit aliud. **Cognoscere enim est fieri rem cognitam.** Sic Anima, quoniam in hac vita non fit Deus, sed res, quae ipsum defective repraesentant, non potest beari [...]

The agreement of `tincta` (ablative singular feminine, modifying `luce`) and `tinctam` (accusative singular feminine, modifying `lucem`) yields a chiasmus that an `s-tinctas-rebus` reading would have lost: the eye sees things through species *delivered by light, which has been tinted by the things themselves*; and then, parallelly, *through light, now tinted by them*, sees the things again. Light is the medium that bears the things' qualities to the eye twice — first into the species, then back into the apprehension. Intrinsecation in the soul's case bypasses this double-mediation: the soul has the direct essence-vision of light that the eye has only of light-already-coloured-by-things.

## Working Translation

> But the soul's intuition is not like the eye's intuition, which sees things by means of species delivered by light tinted by the things themselves: it sees, rather, by *intrinsecation* of the thing into itself and of itself into the thing — just as it sees light through its own essence, as much as it can bear; and then sees the things by light that has been tinted by them.
>
> [...]
>
> Therefore intuitive understanding is intrinsecation, through which one becomes another. **For to know is to become the thing known.** Thus the Soul, because in this life it does not become God, but only the things that defectively represent Him, cannot be beatified [...]

## The Continuation Cassirer Strips

The sentence immediately following `Cognoscere enim est fieri rem cognitam` is more polemically loaded than the philosophical extract suggests. Verified against the JP2:

> Sic Anima, quoniam in hac vita non fit Deus, sed res, quae ipsum defective repraesentant, non potest beari: sicut nec per cibi visionem & tactionem cibari, nisi gustetur. Propterea sacrae literae manducationi assimilant beatam visionem, unde deceptus est Mahometes.

Working translation:

> Thus the Soul, because in this life it does not become God but only the things that defectively represent Him, cannot be beatified — just as one cannot be nourished by the sight and touch of food, unless it be tasted. For this reason the sacred Scriptures liken beatific vision to eating, whence Muhammad was deceived.

Campanella here deploys a standard medieval polemical move (Muhammad mistook the Scriptural eating-metaphor of paradise as literal sensual feasting) but turns it to *metaphysical* work. The argument is: the eating-metaphor is the *correct* metaphor for beatific cognition, because both forms of consumption become their object. *Gustatio* — taste, the bodily sense that intrinsecates its object rather than receiving images of it — is therefore the bodily analogue of intuitive cognition. The eye sees the colour of the food; the hand touches its surface; only the mouth *becomes* it. Muhammad's error was taking the right metaphor in the wrong register.

This re-frames the philosophical content. Intrinsecatio is not floated as an abstract epistemological doctrine; it is named to do the work of explaining why beatitude must be a *gustatory* mode of cognition, and why neither sensory species (the eye's mode) nor discursive abstraction (the syllogism's mode) suffices for it. The Cassirer-extractable phrase sits in the middle of a chain that runs: bodily senses → gustation → intrinsecation → intuitive understanding → beatific vision → consummation in love-flowing-from-wisdom-and-potency.

## Observation

Cassirer's German paraphrase compresses Campanella's argument and abstracts it from its theological frame. The compression makes the move look like a pure epistemological thesis (intuitive cognition = inner Einswerden) that can stand on its own and prefigure Spinoza's *scientia intuitiva*. The original passage is doing eschatology and confessional polemic at once: intrinsecatio is the cognitive form of beatitude, and its imperfection in this life — where the soul intrinsecates only defective representations of God — is precisely *why* beatitude is deferred; and the cognitive form of beatitude is *gustatory*, against the Muslim error of taking the eating-metaphor as sensual feast.

The sentence Cassirer drops, `Cognoscere enim est fieri rem cognitam`, is the sharper and more general claim. It says cognition itself, not merely intuitive cognition, is ontological becoming. Cassirer's quotation isolates the form ("intuitive understanding is intrinsecation") from the substantive claim it depends on ("to know is to become the known"), and from the bodily metaphor that grounds it (taste as the sense that consumes its object by becoming it). The Spinoza connection sits on the form alone; the Bruno / Plotinus / Aquinas-on-De Anima connection would sit on the substance and the bodily figure.

## Latin / German Lexical Bridge

| Latin | Cassirer's German |
|---|---|
| `intuitus animae` | `Die Schau der Seele` |
| `intuitus oculi` | `Schauen des Auges` |
| `species delatas` | `Bilder ... die ihm von außen zugeführt werden` |
| `intrinsecatio rei in se, & sui in illam` | `sie sich in ihn und ihn in sich innerlich verwandelt` |
| `intelligere intuitivum est intrinsecatio` | `Das intuitive Erkennen ist somit das innerliche Einswerden` |
| `per quam unum fit aliud` | `durch welches das eine zum andern wird` |

The Germanization `innerliche Einswerden` for `intrinsecatio` is reasonably literal; `Einswerden` (becoming-one) loses the directional asymmetry that `unum fit aliud` ("one becomes another") preserves. Campanella's term is not symmetric union but a doubled motion: thing into self, self into thing.

## Distribution of the Intrinsec Family Across the Volume

A grep of the ABBYY OCR yields **115 hits across 92 leaves** of the family `intrinsec*` (115 once long-s and OCR-glyph noise are tolerated). The vast majority (91/115) are adjective/adverb forms — `intrinsecus`, `intrinseca`, `intrinsece`, `intrinseco`, `intrinsecum`, `intrinsecam` — used descriptively for intrinsic versus extrinsic properties. The verbal and nominal forms are scarce and conceptually loaded:

| Leaf | Form | Pars / topic | What the term does there |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0023 | `intrinsecatur` | sense-theory chapter on taste (`gustus`) | "this sense alone is intrinsecated into the object, and the object entirely into it." Taste as the foundational figure for intrinsecation. |
| 0028 | `intrinsecatur` | same, on pepper hot-in-the-mouth | "the nature of the thing is intrinsecated into us." Bodily transmission of the thing's nature, not its image. |
| 0210 | `intrinsecato` | Physics, on elemental transformation | "with heat intrinsecated into [matter], earth is made." The verb crosses from cognition into physics: intrinsecation as physical infusion. |
| 0438 | `intrinsecatur` (+ `intrinsecaretur`) | epistemology, on sapientia | "[the fool] is not intrinsecated into [the species]; and even if he were intrinsecated, he would not know that he cognizes by his own measure, not by the measure of being and truth." Wisdom requires intrinsecation; ordinary cognition fails it. |
| 0617 | `intrinsecatur` (+ `extrinsecatur`) | Pars III on the contemplative ascent | "In the Mathematical [register], how the one God is intrinsecated and extrinsecated into all, and contains and preserves all without His division or passion." The divine version: God is inside and outside all things at once. |
| 0906 | `intrinsecatio` (substantive) | Pars III, Lib. XVII, Cap. II, Art. I (beatitude) | "intuitive understanding is intrinsecation, through which one becomes another." The nominalized climax: the doctrine becomes a defined cognitive form. |

The substantive noun `intrinsecatio` appears only once in the volume, on the Cassirer-quoted leaf 906; everywhere else Campanella uses verb or adjective. This is consistent with the noun being a technical coinage Campanella reaches for only when the doctrine has been built up through the earlier registers.

**The arc**: bodily phenomenology of *gustatio* (the sense that becomes its object) → physical infusion of forms into matter → epistemological doctrine of wisdom → metaphysical structure of God's relation to creation (intrinsecation AND extrinsecation) → the nominalized cognitive form of beatitude. Each step generalizes the previous; the climax of the climbing series is the noun.

Cassirer's German paraphrase takes the noun and the verb at the climax (leaf 906) and ignores the chain that gives them their force. The Spinoza connection sits on the form at the top; the connection to Plotinus / Cusanus / Aquinas (who all have versions of God-as-intus-et-extra) would sit at leaf 617's metaphysical register.
