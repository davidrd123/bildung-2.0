## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] assueta anima, et carcere tetro per foramina tantum oculorum videns, putat aliam non remanere sibi vitam. Et spiritus caducus menti quoque timorem addit; quin et corpus sui dissolutionem praesagiens timorem spiritui, et spiritus menti, addunt. Mens autem omnia cognoscit per spiritum cui insidit, eiusque affectionibus afficitur usque ad delirium, sicut lux passionibus aeris.

Ad nonum respondeo spiritum esse corporeum et per occlusos nervos transire non posse. Mentem vero, quae nihil sine eo operatur, praesertim ad corporis regimen, non potest suum instrumentum impellere tum ob opacitatem corporis, tum quia ita colligata est spiritui et corpori ut non nisi ipsorum ritu agat. Cum autem succus cicutae occludit meatus et suffocat spiritum, aut eius calorem extinguit, necessario anima carens instrumento vitae communis discedit, ut dictum est. Anima autem calida non est quae expellat frigus; hoc enim agit per calorem spiritus; neque tenuis, quae penetret aperiendo poros; transit enim non aperiendo, cum sit incorporea, fortasse sibi relicta seque agnoscens amat discessum.

Nam morientes multa vident, quoniam mens a spiritu separatur et incipit cognoscere per se alio modo, conversa de Mundo corporali ad mentalem. Scientia enim a spiritu fuit quasi videre per cribrum aut per fumum et vitrum.

Ad decimum diximus spiritum totum confluentem in oculos ad spectaculum optatae rei suffocari angustia pororum, aut defectu[?] resolvi; mentem vero discedere in Mundum mentalem. Similiter etiam a vaporibus foetidis extingui, qui fuligine foedant spiritus luciditatem, crassitie aggravant tenuitatem, tolluntque motum; calore vero vincunt vires, ac proinde ipsum in crassum [sine?] vertunt. Haec autem totalis mutatio mors est spiritus, ad quem semper sequitur mentis abscessus. Cur anima non tollat tales molestias dictum est paulo ante.

Ad undecimum concedimus haec indicare aeream mobilitatem spiritus, in quo est mens, non autem mentis. Nam quando revera ipsa superis inhiat, rapit in aerem et spiritum et corpus, nullisque externis passionibus turbatur; et cum aliquid constanter vult, perficit corpusque spernit. Ergo indicium est magnum quod ipsa possit per se multum. Sed quia est in mollissimo spiritu, eius affectus sequitur et motiones. Spiritum quacumque ex causa dilatari et constipari scimus sicut aer, ac proinde dilatari et tristari. Sed qui mente in bonum aliud rapiuntur has levitates non admittunt, ut in severis Stoicis novimus, nedum in Bernardo, Benedicto, Paulo, et Dominico.

Ad duodecimum dicimus oblivionem, furorem, deceptionem fieri in mente affecto spiritu. Eius enim exhalatio et nova passio facit oblivionem; fuligo vero obscuritatem, ac proinde timorem et pravas imaginationes, quas et bilis mordicat et efficit. Vehementes quoque alicuius rei cogitationes, cum sint motus, rapere possunt ita spiritum, ut omnia putet esse id quod imaginatur, vel se solum, quoniam in illud immutatus est, ut in rem amaram aut formidatam; quam passionem corpori quoque communicat, ut commotus a cane rabido et tarantula Apula ostendit.

Anima vero has omnes passiones corrigere non potest, quoniam non est separata a spiritu, sed ipso involuta. Idcirco ad eius insanias insanit et furit, quoniam non abunde sapit scientia et affectum[?]. Fit autem deceptio, quoniam spiritus accipit passionem unius rei tanquam sit alterius, ob similitudinem vel affectionem praecedentem; ut quando ex externo et interno obiecto fit quod non est, et quando interrumpuntur discursus a supervenientibus fuliginibus, vel motibus et passionibus obiectorum externorum aut intrinsecus moventium.

Peripatetici autem horum causam reddere non possunt, quoniam ponunt scientiam in anima immortali et non in spiritu, quo cum sentit, intelligit, et memoratur, et afficitur. Et profecto nisi ita dicatur, necessario anima esset mortalis ponenda, quoniam hae passiones non sunt corporales, sed animales; ergo alicuius animae, non divinae, ergo caducae, quae cum illa sit unum. Mirum quidem quopacto Peripatetici unicam immortalem ponentes animam possunt haec evadere argumenta. Profecto Scotus melius sentit, qui compositione unum facit hominem, non simplicitate sed totalitate, uti nos. Sed spiritus functiones paucissimi noverunt. Et Galenus hoc non distinguens mentem mortalem facit, sic Aristoxenus, Lucretius, et alii. Augustinus vero et Ambrosius rectius perscrutatus est.

Sic patet responsio ad tertiumdecimum, quomodo potest deleri scientia ex mente. Quoniam ipsa scit una cum spiritu; idcirco hoc deficiente aut immutato, ipsa nequaquam scit, sed afficitur affectione spiritus. Sed cur in ea non remanet scientia exhalante spiritu? Quoniam est utriusque, non solius. Remanet autem illa solum quae a superis defluit et quae abdita est; sed ab infirmitate et spiritus abscessione, quo cum sapiebat, amittit scientiam communem utrisque ex obiectis corporeis partam. Et sic Aristotelis solvitur ratio.

Ad quartumdecimum appetitiones obscaenas et varias esse corporis et spiritus scimus; mentem vero posse assentire et dissentire, unde pugna saepe oritur in nobis. Et quamvis idem spiritus secum pugnet, ut cum vult bibere aquam, quia ardorem extinguit, et non vult, quia febrem producit; tamen numquam haec pugna fit, nisi quia diversis partibus res eadem nocet et obest, vel delectat simul et molestat. At quando omnino corpori et spiritui prodest et placet, sicut uti coitu in vere cum formosa puella, displicet autem quia lex vetat et religio, tunc illa pugna est in mente, cuius est noscere legem et Deum legislatorum largitorem, et bonum melius super caduca corporalia. Quam qui sequuntur non admittunt turpia et varia desideria, at nascentia statim iugulant decreto aversandi cupitas res.

Quapropter obiectis externis et Caelo obnoxium esse spiritum, quoniam ab eis movetur, fatemur; non tamen mentem nisi velit assentiri. In ipsam enim agunt per accidens et indirecte; solus autem Deus potest in mentem directe, quoniam primalitatibus constat non contractis ad corporea. Sed dicet quis: qua ratione pati mens potest a spiritu sibi coniuncto, eadem potest a Caelo et aere, cum iungitur illis; nam si mediate, potest et immediate. Hoc negandum est, nisi vinculis iisdem alligetur Caelo et aeri, quo alligatur spiritui a Deo per ministerium Angelorum. Hunc autem modum nos non intelligimus; sed similitudinem formae informantis habet, sed non dependenter, quod formae repugnat. Immutato autem corpore ac spiritu, mentem immutari novimus per accidens, quoniam sic informat.

Ad quintumdecimum dicimus animas a corporibus solutas non reverti, quoniam si in Caelum evolant, vel in aerem ut spiritus brutorum, gaudent libertate magis quam carcere; et sicut ignis ascendens sursum non [revertitur?][?].

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] the soul accustomed [to it], and seeing only through the holes of the eyes in a foul prison, thinks that no other life remains for it. And the perishable spirit also adds fear to the mind; indeed the body, foreseeing its own dissolution, adds fear to the spirit, and the spirit [adds it] to the mind. But the mind knows all things through the spirit in which it sits, and is affected by its affections all the way to delirium, just as light [is affected] by the passions of the air.

To the ninth I answer that the spirit is corporeal and cannot pass through closed nerves. But the mind, which operates in nothing without it, especially for the governance of the body, cannot drive on its instrument both because of the opacity of the body and because it is so bound together with spirit and body that it acts only according to their rite. But when the juice of hemlock closes the passages and suffocates the spirit, or extinguishes its heat, necessarily the soul, lacking the instrument of common life, departs, as was said. The soul, however, is not hot so as to expel cold; for it does this through the heat of the spirit. Nor is it subtle so as to penetrate by opening pores; for it passes not by opening, since it is incorporeal, perhaps left to itself and knowing itself, loving departure.

For the dying see many things because mind is separated from spirit and begins to know through itself in another way, turned from the corporeal World to the mental one. For knowledge from spirit was like seeing through a sieve, or through smoke and glass.

To the tenth we said that the whole spirit flowing together into the eyes for the spectacle of a desired thing is suffocated by narrowness of the pores, or dissolved by defect[?]; but the mind departs into the mental World. Similarly it is also extinguished by foul vapors, which by soot defile the brightness of the spirit, by thickness weigh down subtlety, and remove motion; but by heat they conquer the powers, and therefore turn the spirit into something thick [without?][?]. But this total change is the death of the spirit, after which the absence of the mind always follows. Why the soul does not remove such troubles was said a little before.

To the eleventh we concede that these things indicate the aerial mobility of the spirit in which the mind is, not of the mind. For when it truly gapes toward higher beings, it snatches both spirit and body into the air and is disturbed by no external passions; and when it steadily wills something, it completes it and despises the body. Therefore it is a great sign that it can do much through itself. But because it is in the softest spirit, it follows its affections and motions. We know that spirit is dilated and compressed by any cause whatever, like air, and therefore is expanded and saddened. But those who are carried by mind into another good do not admit these lightnesses, as we know in severe Stoics, not to mention Bernard, Benedict, Paul, and Dominic.

To the twelfth we say that forgetfulness, fury, and deception happen in the mind when the spirit has been affected. For its exhalation and new passion make forgetfulness; soot makes obscurity, and therefore fear and depraved imaginations, which bile both bites and effects. Vehement thoughts of some thing too, since they are motions, can so snatch the spirit that it thinks all things to be what it imagines, or itself alone, because it has been changed into that, as into a bitter or feared thing; this passion it also communicates to the body, as one moved by a rabid dog and the Apulian tarantula shows.

But the soul cannot correct all these passions, because it is not separated from the spirit but wrapped in it. Therefore it goes mad and raves according to the spirit's insanities, because it does not know/taste knowledge and affection[?] abundantly. But deception happens because the spirit receives the passion of one thing as though it were of another, because of likeness or preceding affection; as when from an external and internal object there comes to be what is not, and when discourses are interrupted by supervening soot, or by motions and passions of external objects or of things moving from within.

But the Peripatetics cannot give the cause of these things, because they put knowledge in the immortal soul and not in the spirit, with which it senses, understands, remembers, and is affected. And certainly unless it is said in this way, the soul would necessarily have to be posited as mortal, because these passions are not corporeal but animal; therefore [they belong to] some soul, not a divine one, therefore a perishable one, which is one with that. It is indeed strange how the Peripatetics, positing one immortal soul, can escape these arguments. Certainly Scotus thinks better, since he makes the human being one by composition, not by simplicity but by totality, as we do. But very few have known the functions of spirit. And Galen, not distinguishing this, makes the mind mortal; so too Aristoxenus, Lucretius, and others. But Augustine and Ambrose searched it out more rightly.

Thus the response to the thirteenth is clear, how knowledge can be erased from the mind. Because the mind knows together with the spirit, therefore, when the spirit fails or is changed, it by no means knows, but is affected by the affection of the spirit. But why does knowledge not remain in it when the spirit exhales? Because it belongs to both, not to it alone. That knowledge alone remains which flows down from higher beings and which is hidden; but from infirmity and the departure of the spirit, with which it knew/tasted, it loses the common knowledge acquired by both from corporeal objects. And thus Aristotle's argument is solved.

To the fourteenth, we know that obscene and various appetitions belong to body and spirit; but the mind can assent and dissent, whence battle often arises in us. And although the same spirit fights with itself, as when it wants to drink water because it extinguishes burning, and does not want to because it produces fever, nevertheless this battle never happens unless the same thing harms and impedes different parts, or simultaneously delights and troubles. But when it wholly benefits and pleases body and spirit, as using intercourse in spring with a beautiful girl, but displeases because law and religion forbid it, then that battle is in the mind, whose work it is to know law and God, giver of legislators, and the better good above perishable bodily things. Those who follow this do not admit base and various desires, but as they are being born immediately slaughter them by a decree of turning away from the desired things.

Therefore we confess that the spirit is subject to external objects and heaven, because it is moved by them; yet not the mind, unless it wishes to assent. For they act on it accidentally and indirectly; but God alone can act on the mind directly, because it consists of primalities not contracted to corporeal things. But someone will say: by the same reason by which the Mind can suffer from the spirit joined to it, it can suffer from Heaven and air, since it is joined to them; for if it can [suffer] mediately, it can also [suffer] immediately. This must be denied, unless it is bound to Heaven and air by the same bonds by which it is bound to spirit by God through the ministry of Angels. We do not understand this mode, but it has the likeness of an informing form, yet not dependently, which conflicts with form. But when body and spirit are changed, we know that mind is changed accidentally, since it informs in this way.

To the fifteenth we say that souls released from bodies do not return, because if they fly out into Heaven, or into air like the spirits of beasts, they enjoy freedom more than prison; and just as fire ascending upward does not [return?][?].

## Note

This corrected file keeps the answer-sequence to objections 9-15. The key doctrinal division is common object-derived knowledge, which mind has with spirit and can lose when spirit changes, versus hidden or supernal knowledge, which remains because it does not originate from corporeal objects.
