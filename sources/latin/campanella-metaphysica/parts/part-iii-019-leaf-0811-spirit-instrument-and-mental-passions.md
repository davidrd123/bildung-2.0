# Part III 019: Spirit as Instrument of Mental Passions

## Latin

Mens omnia cognoscit per spiritum cui insidit, eiusque affectionibus afficitur usque ad delirium, sicut lux passionibus aeris. Spiritus est corporeus et per occlusos nervos transire non potest. Mens vero, quae nihil sine eo operatur praesertim ad corporis regimen, non potest suum instrumentum impellere tum ob opacitatem corporis, tum quia ita colligata est spiritui et corpori ut non nisi ipsorum ritu agat.

Cum cicuta occludit meatus et suffocat spiritum aut eius calorem extinguit, necessario anima carens instrumento vitae communis discedit. Anima autem calida non est quae expellat frigus; hoc enim agit per calorem spiritus. Neque tenuis est quae penetret aperiendo poros; transit enim non aperiendo cum sit incorporea.

Morientes multa vident, quoniam mens a spiritu separatur et incipit cognoscere per se alio modo, conversa de mundo corporali ad mentalem. Scientia enim a spiritu fuit quasi videre per cribrum aut per fumum et vitrum.

Levitates opinionum indicant aeream mobilitatem spiritus in quo est mens, non autem mentis. Quando vere ipsa superis inhiat, rapit in aerem spiritum et corpus, nullisque externis passionibus turbatur. Sed quia est in mollissimo spiritu, eius affectus sequitur et motiones.

Oblivio, furor, deceptio fiunt in mente affecto spiritu. Exhalatio eius et nova passio facit oblivionem; fuligo obscuritatem et timorem et pravas imaginationes; bilis mordicat et efficit. Vehementes cogitationes alicuius rei, cum sint motus, rapere possunt spiritum ita ut omnia putet esse id quod imaginatur.

Peripatetici horum causam reddere non possunt, quoniam ponunt scientiam in anima immortali et non in spiritu quo cum sentit, intelligit, memoratur, et afficitur. Remanet autem illa scientia solum quae a superis defluit et quae abdita est; communis vero scientia ex obiectis corporeis parta amittitur, deficiente vel immutato spiritu.

Appetitiones obscaenas et varias corporis et spiritus esse scimus; mens vero potest assentire et dissentire, unde pugna saepe oritur in nobis. Obiectis externis et caelo obnoxium esse spiritum fatemur, non tamen mentem nisi velit assentiri. Solus Deus potest in mentem directe, quoniam primalitatibus constat non contractis ad corporea.

Animae a corporibus solutae non revertuntur, quoniam si in caelum evolant vel in aerem ut spiritus brutorum, gaudent libertate magis quam carcere.

## English

The mind knows through the spirit in which it sits, and it is affected by that spirit's affections as light is affected by the air. The spirit is corporeal and cannot pass through closed nerves. The mind, which does nothing without spirit in governing the body, cannot move its instrument when the body's opacity or the spirit's blockage prevents it.

Hemlock kills by closing passages, suffocating the spirit, or extinguishing its heat. The soul is not itself hot so as to expel cold, nor thin so as to open pores; being incorporeal, it passes without opening. Dying people see many things because mind begins to separate from spirit and turns from the corporeal world to the mental world. Knowledge through spirit had been like seeing through a sieve, smoke, or glass.

Mental levity belongs to the airy mobility of spirit rather than mind itself. When the mind truly longs for higher things, it can draw spirit and body upward and is not disturbed by external passions. But because it is wrapped in a very soft spirit, it follows that spirit's affects and motions.

Forgetfulness, madness, and deception occur when mind is affected through spirit. Sooty vapor, bile, heat, and vehement imagination disturb the spirit and therefore mental operation. The Peripatetics cannot explain this because they place knowledge in immortal soul rather than in the spirit through which the soul senses, understands, remembers, and is affected. What remains when spirit fails is supernal and hidden knowledge, not common knowledge acquired from bodily objects.

## Note

This leaf gives the mechanism behind the earlier weakness objections. Campanella protects mind's immortality by making ordinary cognition a mind-spirit operation, while reserving hidden and supernal knowledge to mind more strictly. That distinction matters for the whole self-knowledge chain: common acquired science can vanish with spirit; abdita knowledge does not.
