## Opening Finding

This slice sharpens the leaf-23 pilot. Taste's intrinsecation is not simple access to the thing as it is. The pepper example says the thing's nature is intrinsecated into us when pepper is broken up in the mouth, but that very contact is altered by saliva, tongue, flesh, intervening air, and other accidents. Campanella can therefore affirm a real bodily entry of the thing's nature while still denying that sense gives wisdom as such.

The adjacent section extends the same logic upward: God and angels are known through effects, celestial bodies through altered images, and all visible things may be only images of truer things in the angelic world and in God. Intrinsecation remains important, but it is already caught inside a hierarchy of mediation.

## Corrected Latin

Per aerem, et alterantur, et in sensoriis alterantur, et debilitantur: ergo non sapimus prout sunt.

3. Ipsae quoque tangibiles qualitates alterantur a nostra carne, et ab aere intermedio, et ab extraneis accidentibus, ut sese nobis declarare nequeat. Unde tactus piperis quoque calidissimi frigidus est; sed in ore calidus, quoniam frangitur, et rei natura nobis intrinsecatur: sed tamen alteratur a sputo, lingua, et caruncula; et nunquam sicuti est percipitur.

4. Entia magna neque per imagines proprias sentiuntur, ut Deus et Angeli, sed per effectus, ita ut nullum de his saporem habeamus. Coelestia vero corpora et sidera propter distantiam non percipiuntur uti sunt, neque imagines eorum ad nos nisi alteratissime feruntur: nam aer, in quo habitamus, ita crassus est, coelo comparatus superiori, sicut aqua comparata aeri. Unde Plato opinatur nos sidera non videre, sed quasdam imaginarias [ef]fulsiones: quemadmodum pisces non vident, quae in nostro sunt aere, nisi per simulachra alterata ab aere et aqua, non vere repraesentantia rem. Et recte Apostolus dixit, quod videmus per speculum in aenigmate, ea quoque quae ex parte videmus.

6. Quin omnia quae videmus nos esse imagines aliarum rerum verarum existentium in mundo Angelico, et in Deo, recte docent Platonici et Augustinus. Ergo nos remotissimi sumus a sapientia rerum, tanquam in antro positi umbras transeuntium solummodo conspicientes.

## Working Translation

Through the air they are altered, and in the sense-organs they are altered and weakened. Therefore we do not taste/know them as they are.

3. The tangible qualities themselves are also altered by our flesh, by the intervening air, and by external accidents, so that they cannot declare themselves to us. Hence the touch of even the hottest pepper is cold; but in the mouth it is hot, because it is broken up and the nature of the thing is intrinsecated into us. Yet it is still altered by saliva, tongue, and the little flesh, and is never perceived as it is.

4. Great beings are not sensed through their own proper images, as God and angels are not, but through effects; thus we have no savor of them. Celestial bodies and stars, because of distance, are not perceived as they are, nor are their images borne to us except in a highly altered way. For the air in which we live is as thick compared with the higher heaven as water is compared with air. Hence Plato thinks that we do not see the stars, but certain imaginary [ef]fulsions, just as fish do not see the things that are in our air except through simulacra altered by air and water, not truly representing the thing. And the Apostle rightly said that we see through a mirror in an enigma, even those things that we see in part.

6. Indeed, the Platonists and Augustine rightly teach that all the things we see are images of other true things existing in the angelic world and in God. Therefore we are most remote from the wisdom of things, like people placed in a cave, looking only at shadows of passing things.

## Notes

- This is not a self-contained new article; it is the close of the preceding sensory-skeptical sequence before `Dubitatio VII` begins.
- `rei natura nobis intrinsecatur` gives a second early verbal use of the intrinsecation family. The dative `nobis` is clearer here than the leaf-23 pilot's `obiecto`: the nature of the thing is intrinsecated to/into us through bodily breakup in the mouth.
- The pepper case complicates the naive reading of intrinsecation. Intrinsic entry is real, but it is not a clean guarantee of truth because the medium and organ alter what enters.
- `saporem` keeps the same taste/wisdom pressure as the leaf-23 pilot. We have no "savor" of God and angels because they are known by effects rather than proper images.
- The Plato/fish/cave sequence links Campanella's sensory skepticism to the visual mediation that encounter 001 later contrasts with the soul's intuitive cognition.

## Translation Risk

Textual: `quasdam imaginarias [ef]fulsiones` is not settled. ABBYY and Tesseract agree on something like `fulsiones`, but the likely sense is an image, glimmer, or effulgence rather than a stable term.

Lexical/contextual: `intrinsecatur nobis` may deserve a more directional English than "is intrinsecated into us." Leaf 438 is now available as the epistemological comparison point, while leaf 906 remains the nominalized climax.
