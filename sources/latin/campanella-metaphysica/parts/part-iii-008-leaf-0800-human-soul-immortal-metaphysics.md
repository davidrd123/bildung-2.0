## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] in quantum movent rem sapientem, et in quantum intelligit sapientem mutatam in aliud. Anima ergo non sapit sensu vel intellectu quatenus est corpus; alioquin non nisi corpora saperent; neque quatenus calor, alioquin frigus non sentiret, neque spatium. Ergo quatenus ens, quoniam omne ens sentit, et quia neque accidentia sentiunt, ut accidentia sunt. Neque enim circulus sentit, sed res circulata; neque paternitas sentit, aliaque relatio, sed res relativa; neque vestis sentit quatenus vestis, sed quatenus est ens aliquod subsistens.

Propterea dicimus animam esse aliquid subsistens in cunctis rebus. Nec quia subsistit est immortalis; nam et aer subsistit per se, et lapis, nec tamen immortales sunt. Quapropter anima brutorum immortalis non est, licet subsistens. Videmus enim non esse formam, quando corrumpi potest, non corrupto subiecto, ut necatae muscae in aqua, quae mox ad solem reviviscunt, refecto spiritu; et similiter gelatae aves in Islandia sub hyeme, quae in vere reviviscunt. Unde docemur animam esse spiritum tenuem calidumque.

Nec tamen dicimus subsistentia sola sentire, quandoquidem calor et frigus sentiunt, qui tamen inexistunt. Ergo damus sensum rebus quae sunt actionem et passionem per se habentia. Calor enim agit, et ignis per illum; paternitas autem et vicinitas non agit, sed pater et vicinum, ut in secunda parte dictum est. Animae ergo est scire, et posse, et appetere, quatenus est ens, non quatenus anima, quandoquidem omnia entia his primalitatibus constitui declaravimus.

Quatenus autem anima est sentiens et appetens, ostendit se non esse relationem, neque actionem et passionem, neque formam passivam, ut circulus aut temperies, neque harmoniam, neque aptitudinem, sed ens subsistens vel inexistens activum, aliquid per se potens, licet in alio. Sed quatenus anima habet animare, est id quod dat alteri minus potenti, minusque sapienti, et minus amanti, potentiam et sapientiam et amorem ab intrinseco. Respectu ergo corporis dicitur anima; respectu sui vero, ens.

At ex corpore et anima non fit unum simpliciter, ut ex potentia, sapientia, et amore, neque ut compositum similare ex materia et forma, ut ex calore et tenui corpore ignis, et ex aere et circulo[?], sed unum decompositum, ut molendinum ex aqua, et lapidibus molaribus, et homine; et textrinum ex texente et tela, et filis, et radio, et casside[?], et ceteris partibus. Quapropter frustra quaeritur unitas in animali simplex, in quo apparet multiformitas; sed unum est de compositione, ut et recte Scotus advertit. Verum quia haec decompositio est naturalis, substantialisque in unam formam totius propter finem, datur unitas maior quam pistrino; licet et in hoc conveniant omnes partes in unam formam totius propter finem. Ars enim naturam imitatur; unde et theriacam ex pluribus speciebus conficit unam speciem.

Cum ergo dicunt, id quo primo vivit animal non est corpus, verum est; sed non sequitur ergo est actus corporis, quoniam animal est decompositum. Sed potest corpus tenue et sentiens magis animare corpus crassum et stupidius. At cum addunt, illud corpus tenue non esse vitam in quantum corpus, concedo; nam vita est entitas constans potentia, sapientia, et amore. Unde anima tenuitatis est calor; caloris vero potestas et sensus et amor essendi, vel horum unitas. Idcirco inquirendum est, num alia vita sit in nobis, quae a calido spiritu non dependeat, sed sapiat immortalitatem.

**Animam humanam esse immortalem, secundum rectam metaphysicam. CAP. IV. ART. II.**

At quidem superiora argumenta, quibus ostendimus hominis divinitatem excedentem animalia robustiora et calidiora supra seriem[?], procul dubio ostendunt, hominem non a sole et ab elementis tantum originem habere, sed ab ente diviniori, quod non sit contractum ad esse calidum, frigidum, et corporeum, sed in sola entitate permaneat, quae est potestas, sapientia, et amor. Et quia quo magis ens iste[?] contractum ad aliquam particularem entitatem, eo magis participat non esse et corruptibilitatem, propterea animum hominis a corruptibilitate alienum valde intelligimus, sed non adhuc immortalem; quia infinitus non est. Ergo potest habere id, a quo corrumpatur.

At quia quaecunque facta sunt a Deo infinito volunt infinito vivere tempore, aemulando Deum, et quae non possunt individualiter, saltem specifice contendunt ad immortalitatem; illa autem consequuntur immortalitatem individualem, quae carent contrario et subiecto.

Propterea dicendum est, quod si humana mens non est corpus, nec compositum ex dissolubilibus, ut probatum est, et caret contrario et subiecto, incorruptibilis sit. Non enim a Deo corrumpitur per subtractionem esse, cum institutor naturae non subtrahat quod rebus est naturale.

Quod autem anima careat contrario patet, quoniam ex eius operationibus ostendimus ipsam esse supra elementa, et tamen constare ex primalitatibus, sicut angeli; unde Plato ex eadem mistura in eodem cratere utrosque fabricat in manu Dei. Et ostendimus ex primalitatibus non contractis nec corporatis. Primalitates autem non possunt dissolvi propter coinexistentiam mutuam in invicem, et identitatem realem, et quia sunt absque contrario: potentia enim potentiae contraria non est, sed haec huic; neque sapientia sapientiae, neque amor amori, neque ipsa inter se. Ergo anima est ex hoc incorruptibilis. Nam et omnia fieri potest, quoniam contracta non est ad particularem admixtam entitatem.

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] insofar as they move the knowing thing, and insofar as it understands the knowing thing changed into another. Therefore the soul does not know by sense or intellect insofar as it is body; otherwise only bodies would know. Nor [does it know] insofar as it is heat; otherwise it would not sense cold, nor space. Therefore [it knows] insofar as it is being, since every being senses, and because accidents do not sense as accidents. For a circle does not sense, but a circled thing does; nor does paternity or another relation sense, but the relative thing; nor does a garment sense insofar as it is a garment, but insofar as it is some subsisting being.

Therefore we say that soul is something subsisting in all things. Nor is it immortal because it subsists; for air too subsists through itself, and stone, yet they are not immortal. Therefore the soul of beasts is not immortal, although it is subsisting. For we see that it is not a form when it can be corrupted while the subject is not corrupted, as flies killed in water soon revive in the sun when their spirit has been restored; and similarly birds frozen in Iceland under winter revive in spring. From this we are taught that soul is a thin and hot spirit.

Yet we do not say that subsistence alone senses, since heat and cold sense, though they in-exist. Therefore we give sense to things that have action and passion through themselves. For heat acts, and fire through it; but paternity and vicinity do not act, but the father and the neighbor do, as was said in the second part. Therefore it belongs to soul to know, to be able, and to desire insofar as it is being, not insofar as it is soul, since we have declared that all beings are constituted by these primalities.

But insofar as the soul is sensing and desiring, it shows that it is not a relation, nor action and passion, nor a passive form, such as a circle or temperament, nor harmony, nor aptitude, but a subsisting or actively in-existing being, something able through itself, although in another. But insofar as the soul has animating, it is that which gives to another, less powerful, less wise, and less loving thing, power and wisdom and love from within. Therefore with respect to the body it is called soul; with respect to itself, being.

But from body and soul there does not come to be something simply one, as from power, wisdom, and love, nor a similar composite from matter and form, as fire from heat and subtle body, and as from air and circle[?], but a de-composite one, as a mill from water, millstones, and human being, and a weaving-workshop from the weaver, web, threads, reed, helmet[?], and the other parts. Therefore simple unity is sought in vain in an animal, in which multiformity appears; but it is one by composition, as Scotus too rightly notes. Yet because this decomposition is natural and substantial into one form of the whole for the sake of an end, there is given a greater unity than in a bakery; although even in this all the parts agree in one form of the whole for the sake of an end. For art imitates nature; hence it also makes theriac, one species, from many species.

Therefore when they say that that by which the animal first lives is not a body, it is true; but it does not follow that therefore it is the act of the body, because the animal is de-composite. But a subtle and more sensing body can animate a coarse and more stupid body. But when they add that that subtle body is not life insofar as it is body, I grant it; for life is entity consisting of power, wisdom, and love. Hence the soul of subtlety is heat; but of heat [the soul] is the power and sense and love of being, or the unity of these. Therefore it must be investigated whether there is another life in us which does not depend on the hot spirit, but knows immortality.

**That the human soul is immortal, according to right metaphysics. Chapter IV. Article II.**

Yet indeed the previous arguments, by which we showed the divinity of the human being exceeding stronger and hotter animals above the series[?], undoubtedly show that the human being has origin not from the sun and from the elements alone, but from a more divine being, which is not contracted to hot, cold, and corporeal being, but remains in entity alone, which is power, wisdom, and love. And because the more this being[?] is contracted to some particular entity, the more it participates non-being and corruptibility, therefore we understand that the human mind is very alien to corruptibility, but not yet immortal, because it is not infinite. Therefore it can have that by which it may be corrupted.

But because whatever things have been made by infinite God will to live for infinite time, emulating God, and the things that cannot [do so] individually strive at least specifically toward immortality, those things obtain individual immortality which lack contrary and subject.

Therefore it must be said that if the human mind is not a body, nor a composite from dissoluble things, as has been proved, and lacks contrary and subject, it is incorruptible. For it is not corrupted by God through subtraction of being, since the founder of nature does not subtract what is natural to things.

But that the soul lacks a contrary is clear, because from its operations we have shown that it is above the elements, and yet consists of primalities, like angels; hence Plato makes both from the same mixture in the same bowl in the hand of God. And we have shown [that it is] from primalities not contracted nor embodied. But primalities cannot be dissolved on account of mutual co-inexistence in one another, real identity, and because they are without contrary: for power is not contrary to power, but this to that; nor wisdom to wisdom, nor love to love, nor they themselves among themselves. Therefore from this the soul is incorruptible. For it can also become all things, because it is not contracted to a particular admixed entity.

## Note

This corrected file keeps the full transition from Caput IV's active/passive account into the positive immortality proof. The important pressure is that `anima` is named from its relation to body, while considered in itself it is `ens`; that lets the proof move from pansensism and primalities to lack of contrary and subject.
