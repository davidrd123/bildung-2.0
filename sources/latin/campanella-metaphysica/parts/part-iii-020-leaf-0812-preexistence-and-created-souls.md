## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] fertur, nisi mutetur; sic neque spiritus neque Mens. Si vero ad inferos fertur, per vim retinetur. Quapropter vana est petitio. Quis enim vellet in carcerem reverti post gustatam libertatem? Salomon autem dubitando loquitur, et ex persona dubitantis vel impii. Nos autem novimus multos resuscitatos a sanctis viris, et animas defunctorum allocutas esse vivos ad instructionem.

Et quidem Ficinus Medicus moriens promisit socio se daturum indicium post mortem, et vocavit amicum dicens: frater, anima est immortalis. Et tunc is cum esset in alia domo agnovit transitum illius; accurrit et invenit solum cadaver. Idem accidit Evagrio et Synesio, Ficino et Mercato, ex condicto[?]. Sunt et aliae experientiae apud sanctos viros, quas omnes negare stultissima est impudentia. Scimus quidem Gregorium et Bernardum et Maurum et Augustinum non fuisse mendaces neque ignorantes. Relinquo mille alios. Lege vitas sanctorum. Et in scriptura Dei Samuel suscitatus praedixit Sauli se in crastino futurum ubi ipse erat apud inferos. Dionysius Carthusianus historias Christianas et aliorum verissimas recitat. Et mille animas scimus destructis corporibus apparuisse aereo sub indumento.

Ad decimae rationis partem postremam dicimus corporeum spiritum nasci, crescere, et definere cum corpore, sed non in nihilum. Neque enim corpus annihilatur, sed formatio tantum. Manifeste enim videmus calorem solis, ubi spiritum tenuem intra crassam ingenerat materiam, unde nequeat elabi, illam organizare iis instrumentis quae ad communem vitam commoda sunt. Quod tamen spiritus facere nesciret, nisi sapientia prima per ministerium Angelorum necessitate quadam ac fato ad formationem corporis illum adigat, ut in prima parte dictum est.

Ubi etiam quia corporis formationi incumbit, et variis passionibus agitatur, sui ipsius ignorantia obrui diximus; sed tamen secreta notitia sui numquam destitui, proptereaque semper attenuatam molem ad caelestia evehere, educereque. Et quia in principio parum elabitur, et materias oppositas attenuat et in sui substantiam tenuiorem portionem vertit, crassiorem vero in domus suae substantiam, propterea in principio crescere diximus aucto calore et copia spiritus paulatim.

At mox absolutis purgatisque aedibus in quibus inhabitat, considerare per sensoria conspiciendo Entia exteriora. Et dum tantum corpori additur ac sibi quantum demitur a se exhalante, fieri statum. Cum vero plus demitur, incipere decrementum et sensim, donec exsiccato corpore et aegre nutriri potente, et attenuato eductoque spiritu et remanente imminuto, adeo decrescit, ut tandem totus evolet, sicut flamma lucernae deficiente oleo.

Anima autem a Deo immissa non sic crescit et decrescit secundum se, sed secundum spiritum et corpus, cuius informationi accessit. Et quia per se non operatur neque scit nisi per spiritum, idcirco augetur et minuitur illo. Et quia neque corpus moritur neque spiritus, sed tantummodo separatur, et in aliud Ens convertuntur seorsum, Mentem quoque separari putandum est, non autem in aliud Ens converti secundum substantiam, sed secundum operationem et affectiones accidentales. Nam et ipsa materia incorruptibilis est, et mutatis formis non tamen ipsa in aliud esse essentiale mutatur. Sic et anima, et longe magis, permanet.

**Responsio ad postremum argumentum, et addubitatio, et resolutio, num animae fuerint ante corpora, et propter quid includuntur in corporibus. Articulus V.**

Ad primas argumenti partes nihil potest demonstrative responderi, sed probabiliter. Hoc tamen unum vere affirmamus, animas non fuisse ab aeterno, quoniam cum sint virtutis finitae, infinito aequari non possunt. Nam necesse est ab eo originem habuisse, ut quidam Platonici dixerunt. Utrum autem creatae fuerint simul cum Angelis vel ante corpora, ut putat Origenes, neque asseri neque negari potest demonstratione naturali, sed tantummodo ex revelatione, ex qua procedit Theologia. Rationes quidem contra eum probabiles sunt, et pro eo non minus. Videtur enim illi inaequalitas non convenire nisi ob merita; cui satisfit, si dicamus Deum ad gloriam suam omnia fecisse et secundum gradus ut libuit, non autem ob merita quae non praefuerunt.

Cum vero aiunt: si anima remanet post corpus, ergo fuit etiam ante; nihil demonstrat, sed suadet tantillum. Potest enim quod melius est fieri et ante et post. Et cum quaerit Plato quid est quod corpus elaborat? Fatemur Angelos et animam mundi utentes calore et spiritu seminali et solari. Sed primam Mundi constructionem et elementatorum fuisse a Deo, a quo creati sunt Angeli et didicerunt similiter operari; nec propterea imperfectus sit Mundus quia novae animae creantur, quoniam individui sunt, et non ideata idearum non adhuc expressarum.

Sed nos diximus Mundum perficiendum esse post renovationem praesentis saeculi turbulenti. Nec rationes probantes quod est post potentia[?], et quod si naturale est animabus uniri corporibus, numquam fuerunt separatae, convincunt. Namque et Augustinus ante formationem corporis putat animam Adae creatam esse; et rerum status diversi diversas affectiones parere possunt, ita ut velint et nolint esse in corpore.

Et quidem calor solis nollet in materia crassa detineri, sed dum pugnat eam attenuare, cogitur remanere. Unde videtur et spontanea et coacta formatio animalis et spiritus inclusio. Si ergo ex his licet de mentibus humanis aliquid argumentari, posset quis dicere eas aliqua occasione allici in corpus, et statim cadere in sui oblivionem ac proinde velle manere in eo. Sic Virgilius canit quod post mille annos Letheum bibunt, ut rursus incipiant in corpora velle reverti; sed a Deo prius in fluvium Actis[?].

Verum haec persuasio non cogit, tum quia calor Solis, qui remanet, non est is qui a sole advenit, sed qui ingeneratur ab illo per sui diffusionem. ## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] is carried, unless it is changed; so neither does spirit nor Mind. But if it is carried to the underworld, it is held by force. Therefore the request is empty. For who would want to return into prison after tasting freedom? But Solomon speaks doubtingly, and in the person of one doubting or impious. We, however, know that many have been raised by holy men, and that souls of the dead have spoken to the living for instruction.

And indeed Ficino the physician, dying, promised his companion that he would give him a sign after death, and called his friend, saying: brother, the soul is immortal. And then that man, when he was in another house, recognized his passing; he ran up and found only the corpse. The same happened to Evagrius and Synesius, Ficino and Mercato, by agreement[?]. There are also other experiences among holy men, all of which it is most foolish shamelessness to deny. We know indeed that Gregory, Bernard, Maurus, and Augustine were not liars or ignorant. I leave aside a thousand others. Read the lives of the saints. And in the scripture of God, Samuel, raised up, predicted to Saul that on the next day he would be where Samuel himself was, among the dead. Dionysius the Carthusian recounts Christian histories and the truest histories of others. And we know a thousand souls that, once their bodies were destroyed, appeared under an aerial garment.

To the last part of the tenth reason we say that the corporeal spirit is born, grows, and comes to an end with the body, but not into nothing. For neither is the body annihilated, but only its formation. For we clearly see that the heat of the sun, when it generates a thin spirit inside coarse matter from which it cannot escape, organizes that matter with the instruments that are suitable for common life. Yet the spirit would not know how to do this unless first wisdom, through the ministry of Angels, by a certain necessity and fate, drove it to the formation of the body, as was said in the first part.

There too we said that because it attends to the formation of the body and is agitated by various passions, it is overwhelmed by ignorance of itself; but nevertheless it is never deprived of secret knowledge of itself, and therefore it always lifts and draws out the attenuated mass toward celestial things. And because in the beginning little escapes, and it attenuates the opposed materials and turns the thinner portion into its own substance, but the coarser into the substance of its house, for this reason we said that in the beginning it grows gradually as heat and the abundance of spirit are increased.

But soon, when the houses in which it dwells have been completed and purged, it considers by looking at exterior beings through the sensories. And while as much is added to the body and to itself as is taken away from itself as it exhales, a steady state comes about. But when more is taken away, decrease begins, and little by little, until the body is dried out and able to be nourished only with difficulty, and the spirit attenuated and drawn out and what remains diminished, it decreases so much that at last it all flies out, like the flame of a lamp when the oil fails.

But the soul sent in by God does not grow and decrease in this way according to itself, but according to the spirit and body whose informing it has approached. And because it does not operate or know through itself except through spirit, it is therefore increased and diminished by spirit. And because neither body nor spirit dies, but is only separated, and each is converted separately into another Being, the Mind too must be thought to be separated, but not converted into another Being according to substance, only according to operation and accidental affections. For matter itself too is incorruptible, and when forms are changed, nevertheless matter itself is not changed into another essential being. So too the soul, and far more, remains.

**Response to the final argument, and questioning, and resolution, whether souls existed before bodies, and why they are enclosed in bodies. Article V.**

To the first parts of the argument nothing can be answered demonstratively, but only probably. This one thing, however, we truly affirm: souls did not exist from eternity, because since they are of finite power, they cannot be equaled to the infinite. For it is necessary that they had their origin from it, as certain Platonists said. But whether they were created together with Angels or before bodies, as Origen thinks, can neither be asserted nor denied by natural demonstration, but only from revelation, from which Theology proceeds. The reasons against him are probable, and those for him no less so. For inequality seems to him not to befit [souls] except because of merits; this is satisfied if we say that God made all things for his glory and according to degrees as he pleased, but not because of merits which did not preexist.

But when they say: if the soul remains after the body, then it also existed before, this demonstrates nothing, but persuades a very little. For what is better can come to be both before and after. And when Plato asks what it is that fashions the body, we confess Angels and the soul of the world, using heat and seminal and solar spirit. But the first construction of the World and of elemented things was from God, by whom Angels were created and learned to operate similarly; nor is the World therefore imperfect because new souls are created, since they are individuals and not ideations of ideas not yet expressed.

But we have said that the World must be perfected after the renewal of the present turbulent age. Nor do the reasons prove conclusively that what is after is in potency[?], and that if it is natural for souls to be united to bodies, they were never separated. For Augustine too thinks that the soul of Adam was created before the formation of the body; and different states of things can produce different affections, so that they may wish and not wish to be in a body.

And indeed the heat of the sun would not wish to be held in coarse matter, but while it fights to attenuate it, it is compelled to remain. Hence the formation of the animal and the inclusion of the spirit seem both spontaneous and compelled. Therefore if from these things one may argue something about human minds, someone could say that they are enticed into body by some occasion, and immediately fall into forgetfulness of themselves and therefore want to remain in it. Thus Virgil sings that after a thousand years they drink Lethe, so that again they begin to want to return into bodies; but by God first into the river Actis[?].
