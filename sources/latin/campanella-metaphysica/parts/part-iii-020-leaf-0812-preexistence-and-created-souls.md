## Latin

Animae solutae non revertuntur: quis enim vellet in carcerem reverti post gustatam libertatem? Salomon dubitando loquitur et ex persona dubitantis vel impii. Nos autem novimus multos resuscitatos a sanctis viris et animas defunctorum allocutas esse vivos ad instructionem. Ficinus moriens promisit socio se daturum indicium post mortem, et vocavit amicum dicens: frater, anima est immortalis.

Ad ultimam rationem dicimus corporeum spiritum nasci, crescere, et definere cum corpore, sed non in nihilum. Corpus non annihilatur, sed formatio tantum. Calor solis, ubi spiritum tenuem intra crassam materiam ingenerat unde nequeat elabi, illam organizat instrumentis quae ad communem vitam commoda sunt; hoc tamen spiritus facere nesciret nisi sapientia prima per ministerium angelorum necessitate quadam ac fato ad formationem corporis illum adigat.

Spiritus, quia corporis formationi incumbit et variis passionibus agitatur, sui ipsius ignorantia obrui diximus; sed tamen secreta notitia sui nunquam destitui. In principio crescit aucto calore et copia spiritus paulatim; post absolutas aedes in quibus inhabitat, per sensoria exteriora entia considerat. Cum plus demitur quam additur, incipit decrementum, donec exiccato corpore et attenuato spiritu totus evolet sicut flamma lucernae deficiente oleo.

Anima autem a Deo immissa non sic crescit et decrescit secundum se, sed secundum spiritum et corpus cuius informationi accessit. Quia per se non operatur neque scit nisi per spiritum, ideo augetur et minuitur illo. Corpus et spiritus non annihilantur sed separantur et in aliud ens convertuntur; mentem quoque separari putandum est, non autem in aliud ens converti secundum substantiam, sed secundum operationem et affectiones accidentales.

**Responsio ad postremum argumentum: utrum animae fuerint ante corpora et propter quid includuntur in corporibus. Articulus V.**

Ad primas argumenti partes nihil potest demonstrative responderi, sed probabiliter. Hoc tamen vere affirmamus: animas non fuisse ab aeterno, quoniam cum sint virtutis finitae infinito aequari non possunt. Utrum creatae fuerint simul cum angelis vel ante corpora, ut putat Origenes, neque asseri neque negari potest demonstratione naturali, sed tantummodo ex revelatione.

Rationes contra Origenem probabiles sunt, et pro eo non minus. Inaequalitas non videtur convenire nisi ob merita; cui satisfit si dicamus Deum ad gloriam suam omnia fecisse secundum gradus ut libuit, non autem ob merita quae non praefuerunt.

Cum aiunt: si anima remanet post corpus, ergo fuit etiam ante, nihil demonstrant sed suadent tantillum. Potest enim quod melius est fieri ante et post. Prima mundi constructio et elementatorum fuit a Deo; angeli, anima mundi, calor et spiritus seminalis operantur sub illo. Nec imperfectus est mundus quia novae animae creantur, quoniam individui sunt et non ideae idearum nondum expressarum.

## English

Campanella answers the objection that souls should return and give signs. Why would anyone return to prison after tasting liberty? Solomon speaks doubtfully, or in the person of a doubter. Saints have raised the dead, and souls of the departed have spoken to the living for instruction. Ficino, dying, promised a friend a sign after death and called to him: "brother, the soul is immortal."

The bodily spirit is born, grows, and declines with the body, though not into nothing. Body is not annihilated, only its formation is lost. Solar heat generates thin spirit within dense matter and organizes it into instruments for common life, under first wisdom and angelic ministry. The spirit is occupied by forming the body and by passions, so it is buried in ignorance of itself, though never deprived of secret self-knowledge.

The spirit grows while heat and spirit increase, then stabilizes, then declines when more is lost than added, until it flies out like a lamp flame when oil fails. The God-given soul does not grow and decline in itself, but according to the spirit and body whose informing it joins. Since it does not operate or know by itself except through spirit, its operations rise and fall with spirit. Mind separates; it is not converted into another being substantially, only in operation and accidental affections.

On whether souls existed before bodies, Campanella says natural demonstration cannot settle it. Souls were not eternal, because finite virtue cannot equal the infinite. Whether they were created with angels or before bodies belongs to revelation, not natural proof. Origen's view has probable arguments for and against it. The claim that what remains after body must have existed before body proves little.

## Note

This leaf keeps the created-soul question deliberately open while closing two extremes: souls are not eternal, and their post-bodily survival does not demonstrate preexistence. The important continuity with earlier leaves is the secret self-knowledge of spirit during embodiment and the distinction between spirit's growth/decline and mind's substantial persistence.
