## Opening Finding

Leaf 438 makes the `sapientia` chain harder and more useful. Wisdom is not taught or transferred as a simple content, because the teachable or addable layer depends on an inborn hidden wisdom and on the purity of the receiving spirit. The point is not anti-education: laws, teachers, and external objects can exercise, specify, or occasion wisdom, but they do not create wisdom's root.

The `mensura entis, & veri` formula also limits intrinsecation. Even if the mind were intrinsecated into the diminished species it receives, it would know by the measure of itself, not by the measure of being and truth. This connects the skeptical taste argument, the leaf-85 innate-wisdom argument, and the Part II primalities doctrine: wisdom requires intrinsic contact, but finite knowing remains measured by the knower unless divine or universal order purifies it.

## Corrected Latin

Sapientiam non doceri, neque transfundi, sed ex consensu Mundi trahi a Deo: & ipsam esse virtutum parentem: Et cur praemia poenaeque iuste per leges decernuntur.

ART. V.

Sapientia non videtur posse doceri: discipuli enim sapientum non fiunt omnes sapientes, sed qui sapientia abdita abundant, quae est principium adscitae additaeque ab obiectis, specificantibus illam: unde nec qui plus laborant, plus sapiunt, nisi externa obiecta, & non vere nisi sicut recitatores. Nec sapientia transfunditur per generationem, quandoquidem sapientum filii saepe sunt rudiores: quoniam spiritum tenuem exilemque contrahunt, & imminuibilem passionibus generationis, & formationis, & nutritionis alterabilem, & degenerantem facile: & non capitis spiritus, sed testiculorum effundunt.

Contrahitur ergo sapientia Fato, Necessitate, & Harmonia totius entis ad particulares modos sapiendi: & in hominibus sapientissimis, qui spiritum habent tenuem sed non tenuissimum: is enim statim sentit, & elabitur, & non expectat successorem, cui scientiam communicet, licet communicet sapientiam: hi sunt sagacissimi subito intelligentes, & praesagientes, & divinantes ex modico in causis multum in effectibus, & subito, sed non memorantes. Item nec sapiunt qui spiritum crassum habent, quoniam non suscipit facile passiones illatas, unde externa obiecta iudicantur, nec aptus est a seipso moveri facile, in syllogismos multos. Nec qui habent spiritum in prave formatis cellis aut angustis, quoniam non potest motus suos recolere nisi in spatiosis & mundis cellis. Nec quibus fuligines multae ingenerantur, ut in habentibus cor, & iecur calidissima: fuligo enim timidos facit & perplexos in discernendo, interrumpit motiones spiritus, & syllogismos, & ideo facit taediosos in studio.

Ut ergo bene formentur cellae, viscera temperentur, & spiritus fiant non crassi, non tenues valde, non fuliginosi, non molles, non rigidi, tota rerum natura concurrit, & artifex Deus: qui & mentem mox infundit spiritui: quae [ima?] affectus spiritus iudicat, & nullam habet per se operationem, quae non incipiat a spiritu (nisi cum rapitur ad supera) licet ad ulteriora, & diviniora quam posset spiritus elevetur, quoniam est forma hominis, & non abstracta intelligentia, idcirco & insaniam non aufert, nec spiritus, & viscera immutat in melius: sed semper ad Dei cultum trahit. Hic homo per sese omnia novit, quae possibilia nosse sunt; & tunc sapientissimus est, cum se vere nihil scire agnoscit: quoniam rerum species imminutas, non res, sentit; nec intrinsecatur illis: at etsi intrinsecaretur, nosset se illa cogniturum non mensura entis, & veri, sed mensura sui.

Huic proximus est, qui novit sapientissimo parere: & quae nescit ipse, ab eo discere: & hic non fuliginosus est, neque crassus ut obstinetur in prima impressione, qua putet se sapere: sed tenuis & lucidus, licet cum aliqua fuligine; qui vero crassus est, fuliginosusque, nec per se addiscere potest, quoniam ineptus est ad patiendum ab obiectis ob crassitiem, & quod primo novit, aliter posse esse non intelligit, quoniam non facile pati potest ab aliis; nec discurrere, & investigare ob fuligines, impedientes motum: hi sunt robustissimi, ferocissimi, & ignorantissimi. Virtus igitur est sapientia, quoniam consistit in ipsa puritate, quae prout sunt, res capit, non autem alteratas ab impuritate sua, & ita iudicat & afficitur. Affectio enim ex sapore nascitur, sapor ex posse: ita quod virtus ad extra est regula extensionum Primalitatum ad obiecta exteriora; & sapientia quae est omnis virtus radicaliter huius regulae praeambula non docetur, nec transfunditur, nisi genitor, caelum & sidera, & mundum, & suum spiritum, & corpus, sciat ordinare, & genitricis pariter.

Quia autem cunctis est dolor communis: & ergo timor; similiter voluptas cunctis convenit, ergo & amor; ideo obedientia est communis cunctis hominibus: & propterea possunt ferri leges a Deo, & legiferatoribus, ut amore vel timore illis ad nostrum bonum obediamus: Non autem ut virtus doceatur & sapientia: sed ut exerceatur ad nutum sapientiae communis, quae est ipsa lex, ab aeterna lege, qua cuncta fiunt, & servantur, ortum habens: Et in hoc praemium, & poenae iuste decernere possunt: quoniam omnes possunt obedientiae virtutem habere, cum omnes doloris & voluptatis sint capaces, a quibus ad operandum moventur omnia.

Nota quod sapientia additicia non transfunditur, sed potius docetur, si adsit indita: indita vero, quae est sui notitia, transfunditur, sed non puritas, perfectioque eius, qua recte quis seipsum novit, & postea per se mutatum ab aliis, alia novit, prout sunt, eo pacto, quo sciri possunt: contrahitur autem a toto ente puritas, ut dictum est; & haec ideae correspondet intimius, expressiusque. At si non esset idea, neque quaesita puritas scire possemus, ut palam mox ostendetur.

## Working Translation

That wisdom is not taught or transferred, but is drawn by God from the consensus of the World; and that it is the parent of virtues; and why rewards and punishments are justly decreed through laws.

ART. V.

Wisdom does not seem able to be taught. For not all disciples of the wise become wise, but only those who abound in hidden wisdom, which is the principle of what is assumed and added by objects as they specify it. Hence those who labor more do not know more, except external objects, and not truly except like reciters. Nor is wisdom transferred through generation, since the children of the wise are often more unformed: because they contract a thin and slight spirit, alterable by the passions of generation, formation, and nutrition, easily degenerating; and they pour forth not the spirits of the head, but of the testicles.

Wisdom is therefore contracted by Fate, Necessity, and the Harmony of the whole being into particular modes of knowing. In the wisest human beings this happens when they have a subtle spirit, but not the subtlest; for the latter immediately senses and slips away, and does not wait for a successor to whom it may communicate science, though it communicates wisdom. These are the most sagacious, suddenly understanding, fore-sensing, and divining much in effects from little in causes, and doing so suddenly, but not remembering. Likewise those who have a thick spirit do not know, because it does not easily receive introduced passions, by which external objects are judged; nor is it apt to be moved easily by itself into many syllogisms. Nor do those know who have their spirit in badly formed or narrow cells, because it cannot recollect its motions except in spacious and clean cells. Nor do those in whom many sooty vapors are generated, as in those who have very hot heart and liver: soot makes people timid and perplexed in discerning, interrupts the motions of the spirit and syllogisms, and therefore makes them wearisome in study.

Therefore, so that the cells may be well formed, the viscera tempered, and the spirits made neither thick, nor too thin, nor sooty, nor soft, nor rigid, the whole nature of things concurs, and God the artisan, who also immediately infuses mind into the spirit. This mind judges the [lowest?] affections of the spirit, and has no operation by itself that does not begin from spirit (unless it is seized upward to higher things), although it is elevated to things further and more divine than spirit could reach, because it is the form of the human being and not an abstract intelligence. Therefore it does not remove madness, nor change spirits and viscera for the better; but it always draws toward the worship of God. This human being knows through himself all things that it is possible to know; and he is wisest when he recognizes that he truly knows nothing, because he senses diminished species of things, not the things themselves, and is not intrinsecated into them. But even if he were intrinsecated, he would know that he would cognize them not by the measure of being and truth, but by the measure of himself.

Nearest to this person is the one who knows how to obey the wisest person, and to learn from him what he himself does not know. This person is not sooty, nor thick enough to be stubborn in the first impression by which he thinks he knows; instead he is subtle and lucid, though with some soot. But the person who is thick and sooty cannot learn by himself, because he is unfit for being affected by objects because of thickness; and what he first knows, he does not understand could be otherwise, because he cannot easily be affected by others. Nor can he reason and investigate, because of sooty vapors impeding motion. These people are the most robust, the most fierce, and the most ignorant. Therefore wisdom is virtue, because it consists in that purity itself which receives things as they are, not as altered by its own impurity, and so judges and is affected. Affection arises from savor; savor from power. Thus virtue outwardly is the rule of the extensions of the Primalities toward exterior objects; and wisdom, which is radically every virtue and precedes this rule, is not taught or transferred unless the begetter knows how to order heaven, stars, world, his own spirit and body, and likewise those of the mother.

But because pain is common to all, and therefore fear, and likewise pleasure belongs to all, therefore love also; for this reason obedience is common to all human beings. Therefore laws can be issued by God and by lawgivers, so that through love or fear we obey them for our good. But this is not so that virtue and wisdom may be taught, but so that they may be exercised at the command of common wisdom, which is the law itself, having its origin from the eternal law by which all things are made and preserved. And in this, they can justly decree reward and punishments, because all can have the virtue of obedience, since all are capable of pain and pleasure, by which all things are moved to operate.

Note that added wisdom is not transferred, but rather taught, if inborn wisdom is present. Inborn wisdom, however, which is knowledge of oneself, is transferred; but not its purity and perfection, by which one rightly knows oneself, and afterward, having been changed in oneself by others, knows other things as they are, in the way in which they can be known. Purity, however, is contracted from the whole being, as has been said; and this corresponds more inwardly and more expressly to the Idea. But if there were no Idea, we could know neither what purity is being sought, as will soon be shown clearly.

## Notes

- The slice confirms that `sapientia` remains tied to `sapor`, but with a physiological and metaphysical complication: `Affectio enim ex sapore nascitur, sapor ex posse.` Savor depends on power, and wisdom depends on the purity by which the knower receives things as they are.
- `mensura entis, & veri` is not a triumphant intrinsecation formula. It appears in a limiting clause: even if the knower were intrinsecated into the received species, finite cognition would still be measured by the knower, not by being and truth as such.
- `sapientia additicia / indita` extends the leaf-85 and leaf-86 vocabulary: `notitia superaddita / innata et abdita`, then `scientia innata / illata`, now `sapientia additicia / indita`.
- `sagacitas` returns as a function of a subtle but not excessively subtle spirit: sudden insight, fore-sensing, and divination from little in causes to much in effects, but with weak memory.
- The passage gives an internal bridge from primalities to law: outward virtue is the rule of extensions of the Primalities toward external objects, while common wisdom is law rooted in eternal law. This connects the Part II triad to the leaf-85 claim that wisdom is the health of the world in government and the arts.

## Translation Risk

Textual: the corrected Latin is based on ABBYY extraction and long-s normalization, without full page-image collation. The chief raw-OCR uncertainties are the title's `praemia poenaeque`, the chapter number in the running header, `[ima?] affectus spiritus`, `imminuibilem`, `crassitiem`, and the closing `neque quaesita puritas`.

Lexical: `mensura entis, & veri` may mean "the measure belonging to being and truth" or "the measure of being and of truth." The translation keeps the literal genitive force while leaving the metaphysical construction open.

Contextual: `sapientia ... ex consensu Mundi trahi a Deo` is translated as wisdom being drawn by God from the world's consensus. The direction of agency and source should be checked against surrounding Part II usage of `consensus`, Fate, Necessity, and Harmony.
