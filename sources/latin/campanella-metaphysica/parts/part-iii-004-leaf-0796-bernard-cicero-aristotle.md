## Latin

Hoc quidem eius subsistentiam docet, sed non ostendit immortalitatem. Quarta propinquior est demonstrationi: quoniam si anima spernit corpus propter contemplationem divinorum, ergo ab eo non dependet. Quinta vero satisfacit, si declaretur, et optima est, ut videbimus.

**Rationes Bernardi et Ciceronis concludunt substantiam, sed non subito etiam perpetuitatem animae humanae. Articulus II.**

Bernardus ait: quia sibi ipsi anima est vita, et non mendicat eam a corpore, ergo vivit per se post corpus. Cicero vero: si in corpore mortali animato vivit, ergo multo magis separata a corpore moribundo.

Recte quidem; sed non sequitur quod vivat in aeternum, sed quod possit sine corpore dumtaxat vivere. Circulo quoque aeneo competit rotunditas per se; nec tamen est sine materia.

**Sententia Aristotelicorum varia, secum pugnans, et cum decretis naturae; nec immortalitatem animae probat, nec unitatem, nec pluralitatem. Articulus III.**

Aristoteles, cum debuisset declarare quomodo ex elementis insensatis inanimatisque oriatur anima et sensus, quasi hoc nihil ad doctrinam pertineret, logica methodo de anima determinat: quod quia non est materia neque compositum, sit forma corporis physici organici potentis vita. Deinde ponit animam intellectivam separatam et immistam, sicut Anaxagoras; ex quo sequitur quod nullius corporis sit forma.

Et tamen omnem animam esse formam docuerat. Unde Averroes, Avicenna, et Alexander coacti sunt ponere animam unam separatam omnium hominum, quam dicunt intelligentiam: alii activam et passivam, alii activam dumtaxat praesidentem hominum sphaerae, quoniam immaterialia non multiplicantur numero, et quia intelligere est universalis, non individui.

Alii, ne deserant Aristotelem, ponunt animam intellectivam secundum essentiam esse actum corporis, alioquin intelligere non esset operatio hominis; sed secundum potentiam dicunt nullius corporis esse actum. Et ponunt in intellectivo duplicem potentiam, passivum intellectum et activum intellectum, quoniam in omni rerum genere ubi repetitur patiens reperitur et agens; intelligere autem est quoddam pati et fieri ipsa intelligibilia.

Hinc dicunt dari intellectum activum facientem obiecta immaterialia, ut possint intelligi. Item anima est qua corpus primo vivit; ergo est actus corporis quo vivit. Item anima cognoscit omnia corpora; ergo non est corpus. Item habet operationem propriam, intelligere scilicet absque organo corporali; ergo per se subsistit et est immortalis.

## English

Campanella finishes the Platonic article by saying that some arguments prove subsistence, not immortality. The argument from philosophy's contempt of the body comes closer: if the soul despises the body for contemplation of divine things, then it does not depend on the body. The argument from religion and higher knowledge is best, if properly clarified.

Bernard and Cicero also prove too little. Bernard says the soul is life to itself and does not beg life from the body; therefore it lives through itself after the body. Cicero says that if it lives in an animated mortal body, much more should it live when separated from the dying body. Campanella grants that this can show possible life without the body, but not eternal life. A bronze circle has roundness through itself, yet not without matter.

He then turns sharply against the Aristotelians. Aristotle should have explained how soul and sensation arise from insensate, inanimate elements. Instead he defines soul logically as the form of an organic physical body capable of life, then posits a separate unmixed intellect. This generates conflicting schools: a single separated intellect for all humans, active and passive intellects, or a soul that is act of body by essence but not by potency.

The Peripatetic proof rests on the claim that understanding is a kind of suffering and becoming the intelligible. Therefore, they infer an active intellect to make objects immaterial, a passive intellect to receive them, and immortality from the intellect's proper operation without bodily organ.

## Note

This leaf makes the target of Caput III clear: Campanella is not merely proving immortality, but clearing away proofs that either overreach or contradict the nature of soul. The important retained formula is Peripatetic: `intelligere est quoddam pati et fieri ipsa intelligibilia`. Campanella will keep pressure on that becoming-the-intelligible account while refusing the Aristotelian machinery built around it.
