## Latin

Hoc quidem eius subsistentiam docet; sed non ostendit immortalitatem. Quarta propinquior est demonstrationi: quoniam si anima spernit corpus propter contemplationem divinorum, ergo ab eo non dependet. Quinta vero satisfacit, si declaretur, et optima est, ut videbimus.

**Rationes Bernardi et Ciceronis concludunt substantiam, sed non subito etiam perpetuitatem animae humanae.**

[Fragmentum tituli repetitum in extracto: "tem, sed non subito immortalitatem."] **ARTIC. II.**

Bernardus ait, quia sibi ipsi anima est vita, et non mendicat eam a corpore, ergo vivit per se post corpus. Cicero vero: si, inquit, in corpore mortali animato vivit, ergo multo magis separata a corpore moribundo.

Recte quidem; sed non sequitur, quod vivat in aeternum, sed quod possit sine corpore dumtaxat vivere. Circulo quoque aeneo competit rotunditas per se; nec tamen est sine materia.

**Sententia Aristotelicorum varia, secum pugnans, et cum decretis naturae; nec immortalitatem animae probat, nec unitatem, nec pluralitatem. ARTIC. III.**

Aristoteles autem, cum debuisset nobis declarare, quo pacto ex elementis insensatis inanimatisque, ex quibus omnia ipse producit, oriatur anima et sensus, quasi hoc nihil ad doctrinam pertineret, logica methodo de anima determinat: quod quia non est materia, neque compositum, sit forma corporis physici organici, potentis vitae. Deinde vero ponit animam intellectivam separatam et immixtam, sicut Anaxagoras; ex quo sequitur, quod nullius corporis sit forma; et idcirco immortalis. Et tamen omnem animam esse formam docuerat.

Unde Averroes, Avicenna, et Alexander coacti sunt, sequendo ipsum, ponere animam unam separatam omnium hominum, quam dicunt esse intelligentiam; alii activam et passivam, alii vero activam dumtaxat, praesidentem hominum sphaerae, quoniam immaterialia non multiplicantur numero; et quia intelligere est universalis, et non individui. Illud autem est unum specie, idcirco unus intellectus. Item essent infinitae animae si multiplicarentur, quod Aristotelis positioni non congruit.

Alii vero stultitiam harum opinionum redarguentes, ne tamen deserant Aristotelem, ponunt animam intellectivam secundum essentiam esse actum corporis; alioquin intelligere non esset operatio hominis, sed separatae substantiae, nec homo esset unus nisi aggregatione. Sed tamen secundum potentiam dicunt nullius corporis esse actum. Et ponunt in intellectivo duplicem potentiam cum Aristotele, id est, passivum intellectum, qui est omnia, et activum, qui est omnia facere. Quoniam in omni rerum genere, ubi reperitur patiens, reperitur et agens; intelligere autem est quoddam pati, et fieri ipsa intelligibilia.

Alii interpretantur: non patitur a corporeis obiectis, quia sic esset corporea. Propterea datur intellectus activus faciens obiecta immaterialia, ut possint intelligi.

Et quoniam anima est, qua vivit primo corpus; alioquin si de se viveret corpus, omne corpus viveret; ergo anima est actus corporis quo vivit. Item anima cognoscit omnia corpora; ergo non est corpus, alioquin non cognosceret nisi seipsum, sicut oculus croceus videt omnia crocea. Item habet operationem propriam; ergo per se subsistit, et est immortalis, ut arguit Aristoteles. Quod habet propriam operationem, patet: quoniam ipsum intelligere fit absque organo corporali. Non enim ut visio, et radius, et gustus per aliquod organum determinatum sic intellectio; alioquin non intelligeremus omnia, et immaterialiter, quoniam organum determinaret sibi aliquod intelligibile.

Item intus existens prohibet extraneum; et unum quodque recipitur secundum modum recipientis. Ergo anima est pura potentia, et nullam habet secundum se naturam, sed apta est omnes recipere et cognoscere. Idcirco est immortalis subsistens. Et quia cognoscit absque corporis immutatione non est idem cum sensitiva, et ferina, quae cognoscit cum corporis alteratione. Et hoc probant, quoniam intellectus intelligit universale; sensus vero particulare. Intellectus absque materia, et praesentia materiae; sensus vero cum praesentia materiae: nam videt album in quanto homine, vel pariete.

Item intellectus cognoscit quidditatem; sensus vero esse, et existentiam, et accidentia. Item intellectus perficitur a vehementi intelligibili: si enim intelligit angelos et universalia, facilius intelligit minus intelligibilia, ut hominem et virtutem et amicitiam, quae sensui non obiiciuntur.

## English

This indeed teaches its subsistence; but it does not show immortality. The fourth is nearer to demonstration: because if the soul despises the body on account of contemplation of divine things, then it does not depend on it. But the fifth satisfies, if it is clarified, and is the best, as we will see.

**The reasons of Bernard and Cicero conclude the substance, but not immediately also the perpetuity of the human soul.**

[Title fragment repeated in the extract: "tem, but not immediately immortality."] **Article II.**

Bernard says that, because the soul is life to itself and does not beg it from the body, therefore it lives through itself after the body. Cicero indeed says: if it lives in an animated mortal body, then much more when separated from the dying body.

Rightly indeed; but it does not follow that it lives forever, only that it can live without the body. Roundness also belongs to a bronze circle through itself; and yet it is not without matter.

**The opinion of the Aristotelians is various, fighting with itself and with the decrees of nature; it proves neither the immortality of the soul, nor unity, nor plurality. Article III.**

But Aristotle, when he ought to have declared to us in what way soul and sense arise from insensate and inanimate elements, from which he himself produces all things, determines about the soul by a logical method, as if this had nothing to do with doctrine: that because it is not matter, nor a composite, it is the form of a physical organic body capable of life. Then he posits the intellective soul as separated and unmixed, like Anaxagoras; from which it follows that it is the form of no body, and therefore immortal. And yet he had taught that every soul is form.

Hence Averroes, Avicenna, and Alexander, compelled by following him, posit one separated soul of all human beings, which they call intelligence; some [call it] active and passive, others active only, presiding over the sphere of human beings, because immaterial things are not multiplied in number; and because to understand belongs to the universal, and not to the individual. But that is one in species, and therefore there is one intellect. Likewise there would be infinite souls if they were multiplied, which does not agree with Aristotle's position.

But others, refuting the foolishness of these opinions yet not abandoning Aristotle, posit the intellective soul according to essence as the act of the body; otherwise understanding would not be an operation of the human being, but of a separated substance, nor would the human being be one except by aggregation. Yet according to potency they say it is the act of no body. And they posit in the intellective part a double potency with Aristotle, that is, passive intellect, which is all things, and active intellect, which is to make all things. For in every genus of things, where a patient is found, an agent too is found; but understanding is a certain suffering and becoming the intelligible things themselves.

Others interpret: it does not suffer from corporeal objects, because then it would be corporeal. Therefore an active intellect is given, making objects immaterial so that they can be understood.

And because the soul is that by which the body first lives - otherwise, if the body lived of itself, every body would live - therefore the soul is the act of the body by which it lives. Likewise the soul knows all bodies; therefore it is not a body, otherwise it would know nothing except itself, just as a saffron-colored eye sees all things as saffron. Likewise it has a proper operation; therefore it subsists through itself and is immortal, as Aristotle argues. That it has a proper operation is clear: because understanding itself happens without a corporeal organ. For intellection is not like vision, ray, and taste through some determinate organ; otherwise we would not understand all things and immaterially, because the organ would determine some intelligible for itself.

Likewise what exists within blocks what is foreign; and each thing is received according to the mode of the receiver. Therefore the soul is pure potency and has no nature according to itself, but is apt to receive and know all things. Therefore it is immortal and subsisting. And because it knows without change of the body, it is not the same as the sensitive and animal [soul], which knows with alteration of the body. And they prove this because intellect understands the universal, but sense the particular. Intellect [knows] without matter and the presence of matter; sense, however, with the presence of matter: for it sees white in a determinate human being or wall.

Likewise intellect knows quiddity; but sense knows being, existence, and accidents. Likewise intellect is perfected by a vehement intelligible: for if it understands angels and universals, it more easily understands less intelligible things, such as human being, virtue, and friendship, which are not presented to sense.
