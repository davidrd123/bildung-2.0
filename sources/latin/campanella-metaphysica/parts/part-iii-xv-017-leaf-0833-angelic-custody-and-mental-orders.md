**CAPUT V. ARTICULUS I.**

De ordinibus autem mentalium conveniunt Platonici cum Mercurio. Verum Mercurius videtur post Deos, id est Angelos, ponere Heroas secundo loco: nam super aerem eos locat; daemones vero apud hos in tertio gradu, et tandem animas in infimo. Iamblichus autem post Deos daemones, post daemones ponit Heroas, ultimo tandem ordine animas, hac ratione, ut virtus infima superioris sit prima sui inferioris. Cum vero Porphyrius Deos facit aethereos, daemones aereos, animas terreas, reprehenditur ab Iamblico, quoniam non a corporibus habent distinctionem, sed a suarum essentiarum gradibus et proprietatibus, iuxta quas ad corpora regenda se [exerunt?][?]. Videtur autem sub Bono, qui est Deus, locare Deos, id est Angelos assistentes; his vero assistentes deinde daemones, tanquam ministros Architectis in opificio mundano, patefacientes ea quae in Diis sunt Arcana. Heroes vero daemonibus subsunt in eisdem muneribus, magis tamen declinando ad particularia mobiliaque. Animas heroibus supponit, tanquam ad corpora inclinatas et singularizatas. Essentialia deorum est unitas et permanentia. Virtus vero est causa motionum immobilis. Actio providentia eminens, nihil commune habens cum eis quibus provident. Animarum essentia est declinans ad multitudinem, et motum, coniunctionemque cum Diis. Virtus ad accipiendum ab eis, et motu vitali replenda, et congruendum cum permanentibus et cum rebus fluentibus; et haec actio earum, et ad omnia in se, et per se ipsam contemperanda. Daemones Diis magis, Heroes vero hominibus secundum haec propinquant.

Alibi facit quemlibet deorum ubique, quoniam incorporeus et infinitus est omnis Deus. Et quidem dicitur aereus et caelestis, quoniam ibi magis operatur, licet secundum essentiam et potentiam sit ubique: nam et anima stellae cuiusque est ubique, licet in stella magis operetur, et quaelibet Mundi pars accipit aliquid a quolibet Deo. Quilibet enim Deus cuilibet parti Mundi totus adest et est impassibilis. Imo ait, Daemones et Heroes sunt impassibiles, sequuntur enim ordine semper aequali Deos in Mundi conservatione. Ubi autem est passio, non servatur ordo; imo nec animae patiuntur corporatae secundum essentiam et ideas suas, sed corpus.

Porphyrius passibiles esse daemones mundi rectores putat, quoniam sacrificiis alliciuntur et symbolicis rebus per Sacerdotes; Deos quoque passione tangi autumat, quoniam invocati auxiliantur. Iamblichus vero tunc hominem deorum consequi invocando adiutorium, cum a passione corporis per ecstasim secernitur. Si ergo invocatio liberat nos a passionibus, ergo non infert passionem invocatis Diis. Ergo quia nos efficimur Diis daemonibusque similes, ab illis adiuvamur: nec quia convertuntur ad nos, sed nos ad illos, secundum Boni rationem semper recte operantes. Dicuntur autem irati, quia nos ab eis recedendo in malum ferimur, non quia illi afficiuntur. Puros quoque intellectus, quos precibus non moveri putat Porphyrius, ideoque solos Deos mundanos orari oportere, orandos putat Iamblichus propter sensum intimum, non propter voces supplicationum. Item sacrificia et symbola offerenda, non propter materiam et qualitates corporeas earum, sed propter rationes et species et mensuras incorporeas quae sunt in materialibus congruae Diis. Parva autem congruitas nobis sufficit ad Deos nobis conciliandos, cum sint promptissimi ad benefaciendum. Agens autem praepotens in materiam vel exiliter praeparatam agit.

The distinction between the Platonists and Mercury concerning the order of mental beings, and between Iamblichus and Porphyry concerning the reason for their distinction, and whether all are to be prayed to or those who are lower, and whether, when invoked to our aid, they come by Physical or Mathematical Symbols, and whether the Gods are everywhere.

**CHAPTER V. ARTICLE I.**

Concerning the orders of mental beings, the Platonists agree with Mercury. But Mercury seems to place Heroes in the second place after Gods, that is, Angels, for he locates them above the air; demons, however, among them in the third grade, and finally souls in the lowest. But Iamblichus places demons after Gods, Heroes after demons, and finally souls in the last order, by this reason: that the lowest virtue of the superior is the first of its inferior. But when Porphyry makes Gods aetherial, demons aerial, and souls earthly, he is rebuked by Iamblichus, because they do not have distinction from bodies, but from the grades and properties of their essences, according to which they [put themselves forth?][?] for governing bodies. He seems, however, under the Good, which is God, to locate Gods, that is, assisting Angels; and then demons assisting these, as ministers to Architects in the worldly work, disclosing the Arcana that are in the Gods. Heroes, however, are subject to demons in the same offices, yet declining more toward particular and mobile things. He places souls under Heroes, as inclined toward bodies and singularized. The essentials of the gods are unity and permanence. Virtue, however, is the immobile cause of motions. Action [is] eminent providence, having nothing in common with those for whom they provide. The essence of souls is declining toward multitude, motion, and conjunction with Gods. Their virtue is for receiving from them, being filled by vital motion, and agreeing with permanent things and with flowing things; and this is their action, both for tempering all things in itself and through itself. Demons draw nearer to Gods, but Heroes to human beings according to these things.

Elsewhere he makes each of the gods everywhere, because every God is incorporeal and infinite. And indeed one is called aerial and celestial because he operates more there, although according to essence and power he is everywhere; for the soul of each star too is everywhere, although it operates more in the star, and every part of the World receives something from every God. For every God is wholly present to every part of the World and is impassible. Indeed he says that Demons and Heroes are impassible, for they always follow the Gods in equal order in the conservation of the World. But where there is passion, order is not preserved; indeed embodied souls too do not suffer according to their essence and their ideas, but the body does.

Porphyry thinks that the demons ruling the world are passible, because they are enticed by sacrifices and symbolic things through Priests; he also supposes that Gods are touched by passion because, when invoked, they help. But Iamblichus [says] that then a human being obtains the aid of the gods by invoking, when he is separated from the passion of the body through ecstasy. Therefore if invocation frees us from passions, it does not bring passion into the invoked Gods. Therefore because we are made like Gods and demons, we are aided by them; not because they are converted toward us, but because we [are converted] toward them, always operating rightly according to the reason of the Good. They are said to be angry because, by receding from them, we are borne into evil, not because they are affected. Iamblichus also thinks that pure intellects, which Porphyry thinks are not moved by prayers, and for this reason that only worldly Gods should be prayed to, are to be prayed to because of intimate sense, not because of the voices of supplications. Likewise sacrifices and symbols should be offered, not because of their matter and corporeal qualities, but because of the incorporeal reasons, species, and measures that are in material things, congruent with the Gods. A small congruity is enough for us to reconcile the Gods to us, since they are most ready to do good. But an agent that is very powerful acts on matter even when it has been only slightly prepared.

## Note

This part finishes the Caput IV embodiment argument and then opens Caput V's sorting of mental orders. The main correction of the Platonists is precise: many divine or mental orders may operate throughout the world, but only the one infinite God is strictly everywhere and infinite. The leaf ends mid-sentence on the distinction between operating everywhere and being everywhere as the same one agent.
