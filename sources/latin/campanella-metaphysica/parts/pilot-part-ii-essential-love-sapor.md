## Opening Finding

Cassirer's Vol. 2 p. 78 Campanella quotation is confirmed against the 1638 witness. The passage does say that the love by which we love God is not accidental but essential, and that participated divinity has `saporem`.

The local argument is sharper than the extracted quote alone suggests. Campanella does not oppose self-love to love of God. He argues that we love ourselves because we love being; since God is being simply, the love of our own finite being is already, more essentially, love of God. The `sapor` of participated divinity is therefore not a decorative taste-metaphor added to the argument. It is the affective sign that our finite being is a participation in divine entity.

The page also joins two hidden registers: `Amor abditus, quo se cuncta amant` and `sensus abditus, quo sese cuncta sapiunt`. This ties the Vol. 2 sapor footnote back to the leaf-402 hidden self-intellection and the leaf-23/438 sapor chain.

## Corrected Latin

Nos videamur, vix ergo & amare, ut dictum est cum de Sapientia loqueremur: & sicuti notitia nostri reflexa evidentior est nobis, quam notitia abdita; ita Amor nostri reflexus adscititiusque evidentior est nobis, quam Amor abditus. Et quia Amor abditus, quo se cuncta amant, ab omnibus Philosophis conceditur, concedendus erat & sensus abditus, quo sese cuncta sapiunt: caeterae passiones, quae retrahunt a non esse, ut ira, timor, odium, pertinent ad esse indirecte: adsunt enim, ut conservent esse a non esse: patet ergo, amorem sui essentialem esse, aliorum vero accidentalem; & hunc fluere ab amore essentiali.

Verumtamen, Amor, quo Deum amamus, non est accidentalis, sed essentialis. Nam ideo nosmetipsos amamus, quia esse amamus: ergo magis amamus esse simpliciter, quam secundum quid; ergo magis amamus Deum, & essentialius. Item nos amamus non modo esse quantum & quandiu sumus: sed esse semper aeternumque: & esse ubique, & posse omnia, & scire omnia, & velle omnia, ita ut nihil sit contra voluntatem nostram: & hic amor est essentialis, & innatus. Deus autem est hoc, quod nobis amamus aeternum, omnipotens, omniscium, omnivolumque. Nos vero caduci, finitique Potentia, Sapientia, & Essentia: ergo magis amamus Deum, quam nosmetipsos, dum amamus nosmetipsos, quia quod non sumus nos, sed quod Deus est amamus. Item id quod nos scimus, est umbra quaedam entitatis divinae; & gaudemus esse, quod sumus, quia Divinitas participata talem saporem habet: Divinitas participata est omne cuiusque esse; ergo omne ens seipsum esse amando, magis amat Deum quam se.

Idem probat D. Thomas duplici ratione, quoniam pars magis amat totum, quam seipsam: ut manus exponit se morti propter vitam totius: & quaecumque sunt unum totum, idem servant. Civis enim probus exponit se morti propter patriam, ut Curtius, Scaevola, Camillus, & alii. Deus autem est totum esse, & totum bonum: nostrum vero esse quaedam particula illius, & participatio. Unde Stoici & Horatius vocant animam Divinae particulam aurae: & Moyses, spiraculum Dei: ergo magis amatur Deus a qualibet creatura, quam proprium esse. Item bonum universale amabilius est particulari: ergo cum Deus sit universale bonum, nostrum vero particulare, magis amamus illud suum, quam nostrum: & haec est, inquit, simpliciter & naturalis dilectio, quae non potest esse perversa: perversa autem foret, si magis ferretur in minus bonum, quam in maius.

Cavillus Scoti, & aliorum, quod pars se exponit morti propter bonum suum, quod habet in toto, quocum est unum, & non quia totum est amabilius; & item quia exponitur a toto, non a se, nihil concludit. Nam si a toto exponitur, melior est totius bonitas. Item in Republica Curtius, & Scaevola, & sancti Martyres sponte sua, non aliorum coactione, sese morti exposuerunt. Item pars non propter unitatem, quam habet cum toto, principaliter exponitur morti: sed secundario, sequendo secundum naturam. Manus enim, & civis se exponunt morti abrenunciantes unitatem, & identitatem cum toto, ut servetur totum, & non ut unitas ipsorum cum ipso sui toto.

## Working Translation

We scarcely seem to see ourselves, and therefore scarcely to love ourselves, as was said when we were speaking about Wisdom. And just as our reflexive knowledge is more evident to us than hidden knowledge, so our reflexive and acquired love of ourselves is more evident to us than hidden Love. And because hidden Love, by which all things love themselves, is conceded by all philosophers, a hidden sense also had to be conceded, by which all things taste or know themselves. The other passions, which draw back from non-being, such as anger, fear, and hatred, pertain to being indirectly; they are present so that they may preserve being from non-being. Therefore it is clear that love of oneself is essential, while love of others is accidental, and that this accidental love flows from essential love.

Nevertheless, the Love by which we love God is not accidental, but essential. For we love ourselves because we love being; therefore we love being simply more than being in a certain respect; therefore we love God more, and more essentially. Likewise, we love not only to be as much and as long as we are, but to be always and eternally, to be everywhere, to be able to do all things, to know all things, and to will all things, so that nothing would be contrary to our will. This love is essential and innate. But God is this which we love for ourselves: eternal, omnipotent, omniscient, and omni-willing. We, however, are perishable and finite in Power, Wisdom, and Essence. Therefore we love God more than ourselves when we love ourselves, because we love not what we are, but what God is. Likewise, what we know is a certain shadow of divine entity; and we rejoice to be what we are because participated Divinity has such a savor. Participated Divinity is the whole being of each thing; therefore every being, by loving itself to be, loves God more than itself.

Thomas proves the same thing by a double argument, since a part loves the whole more than itself: as the hand exposes itself to death for the life of the whole, and whatever things make one whole preserve the same thing. For a good citizen exposes himself to death for his fatherland, as Curtius, Scaevola, Camillus, and others did. God, however, is the whole of being and the whole good; our being is a certain little part of that, and a participation. Hence the Stoics and Horace call the soul a particle of the divine breeze; and Moses calls it the breath of God. Therefore God is loved by any creature more than its own being. Likewise, the universal good is more lovable than the particular; therefore, since God is the universal good, while ours is particular, we love that good of his more than our own. And this, he says, is love simply and naturally, which cannot be perverse; it would be perverse if it were borne toward the lesser good more than toward the greater.

The cavil of Scotus and others concludes nothing: that a part exposes itself to death for its own good, which it has in the whole with which it is one, and not because the whole is more lovable; and likewise that it is exposed by the whole and not by itself. For if it is exposed by the whole, the goodness of the whole is better. Likewise, in the Republic Curtius and Scaevola, and the holy martyrs, exposed themselves to death of their own accord, not by the coercion of others. Again, the part is not principally exposed to death because of the unity it has with the whole, but secondarily, following according to nature. For the hand and the citizen expose themselves to death by renouncing unity and identity with the whole so that the whole may be preserved, not so that their own unity with the whole itself may be preserved.

## Notes

- This is the exact Campanella source for Cassirer's Vol. 2 Part 018 footnote to `Metaphysik, Teil II, S. 78`.
- The immediate context is the distinction between essential love of one's own being and accidental love of alien being. Love of God is the exception: God is not merely an alien being loved accidentally, because God is being simply and the causal whole of participated being.
- `sensus abditus, quo sese cuncta sapiunt` links hidden love to hidden self-sense. The phrase belongs beside leaf 402's `intellectione abdita per suam essentiam` rather than only beside the gustus passages.
- `Divinitas participata talem saporem habet` confirms that `sapor` is doing metaphysical and affective work in Part II, not only etymological work in the sensory skepticism opening.
- `Potentia, Sapientia, & Essentia` is witnessed on the page and is also what Cassirer prints. Do not harmonize it silently to `Potentia, Sapientia, & Amor`. Here the phrase names creaturely finitude in power, wisdom, and essence inside the argument for essential love of God.
- The Aquinas proof turns participation into a part-whole argument: every creature loves God more than its own being because its own being is a particular participation in the universal good.

## Translation Risk

Textual: the target quote and page number have been checked against the page image, but the full page remains a working transcription from ABBYY and normalization. Revisit especially `adscititiusque`, `sensus abditus`, `quod nobis amamus`, and the final Scotus paragraph before encounter-grade use.

Lexical: `saporem` is rendered "savor" to keep the taste/knowing pressure alive without making the passage sound merely culinary. The word here marks the felt sweetness or affective evidence of participated divinity.

Syntactic: `Deus autem est hoc, quod nobis amamus aeternum...` is awkward. The working translation takes `hoc` as pointing to the infinite being we love for ourselves: eternal, omnipotent, omniscient, and omni-willing.

Doctrinal: `Potentia, Sapientia, & Essentia` should remain open. It may be a local finite-being register rather than a variant of the primalities triad. The page itself does not authorize replacing `Essentia` with `Amor`.
