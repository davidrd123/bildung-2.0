## Latin

At visu, et caeteris sensibus, in quibus partim corpulentia, partim corporata vis sensitiva insunt, extenditur homo ad plurima loca usque ad stellas, et obvios montes et campum: et quo distantiora corpora, minus comprehenduntur sensu. Igitur ex hoc palam est, quod bruta ex primalitatum participio extenduntur ultra ambientes superficies: sed non ubique sicut homo, qui etiam mente divinas attingit res, et totum ambit orbem. Et in spatio Mathematico etiam designat ideas, et figuras, et lineas rectas nil a corporibus obiectis impedita.

Pertinent etiam diaboli ad Mundum mentalem, alioquin neque cum Angelis, neque cum mentibus humanis negotiarentur. Propterea dicitur etiam in libro Iob, quod inter Angelos coram Deo affuit etiam Sathan, loquens cum illis, unde palam est, quod in Mundo mentali secundum Naturam sunt etiam contrarietates Metaphysicae, et Morales: et quod non omnes mentes propterea sint beatae, quod e corporibus solutae sint. Item quod nec Natura beat, sed exaltatio Naturae ad gradus diviniores per auxilium confortans, illuminans, et afficiens; et quod nulla essentia nascitur beata, sed sit per elevationem ad meliora. Nec idem est esse, et beatum esse nisi in Deo; ubi primalitates nullatenus sunt contractae.

Post haec notandum scalam esse inter Mundos, qua ascendere et descendere queant Angeli, et animae, et res huiusmodi potentes. Quemadmodum enim in microcosmo spiritus unit mentem sanguini, propterea quod utriusque participat naturam, siquidem primalitates illi communes sunt cum mente, cuius infimum attingit: ita calorem, lucem, et tenuitatem communem habet cum sanguine corpulento fluidoque, ex quo fit tenuitas eius supremum. Sanguis vero cum solido habet communem corpulentiam, quo ligantur: Corpus vero cum Tellure, et aqua communicat in iis crassiori modo; et communicationes non fiunt absque mutuis emolumentis.

Etenim spiritus a mente dignificatur, et mens ab eo obiecta sensibilia capit ad scientias: eoque utitur ad motum corporis, et nutritionem. Spiritus capit a sanguine nutritionem, et sanguis a spiritu coctionem, et conversionem ex altera Natura in sanguineam. Sanguis dat Spiritui instaurationem ex sui tenuitatibus; et corpori ex sui crassitiebus, et ab eo emolumentum habet, ut se contineat, tanquam in vase, ne dissipetur, et officinas et culinas, et molendinum suae regenerationis, et aggenerationis.

Sic sunt Mundi. Archetypus enim mentibus dat esse, et operam, et primalitates. Mentalis Mundo unitur Archetypo primalitatibus, et glorificationes; Mathematico vero ideationibus et operationibus. Mathematicus Materiali dimensionibus, et commensurationibus; Mentali apprehensionibus. Materialis [situali?][?] configurationibus, et extensionibus, et numeris, et calore, et frigore, sicut tellus aquae crassitie, et frigoris sensu exteriori. Etenim non est nix absque pluri frigore: nec aqua absque modico extrinsecus formante. Aqua aeri tenuitate et tepore: Aer aetheri calore, et subtilitate. Et Mens toti per spiritum mundi, sunt etiam proportiones mirae inter ista.

Primalitates tamen omnia complent. Ergo transitus sit in gubernatu, sicut in generatione, ut ex crassitie lentor, ex lentore mollities, ex mollitie viscositas: ex hac fluor, ex quo vapor, et Caelum, et e converso. Sic ex mente ad corpora itur, et e converso per dicta media.

Plurima in spatio immenso esse amphitheatra, in quibus divina repraesentatur opes, et systemata alia aliorum esse centra, aliorum alia, nec propterea immobilia; et omnes fixas vocatas instar nostri solis sese habere, circa quas alii ubique planetae, et circa planetas lunae vagantur, nec propterea Copernicum et Cusanum prorsus Veritatem probavisse.

**ARTIC. VII.**

Super extensum est in spatio Caelum immensum, quod innumerabilia [celat?][?] systemata, quae nos sydera vocamus. Oportet autem quemadmodum in hac caeli parte, in qua vivimus nos, theatrum efficiunt unum ipse Sol et Planetae, ipsum ambientes, et Planetarum Planetae, seu Lunae: sic etiam plurima theatra esse, in quibus centrum amoris cuiusque ipsorum, quod omnia servat circa ipsum rotata, solis nostri similitudinem gerat. Unde visum est antiquioribus fixas vocatas stellas esse soles, circa quos alii Planetae circumferantur; et in cunctis Planetis esse eadem, quae apud nos in Telluris systemate.

Probatum est enim a se ipsis non lucere, sed Solis luce instar Lunae nostrae: quod iam patefieri incipit hoc tempore, quo Veneris astrum observamus instar Lunae crescere, et decrescere; et in Iove maculas, quales in Luna. In ignitissimo autem globo, nulla macula perennis, sed temporalis modo, et non inexistens, sed circumvolitans reperiri potest, quemadmodum in sole per Telescopium observamus.

Et quamquam Nolanus et Cusanus Cardinalis putent solem etiam ex terra, aere, aquis, et igne constare; non videri autem nobis nisi ignem, quoniam solus extenditur extra discum solarem, et radiorum multiplicatione, et complicatione luminum interpositas obscuritates terreas, et aqueas nobis tegit: id tamen credibile non est. Etenim maculae eius, quae iam per Telescopium sunt apparentes, circumvolitant quasi nubes: nec in eodem situ et figura perseverant, unde nec stellulae esse possunt, quemadmodum in Physicis quaest. demonstravimus. Praeterea decet alicubi ignem purum reperiri, qui non nisi in sole. Praeterea summus eius candor, et circumgyratio idem probant. ## English

But by sight and the other senses, in which partly corpulence, partly corporeally embodied sensitive power are present, the human being is extended to very many places, all the way to the stars, and to the mountains and plain before him; and the more distant bodies are, the less they are comprehended by sense. Therefore from this it is clear that beasts, by participation in the primalities, are extended beyond the surrounding surfaces, but not everywhere as the human being is, who also by mind reaches divine things and encompasses the whole sphere. And in Mathematical space too he marks out ideas, figures, and straight lines, hindered in no way by objected bodies.

Devils too belong to the mental World; otherwise they would transact neither with Angels nor with human minds. Therefore it is said also in the book of Job that Satan too was present among the Angels before God, speaking with them; whence it is clear that in the mental World according to Nature there are also Metaphysical and Moral contrarieties, and that not all minds are therefore blessed because they have been freed from bodies. Likewise, Nature does not beatify, but the exaltation of Nature to more divine grades through aid that strengthens, illuminates, and affects; and no essence is born blessed, but becomes so through elevation to better things. Nor are being and blessed being the same except in God, where the primalities are in no way contracted.

After these things it must be noted that there is a ladder among the Worlds, by which Angels, souls, and powerful things of this sort can ascend and descend. For just as in the microcosm spirit unites mind to blood, because it participates in the nature of each, since the primalities are common to it with mind, whose lowest [part] it touches, so it has heat, light, and subtlety in common with corporeal and fluid blood, from which its highest subtlety comes. But blood has corpulence in common with the solid, by which they are bound. Body, however, communicates with Earth and water in those things in a grosser mode; and communications do not happen without mutual advantages.

For spirit is dignified by mind, and mind takes sensible objects from it for the sciences, and uses it for the motion and nutrition of the body. Spirit takes nutrition from blood, and blood from spirit takes cooking and conversion from the other Nature into the sanguine. Blood gives restoration to Spirit from its subtleties, and to body from its grossnesses, and has advantage from body, so that it may contain itself as in a vessel, lest it be dissipated, and [may have] workshops, kitchens, and the mill of its regeneration and aggeneration.

So are the Worlds. For the Archetype gives to minds being, operation, and primalities. The Mental [World] is united to the Archetype by primalities and glorifications; but to the Mathematical by ideations and operations. The Mathematical [is united] to the Material by dimensions and commensurations; to the Mental by apprehensions. The Material [is united] to the [situational?][?] by configurations, extensions, numbers, heat, and cold, just as earth [is united] to waters by grossness and by an exterior sense of cold. For there is no snow without more cold; nor water without a little [cold] forming outwardly. Water [is united] to air by subtlety and warmth; Air to aether by heat and subtlety. And Mind [is united] to the whole through the spirit of the world; there are also marvelous proportions among these.

Yet the primalities fill all things. Therefore there is transition in governance as in generation: as from grossness [comes] viscosity, from viscosity softness, from softness stickiness, from this fluidity, from which [comes] vapor and Heaven, and conversely. Thus one goes from mind to bodies, and conversely through the said media.

That there are very many amphitheaters in immense space, in which the riches of divine things are represented; and that some systems are centers of others, others of others, and are not therefore immobile; and that all the so-called fixed [stars] have themselves like our Sun, around which everywhere there are other planets, and around planets moons wander; and that Copernicus and Cusanus have not therefore proved the Truth completely.

**ARTICLE VII.**

Over what is extended in space is immense Heaven, which [conceals?][?] innumerable systems that we call stars. But just as in this part of heaven in which we live, the Sun itself and the Planets surrounding it, and the Planets of Planets, or Moons, make one theater, so there must also be very many theaters in which the center of love of each of them, which preserves all things turned around itself, bears the likeness of our Sun. Hence it seemed to the ancients that the stars called fixed are suns, around which other Planets are carried, and that in all Planets there are the same things as among us in the system of Earth.

For it has been proved that they do not shine from themselves, but by the light of the Sun like our Moon. This now begins to become clear in this time, in which we observe the star of Venus growing and decreasing like the Moon, and spots in Jupiter like those in the Moon. But in a most fiery globe no spot can be found that is permanent, but only temporal, and not inexisting, but flying around, just as we observe in the sun through the Telescope.

And although Nolanus and Cardinal Cusanus think that the sun too consists of earth, air, waters, and fire, but that only fire appears to us, because it alone is extended beyond the solar disk and by multiplication of rays and complication of lights covers for us the interposed terrestrial and watery darknesses, yet this is not credible. For its spots, which now are apparent through the Telescope, fly around like clouds; nor do they persist in the same site and figure, whence they cannot be little stars, as we demonstrated in the Physical questions. Besides, it is fitting that pure fire be found somewhere, which is nowhere except in the sun. Moreover its supreme brightness and turning-around prove the same. Nor indeed does it complete one circuit in a month, as Galileo thinks, arguing so much from the motion of little clouds; for neither do the clouds of our earth keep the motion of the earth.
