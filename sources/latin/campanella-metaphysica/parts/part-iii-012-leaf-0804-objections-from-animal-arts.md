## Latin

Esse corporis[?]: tunc enim ab eo animus separatur et functiones viles corpori relinquit, rapitque illud ad diviniores: evidens signum, quod anima per separationem a corpore non moritur, sed perficitur. Tandem apparitiones animarum post mortem in totis transactis saeculis, ut ex historiis sacris et profanis scimus, animas vivere post corpus, et semper probant.

Appendix. Cum autem operationes non cognovisset Aristoteles, sed eas modo quae spiritui animalium communes sunt, et posuerit animam actum corporis, consequens est ut immortalem animam non cognoverit.

Cum vero facit eam immistam, sumit etiam thesim Anaxagorae, qui de totius mundi autore loquitur, cuiusmodi etiam Christiani Theologi vocant Deum intellectum agentem. Et revera solus Deus est ita activus, quod nullo pacto passivus. Alii vero intellectus omnes patiuntur et agunt, sed non eo modo quo Aristoteles statuit, et ob illum finem, ut ex superioribus patuit. Mirum quomodo Aristoteles operationes divinas animae Sibyllinas vocet deliria atrae bilis, animales vero signa immortalitatis; sed revera confarcinat positionem ex multorum opinionibus, ideo sibi et naturae contrarius est, quod olfacit etiam Scotus, Lactantius, et alii.

Rationes difficiles contra praestantiam et immortalitatem mentis humanae proponuntur dissolvendae. Caput V. Articulus I.

Sed contra animae immortalitatem et divinitatem multa insurgunt argumenta. Primo, quoniam et brutis insunt quaecumque excolunt[?] hominem artes. Habent enim medicinam: clystere[?], phlebotomiam, et purgationem per vomitum, et oculos acuere marathro. Habent artem militarem pisces turmatim proeliantes, sub ducibus et excubiis. Et grues, corvi, apes, simiaeque habent politicam monarchicam; formicae democraticam; locustae anarchiam populariter viventes. Fabricantur domos et nidos aves et apes, et araneae retia sua[?]. In venatione exercentur pisces, araneae, et ferae multae. Equi multi castitatem observant erga matrem, et omnia sciunt quae pertinent ad propriam vitam, non secus ac homo quae ad suam. Et quamvis serviant animalia hominibus, id accidit vilioribus, ut asinis, pecudibus, et canibus, non autem generosis elephantibus et leonibus. At hac ratione etiam homines viles serviunt nobilioribus, et aliquando nobiles, sicut accidit elephantis interdum.

Secundo, item habent animalia religionem, quoniam elephas baptizat se a coitu et peccato, et adorat lunam sicut Gentiles multi, scitque genua flectere. Nec modo homines, sed et herbae religionem habent: heliotropium enim ad solem convertitur quadam religione ipsum observans in motu longitudinali; salices vero et clivaria[?] latitudinali ipsum consequuntur comarum conversione. Potest inter apes esse religio: nam et fucos habent qui sunt sacerdotes; et cum plurimae species vivant in politia, necesse habent religionem aliquam servare.

Tertio, praeterea quomodo scimus nos ea carere litteris, religione, et scientia, cum videamus colloqui, mutuo dare et reddere voces? Et formicas in aestate natas congregare pro hieme? Ergo discunt hoc ab aliis, et extat inter eos doctrina et religio. Et Salomon ad scholam formicae nos mittit.

Quarto, praeterea quomodo scimus bruta carere miraculis, martyrio, et prophetia, spectantibus ad comprobationem religionis, cum palam sit alios ab aliis occidi, atque alios sequi alios cum stupore, ut Phoenices aves; et prophetare de eventibus pluviarum, ventorum, et bellorum nostrorum, ut cornix, bubo, vultures, et mutuis vocibus haec sibi invicem indicare?

Quinto, praeterea rationis participia esse omnia nos in Physiologia ostendimus, et innumeris experientia confirmant. Sentiunt enim et ipsa partem ignis, non totum; et ex parte totum intelligunt, et ex uno homine alios homines, et ex pane hoc alios panes.

Sexto, item utimur sermone et politia, quae absque ratione exerceri non possunt. Utimur insidiis et stratagematis. Quando canis leporem insectatur, praecidit illi viam; et quidam canis passus iniuriam ab altero multas escas abscondit, advocavit canes ad manducandum, et locavit eos in militia, et contra iniuriantem irruerunt et occiderunt. Quis non miretur cancri syllogismum, quo conchiliorum testas iniecto lapillo apertas retinet, ut possit carne vesci et cruribus non teneri adstrictus? Inspice araneas, et plenas syllogismis videbis in captione muscarum, et vulpes in insidiis contra gallinas; et piscis piscator, et polypus astu multo venantur. Nam ut dubitet Plutarchus utrum plus rationalitatis insit marinis quam terrestribus animantibus.

Quapropter si rationalia sunt, et omnia quae homo facit et ipsa faciunt; si qua nos facimus quae illis non conveniunt, putanda sunt eis non necesse[?], quoniam opus non sunt, non autem quia nos melioris ordinis simus, cum praesertim videamus homines quosdam rudiores brutis, et pueros indoctiores canibus et simiis. Differimus ergo per magis et minus tantum.

Septimo, praeterea, ut ait Salomon, nihil habet homo iumento amplius; ut spirant illa, ita et nos, et easdem ob causas morimur. Et omnia revertuntur in terram, et partes in brutis et in nobis eaedem. Unde Vesalius admiratur quod in hominis sectione nihil invenerit plus quam in anatomia brutorum praeter cerebrum maius, sed cellas cerebri easdem. Pythagoras vero putat animas esse easdem brutorum et hominum, et transmigrare ex nobis ad illa, et videri nos sapientiores quoniam organa corporis humani sunt meliora, et ideo melius operamur. Ac animam canis in corpore hominis omnia humana opera edituram. Nonne videmus grues graciliores-

## English

[A kind of death] of the body[?]: for then the mind is separated from it, and it leaves the body's base functions to the body, and snatches it toward more divine ones: an evident sign that the soul does not die by separation from the body, but is perfected. Finally, apparitions of souls after death in all past ages, as we know from sacred and profane histories, prove always that souls live after the body.

Appendix. But since Aristotle did not know the operations, except only those which are common to the spirit of animals, and posited the soul as the act of the body, it follows that he did not know the immortal soul.

But when he makes it unmixed, he also takes up the thesis of Anaxagoras, who speaks of the author of the whole world, such as Christian theologians also call God the agent intellect. And truly God alone is so active that he is in no way passive. But all other intellects suffer and act, though not in the way Aristotle established and for that end, as was clear from the preceding points. It is strange how Aristotle calls the divine operations of the Sibylline soul the deliriums of black bile, but animal operations signs of immortality; but truly he patches together his position from the opinions of many, and therefore he is contrary to himself and to nature, as Scotus, Lactantius, and others also smell out.

Difficult reasons against the excellence and immortality of the human mind are proposed, to be dissolved. Chapter V. Article I.

But against the immortality and divinity of the soul many arguments rise up. First, because all the arts that cultivate[?] the human being are also present in beasts. For they have medicine: clyster[?], phlebotomy, purgation through vomiting, and the sharpening of eyes with fennel. Fish fighting in squadrons, under leaders and watches, have military art. Cranes, crows, bees, and apes have monarchic politics; ants have democratic politics; locusts have anarchy, living popularly. Birds and bees make houses and nests, and spiders their webs[?]. Fish, spiders, and many wild animals practice hunting. Many horses observe chastity toward their mother, and know all things that pertain to their own life, no less than the human being knows what pertains to his own. And although animals serve human beings, this happens with the baser ones, such as asses, sheep, and dogs, not however with noble elephants and lions. But by this reasoning base human beings also serve nobler ones, and sometimes noble ones do too, as sometimes happens with elephants.

Second, animals likewise have religion, because the elephant baptizes itself from intercourse and sin, adores the moon like many Gentiles, and knows how to bend its knees. Nor do human beings alone have religion, but also plants: for the heliotrope turns toward the sun by a certain religion, observing it in longitudinal motion; willows and clivaria[?] follow it latitudinally by a turning of their hair. There can be religion among bees, for they also have drones who are priests; and since very many species live in political order, they must preserve some religion.

Third, how do we know that they lack letters, religion, and science, when we see them converse, mutually give and return voices? And [we see] ants born in summer gathering for winter. Therefore they learn this from others, and teaching and religion exist among them. And Solomon sends us to the school of the ant.

Fourth, how do we know that beasts lack miracles, martyrdom, and prophecy, which look toward the confirmation of religion, when it is clear that some are killed by others, and that some follow others with astonishment, as phoenix birds do; and that they prophesy concerning the outcomes of rains, winds, and our wars, as the crow, owl, and vultures do, and indicate these things to one another by mutual voices?

Fifth, we have shown in the Physiologia that all things are participants in reason, and experience confirms this by innumerable cases. For they also sense a part of fire, not the whole; and from a part they understand the whole, and from one human being other human beings, and from this bread other breads.

Sixth, we use speech and political order, which cannot be exercised without reason. We use traps and stratagems. When a dog chases a hare, it cuts off its path; and a certain dog, after suffering an injury from another, hid many foods, summoned dogs to eat, placed them in battle-order, and they rushed against the offender and killed him. Who would not wonder at the crab's syllogism, by which, after inserting a little stone, it keeps the shells of shellfish open so that it can eat the flesh and not be held fast by its legs? Look at spiders, and you will see them full of syllogisms in catching flies, and foxes in traps against hens; and the angler-fish and the polyp hunt with much craft. Hence Plutarch even doubts whether there is more rationality in sea-animals than in land-animals.

Therefore if they are rational, and if they also do all the things that the human being does, then if we do some things that do not suit them, they should be thought not necessary[?] for them, because they have no use for them, not because we are of a better order, especially since we see some human beings rougher than beasts, and children less taught than dogs and apes. Therefore we differ by more and less only.

Seventh, furthermore, as Solomon says, the human being has nothing more than the beast; as they breathe, so do we, and we die from the same causes. And all things return into earth, and the parts in beasts and in us are the same. Hence Vesalius wonders that in dissecting a human being he found nothing more than in the anatomy of beasts except a larger brain, but the same cells of the brain. Pythagoras, however, thinks that the souls of beasts and human beings are the same, and migrate from us into them, and that we seem wiser because the organs of the human body are better, and therefore we operate better.
