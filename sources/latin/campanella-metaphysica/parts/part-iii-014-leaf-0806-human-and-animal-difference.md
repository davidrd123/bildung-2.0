## Latin

Paratur responsio ad praedictas rationes: et primo de similitudine et differentia inter homines et bruta, quoad excellentiam earundem operationum et directionis earum ad fines alios in hanc vel in aliam vitam.

Caput V.

Quoniam quaecumque ab eadem causa emanant, similitudinem illius gerunt; ipsa quoque diversa entia ab uno quovis producta, quatenus ab illo sunt uno, inter se quoque similitudinem, prolem unitatis illius, habent. Propterea igitur cum omnia entia sint a Deo uno, similia sunt saltem in communibus in eo quod entia. Nam in particularitatibus contracta sunt ad suas differentias. At in quantum entia constituuntur Potestate, Sapientia, et Amore. Ergo in his similia sunt, et differunt gradibus.

Cum ergo animalia non modo conveniant cum hominibus in eo quod entia, sed etiam in genere animalis contrahente ens, consequens est ut in Potestate, Sapientia, et Appetitu sint similiora. Idcirco nos rite speculantes invenimus in brutis sermonem, quo mutuis officiis colligantur, et rationem sensitivam, qua de ignotis iudicant per nota, et similitudinis notionem, quae est in electione herbae in communi, et mali et amicitiarum[?], et artem fabricandi cellas et retia et venandi.

Adeoque ista sunt vera, ut Lactantius Firmianus et Osius Cardinalis religione sola, nos vero etiam libertate, separemus hominem a brutis. Quae licet sponte, non tamen libere, actu ex se elicito, operantur. Nec tamen propterea sunt immortalia, sed sunt nobis similia, quoniam ab eadem sapientia Dei constituta sunt; ideoque eosdem sciendi modos habent, qui in nobis sunt. Sed quoniam in his adeo excedit homo bruta, quod Deus ipsorum potest existimari, propterea diversi ordinis ac divinioris ponendus est, sicut Angelus prae homine, licet sit intellectualis ut homo.

Eadem enim quae bruta faciunt facit homo, ac insuper multo plura ac diviniora. Participant enim lapides naturam plantarum, plantae brutorum, bruta hominis, homo Angeli, Angelus Dei; et quod est per essentiam in superioribus est per participium in inferioribus, ut docet Dionysius et Thomas; et infimum superioris est in supremo inferioris gradatim.

Bruta enim praesenti sensu rei moventur, et non eligunt nisi quod magis blanditur sensui, sicut pueri et insani in genere humano; et renuunt praesentia bona quando aliquo malo arcentur, ut foetore, vel baculo, vel hostili infestatione. Quapropter dicuntur instinctu regi, potius quam ratione. Instinctus enim est subito facere id quod offertur primum, nullo praevio syllogismo, vel exili valde obscuroque, qui ab obiectis inducitur et passionibus, non ab intus elicitur libere, sed instinctu Dei vel obiectorum vel passione.

Homo autem pensat et pensiculat quid mali lateat in bono oblato, laesurum salutem corporis aut animae aut Reipublicae, etiam quando nullis movetur obiectis, sed ex se ipso excogitat seque ad syllogismum movet innata libertate, non addita modo obiectorum motione. Et de his facit libros, doctrinas, praecepta, ratiocinia plurima, et leges, quae brutis non conveniunt, sicut nec pueris nec insanis.

Articulus I.

Et licet in multis ratiocinentur bruta, adeo tamen exilis est ratiocinatio eorum, ut nulla videatur humanae comparata; unde eis damus discursum sensualem, non autem mentalem. Item damus eis discursum a moventibus obiectis superadditum, non ab innata vi sola et per se elicitum.

Nidificant quidem, sed non per Mathematicam scientiam, sed apponendo stramenta iuxta commoditatem corporis. Clystere sibi facit ibis, sed non per scientiam Medicinae; libris enim et studiis caret, sed quia sentit intra anum duritiem, iudicat mollienda aqua. Hippopotamus se sanguine ingurgitari sentiens secat venas, et serpens faeniculo linit oculos, caliginem extrahere cupiens. Et in his omnibus reguntur ab Angelis et anima mundi, sicut nos quando a casu aliquid boni invenimus: ut qui in nemore est et ignorat viam Romanam, quodam casu ambulans incidit in eam, et Romam, quorsum cupiebat, se confert.

Casu quodam quis sulphur et nitrum in mortario ad aliud opus praeparato tetigit igne, et pistillum impetu accensi pulveris evolare impetuose cernens, statim bombardarum opificium excogitavit. Et tamen hic casus fuit in nutu Dei, ut nova arma ad novas monarchias et novum saeculum invenirentur. Et hic quidem est instinctus; et ipsius sensus commotio casualis est instinctus. Et secundum hunc bruta operantur, ut etiam pueri et insani, non tamen ex arte mentali diu quaesita et confecta, uti homines consueverunt.

Item neque ex quacumque occasione sciunt novas artes inventare, ut homo; sed naturalibus dumtaxat reguntur, quae absque studio addiscunt et quodam usu perdiseunt perficiuntque. Multa ignorantes recte faciunt quodam instinctu; sic et bruta. In libro De sensu rerum negavimus instinctum, sed qui est absque sensu instigantis et instigati.

Nec quia homo ab animalibus multa addiscit, discipulus eorum est. Nam et a plantis, et a lapidibus, et cunctis rebus addiscit homo tamquam discipulus Dei inspiciens opera illius ut exemplaria suorum, et non ut rerum ipsarum discipulus. A scriptura sapientiam discit et ab horologio tempus; nec tamen est scripturae et horologii discipulus, sed scribentis et significantis per haec instrumenta. Neque enim animalia docent in scholis nostris, sed sunt signa in schola Dei. At animalia discunt ab homine, ut equi equestrem artem, simii ludos; et vidimus asinum et capram tripudium et choream etiam super funem more hominis funambuli assuefactione agere edoctum.

Sed hoc rarum est, operosumque negotium illis ac nobis, et mirum est quia est in eis; at in homine tot mirabiliora insunt, ut illud non reputetur. Dic ut ludant latrunculis, et scribant poemata, et metiantur palmo vel astrolabio distantias, et suant calceos, et remigium regant. Quae opera mechanica et nulla sunt, aut reputantur in homine:

## English

The response to the foregoing reasons is prepared: and first concerning the similarity and difference between human beings and beasts, with respect to the excellence of the same operations and the direction of those operations to other ends in this life or in another life.

Chapter V.

Since whatever things emanate from the same cause bear a likeness of it, diverse beings themselves, produced by any one thing, insofar as they are from that one thing, also have likeness among themselves, the offspring of that unity. Therefore, since all beings are from one God, they are similar at least in common things, in that they are beings. For in particularities they are contracted to their differences. But insofar as they are beings, they are constituted by Power, Wisdom, and Love. Therefore in these they are similar, and they differ by degrees.

Therefore since animals agree with human beings not only in being beings, but also in the genus of animal, which contracts being, it follows that in Power, Wisdom, and Appetite they are more similar. Therefore, when we speculate rightly, we find in beasts speech, by which they are bound together in mutual services, and sensitive reason, by which they judge unknown things through known things, and a notion of likeness, which is present in choosing an herb in common, and of evil and of friendships[?], and the art of making cells and webs and of hunting.

And these things are so true that Lactantius Firmianus and Cardinal Osius separate the human being from beasts by religion alone, but we also by freedom. Although beasts act spontaneously, they do not act freely, by an act elicited from themselves. Nor nevertheless are they immortal on that account; but they are similar to us, because they were constituted by the same wisdom of God, and therefore they have the same modes of knowing that are in us. But because in these the human being exceeds beasts to such a degree that he can be judged their god, he must therefore be posited as of a different and more divine order, just as the angel is before the human being, although it is intellectual as the human being is.

For the human being does the same things that beasts do, and in addition many more and more divine things. Stones participate in the nature of plants, plants in the nature of beasts, beasts in the nature of the human being, the human being in the nature of the Angel, the Angel in the nature of God; and what is by essence in higher things is by participation in lower things, as Dionysius and Thomas teach; and the lowest of the higher is in the highest of the lower, step by step.

For beasts are moved by the present sense of the thing, and do not choose except what more flatters sense, as children and the insane do in the human genus; and they reject present goods when they are kept off by some evil, such as stench, a stick, or hostile attack. For this reason they are said to be ruled by instinct rather than reason. For instinct is to do suddenly what is offered first, with no prior syllogism, or with a very slight and very obscure one, which is induced by objects and passions, not elicited freely from within, but by the instinct of God or of objects or by passion.

The human being, however, weighs and weighs carefully what evil may lie hidden in an offered good, harmful to the health of body or soul or republic, even when he is moved by no objects, but from himself thinks out and moves himself to a syllogism by inborn freedom, not merely by the added motion of objects. And from these things he makes books, teachings, precepts, very many reasonings, and laws, which do not belong to beasts, just as they do not belong to children or the insane.

Article I.

And although beasts reason in many things, nevertheless their reasoning is so slight that it seems nothing compared with human reasoning; hence we give them sensual discourse, but not mental discourse. Likewise we give them discourse superadded by moving objects, not elicited by innate force alone and through itself.

They do nest, but not through mathematical science, but by placing straw according to the convenience of the body. The ibis gives itself a clyster, but not through the science of medicine, for it lacks books and studies; rather, because it senses hardness inside the anus, it judges that it must be softened by water. The hippopotamus, sensing that it is gorged with blood, cuts its veins; and the serpent smears fennel on its eyes, desiring to draw out dimness. And in all these things they are ruled by Angels and the world-soul, just as we are when by chance we find something good: as one who is in a grove and does not know the Roman road, walking by a certain chance, falls upon it, and brings himself to Rome, where he wanted to go.

By a certain chance someone touched with fire sulphur and nitre in a mortar prepared for another work, and seeing the pestle fly out violently by the force of the ignited powder, immediately devised the craft of cannons. And yet this chance was in the nod of God, so that new weapons would be discovered for new monarchies and a new age. And this indeed is instinct; and the chance stirring of sense itself is instinct. According to this beasts operate, as children and the insane also do, but not from mental art long sought and completed, as human beings are accustomed to do.

Likewise, from just any occasion they do not know how to invent new arts, as the human being does; but they are ruled only by natural things, which they learn without study and by a certain use learn thoroughly and perfect. Knowing nothing, they do many things rightly by a certain instinct; so too beasts. In the book De sensu rerum we denied instinct, but the kind that is without sense of the instigator and the instigated.

Nor because the human being learns many things from animals is he their disciple. For the human being also learns from plants, stones, and all things as a disciple of God, looking at his works as exemplars of his own, and not as a disciple of the things themselves. From writing he learns wisdom, and from a clock the time; nevertheless he is not a disciple of the writing and the clock, but of the one writing and signifying through these instruments. For animals do not teach in our schools, but are signs in the school of God. But animals learn from the human being, as horses learn the equestrian art and apes games; and we have seen an ass and a goat taught by habituation to do a dance and chorus even on a rope, in the manner of a human tightrope-walker.

But this is rare and an effortful business for them and for us, and it is marvelous because it is in them; but in the human being so many more marvelous things are present that this is not counted.
