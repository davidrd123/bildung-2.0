## Latin

**Paratur responsio ad praedictas rationes: de similitudine et differentia inter homines et bruta.**

Quoniam quaecumque ab eadem causa emanant similitudinem illius gerunt, et omnia entia sunt a Deo uno, similia sunt saltem in communibus in eo quod entia. In particularitatibus contracta sunt ad suas differentias. In quantum entia constituuntur potestate, sapientia, et amore; ergo in his similia sunt et differunt gradibus.

Cum animalia conveniant cum hominibus non modo in eo quod entia, sed etiam in genere animalis contrahente ens, consequens est ut in potestate, sapientia, et appetitu sint similiora. Idcirco invenimus in brutis sermonem quo mutuis officiis colligantur, rationem sensitivam qua de ignotis iudicant per nota, similitudinis notionem, artem fabricandi cellas et retia et venandi.

Adeo ista sunt vera ut Lactantius Firmianus et Osius Cardinalis religione sola, nos vero etiam libertate, separemus hominem a brutis. Quae licet sponte, non tamen libere, actu ex se elicito, operantur. Nec propterea sunt immortalia, sed sunt nobis similia, quoniam ab eadem sapientia Dei constituta sunt.

Quoniam in his adeo excedit homo bruta quod Deus ipsorum potest existimari, diversi ordinis ac divinioris ponendus est, sicut angelus prae homine licet sit intellectualis ut homo. Participant lapides naturam plantarum, plantae brutorum, bruta hominis, homo angeli, angelus Dei; quod est per essentiam in superioribus est per participium in inferioribus.

Bruta praesentis sensus rei moventur et non eligunt nisi quod magis blanditur sensui; dicuntur instinctu regi potius quam ratione. Instinctus est subito facere id quod offertur primum, nullo praevio syllogismo vel exili valde obscuroque, qui ab obiectis inducitur et passionibus, non ab intus elicitur libere.

Homo autem pensat et pensiculat quid mali lateat in bono oblato, etiam quando nullis movetur obiectis, sed ex se ipso excogitat seque ad syllogismum movet innata libertate. De his facit libros, doctrinas, praecepta, ratiocinia plurima, et leges, quae brutis non conveniunt sicut nec pueris nec insanis.

Nidificant bruta, sed non per mathematicam scientiam, sed apponendo stramenta iuxta commoditatem corporis. Clysterem sibi facit ibis, hippopotamus venas secat, serpens faeniculo linit oculos; in his reguntur ab angelis et anima mundi, sicut nos quando casu aliquid boni invenimus. Casus ipse est in nutu Dei.

Nec quia homo ab animalibus multa addiscit discipulus eorum est. A plantis, lapidibus, et cunctis rebus addiscit homo tanquam discipulus Dei inspiciens opera illius ut exemplaria suorum. Animalia sunt signa in schola Dei; animalia vero discunt ab homine, ut equi artem equestrem, simiae ludos, asinus et capra tripudium super funem.

## English

Campanella's reply begins by conceding similarity. Whatever comes from the same cause bears some likeness to it. Since all beings come from one God, they are alike at least in being; as beings they are constituted by power, wisdom, and love, differing by degree and contraction. Animals are even more like humans because both are animals.

So animals really do have speech, mutual offices, sensitive reason, recognition of similarity, and arts of building, hunting, and making webs or cells. Lactantius and Osius separated humans from beasts by religion alone; Campanella adds liberty. Beasts act spontaneously but not freely, not by an act elicited from themselves.

Similarity does not make animals immortal. They are similar because formed by the same wisdom of God. Human beings exceed them so much that they can be considered gods over them, just as angels exceed humans. The chain is participatory: stones share something of plants, plants of beasts, beasts of humans, humans of angels, angels of God. What is essentially in higher beings is participatively in lower ones.

Beasts are moved by present sense and choose what flatters sense unless some present evil blocks it. This is instinct: an immediate act induced by objects and passions, without a prior syllogism, or with a very small and obscure one. Humans weigh what hidden evil may be in a presented good, even when no object is moving them, and move themselves to reasoning by inborn freedom.

Animal arts are therefore real but not mental science. Birds nest by bodily fit, not mathematics; the ibis, hippopotamus, and serpent use medical operations by instinct, under angels and world-soul. Humans learn from animals and plants as signs in God's school, not as disciples of animals themselves. Animals can learn from humans, but rarely, laboriously, and only in minor arts compared with the ordinary range of human works.

## Note

This leaf gives the cleanest answer so far to the pansensist objection. Campanella does not deny animal reason; he grades it. The human difference is not absence versus presence of cognition, but instinctive object-induced operation versus inwardly elicited free mental discourse.
