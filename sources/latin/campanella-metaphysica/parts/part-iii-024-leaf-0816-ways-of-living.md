## Latin

**Dicendum esse de vita divinitus comparanda, angelitus, caelitus, humanitus, bestialiter, et de cunctis. Caput VII.**

Onotrias summa, omnipotens, omniscia, omnivola, sicut seipsam communicavit essentias ideales ad existentiam deducendo mirificentissime, ita et res cunctas colligavit, imo et physice et moraliter communicari voluit. Corpora non modo participio primalitatum constant, sed etiam angelorum et animorum gubernatione reguntur.

Animas quoque corporibus communicant quibus vitae vinculis alligantur; angeli autem vinculis assistentiae et regiminis. Corpora mathematicis sustentantur; mathematicum spatium ad se corpora trahit repleri cupiens; calor et frigus omnibus communicant dum corporibus occupatis uti satagunt.

Cogimur hinc admirari summam Dei potentiam, sapientiam, et amorem, qui potuerit, sciverit, et voluerit mathematica corporibus, corpora mentibus, mentes utrisque, res omnes omnibus ita colligare ut velint ita esse.

Nihil mirabilius est quam quod anima velit in corpore vivere et corporis mortem aufugiat abhorreatque, non secus ac si ipsa moreretur; cum tamen quando seipsam in suis operationibus intuetur, quae extra mundos efferuntur et omnia transvolant corpora, incessanter experiatur se a corpore coartari, minui, et carcerari potius quam iuvari. Trismegistus et alii vocant corpus mortem vivam, sepulchrum portatile, ergastulum, indumentum pravitatis et ignorantiae.

Quando ita contingit, volente Deo, experimur mentem sibi vitam corporalem comparasse et corpori fabricando regendoque addici. Dum hoc facit, rebus corporalibus exterioribus indigere agnoscit et sibi comparat vitam exteriorem. Alii bestialiter vivunt, bestiarum moribus delectati, ut Epicurii et deliciosi; alii humanitus, qui politica delectantur et sophia; alii caelitus, qui caelestia investigant incessanter; alii angelitus, malis angelis adhaerendo ut magi et lamiae, aut bonis ut eremitae; alii divinitus, ut Petrus, Paulus, Maria, Moyses.

Propterea in sequentibus de vitabus dicendum restat: quomodo reversio ad Deum hominibus apprime conveniens sit.

## English

Caput VII opens by saying that life can be acquired or lived divinely, angelically, celestially, humanly, bestially, and in all the other modes. The supreme Onotrias, omnipotent, omniscient, and all-willing, communicates itself by bringing ideal essences into existence and by tying all things together physically and morally.

Bodies are not only constituted by participation in the primalities; they are ruled by angels and souls. Souls are bound to bodies by bonds of life; angels by bonds of assistance and rule. Mathematical space draws bodies into itself to be filled. Heat and cold communicate with all things as they try to use the bodies they occupy.

The astonishing thing is that soul wants bodily life and flees bodily death as if it itself were dying. Yet when it looks at itself in its own operations, which are carried beyond worlds and fly across all bodies, it experiences body as narrowing, diminishing, and imprisoning it. Hence the Hermetic language of body as living death, portable tomb, prison, and garment of depravity and ignorance.

Still, by God's will mind has acquired bodily life for itself and is devoted to fabricating and governing the body. From this arise different lives: bestial, as in Epicureans and the pleasure-loving; human, in politics and wisdom; celestial, in continual investigation of the heavens; angelic, either with evil angels in magic and witchcraft or good angels among hermits; divine, as in Peter, Paul, Mary, and Moses. The following discourse will ask how return to God most properly belongs to humans.

## Note

This is a clean bridge from the human-soul book into the religion book. Liber XIV ends by classifying ways of life according to what the soul attaches itself to, while Liber XVI will open from the soul's recognition that it is outside its homeland. The body-as-prison language is back, but now without Origenist preexistence: prison is a divinely permitted condition for exercise and return.
