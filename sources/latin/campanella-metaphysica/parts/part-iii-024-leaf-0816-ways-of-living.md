## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] constant, sed etiam Angelorum et animorum gubernatione reguntur. Animas quoque corporibus communicant, quibus vitae vinculis alligantur. Angeli autem vinculis assistentiae et regiminis, ut saepe dictum est. Corpora Mathematicis sustentantur; Mathematicum spatium ad se corpora trahit, repleri cupiens. Calor et frigus omnibus communicant, dum corporibus occupatis uti satagunt.

Hinc contingit ut alia Entia propriis vitis, alia alienis magis vivant. Cogimur hinc admirari summam Dei Potentiam, Sapientiam, et Amorem, qui potuerit, sciverit, et voluerit Mathematica corporibus, et corpora mentibus, et mentes utrisque, et res omnes omnibus ita colligare, ut velint ita esse. Unde nihil mirabilius quam quod anima velit in corpore vivere et corporis mortem aufugiat abhorreatque, non secus ac si ipsa moreretur, cum tamen, quando seipsam in suis operationibus intuetur, quae extra Mundos efferuntur et omnia transvolant corpora, non modo quia ipsa intra se per ipsorum notiones scit, sed etiam quia ad ipsa extra se per notiones illas refertur et quasi egreditur, ita ut quidam dicat esse animam magis ubi amat quam ubi animat, incessanter experiatur se a corpore coartari et minui et carcerari potius quam iuvari, ita ut Trismegistus et alii vocent iure corpus mortem

vivam, et sepulchrum portatile, et ergastulum, et indumentum pravitatis et ignorantiae. At quando ita contigit, volente Deo, experimur mentem sibi vitam corporalem comparasse, et corpori fabricando regendoque addici. Et dum hoc facit et rebus corporalibus exterioribus ad hoc se indigere agnoscit, etiam sibi comparare vitam exteriorem, ita ut alii bestialiter vivant bestiarum moribus delectati, ut Epicurii et deliciosi; alii humanitus, qui politica delectantur et sophia; alii caelitus, qui caelestia investigant incessanter; alii angelitus, sed quidem malis Angelis adhaerendo, ut magi et lamiae, Simon Magus, necromanti; alii bonis, ut heremitae multi, Paulus, Antonius, Hilarion; alii divinitus, ut Petrus, Paulus, Maria, Moyses. Propterea in sequentibus de vitabus dicendum restat.

Sunt enim qui de vita caelitus comparanda scripsere, ut Ficinus. Sunt qui de vita divinitus comparanda, ut Theologi Christiani. Sunt et qui de vita diabolice comparanda, ut Agrippa et alii pseudomagi. Sunt et qui de vita humanitus, ut Politici et Plutarchus et Cicero. Et horum studium omnium et singulorum quantum veritatis habeat in sequentibus manifestabitur sermonibus, et quomodo reversio ad Deum hominibus apprime conveniens sit.

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] they consist, but they are also ruled by the governance of Angels and souls. Souls too communicate with bodies, to which they are bound by the bonds of life. Angels, however, [communicate] by bonds of assistance and rule, as has often been said. Bodies are sustained by Mathematicals; mathematical space draws bodies to itself, desiring to be filled. Heat and cold communicate with all things while they strive to use the bodies they occupy.

From this it happens that some Beings live more by their own lives, others more by alien lives. From this we are compelled to admire the supreme Power, Wisdom, and Love of God, who could, knew how, and willed to bind Mathematicals to bodies, bodies to minds, minds to both, and all things to all things in such a way that they want to be so. Hence nothing is more marvelous than that the soul wants to live in the body and flees and abhors the death of the body just as if it itself were dying, although when it intuits itself in its operations, which are carried outside Worlds and fly across all bodies, not only because it knows them within itself through their notions, but also because it is referred to them outside itself through those notions and as it were goes out, so that someone says that the soul is more where it loves than where it animates, it incessantly experiences itself as narrowed, diminished, and imprisoned by body rather than helped, so that Trismegistus and others rightly call the body a living

death, a portable tomb, a prison, and a garment of depravity and ignorance. But when this happens, God willing it, we experience that mind has acquired bodily life for itself and is assigned to fabricating and governing the body. And while it does this and recognizes that it needs exterior corporeal things for this, it also acquires exterior life for itself, so that some live bestially, delighted by the habits of beasts, as Epicureans and the pleasure-loving; others humanly, who delight in politics and wisdom; others celestially, who investigate celestial things without ceasing; others angelically, but by adhering either to evil Angels, as magi and witches, Simon Magus, necromancers, or to good ones, as many hermits, Paul, Antony, Hilarion; others divinely, as Peter, Paul, Mary, Moses. Therefore it remains to speak in what follows about lives.

For there are those who have written of life acquired celestially, as Ficino. There are those who [have written] of life acquired divinely, as the Christian Theologians. There are also those who [have written] of life acquired diabolically, as Agrippa and other false magi. There are also those who [have written] of life humanly, as Political writers and Plutarch and Cicero. And how much truth the study of all and each of these has will be manifested in the following discourses, and how return to God is especially fitting for human beings.

## Note

This corrected file closes the Liber XIV sequence by classifying lives according to the relations a mind takes up: bestial, human, celestial, angelic, diabolic, and divine. The body-as-prison language remains, but the point is now comparative: the soul is more where it loves than where it animates.
