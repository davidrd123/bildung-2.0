## Latin

**Thomae Campanellae. Pars Tertia Metaphysicorum. Liber Decimus Sextus, in quo de Religione, et Virtute, et Lege, et Propheta Legislatore.**

**Humanam mentem agnoscere se extra propriam regionem esse evidentibus argumentis; ideoque similem deliranti, aegrotanti, et in tenebris degenti; proptereaque indigere auxiliis divinis, quibus ad patriam regredi possit, nec mala sua, et quae declinare vult non potest, ex natura materiae elementorum, sed ex aliqua culpa sui accidisse; ad quam delendam non nisi Deum Deorum colere. Caput I. Articulus I.**

Mens humana extra regionem propriam sese vivere ex eo novit, quoniam seipsam ignorare seipsam experitur, sicut qui est in tenebris seipsum non videns. Similiter quoniam cognoscit se non posse cognoscere cognoscibilia meliora; et quod non prout sunt cetera novit, sed prout apparent. Ergo ex hoc scit se esse in loco ubi non potest ex sui natura et appetitu scire ac operari, ergo extra patriam suam.

Similiter, quoniam quae bona et iusta vult exequi, non vult aut non potest aut nescit omnino; imo videt meliora probatque et deteriora sectatur. Cognoscere autem bonum et pulchrum et iustum est divinitatis in ea inditum; non facere autem prout novit est in regione non sua extare indicium. Nullus umquam, prout praedicat sanctissime vivendum, vivit; nec ulla Respublica et vivendi modus a Philosophis mirifice descriptus et a divis imperatus habuit executionem adhuc in nostro mundo taliter, quod fiat voluntas Dei in terra mundi nostri sicut fit in caelo mundi Angelici. Sumus ergo fragiles in potestativo, ita ut velimus quod scimus esse noxium, et hoc cognoscimus et verecundamur.

Verecundia autem est summae excellentiae humanae indicium, qua omne malum, etiam a fortuna illatum, verecundamur, nedum ex nostra culpa natum. Ita ut etiam se natos de vili progenie et patria, et ruptos gestare calceos, [stupere?][?], et stercus excernere, et manducare publice, magis autem coire, quoniam sunt signa nostrae mortalitatis et medicamina, homines verecundemur; et quasi indignatur Animus vivere in corpore mortali, quoniam hoc non sibi naturale nec a Deo. Ergo aliquam culpam totius generis nostri ex hoc cognoscere licet, quam luere et sciri erubescimus. Etenim ubi nulla est culpa, non verecundamur.

Quapropter non recte Platonici et Philosophorum sectae causas nostrarum calamitatum in materiam reiciunt. Ipsa enim de se non est mala, cum sit sedes et basis formarum pulchrarum et bonarum, omniumque operationum, aptissima ad omnia bona ideantis Dei, et numerantis, et concinnantis, suscipiendum. Nec putandum est Deum reiecisse Animas de mentali in corporalem mundum in poenam, de qua poena verecundentur, sicut scribunt Platonici et Origenes. Hoc enim saepe ostendimus esse falsum. Neque putandum est Deum materiae malae et non suae mentes bonas et suas alligasse. Ergo

## English

**Of Thomas Campanella. Third Part of the Metaphysics. Sixteenth Book, in which [he treats] of Religion, Virtue, Law, and the Prophet Legislator.**

**That the human mind recognizes by evident arguments that it is outside its proper region; and therefore like one delirious, sick, and dwelling in darkness; and for this reason needs divine helps by which it can return to its homeland, and that its evils, and those which it wants to avoid but cannot, have happened not from the nature of elemental matter, but from some fault of its own; to erase which it must worship none but the God of gods. Chapter I. Article I.**

The human mind knows from this that it lives outside its proper region: that it experiences itself as ignorant of itself, like one who is in darkness, not seeing himself. Likewise because it knows that it cannot know better knowables; and because it knows other things not as they are, but as they appear. Therefore from this it knows that it is in a place where it cannot know and operate from its own nature and appetite; therefore [it is] outside its homeland.

Likewise, because the good and just things that it wants to carry out, it does not will, or cannot, or does not know at all; indeed it sees and approves better things and follows worse. But to know the good and beautiful and just is something implanted in it from divinity; not to do as it knows is an indication that it exists in a region not its own. No one ever lives as he preaches that one must live most holily; nor has any Republic and mode of living, marvelously described by Philosophers and commanded by divine ones, yet had execution in our world in such a way that the will of God would be done on the earth of our world as it is done in the heaven of the Angelic world. Therefore we are fragile in the power-part, so that we will what we know to be harmful, and we recognize this and are ashamed.

But shame is an indication of the highest human excellence, by which we are ashamed of every evil, even one brought by fortune, not to mention one born from our own fault. So much so that we human beings are ashamed even to have been born from base stock and country, to wear torn shoes, to [stumble?][?], to excrete dung, to eat publicly, and more especially to have intercourse, because these are signs and remedies of our mortality; and the Mind as it were disdains living in a mortal body, because this is natural neither to itself nor from God. Therefore from this it is possible to recognize some fault of our whole race, which we blush to expiate and to have known. For where there is no fault, we are not ashamed.

For this reason the Platonists and sects of Philosophers do not rightly cast the causes of our calamities back onto matter. For matter is not evil of itself, since it is the seat and basis of beautiful and good forms and of all operations, most apt to receive all the goods of God ideating, numbering, and harmonizing. Nor should it be thought that God cast Souls down from the mental world into the corporeal world as punishment, of which punishment they are ashamed, as the Platonists and Origen write.
