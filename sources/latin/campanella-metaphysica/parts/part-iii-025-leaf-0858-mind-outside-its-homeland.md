## Latin

**Liber Decimus Sextus. In quo de religione, virtute, lege, propheta, legislatore.**

Humanam mentem agnoscere se extra propriam regionem esse evidentibus argumentis; ideoque similem deliranti, aegrotanti, et in tenebris degenti; propterea indigere auxiliis divinis quibus ad patriam regredi possit, nec mala sua quae declinare vult et non potest ex natura materiae elementorum, sed ex aliqua culpa sui accidisse; quam delendam non nisi Deum deorum colere.

Mens humana extra regionem propriam sese vivere ex eo novit, quoniam seipsam ignorare seipsam experitur, sicut qui est in tenebris seipsum non videns. Similiter quoniam cognoscit se non posse cognoscere cognoscibilia meliora, et quod non prout sunt cetera novit sed prout apparent; ergo ex hoc scit se esse in loco ubi non potest ex sui natura et appetitu scire ac operari, ergo extra patriam suam.

Similiter, quoniam quae bona et iusta vult exequi non vult aut non potest aut nescit omnino. Videt meliora probatque et deteriora sectatur. Cognoscere autem bonum, pulchrum, et iustum est divinitatis in ea inditum; non facere autem prout novit est indicium eam in regione non sua extare.

Nullus umquam prout praedicat sanctissime vivendum vivit, nec ulla respublica et vivendi modus a philosophis mirifice descriptus et a divinis imperatus habuit executionem adhuc in nostro mundo taliter, quod fiat voluntas Dei in terra mundi nostri sicut fit in caelo mundi angelici.

Sumus ergo fragiles in potestativo, ita ut velimus quod scimus esse noxium, et hoc cognoscimus et verecundamur. Verecundia est summae excellentiae humanae indicium, qua omne malum, etiam a fortuna illatum, verecundamur, nedum ex nostra culpa natum.

Verecundamur de vili progenie et patria, ruptos gestare calceos, stercus excernere, manducare publice, magis autem coire, quoniam sunt signa nostrae mortalitatis et medicamina. Quasi indignatur animus vivere in corpore mortali, quoniam hoc non sibi naturale nec a Deo. Ergo aliquam culpam totius generis nostri ex hoc cognoscere licet, quam luere et sciri erubescimus.

Non recte Platonici et philosophorum sectae causas nostrarum calamitatum in materiam reiciunt. Ipsa enim de se non est mala, cum sit sedes et basis formarum pulchrarum et bonarum, aptissima ad omnia bona ideantis Dei suscipienda. Nec putandum est Deum reiecisse animas de mentali in corporalem mundum in poenam, sicut scribunt Platonici et Origenes.

## English

Liber XVI begins on religion, virtue, law, prophet, and legislator. The human mind recognizes by evident arguments that it is outside its proper region, like someone delirious, sick, or living in darkness. It therefore needs divine helps by which it can return to its homeland. The evils it wants to avoid but cannot do not come from elemental matter's nature, but from some fault of its own, to be erased only by worship of the God of gods.

The mind knows it lives outside its proper region because it experiences itself as ignorant of itself, like someone in darkness who cannot see himself. It also knows it cannot know better knowables, and that it knows other things not as they are but as they appear. It is therefore in a place where it cannot know and act according to its own nature and appetite: outside its homeland.

It also fails morally. It sees and approves the better but follows the worse. Knowing the good, beautiful, and just is something divine implanted in it; failing to do what it knows shows that it is not in its own region. No one lives as he preaches one ought to live, and no philosophical republic or divine command has yet been carried out on earth as God's will is done in the angelic heaven.

Shame returns as evidence. We are ashamed of bad fortune, base ancestry, broken shoes, excretion, eating in public, and especially sex, because these are signs and remedies of mortality. The mind seems indignant to live in a mortal body. This points to some fault of the whole human race, not to matter as evil in itself and not to a Platonic-Origenist punishment of souls dropped from the mental world.

## Note

Liber XVI opens exactly where Liber XIV ended: body as prison or exile, but without blaming matter or preexistent soul-punishment. The new word is patria. Religion begins as return from the wrong region to the mind's homeland, under the pressure of self-ignorance, moral weakness, and shame.
