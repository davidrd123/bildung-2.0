## Opening Finding

Part II gives the doctrinal triad as `Potentia, Sapientia, & Amor`, not `voluntas`. That does not simply cancel the leaf-85 formula `Sapientia est primalitas sicut potestas & voluntas`; it clarifies the question. At the level of constitutive primalities, Campanella's open triad here is power, wisdom, and love. `Voluntas` now looks like it may belong to the outward-facing principle or faculty register, but that remains to be checked.

The slice also separates three levels: as they constitute being, the three are `primalitates`; as they look toward work, `principia`; as they go out toward causing, they produce causes. This is a useful guardrail against translating every occurrence of "principle" as if it named the same level.

## Corrected Latin

REASSUMITUR TRACTATIO,

Quod omnia vere entia essentiantur tribus Primalitatibus.

OMNIA ENTIA CONSTITUI AMORE, Sapientia, & Potestate, tanquam ex tribus principiis eminentialibus; & a Potestate Sapientiam, & ab utrisque Amorem processione intrinseca, & essentiali, manare.

CAP. XI. ART. I.

Quoniam iam declaravimus, ens nullum esse, aut operari, aut agere, nisi quia potest, & scit, & vult, esse, operari, & agere (posse enim pati ad impotentiam, & non esse spectat) manifestum esse debet, Potentiam, Sapientiam, Amorem primordia esse altissima constitutiva non modo rerum compositarum, sed & simplicissimarum. Quinimo in eis, & ex eis consistere omnium esse, ac potius primalitates & primordia dici debere: distinctionem quoque originis inter haec ipsa inveniri. Non enim amatur ignotum, neque impossibile. Amor ergo oritur a Sapientia, & Potestate; & quia non cognoscitur quod non potest cognosci, licet cognoscatur quod non amatur, idcirco Sapientiam a Potentia sola oriri affirmare licet.

Nec enim potest cognosci, quod non movet cognoscentem, quale est ipsum nihilum, & entia remotissima. Nec quod excedit potentiam cognoscentis in eo quod excedit: ut verum sit, cognitionem praesupponere potentiam; non autem amorem, quoniam Amor non fertur nisi in cognitum; sed cognitio fertur in non amatum. Igitur principium triplex est, Potentia, Sapientia, & Amor. Nam nec obstat Amori, & Sapientiae esse ab alio, quo minus sint principia: quoniam ipsa Potentia est tota in eis, & se totam communicat; & nec potest, nisi sciat, & velit, ut declarabimus.

Igitur Empedocles, & Hesiodus recte posuerunt Amorem principium rerum omnium: sed perperam adiunxit odium Empedocles. Odium enim non est nisi Amoris umbra, & spectat ad non esse, ut dicemus, & est ab Amore: ideo enim odimus aliqua, quia corrumpunt nostrum esse, aut disponunt ad corruptionem esse nostrum amatissimum bonum. Rectius Anaxagoras posuit intelligentiam principium rerum, non autem ignorantiam: quoniam non amamus, nec facimus, quae ignoramus, quamvis multa fiant ex ignorantia.

Itaque intellectus Anaxagorae Amorem Empedoclis praecedit, sed non recte sine amore ponitur: sed quia nec Amor, nec Sapientia sunt, aut operantur, nisi possint. Ergo Potestas erat praeponenda adhuc, ut sit trinitas principiorum summorum in unitate entis: & fabulosa philosophia hoc docuit, dum Venerem, Palladem, & Iunonem introducit Iovis sorores, & filias. Potestas enim est Iehovae, entis primi, coniux, & soror, & unum ergo cum eo. A Potestate emanat Sapientia filia; ab utrisque Amor seu voluptas procedit supereminentiali modo: & Ecclesiasticus id confirmat dicens, quod Rex omnipotens creavit Sapientiam, id est, quia omnipotens: & ait Sapientia ibidem: Ego mater pulchrae dilectionis, id est, Amoris.

Omnis praeterea actio idem probat. Propterea enim eo in plateam, quia volo: & propterea volo, quia scio, & possum ire: & propterea scio, quia possum, non quia volo. Et dum eo in plateam, si deesse contingit posse ire, subito annihilatur ipsum ire; & si deficit scire ire, statim cessat ire; & si deficit velle ire, similiter deficit ire. Quod autem in artibus est, est & in actionibus, & in ente: quod nimirum statim non est, ac essendi perdit amorem, aut sensum essendi, aut potentiam essendi. Dictum est etiam, quod ut essentiant ens, vocari debent primalitates; ut respiciunt ad opus, principia; & ut ad causandum exeunt, causas producunt.

## Working Translation

THE TREATMENT IS RESUMED,

That all true beings are essentiated by three Primalities.

ALL BEINGS ARE CONSTITUTED BY Love, Wisdom, and Power, as by three eminent principles; and Wisdom flows from Power, and Love from both, by an intrinsic and essential procession.

CAP. XI. ART. I.

Since we have already shown that no being exists, operates, or acts except because it can, knows, and wills to exist, operate, and act (for being able to suffer belongs to impotence and non-being), it ought to be clear that Power, Wisdom, and Love are the highest constitutive primordia not only of composite things but also of the simplest things. Indeed, the being of all things consists in them and from them, and they ought rather to be called primalities and primordia. A distinction of origin is also found among these very things. For the unknown is not loved, nor is the impossible. Love therefore arises from Wisdom and Power; and because what cannot be known is not known, although what is not loved can be known, it is permitted to affirm that Wisdom arises from Power alone.

For what does not move the knower cannot be known, such as nothing itself and the most remote beings. Nor can what exceeds the knower's power be known in the respect in which it exceeds it. So it is true that cognition presupposes power, but not love, since Love is directed only toward what is known, whereas cognition is directed toward what is not loved. Therefore the principle is threefold: Power, Wisdom, and Love. Nor does it prevent Love and Wisdom from being principles that they are from another, because Power itself is whole in them and communicates itself wholly; and it cannot have power unless it knows and wills, as we shall show.

Therefore Empedocles and Hesiod rightly posited Love as the principle of all things, but Empedocles wrongly added hatred. Hatred is nothing but the shadow of Love; it pertains to non-being, as we shall say, and it comes from Love. For we hate some things because they corrupt our being, or dispose our most beloved good, our being, toward corruption. Anaxagoras more rightly posited intelligence as the principle of things, not ignorance, because we do not love or make the things we do not know, although many things happen from ignorance.

And so Anaxagoras's intellect precedes Empedocles's Love, but it is not rightly posited without love; and neither Love nor Wisdom are or operate unless they can. Therefore Power still had to be placed before them, so that there would be a trinity of highest principles in the unity of being. Mythic philosophy taught this when it introduced Venus, Pallas, and Juno as Jove's sisters and daughters. For Power is the spouse and sister of Jehovah, the first being, and therefore one with him. From Power emanates Wisdom as daughter; from both proceeds Love, or delight, in a supereminent mode. Ecclesiasticus confirms this when it says that the omnipotent King created Wisdom, that is, because he is omnipotent; and Wisdom says in the same place, "I am the mother of beautiful love," that is, of Love.

Moreover every action proves the same thing. I go into the square because I will to; and I will to because I know and can go; and I know because I can, not because I will. And while I go into the square, if the ability to go happens to be lacking, the very going is at once annihilated; if knowing how to go fails, the going immediately ceases; and if willing to go fails, the going likewise fails. What is in arts is also in actions and in being: a thing is at once not, once it loses love of being, or sense of being, or power of being. It has also been said that, insofar as they essentialize being, they ought to be called primalities; insofar as they look toward work, principles; and insofar as they go out toward causing, they produce causes.

## Notes

- The doctrinal triad here is `Potentia, Sapientia, & Amor`. This gives an internal check on leaf 85's `Sapientia est primalitas sicut potestas & voluntas`: `voluntas` is not yet wrong, but it is now marked as a local or register-specific variant unless further Part II evidence says otherwise.
- `primalitates`, `principia`, and `causae` are not interchangeable. Campanella distinguishes them by level: constituting being, looking toward work, and going out toward causation.
- `Amor non fertur nisi in cognitum` is corrected from ABBYY's apparent `incognitum`; the surrounding argument requires "toward what is known" because Campanella has just said `Non enim amatur ignotum`.
- The passage rejects hatred as a coordinate primality. Hatred is an `Amoris umbra` and belongs on the side of non-being.
- `Ego mater pulchrae dilectionis` is Ecclesiasticus/Sirach 24:18 in the older Vulgate enumeration (24:24 in the Nova Vulgata). Campanella uses the Wisdom verse to identify Wisdom as mother of Love.
- Love is not blind in itself. Only accidental love can be blind when it attaches to a small mixed good and misses the hidden evils around it; the immediately preceding upper-leaf material sets up that point.

## Translation Risk

Textual: the slice is corrected from ABBYY output and contextual repair, not yet from full page-image collation. The main repairs to revisit against the image are `in cognitum`, `se totam`, `velit`, and `disponunt`.

Lexical: `processione intrinseca, & essentiali` is rendered as "intrinsic and essential procession" without yet deciding how heavily the Trinitarian register should govern the metaphysical reading.

Contextual: `Iehovae, entis primi, coniux, & soror` is syntactically and doctrinally delicate. The current translation takes `Iehovae` as genitive, "of Jehovah," with `Potestas` as the implicit subject; a dative relation, "to Jehovah," remains a case-grammar possibility to check against the page image and surrounding doctrine. The following `& unum ergo cum eo` supports the current reading, but the relation carries doctrinal weight.
