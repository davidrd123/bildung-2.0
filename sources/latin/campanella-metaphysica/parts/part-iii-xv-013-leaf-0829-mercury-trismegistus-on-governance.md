Mercurii Trismegisti de Monotriade summa de processione praeexistentium in Verbo: de Diis, daemonibus, heroibus et animabus, distributis ad gubernatum sensibilis Mundi; de Fato, et eius supergressu per animas ad Deum conversas; de datore formarum, sigillis et ideis, unde manant; de seminatione Mentium in Mundum corporalem. De Deo primo Ente; de Deo secundo, qui est Mundus; de Deo tertio, qui est homo; deque Religione regeneratione hominis; de Fabrica statuarum, et factorum Deorum virtute hominis; de illecebris Deorum, et gubernatu.

**CAP. III.**

**ART. I.**

Mercurius, quem vocant Trismegistum, cum Deum omnipotentem rerum omnium autorem celebrasset, ac Hylem, mundum sensibilem, et hominem natos: qui tamen non nati, erant in verbo Dei vita et Deus. Deumque cum verbo suo, per quod fecit omnia, genuisse tertiam mentem, quae Deus est, et spiritusque Numen, ut ipse inquit.

Cum ad rerum conservationem accedit declarandam, scripsit rem nullam vere mori, quoniam non adnihilatur, sed solum mutari, et partes rerum dissolvi: ipsumque compositum corrumpi: caeterum partes semper vivere, et manifestari et occultari sub diversis vitarum speciebus: eo quod extra Mundum, Dei filium, ire non possunt, nec extra Deum, qui dat vitam Mundo.

Ubi autem ad gubernatum rerum devenit, Deos, Daemonas, et Heroas, et animas humanas, tanquam pertinentes ad Mundum intelligibilem, quo putat circumdari, et quasi vestiri mundum sensibilem, [sic?][?] gubernari. Et sicuti sensibilis mundi membra sunt caelum, aer, aqua, tellus: ita Dei membra sunt, vita, immortalitas, necessitas, providentia, natura, anima, mens.

Mentem autem de essentia Dei, cui unita est, ut lumen Soli, participari ab hominibus, et vocari animam; brutis itidem, et vocari in eis Naturam, passionibus voluptatis, et doloris ita obnoxiam, ut resistere nequeat, quemadmodum et in quibusdam hominibus, quibus lex ut frenum posita est, et poena post mortem. Alii enim homines mentem sequentes vincunt Fatum, id est, causarum seriem, et fiunt similes Diis, nec patiuntur id, quod instituit Fatum.

Alibi ponit Fatum, Necessitatem, et ordinem a Deo tanquam Parcas, Clothon, Lachesim, Atropon. Sed Fatum quod vocant [Heimarmenen?][?] praeponit, quasi sit praedestinatio divina: Necessitas essendi subsequatur: ordo, quo servantur, accedat.

Ponit insuper Deum unum, caelestibus praesidentem; alterum aeri, alterum aquis, alterum telluri: quibus pedissequos daemones, et Heroas adiungit ad gubernandum: et in circulo Planetarum Deum Pantophormium, a quo omnes rerum species et formas sigillari in Mundo nostro opinatur iuxta 36 horoscopios, quos ipse curat. Puto autem 36 facies vocatas Egyptiis in Zodiaco esse horoscopios apud Mercurium: ex quibus facies inferiorum deducit, quemadmodum Mundus, et qui praesidet Mundo supra Caelum Deus, ab ideis aeternis eas in Zodiaco affixit.

Singulis autem momentis temporis variari horoscopium, palam est, quae momenta possunt intelligi, et minuta 24 horarum, quibus circulus omniformis horizontem scandit et deserit; vel minutorum secunda, vel secundorum tertia. Proptereaque ait non modo diversae sunt facies specierum, sed individuorum etiam eiusdem speciei, ita ut nusquam videas duos homines prorsus similes in facie, ergo neque in caeteris rebus. Potest et videtur ipse velle, fixarum esse cum Zodiaco concursus facere tanquam diversitatem: siquidem in fixis etiam horoscopios ponit. Sed Pantophormium in Planetarum sphaera, quasi qui per Planetas generationem formarum in inferioribus, ac transmutationem, curet.

Creatum esse Mundum sensibilem, non Natura, sed voluntate Dei summi boni, docet; et quidquid a bono est, esse bonum, et Deum participio. Deum esse omne Ens, omneque nomen per essentiam. Praecipue tamen, Deus, Bonum, et Pater. Quaerenti autem, cur hominem mentis compotem intra Mundum corporalem creavit, et non potius intra intelligibilem posuit.

Respondet in Ascl. 4. quod Deus creavit sensibilem mundum bonum, omnibus gradibus bonitatis plenissimum: idcircoque amavit. Proptereaque voluit esse etiam, qui mundum a se [factum?][?] miraretur, et celebraret diligenter. Cumque non posset mens esse diligens rerum corporearum mundanarum, tegumento corporeo mundano texit eam tanquam domo.

Igitur ex immortali, mortalique Natura, ut utrique origini satisfacere posset. Videlicet intueri aeterna, ac divina colere, et orare; ac simul terrestria, et corporea gubernare, et incolere, per artes Mechanicas, et Morales, et speculativas, per quas omnium rerum, et notitiam, et usum habet homo: sine quibus artibus non videbatur mundus esse effectus.

## English

and formations, there must also be many Rulers there, just as among us, and every corporeal creature must be governed by a spiritual one, until all things are converted to the mental World without resistance; and, according to their measure and capacity, bodies will therefore be consummated into purifying and vivifying fire, as into the optimum of others. But that minds will return into the best of minds, all the way to the Archetypal World from which they go out toward the corporeal, and be preserved, is a rational doctrine.

The summary of Mercury Trismegistus concerning the supreme Monotriad, concerning the procession of things preexisting in the Word; concerning Gods, demons, heroes, and souls distributed for the governance of the sensible World; concerning Fate and its being overstepped by souls converted to God; concerning the giver of forms, seals, and ideas, whence they flow; concerning the sowing of Minds into the corporeal World. Concerning God the first Being; concerning the second God, who is the World; concerning the third God, who is the human being; and concerning Religion, the regeneration of the human being; concerning the Fabrication of statues, and the virtue of made Gods by human being; concerning the enticements of Gods and governance.

**CHAPTER III.**

**ARTICLE I.**

Mercury, whom they call Trismegistus, after he had celebrated God the omnipotent author of all things, and Hyle, the sensible world, and the human being as born, which nevertheless, when they were not born, were in the Word of God life and God; and [after he had said] that God with his Word, through which he made all things, begot a third mind, which is God and a spiritual Divinity, as he himself says.

When he comes to declaring the conservation of things, he wrote that no thing truly dies, because it is not annihilated, but only changed, and the parts of things are dissolved, and the composite itself is corrupted; but otherwise the parts always live, and are manifested and hidden under diverse species of lives, because they cannot go outside the World, the son of God, nor outside God, who gives life to the World.

But where he comes to the governance of things, Gods, Demons, Heroes, and human souls, as pertaining to the intelligible World, by which he thinks the sensible world is surrounded and as if clothed, are [thus?][?] governed. And just as the members of the sensible world are heaven, air, water, earth, so the members of God are life, immortality, necessity, providence, nature, soul, mind.

But mind from the essence of God, to which it is united as light to the Sun, is participated by human beings and called soul; likewise [it is participated] by beasts and called Nature in them, so subject to passions of pleasure and pain that it cannot resist, as also in certain human beings, for whom law has been set as a bridle and punishment after death. For other human beings, following mind, conquer Fate, that is, the series of causes, and become similar to Gods, and do not suffer what Fate establishes.

Elsewhere he posits Fate, Necessity, and order from God as the Fates, Clotho, Lachesis, Atropos. But he places before them Fate, which they call [Heimarmene?][?], as if it were divine predestination; Necessity of being follows; order, by which they are preserved, comes next.

He posits besides one God presiding over celestial things, another over air, another over waters, another over earth, to which he joins attendant demons and Heroes for governing; and in the circle of the Planets, the God Pantophormius, from whom he thinks all species and forms of things are sealed in our World according to 36 horoscopes, which he himself cares for. But I think the 36 faces called by the Egyptians in the Zodiac are horoscopes in Mercury, from which he derives the faces of inferior things, just as the World, and God above Heaven who presides over the World, fixed them in the Zodiac from the eternal ideas.

But it is clear that the horoscope is varied in single moments of time, and these moments can be understood as the minutes of the 24 hours by which the all-form circle climbs and leaves the horizon, or the seconds of minutes, or thirds of seconds. For this reason he says not only that the faces of species are diverse, but also of individuals of the same species, so that nowhere do you see two human beings wholly similar in face, and therefore neither in other things. He can and seems to mean that the concurrences of the fixed [stars] with the Zodiac make diversity, since he also places horoscopes in the fixed [stars]. But Pantophormius is in the sphere of the Planets, as one who, through the Planets, cares for the generation and transmutation of forms in inferior things.

He teaches that the sensible World was created not by Nature, but by the will of God, the highest good; and that whatever is from the good is good, and God by participation. God is every Being and every name by essence; yet chiefly God, Good, and Father. But to one asking why he created the human being, possessing mind, within the corporeal World, and did not rather place him within the intelligible [World],

he answers in Asclepius 4 that God created the sensible world good, most full of all grades of goodness, and for that reason loved it. For that reason he also wished there to be one who would diligently admire and celebrate the world [made by himself?][?]. And since mind could not be attentive to corporeal worldly things, he clothed it with a worldly corporeal covering as with a house.

Therefore [the human being is made] from immortal and mortal Nature, so that he could satisfy each origin: namely, intuit eternal things, cultivate and pray to divine things; and at the same time govern and inhabit terrestrial and corporeal things through Mechanical, Moral, and speculative arts, through which the human being has both knowledge and use of all things. Without these arts the world did not seem to have been completed.
