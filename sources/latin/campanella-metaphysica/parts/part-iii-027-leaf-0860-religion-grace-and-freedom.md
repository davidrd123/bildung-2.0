## Latin

Accepimus leges Dei per viros sanctissimos qui probarunt sese a Deo missos. Adhuc agnovimus nec leges exteriores prodesse nisi ad docendum, non ad exequendum, nisi sibi unusquisque esset lex. Igitur virtutes colere oportuit, quae sunt regulae tam affectionum, passionum, notionum nostrarum, quam operationum actionumque.

Cum de virtutibus et vitiis, de recto et aequo satis superque in genere humano disputatum esset et alii aliter saperent, intelleximus nos indigere doctore Deo, a quo per prophetas a se illuminatos et angelorum magisterio perfectos moralia cuncta cognovimus.

Cum mox oblivio et affectio sensibilium bonorum nos caperet et a monstrata via nos flecteret, quisque aliis bene praedicaret, ipse autem male operaretur, indiguimus notitia altiori. Haec autem divinitus tradita, e cordibus emergens nostris religio, nos divinitus docens ac divino cultui mancipans, ut gustaremus Deo adhaerentes omnes veritates, nec cultu rerum aliarum affecti affectiones novas nos alienantes a Deo indueremus.

Ostensa religione, quae natura quae divina ars est in mentibus nostris latebat, mox parentum disciplina et rerum pulchritudine testante excitati, per extasim ad superiora rapti, et mundi archetypi bona gustantes plurimi homines nobis annuntiaverunt ea. Quemadmodum sciebamus ex malis huius mundi nos esse extra patriam, nunc intelligeremus quae bona nos manent in mentali patria.

Cum indigeret corpus cibo, potu, et vestimento, et homo suam originem admiratus ambitiose viveret velletque quasi Deus etiam ab hominibus coli, affectiones induit novas. Qui optimi erant inter homines agnoverunt sese auxilio longe celsiori egere, ut possint agere quae sciunt agi oportere. Hoc auxilium est nova participatio deitatis, vocata a theologis gratia gratum faciens, quae potestativum roborat, cognoscitivum illustrat, et volitivum sanctificat in suo fine.

Theologi Christi docuere parcum et defectuosum esse omnem modum revertendi ad Deum, nisi ipse Deus nobis non potentibus ad ipsum ascendere condescenderet factus homo, ut homo deificaretur tali copula et elevaretur in archetypum ex corporeo perfectissime. Sacrificia albae magiae et symbola inferiora nihil valent ad erigendum hominem ad Deum, quando neque scientiae neque virtutes neque leges id facere potuerunt nisi dispositive absque speciali gratia Dei.

Avicenna probat esse necessarios prophetas: si Deus in naturalibus minutioribus non deest, multo magis providet toti reipublicae hominum indigentium doctoribus divinis prophetis, quorum est audire verbum Dei per angelos, videre eos transfiguratos, dogmata et promissa Dei miraculis comprobare, et sibi successorem parare summum sacerdotem ut vicarium Dei.

**Articulus III.** Quaeritur quomodo homo, tot legibus divinis, humanis, naturalibus, publicis et privatis subiectus, possit dici liber. Anima semper seipsam movet et vult; specificatur tamen eius motio et volitio a causis naturalibus dispositive, ab intellectualibus etiam suggestive. Hoc verum est quoad specificationem actus, non quoad exercitium.

## English

Even divine laws received through holy men are not enough if they remain external. They teach, but they do not execute, unless each person becomes law to himself. Therefore virtues are needed as rules of affections, passions, notions, operations, and actions. But disputes about virtues and vices showed the need for God as teacher, instructing through prophets illuminated by God and perfected by angelic teaching.

Forgetfulness and attachment to sensible goods bend humans away from the shown path. People preach well to others but act badly themselves, mistaking themselves for deceivers rather than the deceived. A higher knowledge is needed: religion divinely handed down and emerging from our hearts, teaching us divinely and binding us to divine cult so that, adhering to God, we might taste all truths and not be alienated by cults of lesser things.

Religion had lain hidden in our minds as nature and divine art. Awakened by parents' discipline and the beauty of things, many humans were carried by ecstasy upward and tasted the goods of the archetypal world. From the evils of this world we knew we were outside our homeland; now we learn what goods await in the mental homeland.

Even the best recognize that they need higher aid to do what they know ought to be done. This aid is a new participation in deity, the theologians' grace making one pleasing: it strengthens the power-part, illuminates the knowing-part, and sanctifies the willing-part in its end. Christianity teaches that every return to God is defective unless God condescends to us as human, so that the human may be deified by that union and lifted from the corporeal into the archetypal.

Lower symbols, sacrifices, magic, sciences, virtues, and laws can only dispose; they cannot raise humans to God without special grace. Prophets are necessary because divine providence would not fail the whole human republic in what it needs most. Article III opens the freedom question: though humans are subject to many laws and moved by objects, heavens, and intelligences, these specify acts; the soul moves and wills itself in the exercise of action.

## Note

The sequence from law to virtue to prophet to religion to grace is explicit. Religion is not merely external cult: it is a divine art hidden in minds, emerging from hearts, and giving a taste of all truths through adhesion to God. The new freedom problem is carefully framed: external causes specify what the act becomes, but do not replace the soul's own exercise of willing.
