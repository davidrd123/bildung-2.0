## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] probarunt sese a Deo missos. Adhuc agnovimus nec leges prodesse exteriores nisi ad docendum, non ad exequendum, nisi sibi unusquisque esset lex. Igitur virtutes colere oportuit, quae sunt regulae tam affectionum et passionum et notionum nostrarum quam operationum actionumque.

Cumque de virtutibus et vitiis, et de recto et aequo, satis superque in genere humano disputatum esset, et alii aliter saperent, intelleximus nos indigere doctore Deo, a quo per Prophetas a se illuminatos et Angelorum magisterio perfectos moralia cuncta cognovimus.

Cumque mox oblivio et affectio sensibilium bonorum nos caperet, et a monstrata via nos flecteret, et quisque aliis bene praedicaret, ipse autem male operaretur, putaretque se deceptorem aliorum, non potius deceptum, sicuti erat secundum veritatem, et fraudes alter contra alterum strueret, indiguimus notitia altiori, maxime cum putaremus bonis huius Mundi, non sufficientibus nostro immenso desiderio, nonnulla alia esse maiora et veriora et sufficientissima, ac deceptos esse eos qui aliter praedicant. Haec autem divinitus tradita, e cordibus emergens nostris religio, nos divinitus docens ac divino cultui mancipans, ut gustaremus, Deo adhaerentes, omnes veritates, nec cultu rerum aliarum affecti affectiones novas nos alienantes a Deo indueremus.

Ostensa autem religione, quae Natura, quae divina Ars est, in mentibus nostris latebat; nam et cum fidelibus, inquit Ecclesiasticus[?], in vulva concreatus est timor Domini, id est Religio; mox parentum disciplina et rerum pulchritudine testante exciti, per extasim ad superiora rapti, et Mundi Archetypi bona gustantes, plurimi homines nobis annuntiaverunt ea; ut quemadmodum sciebamus ex malis huius Mundi nos esse extra patriam, nunc intelligeremus quae bona nos manent in mentali Patria. Nec sufficit annuntiatio Angelorum, nisi et homo per raptum ipsa cognoscat.

Sed cum indigeret corpus cibo et potu et vestimento, et homo suam originem admiratus ambitiose viveret velletque quasi Deus etiam ab hominibus coli, affectiones induit novas, et cum harum puderet, per hypocrisim eas occultavit. Et qui optimi erant inter homines agnoverunt sese auxilio longe celsiori egere, ut possint agere quae sciunt agi oportere. Hoc auxilium est nova participatio deitatis. Omne enim datum optimum est a Patre luminum, vocatum a Theologis gratia gratum faciens, quae potestativum roborat, cognoscitivum illustrat, et volitivum sanctificat in suo fine. Propterea prima virtus est sanctitas, sancions[?] de vero fine confirmansque in illo, ne non veros et medios pro vero ultimo captaremus.

Propterea Theologi Christi seri[?] parcum et defectuosum esse omnem modum revertendi ad Deum docuere, nisi ipse Deus, nobis non potentibus ad ipsum ascendere, condescenderet factus homo, ut homo deificaretur tali copula et elevaretur in Archetypum ex corporeo perfectissime. Igitur sacrificia Albae Magiae et symbola inferiora exhibita ad captandum influxum superiorum nihil valent ad erigendum hominem ad Deum, quando neque scientiae neque virtutes neque leges id facere potuerunt nisi dispositive aliqualiter absque speciali gratia Dei.

Ut autem evidenter ac vere credibile sit Deum fieri hominem, ut legislatorem per sapientiam et redemptorem per potentiam et glorificatorem per amorem, sequens libellus monstrabit, satis in Theologia demonstratum. Probe quidem probatum est in superioribus quod, si Deus non deficit in necessariis, sui nobis notitiam et auxilium dat, et Prophetas qui doceant vias suas. Unde Avicenna in tractatu 10 suae Metaphysicae, capite 2, probat esse necessarios Prophetas, quoniam si Deus in naturalibus minutioribus non deest, ut cum palpebris dat pilos, et plantae manus cavitatem et digitos, aliasque utilitates, multo magis providet toti Reipublicae hominum indigentium doctoribus divinis Prophetis, ut probatum est; quorum est audire, ait, Verbum Dei per Angelos, et videre eos transfiguratos, ad Animastica[?], ex Animasticis ad intelligentias elevare, ut ait in primo capite ibidem; et miraculis comprobare dogmata et promissa Dei; et sibi successorem parare summum Sacerdotem ut Vicarium Dei pro se, sicut in ultimo capite concludit.

**Hominem quamquam pluribus legibus, custodibus, et corporibus subditum, nihilominus esse liberum mente a coactione, non a miseria; et potius liberum in volendo quam in exequendo; Deum ac Angelos non [iuvare per se?][?], sed daemones et mala inferiora iuvare per accidens; et culpas et poenas esse praeter fatum legis aeternae, nec satis solvi nisi per Religionem; per hanc autem vere liberari. Articulus III.**

Quaeret autem quis qua ratione, cum homo tot legibus divinis, humanis, naturalibus et publicis, quae propriae leges vocantur, et privatis, quae dicuntur virtutes, subiaceat et tot indigeat exterioribus auxiliis, possit dici secundum veritatem liber. Porphyrius anxius in his excipitur a Iamblico, docente quod, etsi homo quoad animam sentientem, quam a caelestibus habet revolutionibus, fato sit alligatus ut servus nec possit aliter ac solet operari, movetur enim ad agendum ex appetitu; appetitus autem ex cognitione; cognitio ex passione; prout enim bene vel male afficimur, sapimus et amamus atque odimus.

Hae autem affectiones sunt ab obiectis, a calore frigoreque, et a Caelo, et motu, et luce, et aspectu. Similiter ab intelligentiis immutantibus vel transponentibus phantasmata. Unde Avicenna probat quod nulla volitio nova est in nobis ab alia volitione; sic enim itur in infinitum; nec a natura volitiva, sic enim semper inesset. Ergo, ait, ab extrinsecis obiectis et motoribus naturalibus vel intellectualibus. Quod verum est quoad specificationem actus, non quoad exercitium. Anima enim semper seipsam movet et vult. Specificatur tamen eius motio et volitio a causis naturalibus dispositive, ab intellectualibus etiam suggestive. Adeo tandem

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] proved that they had been sent by God. We further recognized that exterior laws are of no use except for teaching, not for executing, unless each person were a law to himself. Therefore it was necessary to cultivate virtues, which are rules as much of our affections, passions, and notions as of our operations and actions.

And since there had been more than enough disputation in the human race concerning virtues and vices, and concerning the right and equitable, and different people knew/tasted differently, we understood that we needed God as teacher, from whom, through Prophets illuminated by him and perfected by the teaching of Angels, we knew all moral things.

And when soon forgetfulness and affection for sensible goods seized us, and bent us from the path shown, and each person preached well to others but himself acted badly, and thought himself a deceiver of others rather than the one deceived, as he was according to truth, and one person constructed frauds against another, we needed a higher knowledge, especially since we thought, because the goods of this World were not sufficient for our immense desire, that there were some other goods, greater and truer and most sufficient, and that those who preach otherwise are deceived. But this religion, handed down divinely and emerging from our hearts, teaching us divinely and binding us to divine worship, [was given] so that, adhering to God, we would taste all truths, and, affected by worship of other things, would not put on new affections alienating us from God.

But once religion had been shown, which is Nature, which is divine Art, and had lain hidden in our minds; for with the faithful too, Ecclesiasticus[?] says, the fear of the Lord, that is Religion, was created together in the womb; soon, stirred by the discipline of parents and the witnessing beauty of things, carried by ecstasy to higher things, and tasting the goods of the Archetypal World, very many human beings announced them to us, so that just as we knew from the evils of this World that we are outside our homeland, now we would understand what goods await us in the mental Homeland. Nor is the announcement of Angels sufficient, unless the human being too knows those things themselves through rapture.

But since the body needed food, drink, and clothing, and the human being, admiring his own origin, lived ambitiously and wished to be worshiped even by human beings as if he were God, he put on new affections; and since he was ashamed of these, he hid them by hypocrisy. And those who were best among human beings recognized that they needed far higher aid so that they might be able to do what they know ought to be done. This aid is a new participation of deity. For every best gift is from the Father of lights, called by Theologians grace making one pleasing, which strengthens the power-part, illumines the cognitive part, and sanctifies the volitive part in its end. Therefore the first virtue is holiness, sanctioning[?] concerning the true end and confirming [us] in it, lest we seize upon ends that are not true and intermediate ends as the true ultimate.

For this reason serious[?] Theologians of Christ taught that every mode of returning to God is meager and defective unless God himself, since we are unable to ascend to him, condescended by being made human, so that the human being would be deified by such a union and raised most perfectly from the corporeal into the Archetype. Therefore sacrifices of White Magic and inferior symbols displayed for capturing the influx of higher things have no value for raising the human being to God, since neither sciences nor virtues nor laws could do this except somehow dispositively, without the special grace of God.

But that it may be evident and truly credible that God becomes human, as legislator through wisdom, redeemer through power, and glorifier through love, the following little book will show, sufficiently demonstrated in Theology. It was indeed well proved above that, if God does not fail in necessities, he gives us knowledge of himself and help, and Prophets who teach his ways. Hence Avicenna, in tractate 10 of his Metaphysics, chapter 2, proves that Prophets are necessary, since if God is not lacking in smaller natural things, as when he gives hairs to eyelids, and the hollow of the hand and fingers to the plant[?], and other utilities, much more does he provide for the whole Republic of human beings needing divine teachers, Prophets, as was proved; whose office, he says, is to hear the Word of God through Angels, and to see them transfigured, to raise [things] to Animastics[?], from Animastics to intelligences, as he says in the first chapter there; and to confirm by miracles the dogmas and promises of God; and to prepare for himself a successor, the highest Priest as Vicar of God in his place, as he concludes in the last chapter.

**That the human being, although subject to many laws, guardians, and bodies, nevertheless is free in mind from coercion, not from misery; and is free rather in willing than in executing; that God and Angels do not [help through themselves?][?], but demons and inferior evils help accidentally; and that faults and punishments are outside the fate of eternal law and are not sufficiently solved except through Religion; but through it are truly freed. Article III.**

But someone will ask by what reason, since the human being lies under so many divine, human, natural, and public laws, which are called proper laws, and private laws, which are called virtues, and needs so many exterior helps, he can be called free according to truth. Porphyry, anxious in these matters, is met by Iamblichus, who teaches that even if the human being, with respect to the sensing soul which he has from celestial revolutions, is bound to fate as a slave and cannot operate otherwise than he is accustomed, for he is moved to acting from appetite; appetite, however, from cognition; cognition from passion; for according as we are well or badly affected, we know/taste and love and hate.

But these affections are from objects, from heat and cold, and from Heaven, and motion, and light, and aspect. Likewise from intelligences changing or transposing phantasms. Hence Avicenna proves that no new volition is in us from another volition; for in this way one goes to infinity; nor [is it] from volitive nature, for in that case it would always be present. Therefore, he says, [it is] from extrinsic objects and natural or intellectual movers. This is true with respect to the specification of the act, not with respect to exercise. For the soul always moves itself and wills. Nevertheless its motion and volition are specified by natural causes dispositively, and by intellectual causes also suggestively.
