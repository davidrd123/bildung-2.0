## Latin

Haec[?], quoniam divinus est. In brutis autem sunt mirabilia, quia bruta sunt. Item bruta serviunt homini, non autem homo brutis. Et licet homo serviat homini, non autem sicut vi et studio actus alieno, sed propria voluntate, quam violare[?] nullus potest. Videmus enim Machabaeos mortem alacriter suscipere potius quam Antiocho regi obediant, et Scaevolam ignem non timere neque regem Porsennam pro patriae libertate. [Iehovae?][?] Ergo servit homo, acceptoque praecepto utitur ex se.

At equus indiget fraeno et calcaribus, bos stimulo, simius flagello; et dolore et voluptate rapiuntur et assuescunt. Homo vero spernit dolores et blanditias, et ratione utitur superiori, qua nec infans nec canis suaderi possunt ut ieiunent et ut patiantur disciplinam[?]. Nec quia canis de lata[?] in domum carnem portat et litteras, erit itidem rationalis. Hoc enim sensu affecto per usum facit, et non nisi unum. At homo hoc pro nihilo habet, et ad mille amicos praecepta deferre, et munuscula, et cuique sua distribuere. Vadit homo etiam quo numquam perductus est, sola significatione itineris praemunitus. At canis non nisi quo saepius perductus est ab alio ad illud opus; et propter id ridiculum est comparare illum homini.

At quis umquam homo, vel stupidus, fuit servus leonis aut elephantis, animalium fortissimi et sapientissimi? Nec quidem homo huic se subderet, ut hic nobis; neque hic imperare sciret, quoniam parere natus est. Omnia tamen animalia homini serviunt, nisi quae nimia ignorantia reddit inepta servitio. Non enim obedit musca tenuiusculo spiritu donata et memoriae expers. Oportet enim disciplinabilia esse quae homini serviunt. Ferocia vero aut indisciplinabilia sunt et parum symboli nobiscum habent, aut quia nos timemus a ferocia illorum irrationali, nobis non mancipantur.

Praeterea telas aranearum, et cellas apum, et rempublicam, et regna, et militarem, nos habemus meliora quam bruta, et alia insuper longe excellentiora, ut dictum est. Religionem autem inesse brutis proprie negandum videtur. Haec enim est cultus veri Dei cum despectione praesentis vitae propter aeternam a Deo adipiscendam. Scimus autem bruta praesentibus modo inhiare bonis, et nihil de Deo et aeternitate inquirere, et quam habent religionis speciem ad praesentem vitam dirigere. Elephas namque lunam adorat, quoniam in nocturnis itineribus ab ea beneficium accipit, ut videat quo vadat. Baptizatur a coitu ad sordes corporis abstergendas, non autem animae.

Solis et lunae herbae sectantur motum, quoniam moventur illorum viribus et quadam symbolica[?] suavitate, sicut pulegium floret in solstitiis, et magnes spectat polum, et sub polis paululum divertit. Quae omnia fiunt a consensu et dissensu rerum, ut dictum est in secunda parte. Nec enim quia replent vacuum fluxilia corpora, religione moventur. Nec aquila et phoenix religiose ad solem aspiciunt, sed huius vigore delectatae. Et quamvis multae nationes colant animalia, non ob id sunt hominibus nobiliora, quoniam cultus eis exhibetur ab homine decepto et putante in eis latere aliquod numen. Ipsa vero non imperant homini, nec leges ferunt, nec [gaudent gradu?] regum[?], sed significant aliqua garritu et volatu et gestibus, instinctu sensuali, non intendendo oracula dare, sed proprias passiones sectando; suntque ut signa, non ut in scholis doctores. Pecus et bos multum comedentes indicant pluviam, quoniam sentiunt in aere illam parari a vaporibus et frigore. Idcirco cupiunt plenis ventribus reperiri[?].

Et quidem cum passionibus corporis operam attentiorem dent bruta quam nos, qui negotiorum plurimorum mole et excellentiorum occupati, haec vix sentimus, sicut qui attente audit non videt, et qui iratus est blanditiis non afficitur; idcirco melius agnoscunt passiones aeris easque praedicunt, et corvi crocitant sese admonentes mutuo, uti et nos quando quid exspectamus mali vel boni ingentis. Qui hernia laborant aut cicatricibus prius praevident tempestatem et frigus quam ceteri, quoniam facilius ab aere patiuntur.

In somnis quoque plura nobis ab aere et caelo significantur quam in vigilia, quoniam spiritus non vacat negotiis externis; quasi feriatus[?] facile a passionibus caeli per inspiratum aerem subeuntibus afficitur. Et non modo aer defert passiones, sed etiam sensationes. Siquidem et ipse sentit et communicat aereo spiritui propriam imaginationem, quam ex belli apparatu et negotiatione apprehendit, eamque communicat quiescenti, non autem agitato spiritui. Motus enim debiles non nisi in sedato maioribus motibus, vacuo spiritu et aere, imprimuntur. Unde diximus melancholicos ob tenuitatem praesagire; amantes vero ob inditam passionem facile a simili excitabilem, ut cicatrix.

Quapropter prophetia animalium est ab aere in spiritu aereo affecto ab initiis et causis futurorum. Hominis vero non solum ab aere et caelo, sed ab Angelis et Deo. Nec modo de pluviis, tempestatibus, et bello per Astrologiam et affectionem sagacem prophetat, sed de regnorum interitu, nominibus futurorum hominum, gestis, Deo, Paradiso, inferis, sermonem propalat et arcana. Nec passione modo admonitus ut bruta, neque arte sola dignoscendi signa, qua carent bruta, sed alloquio Dei, quod excedit hominem et bruta. Nec dicas hoc esse per accidens per instinctum, sicut bruta medicamen inveniunt, et assuescunt [psallere funere?][?]. Hoc enim[?] hominibus cunctis Deo charis et piis id evenit. Et hoc miraculis et prophetia et sanguine probant.

Cum vero dicebas: Quis scit an et in brutis ista sint? Respondeo, non reperiri, quoniam tractarent nobiscum de rebus ad aliam vitam spectantibus, sicut nos cum Angelis, et in templo orarent, et facerent quae ad religionem spectant. Item haberent instrumenta, quibus ista agerent. Sed carent manibus, studio, libris, et lege. Sed ais: Quis scit an utantur lege per traditiones, et sic religione et aliis quae sunt apud nos instrumentis? Nonne Lycurgus leges tulit Spartanis, noluitque eas scriptura, sed operibus servari? Videmus praeterea apes lege in officio retineri, et militias habere, et excubias, et praefectos operum, et punitionem ignavorum.

Resp. Naturam non deesse in necessariis; et si gregalibus[?] necessarium esse sermonem, et legem, obedientiam, et praeceptum, ut grues manifeste ostendunt, idcirco eis ista non negamus secundum similitudinem nostram. Sed tamen ad aliam vitam post mortem dirigi negamus. Nihil enim ultra cibum et coitum in Republica eorum curari novimus. Omnia sentiunt Creatorem secreto sensu, teste sancto Hieronymo et Platonicis. At per artem et cultum non nisi homines aliam sperant vitam. Et licet bruta ob honorem vel amissam voluptatem se sponte interficiant, id non faciunt nisi desperando vitam praesentem, non a futura sperando; nec propter Deum, sicut et homines aliqui propter amorem muliebrem aliaque bona omissa, sine-

## English

These[?], because he is divine. But in beasts they are marvelous because they are beasts. Likewise beasts serve the human being, but the human being does not serve beasts. And although the human being serves a human being, he does not do so as by force and the effort of another's act, but by his own will, which no one can violate[?]. For we see the Maccabees cheerfully accept death rather than obey King Antiochus, and Scaevola not fear fire or King Porsenna for the liberty of his fatherland. [Jehovah?][?] Therefore a human being serves, and when he has received a command he uses himself from himself.

But the horse needs bridle and spurs, the ox a goad, the ape a whip; they are snatched along and habituated by pain and pleasure. The human being, however, despises pains and allurements, and uses a higher reason, by which neither an infant nor a dog can be persuaded to fast or to undergo discipline[?]. Nor because a dog carries meat and letters into a house from what was delivered[?] will it therefore be rational in the same way. For it does this by affected sense through use, and only one thing. But the human being holds this as nothing, and carries commands and little gifts to a thousand friends, and distributes to each his own things. The human being also goes where he has never been led, furnished only with the indication of the route. But the dog goes only where it has often been led by another for that work; and for that reason it is ridiculous to compare it to the human being.

But what human being, even a stupid one, was ever the slave of a lion or elephant, the strongest and wisest animals? Nor indeed would a human being subject himself to this animal as this animal does to us; nor would this animal know how to command, because it was born to obey. Nevertheless all animals serve the human being, except those which too much ignorance renders unfit for service. For a fly does not obey, since it is endowed with a very thin spirit and lacks memory. For the things that serve the human being must be capable of discipline. But fierce animals are either incapable of discipline and have little symbolic kinship with us, or, because we fear their irrational ferocity, they are not made subject to us.

Furthermore, we have webs of spiders, cells of bees, republic, kingdoms, and military order better than beasts do, and in addition other things far more excellent, as has been said. But that religion properly exists in beasts seems to have to be denied. For religion is worship of the true God with contempt of the present life for the sake of the eternal life to be attained from God. We know, however, that beasts gape only toward present goods, inquire nothing about God and eternity, and direct whatever appearance of religion they have toward present life. For the elephant adores the moon because in nocturnal journeys it receives a benefit from it, so that it may see where it is going. It is baptized after intercourse to wipe away the filth of the body, not of the soul.

Plants follow the motion of the sun and moon because they are moved by their powers and by a certain symbolic[?] sweetness, just as pennyroyal flowers at the solstices, and the magnet looks toward the pole and turns aside a little under the poles. All these things happen from the consensus and dissensus of things, as was said in the second part. For fluid bodies are not moved by religion because they fill a vacuum. Nor do the eagle and phoenix look religiously toward the sun, but are delighted by its strength. And although many nations worship animals, they are not on that account nobler than human beings, because worship is shown to them by a deceived human being who thinks that some divinity lies hidden in them. But they themselves do not command the human being, nor make laws, nor [enjoy the rank?] of kings[?], but signify some things by cries, flight, and gestures, by sensual instinct, not by intending to give oracles, but by following their own passions; and they are like signs, not like teachers in schools. Sheep and oxen, by eating much, indicate rain, because they sense that it is being prepared in the air from vapors and cold. For that reason they desire to be found[?] with full bellies.

And indeed because beasts give more attentive effort to the passions of the body than we do, since we are occupied by the mass of very many and more excellent affairs, we scarcely sense these things, just as one who listens attentively does not see, and one who is angry is not affected by allurements. Therefore they recognize the passions of the air better and predict them, and crows caw, warning one another mutually, as we too do when we expect some great evil or good. Those who suffer from hernia or scars foresee storm and cold before others do, because they are more easily affected by the air.

In dreams too more things are signified to us by the air and heaven than in waking, because the spirit is not free for external business; as if on holiday[?], it is easily affected by the passions of heaven entering through inspired air. And air carries not only passions, but also sensations. Indeed it too senses, and communicates to the aerial spirit its own imagination, which it apprehends from the preparation of war and from business, and communicates it to a resting spirit, but not to an agitated spirit. For weak motions are impressed only in what has been stilled from greater motions, with spirit and air vacant. Hence we said that melancholics foretell because of subtlety; lovers, however, because of an inborn passion easily stirred by a similar thing, as a scar is.

Therefore animal prophecy is from the air, in the aerial spirit affected by the beginnings and causes of future things. But the prophecy of the human being is not only from air and heaven, but from Angels and God. Nor does he prophesy only about rains, storms, and war through Astrology and sagacious affection, but he publishes speech and secrets about the fall of kingdoms, the names of future human beings, deeds, God, Paradise, and the underworld. Nor is he warned only by passion, as beasts are, nor by the art alone of discerning signs, which beasts lack, but by the address of God, which exceeds the human being and beasts. Nor should you say that this is by accident through instinct, as beasts find medicine and become accustomed to [sing at a funeral?][?]. For this happens to all human beings dear and pious to God[?]. And they prove it by miracles and prophecy and blood.

But when you were saying, who knows whether these things are also in beasts? I answer: they are not found, because they would treat with us about things pertaining to another life, just as we do with Angels, and they would pray in a temple and do the things that pertain to religion. Likewise they would have instruments by which they did these things. But they lack hands, study, books, and law. But you say: who knows whether they use law through traditions, and thus religion and other instruments that exist among us? Did not Lycurgus give laws to the Spartans, and wish them to be preserved not by writing but by deeds? We see besides that bees are kept in duty by law, and have armies, watches, prefects of works, and punishment of the lazy.

Response. Nature is not lacking in necessary things; and if for gregarious beings[?] speech, law, obedience, and command are necessary, as cranes clearly show, for that reason we do not deny them these things according to their likeness to us. But nevertheless we deny that they are directed to another life after death. For we know that nothing beyond food and intercourse is cared for in their Republic. All things sense the Creator by a secret sense, as Saint Jerome and the Platonists testify. But through art and worship only human beings hope for another life. ## Note

This corrected file keeps both normalized body blocks and preserves the strong concessions: animals may have signs, social law, atmospheric prophecy, and a secret sense of the Creator. The line held against them is not bare cognition but religion ordered by art and cult toward another life after death, and prophecy from divine address rather than only air, heaven, and bodily sensitivity.
