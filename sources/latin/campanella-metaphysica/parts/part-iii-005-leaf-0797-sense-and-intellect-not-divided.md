## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens: "... partes, et operationes notasse; ut inde sensus no[?]"]

At sensus a vehementi sensibili corrumpitur. Qui enim intuetur solem, non potest videre acum, et qui calore multo afficitur non sentit minorem. Ergo quoniam intellectus intelligit universalia, immaterialis est; et quoniam intelligit hominem, quem a materialibus suscipit individuis, idcirco oportet esse intellectum agentem, qui spoliat formas materialitatibus, et faciat immateriales, aptasque ab intellectu possibili, qui acquisitis speciebus intelligibilibus sit intellectus in habitu, qui potest mox actu quaecunque considerare. Idcirco ergo pueros non intelligere, quoniam adhuc intellectum adeptum, seu habitualem, non habent. Et quoniam universale est aeternum et incorruptibile, ergo intellectus est incorruptibilis. Et quoniam intellectio est eius propria, et non communis corpori, veluti sensus et imaginatio, idcirco contingit ipsum separari a corpore, sicut incorruptibile a corruptibili. Haec ille.

Nos autem in primo libro De sensu rerum, et in Physiologia, et in prima parte huius ostendimus, intellectum quem Aristoteles cognovit, non differre a sensu, quia eodem sensu sentimus particularia et universalia, et fortiter et languide moventia, et prope et a longe; et quae intelligimus sunt quae sentimus. Ut cum video Petrum longe, dico esse hominem; prope vero Petrum. Ergo Aristoteles petere principium: hoc enim quaerimus, utrum eadem vis cognoscat praesentia et absentia; ipse vero eam dividit per hoc, quod altera sentit praesentia, altera absentia.

Item immaterialia a virtute sentiente cognosci. Nam amicitiam et despicientiam et dolos cognoscunt bruta; et canis non hanc modo vulpem novit singularem, sed omnem vulpem per illam, quia similitudinem eius in memoria retinet, quae est ipsum universale. Et latrat quidem noctu ad hominem, sed non ad hunc hominem, cum mox agnoscit herum, quia a longe primo universale, deinde singulare discernit. Ut pueri omnes homines patres vocant, deinde discernunt. Uti nos omnia ova eiusdem esse gallinae putaremus; non autem gallina experta, cuiusmodi quae pariat ovum.

Videmus in Petro hominem, et hunc hominem; hoc est similitudinem cum aliis, et proprietatem suam. Et argumenta Peripatetica fiunt vana, quoniam sensus sit cum immutatione, quoniam praesens obiectum movet magis; intellectus vero, qui similitudines languidas spectat, non ita immutatur. Sed contemplantes attente, manifeste immutantur et corpore, digestionem impediunt, et saepe insaniunt.

Alia ratio est petitio principii, quia cum praesentia et sine praesentia materiae non faciunt duplex esse; nam sicut vulnus et cicatrix sunt, illud magis, ista minus afficit; illud cum praesentia agentis, ista cum relicto affectu tantum. Et quia intelligibile est absens et minus movens sensum, sensibile autem est praesens, idcirco invalida est comparatio postremae rationis. Sed debet comparari minimum sensibile magno intelligibili; et ita recte evenit. Nam sensus videns a longe et languida obiecta, melius videt proxima et robusta; et sic est intellectus.

Sensus quoque quidditatem cognoscit, quia universale et particulare, species et differentias cognoscit. Non quidem oculus, sed vis sentiens in oculo, ut in prima parte dictum est. Non reddit autem rationem, quoniam ratio est ab universalibus, et ab aliis experimentationibus collectis, quae conficiunt scientiam. Ideo oportet non sensum praesentem unum, sed multorum, quaecunque latent causae in obiecto, elicere rationem, ut in prima parte dictum est; ubi a sensu scientias omnes ortas, sed saepius sentiente eadem, et memorante, et conferente cum his, quae alii senserunt, declaravimus.

Memoriam quoque esse sensum anticipatum. Reminiscentiam expergefactionem praeteriti sensus per similem praesentem sensationem. Sensumque communem esse eumdem spiritum in pluribus organis diverso modo diversa obiecta admittentem. Phantasiam vero sensibilium passionum recollectionem, vel collationem cum aliis, copulando et dividendo. Rationem vero in notis ignotum sentire. Intellectum Peripateticum a longe sentire, et languide; apud nos vero iaculum rationis[?].

Quapropter ostendimus, esse nulla Aristotelis ratione, qua dividit animam in tot partes. Si enim simplicissima res, ut ait, utique talem divisionem non admittit, nisi operationum; multo autem minus sensum ab intellectu separat. Illa enim anima quae in oculo sentit, intelligit intus, quod ibi sentit. Intelligere enim est intus legere; sed ipsa sentiens intus et foris, a se ipsa non differt nisi in modo operandi. Et quia intus obiecta remanent languida per modum quo imprimunt, et per plures sensus plura obiecta percipit spiritus, aptus est intus separare et copulare, et sentire unum sine alio, ut albedinem sine pariete; et ea praesertim, quae in natura non habent connexionem necessariam.

Sentit enim circulum sine ligno et carne, quoniam sine his foris sentiri potest, eo quod omnes circumeat materias. Sed non sentit hominem sine carne, sed sine Petro et Paulo; sed in aliis videt extrinsecus hominem, ut de circulo dictum est. Qui si sibi determinaret materiam, sine illa non intelligeretur, sed sine individuis, quoniam nullum ita sibi determinat, quin in alio non possit subsistere. At solum Deum infinitum non possumus intelligere in pluribus individuis. Extra enim infinitum aliud concipi non potest, sicut extra Petrum Paulus.

Profecto enim si substantia simplex est anima, non potest partiri, ita ut alia sit pars eius sentiens, alia intelligens, alia imaginans. Quod autem non sit forma corporis sensitiva vis, ostensum est prius.

Ostendimus quoque, non recte Aristotelem posuisse vim sensitivam incorpoream, puramque potentiam: tum quia ab humore interlabente impeditur; tum quia accedit et recedit ab oculo, et inflat et detumescit, ut patet in omni membro; tum quia non posset sentire plures res, et praesertim contrarias simul, nisi diversis sui partibus, ac proinde partibilis sit; imo nec in diversis partibus, si ipsa est impartibilis.

At ex eo, quod noscit omnia, bene concluditur non esse ea, quae noscit. Sed quoniam multa non cognoscit, de illorum genere, quae non cognoscit (cognitione, inquam, superaddita) ponenda erat, ut pura, vel aer, vel sicut Angelus[?]. Ipse enim tactus non sentit calorem languidiorem suo innato, sed robustiorem; ergo in mediocritate caloris ponendus est.

Praeterea ostendimus, non esse oculum, qui videt, neque linguam, quae gustat, sed spiritum, qui in eis inest, et accedit et recedit, ut patet in deliquiis et somno; et per consequens vis intellectiva, nisi tota incorporea, non est. Nam et speculum suscipit sine materia effigiem, et cera sigillum aureum sine auro, et spiritus bestiae amicitiam et inimicitiam in universali et in particulari. Neque quia percipit corpora, sit incorporea: non enim aerem percipit, neque spiritum.

## English

[Damaged continuation from the preceding leaf: "... parts and operations noted, so that from there sense no[?]"]

But sense is corrupted by a vehement sensible. For one who looks at the sun cannot see a needle, and one who is affected by much heat does not sense a lesser heat. Therefore, since intellect understands universals, it is immaterial; and since it understands human being, which it receives from material individuals, therefore there must be an agent intellect which strips forms of materialities and makes them immaterial and fit to be received by the possible intellect, which, after intelligible species have been acquired, becomes an intellect in habit, able soon to consider anything whatever in act. Therefore children do not understand because they do not yet have an acquired or habitual intellect. And since the universal is eternal and incorruptible, therefore the intellect is incorruptible. And since intellection is proper to it, and not common to the body as sense and imagination are, therefore it can be separated from the body as the incorruptible from the corruptible. These are his words.

But we, in the first book of On the Sense of Things, and in the Physiology, and in the first part of this work, have shown that the intellect which Aristotle knew does not differ from sense, because by the same sense we sense particulars and universals, things moving strongly and weakly, near and from afar; and the things we understand are the things we sense. As when I see Peter from far away, I say that he is a human being; nearby, that he is Peter. Therefore Aristotle begs the question: for this is what we ask, whether the same power knows present things and absent things; but he divides it by this, that one [power] senses present things and another absent things.

Likewise immaterial things are known by the sensing power. For beasts know friendship and contempt and deceits; and a dog knows not only this singular fox, but every fox through that one, because it retains its likeness in memory, which is the universal itself. And at night it barks at a human being, but not at this human being, since it soon recognizes its master, because from afar it first discerns the universal, then the singular. Thus children call all human beings fathers, and later distinguish. So too we would think all eggs belonged to the same hen, but not a hen experienced in what sort of egg each lays.

We see in Peter a human being and this human being: that is, his likeness with others and his own property. And the Peripatetic arguments become empty, because sense is with change, since a present object moves more; but intellect, which beholds faint likenesses, is not changed in that way. But those who contemplate attentively are manifestly changed also in body, impede digestion, and often become mad.

Another reason is a begging of the question, because with the presence and without the presence of matter do not make a double being. For as a wound and a scar exist, the former affects more, the latter less; the former with the presence of the agent, the latter only with the affection left behind. And because the intelligible is absent and moves sense less, while the sensible is present, therefore the comparison of the last reason is invalid. But the least sensible must be compared to a great intelligible; and so it comes out rightly. For sense, seeing from far away and seeing faint objects, sees nearby and robust objects better; and so it is with intellect.

Sense too knows quiddity, because it knows the universal and the particular, species and differences. Not the eye, to be sure, but the sensing power in the eye, as was said in the first part. But it does not render the reason, because reason comes from universals and from other collected experiences, which make up science. Therefore reason must be elicited not from one present sense, but from many, whatever causes lie hidden in the object, as was said in the first part; where we declared that all sciences arise from sense, but from sense more often sensing the same things, remembering, and comparing with those things which others have sensed.

Memory too is anticipated sense. Reminiscence is the awakening of a past sense through a similar present sensation. Common sense is the same spirit in several organs admitting diverse objects in a diverse way. Imagination is the recollection of sensible passions, or comparison with others, by joining and dividing. Reason is sensing the unknown in known things. The Peripatetic intellect is sensing from afar and faintly; but for us, the dart of reason[?].

Therefore we have shown that there is no reason of Aristotle by which he divides the soul into so many parts. For if it is a most simple thing, as he says, surely it does not admit such a division except of operations; much less, then, does he separate sense from intellect. For the very soul that senses in the eye understands within what it senses there. To understand is to read within; but the sensing soul itself, within and without, differs from itself only in mode of operation. And because objects remain within faintly, according to the mode in which they impress, and because the spirit perceives many objects through several senses, it is apt within to separate and join, and to sense one thing without another, as whiteness without a wall; especially those things which in nature do not have a necessary connection.

For it senses a circle without wood and flesh, because without these it can be sensed outwardly, since it goes around all matters. But it does not sense human being without flesh, but without Peter and Paul; yet in others it sees human being outwardly, as was said about the circle. If it determined matter for itself, it would not be understood without that matter, but it is understood without individuals, because it determines none for itself in such a way that it cannot subsist in another. But only infinite God we cannot understand in several individuals. For outside the infinite, another cannot be conceived, as Paul can be conceived outside Peter.

Certainly, then, if the soul is a simple substance, it cannot be divided so that one part of it is sensing, another understanding, another imagining. But that the sensitive power is not the form of the body has been shown before.

We have also shown that Aristotle did not rightly posit the sensitive power as incorporeal and as pure potency: both because it is impeded by moisture intervening; and because it approaches and recedes from the eye, and swells and deflates, as is clear in every member; and because it could not sense several things, and especially contraries, at the same time, except by diverse parts of itself, and therefore it is divisible; indeed not even in diverse parts, if it is indivisible.

But from the fact that it knows all things, it is well concluded that it is not those things which it knows. Yet because it does not know many things, it ought to have been placed in the genus of those things which it does not know (by superadded cognition, I mean), as pure, or air, or like an angel[?]. For touch itself does not sense heat weaker than its inborn heat, but stronger heat; therefore it must be placed in a mean of heat.

Furthermore we have shown that it is not the eye that sees, nor the tongue that tastes, but the spirit which is in them and approaches and recedes, as is clear in fainting and sleep; and consequently the intellective power, unless it is wholly incorporeal, is not. For a mirror also receives an image without matter, and wax the golden seal without gold, and the spirit of a beast friendship and enmity in universal and particular. Nor, because it perceives bodies, is it incorporeal: for it does not perceive air, nor spirit.
