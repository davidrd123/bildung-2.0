# Part III 005: Sense and Intellect Are Not Divided

## Latin

At sensus a vehementi sensibili corrumpitur: qui enim intuetur solem non potest videre acum, et qui calore multo afficitur non sentit minorem. Ergo, quoniam intellectus intelligit universalia, immaterialis est; et quoniam intelligit hominem quem a materialibus suscipit individuis, idcirco oportet esse intellectum agentem qui spoliet formas materialitatibus et faciat immateriales aptasque ab intellectu possibili recipi.

Haec ille. Nos autem in libro De sensu rerum, in Physiologia, et in prima parte huius ostendimus intellectum quem Aristoteles cognovit non differre a sensu. Eodem sensu sentimus particularia et universalia, fortiter et languide moventia, prope et a longe. Quae intelligimus sunt quae sentimus. Cum video Petrum longe, dico esse hominem; prope vero Petrum.

Item immaterialia a virtute sentiente cognosci: amicitiam, despicientiam, et dolos cognoscunt bruta. Canis non hanc modo vulpem novit singularem, sed omnem vulpem per illam, quia similitudinem eius in memoria retinet, quae est ipsum universale. Noctu latrat ad hominem, non ad hunc hominem; mox autem agnoscit herum, quia a longe primo universale, deinde singulare discernit.

Videmus in Petro hominem et hunc hominem: similitudinem cum aliis et proprietatem suam. Argumenta Peripatetica vana fiunt. Cum praesentia et sine praesentia materiae non faciunt duplex esse; sicut vulnus et cicatrix idem sunt, illud magis, ista minus afficit. Intelligibile est absens et minus movens sensum; sensibile est praesens.

Sensus quoque quidditatem cognoscit, quia universale et particulare, species et differentias cognoscit; non quidem oculus, sed vis sentiens in oculo. Ratio est ab universalibus et aliis experimentationibus collectis, quae conficiunt scientiam. Memoria est sensus anticipatus; reminiscentia expergefactio praeteriti sensus per similem praesentem sensationem; sensus communis idem spiritus in pluribus organis diversa obiecta admittens; phantasia sensibilium passionum recollectio vel collatio.

Quapropter nulla est Aristotelis ratio qua dividit animam in tot partes. Illa enim anima quae in oculo sentit intelligit intus quod ibi sentit. Intelligere enim est intus legere. Ipsa sentiens intus et foris a se ipsa non differt nisi modo operandi.

## English

Campanella first lets the Aristotelian argument state itself: sense is corrupted by strong sensibles, while intellect becomes stronger by greater intelligibles. Since intellect understands universals taken from material individuals, an agent intellect must strip forms of materiality and make them fit for the possible intellect.

His reply is direct: the intellect Aristotle knows does not differ from sense. The same sensing power grasps particulars and universals, strong and weak movers, things near and far. What we understand is what we sense. When I see Peter from far away, I say "a human"; when near, "Peter."

Animals already show universality. A dog knows not only this individual fox but foxes as such, because memory retains the likeness, which is the universal. At night it barks at a human before recognizing its master. Children likewise call all men fathers before distinguishing.

Presence and absence of matter do not make two kinds of being. A wound and a scar differ as stronger and weaker affection, not as two ontological registers. The intelligible is the absent and weaker sensible; the sensible is the present and stronger one.

Sense also knows quiddity, not the eye as eye but the sensing power in the eye. Reason arises from universals and collected experiments; memory, reminiscence, common sense, fantasy, reason, and intellect are differentiated operations of the same spirit. Understanding is literally reading inwardly: `intelligere est intus legere`.

## Note

This is one of the strongest Part III continuations of the `sensus rerum` line. Campanella collapses the Peripatetic sense/intellect split by treating universality as a function of distance, weakness, memory, and comparison. The dog-and-fox example is important: universals are not the privilege of a separate immaterial intellect but can arise inside animal sensing itself.
