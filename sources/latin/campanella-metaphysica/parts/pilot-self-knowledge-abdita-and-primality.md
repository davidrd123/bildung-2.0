## Opening Finding

Leaf 85 gives the missing hinge between the skeptical opening and the later Cassirer/Descartes self-consciousness problem. Campanella does not simply say that knowing is passive alteration. He says wisdom is not formally a passion but a perception and judgment about passion; the object supplies an occasion and specification, while wisdom itself comes from an innate and hidden ground.

That makes the passage important in two directions. Backward, it answers the alienation problem from leaf 23 and Cassirer's Vol. 1 Campanella section: if knowing means becoming other, why is it not madness? Forward, it gives a primary Latin basis for Cassirer's claim that Campanella is a mediator between Augustine and Descartes. Self-knowledge is prior and hidden: every being first knows itself, and only then knows others by sensing itself altered.

The leaf-86 continuation finishes the answer by refusing the Ecclesiastes-style collapse of wisdom into affliction. The vain thing is not wisdom as such, but the sophistical fantasy of knowing all God's works totally. Sober wisdom knows both more things and its own greater ignorance; wisdom is finally called `sanitas orbis terrarum`, the health of the whole world in government and the arts.

## Corrected Latin

RESPONSIO AD NONAM Dubitationem.

CAP. IX. ARTIC. IX.

Ad nonum dicimus, sapientiam formaliter non esse passionem, sed perceptionem & iudicium de passione, ac proinde de obiecto, passionem inferente. Quoniam suam entitatem effundit omne agens.

Et quidem prius cognoscit seipsum omne ens, alioquin non amaret esse proprium ignotum. Cognoscit autem se, quia est id quod est: sentit autem deinde alia dum sentit se immutatum in alia; & adeo assiduis immutationibus assuescit alia nosse, quod sui obliviscitur, vel sui cognitionem mutat: proptereaque anima non videtur seipsam nosse.

At hoc verum est de notitia superaddita, non de innata & abdita: sic etiam videtur oculus non videre colorem pupillae supervenientem a lapide, figura lapidis figuratum, sed lapidem forinsecus: & tamen non videt lapidem forinsecus, nisi quia in pupilla intrinsecus receptum lapideum prius sentit colorem, a quo subito refert notitiam ad extrinsecum. Neque tactus ignem nisi quia susceptum in se calorem igneum sentit.

Fatendum ergo, quod scire sit pars alienationis: non autem est delirare, nisi prorsus alienetur; sicut qui rabie, vel amore, vel ira, vel timore nimis afficitur, ita ut afficientem mutetur in rem, adeo, ut non possit de reliquis & de se iudicare. At si insuper mutatum fuerit prorsus, dicetur corruptio: verumtamen hoc mutare non est scire, sed sciendi causa vel occasio; igitur cum scire sit iudicare, donec perdatur iudicium, remanet.

Rursus passio sciendi causa activa non est, quae causet scientiam, sed occasio specificativa scientiae, quae ex innata sapientia prodit: siquidem sapientia inest: sapor accedit, obiectorum communione: & scientia non acquiritur, sed scibilia acquiruntur. Sapientia est primalitas sicut potestas & voluntas: & non habet causas, sed specificationes in obiectis. Non enim indoctus lapis docet me, quid est lapis.

Qui enim sese scit, specificatur a calore ut sciat calorem per hoc quod scit se calefactum & calefaciente: semper ergo scire est sui: & hoc latet non considerantes quod omne Ens amat se: quia sentit se sensu abdito per se: per accidens vero & reflexe sensu addito, dum propriam sentit operationem, sed de his in 2. Parte.

Hic animadvertendum, quod mutari in Deum cognitum & amatum non est perdi, sicut in cetera: quoniam Deus est omnis Entitas eminenter. Ergo & nostra. Franciscus ergo videtur insanus nobis, sed non naturae cum per deificationem alienatur: mutari vero in res alias est perdi quod eramus, ut probatum est.

Verum quia fieri alia & plurima per tales immutationes est insuper dilatari proprium esse, nempe ex uno fieri plurima: adhuc divina res est scire etiam per passionem; unde gaudemus, passos esse etiam mala, quoniam scimus per hoc cavere mala & sectari meliora. Licet ergo interveniat vanitas, & afflictio spiritus, tamen sapientia non est afflictio formaliter, sed acquiritur per afflictionem dulcedine conditam: & dicitur a Salomone occupatio pessima ipsa sophistica, per quam putant homines se posse scire opera Dei tota & totaliter: non autem si quis novit ex hoc se plura, & plus ignorare, quam sciat sobrie sapiendo: unde ipsemet dixit, quod tantum differt sapiens ab insipiente, quantum lux a tenebris.

Quod vero multa sit vanitas in scientiis; solaque divina sit vanitatis expers, nos quoque fatemur. Nec quia sapientes subiacent morbis & persecutionibus tyrannorum, stulti vero dominantur, sapientia sit mala: non enim ipsa est causa omnium malorum: sed aliorum ignorantia est causa omnium malorum, quae eveniunt & bonis & malis. Sapientia autem est sanitas orbis terrarum in omni gubernatu & artibus: sapientes post mortem venerantur, & ex malis sciunt elicere gaudium: & animae eorum in manu Dei, quanquam visi sunt oculis insipientium mori.

Clarior est in terra & in caelo Petrus occisus, quam Nero occisor: imo & Lucanus clarior Nerone, & Socrates Melito, ceterisque suis persecutoribus. Sapientum vita, dicta, ac facta gubernant homines, & dominantur etiam post mortem. Molestias suscipiunt hilariter tanquam in comoedia per iocum, spectante Deo & Angelis eius, verberati, vitam sibi divinam exinde praesentiscentes: stulti vero neque ex iucundis rebus gaudere sciunt: & natos ad mortem sese in perpetuum sentiunt, & eorum memoria in maledictione est. Moriuntur in se, in fama, & in Deo. Sapientes, licet in se mori videantur, vivunt in se, in fama & in Deo, ut in 3. Parte.

## Working Translation

RESPONSE TO THE NINTH Dubitation.

CAP. IX. ARTIC. IX.

To the ninth, we say that wisdom is not formally a passion, but a perception and judgment concerning a passion, and therefore concerning the object that brings the passion about. For every agent pours forth its own entity.

And indeed every being first knows itself; otherwise it would not love its own unknown being. It knows itself because it is what it is. It then senses other things when it senses itself changed into other things; and it becomes so accustomed, by constant alterations, to knowing other things that it forgets itself, or alters its knowledge of itself. For that reason the soul does not seem to know itself.

But this is true of superadded knowledge, not of innate and hidden knowledge. So too the eye seems not to see the color coming upon the pupil from the stone, shaped by the figure of the stone, but the stone outside; and yet it does not see the stone outside except because the pupil, after receiving the stone-color intrinsically, first senses the color, from which it immediately refers knowledge to the external thing. Nor does touch [sense] fire except because it senses the fiery heat received into itself.

Therefore it must be admitted that knowing is part of alienation. Yet it is not madness unless one is utterly alienated, as someone is who is too strongly affected by rabies, love, anger, or fear, so that he is changed into the affecting thing to such a degree that he cannot judge about other things and about himself. But if, beyond this, he has been changed altogether, it will be called corruption. Nevertheless, this changing is not knowing, but the cause or occasion of knowing. Therefore, since knowing is judging, it remains until judgment is lost.

Again, passion is not the active cause of knowing, as though it caused knowledge, but the specifying occasion of knowledge, which proceeds from innate wisdom. For wisdom is within; flavor is added by communion with objects; and knowledge is not acquired, but knowables are acquired. Wisdom is a primality, like power and will, and it does not have causes, but specifications in objects. For the untaught stone does not teach me what a stone is.

For the one who knows himself is specified by heat so that he knows heat through the fact that he knows himself as heated and by the heating thing. Therefore knowing is always of oneself; and this escapes those who do not consider that every Being loves itself, because it senses itself by a hidden sense through itself, but accidentally and reflexively by an added sense when it senses its own operation. But about these things, [see] the second Part.

Here it must be observed that to be changed into God, known and loved, is not to be lost, as it is in other things, because God is all Entity eminently. Therefore He is also ours. Francis therefore seems mad to us, but not to nature, when he is alienated by deification; to be changed into other things, however, is to lose what we were, as has been proved.

But since to become other and many through such alterations is further to expand one's own being, namely to become many from one, knowing even through passion is still a divine thing. Hence we rejoice at having suffered even bad things, because through this we know how to beware of bad things and pursue better ones. Therefore, although vanity and affliction of spirit intervene, wisdom is not formally affliction, but is acquired through affliction seasoned with sweetness. And Solomon calls the sophistical occupation itself the worst occupation, by which human beings think they can know the works of God wholly and totally; but not if someone knows from this that he knows more things, and is more ignorant than he knows, by being soberly wise. Hence he himself said that the wise person differs from the unwise as light from darkness.

But as for the claim that there is much vanity in the sciences, and that only divine [science] is free from vanity, we too admit it. Nor is wisdom evil because the wise are subject to illnesses and persecutions by tyrants while fools rule. For wisdom itself is not the cause of all evils; rather, the ignorance of others is the cause of all evils that happen to the good and the bad. Wisdom, however, is the health of the world in every government and in the arts; the wise are venerated after death, and they know how to draw joy out of evils; and their souls are in the hand of God, although they seem to the eyes of the unwise to die.

Peter killed is brighter on earth and in heaven than Nero the killer; indeed Lucan is brighter than Nero, and Socrates than Meletus and the rest of his persecutors. The life, sayings, and deeds of the wise govern human beings and rule even after death. They take up troubles cheerfully, as if playfully in a comedy, while God and his angels look on; beaten, they already sense from this a divine life for themselves. But fools do not know how to rejoice even from pleasant things; they feel themselves born to death forever, and their memory is in curse. They die in themselves, in fame, and in God. The wise, although they seem to die in themselves, live in themselves, in fame, and in God, as [will be treated] in the third Part.

## Notes

- `sapientiam formaliter non esse passionem` is the crucial correction to a flat passivity reading. Passion is not wisdom's form; judgment about passion is.
- `notitia superaddita` versus `innata & abdita` is the local form of Ernst's abdita/addita signal. The soul's apparent self-forgetting applies to added knowledge, not to hidden innate self-knowledge.
- The pupil/stone-color example repeats the leaf-28 mediation problem in a more epistemological key. We see the external stone only because an intrinsic received color in the pupil is first sensed and then referred outward.
- `scire sit pars alienationis` connects directly to Cassirer's Vol. 1 alienation passage. Campanella accepts the alienation premise but narrows madness to total alienation, where judgment of self and other is lost.
- `scientia non acquiritur, sed scibilia acquiruntur` is the strongest local formulation of the occasional-cause model. Objects do not make wisdom; they specify an innate power.
- `Sapientia est primalitas sicut potestas & voluntas` links the epistemology of self-knowledge to the primalities doctrine. Wisdom is not a derivative possession but one of the basic determinations of being. The third primality appears here as `voluntas`, while Ernst/Adami foreground `amor`; this lexical variation remains open.
- The Francis/deification sentence now reads `insanus`, not ABBYY's `infimus`. The likely reference is Francis of Assisi; the point is that saintly deifying alienation may look mad to us without being unnatural, because being altered into God is not destructive self-loss.
- The leaf-86 continuation answers Ecclesiastes without denying him: there is vanity in the sciences, and only divine science is vanity-free, but wisdom itself is not formally affliction. The sophistical fantasy is total knowledge of God's works; sober wisdom includes knowing one's greater ignorance.
- `Sapientia autem est sanitas orbis terrarum` is the public/political turn of the passage. Wisdom heals and governs the world even when wise people are persecuted or killed.
