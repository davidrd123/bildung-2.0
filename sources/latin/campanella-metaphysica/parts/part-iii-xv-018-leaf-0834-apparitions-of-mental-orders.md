## Latin

tamen simul ubique unus est idem operans, vel quia ambit totum mundum corporalem, et intellectio non repugnat eum, qui est in terra, esse successive in cunctis systematis; vel quia Mundus mentalis porrigitur in infinitum.

De muneribus gubernantium, scilicet Dei, Deorum, Daemonum, Heroum, et minimorum erga visibilem et humanum Mundum; item quod conferant nobis septem mentales ordines Iamblico additi, cum apparent, ex sententia Iamblichi et Procli.

**ARTICULUS II.**

De finitionibus gubernantium visibilem Mundum, haec Iamblichus: Deus, ait, primus dat omnia omnibus; Dii sequentes aliqua omnibus; daemones atque animae aliqua aliquibus. Deum deorum est omnia facere, unificare; Daemonum est opificium circa Naturas mundanas usque ad singula simul et perficere, et animas ligare corporibus. Heroum vivificare, ratiocinarique, et animas custodire, easque a corporibus solvere. Animarum est varias ex se vivendi formas promere, et tam Diis quam inferioribus se coniungere, et etiam sine mediis heroibus et daemonibus, uti solet se praeter solitum coniungere Divus.

Et cum sese hominibus superi praesentant, dant corpori sanitatem, virtutem animae; fovent calorem, efficaciorem reddunt ad vitam; omnia nostra revocant ad propria principia et ad harmoniam deducunt.

Mox Iamblichus [novem?][?] ordines vel ordinum cohortes ponit, eosque apparitionibus manifestat. Nam post Deos Archangeli, inquit, eadem faciunt quod Dii, sed non adeo sufficientia nec permanentia supra proportionem nostram. Angeli bona magis particularia insuper divina[?] tribuunt. Daemones praesentia sua corpus gravant, morbis affigunt, taliter puniunt; animas ad Naturam trahunt, eosque qui ad caelestem divinum ignem contendunt circa locum inferiorem detinent, nec a fati vinculis [solvunt?][?]. Heroes eadem quae Daemones faciunt, et insuper ad opera quaedam generosa magnaque nos suscitant. Principatus bona largiuntur mundana, resque vitae huius omnes suppeditant. Principes materialia, terrenaque bona. Animae cum apparent, si purae sunt, salutem animo, donaque sacra, spem sacram praebent. Si impurae, ad generationem detorquent, spem frustrantur, passionibusque replent, animam corpori copulantibus.

Proclus vero Deos alios facit opifices rerum; alios propagatores vitarum individuorum; alios servatores divini ordinis, id est, fati. Daemones vero sub his: alios purgationi, alios generationi, alios perfectioni praeficit. Item alios curae animarum in syderibus, alios ascensui et descensui eorum, alios affectionibus eorum, alios inspirationi vitarum particularium, et ordini et rationibus eorum; alios corporum copulis cum incorporeis; alios caelestium virium distributioni, et ad ornatum et picturam materiei inferioris concinnandum et contexendum. Non longe secus Varro philosophatur.

Ex sexdecim differentiis eventorum in apparitionibus octo ordinum mentalium, ex Mundo Metaphysico in corporalem egrediendum, eorum differentias agnitas fuisse Sacerdotibus, et quomodo, Iamblichum testari.

**ARTICULUS III.**

Unde autem innotescere potuerint hi ordines mentales Aegyptiis Sacerdotibus, docens nos Iamblichus, sexdecim varietates in apparitionibus eorum ad homines, praecipue autem ad Sacerdotes in sacrificiis, enumerat. Dii: 1. apparent in salutares; 2. habent imagines penitus immutabiles; 3. cum quiete et ordine; 4. cum pulchritudine inaestimabili supra modum delectante; 5. actionesque edunt velociores quam intelligi queant; 6. eorum magnitudo totum caelum occupare videtur, quam incedentem nec terra sustinere posse; 7. eorum spectacula sunt praelucida fulgentiaque ad summum, et splendore [dearticulata?][?] mirifice, et magis quam sint revera; 8. quamplurimo splendore coruscant; 9. tanquam ignis individuus inestabilisque implens; 10. tanquam ignis stabilis omnino in aspectu; 11. praefulgidus, purus in seipso, non in alio permanens; 12. Dii materiam repente consumunt; 13. ostendunt Deos vel Angelos circa se assistentes; 14. demonstrant omnia ordinum principia in se tanquam in supremis; 15. tenuitate luminis tanta fulgent, ut corporis oculi non possint capere, sicut pisces ab aqua turbida in aerem purum elevati deficiunt; 16. veritatem, Potestatem, et actionem nostram ad summum corroborat, et bona praebent amplissima eorum apparitio.

Archangeli in suis apparitionibus ita correspondent Diis: 1. apparent horribiles simul et mansueti; 2. minus immutabiles tamen quam Dii; 3. cum quiete et ordine et efficacia; 4. consimiles Diis in pulchritudine, sed minori; 5. celeritas actionis eorum miscetur in gradu minori; 6. ipsis apparentibus, partes Mundi quaedam commoventur, praeceditque lumen in se divisum; 7. spectacula vera perfectaque.

## English

nevertheless at the same time everywhere one and the same [God] is operating, either because he encompasses the whole corporeal world, and intellection does not make it repugnant that one who is on earth should be successively in all systems; or because the mental World stretches into infinity.

Concerning the offices of those governing, namely God, Gods, Demons, Heroes, and the least [orders], toward the visible and human World; likewise what the seven mental orders added by Iamblichus confer on us when they appear, according to the opinion of Iamblichus and Proclus.

**ARTICLE II.**

Concerning the definitions of those governing the visible World, Iamblichus [says] these things: God, he says, the first, gives all things to all; the following Gods [give] some things to all; demons and souls [give] some things to some. It belongs to the God of gods to make all things, to unify. It belongs to Demons to work around worldly Natures all the way to single things together and to perfect them, and to bind souls to bodies. It belongs to Heroes to vivify and reason, and to guard souls and release them from bodies. It belongs to souls to bring forth various forms of living from themselves, and to join themselves both to Gods and to inferior things, and even without the mediating Heroes and demons, as the Divine one is accustomed beyond custom to join himself.

And when the higher beings present themselves to human beings, they give health to the body, virtue to the soul; they foster heat, make it more efficacious for life; they recall all our things to their proper principles and lead them back to harmony.

Soon Iamblichus posits [nine?][?] orders or cohorts of orders and manifests them from apparitions. For after Gods, Archangels, he says, do the same as the Gods, but not with such sufficiency nor such permanence above our proportion. Angels additionally distribute more particular divine[?] goods. Demons by their presence weigh down the body, fasten it with diseases, punish in such a way; they draw souls toward Nature, and those who strive toward celestial divine fire they detain around a lower place, nor do they [release?][?] them from the bonds of fate. Heroes do the same things as Demons, and in addition rouse us to certain generous and great works. Principalities bestow worldly goods and supply all the things of this life. Princes [bestow] material and earthly goods. Souls, when they appear, if they are pure, provide health for the soul, sacred gifts, sacred hope. If they are impure, they turn [one] aside toward generation, frustrate hope, and fill [one] with passions that join the soul to the body.

But Proclus makes some Gods artificers of things; others propagators of the lives of individuals; others preservers of the divine order, that is, of fate. Demons, however, under these: some he sets over purgation, others over generation, others over perfection. Likewise some [he sets] over the care of souls in the stars, others over their ascent and descent, others over their affections, others over the inspiration of particular lives and their order and reasons; others over the unions of bodies with incorporeal things; others over the distribution of celestial powers, and for fitting and weaving together the ornament and picture of lower matter. Varro philosophizes not far otherwise.

That from sixteen differences of events in the apparitions of eight mental orders, when they go out from the Metaphysical World into the corporeal, their differences were known to the Priests, and how, Iamblichus testifies.

**ARTICLE III.**

But whence these mental orders could have become known to the Egyptian Priests, Iamblichus, teaching us, enumerates sixteen varieties in their apparitions to human beings, and especially to Priests in sacrifices. Gods: 1. appear as salutary; 2. have images wholly immutable; 3. with quiet and order; 4. with inestimable beauty delighting beyond measure; 5. and they produce actions swifter than can be understood; 6. their magnitude seems to occupy the whole heaven, and the earth seems unable to sustain it as it advances; 7. their spectacles are pre-luminous and shining to the highest degree, and wondrously [articulated?][?] with splendor, and more than they are in reality; 8. they flash with very great splendor; 9. as an individual fire filling unstably; 10. as a fire wholly stable in aspect; 11. pre-shining, pure in itself, remaining not in another; 12. Gods suddenly consume matter; 13. they show Gods or Angels standing around them; 14. they demonstrate all principles of orders in themselves as in the supreme beings; 15. they shine with such subtlety of light that bodily eyes cannot receive it, just as fish raised from turbid water into pure air fail; 16. their apparition strengthens our truth, Power, and action to the highest degree, and provides the most ample goods.

Archangels in their apparitions correspond to Gods in this way: 1. they appear terrible and gentle at once; 2. nevertheless less immutable than Gods; 3. with quiet and order and efficacy; 4. very like Gods in beauty, but with lesser [beauty]; 5. the swiftness of their action is mixed in a lesser grade; 6. when they appear, certain parts of the World are moved, and a light divided in itself precedes; 7. the spectacles [are] true and perfect.

## Note

This part moves from the metaphysical omnipresence correction into Iamblichus's functional taxonomy of governing orders. Article III begins a highly concrete diagnostic catalog of apparitions: the mental orders are distinguished by visible effects, light, stability, bodily impact, and what goods or dangers they confer.
