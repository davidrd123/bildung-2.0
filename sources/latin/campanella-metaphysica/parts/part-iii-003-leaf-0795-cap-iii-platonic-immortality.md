## Latin

**Rationes Platonicae pro immortalitate animorum examinantur.**

**CAP. III.** Sed haudquaquam subito est etiam immortalis, licet tanta polleat excellentia; nam Stoicis anima et genius sunt particula Dei, et tamen post mortem non vivunt, sed vertuntur in Deum, uti spiritus in aerem, id est in universitatem. Quapropter rationes adducere oportet, quibus immortalitas demonstretur.

Plato quidem ex eo quod anima est numerus seipsum movens, concludit immortalitatem. Quod enim seipsum a seipso movet, non indiget alio, neque destituit seipsum in motu, sed semper movetur. Anima autem est huiusmodi; ipsa enim movet corpus. Ergo cum non possit dare quod non habet, consequens est, ut ipsa habeat motum a se. Nec movetur ad suos actus vitales ab obiectis, sed solum occasionaliter.

Item in libro De legibus ait: anima est antiquior corpore, quia nobilius et agens prius est ignobiliori ac facto. Anima est nobilior, et ipsa facit corpus, id est, organizat ad proprios usus. Ergo non pendet a corpore. Ergo corrupto corpore non corrumpitur.

In Axiocho probat animae divinitatem immortalem ex eo, quod caelum noscit, aequinoctia, tropicos, et praedicit futuros sidereos aspectus, nat[?] maria. **ART. I.** Rempublicam condit, leges et literas invenit. Item in Phaedone ex studio philosophiae, quod alienat animam a corpore tanquam immortalem a mortali. Alibi vero probat idem ex religione, et idearum, et superum notitia.

Sed prima non est sufficiens. Namque et ignis per se movetur, et tamen corrumpitur a frigore. Verum dicit, quod a se non pereat, sed non quod non ab alio; idcirco ostendendum erat, quod careret contrario, et sic satisfecisset. Utuntur tamen patres hac ratione, sumendo motum pro appetitione, potestatione, et notitia, quae non habet ab obiectis nisi occasionaliter, sed [a se tergo independens][?] et immortalis.

Secunda est vera de anima mundi, formante corpora, sed non de omni anima. Et licet Plato putet omnem animam aeternam, unde et bruta immortali anima donat; nam et in illis est numerus seipsum movens, per se subsistens Platoni, et omnis corporatio ab anima formatur mundi; nihilominus mox ostendemus, non esse ponendas animas ab aeterno. Posse tempore ignobilius procedere, non autem dignitate; et corpora per caloris moderationes sub angelis a spiritu vitali efformari. Secunda ratio rite probat animae divinitatem.

## English

**Platonic arguments for the immortality of souls are examined.**

**Chapter III.** But it is not immediately also immortal, although it has so much excellence. For according to the Stoics soul and genius are a particle of God, and yet after death they do not live, but are turned into God, as spirit into air, that is, into universality. Therefore reasons must be brought forward by which immortality may be demonstrated.

Plato indeed concludes immortality from the fact that the soul is a self-moving number. For what moves itself by itself does not need another, nor does it abandon itself in motion, but is always moved. But the soul is of this sort; for it moves the body. Therefore, since it cannot give what it does not have, it follows that it has motion from itself. Nor is it moved to its vital acts by objects, except only occasionally.

Likewise in the book On Laws he says: the soul is older than the body, because what is nobler and active is prior to what is ignoble and made. The soul is nobler, and itself makes the body, that is, organizes it for its proper uses. Therefore it does not depend on the body. Therefore, when the body is corrupted, it is not corrupted.

In the Axiochus he proves the immortal divinity of the soul from the fact that it knows the heavens, the equinoxes, the tropics, and predicts future starry aspects, [and] sails the seas[?]. **Article I.** It founds a commonwealth, invents laws and letters. Likewise in the Phaedo, from the study of philosophy, because it alienates the soul from the body as an immortal thing from a mortal one. Elsewhere he proves the same from religion, and from knowledge of ideas and of higher beings.

But the first is not sufficient. For fire too is moved by itself, and yet is corrupted by cold. He truly says that it does not perish from itself, but not that it does not perish from another; therefore it had to be shown that it lacked a contrary, and then he would have satisfied. Yet the fathers use this reason, taking motion for appetition, power, and knowledge, which it does not have from objects except occasionally, but is [from itself / from behind, independent][?] and immortal.

The second is true of the world-soul forming bodies, but not of every soul. And although Plato thinks every soul eternal, whence he also gives beasts an immortal soul - for in them too there is, for Plato, a self-moving number subsisting through itself, and every embodiment is formed from the soul of the world - nevertheless we will soon show that souls are not to be posited from eternity. What is more ignoble can proceed in time, but not in dignity; and bodies are formed by a vital spirit, under angels, through moderations of heat. The second reason rightly proves the soul's divinity.
