## Latin

**Rationes Platonicae pro immortalitate animorum examinantur.**

Sed haudquaquam subito est etiam immortalis, licet tanta polleat excellentia. Nam Stoicis anima et genius sunt particula Dei, et tamen post mortem non vivunt, sed vertuntur in Deum, uti spiritus in aerem, id est in universitatem. Quapropter rationes adducere oportet, quibus immortalitas demonstretur.

Plato quidem ex eo quod anima est numerus seipsum movens concludit immortalitatem. Quod enim seipsum a seipso movet non indiget alio neque destituit seipsum in motu, sed semper movetur. Anima autem est huiusmodi; ipsa enim movet corpus. Ergo cum non possit dare quod non habet, consequens est ut ipsa habeat motum a se. Nec movetur ad suos actus vitales ab obiectis, sed solum occasionaliter.

Item in libro De legibus ait animam esse antiquiorem corpore, quia nobilius et agens prius est ignobiliori ac facto. Anima est nobilior et ipsa facit corpus, id est organizat ad proprios usus. Ergo non pendet a corpore; ergo corrupto corpore non corrumpitur.

In Axiocho probat animae divinitatem immortalem ex eo quod caelum noscit, aequinoctia, tropicos, et praedicit futuros sidereos aspectus, nat maria, rempublicam condit, leges et literas invenit. Item in Phaedone ex studio philosophiae, quod alienat animam a corpore tanquam immortalem a mortali. Alibi vero probat idem ex religione, idearum, et superum notitia.

Sed prima non est sufficiens. Nam et ignis per se movetur, et tamen corrumpitur a frigore. Verum dicit quod a se non pereat, sed non quod non ab alio; idcirco ostendendum erat quod careret contrario, et sic satisfecisset. Utuntur tamen patres hac ratione, sumendo motum pro appetitione, potestatione, et notitia, quae non habet ab obiectis nisi occasionaliter, sed a se tergo independens et immortalis.

Secunda est vera de anima mundi formante corpora, sed non de omni anima. Et licet Plato putet omnem animam aeternam, unde et bruta immortali anima donat, nihilominus mox ostendemus non esse ponendas animas ab aeterno. Posse tempore ignobilius procedere, non autem dignitate; et corpora per caloris moderationes sub angelis a spiritu vitali efformari.

## English

The human soul's excellence does not immediately prove that it is immortal. For the Stoics, soul and genius are particles of God, yet after death they do not survive as individual souls but are turned back into God, as spirit into air, that is, into the whole. So arguments have to be brought forward that actually demonstrate immortality.

Plato first argues that soul is a self-moving number. What moves itself from itself does not need another mover, nor does it fail itself in motion; it always moves. Since the soul moves the body and cannot give what it does not have, it must have motion from itself. Its vital acts are not moved by objects except occasionally.

Plato also says in the Laws that soul is older than body: the nobler and active is prior to the ignoble and made. Soul is nobler and organizes the body for its uses; therefore it does not depend on the body and is not corrupted when the body is corrupted. In the Axiochus, its immortal divinity is argued from knowledge of heaven, equinoxes, tropics, future star-aspects, navigation, politics, laws, and letters. In the Phaedo, philosophy alienates the soul from the mortal body as though the soul were immortal; elsewhere religion, ideas, and knowledge of higher beings prove the same.

Campanella accepts only part of this. The first argument is insufficient because fire also moves by itself and is still corrupted by cold. It would work only if Plato showed that soul has no contrary. The second argument applies to the world-soul forming bodies, not to every soul. Plato's doctrine that every soul is eternal would also give immortal souls to beasts, which Campanella says he will not accept.

## Note

Caput III begins by separating human excellence from immortality. Campanella is willing to use Platonic arguments, but he refuses to let "noble," "self-moving," or "older than body" carry more than they prove. The decisive test is already coming into view: immortality requires absence of contrary and absence of corrupting subject, not just divinity or superiority.
