## Latin

Si anima, cum sit incorporea, immortalis, et simplex, habet in se obiecta, semper illa intelligeret. Unde Theophrastus quaerit cur non ab initio intelligimus, si tantus est hic intellectus qui spoliat formas materialitatibus et eas reddit immateriales. Solius enim Dei est facere de corporeo incorporeum.

Item, postquam semel intelligit, cur non semper intelligit? Unde est oblivio et deceptio? Si enim factus in actu a rebus intellectis fit unum cum illis, semper debet illas intelligere, sicut lignum ignitum factumque ignis semper calefacit. Cur cessat intelligere? Cur decipitur et obliviscitur?

Omitto rationes contra eos qui faciunt animam unam in cunctis et nunquam declararunt quomodo non idem scimus omnes, quomodo intelligimus cum intelligimur, quomodo anima illa hominibus illabitur et non brutis, quomodo in aere manens in corpus trahitur, et quomodo a corpore oblivionem patitur atque rursus illabi cupit.

Quapropter videamus si qua sunt rationes probantes animae aeternitatem secundum veram philosophiam.

**In anima, ut in omni ente finito, dari partem passivam et partem activam; sed non Aristotelico modo. Caput IV. Articulus I.**

Invenitur quidem in anima nostra agere et pati. Prius enim recipit rerum motiones, a quibus afficitur, deinde vero de rebus moventibus iudicat. Ergo datur in anima pars passiva et activa. Sed pars passiva non est animae in quantum anima, sed in quantum natura deficiens. Quaecumque enim natura caret aliqua entitate potest illam recipere.

Iudicare autem de re inferente passionem est actus animae in quantum sapiens est. Est quaedam actio, sed potius operatio interna dicenda foret. Ergo non datur pars activa in anima separata a passiva. Eadem enim quae patitur ipsa iudicat de eo a quo patitur. Non enim ego possum iudicare de dolore quem pateris tu.

Neque pars activa est quae spoliat obiecta materialitatibus, sed potius vestit: ex parte communique passione partis rei iudicat de tota re et de individuis cunctis. Sensus enim, ex eo quod videt aliquid figuratum instar hominis a longe, iudicat esse hominem cum carne et osse et oculis et pulmone et ceteris materialitatibus quas non videt. Sic et intellectus.

In prima parte demonstratum est obiecta non movere animam neque primalitatem ad exercitium, sed ad specificationem tantum.

## English

Campanella closes the Aristotelian critique with a memory problem. If the immaterial intellect has objects in itself, why does it not always understand them? If understanding makes the intellect one with the understood things, why is there forgetting, deception, or interruption? If a piece of wood becomes fire, it keeps heating; why does the intellect cease to understand?

The single-intellect doctrine fares no better. It cannot explain why we do not all know the same things, why the one soul enters humans and not beasts, why it would be drawn from the air into a body that makes it suffer and forget, or why it would desire to enter bodies again.

Caput IV begins Campanella's own account. In the soul, as in every finite being, there is active and passive, but not in the Aristotelian sense. The soul first receives motions from things and is affected by them; then it judges the things that moved it. Passivity belongs to the soul insofar as it is a deficient nature lacking some entity it can receive. Judgment belongs to the soul insofar as it is wise.

The active part is not a separate power that strips objects of materiality. It does almost the reverse: from a partial and common passion it supplies the whole thing. Seeing a human-like figure from far away, sense judges that there is a human with flesh, bones, eyes, lungs, and all the materialities it does not see. Intellect works in the same way.

## Note

This is the pivot from demolition to construction. The important reversal is `spoliat` versus `vestit`: cognition does not strip matter off a phantasm, but clothes a partial affection with the unseen whole. That keeps the earlier "objects specify, they do not activate wisdom" doctrine in play while giving ordinary perception a constructive role.
