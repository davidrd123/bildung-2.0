## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] Aristoteli; ergo, inquam, phantasia est aliud ab anima, vel locus non informatus anima. Ergo non utitur phantasmatibus ut obiectis, quae iam habet, sed ut organis. Item corrupto organo phantasiae, homo insanit, et nil recte intelligit iuxta ipsorum dogmata. Ergo utitur eis organice.

Nam praeterea anima, cum sit incorporea, et immortalis, et simplex, cum habet in se obiecta, semper illa intelligeret. Unde Theophrastus quaerit, cur non ab initio intelligimus, si tantus est hic intellectus, qui spoliat formas materialitatibus et eas reddit immateriales, et copulat passivo intellectui, et, sicut dicit Aphrodisiensis, est efficacissimus sicut ignis, qui devorat omnem materialitatem. Hoc tamen falsum; solius enim Dei est facere de corporeo incorporeum.

Item, inquam, postquam semel intelligit, cur non semper intelligit? Et unde est oblivio et deceptio? Si enim factus in actu a rebus intellectis fit unum cum illis, semper debet illas intelligere, sicut lignum ignitum, factumque ignis, semper calefacit. At cur cessat intelligere? Cur decipitur et obliviscitur? Non enim potest res immaterialis, impassibilis, actuosissima[?], quocunque levi motu et causa a se alienari decipique. Item cur non omnia a principio cognovimus, si intellectus activus est omnia? Vel cur non reminiscimur, quae alibi intelligit intellectus? Haec ratio procedit contra Averroem et Alexandrum. Neque enim ex immortali impassibilique formae impressae delentur; et si delentur illae, quibuscum factum est unum, magisque quam cum ex materia et forma, profecto ipse intellectus corrumpitur et deletur. Item quomodo una forma percepta potest alias suscipere? Neque enim tabula rasa suscipit aliam figuram nisi prima deleta, ut scimus in exemplo eorum. Profecto si propriam formam amittit, corrumpitur; id enim est corrumpi.

Omitto rationes contra eos, qui faciunt animam unam in cunctis, et nunquam declararunt quomodo non idem scimus omnes; et quomodo intelligimus cum intelligimur; et quomodo anima illa hominibus illabitur, et non brutis; et quomodo in aere manens in corpus trahitur, a quo mala multa patietur, et praesertim scientiae oblivionem alibi adeptae. A quo fato cogitur ad hanc copulationem, si non sponte? Et quomodo a principio non omnia scimus? Et quomodo a corpore oblivionem patitur, et tamen in illud rursus illabi cupit? Et quomodo Aristoteles hanc animae unitatem tacuit? Et cum ipsa non patiatur a sensibilibus nisi in virtute intellectus agentis, quomodo oblivionem et deceptionem a phantasia patiatur? Numquid intellectus agens dat phantasiae, ut possit facere malum in intellectu, et ignorantiam, sicut dat, ut possit bonum et scientiam videre, dum illa illuminat, sicut sol colores, ut ipsi dicunt? Quod si id potest agere absque intellectu agente, ergo frustra ponitur etiam ob alias actiones, et fabulosus iste intellectus agens.

Item si anima spoliat materialitate formas, facitque intelligibiles, tunc multo magis intelligeret angelos, formasque per se immateriales, praesertim cum non sit corporis forma secundum Averroem. Quod tamen non experimur. Vel secundum Alexandrum intelligeret ipsum intellectum agentem longe magis ac prius quam intelligibilia, quae materialitatibus spoliat. Immo materialia minus intelligeret quam angelos, quoniam nunquam percipit corpora, sed incorporea reddita phantasmata, ut angelus est. Sed haec omnia vana sunt. Quapropter videamus si quae sunt rationes probantes animae aeternitatem secundum veram philosophiam.

**In anima, ut in omni ente finito, dari partem passivam et partem activam; sed non Aristotelico modo, et ipsum sapere et amare in quantum ens; sed in quantum anima dare alteri sapere, posse, amare, et secundum se, vel subsistere, vel ut causa agens inexistere. Et animal esse unum non simpliciter, neque compositione, sed decompositione. Et utrum quo primo sapit homo, sit immortale, inquirendum esse iuxta nostram philosophiam. CAP. IV. ART. I.**

Invenitur quidem in anima nostra agere et pati. Prius enim recipit rerum motiones, a quibus afficitur, deinde vero de rebus moventibus iudicat. Ergo datur in anima pars passiva et activa. Sed pars passiva non est animae in quantum anima, sed in quantum natura deficiens. Quaecunque enim natura caret aliqua entitate, potest illam recipere. Quapropter in angelo datur pars passiva, quatenus recipit illuminationem a superioribus; et in sensu, et in materia omnium maxime.

Iudicare autem de re inferente passionem est actus animae, in quantum sapiens est, et est quaedam actio, sed potius operatio interna dicenda foret. Ergo non datur pars activa in anima separata a passiva. Eadem enim, quae patitur, ipsa iudicat de eo a quo patitur. Non enim ego possum iudicare de dolore, quem pateris tu.

Neque pars activa est, quae spoliat obiecta materialitatibus, sed potius vestit; quoniam ex parte communique passione partis rei iudicat de tota re, et de individuis cunctis. Sensus enim, ex eo quod videt aliquid figuratum instar hominis a longe, iudicat esse hominem cum carne et osse, et oculis et pulmone, et cum ceteris materialitatibus, quas non videt. Sic et intellectus. Unde Scotus recte dicit intuitive ab eo singularia cognosci, sed ruditer admittit intellectum agentem alium diudicante[?].

Insuper in prima parte demonstratum est, obiecta non movere animam neque primalitatem ad exercitium, sed ad specificationem tantum. Omnis enim sapientia semper sapit id quod est illud[?], cum sapientia est, vel abdito modo. Cetera vero

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] for Aristotle; therefore, I say, phantasy is something other than the soul, or a place not informed by the soul. Therefore it does not use phantasms as objects, which it already has, but as organs. Likewise, when the organ of phantasy is corrupted, a human being goes mad and understands nothing rightly according to their own dogmas. Therefore it uses them organically.

For furthermore, since the soul is incorporeal, immortal, and simple, when it has objects in itself it would always understand them. Hence Theophrastus asks why we do not understand from the beginning, if this intellect is so great that it strips forms of materialities, renders them immaterial, and joins them to the passive intellect, and, as Aphrodisias says, is most efficacious like fire, which devours all materiality. Yet this is false; for it belongs to God alone to make an incorporeal thing from a corporeal one.

Likewise, I ask, after it once understands, why does it not always understand? And whence come forgetfulness and deception? For if, made in act by the things understood, it becomes one with them, it ought always to understand them, just as kindled wood, made fire, always heats. But why does it cease to understand? Why is it deceived and forgetful? For an immaterial, impassible, most active[?] thing cannot be alienated from itself and deceived by any light motion and cause. Likewise, why did we not know all things from the beginning, if the active intellect is all things? Or why do we not remember the things that the intellect understands elsewhere? This reason proceeds against Averroes and Alexander. For impressions of forms are not erased from what is immortal and impassible; and if those things with which it has become one are erased, more than with matter and form, surely the intellect itself is corrupted and erased. Likewise, how can one perceived form receive others? For a blank tablet does not receive another figure unless the first is erased, as we know in their example. Surely if it loses its own form, it is corrupted; for that is what it is to be corrupted.

I pass over the reasons against those who make the soul one in all things and have never explained how we do not all know the same thing; how we understand when we are understood; how that soul enters human beings and not beasts; how, while remaining in the air, it is drawn into a body from which it will suffer many evils, and especially forgetfulness of the science acquired elsewhere; by what fate it is compelled to this coupling, if not willingly; how we do not know all things from the beginning; how it suffers forgetfulness from the body and yet wants to enter it again; how Aristotle was silent about this unity of the soul; and, since it does not suffer from sensibles except in the power of the agent intellect, how it suffers forgetfulness and deception from phantasy. Does the agent intellect give phantasy power to make evil and ignorance in the intellect, just as it gives it power to see good and science when it illuminates it, as the sun illuminates colors, as they say? But if phantasy can do this without the agent intellect, then the agent intellect is posited in vain also for other actions, and this agent intellect is fabulous.

Likewise, if the soul strips forms of materiality and makes them intelligible, then it would understand angels and forms immaterial through themselves much more, especially since according to Averroes it is not the form of the body. Yet we do not experience this. Or according to Alexander, it would understand the agent intellect itself much more and before the intelligibles which it strips of materialities. Indeed it would understand material things less than angels, since it never perceives bodies, but phantasms rendered incorporeal, as an angel is. But all these things are empty. Therefore let us see whether there are any reasons proving the eternity of the soul according to true philosophy.

**In the soul, as in every finite being, a passive part and an active part are given; but not in the Aristotelian way, and knowing and loving themselves [belong] insofar as it is being; but insofar as it is soul, it gives another knowing, being-able, and loving, and according to itself either subsists or exists-in as an acting cause. And the animal is one not simply, nor by composition, but by decomposition. And it must be investigated according to our philosophy whether that by which the human being first knows is immortal. Chapter IV. Article I.**

There is indeed found in our soul acting and suffering. For first it receives the motions of things by which it is affected; then it judges about the things moving it. Therefore there is given in the soul a passive part and an active part. But the passive part does not belong to the soul insofar as it is soul, but insofar as it is a deficient nature. For whatever nature lacks some entity can receive it. Therefore in an angel too there is a passive part, insofar as it receives illumination from superiors; and in sense, and most of all in matter.

But to judge about the thing bringing in passion is an act of the soul insofar as it is wise, and is a certain action, though it should rather be called an internal operation. Therefore no active part is given in the soul separated from the passive. The very same thing which suffers judges about that from which it suffers. For I cannot judge about the pain which you suffer.

Nor is the active part that which strips objects of materialities, but rather it clothes them; because from the part and from the common passion of a part of the thing, it judges about the whole thing and about all individuals. For sense, from the fact that it sees something shaped like a human being from afar, judges that it is a human being with flesh and bone, eyes and lung, and with the other materialities which it does not see. So too intellect. Hence Scotus rightly says that singulars are known intuitively by it, but crudely admits another agent intellect as judge[?].
