## Opening Finding

This leaf gives the bodily base of Campanella's later `intrinsecatio` doctrine. Taste receives the name of wisdom because it seems to reach not only surfaces but intrinsic, hidden, weak natures: `solus hic sensus intrinsecatur obiecto`. But Campanella is making this point inside a skeptical `Dubitatio`: because taste varies by body, age, illness, temperament, and animal nature, the very sense that gives `sapientia` its name also shows why wisdom is not given through sense, even "in part."

That matters for the Cassirer anchor. Leaf 906 can use eating/tasting as the figure for beatific cognition only because leaf 23 first makes taste the sense of intrinsic contact, and then shows why sensory taste itself cannot satisfy the demand for true wisdom.

## Corrected Latin

DUBITATIO III.

Quod non datur scire ex eo quod non prout sunt sapiunt, quae sapere nobis dicuntur. Nullus enim sensus sentit rem sicuti est, sed sicuti afficitur, et alii aliter; licet putent quandoque idem sapere, non sapiunt idem. Vel ergo nos non sapimus, aut res non sunt uti esse videntur, aut aliis aliae sunt, aut nos non sumus quod videmur.

TERTIUM: neque ex parte contingit sapere. Cum enim omnis sapientia a sensu sit, et per sensum; quae enim cogitamus non per sensum, aut per ea quae sentimus, chimaerica sunt, et caecus non iudicat de colore; sensus vero nihil vere sapiat. Consequens est, ut nulla sit sapientia.

Sapientia enim dicitur a sapore, et gustu sapiente res prout sunt. Et magis hic sensus dat nomen sapientiae quam alii; quoniam non modo sentit rerum naturas extrinsecas, robustasque, sed etiam intrinsecas, latentes, languidas.

Tactus in vino cognoscit frigus extrinsecus, occupans vinum, et ligans eius vires: gustus vero et frigus sentit extrinsecum, et calorem intrinsecum latentem per saporem, qui est calor nativus cum mole attrita in minutias, vel fluente, linguae subiens poros, et spiritum in eius nerviculis inclusum afficiens, seque illis manifestans, ut alibi probatum est.

Quapropter solus hic sensus intrinsecatur obiecto, et obiectum illi totum; et propterea sapiens est, qui rem intrinsecus novit, non qui superficiem modo per intellectum. Sed intellectus nil intus legit, nisi deforis acceperit per sensum; sensus autem nullus sapit nisi gustus: gustus vero nec vere sapit; nam saepe dulcia iudicat esse amara, et e contra.

At sensus sicut patitur, ita iudicat. Ergo sensibile agit in ipsum; ergo dulce facit, et amare facit ipsum. Ergo vel entia non sunt, ut nobis sese pandunt agendo; vel nos non sapimus, et mutamur, et non sumus quod sumus. Gustus ergo nihil sapit, dum dulcia iudicat esse amara, et salsa insipida, ut patet in his qui perfusam bile linguam habent, aut febriunt.

Item quod uni sapit dulce et suave, alteri est insuave; caro pinguis displicet mihi, alteri vero macra. Genista caprae est dulcis, nobis amara; pueris placent dulcia, senibus displicent, sed subamara bene sapiunt. Item quod sapit sanis, insipidum est aegris; et in aetatibus variatur gustus. Cur autem magis puero quam seni prudentiori credamus? et cur magis seni defectum patienti quam robusto iuveni? Item cholerici, phlegmatici, melancholici, sanguinei differunt gustu; et alii aliis delectantur saporibus. Unde proverbium quod, de gustibus non est disputandum, quoniam varii incertique sunt. Si ergo gustus vires rerum ostendit intrinsecus, ita ut sapientiae nomen det, et tam varius est, quomodo credemus illi? Ergo sapientia per sensum non datur, neque ex parte.

## Working Translation

DUBITATION III.

That knowing is not given from the fact that the things said to taste a certain way to us do not taste as they are. For no sense senses the thing as it is, but as it is affected, and different senses or people are affected differently. Although they sometimes think they taste the same thing, they do not taste the same thing. Therefore either we do not taste/know, or things are not as they seem to be, or they are different things for different people, or we are not what we seem to be.

THIRD: it does not happen that one knows even in part. For since all wisdom comes from sense and through sense, while the things we think not through sense, or through those things we sense, are chimerical, and the blind person does not judge concerning color, but sense itself would not truly know anything, it follows that there is no wisdom.

For wisdom is said from flavor, and from taste tasting things as they are. This sense, more than the others, gives wisdom its name, because it senses not only the extrinsic and robust natures of things, but also the intrinsic, hidden, and weak ones.

Touch, in wine, knows cold extrinsically as something occupying the wine and binding its powers. Taste, however, senses both the extrinsic cold and the intrinsic heat hidden through flavor: the native heat, with matter ground down into tiny particles or flowing, entering the pores of the tongue and affecting the spirit enclosed in its little nerves, and making itself manifest to them, as has been proved elsewhere.

Therefore this sense alone is intrinsecated with the object, and the object wholly [is present] to it. For that reason the wise person is the one who knows the thing from within, not the one who knows only the surface by intellect. But the intellect reads nothing inside unless it has received it from outside through sense; and no sense tastes/knows except taste. Yet taste itself does not truly taste/know: for it often judges sweet things to be bitter, and conversely.

But sense judges as it is acted upon. Therefore the sensible thing acts upon it; therefore it makes it sweet and makes it bitter. Therefore either beings are not as they disclose themselves to us by acting, or we do not know/taste, and we are changed, and we are not what we are. Taste, therefore, knows nothing when it judges sweet things to be bitter and salty things tasteless, as happens in those whose tongue is suffused with bile or who are feverish.

Likewise, what tastes sweet and pleasant to one person is unpleasant to another. Fat meat displeases me, while lean meat displeases someone else. Broom is sweet to a goat, bitter to us. Sweet things please children and displease old people, while slightly bitter things taste good to them. Likewise, what tastes good to the healthy is tasteless to the sick; taste varies across ages. Why should we trust a child more than an older and wiser person? And why trust an old person suffering a defect more than a robust young person? Likewise choleric, phlegmatic, melancholic, and sanguine people differ in taste, and different people are delighted by different flavors. Hence the proverb: there is no disputing about tastes, because they are various and uncertain. So if taste shows the powers of things from within, so that it gives wisdom its name, and yet is so variable, how shall we trust it? Therefore wisdom is not given through sense, not even in part.

## Notes

- `sapientia`, `sapere`, and `sapor` are deliberately fused. English cannot keep the triple sense of wisdom, tasting, and savor without awkwardness, so the translation uses "taste/know" at the pressure points.
- `solus hic sensus intrinsecatur obiecto` is the first major verbal foothold for the later noun `intrinsecatio`. At this stage the term is still phenomenological: taste has the object inside its operation, not merely in an image or surface.
- Case note: `obiecto` is dative. The translation's "with the object" is provisional English, not a decision that the relation is mere accompaniment; revisit the case grammar when leaf 438 brings `intrinsecatur` and `intrinsecaretur` back under epistemological pressure.
- The phrase `obiectum illi totum` is elliptical in the print/OCR. The translation supplies "[is present]" only to make the syntax readable; the Latin should remain open until checked against a high-resolution page crop.
- The argument is double-edged. Taste is the sense that most nearly deserves the name of wisdom, but its variability is precisely what blocks sense from being wisdom.
- The right column of leaf 23 continues under `ART. III` into animals, smell, touch, and weight. This part stops at the end of the left-column taste argument.
- Encounter 001's beatitude passage depends on this logic: sight and touch do not nourish; only tasting/eating does. Leaf 23 explains why tasting can bear that cognitive metaphor and why sensory tasting still needs to be surpassed.

## Translation Risk

Lexical/contextual: `sapere` oscillates between "to taste" and "to be wise/know." Preserving the oscillation is more important than choosing one English equivalent consistently.

OCR/textual: `obiectum illi totum` and the physical account of `calor nativus cum mole attrita in minutias, vel fluente` need direct image collation before the translation is treated as settled.
