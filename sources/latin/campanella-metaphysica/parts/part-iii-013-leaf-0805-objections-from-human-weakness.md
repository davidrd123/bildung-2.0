## Latin

Gallinis, et tamen melius volare, quoniam meliores habent alas? Eandem sententiam tuentur Brachmani hodie, et Talmudistae ex parte, et olim Pharisaei. Et profecto dicunt spiritus vexantes homines se esse animas defunctorum nominatim, unde videri potest vera transmigratio animarum. Ergo vel omnes animae sunt immortales et transmigrant, vel omnes mortales; et homo excedit caetera organis, non autem virtute.

Saepe locuta esse vocibus humanis bruta legimus in historiis et in sacris, asinam Balaam et serpentem. Verum quia non habent organa vocis eadem nobiscum, nos eorum loquelam non percipimus. Nec enim Scytharum percipimus. Sed tamen canes linguam nostram intelligere palam est. Et Polonus canis, delatus Neapolim, non intelligebat quae sibi dicebantur Neapolitane, sed Polonice loquentes audiebat et obediebat dictis. Psittaci tamen linguam nostram imitantur, quoniam concavum uti nos palatum habent.

Octavo, praeterea si anima humana esset immortalis, potens esset valde, sicut et sapiens et capiens; et sicut sapientia vincit omnia corpora, ita etiam potentia, cum istae primalitates sint unum. At homo non modo est pigrior et imbecillior sole et igne, sed etiam multis animalibus. Etenim leo, elephas, et equus exuperant viribus hominem; quin corvi et cervi diuturniorem vivunt vitam. Quod si anima hominis esset incorruptibilis, utique posset resistere infirmitatibus, et corpori dominari et praevalere, sicut invictura immortale mortali. Potentissimum enim est quod semper durat. At e contra videmus animas corporibus obrui et timere ab eis discedere, quasi diffidentes absque corpore vivere.

Nono, praeterea quacumque ex causa moritur homo sicut bestia, et solus cicutae succus ipsum occidit, replendo meatus quos intercurrit spiritus, vel extinguendo calorem. Si autem vivacissima esset mens immortalisque, statim has mortis occasiones reprimeret evaderetque.

Decimo, item ex laetitia nimia moritur homo, et ex vapore in antro Agnanae, et ex fumo carbonum in angusto cubiculo; et fluor[?] occludit nervos ne possit interlabi. Nec dicas spiritui id officere modo: nam anima illa divina est in spiritu, et suo robore ac sapientia tales molestias posset dissolvere.

Undecimo, praeterea levitas opinionum humanarum, in quibus mens non perseverat, sed quacumque ex causa mutatur in timorem, laetitiam, iram, dolorem, nec potest manere attenta negotio praefixo. Ego saepe cupio ante Deum preces attente proferre, et non possum, quoniam aliae cogitationes et aliae succedunt in animo meo, sicut motus in aqua vel in aere quacumque ex causa fluctuantes. Quod manifeste animae mutabilitatem aeream indicat, expertem illius immortalitatis quae entibus convenit immortalibus. Unde Lucretius ad hoc: Nam quodcumque suis mutatum finibus exit, continuo hoc est mors illius quod fuit ante.

Duodecimo, praeterea scientia est motus animae, in Physicis probavimus; et falsitas et mutabilitas opinionum indicant eius mutabilitatem. Adde furorem et insaniam et oblivionem, qui morbi rapiunt animam: quae si esset immortalis nequaquam raperetur a fuliginibus, caliditate, bile, et imaginatione forti, sed ista omnia vinceret.

Tertiodecimo, mirum praeterea quomodo in anima immortali recepta scientia potest deleri, et fieri oblivio et receptio[?], praesertim a tenui vapore vini, vel fuligine atrae bilis, vel calore attenuante spiritum, vel adventu alterius scientiae ac motionum.

Quartodecimo, praeterea idem ostendit instabilitas appetituum humanorum. Nunc eandem rem volumus, nunc odimus, nunc indignissima cupimus et obsequia, et quae nihil ad immortalem vitam pertinent, sed prorsus contraria sunt. Et omnes fatemur paucissimos homines ratione regi et sapientia, sed omnes sequi sensationem praesentem et voluptatem vilem.

Dolor quoque, qui mors est inchoative, potius animae quam corpori convenit insensato, et potius homini quam bruto. Ergo et mors. Videmus etiam aeris et caeli affectiones nobis communicari. Nam sub nubilo tristes sumus, sub sereno laeti, sub calido iracundi et fastidiosi, sub frigido autem crasso torpidi. Et astrologi ostendunt nos caelo subditos esse. Ergo frustra probavimus superiorem esse mentem caelo, elementis, et animalibus.

Quintodecimo, praeterea, si anima esset immortalis, reverteretur in corpus post mortem, daret nobis indicium. Et si quid a morientibus postulamus, obtineremus. Sed, ut ait Salomon: non est agnitus qui sit reversus ab inferis; sed spiritus noster dissolvitur tamquam mollis aer.

Sextodecimo, praeterea, si anima est immortalis, est prior corpore. Cum ergo intrat in corpus, in quo perdit scientiam etiam sui, ne dum aliarum rerum? Si a se ipsa, ut Virgilius ait, quoniam obliviscitur malorum praeteritorum in corpore, ergo in omne corpus intrat, quoniam nullius mala novit; et sic etiam inest in brutis. Si vero a Deo compellitur, mirum quomodo Deus nobilissimam mentem tetro corpori includat. Numquid ob peccatum, ut Origenes, quo peccavit in caelo? an quia delectant Deum mala nostra, ut Calvino convenit dicere? Et quomodo peccant apud superos, et cur, videndum esset.

Si vero creatur et immittitur corpori simul, ad quid creare ens nobilissimum et ignobilissimo claudere in corpore? Et cur id non recordamur? Et cur corporeae labes possunt incorpoream immortalemque mentem foedare et ignorantia sui obruere? Et cur in locum peccati animam non peccatricem deiicit Deus? Et cur anima crescit sapientia et viribus, sicut corpus crescit, et similiter decrescit in senectute, si divina est immortalisque? Videtur enim quod si habet ortum et augmentum corporeis simile, habere quoque mortem similiter, ut et Hippocrates docet.

## English

Than hens, and yet fly better, because they have better wings? The Brachmans today defend the same opinion, and the Talmudists in part, and formerly the Pharisees. And indeed they say that spirits vexing human beings are by name the souls of the dead, whence true transmigration of souls can seem to be present. Therefore either all souls are immortal and transmigrate, or all are mortal; and the human being exceeds the rest by organs, but not by power.

We read in histories and in sacred writings that beasts have often spoken with human voices, the ass of Balaam and the serpent. But because they do not have the same organs of voice as we do, we do not perceive their speech. For neither do we perceive [the speech] of Scythians. Nevertheless it is plain that dogs understand our language. And a Polish dog, brought to Naples, did not understand what was said to it in Neapolitan, but heard those speaking Polish and obeyed what was said. Parrots, however, imitate our language, because they have a hollow palate as we do.

Eighth, furthermore, if the human soul were immortal, it would be very powerful, as it is wise and capacious; and just as wisdom conquers all bodies, so too would power, since those primalities are one. But the human being is not only slower and weaker than the sun and fire, but also than many animals. For the lion, elephant, and horse surpass the human being in strength; indeed crows and deer live a longer life. But if the soul of the human being were incorruptible, surely it could resist infirmities, dominate the body, and prevail, as an immortal thing that will conquer over a mortal thing. For what always lasts is most powerful. But on the contrary we see souls overwhelmed by bodies and fearing to depart from them, as if mistrusting that they can live without a body.

Ninth, furthermore, the human being dies from any cause whatever just as a beast does, and the mere juice of hemlock kills him, by filling the passages through which the spirit runs, or by extinguishing heat. But if the mind were most living and immortal, it would immediately repress these occasions of death and escape them.

Tenth, likewise the human being dies from too much joy, from vapor in the cave of Agnano, and from the smoke of coals in a narrow room; and a flux[?] closes the nerves so that [the spirit] cannot slip through. Nor should you say that this harms only the spirit; for that divine soul is in the spirit, and by its strength and wisdom it could dissolve such troubles.

Eleventh, furthermore, [there is] the lightness of human opinions, in which the mind does not persevere, but from any cause whatever is changed into fear, joy, anger, grief, and cannot remain attentive to the task set before it. I often desire to utter prayers attentively before God, and cannot, because other thoughts, and still others, succeed in my mind, like motions in water or air fluctuating from any cause whatever. This clearly indicates the airy mutability of the soul, lacking that immortality which belongs to immortal beings. Hence Lucretius says for this point: for whatever goes out changed from its own limits, immediately this is the death of what it was before.

Twelfth, furthermore, we have proved in the Physics that knowledge is a motion of the soul; and falsity and mutability of opinions indicate its mutability. Add fury, insanity, and forgetfulness, which diseases seize the soul: if it were immortal, it would by no means be seized by sooty fumes, heat, bile, and strong imagination, but would conquer all those things.

Thirteenth, furthermore, it is strange how in an immortal soul received knowledge can be erased, and forgetfulness and reception[?] come about, especially from the thin vapor of wine, or the soot of black bile, or heat attenuating the spirit, or the arrival of another knowledge and other motions.

Fourteenth, furthermore, the instability of human appetites shows the same thing. Now we want the same thing, now we hate it, now we desire the most unworthy things and services, and things that have nothing to do with immortal life but are wholly contrary to it. And we all confess that very few human beings are ruled by reason and wisdom, but that all follow present sensation and base pleasure.

Pain too, which is death inchoatively, belongs more to the soul than to an insensate body, and more to the human being than to the beast. Therefore death does too. We also see that the affections of air and heaven are communicated to us. For under a cloudy sky we are sad, under a clear sky happy, under heat angry and disgusted, but under thick cold sluggish. And astrologers show that we are subject to heaven. Therefore we have proved in vain that the mind is superior to heaven, the elements, and animals.

Fifteenth, furthermore, if the soul were immortal, it would return into the body after death and give us a sign. And if we asked something from the dying, we would obtain it. But, as Solomon says: no one is known who has returned from the underworld; but our spirit is dissolved like soft air.

Sixteenth, furthermore, if the soul is immortal, it is prior to the body. Therefore when it enters the body, in which it loses knowledge even of itself, not to mention of other things? If [it enters] by itself, as Virgil says, because it forgets past evils in the body, then it enters every body, because it knows the evils of none; and so it is also present in beasts. But if it is compelled by God, it is strange how God encloses the noblest mind in a foul body. Is it because of sin, as Origen [says], by which it sinned in heaven? Or because our evils delight God, as it suits Calvin to say? And how souls sin among the higher beings, and why, would need to be seen.

But if it is created and sent into the body at the same time, to what end is the noblest being created and shut in the most ignoble body? And why do we not remember it? And why can bodily stains defile the incorporeal and immortal mind, and overwhelm it with ignorance of itself? And why does God cast a soul that has not sinned into a place of sin? And why does the soul grow in wisdom and powers as the body grows, and similarly decrease in old age, if it is divine and immortal? For it seems that if it has an origin and growth similar to bodily things, it likewise has death, as Hippocrates too teaches.

## Note

This corrected file preserves the continuation of the Pythagorean objection and objections eight through sixteen. The pressure falls on exactly the points the prior proofs used positively: unity of primalities, `sapientia` and `potentia`, hidden self-knowledge, bodily dependence, distraction in prayer, and the soul's supposed superiority to heaven and animals.
