## Latin

Pythagoras putat animas esse easdem brutorum et hominum, et transmigrare ex nobis ad illa; videri nos sapientiores quoniam organa corporis humani sunt meliora, et ideo melius operamur. Nonne videmus grues graciliores gallinis et tamen melius volare, quoniam meliores habent alas?

Saepe locuta esse vocibus humanis bruta legimus in historiis et in sacris, asinam Balaam et serpentem. Verum quia non habent organa vocis eadem nobiscum, nos eorum loquelam non percipimus. Canes linguam nostram intelligere palam est; Polonus canis delatus Neapolim non intelligebat quae sibi dicebantur Neapolitane, sed Polonice loquentes audiebat et obediebat dictis.

Si anima humana esset immortalis, potens esset valde sicut et sapiens et capiens. Sicut sapientia vincit omnia corpora, ita etiam potentia, cum istae primalitates sint unum. At homo est pigrior et imbecillior sole, igne, et multis animalibus; leones, elephantes, equi viribus hominem exuperant, et corvi cervique diuturniorem vivunt vitam.

Quacumque ex causa moritur homo sicut bestia. Solus cicutae succus ipsum occidit, replendo meatus quos intercurrit spiritus vel extinguendo calorem. Ex laetitia nimia moritur homo, ex vapore in antro Agnanae, ex fumo carbonum in angusto cubiculo. Si anima illa divina est in spiritu, suo robore ac sapientia tales molestias posset dissolvere.

Leuitas opinionum humanarum indicat mutabilitatem animae. Mens non perseverat; quacumque ex causa mutatur in timorem, laetitiam, iram, dolorem, nec potest manere attenta negotio praefixo. Ego saepe cupio ante Deum preces attente proferre et non possum, quoniam aliae cogitationes succedunt in animo meo sicut motus in aqua vel aere fluctuantes.

Scientia est motus animae; falsitas et mutabilitas opinionum indicant eius mutabilitatem. Adde furorem, insaniam, oblivionem, qui morbi rapiunt animam. Si esset immortalis, nequaquam raperetur a fuliginibus, caliditate, bile, et imaginatione forti.

Instabilitas appetituum idem ostendit. Nunc eandem rem volumus, nunc odimus; nunc indignissima cupimus. Pauci homines ratione et sapientia regi fatentur, sed omnes sequuntur sensationem praesentem et voluptatem vilem. Aeris et caeli affectiones nobis communicantur: sub nubilo tristes sumus, sub sereno laeti, sub calido iracundi, sub frigido torpidi.

Si anima est immortalis, cur non reverteretur in corpus post mortem et daret indicium? Si est prior corpore, cur intrat corpus in quo perdit scientiam etiam sui? Si creatur et immittitur corpori simul, ad quid creare ens nobilissimum et claudere in corpore ignobilissimo?

## English

The objections continue by pushing toward transmigration or parity between human and animal souls. Pythagoras says the souls of humans and beasts are the same and migrate between bodies; humans seem wiser only because their organs are better. Stories of Balaam's ass and the serpent, or human-speaking animals, suggest that animal speech may simply be inaccessible to us because their vocal organs differ. A Polish dog brought to Naples obeyed Polish but not Neapolitan.

Human weakness also counts against immortality. If human soul were immortal, powerful, wise, and capacious, its power should match its wisdom because the primalities are one. Yet humans are weaker than the sun, fire, lions, elephants, horses; they live shorter lives than crows and deer. Hemlock, vapors, smoke, joy, and blocked passages kill them like beasts.

Mental instability seems still worse. The mind changes with fear, joy, anger, grief, and distraction. Campanella gives himself as evidence: he often wants to pray attentively before God and cannot, because other thoughts flow in like movements in water or air. Madness, insanity, forgetfulness, bile, heat, soot, and strong imagination seize the soul.

Mutable appetites, vile pleasures, weather, and celestial influences all suggest bodily dependence. If the soul were immortal, why does it not return after death and give signs? If it preexists body, why does entering a body make it forget even itself? If God creates it with the body, why enclose such a noble being in such an ignoble prison?

## Note

The objections are not straw men. They strike the exact points Campanella has used positively: primality unity, animal cognition, hidden self-knowledge, religious attention, and superiority to the heavens. The personal prayer example is especially useful because it turns the immortality proof's inner confidence into an observed instability.
