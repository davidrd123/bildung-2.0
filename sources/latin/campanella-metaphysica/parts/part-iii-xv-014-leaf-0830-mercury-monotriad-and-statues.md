## Latin

Alibi tres ponit Deos, ipsum scilicet Bonum, autorem Mundi per essentiam Deum: ad cuius imaginem factus est Mundus, secundus Deus, omnia in se comprehendens Entia, sicuti Deus primus Verbo, sunt ubi omnia quae facta sunt vita. Item circularem hunc Deum absque principio et fine, et dum movetur stat, sicut Deus, et dum stat, movetur, sicut Deus; qui evectis mobilibus est mobilior, et immotus stans, dat cuncta moveri, et immutabilis mutari.

Tertium Deum dicit esse hominem, perfectam imaginem secundi Dei. Nihil enim est in Mundo, quod in homine non sit: secundum vero imaginem primi Dei: prior enim est mundus homine, et mundus constat intellectibus et corporibus, et homo sua mente, et corporibus, et qualitatibus, et quantitatibus, et coloribus, et motibus, et quietibus, et elementis et caelis, quae sunt in mundo. Proptereaque omnes scientias addidit, quibus caelum et terram, et omnia metitur, et Angelos sentit et Deum, et canit Deum cum illis, eorumque gaudet consortio.

Idcirco, regulam, ad cuius praescriptum hominis vita ducenda est, putat esse Religionem: per quam scimus omnia, et divinum numen, autorem omnium: et colimus et gratias agimus et meliorationem nostri petimus. Unde et aliam vitam praesentibus miseriis vacuam, sperare licet, a Deoque petere.

Propterea enim Deus, nec Mundum malitia, quam ab Hyle putat oriri, ut ex ferro rubigo, non ex ferrario, privat, ut incentiva sint ad boni adeptionem, et exercitamentum. Deinde neminem posse Deo iungi, ac deificari, nisi per regenerationem. Quam silentio et contemplatione divinorum, et despectu corporeorum, fieri decet: Autoremque regenerationis vocat Dei filium verbum, hominem Dei voluntate. Tunc regeneratum hominem duodecim ultricibus vitiis spoliari per decem virtutes, totum Deo inhaerere, fierique unum, spiritu, et laudibus eius inebriari. Corpus autem non putat ita deificari, sed mentem.

Circa gubernatum docet nobiscum non modo Deum ita copulari, sed per daemones nobiscum agi, et per Heroas, quas in sublimiori locat aere, ubi nebulae et venti nostri sunt. Praeterea ad divinitatem hominis declarandam, docet, quod sicuti Deus ad sui similitudinem creavit Deos caelestes, Mundus animalia sensibilia ex corpore, et spiritu mundano, ita homo ad sui imaginem etiam facit, sicuti moris erat eorum, qui statuas loquentes, et responsa dantes in Aegypto constituebant, et passim in toto sit Gentilismo.

Ex corporibus enim mundanis statuas similes sibi faciunt; et quoniam animas dare non possunt, evocant animas daemonum, vel Angelorum, easque includunt imaginibus et statuis: quae et benefaciendi, et malefaciendi ex inclusis numinibus sortiuntur potestatem; et qui in hoc claruerunt, etiam in statuis habent domicilium et virtutem, ut Aesculapius, Mercurius, Isis, Osiris. Quorum etiam irasci est, nedum propitiari: Posse autem irasci, quoniam Dii factitii sunt extra Naturam facti.

Praeterea illiciuntur divinis corporalibus rebus, symbolum habentibus cum eis: sunt enim lapides et plantae et metalla, aromata, et harmonia, cantusque vim divinam habentes; per quas sub certis constellationibus alliciuntur daemones in statuas, et animat. Et sicut Dii caelestes caelum complent, quisque in suo ordine; ita nostri Dii Terreni sortibus et divinationibus nos gubernant, et providentia, et amica cognitione auxiliantur. Quam sententiam una cum Platonicorum decretis examinandam, postmodum moramur.

Examen sententiae Mercurii de summa monotriade, habitum in 1. p. repetitur per collationem ad sententiam Iamblichi, et Procli Platonicorum. Similiter de productione materiae, et quomodo melius senserit Mercurius.

**ARTIC. II.**

Recte quidem Mercurius Deum creatorem, et gubernatorem rerum omnium agnoscit, eundemque ob primalitates, Trinum, et unum ex sanctiorum Patrum, credo, traditione, vel revelatione sibi facta caelitus, ut ipse fatetur. Nam et Patrem et verbum eius filium, et spiritum tertiam mentem, simulque Deum, celebrat. Iamblichus de mysteriis Aegyptiorum sermonem faciens contestatur huic sententiae, sed cum humana ratione ipsa exponeret, Deum unum in Trinitate non tam belle explicat. Quamquam Deum autorem, et verbum suum vocat Deum sui Patrem, et sui filium; tacet autem de spiritu, quem potius cum Mundi anima confundit, ob primalitatum ignorantiam.

Praeterea dicit Verbum Dei effigiem, et opificem Intellectum sortiri multa nomina. Quatenus enim explicat ideas in Creaturis, dicitur [Amun?][?]; quatenus artificiose, et cum veritate perficit omnia, vocatur Phtha, Aegyptiis, et Vulcanus Graecis. Quatenus effector bonorum vocatur Osiris, ut etiam Seneca interpretatur. Sed profecto Mercurius secundorum Deorum, et tertiorum facit has appellationes.

Melius Proclus unum super Ens primo loco ordinat. Secundo unum Ens, id est, intellectus intelligibilis; tertio intellectus intelligibilis, et intellectualis simul, et expressio prima Iethon; quarto Intellectus intellectualis Emeph, Dux caelestium; quinto intellectus opifex, Dux Opificum mundanorum, qui sunt Dii caelestes, Empyrei, et Aetherei. Hic forsitan est intellectus animae Mundi, vocatus [Arnum?][?], Phtha, Vulcanus, Osiris. ## English

Elsewhere he posits three Gods, namely the Good itself, the author of the World, God by essence; according to whose image the World was made, the second God, comprehending in itself all Beings, just as God the first [comprehends them] in the Word, where all things that were made are life. Likewise [he posits] this circular God without beginning and end, and while it is moved, it stands, like God, and while it stands, it is moved, like God; being more mobile than moving things, and standing unmoved, it gives all things to be moved, and being immutable, [gives all things] to be changed.

He says the third God is the human being, the perfect image of the second God. For there is nothing in the World that is not in the human being, but [the human being is] according to the image of the first God. For the world is prior to the human being, and the world consists of intellects and bodies; and the human being [consists] of his mind, bodies, qualities, quantities, colors, motions, rests, elements, and heavens, which are in the world. For this reason he added all the sciences by which he measures heaven, earth, and all things, senses Angels and God, sings God with them, and rejoices in their fellowship.

Therefore he thinks Religion is the rule according to whose prescription the life of the human being must be led: through it we know all things and the divine divinity, the author of all things; and we worship, give thanks, and seek our improvement. Whence it is permitted to hope for another life free from present miseries, and to seek it from God.

For this reason God does not deprive the World of evil, which he thinks arises from Hyle, as rust from iron, not from the ironsmith, so that there may be incentives to the obtaining of good and exercise. Then [he says] that no one can be joined to God and deified except through regeneration. This ought to happen by silence, contemplation of divine things, and contempt of corporeal things; and he calls the author of regeneration the Word, Son of God, human being by the will of God. Then the regenerated human being, stripped of twelve avenging vices through ten virtues, adheres wholly to God, becomes one, and is made drunk with his spirit and praises. He does not think the body is deified in this way, but the mind.

Concerning governance, he teaches that not only God is thus coupled with us, but that action is carried on with us through demons and through Heroes, whom he locates in the more sublime air, where our clouds and winds are. Moreover, to declare the divinity of the human being, he teaches that just as God created celestial Gods according to his likeness, and the World [created] sensible animals from body and worldly spirit, so the human being also makes [things] according to his own image, as was the custom of those who established speaking statues giving responses in Egypt, and everywhere in the whole of Gentilism.

For from worldly bodies they make statues similar to themselves; and since they cannot give souls, they call forth souls of demons or Angels and include them in images and statues, which obtain from the included divinities the power of doing good and doing evil. And those who were famous in this also have dwelling and virtue in statues, such as Aesculapius, Mercury, Isis, Osiris. They can also grow angry, not only be propitiated; and they can grow angry because they are factitious Gods made outside Nature.

Moreover they are enticed by divine corporeal things having a symbol with them: for stones, plants, metals, aromatics, harmony, and song have divine force. Through these, under certain constellations, demons are enticed into statues and animate [them]. And just as heavenly Gods fill heaven, each in his own order, so our Earthly Gods govern us by lots and divinations, and aid us by providence and friendly cognition. This opinion, together with the decrees of the Platonists, we delay to be examined later.

The examination of Mercury's opinion concerning the supreme monotriad, held in Part 1, is repeated through comparison with the opinion of Iamblichus and Proclus the Platonists. Likewise concerning the production of matter, and how Mercury thought better.

**ARTICLE II.**

Mercury indeed rightly recognizes God as creator and governor of all things, and the same as Three and one because of the primalities, from, I believe, a tradition of the holier Fathers or from revelation made to him from heaven, as he himself confesses. For he celebrates Father, his Word as Son, and Spirit as the third mind, and at the same time God. Iamblichus, speaking concerning the mysteries of the Egyptians, bears witness to this opinion, but when he explained it by human reason, he did not explain one God in Trinity so beautifully. Although he calls God the author and his Word God, Father of himself and Son of himself, he is silent about the Spirit, whom he rather confuses with the soul of the World because of ignorance of the primalities.

Moreover he says that the Word of God, the image and worker Intellect, obtains many names. Insofar as it unfolds ideas in Creatures, it is called [Amun?][?]; insofar as it artfully and truly perfects all things, it is called Phtha by the Egyptians, and Vulcan by the Greeks. Insofar as it is the maker of goods it is called Osiris, as Seneca also interprets. But certainly Mercury gives these appellations to the second and third Gods.

Proclus better orders the one above Being in the first place; second, the one Being, that is, intelligible intellect; third, intellect both intelligible and intellectual, and the first expression, Iethon; fourth, intellectual Intellect, Emeph, Leader of celestial things; fifth, worker intellect, Leader of the worldly Workers, who are the celestial, Empyrean, and Aetherial Gods. This perhaps is the intellect of the soul of the World, called [Arnum?][?], Phtha, Vulcan, Osiris.
