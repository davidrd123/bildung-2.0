## Latin

Appendix ad idem.

Quod autem bruta non pertineant ad vitam supercaelestem quam homo expectat post mortem patet, quoniam ipsa non sunt propter se, sed propter hominem. Alioquin et herbae ad vitam illam ordinatae essent, et mundus totus. Quod quidem concedimus ex parte, quoniam liberabitur a servitute corruptionis et innovabitur propter nos. Animalia autem, lapides, et concretiones non remanebunt, quoniam spectant ad corruptionem, et mixturam, et usum propter hominem. Quod autem est propter aliud non remanet in aeternum, sed expleto usu perditur et mutatur in aliud in quo est usui, sicut dictum est in primo libro tertiae partis et alibi ostendimus.

Quod si omnia quae fuerunt in mundo animalia instauranda essent ad immortalitatem ex vi animae immortalis, tunc oporteret totam terram in ipsorum corpora converti, et forte nec sufficeret. Argumentum illud, quod si fieret transmutatio, vel de caelo delapsus in corpora, tunc recordaremur quae in aliis corporibus vel caelo sciebamus, non attulit[?], quoniam nullum est; nam et nunc obliviscimur, et quanto magis in illa mutatione maxima quae fit in corporatione animae. Quapropter videtur fabulas finxisse Pythagoras ad populum, quod ipse meminerit se fuisse Euphorbium, et Ennius se fuisse Homerum. Non enim iuxta solitum Naturae cursum haec recordatio possibilis est; et quae Lucianus fingit ridicula sunt. Ex his soluta remanent [suo modo?] priora argumenta.

**Responsio ad reliqua argumenta contra praestantiam et immortalitatem animorum, et confutatio. Articulus IV.**

Ad octavum dicimus immortalitatem animae non a robore virium corporatarum dependere, sed ex eo quod non habet contrarium et constituitur ex primalitatibus non contractis. Potest autem quod contractum est robustius esse non contracto, non autem purius, ut aqua facta brodio[?], et liquor contractus in vinum, et ignis in ferro. Sic potentior est corporaliter leonis anima contracta ad igneam naturam quam humana remanens in primalitatibus. Sed mirum quomodo castigata [reriusaque?][?] mixta elementata sint robustiora elementis; sed quidem non toto elemento sed parte. Mirum est, ut ferrum ignitum potentius est aliquo calore, sed non tota caloris natura.

Anima autem hominis est participatio Entis primi quaedam, et potest ab aliquo Ente contracto viribus vinci, non autem puritate. Idcirco mens humana purius intelligit quam quaelibet brutorum anima, et limpidius, et longe plura Entia et diviniora, quia non est contracta ad hoc. Sicut enim oculus croceus omnia crocea videt, at purus omnes colores, ne dum croceum, sic est anima humana secundum suam Entitatem comparata animabus brutorum corporatis contractisque ad tenuitatem calidam.

Nec sola scientia, sed etiam potentia animae humanae extenditur ad plura quam potentia ignis et leonis, quoniam ad miracula facienda, ad artificia fabricanda, ad Palatia, ad leges, ad virtutis opera plurima, quae ceteris rebus, licet robustioribus robore contracto, impossibilia sunt.

Item adeo potens est mens, ut tormentis corporis et spiritus nullis cedat, nec possit cogi dicere quod non vult. Quod non solum sanctis viris accidit, sed omnibus suum honorem colentibus. Equidem quadraginta horis ego atrocissime tortus respondere iudicibus ad mortem me adigentibus nolui. De Attilio et Scaevola et aliis vide exemplar fortitudinis, omnem leoninam et caelestem superantes.

Tandem mens humana non per se operatur in corpore, sed per spiritum, qui robustior est in leone quam in nobis, ut noscamus divinitatem hominis non ab igne, quo leo abundat, sed a Deo participare, et in primalitatibus et arbitrio libero residere.

Nec quia facile moritur homo, et prius quam animalia, sit inferior. Homo enim constat corpore, spiritu, et mente, ut ait Augustinus. Spiritus autem tenuis est, calidusque subsistentia ex sanguine nutrita, quoniam continuo exhalat ad cognatum caelum, propria educta calore. Corpus vero plurimis gracilibusque organis et vasis est compositum; propterea quocumque disrupto vase in quo spiritus inhabitat, vel ipso spiritu suffocato aut elabente, moritur homo, quoniam mens regit corpus per spiritum, eique unitur hoc vinculo utrique simili. Quo abeunte aut perempto, ipsa necessario abit; non enim potest amplius illud regere.

Ergo eadem ratione moritur homo qua cetera animalia; spiritu enim movetur et vivificatur, mente vero fit studiosus, religiosus, sapiens super animalia. Et profecto si ipsa mens discederet sola, non moreretur homo, sed quod remaneret esset animal, non homo, veluti est ante animae infusionem. Quod utrum alicui umquam evenerit nescimus. Daijthes[?] enim fabulatur [officiosae?][?]; sed furiosis suspicamur id accidere. At cum saepe per Medicinam sanentur, non videtur mens discessisse.

Neque diuturnitas vitae compositi indicat minime immortalitatem, sed praestantia mentis. Alioquin melius etiam ferrum auro, et lapis planta[?]. Immo quia alterius ordinis est mens, facilius recedit quam spiritus corpori similior.

Timor mortis est in homine incerto futurae vitae, quoniam anima corpori immissa cadit in oblivionem sui et caloris[?]. Dubitat autem utrum post mortem alia remaneat, quoniam ipsa non novit se, nisi abdita notitia; ad quam cum revertitur, statim incipit velle dissolvi corpus, ut Platonici multi, et Apostoli Christi, et sancti viri aliam intelligentes vitam post hanc, ad illam contendunt. At et bruta ipsa timent mori, et tamen eorum spiritus melius vivit in aere quam in corpore opaco; et hoc, quia nesciunt quae sibi restat vita. Et hoc voluit Deus, ne entia vellent mori antequam usui praedestinato subserviant, ut dictum est. Multos vidi remigantes et captivos in carcere diuturno nolle exire in libertatem, quoniam meliorem ignorant aut diffidunt vivere vitam.

Consuetudo enim in naturam transit. Et sub tyrannide assueti vivere amant tyrannum et Rempublicam liberam oderunt; remigio brachiorum, Arcturum-

## English

Appendix to the same.

But that beasts do not pertain to the supercelestial life which the human being expects after death is clear, because they are not for themselves, but for the human being. Otherwise plants too would be ordered to that life, and the whole world. This indeed we concede in part, since it will be freed from the servitude of corruption and renewed for our sake. But animals, stones, and concretions will not remain, because they look toward corruption, mixture, and use for the human being. What exists for the sake of another does not remain eternally, but when its use has been completed it is lost and changed into something else in which it is useful, as was said in the first book of the third part and as we have shown elsewhere.

But if all animals that have existed in the world had to be restored to immortality by the power of an immortal soul, then the whole earth would have to be turned into their bodies, and perhaps it would not suffice. That argument, that if transmutation happened, or a fall from heaven into bodies, then we would remember what we knew in other bodies or in heaven, has not carried weight[?], because it is nothing; for even now we forget, and how much more in that greatest change which happens in the incorporation of the soul. For this reason Pythagoras seems to have fashioned tales for the people, that he himself remembered that he had been Euphorbus and that Ennius had been Homer. For this recollection is not possible according to the usual course of Nature; and the things Lucian imagines are ridiculous. From these points the former arguments remain solved [in their way?].

**Response to the remaining arguments against the excellence and immortality of minds, and refutation. Article IV.**

To the eighth we say that the immortality of the soul does not depend on the strength of embodied powers, but on the fact that it has no contrary and is constituted from uncontracted primalities. But what is contracted can be more robust than what is not contracted, though not purer, as water made into broth[?], liquor contracted into wine, and fire in iron. So the soul of a lion, contracted to fiery nature, is more powerful corporeally than the human soul remaining in the primalities. But it is strange how purified [and rarefied?][?] mixed, elemented things are more robust than the elements; yet not than the whole element but than part. It is strange, as ignited iron is more powerful than some heat, but not than the whole nature of heat.

But the soul of the human being is a certain participation in the first Being, and can be conquered in strength by some contracted Being, but not in purity. Therefore the human mind understands more purely than any soul of beasts, and more clearly, and far more beings and more divine ones, because it is not contracted to this. For just as a saffron-colored eye sees all things as saffron, but a pure eye sees all colors, not only saffron, so is the human soul according to its Entity compared with the souls of beasts embodied and contracted to hot subtlety.

Not only the science, but also the power of the human soul extends to more things than the power of fire and of the lion, since it extends to making miracles, fabricating arts, palaces, laws, and very many works of virtue, which are impossible for other things, although they are stronger with contracted strength.

Likewise the mind is so powerful that it yields to no torments of body and spirit, nor can it be compelled to say what it does not want. This happens not only to holy men, but to all who cultivate their honor. Indeed I, tortured most atrociously for forty hours, refused to answer the judges who were driving me to death. Concerning Attilius, Scaevola, and others, see the example of fortitude surpassing every leonine and celestial [fortitude].

Finally, the human mind does not operate through itself in the body, but through spirit, which is more robust in the lion than in us, so that we may know that the divinity of the human being participates not from fire, in which the lion abounds, but from God, and resides in the primalities and free choice.

Nor, because the human being dies easily and sooner than animals, is he inferior. For the human being consists of body, spirit, and mind, as Augustine says. But spirit is subtle and hot, a subsistence nourished from blood, since it continually exhales toward its kindred heaven, drawn out by its own heat. But the body is composed of very many delicate organs and vessels; therefore, when any vessel in which the spirit inhabits is broken, or the spirit itself suffocated or escaping, the human being dies, because the mind governs the body through spirit, and is united to it by this bond similar to each. When this goes away or is destroyed, the mind necessarily goes away; for it can no longer govern the body.

Therefore the human being dies by the same reason as the other animals; for he is moved and vivified by spirit, but by mind he becomes studious, religious, wise above animals. And certainly if the mind itself alone departed, the human being would not die, but what remained would be an animal, not a human being, as it is before the infusion of the soul. Whether this has ever happened to anyone we do not know. For Daijthes[?] tells an [officious?][?] tale; but we suspect that it happens to the mad. Yet since they are often healed by Medicine, the mind does not seem to have departed.

Nor does the duration of the life of the composite indicate immortality least of all, but rather the excellence of the mind [does]. Otherwise iron would be better than gold, and stone than plant[?]. Indeed, because mind is of another order, it withdraws more easily than spirit, which is more similar to the body.

Fear of death is in the human being uncertain of future life, because the soul sent into the body falls into forgetfulness of itself and of heat[?]. But it doubts whether another life remains after death, because it does not know itself except by hidden knowledge; when it returns to this, it immediately begins to want the body to be dissolved, as many Platonists, the Apostles of Christ, and holy men, understanding another life after this one, strive toward it. And beasts themselves too fear to die, and nevertheless their spirit lives better in air than in an opaque body; and this [happens] because they do not know what life remains for them. And God willed this so that beings would not want to die before they serve the use predestined for them, as was said. I have seen many rowers and captives in long imprisonment not want to go out into freedom, because they do not know the better life or mistrust that they can live it.

For custom passes into nature. And those accustomed to live under tyranny love the tyrant and hate the free Republic; [accustomed] to the rowing of their arms, Arcturus-

## Note

This corrected file keeps the appendix and the opening of Article IV together. The central distinction is robustness versus purity: animal or elemental powers may be stronger in contracted bodily force, but human mind is purer because it remains in uncontracted primalities and free choice.
