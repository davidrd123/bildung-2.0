# Part III 018: Immortality Is Not Bodily Force

## Latin

Quod bruta non pertineant ad vitam supercaelestem quam homo expectat post mortem patet, quoniam ipsa non sunt propter se, sed propter hominem. Alioquin et herbae ad vitam illam ordinatae essent et mundus totus. Concedimus ex parte mundum liberandum a servitute corruptionis et innovandum propter nos; animalia autem, lapides, et concretiones non remanebunt.

Si omnia quae fuerunt in mundo animalia instauranda essent ad immortalitatem ex vi animae immortalis, oporteret totam terram in ipsorum corpora converti, et forte nec sufficeret. Argumentum de memoria transmigrationis nihil est; nunc quoque obliviscimur, quanto magis in mutatione maxima quae fit in incorporatione animae.

**Responsio ad reliqua argumenta contra praestantiam et immortalitatem animorum. Articulus IV.**

Diximus immortalitatem animae non a robore virium corporatarum dependere, sed ex eo quod non habet contrarium et constituitur ex primalitatibus non contractis. Potest quod contractum est robustius esse non contracto, non autem purius. Potentior est corporaliter leonis anima contracta ad igneam naturam quam humana remanens in primalitatibus.

Anima hominis est participatio Entis primi quaedam, et potest ab aliquo ente contracto viribus vinci, non autem puritate. Mens humana purius intelligit quam quaelibet brutorum anima, et longe plura entia ac diviniora, quia non est contracta ad hoc. Sicut oculus croceus omnia crocea videt, at purus omnes colores, sic est anima humana comparata animabus brutorum contractis ad tenuitatem calidam.

Nec sola scientia, sed etiam potentia animae humanae extenditur ad plura quam potentia ignis et leonis: ad miracula facienda, artificia fabricanda, palatia, leges, virtutis opera plurima.

Adeo potens est mens ut tormentis corporis et spiritus nullis cedat, nec possit cogi dicere quod non vult. Equidem quadraginta horis ego atrocissime tortus respondere iudicibus ad mortem me adigentibus nolui. De Attilio et Scaevola vide exemplar fortitudinis, omnem leoninam et caelestem superantes.

Mens humana non per se operatur in corpore, sed per spiritum, qui robustior est in leone quam in nobis. Eadem ratione moritur homo qua cetera animalia: spiritu movetur et vivificatur, mente vero fit studiosus, religiosus, sapiens super animalia. Timor mortis est in homine incerto futurae vitae et quia anima corpori immissa cadit in oblivionem sui et caloris.

## English

Brutes do not belong to the supercelestial life humans expect after death, because they exist for humans, not for themselves. If every animal that had ever lived had to be restored by an immortal soul, the whole earth would have to become their bodies and perhaps would not suffice. Memory arguments for transmigration are weak; we forget now, and would forget still more in the greatest change of embodiment.

Campanella then answers the objection from bodily weakness. Immortality does not depend on bodily force but on lacking contrary and being constituted by uncontracted primalities. A contracted thing may be stronger bodily than an uncontracted one, but not purer. A lion's soul contracted to fiery nature may be bodily stronger than the human soul, while human mind remains purer.

Human mind is a participation in the first Being. It can be overcome in bodily force by contracted beings, but not in purity. Like a pure eye receiving all colors while a saffron-colored eye sees everything saffron, human soul understands more beings and more divine beings because it is not contracted to one bodily range.

Human power also reaches farther than brute force: miracles, arts, palaces, laws, and virtues. Its strength appears under torture. Campanella names his own forty hours of torture, during which he refused to answer judges pressing him toward death. Bodily death happens as in animals because mind governs body through spirit; when the spirit's instrument fails, the mind departs.

## Note

The distinction between robustness and purity is load-bearing. Human immortality is not disproved by animal strength or longer life because the proof concerns uncontracted primalities, not bodily power. The torture passage is a major autobiographical anchor inside the metaphysics.
