## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] exhalantem ex corpore animalis, nisi per discursum, sicut seipsam. Ergo de illorum est natura. Cumque ipsa sit substantia, ens, non perciperet substantias. Sed profecto intelligitur recipiens denudatum a natura recepti, secundum id quod recipit, non secundum totum esse.

Oculus quippe albus est, sed albedine simplicissima, qualis inest diaphanis rebus a calore albo devictis[?]; albedo autem quovis colore tingitur propter suam simplicitatem; similiter et lux. Et tamen lux color est[?]. Potest ergo quod recipit non esse id quod recipit in eo gradu, sed in alio simpliciori. Oculus croceus omnia crocea videret, quoniam croceitas participaret opacum, quod tingit omnia seipso; similiter bilis amaras reddit escas. Sed tamen caro insipida est nobis nostra, non autem cani vel pisci; et quia insipida, sapores excedentes ipsam sentit, non autem infra; sicut robur nostrum non sentit pondera formicarum et apis, nisi ad unciam pertingat. Nobis leve putatur; tamen videmus deorsum cadere grana et drachmas, quoniam gravia sunt.

Sensus igitur noster corporeus est in mediocritate virium, et percipit ea, quae nos excedunt et movent. Cetera vero discursu a simili: qui autem simile novit, is est idem qui discurrit ad ignotum, virtus videlicet sentiens.

Quoniam vero Aristoteles non cognovit quid deferat sensibilia ad sentientem animam, et quid eas[?] de materia abstrahit, neque hoc docuit, idcirco debuisset ponere sensum agentem, sicut posuit intellectum, et longe magis. Quem cum reprobent alii, propterea quod ipsa sensibilia sunt activa et possunt movere sensum, et non doceant quo pacto lapidis forma veniat ad oculum - ipsa enim non deserit proprium subiectum - inertiae condemnandi sunt. Sed nos lucem tingi vultibus rerum docuimus, et in oculum insilire, et manifestare res, quibus est tincta. Odores vero esse tenuem substantiam, ab exhalante calore delatam; sonum esse motum per aerem communicatum; idcirco neque intellectus agens poni debet.

Ut enim abstrahatur ab individuis simulachrum, non requiritur opus, sed languor operis. Movent enim sensibilia particularia sensum; et quia particularitates sunt paucae, id vero in quo conveniunt est multum, quia in omnibus est, idcirco remanet in sentiente virtute ipsum universale potius quam particularia. Et in absentia videtur sentire, post modum esse universale abstractum nosse[?]. Et hoc stulte tribuitur intellectui agenti, quod languiditatis est effectus. A longe enim video hominem, et non Petrum, quoniam Petri singularitas, ut facies et color, non possunt usque ad me suas vires extendere; motus vero et figura humana extendunt, quoniam ex aliis ea nota fuere mihi, et plus movent, et passiones inditas ergo plus et facilius excitant.

Nec propterea dixeris esse virtutem in sensu, quae abstrahat hominem a Petro, sed imbecillitatem et paucitatem particularitatum non se satis ostentare. Cognosco hanc esse aleam, sed non tanti valoris in ludo, quoniam illud commune habet cum multis, et in multis novi; hoc vero particulare solum in ipsa. Sed lusor bene ipsam agnoscit, qui particularia notare didicit. Ergo abstractio est vana. Nam et oculus videt colorem non viso sapore; ergo etiam ipse abstrahit. Et quare? Quia non est aptus pati a sapore. Sic sensus distans, quem vocant intellectum, ex eo quod distat, moveri nequit a distantibus. Similitudines autem super similitudines intelligere, opus est ratiocinantis sensus, ut dictum est. Nam sicut ex particulari ad aliud particulare discurrit, sic ex universali ad aliud universale.

Cum aiunt, intellectum non posse moveri a phantasmate nisi illustretur ab agente, falluntur. Quoniam si intellectus est forma corporis, erit etiam organi, in quo est phantasia; ergo affecta phantasia, afficitur forma, in qua est ipsa. Distinctio enim cellularum vana est. Sic etiam affecto oculo, afficitur anima intellectiva, quae ipsum informat. Si vero anima non est forma, sed spiritus, tunc in eo fiunt sensationes et imaginationes, et non relinquitur aliqua illustratio, nisi divina omnibus in rebus eminenter inexistens. Sed de his alibi.

Quapropter rationes Peripateticorum, quibus probant animae immortalitatem, vanissimae sunt, et sibi mutuo adversae. Non enim probant substantialitatem[?] animae, dum eam ponunt ut formam. Qui vero ponunt ipsam abstractam a corpore, non faciunt hominem intelligere, sed intelligi, ut Avicenna etiam novit. Qui autem formam corporis, non declarant quomodo ex elementis inanimatis fiat anima. Qui autem dicunt esse formam secundum essentiam, non autem secundum potentias, non probant quod volunt. Nam potentia non potest ab essentia separari, nisi cogitatu nostro. Si ergo essentia est forma corporis, utique et potentiae; sive enim potentia sit ipsa anima potens, sive quid ab ea effluens, cum extra corpus non effluat, non potest corporis fieri expers. Et mirum quo pacto eadem res sit activa et passiva forma. Aristoteles semper negat in eodem reperiri actum et potentiam. Neque enim semen fieri materiam situs[?] concedit propterea quod est agens; et nunc quomodo eadem anima est patiens et agens intellectionem?

Item si patitur cum sit forma corporis, patitur simul corpus receptionem specierum immaterialium. Nam et ipsa luce, a corpore non dependente, affecta, afficitur corpus aeris et oculi, in quo ipsa recipitur. Ergo imperite faciunt ipsam immaterialem, ut suscipiat universalia. Speculum quoque suscipit imaginem rei et hominis. Et quidem a longe positum non repraesentat Petrum, sed hominem. Et sic est sensus et intellectus Aristotelicus.

Ratio ergo Aristotelis, qua probat animae immortalitatem, nulla est. Si enim, inquit, propriam habeat operationem, in qua corpus non communicat, contingit ipsam separari sicut incorruptibile a corruptibili; quod falsum est. Nam neque nauta est incorruptibilis, qui propriam habet operationem a navi distinctam; neque spiritus equinus. Spiritus enim videt in oculo, et non videt ipse oculus, sed quasi fenestra eius est; et tamen discedit in aerem, qui corruptibilis est. Sequitur ergo quod sit separabilis, sed non quod incorruptibilis.

Praeterea falsum est in doctrina Aristotelis, quod anima propriam habet operationem, in qua corpus non communicat. Ipsi enim intelligere est pati; forma autem non patitur, nisi compatiatur subiectum. Item anima intelligendo laborat, ut patet. Item utitur phantasmate intelligendo. Neque enim ipsum universale potest anima intelligere secundum Aristotelem, nisi in phantasmate inspiciat particularia, non modo quando primum intelligit aliquid, sed etiam postquam illius habet scientiam in habitu; quandocunque vult intelligere, respicit phantasmata, ut et Avicenna asserit. Ergo non est absque organo. Et licet dicat phantasmata concurrere tum ut obiectum: hoc verum esset quando primum sentit seu intelligit aliquid; sed postquam species intelligibilis est in intellectu, non potest intellectus, quando vult intelligere, nisi phantasmata speculetur.

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] exhaling from the body of an animal, except by discourse, as it knows itself. Therefore it is of their nature. And since it is itself substance, being, it would not perceive substances. But in fact the receiver is understood as stripped from the nature of the thing received, according to that which it receives, not according to the whole being.

For the eye is white, but with a most simple whiteness, such as exists in transparent things overcome by white heat[?]; but whiteness is tinted by any color because of its simplicity; likewise light too. And yet light is color[?]. Therefore what receives need not be what it receives in that grade, but in another, simpler one. A saffron-colored eye would see all things as saffron, because saffronness would participate in the opaque, which tints all things with itself; similarly bile makes foods bitter. Yet our own flesh is tasteless to us, though not to a dog or fish; and because it is tasteless, it senses flavors that exceed it, but not those beneath it. So our strength does not sense the weights of ants and bees unless it reaches an ounce. To us it is thought light; yet we see grains and drachmas fall downward, because they are heavy.

Therefore our bodily sense is in a middle range of powers, and perceives the things which exceed and move us. But the rest it perceives by discourse from the similar; and the one who knows the similar is the same one who discourses to the unknown, namely the sensing power.

But because Aristotle did not know what carries sensibles to the sensing soul, and what abstracts them[?] from matter, nor did he teach this, he therefore ought to have posited an agent sense, just as he posited an intellect, and much more so. Since others reject this because the sensibles themselves are active and can move sense, and yet do not teach how the form of the stone comes to the eye - for it does not leave its own subject - they must be condemned for idleness. But we have taught that light is tinted by the faces of things, leaps into the eye, and manifests the things by which it is tinted; that odors are a thin substance carried by exhaling heat; that sound is motion communicated through air. Therefore no agent intellect should be posited either.

For in order that an image be abstracted from individuals, a work is not required, but a weakness of the work. For particular sensibles move sense; and because particularities are few, while that in which they agree is much, because it is in all, therefore the universal itself rather than the particularities remains in the sensing power. And in absence it seems to sense, and afterwards to know abstract universal being[?]. And this is foolishly attributed to the agent intellect, though it is the effect of weakness. For from far away I see a human being and not Peter, because Peter's singularity, such as face and color, cannot extend its powers all the way to me; but motion and human shape do extend, because these were known to me from other cases, and they move more, and therefore excite the inborn passions more and more easily.

Nor for that reason should you say that there is a power in sense which abstracts human being from Peter, but rather that weakness and paucity of particularities do not sufficiently show themselves. I know that this is a die, but not of what value in the game, because the former it has in common with many and I have known it in many; but the latter is particular only in it. But the player recognizes it well, since he has learned to notice particularities. Therefore abstraction is empty. For the eye also sees color without seeing taste; therefore it too abstracts. And why? Because it is not apt to suffer from taste. So distant sense, which they call intellect, because it is distant, cannot be moved by distant things. But to understand likenesses upon likenesses is the work of reasoning sense, as has been said. For just as it discourses from one particular to another particular, so it discourses from one universal to another universal.

When they say that the intellect cannot be moved by a phantasm unless it is illuminated by an agent, they are deceived. For if intellect is the form of the body, it will also be the form of the organ in which phantasy is; therefore, when phantasy is affected, the form in which it is is affected. For the distinction of cells is empty. So too, when the eye is affected, the intellective soul that informs it is affected. But if the soul is not a form, but a spirit, then sensations and imaginations happen in that spirit, and no illumination is left except the divine illumination eminently existing in all things. But on these matters elsewhere.

Therefore the reasons of the Peripatetics by which they prove the immortality of the soul are utterly empty and mutually opposed. For they do not prove the substantiality[?] of the soul while they posit it as a form. But those who posit it abstracted from the body do not make the human being understand, but be understood, as Avicenna too knew. Those who call it the form of the body do not explain how soul comes to be from inanimate elements. Those who say that it is form according to essence but not according to powers do not prove what they want. For potency cannot be separated from essence except in our thought. Therefore if the essence is the form of the body, the powers are too; for whether potency is the soul itself as able, or something flowing from it, since it does not flow outside the body, it cannot become free of the body. And it is strange in what way the same thing is an active and passive form. Aristotle always denies that act and potency are found in the same thing. For he does not concede that seed becomes the matter of situation[?] because it is an agent; and now how is the same soul patient and agent of intellection?

Likewise, if it suffers while it is the form of the body, the body simultaneously suffers the reception of immaterial species. For when light itself, which does not depend on the body, is affected, the body of the air and of the eye in which it is received is affected. Therefore they unskillfully make it immaterial so that it may receive universals. A mirror too receives the image of a thing and of a human being. And indeed, when placed at a distance, it does not represent Peter, but a human being. And so it is with Aristotelian sense and intellect.

Therefore Aristotle's reason by which he proves the immortality of the soul is null. For if, he says, it has a proper operation in which the body does not share, then it happens that it is separated as the incorruptible from the corruptible; this is false. For neither is a sailor incorruptible, though he has a proper operation distinct from the ship; nor is the horse's spirit incorruptible. For the spirit sees in the eye, and the eye itself does not see, but is as it were its window; and yet the spirit departs into air, which is corruptible. Therefore it follows that it is separable, but not that it is incorruptible.

Furthermore it is false in Aristotle's doctrine that the soul has a proper operation in which the body does not share. For to understand, for him, is to suffer; but form does not suffer unless the subject suffers along with it. Likewise the soul labors in understanding, as is clear. Likewise it uses a phantasm in understanding. For according to Aristotle the soul cannot understand the universal itself unless it inspects particulars in a phantasm, not only when it first understands something, but even after it has knowledge of it in habit; whenever it wants to understand, it looks back to phantasms, as Avicenna also asserts. Therefore it is not without an organ. And although he says that phantasms concur then as an object, this would be true when it first senses or understands something; but after the intelligible species is in the intellect, the intellect cannot understand, when it wants to, unless it gazes at phantasms.

## Note

This corrected file preserves the full anti-agent-intellect argument rather than only its conclusion. The leaf keeps shifting the problem downward into physical mediation: light, odor, sound, distance, weakness, mirror-images, and phantasmata all do the explanatory work the Peripatetics assign to an immaterial agent intellect.
