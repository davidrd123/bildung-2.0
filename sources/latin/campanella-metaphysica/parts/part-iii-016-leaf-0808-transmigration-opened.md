## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] quibus vivere se non posse putant, sibi mortem [consciscunt?], sine quibus alii nollent vivere; et hoc faciunt coacti, non considerantes. Sed tamen omnes propter religionem spernunt vitam et bona, considerantes et aliam vitam sperantes.

**Transmigrationem animarum humanarum in corpora brutorum, et e converso, fabulosam esse, et rationes pro ea inanes. Articulus II.**

Et, quoniam non modo olim Pythagorici, et Platonici, atque Pharisaei, verum etiam Talmudistae hodierno tempore putant animas brutorum nobis similes esse, et fieri transmigrationem. Item et Indi Orientales, et Tartari, et multi Americani idem sentiunt. Quin etiam Mahometes scribit, loquutum se fuisse cum lupis, qui dicebant se velle fieri Saracenos, id est de secta Mahometis.

Et vidi Florentinum quendam, qui licet transmigrationem negaret, tamen brutorum animas praemiorum et poenarum post mortem capaces esse putabat, et in hoc laudabat Mahometem. Et posset hoc confirmari testimonio sancti Blasii, qui praedicabat virtutem animalibus et disciplina Christiana illa corrigebat; et Eremitae plurimi a brutis exigunt obedientiam; et D. Franciscus appellat fratres ipsa animalia, et imperabat eis, et imponebat silentium, et obiurgabat.

Propterea hanc opinionem examinare iterum ne gravemur, cum praesertim nihil se vidisse plus in nobis quam in brutis dissectis miretur Vesalius; et Salomon dicat nihil hominem amplius iumento habere; et Moyses vitam aliam homini non polliceatur. Unde vel illa et nos immortales sumus, vel utrique mortales.

Equidem potius nos et bruta immortalitate donarem, quam utrosque privarem: non quia mentis gratissimus error, ut ait Horatius, nec legis timor, ut Lucretius, nec quia consuetudo, ut Averroes dicit, id suadet. Novi enim ab his omnibus solutus vivere. Sed ne peccarem in hominis divinitatem et virtutem tantam quantam in Apostolis et Prophetis, et Philosophis, et in Christo, experti sumus.

Et quidem quod post corpus quaelibet anima vivat, ratione efficaci novimus; et brutorum animam spiritum esse, qui in aerem abit, sicut omnis aqua in mare, et vivit aeream vitam. Animam vero hominis usque ad Deum elevari et vitam divinam victuram esse, eius operationes docuerunt, supra caelum et solem ipsam elevantes; et communicatio cum Angelis et Deo. Utrum autem et bruta adeo altissimas operentur operationes non novimus, sed eorum praxis contrarium monstrat. Canes enim et equi et pecudes non nisi cibum et coitum sequuntur, et ad caelestia non respiciunt, neque ieiunio et studio vacant, ut alia acquirant bona aeterna, neque nobiscum communicant.

Sed sunt apud nos sicut homines stulti et longe insipientiores, nil discurrentes nisi quod praesenti congruit statui. Et de hoc non ambigimus. Idcirco piscium, gruum, apum, et formicarum disciplinam non meliorem esse arbitramur, neque dignam quae humanae conferatur. Et cum dicit Salomon nil inveniri in homine quod non in iumento, loquitur ex persona insipientis vel argumentantis. Ipse enim in vita sua hoc argumento contortus est, sicut et ego et alii rerum exploratores. Sed tandem vidimus omnes inesse homini operationes divinas: et negotium de vita futura cum superis, et prophetiam, et miracula, et inspirationes Dei, et extasim.

Quod vero Vesalius dicit nil plus in homine inveniri nisi cerebrum maius, recte dicit, quoniam ipse secabat corpus, non animam. In corpore autem cum brutis convenimus, et in sanguine et in spiritu corporeo; sed hoc abundantiori nos vigemus. Idcirco maiori cerebro et cellis intra ipsum formatis grandioribus opus habemus, tum quia melior est temperies humana quam brutorum, tum quia mens spiritui primum unitur instrumento suo; ideoque multam esse oportet, qui tanta subserviat menti.

Praeterea ex rectitudine figurae corporeae, et ex manus artificio ostendimus corpus humanum non esse sedem spiritus aerei terram spectantis, unde nutritur et operationes vulgares exercentis, sed longe divinioris, caelo inhiantis et opificia mirifica fabricantis; sicut cithara se citharistae instrumentum, et ligo fossorem indicat, non scriptorem.

Item ex eo quod homo caret squamis, pilis, cornibus, unguibus, exertis dentibus, ceterisque vestibus et armis, quae natura dat animalibus, intelligimus ipsum diviniore praeditum esse anima, qui haec sibi parare per manus instrumentum sciret. Et aliunde. Bruta enim de innata substantia, non de ascita quam sibi adaptarent, sciunt, habent arma ut squamas, spinas, cornua, ungues; similiter vestes ex adiectis pilis, pennis, testis. Immo aranea, etsi sagax, ex suis visceribus educit fila per naturam parata, ex quibus retia texit; similiter vermis sericus ad folliculos; et nullo docente magistro, nullaque experientia, sic operantur. Homo autem ex plantis, ex brutorum armis et vestibus, ex metallis, ex terrae fructificatione, haec et alia innumera sibi suopte ex ingenio parat.

Nec dicat Pythagoras quod si brutis eadem instrumenta data essent, easdem operarentur operationes. Non enim Natura deficit in necessariis, neque abundat in superfluis. Quapropter si scirent opificia humana tractare, utique et manus bruta haberent humanas. Pater filiis stultis vestes et pecuniam; sapientibus vero artes et doctrinas. Sic Natura brutis villos, squamas, cornua, ungues, et rostrum dedit; homini vero manum, qua exercet ingenium.

Posset adhuc obiicere Pythagoras quod cellas cerebri latiores et spiritum multum dedit homini, quo mens humana melius exercet ingenium. Brutis vero in poenam dedit parum cerebri, et spiritum exilem vel crassum, ineptum scientiae organum.

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] without which they think they cannot live, [bring?] death upon themselves, while without those things others would not want to live; and they do this under compulsion, not considering. Yet nevertheless all people, on account of religion, despise life and goods while considering and hoping for another life.

**That the transmigration of human souls into the bodies of beasts, and conversely, is fabulous, and the reasons for it empty. Article II.**

And since not only the Pythagoreans, Platonists, and Pharisees of old, but also the Talmudists in the present time think that the souls of beasts are similar to us and that transmigration happens; likewise the East Indians, Tartars, and many Americans think the same. Indeed Muhammad also writes that he spoke with wolves who said that they wanted to become Saracens, that is, of the sect of Muhammad.

And I saw a certain Florentine who, although he denied transmigration, nevertheless thought that the souls of beasts were capable of rewards and punishments after death, and in this he praised Muhammad. And this could be confirmed by the testimony of Saint Blasius, who preached virtue to animals and corrected them by Christian discipline; and very many hermits demand obedience from beasts; and Saint Francis calls the animals themselves brothers, commands them, imposes silence on them, and rebukes them.

For this reason let us not find it burdensome to examine this opinion again, especially since Vesalius wonders that he saw nothing more in us than in dissected beasts; and Solomon says that the human being has nothing more than the beast; and Moses does not promise another life to the human being. Hence either those souls and we are immortal, or both are mortal.

Indeed I would rather grant immortality to us and to beasts than deprive both of it: not because the mind's most pleasing error, as Horace says, nor fear of law, as Lucretius [says], nor custom, as Averroes says, persuades this. For I know how to live released from all these. But [I would do it] so that I would not sin against the divinity of the human being and so great a virtue as we have experienced in Apostles, Prophets, Philosophers, and in Christ.

And indeed we know by effective reason that every soul lives after the body; and that the soul of beasts is a spirit which goes into the air, just as all water [goes] into the sea, and lives an aerial life. But that the soul of the human being is raised all the way to God and will live a divine life, its operations have taught, lifting it above heaven and the sun; and communication with Angels and God [has taught this]. Whether beasts too perform such highest operations we do not know, but their practice shows the contrary. For dogs, horses, and cattle follow only food and intercourse, do not look toward heavenly things, and are not free for fasting and study so that they may acquire other eternal goods, nor do they communicate with us.

But among us they are like foolish human beings, and far more unwise, reasoning out nothing except what fits their present state. And about this we do not doubt. Therefore we judge that the discipline of fish, cranes, bees, and ants is not better, nor worthy to be compared with human discipline. And when Solomon says that nothing is found in the human being that is not in the beast, he speaks in the person of a fool or of one arguing. For he himself was twisted by this argument in his life, just as I and other explorers of things were. But at last we all saw that divine operations are present in the human being: business concerning future life with higher beings, prophecy, miracles, inspirations of God, and ecstasy.

But when Vesalius says that nothing more is found in the human being except a larger brain, he says it rightly, because he was cutting the body, not the soul. In body, however, we agree with beasts, and in blood and in corporeal spirit; but we flourish with this spirit more abundantly. Therefore we need a larger brain and larger cells formed within it, both because human temperament is better than that of beasts, and because mind is first united to spirit as to its instrument; and therefore there must be much [spirit] to serve so great a mind.

Furthermore, from the straightness of the bodily figure and from the craft of the hand, we have shown that the human body is not the seat of an aerial spirit looking toward earth, from which it is nourished and exercises vulgar operations, but of a far more divine one, gaping toward heaven and making marvelous works; just as a lyre indicates itself to be the instrument of a lyre-player, and a mattock indicates a digger, not a writer.

Likewise, from the fact that the human being lacks scales, hairs, horns, claws, protruding teeth, and the other garments and weapons that nature gives to animals, we understand that he is furnished with a more divine soul, which would know how to prepare these things for itself through the instrument of the hand. And from elsewhere. For beasts know from innate substance, not from an acquired one which they might adapt to themselves; they have weapons such as scales, spines, horns, claws; likewise garments from added hairs, feathers, shells. Indeed the spider, although sagacious, draws from its entrails threads prepared by nature, from which it weaves nets; similarly the silkworm [draws threads] for cocoons; and with no teacher teaching and no experience, they operate in this way. But the human being prepares for himself, by his own ingenuity, these and innumerable other things from plants, from the weapons and garments of beasts, from metals, and from the fructification of the earth.

Nor should Pythagoras say that if the same instruments had been given to beasts, they would perform the same operations. For Nature does not fall short in necessities, nor abound in superfluities. Therefore if beasts knew how to handle human works, beasts would surely have human hands too. A father [gives] clothing and money to foolish sons; to wise ones, however, arts and teachings. So Nature gave hair, scales, horns, claws, and beak to beasts; but to the human being the hand, by which he exercises ingenuity.

Pythagoras could still object that broader cells of the brain and much spirit were given to the human being, by which the human mind exercises ingenuity better. But to beasts as punishment [Nature] gave little brain and a thin or thick spirit, an organ unsuitable for science.
