## Latin

**Transmigrationem animarum humanarum in corpora brutorum, et e converso, fabulosam esse, et rationes pro ea inanes. Articulus II.**

Quoniam non modo olim Pythagorici, Platonici, atque Pharisaei, verum etiam Talmudistae hodierno tempore putant animas brutorum nobis similes esse et fieri transmigrationem; item Indi Orientales, Tartari, et multi Americani idem sentiunt; propterea hanc opinionem examinare iterum non gravemur.

Vesalius miratur nihil se vidisse plus in nobis quam in brutis dissectis; Salomon dicat nihil hominem amplius iumento habere; Moyses vitam aliam homini non polliceatur. Unde vel illa et nos immortales sumus, vel utrique mortales.

Equidem potius nos et bruta immortalitate donarem quam utrosque privarem, non quia mentis gratissimus error aut legis timor aut consuetudo id suadet, sed ne peccarem in hominis divinitatem et virtutem tantam quantam in Apostolis, Prophetis, Philosophis, et in Christo experti sumus.

Quod post corpus quilibet anima vivat ratione efficaci novimus: brutorum animam spiritum esse qui in aerem abit, sicut omnis aqua in mare, et vivit aeream vitam. Animam vero hominis usque ad Deum elevari et vitam divinam victuram esse operationes eius docent, supra caelum et solem ipsam elevantes, et communicatio cum angelis et Deo.

Utrum bruta adeo altissimas operentur operationes non novimus, sed praxis eorum contrarium monstrat. Canes, equi, et pecudes non nisi cibum et coitum sequuntur, et ad caelestia non respiciunt, neque ieiunio et studio vacant ut alia acquirant bona aeterna, neque nobiscum communicant.

Vesalius recte dicit nihil plus in homine inveniri nisi cerebrum maius, quoniam ipse secabat corpus, non animam in corpore. In corpore cum brutis convenimus, in sanguine et in spiritu corporeo; sed abundantiore spiritu vigemus et maiori cerebro opus habemus, tum quia melior est temperies humana, tum quia mens spiritui ut instrumento suo primo unitur.

Ex rectitudine figurae corporeae et ex manus artificio ostendimus corpus humanum non esse sedem spiritus aerei terram spectantis, sed longe divinioris, caelo inhiantis et opificia mirifica fabricantis. Homo caret squamis, pilis, cornibus, unguibus, exertis dentibus, ceterisque vestibus et armis, quia diviniore anima praeditus haec sibi per manum parare scit.

Nec dicat Pythagoras quod si brutis eadem instrumenta data essent easdem operarentur operationes. Natura non deficit in necessariis neque abundat in superfluis. Si scirent opificia humana tractare, bruta manus humanas haberent.

## English

Campanella opens the article on transmigration because many traditions hold it: Pythagoreans, Platonists, Pharisees, Talmudists, East Indians, Tartars, and many Americans. Vesalius found nothing in dissected humans beyond what appears in beasts except a larger brain; Solomon says humans have nothing over animals; Moses does not explicitly promise humans another life. So either both humans and beasts are immortal, or both are mortal.

Campanella says he would rather grant immortality to both than deny it to both, but not from fear, custom, or comforting error. He refuses to sin against the divinity and virtue experienced in apostles, prophets, philosophers, and Christ. Effective reason shows that every soul lives after body in some way: the soul of beasts is spirit returning into air, as water into sea, living an aerial life; the human soul rises to God and divine life.

Animal practice does not show the highest operations. Dogs, horses, and cattle seek food and sex; they do not turn toward the heavens, fast, study, or seek eternal goods, nor do they communicate with us about such things.

Vesalius dissected bodies, not soul in body. In body, blood, and corporeal spirit humans share much with beasts, but the human body is fitted for a more divine principle. Upright posture, the hand, and the lack of natural weapons or coverings show that nature gave humans not fur, claws, or horns but an organ for ingenuity. If beasts were meant to do human arts, they would have human hands.

## Note

This article keeps the animal question alive without collapsing it. Campanella grants beast-soul survival as an aerial spirit, but separates it from divine life. The human body itself becomes evidence: not because it is stronger than animal bodies, but because its deficiencies presuppose hand, art, and a diviner soul.
