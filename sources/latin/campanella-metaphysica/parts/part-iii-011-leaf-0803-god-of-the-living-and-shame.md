## Latin

Confirmant, neque hoc faciunt, nisi quatenus credunt veram esse religionem de futuro saeculo post mortem, ut ostendimus. At qui velit mori pro doctrina Averrois, Epicuri, Galeni, Aristotelis, et aliorum negantium religionem, non invenitur: pro religione autem innumeri. Ergo divina res est, et profecto, cum ipsa sit cultus Dei regis caeli et terrae, necessario dat constantiam et robur pugnantibus pro se.

Item, ut probavimus, Deus est et providentiam habet de cunctis rebus, maxime autem de nobilioribus. Videmus quoque hominem esse creaturarum nostri mundi nobilissimum, quippe qui excedit elementa et animalia in intelligendo, appetendo, et operando mirifica. Ergo religio est: decet enim coli superiorem ab inferiori, qui ab eo bona tanta percipit; et hoc naturale esse principum observantia a subditis, et patrum a filiis, monet.

Praeterea tandem argumentum Iesu Christi contra Sadducaeos inexpugnabile est. Deus enim, inquit, apparuit Moysi et dixit: Ego sum Deus Abraham, et Deus Isaac, et Deus Iacob. Hoc autem dixit, quando Abraham, Isaac, et Iacob iam a praesenti vita decesserant. Deus autem non est mortuorum Deus, sed vivorum. Ergo qui moriuntur homines aliam adhuc habent vitam. Anima ergo ipsorum vivit, quandoquidem corpora iam destructa videmus.

Hoc argumentum robur habet a testimonio, non ab opinione. Si enim Deus declaravit se Patrem esse hominum, et particularem gerit ipsorum curam, et ostendit se eis, ut Moysi et Prophetis, quamvis enim in suo esse Deus non possit nobis videri; potest tamen in effectibus et symbolis. Non enim infinitum in corpore humano aut in flamma ignis potest cohiberi, sed ubique operatur per corpus quodlibet, uti vult. Hoc autem negare impudentia est, non secus ac negare Romam esse et Caesarem fuisse in mundo. Qui enim id testantur sunt indubitata fide digni.

Quapropter si Deus se Patrem illorum declaravit Moysi et aliis Prophetis, se Patrem omnium hominum ostendit, et tantam curam habet nostri, ut et nobis se manifestet; profecto immortales homines esse oportet. Dedecus enim et inutilitas esset Deo tantam gerere curam eorum quae paululum vivunt et perpetuo mortua sunt. Et cum dixerit se Deum non mortuorum, profecto et mortuos vivere oporteret. Haec ratio semper agitavit animum meum. Et David olim, unde ait: Domine, quid est homo, quia innotuisti ei? aut filius hominis, quia reputas eum? Et: quid est homo quod memor es eius? Non quidem res vilis et perpetuo moritura, sed nobilissima et perpetuo victura. Alioquin tanta Dei cura erga nos esset vana, et tanta reputatio hominis inanis Deoque indignissima.

Item, verecundia de culpis et de quocumque defectu nostro, et parentelae nostrae, et originis, et vestis fractae, et calcei rupti, et vero et opinabili seu apparenti, quae verecundia est quasi vindicta nostri contra nos ipsos, ut plenius in Ethicis probavimus, manifeste ostendit non solum praestantiam mentis, sed etiam immortalitatem et consortium cum divinitate, cum qua nihil coinquinatum habitare potest.

Nam homo verecundatur de corpore suo quando ostendit signum suae mortalitatis, unde manducatio, coitus, cacatio, et minctio, quibus morti individui et speciei medicamur, in secreto fiunt, praesertim a melioribus, prudentioribus, et religiosioribus; et a cunctis fragilitas, imbecillitas, corruptela, et mors occultatur. Indignaturque anima moribundum colere corpus, ut suam nimirum praestantiam et immortalitatem tueatur. Alioquin vana esset tanta verecundia, tantusque zelus honoris veri intra conscientiam, et amor non datus brutis et infantibus insipientibus, sed viris: custos sane mirifici thesauri verae innatae nobilitatis, cuius etiam umbra aliquando solum circa honorem relucet in brutis, ex participio primalitatum.

Utrum autem in futuris saeculis animae humanae annihilentur, nemo scit. Sed ratio negat: si enim bonum est eas esse, et Deo coniungentur in altera vita et meliorabuntur, utique semper futuras esse oportet. Nec enim res ullae ex divinitatis adeptione perduntur, sed permanent sub quacumque forma et plenitudine, et melioribus perficiuntur. Sic et melius de anima sentiendum est: ipsa enim non recipit scientiam et robur sicut calor et frigus, cum sui deterioratione et perditione, sed sicut materia formas, cum sui amplificatione, quin etiam cum maiori perfectione de lucro[?]. Per scientias enim fit omnia, ut dictum est. Permanebit ergo in receptione divinitatis amplior, gloriosior, et maior. Unde secundum nos potest omnia fieri, quia primalitatibus constituta est, non contractis, et non quia est nihil actu.

Stoici autem putant Deum esse corpus tenuissimum, et animas ac genios de eius substantia; cui uniuntur sicut guttae aquae mari et vapor aeri, qui suo toti incorporati propriam subsistentiam amittunt. Nos autem ostendimus Deum non esse corpus tenue, sed incorporeum; nec res finitas posse ipsi infinito sic identificari, ut distinctio non remaneat.

Tandem licet alibi nos nullam propriam operationem dederimus animae humanae, quae spiritui corporeo communis non sit, tamen cognoscere quod res non sunt sicut nobis apparent, non datum brutis, et intelligentiam divinorum et ecstasim ut propriam damus, et temporum, corporum, et locorum intelligendo et amando infinitum ambitum mentis propria videmus. Nam etsi spiritui communicet, tamen a mente incipere, non a spiritu dicimus. Sapere enim est antequam esse calidum, tenue, et mobile.

Sed quia exteriora nescimus nisi nos moveant, nec mentem movere possunt nisi prius moveant spiritum, idcirco a spiritu et cum spiritu scientiam et operationem omnem fieri accidit. Sed tamen ipsa scientia praecedit spiritum, qui est entitas, veluti et potestas et amor. Deus ergo prius illabitur menti, vel sese manifestat, quoniam in nobis est, quam spiritui. Entia vero alia prius spiritui. De angelis tamen haereo utrum menti an spiritui prius communicent. Sed revera de solo Deo certi sumus.

Et quoniam anima a corpore alienata, vel in somnis, vel in syncope, vel in contemplatione vehementi, divina videt et arcana Dei, consequens est ut haec illi propria assignetur operatio, et non ideae universalis confusa notio[?], quae brutis et pueris convenit, sed ideae[?]. Extasis autem spiritui et corpori communicatur ob unionem, sed non ab eis incipit, ut docuimus in prima parte exemplo Bernardi et proprio. Patet autem hanc non esse animalis spiritus operationem, quoniam ipse per se non intelligit neque appetit quae sibi incommoda sunt et laboriosa. Nihil autem magis vires animales, corpus, et vitam prurientem destruit quam contemplatio superum, et hanc esse mortem quandam corporis[?] Socrates dicit[?].

## English

They confirm it, and they do not do this except insofar as they believe that religion concerning the future age after death is true, as we have shown. But no one is found who would want to die for the doctrine of Averroes, Epicurus, Galen, Aristotle, and others denying religion; for religion, however, there are innumerable people. Therefore it is a divine thing, and certainly, since it is the worship of God, king of heaven and earth, it necessarily gives constancy and strength to those who fight for it.

Likewise, as we have proved, God exists and has providence over all things, but especially over the nobler things. We also see that the human being is the noblest of the creatures of our world, since he exceeds the elements and the animals in understanding, desiring, and doing marvelous things. Therefore religion exists: for it is fitting that the superior be worshiped by the inferior, who receives such great goods from him; and the observance of rulers by subjects, and of fathers by sons, warns that this is natural.

Furthermore, finally, the argument of Jesus Christ against the Sadducees is unassailable. For God, he says, appeared to Moses and said: I am the God of Abraham, and the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob. But he said this when Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob had already departed from the present life. God, however, is not God of the dead, but of the living. Therefore human beings who die still have another life. Therefore their soul lives, since we see that their bodies have already been destroyed.

This argument has its strength from testimony, not from opinion. For if God declared himself to be Father of human beings, and bears particular care for them, and showed himself to them, as to Moses and the Prophets, although God cannot be seen by us in his own being; nevertheless he can be seen in effects and symbols. For the infinite cannot be contained in a human body or in a flame of fire, but operates everywhere through any body whatever, as he wishes. To deny this, however, is shamelessness, just as much as denying that Rome exists and that Caesar existed in the world. For those who testify to it are worthy of undoubted trust.

Therefore if God declared himself Father of those men to Moses and the other Prophets, he showed himself Father of all human beings, and he has such care for us that he also manifests himself to us; certainly human beings must be immortal. For it would be a disgrace and uselessness in God to bear such care for things that live a little while and are forever dead. And since he said that he is not God of the dead, certainly the dead too would have to live. This reason has always stirred my mind. And David once, whence he says: Lord, what is man, that you have made yourself known to him? or the son of man, that you account him? And: what is man, that you are mindful of him? Not, to be sure, a vile thing that will die forever, but a most noble thing that will live forever. Otherwise such great care of God toward us would be vain, and such great accounting of the human being empty and most unworthy of God.

Likewise, shame concerning faults and concerning any defect of ours, and of our kinship, origin, torn clothing, ripped shoe, and concerning what is true and what is opinable or apparent, which shame is as it were a revenge of ourselves against ourselves, as we have proved more fully in the Ethics, clearly shows not only the excellence of the mind but also immortality and fellowship with divinity, with which nothing defiled can dwell.

For a human being is ashamed of his body when it shows a sign of his mortality. Hence eating, intercourse, defecation, and urination, by which we remedy the death of the individual and of the species, are done in secret, especially by better, more prudent, and more religious people; and frailty, weakness, corruption, and death are hidden by everyone. And the soul disdains to tend a dying body, so that it may defend, namely, its own excellence and immortality. Otherwise so much shame would be vain, and so much zeal for true honor within conscience, and a love not given to beasts and foolish infants but to men: surely the guardian of the marvelous treasury of true innate nobility, whose shadow sometimes shines forth only around honor even in beasts, from participation in the primalities.

Whether, however, human souls are annihilated in future ages, no one knows. But reason denies it: for if it is good that they exist, and they will be joined to God in another life and made better, surely they must exist forever. For no things are lost from attainment of divinity, but remain under whatever form and fullness, and are perfected by better things. Thus one must think better of the soul too: for it does not receive knowledge and strength as heat and cold do, with deterioration and destruction of itself, but as matter receives forms, with amplification of itself, indeed even with greater perfection as gain[?]. For through the sciences it becomes all things, as has been said. Therefore in receiving divinity it will remain more ample, more glorious, and greater. Hence according to us it can become all things because it is constituted by primalities, not contracted ones, and not because it is nothing in act.

The Stoics, however, think that God is a most subtle body, and that souls and genii are from his substance; they are united to him as drops of water to the sea and vapor to the air, and when incorporated into their whole they lose their own subsistence. But we have shown that God is not a subtle body, but incorporeal; nor can finite things be so identified with the infinite that distinction does not remain.

Finally, although elsewhere we have given no proper operation to the human soul which is not common to corporeal spirit, nevertheless we give as proper to it knowing that things are not as they appear to us, which is not given to beasts, and understanding of divine things and ecstasy; and we see as proper to mind the infinite sweep through times, bodies, and places in understanding and loving. For even if it communicates this to the spirit, we say nevertheless that it begins from mind, not from spirit. For to know/taste is prior to being hot, subtle, and mobile.

But because we do not know external things unless they move us, and they cannot move the mind unless they first move the spirit, for that reason it happens that every knowledge and operation is made from the spirit and with the spirit. Nevertheless knowledge itself precedes spirit, which is entity, just as power and love also are. Therefore God first slips into the mind, or manifests himself, because he is in us, before he does so to the spirit. Other beings, however, first [communicate] to the spirit. Concerning angels I am uncertain whether they communicate first to mind or to spirit. But truly we are certain about God alone.

And because the soul, alienated from the body, whether in dreams, or in syncope, or in vehement contemplation, sees divine things and the secrets of God, it follows that this operation is assigned to it as proper, and not the confused notion of a universal idea[?], which belongs to beasts and children, but of an idea[?]. Ecstasy, however, is communicated to spirit and body because of union, but it does not begin from them, as we taught in the first part by Bernard's example and my own. It is clear that this is not an operation of animal spirit, since it of itself neither understands nor desires the things that are inconvenient and laborious for it. Nothing destroys the animal powers, the body, and luxuriant life more than contemplation of higher things, and Socrates says that this is a kind of death of the body[?].
