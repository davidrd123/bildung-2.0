## Latin

Qui vellet mori pro doctrina Averrois, Epicuri, Galeni, Aristotelis, et aliorum negantium religionem, non invenitur; pro religione autem innumeri. Ergo divina res est, et cum ipsa sit cultus Dei regis caeli et terrae, necessario dat constantiam et robur pugnantibus pro se.

Deus est, et providentiam habet de cunctis rebus, maxime autem de nobilioribus. Videmus quoque hominem esse creaturarum nostri mundi nobilissimum, qui excedit elementa et animalia intelligendo, appetendo, et operando mirifica. Ergo religio est; decet enim coli superiorem ab inferiori qui ab eo bona tanta percipit.

Argumentum Iesu Christi contra Sadducaeos inexpugnabile est. Deus apparuit Moysi et dixit: Ego sum Deus Abraham, Deus Isaac, et Deus Iacob. Hoc dixit quando Abraham, Isaac, et Iacob iam de praesenti vita decesserant. Deus autem non est mortuorum Deus, sed vivorum. Ergo qui moriuntur homines aliam adhuc habent vitam; anima ergo ipsorum vivit, quandoquidem corpora iam destructa videmus.

Si Deus se Patrem illorum declaravit Moysi et aliis prophetis, se Patrem omnium hominum ostendit. Dedecus enim et inutilitas esset Deo tantam gerere curam eorum quae paululum vivunt et perpetuo mortua sunt. Haec ratio semper agitavit animum meum.

Verecundia de culpis et de quocumque defectu nostro, de parentela nostra, origine, veste fracta, calceo rupto, vero et opinabili, quasi vindicta nostri contra nos ipsos, manifeste ostendit non solum praestantiam mentis, sed etiam immortalitatem et consortium cum divinitate, cum qua nihil coinquinatum habitare potest.

Homo verecundatur de corpore suo quando ostendit signum suae mortalitatis. Manducatio, coitus, cacatio, et minctio, quibus morti individui et speciei medicamur, in secreto fiunt, praesertim a melioribus, prudentioribus, et religiosioribus. Indignatur anima moribundum colere corpus, ut suam praestantiam et immortalitatem tueatur.

Utrum in futuris saeculis animae humanae annihilentur nemo scit; sed ratio negat. Si bonum est eas esse, et Deo coniungentur in altera vita et meliorabuntur, utique semper futuras esse oportet. Nec res ullae ex divinitatis adeptione perduntur, sed permanent sub quacumque forma et plenitudine, et melioribus perficiuntur.

Tandem, licet alibi nullam propriam operationem dederimus animae humanae quae spiritui corporeo communis non sit, tamen cognoscere quod res non sunt sicut nobis apparent, intelligentiam divinorum, extasim, et infinitum ambitum mentis intelligendo et amando, propria videmus. Exteriora nescimus nisi nos moveant; sed Deus prius illabitur menti vel se manifestat, quoniam in nobis est.

## English

Campanella presses martyrdom and religion as evidence. No one dies for the doctrine of Averroes, Epicurus, Galen, or Aristotle denying religion; innumerable people die for religion. Since religion is worship of the God who rules heaven and earth, it gives strength to those who fight for it.

Christ's argument against the Sadducees is decisive for him. God said to Moses, "I am the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob," after they had already died. But God is not God of the dead but of the living. Therefore those who die still have another life; their souls live, since their bodies are destroyed. Campanella says this argument always moved his mind.

Shame is another sign. Humans are ashamed of sins, defects, ancestry, broken clothing, torn shoes, and bodily mortal functions. Shame is like a revenge of oneself against oneself, and it shows both the mind's excellence and its fellowship with divinity, because nothing stained can dwell with the divine. The soul resents serving a dying body.

Whether human souls could be annihilated in future ages, no one knows; reason denies it. If it is good for them to be, and if union with God makes them better, they ought to continue. Union with divinity does not destroy things but perfects them.

Campanella finally names several proper operations of the human soul: knowing that things are not as they appear to us, understanding divine things, ecstasy, and the infinite sweep of mind in knowing and loving. External things reach us through bodily spirit, but God can enter or manifest himself first to mind, because God is already inward to us.

## Note

This leaf is unusually autobiographical: `Haec ratio semper agitavit animum meum`. The argument from God as God of the living clearly mattered to Campanella. The other major addition is shame as evidence of immortality: bodily embarrassment is not social decorum only, but the soul's protest against its mortal vehicle.
