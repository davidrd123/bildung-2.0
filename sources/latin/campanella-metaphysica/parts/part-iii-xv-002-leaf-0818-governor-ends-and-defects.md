## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] gubernatione rerum dicamus quo pacto per seipsas invicem mutuis officiis definiantur ad fines. Et quae causa cui effectui praesit in gubernatu, et ubi et quomodo. Hoc praesupposito, quod gubernationis participium dedit Deus solis causis activis, sive instrumentalibus, ut calor et frigus et Caelum, sive principalibus, ut Angelus, mens. At conservationis participium cunctis impertitus est rebus, etiam quae ad finem non movent, nec praecognoscunt. Et materia enim formas, et spatium substentans corpora; et formae ipsae essentiales praecipue conservant res per se; per accidens autem etiam contraria, et obiecta, et exempla, etc.

Gubernatorem omnem praescribere atque esse finem rerum gubernandarum quarum ipse est et causa et gubernator. Quorum vero solum gubernator, praescire et praedestinare finem et media, et providere. Gubernatum hominum esse vanum, si non habet deificationem pro fine. Quod videtur propter finem esse in Natura et arte et religione; et habere finem vel praeordinationis, vel praedestinationis, vel utriusque; et in hoc variari dogmata nationum. Item in nostro dogmate omnia quae videntur esse praeter finem ac defectu [ex nihilo?][?] cooriri, et propter finem exercitii, modificationis mediorum tendentium ad finem, evenire; ac defectus ipsos esse occasiones uniendi gubernatores gubernandis, et quid praestent illi istis in coexistentiarum colligatione. Artic. II.

Omnis gubernator praefigit finem ad quem, et media per quae qui gubernantur agendi sunt. Et quando idem est autor et gubernator, seipsum modo habet pro fine extremo summoque, ut [condit?][?] domum quam ipse habitaturus est. Etenim et fenestras, et ianuas, et salam, et aedes superiores et inferiores ad certos proprios usus architectatur et dirigit per media certa. Media enim sunt formae horum vasorum et materiae. Non enim ianuam de lapide, nec quinquangularem, sed de ligno et ferro et quadrangularem facit; sicuti neque autor Naturae pulmonem unitum et osseum facit, sed in lobos distinctum, et carnis levissimae ac fistulosae, prout suo congruit fini.

Quando vero non est idem gubernator et autor, dirigit quos gubernat ad finem autoris: ut pastor oves ad usus heri, et navifactor naves ad usus Regis. Subordinantur autem fines omnes ad finem Autoris: nam frenifactor facit frenum ad usum equitis. Eques utitur equo ad usum Ducis in bello. Dux belligerat ut victoriam Reip. pariat. Victoria autem est propter pacem et conservationem civium. Cives autem sunt non propter Regem, sed Rex propter cives. Isti enim fecerunt sibi Regem, in quo per unionem virtutis communis, in eo enim reponunt robur et disciplinam, contineantur serventurque mutuis officiis. At si haec conservatio ulteriorem non haberet finem, vana esset; moriuntur enim et non servantur homines neque Civitates perpetuo prout cupiunt. Ergo alius est finis eorum, sicuti et brutorum finis videtur homo, et plantarum bruta, quae ex eis servantur.

Difficile est quidem omnium rerum fines invenire; multa enim videntur esse praeter finem, ut pulices, coluber, [scatectio ollae?][?], casus olei in vestem novam, et alia paene innumerabilia, quae Epicureos et Democriticos, ut tribuerent omnia temerario casui, occasioni fuerunt. Alii autem, cum viderent plurima propter usus mirificos esse, omnia propter finem a Deo gubernari asseruerunt. Alii, considerantes casualia et finem certum assequentia, tribuerunt partem gubernatori rerum, partem casui et fortunae; quae sane causae non sunt, sed causarum defectus.

Quomodo autem isti defectus habeant fines suos, nemo exacte intellexit, nisi Christiani Philosophi: qui respectu ordinum secundarum causarum dant defectus casuales, sed respectu ordinis primae causae universalissimae nihil putant casu fieri, sed propter fines. Et cum quaero de fine peccatorum in Natura, ut monstrorum et florum defluentium antequam in fructus vertantur; et in arte cur cecidit oleum super vestem novam, et cur ruit domus cum aedificatur, et cur cecidit stilla atramenti in hac pagina et foedavit scripturam; et tandem in moribus et in religione, ut cur homo adulterium perpetravit et blasphemavit Deum; et cur Adam peccavit, et nati sunt homines reprobi, qui omnes nascituri erant praedestinati ad gloriam Dei; unde non modo videtur esse casus iste secundorum ordinum, sed primi, quandoquidem contra praeceptum et ordinationem immediatam Dei, causae universalissimae, peccaverunt homines et reprobationem incurrerunt.

Qui autem negant has historias, effugere non possunt argumentum quaestionis. Etenim videmus nunc homines peccare contra autorem Naturae: blasphemando Deum, adorando bestiolas pro diis, et amicos et res vilissimas, procurando mortes aliorum hominum et abortus et Reip. destructionem, insidias, fraudes, mendaciaque struendo. Quae omnia evenire scimus contra ordinem divinum, et vituperamus, per leges declinare curamus; et tamen facimus quae in aliis detestamur, propriis passionibus ex rerum serie et coexistentia actionum, passione et obiecto et temperamentis nostris et illarum occasione natis quasi compulsi.

Et quidem Mahomet. et Calviniani omnia a Deo praedestinari et fieri, nec posse aliter evenire, et liberum arbitrium esse titulum in nobis sine re. ## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] concerning the governance of things, let us say in what way through themselves and among one another they are determined to ends by mutual offices; and what cause presides over what effect in governance, and where and how. This being presupposed: that God gave a participation in governance only to active causes, whether instrumental, as heat and cold and Heaven, or principal, as Angel and mind. But he imparted a participation in conservation to all things, even those that do not move toward an end nor foreknow it. For matter, sustaining forms, and space, sustaining bodies, and especially the essential forms themselves, conserve things through themselves; accidentally, however, even contraries, objects, examples, and so on [conserve].

That every governor prescribes and is the end of the things to be governed, when he himself is both cause and governor of them. But of those things of which he is only governor, he foreknows and predestines the end and the means, and provides. That the governance of human beings is vain if it does not have deification as its end. That what exists for the sake of an end seems to exist in Nature, art, and religion; and to have an end either of preordination, or of predestination, or of both; and that in this the dogmas of nations vary. Likewise, in our teaching, all things that seem to be outside an end and to arise by defect [from nothing?][?] occur for the sake of the end of exercise, of the modification of means tending to the end; and the defects themselves are occasions of uniting governors to the governed, and [of showing] what those give to these in the binding-together of coexistences. Article II.

Every governor fixes beforehand the end to which, and the means through which, those who are governed are to be led. And when the same one is author and governor, he has himself in some way as the extreme and highest end, as when he [builds?][?] a house that he himself is going to inhabit. For he designs and directs windows, doors, the hall, and upper and lower rooms to certain proper uses through certain means. For the means are the forms and matters of these vessels. He does not make a door from stone, nor five-sided, but from wood and iron and four-sided; just as the author of Nature does not make the lung unified and bony, but distinct into lobes and of very light and fistulous flesh, as suits its own end.

But when the governor and the author are not the same, he directs those whom he governs to the author's end: as a shepherd [directs] sheep to the uses of the master, and a shipwright [directs] ships to the uses of the King. All ends, however, are subordinated to the end of the Author: for the bridle-maker makes the bridle for the use of the rider. The rider uses the horse for the use of the Leader in war. The Leader wages war so that he may produce victory for the Republic. Victory, however, is for the sake of peace and the conservation of citizens. Citizens, however, are not for the sake of the King, but the King for the sake of citizens. For they made a King for themselves, in whom, through the union of common virtue, for in him they place strength and discipline, they may be contained and preserved by mutual offices. But if this conservation had no further end, it would be vain; for human beings die, and neither human beings nor Cities are preserved forever as they desire. Therefore there is another end of theirs, just as the end of beasts seems to be the human being, and the end of plants the beasts that are preserved from them.

It is indeed difficult to find the ends of all things; for many things seem to be outside an end, such as fleas, the snake, [the boiling-over of a pot?][?], the fall of oil onto a new garment, and almost innumerable other things, which were the occasion for Epicureans and Democriteans to attribute everything to reckless chance. Others, however, when they saw that very many things exist for marvelous uses, asserted that all things are governed by God for the sake of an end. Others, considering things that are casual and yet attain a certain end, assigned one part to the governor of things, another part to chance and fortune; which, to be sure, are not causes, but defects of causes.

But how those defects have their own ends, no one understood exactly except the Christian Philosophers: they grant casual defects with respect to the orders of secondary causes, but with respect to the order of the first and most universal cause, they think nothing happens by chance, but for the sake of ends. And when I ask about the end of sins in Nature, such as monsters and flowers falling off before they turn into fruits; and in art, why oil fell on a new garment, and why a house falls while it is being built, and why a drop of ink fell on this page and spoiled the writing; and finally in morals and in religion, as why a human being committed adultery and blasphemed God; and why Adam sinned, and reprobate human beings were born, all of whom, before they were going to be born, had been predestined to the glory of God: from this, that chance seems not only to belong to the secondary orders, but to the first, since human beings sinned against the precept and immediate ordination of God, the most universal cause, and incurred reprobation.

But those who deny these histories cannot escape the argument of the question. For we now see human beings sinning against the author of Nature: blaspheming God, worshiping little beasts as gods, and friends and the vilest things, procuring the deaths of other human beings and abortions and the destruction of the Republic, constructing plots, frauds, and lies. We know that all these things occur against divine order, and we blame them and try through laws to avoid them; and yet we do the things that we detest in others, as if compelled by our own passions born from the series of things and the coexistence of actions, passion, object, and our temperaments, and the occasions of those things.

The Theologians answer that all these things were preordained by God for his own glory, but by hidden modes and ends; that some things [by right?] in other [modes?][?] are nevertheless done by God and not predestined, but that God nevertheless uses things that come-to-be. And indeed Muhammad and the Calvinists [hold] that all things are predestined and done by God, and cannot happen otherwise, and that free will is a title in us without a reality. ## Note

Article II turns governance into a problem of ends, defects, and coexistence. The crucial shift is that apparent defect, accident, and even moral evil become occasions for governance rather than evidence against governance; Campanella distinguishes chance in the order of secondary causes from providential order in the first cause. The page is also self-referentially useful: the fallen ink-drop on "this page" is one of his examples of apparent accident.
