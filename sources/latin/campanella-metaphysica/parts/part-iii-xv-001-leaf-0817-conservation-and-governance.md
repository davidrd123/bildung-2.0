## Latin

Thomae Campanellae Stylensis Ord. Praed. Pars Tertia Metaphysicorum. Liber Decimus Quintus, in quo de Conservatione et Gubernatione rerum.

Entia omnia a primo Ente, a quo sunt, conservari continuante esse quod largitur; et esse propter ipsum primum Ens, ideoque ab ipso gubernari. Et quoniam alia aliorum altius pervenientium ad primum Ens gratia sunt, Deum communicasse rebus non solum vim causandi esse quoquo pacto pro rata sua, sed etiam conservandi et gubernandi ad certos fines in mutua coexistentia; ac posse seorsum Deo servari, non autem gubernari. Cap. I. Artic. I.

Quoniam entia finita sunt omnia, excepto primo, a quo habent ut sint. Dicitur enim unum quodque ens. Igitur unitate qua sunt et entitatem quod sunt, a Primo, id est unissimo uno Ente, sunt. A quo autem habent ut sint, habent et ut conserventur. Conservari enim est permanere in suo esse et continuari suae origini, sive temporaliter, cum transmutationibus concomitantibus, sive intemporaliter supra transmutationem.

Et quoniam facta entia non modo sunt, sed ad certos fines sunt, idcirco et Deo conservante in esse et Deo gubernante, id est perducente ad certum finem propter quem sunt, opus habent; sicut filius indiget patre, ut sit; et ut permaneat indiget eodem patre educatore et instructore; et iterum ut serviat Reip. in aliqua arte vel officio, indiget patre instructore et promotore; utque in Deum referatur [usus?][?] eius erga Remp. et ad unum, a quo et propter finem sunt, indiget itidem ut Vicario illius patris, in quo omnis paternitas in Caelo et in terra nominatur, sicut dicit Apostolus.

Et quia pater carnalis est instrumentum veri patris, et potius causa in fieri et existentialiter quam in facto esse et essentialiter, idcirco multiplicatur paternitas, ita ut alius generet apud nos, alius educet, alius doceat, quem vocamus magistrum, quasi particula magis habentem, non totum. Alius denique nos ad Deum dirigat, qui non magister sed et magus sit, callens Religionem et Theologiam et omnes artes eius ancillas.

## English

Of Thomas Campanella of Stilo, of the Order of Preachers. Third Part of the Metaphysics. Fifteenth Book, in which [he treats] of the conservation and governance of things.

That all beings are conserved by the First Being, from which they are, by its continuing the being that it grants; and that they exist for the sake of that First Being itself, and therefore are governed by it. And since some things exist for the sake of others that reach higher toward the First Being, God has communicated to things not only a power of causing being in whatever way, according to their measure, but also [a power] of conserving and governing toward certain ends in mutual coexistence; and they can be conserved separately by God, but not governed [separately]. Chapter I. Article I.

Since all beings are finite, except the first, from which they have that they are. For each thing is called one being. Therefore, by the unity by which they are, and the entity that they are, they are from the First, that is, from the most-one one Being. But from that from which they have that they are, they also have that they are conserved. For to be conserved is to remain in one's own being and to be continued to one's origin, either temporally, with accompanying transformations, or intemporally above transformation.

And since made beings not only are, but are for certain ends, for that reason they need both God conserving in being and God governing, that is, leading [them] to the certain end for the sake of which they are; just as a son needs a father in order to be, and in order to remain he needs the same father as educator and instructor; and again, in order to serve the Republic in some art or office, he needs the father as instructor and promoter; and in order that [his use?][?] toward the Republic may be referred into God and to the one from which and for the sake of which they are, he likewise needs him as vicar of that father in whom all fatherhood in heaven and on earth is named, as the Apostle says.

And because the carnal father is the instrument of the true father, and is a cause rather in coming-to-be and existentially than in the thing's having-been-made and essentially, therefore fatherhood is multiplied, so that one person generates among us, another educates, another teaches, whom we call master, as if having a particle of "more," not the whole. Finally another directs us to God, who is not only master but also magus, skilled in Religion and Theology and all the arts that are their handmaids.

For this reason God, omnipotent, omniscient, and [omni-willing?][?], could indeed produce and conserve all things and each thing separately, but could not lead them to the ends for which they are unless they are together and coexist. For the lung cannot arrive at the retention of heat in the heart and the generation of spirits, for the sake of which end it exists, unless it is in the chest of an animal. Put it in the center of the earth, and it will serve you in nothing. Therefore God fitted all things together in their coexistence, so that all things and each thing exist for the sake of all things and each thing.

Likewise he not only gave them being, but communicated to them the power of causing being and of conserving and of leading to the ends of coexistences and to God himself. And because in this most arduous matter they fail more, he adds his own immediate and superessential help, as is proved in Theology. It was necessary that they exist together and be conserved and be affected by mutual advantages, since they are from the same one God, and within one and toward one. ## Note

Liber XV opens by distinguishing conservation from governance. God can conserve things separately, but governance requires coexistence: the lung has its end only in the living chest, and creatures are ordered as all-for-all and each-for-each. This is the metaphysical bridge from Liber XIV's soul doctrine into the governance/magic/religion material that the gap was hiding.
