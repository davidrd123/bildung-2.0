## Opening Finding

The tenth-dubitation response makes explicit what the ninth response had implied: Campanella's epistemology is not simply "sense versus intellect" but a distinction between innate/infused knowledge and added or introduced knowledge. The soul never loses innate knowledge of its origin, but its use is interrupted by corporeal mass and introduced passions.

That lets Campanella explain dreams, illness, ecstasy, prophecy-like revelation, and ordinary waking judgment without collapsing them into one scale. For bodily matters, waking and healthy perception has priority. For hidden passions and divine matters, the sick, weak, ecstatic, or religious can sometimes be better witnesses because they are more easily affected by subtler or higher beings.

## Corrected Latin

RESPONSIO AD DECIMAM DUBIT.

ubi de scientia innata, & illata, & de causis delirii, & scientia somniantis, & aegri, & ecstatici, & oppositorum.

CAP. IX. ART. V.

Ad decimum ergo patet responsio, quod anima indita corpori, est extra originem suam. Sicut solaris calor in visceribus terrae, & dum a variis rebus movetur, patiturque, sui oblivionem & ignorantiam incurrit. Verum, sicuti calor solis, licet sit factus lapidis, & plantae, & animalis, in quodam gradu, essentia; & vitam animalis procurat, & perpetuum nutrit carcerem: nunquam tamen intermittit facere tenuitates, & sursum ad cognatum solem evolare: quem innata scientia novit: ast illata erat oblitus.

Sic nunquam anima a Deo immissa, cessat ad Deum reverti, ubi tantillum a corporea mole, & illatis passionibus liberatur, quoniam innatam notitiam non amittit, licet eius usum ob additas passiones intermittat. Quemadmodum filius semper amat patrem, licet inimicus patris sit factus ob rixas supervenientes. Hoc autem religio ostendit, quae est reversio ad suum principium.

Item somnia & ecstases, in quibus anima quasi semilibera a passionibus, negotiisque, coniungitur superis. Quod & in aegritudinibus mortiferis palam fit, teste experientia, & divo Gregorio, & Thoma, caeterisque Theologis: imo & Poetis & Philosophis melioribus: quod Aristoteles stulte irridet: sed de his in 2. & 3. Parte.

Concedo ergo hominis sapientiam accessoriam, quatenus mixtam passionibus additis, adducere delirium: sed quatenus passio, non quatenus sapientia. Ergo & anima ignorat seipsam, & quaerit seipsam propter additam deceptionem modicae alienationis. Ergo vere sapiunt qui religioni vacant: quoniam ad suum revertuntur principium & passiones superant illatas: sed secundum esse corporalis vitae stulti apparent, quoniam corpus maceratur & mors acceleratur: unde corruptio corporis subsequitur, derelicti instar extincti carbonis, vitali lucido calore privati.

Discrepantia opinionum de animae quidditate, & functionibus, nascitur ex scientia illata, non ex innata. Secundum hanc enim omnes norunt animae divinitatem: verum quia involuta est corporeo spiritu, patitur cum eo alienationes in peius ad inferiora, uti per Primalitates suas ad superiora, videtur aliis corporea, aliis incorporea, aliis calor, aliis temperies, aliis sanguis: quoniam alterationes fiunt in subiecto illi spiritu, nutrito ex sanguineo vapore atque communiter afficiuntur.

Et errant Philosophi in hoc, quoniam non omnes functiones examinant & operationes, & passiones, & accidentia, & similitudines; palam enim motus, concoctio, nutritio, agitatio, est ab igne: sensus vero in omni re quodammodo: sapere divina a Deo: ergo haec simul commista videri debuissent: sed de hoc in 2. Parte erit sermo.

Ad id vero quod probatum est, nos esse in sepulchro, & in regione mortis, & carcere: Respondemus verum esse: & hoc scire sapientiam esse: & qui hoc negat, desipere: sicut enim calor in sole vivit, & intra terram est in mortis sepulchro: sic anima in Deo vivit: At intra corpus, sepulta est. Non enim mori est annihilari, sed extra proprium bonum principiumque vitale, esse separatum.

Cyrillus contra Origenem negat, nos esse in carcere ad poenam peccati olim in caelo commissi, sed non inficiabitur, quod simus in loco exercitii ad meritum comparandum, ut probant alij doctores cum Nazianzeno & Lactantio: & Ambrosius saepe, praecipue in libro de bono mortis, in sepulchro & carcere animam esse, convincunt: & quod peregrinamur a vita Deo, ut Apostolus admonet.

Proptereaque religio quamvis innata, indiget revelatione & fide: quoniam passiones carceris nos alienant a nostri, nostraeque originis notitia: & sumus in cavea stultorum & videntium per foramina in aenigmate & ex parte. Exemplum Platonis conceditur.

Atque alia entia, diviniora sensibilibus reperiri, quod discursu scimus: imo potius sagacitate, ad quam discursus non attingit (sagacitas enim est innatae notitiae scintilla) & revelatione tandem, quasi in carceribus mundi indigemus. Deus autem indigentiae supplet.

At non propterea quod res huius mundi sunt alterius umbrae & mutantur, non datur scientia, & differentia inter somniantem & vigilantem. Nam scire quod mutantur & sunt sicut umbrae, est aliquid scire. Item somnus vocatur haec vita per comparationem ad meliorem extra corpus: neque enim hoc sciremus, nisi somnia a vigiliis prius discrevissemus.

Nec somnio est credendum aeque ac vigiliae circa corporalia. Si enim somniavi, me comedisse, non propterea sum satur, veluti cum vigilans comedo: neque si somnio me esse Romae, dum sum Neapoli, vadam surgens de lecto ad templum Capitolinum.

Revelationes autem factae in somnis, non sunt somni visiones, quae sunt imaginum remanentium ex notitiis diurnis: sed sunt ab Angelis aut a Deo, aut a daemone, aut ab aeris affectione: quae in vigilia etiam fiunt, non secus ac in somnio. Visio autem amici in vigilia & in somno, non est eadem, quemadmodum revelatio.

Qui autem ambulant in somno, somniantes ambulare, imaginationem sequuntur vehementem sopitis exterioribus organis, quando eunt corporaliter: sensus enim imaginans videt intus, quae foris viderat, & per visum interius, divinat ambulando exterius visam viam: si tamen non est noviter impedita. Namque offendiculum non declinat noctambuli, sed cadunt incursantes in ignotum impedimentum, ut experti sumus.

Et quemadmodum peritissimus cytharista ex praevia scientia digitos movet ad unamquamque chordam artificiose cytharizando, dum simul loquitur, aliudque attendit, consideratque, quam sonum: & si aliquid novi in fidibus accidit, unde offenditur musica, non considerat: ineruditus vero Tyro singulas chordas, singulasque digitorum motiones attendit, aptat, circunspicit: & si quid accidit, etiam considerat. Sic noctambuli ex praeviis notitiis gressus regunt offendiculaque nova minime circunspectant.

Sed lamiae aliquando corporaliter feruntur in nocte a daemone vectatae, aliquando in imaginatione per praesentata obiecta delusae. Ambrosius ab Angelo exequiis Martini praesentatur. Scotus putat, posse idem corpus in pluribus esse locis, quia esse praesens prius est, quam in loco: negant Thomistae. Nos hoc credimus: sed multiplicationem praesentiae esse a superis. Sed de hoc alibi: secundum ergo scientiam ordinariam vigilantibus adhibetur fides.

Sanis quoque magis quam aegris in naturalibus, inquam, secundum hominem: namque magis patitur aeger, putatque se videre plus quam videt: quia plus quam vigilans afficitur, & phantasiantis idola illi applaudunt, & fuligines, & passiones, apparitiones commiscent, ut dictum est prius.

At circa pondera imbecillioribus credendum est secundum naturam: secundum nos neque fortibus, neque flaccis: illis enim leviora, istis graviora apparent.

Circa passiones occultas aegris & imbecillioribus assentiendum, quippe qui facilius patiuntur a minimis.

Circa divina, probantibus per miracula, & transhumanationem, ut in 3. Parte declarabitur: qui enim coniungitur superioribus, dominatur inferioribus. Idcirco videntur nobis Idiotae secundum sapientiam illatam, acquisitamque, sapientissimi tamen sunt secundum inditam infusamque. Item divina patiuntur cum lucro: Physiologi vero mortalia cum detrimento, ex parte, inquam, passionis, ut dictum est. Quapropter hi non possunt de illis iudicare, sed spiritualis omnia iudicat, ut dixit Apostolus. Inventores artium numine praeeunte attigerunt: vel inspectione idearum naturae, quas scrutantur, non autem Sophistae.

Ignorantia autem, quatenus ignorantia, in his & in illis, pessima est. Qui vero novit se ignorare, sapit: qui putat se scire, quae nescit, dupliciter desipit, ignoratque, ut Lactantius Antipodas non extare. Quapropter, non omnis, qui videtur sapiens, est sapiens in omni re. Neque ignarus est omnis ignarus semper. Atqui dormiens, est quandoque vigilans, ut qui non vacat Deo, dormit Deo. Nam qui dormit exterius, vigilat interius: spiritus enim nutritioni vacat, vincendisque vaporibus infestationibusque: sicut qui Palatium claudit ut manducet, aut vulneri medeatur.

Excitat nos Apostolus a somno peccati illatarumque passionum, ad vigiliam inditi luminis, divinaeque notitiae. Hoc stultum reputatur Aristoteli putanti, nos divinis iungi non posse. Quapropter sapiunt omnes aliquid in sensu suo: sed meliores melius, quia puriori modo, vel melioribus quoque afficiuntur entibus.

## Working Translation

RESPONSE TO THE TENTH Dubitation.

where [the question is] about innate and introduced knowledge, the causes of delirium, and the knowledge of the dreamer, the sick person, the ecstatic, and of opposites.

CAP. IX. ART. V.

To the tenth, therefore, the response is clear: the soul, once placed in the body, is outside its own origin. It is like solar heat in the bowels of the earth, which, while it is moved by various things and undergoes them, falls into forgetfulness and ignorance of itself. Yet just as the sun's heat, even though it has become in some degree the essence of stone, plant, and animal, and procures the life of the animal and continually nourishes its prison, nevertheless never ceases to make rarefactions and fly upward toward its kindred sun, which it knows by innate knowledge, though by introduced knowledge it had forgotten it.

So the soul sent in from God never ceases to return to God when it is even a little freed from the bodily mass and introduced passions, because it does not lose innate knowledge, although it interrupts the use of that knowledge because of added passions. In the same way a son always loves his father, even if he has become an enemy of his father because of quarrels that have come over him. Religion shows this, for religion is a return to one's own principle.

Likewise dreams and ecstasies show it, in which the soul, as if half-free from passions and affairs, is joined to higher things. This is also made plain in mortal illnesses, as experience, Saint Gregory, Thomas, and the other theologians testify; indeed also the better poets and philosophers. Aristotle foolishly mocks this; but these things are treated in the second and third Part.

I therefore concede that human accessory wisdom, insofar as it is mixed with added passions, brings on delirium; but it does so insofar as it is passion, not insofar as it is wisdom. Therefore the soul both does not know itself and seeks itself because of the added deception of a moderate alienation. Therefore those who devote themselves to religion are truly wise, because they return to their own principle and overcome introduced passions. But with respect to the being of bodily life they appear foolish, because the body is worn down and death is hastened; hence the corruption of the body follows, like an abandoned extinguished coal deprived of vital, shining heat.

The disagreement of opinions about the soul's whatness and functions arises from introduced knowledge, not from innate knowledge. According to innate knowledge, everyone knows the soul's divinity. But because the soul is wrapped in a bodily spirit and undergoes with it alienations downward toward worse and lower things, while by its Primalities it is turned toward higher things, it appears to some as corporeal, to others incorporeal, to others heat, to others temperament, to others blood, because alterations occur in the subject, in that spirit nourished from bloody vapor, and they are affected together.

Philosophers err in this because they do not examine all functions and operations, passions, accidents, and likenesses. Motion, digestion, nutrition, and agitation are plainly from fire; sense, however, is in every thing in some way; tasting/knowing divine things is from God. Therefore these things ought to have been seen as mixed together. But there will be discussion of this in the second Part.

As to what was proved, that we are in a tomb, in the region of death, and in a prison: we answer that it is true, and that to know this is wisdom, and whoever denies it is foolish. For just as heat lives in the sun and is inside the earth in the tomb of death, so the soul lives in God; but inside the body it is buried. For to die is not to be annihilated, but to be separated outside one's proper good and vital principle.

Cyril, against Origen, denies that we are in prison as punishment for sin once committed in heaven; but he will not deny that we are in a place of exercise for acquiring merit, as other doctors prove with Nazianzen and Lactantius. Ambrose often, especially in the book *On the Good of Death*, proves that the soul is in a tomb and prison, and that we are pilgrims away from life in God, as the Apostle teaches.

Therefore religion, although innate, needs revelation and faith, because the passions of the prison alienate us from knowledge of ourselves and of our origin; and we are in the cage of fools, seeing through holes, in an enigma and in part. Plato's example is granted.

And that there are other beings, more divine than sensible things, is something we know by discourse, or rather by sagacity, which discourse does not reach. Sagacity is a spark of innate knowledge. Finally, we need revelation as though we were in the prisons of the world; and God supplies what is lacking.

But it does not follow that because the things of this world are shadows of another and change, there is no knowledge and no difference between the dreamer and the waking person. For to know that they change and are like shadows is to know something. Likewise this life is called sleep by comparison with a better life outside the body; but we would not know this unless we had first distinguished dreams from waking.

Nor should a dream be believed equally with waking in bodily matters. For if I dreamed that I had eaten, I am not for that reason full, as when I eat while awake; nor, if I dream that I am in Rome while I am in Naples, will I rise from bed and go to the Capitoline temple.

Revelations made in dreams, however, are not dream-visions, which are images remaining from daytime notions. They are from angels, or from God, or from a demon, or from an affection of the air; and these also occur in waking, no differently than in sleep. But the vision of a friend in waking and in sleep is not the same, just as revelation is not the same.

Those who walk in sleep, dreaming that they are walking, follow a vehement imagination while the exterior organs are put to sleep, when they move bodily. For the imagining sense sees inwardly what it had seen outwardly, and through inward vision it divines, while walking, the way seen outwardly, if it has not been newly obstructed. The sleepwalker does not avoid an obstacle, but falls when he runs into an unknown impediment, as we have experienced.

And just as a highly skilled harpist, from prior knowledge, moves his fingers to each string and plays artfully while at the same time speaking and attending to and considering something other than the sound; and if something new happens to the strings that disturbs the music, he does not consider it; while an untaught beginner attends to each string and each motion of the fingers, adjusts and inspects, and if anything happens, considers it too. So sleepwalkers direct their steps from prior notions and do not at all inspect new obstacles.

But witches are sometimes carried bodily in the night, conveyed by a demon, and sometimes they are deceived in imagination by objects presented to them. Ambrose is presented by an angel at Martin's funeral. Scotus thinks that the same body can be in several places, because being present is prior to being in a place; the Thomists deny it. We believe this, but that the multiplication of presence comes from higher powers. But about this elsewhere. Therefore, according to ordinary knowledge, trust is given to those who are awake.

Trust is also given more to the healthy than to the sick in natural matters, I mean according to the human measure. For the sick person undergoes more and thinks he sees more than he sees, because he is affected more than the waking person, and the images of the fantasizing power applaud him, and soot, passions, and apparitions become mixed, as was said earlier.

But regarding weights, one should believe the weaker according to nature; according to us, neither the strong nor the feeble, for to the strong things appear lighter, to the feeble heavier.

Regarding hidden passions, one should assent to the sick and the weak, since they are more easily affected by very small things.

Regarding divine things, one should assent to those who prove by miracles and transhumanation, as will be declared in the third Part: for whoever is joined to higher things rules over lower things. For this reason idiots seem to us fools according to introduced and acquired wisdom, yet they are very wise according to inborn and infused wisdom. Likewise they undergo divine things with profit, while natural philosophers undergo mortal things with loss, partly, I mean, on the side of passion, as was said. Therefore these cannot judge about those, but "the spiritual person judges all things," as the Apostle said. The inventors of the arts have touched [truth] with the divine power going before, or by inspection of the ideas of nature, which they investigate; sophists have not.

Ignorance, however, insofar as it is ignorance, is worst in both cases. But the one who knows that he is ignorant is wise; the one who thinks he knows what he does not know is doubly foolish and ignorant, as Lactantius was about the Antipodes not existing. Therefore not everyone who seems wise is wise in every matter, nor is every ignorant person always ignorant. And yet someone sleeping is at times awake, just as someone who is not free for God sleeps to God. For one who sleeps outwardly is awake inwardly: the spirit is then attending to nutrition and to overcoming vapors and infestations, as someone closes a palace in order to eat or tend a wound.

The Apostle awakens us from the sleep of sin and of introduced passions to the wakefulness of inborn light and divine knowledge. This is judged foolish by Aristotle, who thinks that we cannot be joined to divine things. Therefore all know something in their own sense; but better people know better, because they are affected in a purer mode, or also by better beings.

## Notes

- `scientia innata` / `scientia illata` extends the leaf-85 distinction between hidden innate knowledge and superadded knowledge. Here the contrast is more dynamic: innate knowledge remains, but introduced passions interrupt its use.
- `religio ... reversio ad suum principium` is the slice's strongest formula. Religion is not first a historical institution here; it is the soul's return to its origin.
- `sagacitas` earns a glossary entry because Campanella defines it as `innatae notitiae scintilla`, a spark of innate knowledge, beyond the reach of discursive reasoning.
- Dreams and waking are not flattened. Campanella grants higher dream-revelation and ecstasy, but preserves ordinary waking evidence for corporeal matters.
- The sleepwalking and harpist examples make prior knowledge operational. The body can act from prior inner images without present outward inspection; that is why new obstacles expose the difference.
- `sapientia illata/acquisita` versus `indita/infusa` gives another form of the open abdita/addita problem. The person who looks foolish under acquired standards can be wise under inborn/infused wisdom.
- The line `uti per Primalitates suas ad superiora`, followed by `sed de hoc in 2. Parte erit sermo`, makes the passage itself point toward the Part II primalities discussion. That is now an internal warrant for opening the primalities zone if the campaign follows this question.
- The `CAP. IX. ART. V.` heading was checked against the leaf-86 page image. The print has `CAP. IX.` over the left column and `ART. V.` over the right column on the same heading line under `Responsio ad Decimam Dubitationem`; this is a split heading line, not a new article beginning at `Discrepantia opinionum`.
- The closing formula keeps the Socratic thread alive: knowing ignorance is wisdom; thinking one knows what one does not know is double foolishness.

## Translation Risk

Textual: `subiecto illi spiritu` is retained awkwardly in the Latin and translated as "in that spirit"; the exact case relation wants fuller collation or comparison with another witness.

Lexical: `illata` is rendered "introduced" rather than "superadded" to keep it distinct from leaf 85's `superaddita`. This may need revision if the larger usage shows they are interchangeable.

Contextual: the `lamiae`, Ambrose/Martin, Scotus, and Thomist bilocation paragraph is dense and only lightly contextualized here. It may belong with a later religion/miracle/prophecy slice if that thread becomes active.
