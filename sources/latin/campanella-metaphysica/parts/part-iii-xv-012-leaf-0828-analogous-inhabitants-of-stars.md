## Latin

alium illi motum in 24 horis, alium Lunae pedissequae in 18 diebus, alium nubibus circa terram agnoscant.

Stellas autem fixas non esse soles puros, colorum varietas ostendit, qualis in Planetis. Siquidem Aldebaram refert Martem, Canicula Saturnum et Venerem, [Fomalhaut?][?] Iovem. At non propterea Planetarum circumvolutionibus non ambiri existimamus; immo maxime asserimus, et circa singulas fixas amphitheatrum Planetarum agnoscimus. Etenim et Planetas nostros ambiunt alii Planetae: multo ergo magis fixas longe ampliores. Ergo circumducunt radiorum vi, ex ignis Natura manantium, suos Planetas, qui sponte obaudiunt, quoniam in hoc sensantur. Et quidem haec omnia tali distantiarum proportione et luminis copia et inopia tanta, in mutuis officiis contemperavit Deus, ut omnes partes omnibus utiles sint. Et dum proprium procurant bonum, in aliarum partium Mundi bonum laborent, quemadmodum partes corporis humani mutuis emolumentis compaginatae.

Nec quia non videntur, non adsint circumlati Planetae ad fixas: nam nec ante Telescopii inventionem [innotuere?][?] Medicei. Excellentiori autem instrumento forsitan aliquando conspicientur. Nec propterea quod fixae sunt aliarum centra, erunt immobiles ut videtur Cardinali Cusano, et Copernico, et noster sol immotus in centro nostri Amphitheatri sit. Namque et centrum Mediceorum est Iuppiter, et suorum asseclarum Saturnus; nec tamen immobiles. Tellus quoque nostra, quae centri [ordinis?][?] rationem habet, non propterea sit immobilis, quod circa ipsam rotatur Sol et Luna. Nam et circa alias Stellas, illarum accolis rotari videtur, etiam mobiles. Quapropter et situm terrae, et operationem, ignotam esse suis habitatoribus visum est grandiloquis Theologis nostris, ut in speciali libello ad Cardinalem Caetanum probavimus.

Si Stellae fixae atque errantes systemata sunt solis luce, aut aliunde, vel proprio igne mixto aliis elementis lucentia: et mutuatis radiis collucentia: oportere insuper alia genera rerum, eius quae apud nos sunt, etiam in eis esse, et homines non univocos, sed Analogos nostratibus, et caetera consimiliter; ergo et Angelos rectores et forsan diabolos. Veramque beatitatem in solo mentali Mundo, quatenus Archetypo mirificatus iungitur, reperiri.

**ARTIC. VIII.**

Cum ergo compertum sit, stellas non esse prorsus igneos globos, sed ex aliis corporibus constare, sicut Luna: consequens videtur, ut non unius speciei accolas habeant, sicuti neque tellus nostra. Etenim a tot stellis et configurationibus et luminarium actionibus affectum variis astrum modis formationes varias concipiat, pariatque oportet. Cum praesertim et numeros in eis Deus sparserit, sicut distinctio in Luna ostendit; et mensuras, sicuti finitudo singulorum admonet; et pondus, quo sua ad similia feruntur omnia, et in unum centrum suae conservationis conspirant. Unitas enim sola conservat, ut in pr. et 1. p. declaratum est.

At quidem non esse univocas nostratibus species rerum viventium in aliis stellis, apprime contendimus. Luna enim habet diem aequalem quindecim diebus nostratibus, et noctem totidem noctibus. Non enim in seipsam rotatur. Similiter etiam Iupiter, quoniam non in se rotatur uti Sol, sed sicut Luna solum motum absque titubatione in Zodiaco habet, uti maculae eius semper ad nos eodem modo spectantes monent, diem habet perpetuum, qua solem intuetur; noctemque perpetuam, qua fixas, proptereaque quatuor Lunis circumdatur, quae vices temporum longe alias atque nostrae sunt, faciant; et dies Lunares longe minores nostris solaribus horariis 24. Ita de caeteris iudicandum astris accolisque proportionaliter.

Quapropter non est arbitrandum, homines nostrae speciei esse in astris, quamvis qui sub polis degunt, videantur nobis similes: quanquam diem semestrem paremque noctem habent. Hinc enim temperies communis habitationis, et influentiarum facit homines eiusdem speciei, et tamen alios cum varietate multa, quales Pygmaei, Acephali, Astomi, Cyclopes, Lestrigones, et qui nascuntur ex simiis, et hominibus: ita quidem ut varient speciem extra univocationem ad analogiam; sunt enim et homines marini, et subterranei, quasi homunculi, et satyri, nobis specie distincti apud nos.

Igitur accolas stellarum esse alterius generis putandum: et in cunctis Amphitheatris Deum extendere divitias opulentiae divinitatis suae miris modis. Ergo et Angelos ibi esse rectores, et daemones forsan. Nam quidam diabolus interrogatus respondit se habere domicilium in orbe Lunae: et quamvis mentiri soleant, non est irrationale prorsus, et Apostolus etiam spiritualia nequitiae nominat in caelestibus.

Et si qui peccaverunt ibi, uti apud nos Adam, Christus etiam per suam incarnationem illis contulisset; cum Apostolus dicat, quod pacificavit omnia, sive quae in Caelis, sive quae in Terris sunt per sanguinem crucis. Et in hymno canat Ecclesia, Sanguis unda profluit, terra, pontus, astra, mundus, quo lavantur flumine. Sed quoniam non habemus de his revelationem certam, exponendus est Apostolus, quod spiritualia nequitiae in caelestibus, sint aerea; et astra lavantur sanguine Christi, sicut et ipse mundus non respectu sui, sed hominum. Nam et Caelum immutabitur, et creatura liberabitur a servitute corruptionis in libertatem gloriae filiorum Dei, sicut dicit Apostolus, exponente Chrysostomo, quod sicuti per peccatum Adae deteriorata sunt caelestia et terrestria, ita per glorificationem humanam in sanguine Christi, omnia meliorabuntur.

Interim vero cum probatum sit esse in astris varietatem substantiarum et actionum, et passionum,

## English

recognize one motion for it in 24 hours, another for the attendant Moon in 18 days, another for the clouds around the earth.

But the variety of colors shows that the fixed stars are not pure suns, as is also the case in Planets. For Aldebaran resembles Mars, Canicula Saturn and Venus, [Fomalhaut?][?] Jupiter. Yet we do not for that reason think that they are not surrounded by the revolutions of Planets; rather, we assert this especially, and around each of the fixed [stars] we recognize an amphitheater of Planets. For other Planets surround our Planets too; therefore much more [they surround] the fixed [stars], which are far greater. Therefore by force of rays flowing from the Nature of fire they lead around their Planets, which obey spontaneously, because in this they are sensed. And indeed God has tempered all these things in such proportion of distances and in such supply and lack of light, in mutual offices, that all parts are useful to all. And while they procure their own good, they labor for the good of other parts of the World, just as the parts of the human body are joined by mutual advantages.

Nor, because they are not seen, are Planets carried around the fixed [stars] not present; for neither did the Medicean [stars] become known[?] before the invention of the Telescope. But perhaps with a more excellent instrument they will someday be seen. Nor, because the fixed [stars] are centers of others, will they be immobile, as it seems to Cardinal Cusanus and Copernicus, and as our sun is immobile in the center of our Amphitheater. For Jupiter is the center of the Mediceans, and Saturn of its attendants, and yet they are not immobile. Our Earth too, which has the character of a center of [order?][?], is not therefore immobile because the Sun and Moon are turned around it. For even around other Stars, to their inhabitants, [things] seem to rotate, even though they are mobile. Therefore both the site of the earth and its operation seemed to our grandiloquent Theologians to be unknown to its own inhabitants, as we proved in a special little book to Cardinal Cajetan.

If the fixed and wandering Stars are systems shining by the light of the sun, or from elsewhere, or by their own fire mixed with other elements, and shining together by mutually borrowed rays, it is further necessary that other genera of things, of the sort that are among us, also exist in them, and human beings not univocal but Analogous to ours, and the rest similarly; therefore also ruling Angels and perhaps devils. And true beatitude is found only in the mental World, insofar as it is joined, wondrously made, to the Archetype.

**ARTICLE VIII.**

Therefore, since it has been discovered that the stars are not wholly fiery globes, but consist of other bodies, like the Moon, it seems to follow that they have inhabitants not of one species, just as our earth does not. For from so many stars, configurations, and actions of luminaries, a star affected in various ways must conceive and bring forth various formations in various modes. Especially since God has also scattered numbers in them, as distinction in the Moon shows, and measures, as the finitude of single things warns, and weight, by which all their things are borne toward like things and conspire into one center of their conservation. For unity alone conserves, as was declared in the Proem and Part 1.

But indeed we strongly contend that the species of living things in other stars are not univocal with ours. For the Moon has a day equal to fifteen of our days, and a night of just as many nights. For it is not rotated into itself. Likewise Jupiter too, since it does not rotate in itself as the Sun does, but like the Moon has only motion without wavering in the Zodiac, as its spots always looking toward us in the same way warn, has perpetual day where it looks at the sun, and perpetual night where it looks at the fixed [stars]; and for this reason it is surrounded by four Moons, which make turns of times far different from ours, and Lunar days far smaller than our solar 24-hour [days]. So one must judge proportionally concerning the other stars and their inhabitants.

Therefore one must not judge that human beings of our species exist in the stars, although those who live under the poles seem similar to us, although they have a half-year day and an equal night. For from this a common temperament of habitation and influences makes human beings of the same species, and yet others with much variety, such as Pygmies, Acephali, Astomi, Cyclopes, Laestrygonians, and those who are born from apes and human beings; indeed in such a way that they vary species beyond univocation toward analogy. For there are also marine human beings, subterranean human beings, as if little humans, and satyrs, distinct from us by species among us.

Therefore the inhabitants of the stars must be thought to be of another genus; and in all the Amphitheaters God extends in marvelous modes the riches of the opulence of his divinity. Therefore also ruling Angels are there, and perhaps demons. For a certain devil, when interrogated, answered that he had a dwelling in the orb of the Moon; and although they are accustomed to lie, this is not wholly irrational, and the Apostle also names spiritual things of wickedness in the heavens.

And if some sinned there, as Adam did among us, Christ would have conferred [benefit] on them too through his incarnation; since the Apostle says that he pacified all things, whether those that are in the Heavens or those that are on Earth, through the blood of the cross. And in the hymn the Church sings: the wave of blood flows forth, earth, sea, stars, world are washed by that stream. But since we do not have certain revelation about these things, the Apostle must be explained that the spiritual things of wickedness in the heavens are aerial; and the stars are washed by the blood of Christ, as the world itself is too, not with respect to itself, but with respect to human beings. For Heaven too will be changed, and creation will be freed from the servitude of corruption into the freedom of the glory of the sons of God, as the Apostle says, Chrysostom explaining that just as by Adam's sin heavenly and earthly things were made worse, so by human glorification in the blood of Christ all things will be made better.

## Note

Article VIII extends the celestial-governance material into inhabited stars. Campanella allows analogous inhabitants, ruling angels, and perhaps demons in other stellar systems, while refusing a univocal "humans like us" claim. The theological caution is explicit: the speculation is constrained because there is no certain revelation about sin, incarnation, or redemption in the stars.
