## Corrected Latin

ART. III.

Omitto quod animalibus aliis alii sunt sapores. Gallinae sapit frumentum, & hominis stercus; bovi carduus & ficulnea, coturnici helleborus; quae nobis displicent aut sunt venena. Item puella veneno nutrita missa ad Alexandrum Magnum indicat gustum in assuefactione consistere, & non esse sensum scientiae, sed apparentiae. Nam & nationum usus diversi.

Si vero odoratum consulas, non sentit, nisi tenuem exhalationem, quae saepe mentitur rei substantiam. Odor enim lilii bonus est, sapor malus; odor cimicis pravus est nobis, rosae autem bonus; & tamen cimex propinquior est nostrae naturae quam rosa; illa enim animal est uti nos, non autem ista.

Item nos non sentimus odorem lapidis, neque hominis, neque ferarum, uti sentit canis, & olfactu sumus illis deteriores: & tamen quae nobis foetent, canibus olent. Ergo decipimur, aut non sentimus res, neque ex parte.

Praeterea quod aegris putet, sanis bene olet, & quod senibus olet, [iuvenibus?] displicet. & iuxta temperiem hominum alii aliis placent odores. Ergo non sunt secundum naturam nisi apparentiae ad sensum nostrum. Quod enim est in natura, est omnibus. At foetor & odor non est omnibus. Vinum enim foetet aliis, aliis olet: & alius est nobis & brutis. Foetor autem & odor sunt differentiae specificae huius generis, qui est odor, sicut homo & equus species animalis. Ergo sicut sensibus species commutantur, ita & intellectu; ergo aliis videtur homo rationalis, aliis asinus rationalis, & homo irrationalis, ut patet in disputatione Grylli, & Ulyssis apud Plutarchum; & qui sapiens videtur Scythis, nobis videtur stultus: & quod [laudatum?] illis, nobis profanum; & nihil vere sapimus neque ex parte, sed secundum apparentiam exteriorem fallacem.

Item probatur ex tactu; nam manus Medici febricitanti apparet frigida; sano vero calida. Item duorum sanorum alteri calida, alteri frigida, utri credendum est? Item aqua & vinum & aer frigida apparent tactui nostro, & tamen rationibus multis patet calida, ut probat Telesius de aqua, & omnes Philosophi de aere, excepto Cardano, & Empedocle. Praeterea Aethiopibus Regio nostra videtur frigida, nobis temperata, Scythis vero calida. Ergo calor & frigus non sunt nisi apparentiae, & respectus. Similiter pro temperamento, & aetatis differentia variantur tactus & cognitio caloris frigerisque. Quinimo manibus aliquod appositum videtur calidum, quod pectori & testiculis appositum videtur frigidum.

Idem docet Pondus, nam puero sestertia duo gravia sunt, & aegro & seni, iuveni vero levia. Cui credendum est tribus testibus, an uni? Item formicae granum frumenti grave est, nobis leve: pluma levis videtur, & tamen cadit deorsum, ergo est gravis; & nos fallimur. Item in aqua natant mille sestertia lignorum, dragma autem lapidis gravat in profundum, ergo non datur grave & leve, vel ipsorum scientia. Item multus ignis gravat sursum plus quam paucus, sicuti multum lapidis gravat deorsum plus quam parum; & in hoc deceptio est, quoniam lignum natat multum, non autem lapillus: unde Plato aestimat non dari grave & leve, ergo neque rarum, neque densum; nam rarus est vapor aquae comparatus, densus vero aeri; ergo sunt respectus, non autem entitates. Similiter molle lutum comprimenti, non autem super tangenti vermiculo. Puer puero durus, seni blandus mollisque apparet; sic de levi & aspero dices, & de ceteris tangibilibus. Et quod mirabile est, grando & glacies duriores aquis, sunt tamen leviores, quippe quae in eis supernatant. Quapropter nihil vere sapimus circa tangibilia. Nonne piscibus aqua, & arboribus nix, est calida, nobis vero frigida? & pro diversitate animalium diversae sunt apparentiae eiusdem tangibilis obiecti.

## Working Translation

ART. III.

I omit the fact that different flavors belong to different animals. Grain and human dung taste good to a hen; thistle and fig-tree to an ox; hellebore to a quail; these displease us or are poisons to us. Likewise, the girl nourished on poison and sent to Alexander the Great shows that taste consists in habituation, and is not a sense of knowledge but of appearance. For the practices of nations also differ.

But if you consult smell, it senses only a thin exhalation, which often lies about the substance of the thing. The odor of the lily is good, its flavor bad; the odor of the bedbug is bad to us, but the odor of the rose is good. And yet the bedbug is nearer to our nature than the rose, since the former is an animal as we are, while the latter is not.

Likewise we do not sense the odor of stone, or of human beings, or of wild animals as a dog senses it, and in smell we are inferior to them. Yet things that stink to us smell good to dogs. Therefore we are deceived, or we do not sense things, not even in part.

Furthermore, what stinks to the sick smells good to the healthy, and what smells good to the old [displeases the young?]. According to human temperament, different odors please different people. Therefore these are not according to nature except as appearances to our sense. For what is in nature belongs to all. But stench and odor do not belong to all. Wine stinks to some and smells good to others; it is different for us and for animals. Stench and odor are the specific differences of this genus, which is odor, just as human being and horse are species of animal. Therefore, just as species are changed in the senses, so too in the intellect: to some the human being seems rational, to others the ass rational and the human being irrational, as is clear in the dispute of Gryllus and Ulysses in Plutarch. And someone who seems wise to the Scythians seems foolish to us; what is [praiseworthy?] to them is profane to us. We know nothing truly, not even in part, but according to deceptive outward appearance.

The same is proved from touch. The physician's hand appears cold to the feverish person, but warm to the healthy person. Likewise, of two healthy people it appears warm to one and cold to the other; which is to be believed? Water, wine, and air appear cold to our touch, and yet by many reasons they are evidently warm, as Telesio proves about water and all philosophers about air, except Cardano and Empedocles. Furthermore, our region seems cold to Ethiopians, temperate to us, but warm to Scythians. Therefore heat and cold are nothing but appearances and relations. Similarly, by difference of temperament and age, touch and cognition of heat and cold vary. Indeed, something applied to the hands seems warm which, applied to the chest and testicles, seems cold.

Weight teaches the same thing. For two sesterces are heavy to a boy, and to the sick person and the old person, but light to the young man. Should three witnesses be believed, or one? Likewise, a grain of wheat is heavy to an ant, light to us; a feather seems light, and yet falls downward, therefore it is heavy, and we are deceived. Likewise in water a thousand sesterces' worth of wood floats, but a drachma of stone weighs down into the depths; therefore heavy and light are not given, or at least knowledge of them is not. Much fire weighs upward more than a little, just as much stone weighs downward more than a little; and in this there is deception, because much wood floats but a little stone does not. Hence Plato thinks that heavy and light are not given, therefore neither rare nor dense: for vapor is rare compared with water, but dense compared with air. Therefore they are relations, not entities. Similarly mud is soft to the one pressing it, but not to the little worm touching it from above. A boy appears hard to a boy, gentle and soft to an old man; say the same of light and rough, and the other tangibles. And what is remarkable, hail and ice are harder than waters, yet lighter, since they float upon them. Therefore we know nothing truly about tangibles. Is not water warm to fish, and snow warm to trees, but cold to us? According to the diversity of animals, there are diverse appearances of the same tangible object.

## Notes

- This is the first numeric page-order part. It resumes from the omitted right column of leaf 23 rather than from a new concept-chain jump.
- The slice shows why page-order units cannot be purely physical-page units. Leaf 23 breaks at the beginning of the weight argument, so the part continues into leaf 24 and stops before the next numbered sense, hearing.
- `gustus` is now more explicitly species-relative and habit-relative: the poison-fed girl and animal examples make taste a product of habituation and animal constitution, not a transparent sense of things as they are.
- Smell is weaker than taste for wisdom because it senses only a thin exhalation that can misreport substance.
- Touch and weight push the argument toward `respectus`: heat/cold, heavy/light, rare/dense, hard/soft appear less like stable entities than relations to body, medium, age, strength, and animal kind.
- The Plutarch Gryllus/Ulysses example makes sensory relativity slide into intellectual relativity: if species change in the senses, they also change in the intellect, so the human/ass/rational/irrational contrast becomes unstable across standpoints.
