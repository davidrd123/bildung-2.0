## Latin

Hominis autem studium non ad praesentem dirigit conservationem, sed ad futuram cum superis, quibuscum init commercium et de quibus inquirit. Natura autem non dedisset frustra hanc sollicitudinem infeliciter optimo animalium. Ergo evidens signum, quod aliud bonum post mortem illi reservetur atque conveniat.

Praeterea animus non cognosceret, neque inquireret, neque appeteret voluntate practica, quae non ad se pertinent bona. Cum ergo cognoscat bona immortalia et immortalitatem, conveniat illi oportet. Sicut enim natura non dedit manum frustra, neque pedem frustra, sic neque hanc intelligentiam et appetitum bonorum divinorum. Nam multo sapientior est in ordinandis quae ad animum et sapientiam spectant, quam in corporeis[?]. In his autem nihil est vanum, si spectes folia, spinas, et fructus, et cortices arborum, et usus. Homini autem non dedit cornua neque squamas, quoniam superflua essent, cum acceperit manus et ingenium, qua ista sibi parare possit, quae animalia non possunt. Ergo si corpori humano non dedit quod necessarium est, videlicet vestem villosam, ne sit superflua, quia dederat solertiam ad ista paranda, multo minus affirmare licet superfluam esse hominis inquisitionem et appetitum alterius vitae et rerum divinitatis.

Praeterea ipsa philosophia universalis religio ostendit homini aliam post mortem deberi vitam. Homo enim religans se Deo spernit vitam praesentem, ieiunat, se flagellat, surgit in nocte ad contemplandum et canendum Deum; summoque superis inhiat amore. Hanc autem religionem in cunctis nationibus invenimus. Non enim discordant religione, sed in ritu et notione divinorum, quoniam aliter aliae nationes intelligunt coluntque Deum. Sed omnes omnia posthabent religioni. Hanc autem non est ullo pacto asserendum esse vanam, quoniam omnibus hominibus convenit. Neque respublica et coetus hominum uno die absque illa subsistere possent. Neque in republica sola, sed in speluncis et nemoribus homines secedentes colunt Deum, ab eoque sperant bona aeterna, et spernunt corpus diviniore sui parte confisi. Ergo remanet aliqua vita post mortem.

Sed dicunt Averroistae et Aristotelistae hanc esse superstitionem animorum deceptorum, neque vere sapientes et potentiores religioni vili addictos esse, sed eam praedicare ut incutiant metum populis, quo sint deorum metu sibi obedientes et in officio remaneant. Sed haec sententia stulta est: nam qui religioni addicuntur non sunt rudiores et plebei modo, sed hominum sapientissimi et optimi, quos deceptos fuisse et decipere non est arbitrandum.

Praeterea veritatem religionis ostendunt miraculis[?], prophetia, et colloquio superum; et praedicant eam contra sophistas et tyrannos. Quapropter et si sit aliqua falsa religio, ipsa tamen religio non est falsa. Neque enim quia plurimi sunt sophistae, et hypocritae, et medici imperiti, et tyranni, non extant etiam philosophi veri, et viri vere probi, et medici sapientes, et principes benefici humano generi, ut infra demonstrabimus. Immo prius fuit aurum bonum quam falsum; ideo enim falsum scitur esse, quia deficit a vero et bono. Ergo et prior philosophia et religio, quam sophistica et superstitio.

Praeterea neque docebit nos Averroes quomodo fiant miracula, et futura praenoscantur, et cum angelis colloquia habeantur per religionem, si vana est. Sed illi hoc negant. Nos autem vidimus et testamur; et qui haec testantur sunt sapientes et optimi, et non loquuntur ex opinione, sed ex testimonio, quod triumphat de omnibus opinionibus, ut patuit in prima parte. Nec quia videntur impossibilia iuxta philosophiam Aristotelicam, erunt omnino impossibilia. Nam et Augustino, et Lactantio, et Xenophani, et aliis impossibile videbatur quod esset alterum hemisphaerium; multisque philosophis et astronomis videbantur inhabitabiles aequinoctialis plaga et suppolaris. Nihilominus experientia recens testatur contra opinionem et philosophiam ipsorum.

Praeterea Stoici, et Platonici, et Pythagorici, et Esseni, philosophi sanctissimi et doctissimi, qui sunt sanctitas mundi, religionem veram esse contendunt, et extare inferos et superos, et poenam et praemium post mortem. Unde Athenagoras et praedicti omnes probant vel futuram resurrectionem, vel transmigrationem in melius aut in peius, ut militia Dei ostendatur. Qui vero negant religionem sunt indocti, sophistae ac scelesti, exitium mundi, ut Aristotelici, Sadducaei, Averroes, Epicurus, et Machiavellus; et probant proprio arbitratu quod dicunt, non virtute et experientia. Sed veritas facit homines probos, falsitas scelestos. Ergo falsarii sunt hi; illi vero veridici.

Praeterea si religio non est et futura vita, ipsa natura stulta et impia esset, quae probis colentibus pietatem, continentiam, studium divinorum, iustitiam, veritatem, nullum dat praemium, sed infirmitatem, afflictionem, et sollicitudinem hanc cassam et deceptionem; improbis vero iniustis fraudulentis praesentia largitur bona, et veritarum[?], si non ipsi sed illi decipiuntur. Unde Seneca, qui ubique mortalitatem animorum docet, tandem in libro intitulato Cur bonis mali, si Deus est, concludit ex hoc argumento animas esse immortales, et post mortem esse locum iustitiae ad poenas et praemia.

## English

But the study of the human being does not direct itself to present preservation, but to a future one with higher beings, with whom he enters commerce and about whom he inquires. But nature would not have given this solicitude uselessly and unhappily to the best of animals. Therefore it is an evident sign that another good after death is reserved for him and belongs to him.

Furthermore the mind would not know, inquire into, or desire with practical will goods that do not pertain to it. Therefore, since it knows immortal goods and immortality, it must be that they belong to it. For just as nature did not give a hand in vain, nor a foot in vain, so neither [did it give] this intelligence and appetite for divine goods. For it is much wiser in ordering the things that regard mind and wisdom than in corporeal things[?]. But in these nothing is vain, if you look at leaves, thorns, fruits, tree-barks, and uses. But to the human being it did not give horns or scales, because they would be superfluous, since he has received hands and skill by which he can prepare such things for himself, which animals cannot do. Therefore if to the human body it did not give what is necessary, namely a shaggy garment, lest it be superfluous, because it had given cleverness for preparing these things, much less is it permitted to affirm that the human being's inquiry and appetite for another life and for divine things is superfluous.

Furthermore philosophy itself, universal religion, shows that another life after death is owed to the human being. For the human being, binding himself to God, despises the present life, fasts, lashes himself, rises in the night to contemplate and sing God, and gapes toward higher beings with the highest love. But we find this religion in all nations. For they do not differ in religion, but in rite and notion of divine things, because different nations understand and worship God differently. But all put all things after religion. It must not be asserted in any way that this religion is vain, because it belongs to all human beings. Nor could a republic and gathering of human beings subsist one day without it. And not only in a republic, but in caves and groves, human beings withdrawing worship God, hope for eternal goods from him, and despise the body, trusting in the more divine part of themselves. Therefore some life remains after death.

But the Averroists and Aristotelians say that this is a superstition of deceived souls, and that the truly wise and more powerful are not devoted to cheap religion, but preach it so that they may strike fear into peoples, by which, through fear of the gods, they may be obedient to them and remain in duty. But this opinion is foolish: for those who devote themselves to religion are not only the more ignorant and common people, but the wisest and best of human beings, whom it must not be judged to have been deceived and to deceive.

Furthermore they show the truth of religion by miracles[?], prophecy, and conversation with higher beings, and they preach it against sophists and tyrants. Therefore even if there is some false religion, religion itself is nevertheless not false. For neither, because very many are sophists, hypocrites, unskilled physicians, and tyrants, do true philosophers, truly upright men, wise physicians, and princes beneficial to the human race fail to exist, as we will demonstrate below. Indeed good gold was prior to false gold; for false gold is known to be false because it falls short of the true and good. Therefore philosophy and religion are also prior to sophistry and superstition.

Furthermore Averroes will not teach us how miracles happen, future things are foreknown, and conversations with angels are had through religion, if it is vain. But they deny this. We, however, have seen and testify; and those who testify to these things are wise and best, and do not speak from opinion, but from testimony, which triumphs over all opinions, as was clear in the first part. Nor because they seem impossible according to Aristotelian philosophy will they therefore be altogether impossible. For to Augustine, Lactantius, Xenophanes, and others it seemed impossible that there should be another hemisphere; and to many philosophers and astronomers the equinoctial and subpolar region seemed uninhabitable. Nevertheless recent experience testifies against their opinion and philosophy.

Furthermore the Stoics, Platonists, Pythagoreans, and Essenes, most holy and learned philosophers, who are the holiness of the world, contend that religion is true, and that lower and higher beings exist, and punishment and reward after death. Hence Athenagoras and all those named above prove either a future resurrection or transmigration into better or worse, so that the soldiery of God may be shown. But those who deny religion are unlearned, sophists and criminals, the ruin of the world, such as Aristotelians, Sadducees, Averroes, Epicurus, and Machiavelli; and they prove what they say by their own choice, not by virtue and experience. But truth makes human beings upright, falsity makes them criminal. Therefore these are falsifiers; the others are truth-tellers.

Furthermore, if there is no religion and future life, nature itself would be foolish and impious, since it gives no reward to the upright who cultivate piety, continence, study of divine things, justice, and truth, but gives infirmity, affliction, and this empty solicitude and deception; while to the impious, unjust, and fraudulent it grants present goods, and truths[?], if not they but those others are deceived. Hence Seneca, who everywhere teaches the mortality of souls, finally in the book entitled Why Are There Evils for the Good, If God Exists? concludes from this argument that souls are immortal and that after death there is a place of justice for punishments and rewards.

First, because they despise these cults and honors and hold them for nothing, and spend and afflict life itself. Second, because the same honors are given to tyrants, criminals, and false gods, to Venus, Bacchus, Alexander, Epicurus, Muhammad, and Nero; and often to those who were not in the world, such as perhaps Achilles and Orlando, as they are depicted by poets, and Roger, who never existed, and others sung by poets.
