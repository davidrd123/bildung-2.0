## Latin

Hominis studium non ad praesentem conservationem dirigitur, sed ad futuram cum superis, quibuscum init commercium et de quibus inquirit. Natura autem non dedisset frustra hanc sollicitudinem infeliciter optimo animalium. Ergo evidens signum est quod aliud bonum post mortem illi reservetur atque conveniat.

Anima non cognosceret neque inquireret neque appeteret voluntate practica quae non ad se pertinent bona. Cum ergo cognoscat bona immortalia et immortalitatem, conveniat illi oportet. Sicut natura non dedit manum frustra neque pedem frustra, sic neque hanc intelligentiam et appetitum bonorum divinorum.

Praeterea ipsa philosophia universalis religio ostendit homini aliam post mortem deberi vitam. Homo enim religans se Deo spernit vitam praesentem, ieiunat, se flagellat, surgit in nocte ad contemplandum et canendum Deum, summoque superis inhiat amore. Hanc religionem in cunctis nationibus invenimus. Non discordant religione, sed ritu et notione divinorum.

Non est ullo pacto asserendum hanc esse vanam, quoniam omnibus hominibus convenit. Neque respublica et coetus hominum uno die absque illa subsistere possent. Neque in republica sola, sed in speluncis et nemoribus homines secedentes colunt Deum, ab eoque sperant bona aeterna, et spernunt corpus diviniore sui parte confisi.

Dicunt Averroistae et Aristotelistae hanc esse superstitionem animorum deceptorum, neque vere sapientes et potentiores religioni additos esse, sed eam praedicare ut incutiant metum populis. Sed haec sententia stulta est: qui religioni addicuntur non sunt rudiores et plebei modo, sed hominum sapientissimi et optimi.

Veritatem religionis ostendunt miracula, prophetia, et colloquium superum. Et si sit aliqua falsa religio, ipsa tamen religio non est falsa. Neque enim quia plurimi sunt sophistae, hypocritae, medici imperiti, et tyranni, non extant etiam philosophi veri, viri vere probi, medici sapientes, et principes benefici humano generi.

Qui haec testantur sunt sapientes et optimi, et non loquuntur ex opinione sed ex testimonio, quod triumphat de omnibus opinionibus. Nec quia videntur impossibilia iuxta philosophiam Aristotelicam erunt omnino impossibilia. Augustino, Lactantio, Xenophani, et aliis impossibile videbatur alterum hemisphaerium; experientia recens testatur contra opinionem et philosophiam ipsorum.

Stoici, Platonici, Pythagorici, et Esseni, philosophi sanctissimi et doctissimi, religionem veram esse contendunt, et extare inferos et superos, poenam et praemium post mortem. Qui vero negant religionem sunt indocti, sophistae, scelesti, exitium mundi: Aristotelici, Sadducaei, Averroes, Epicurus, et Machiavellus.

## English

Human concern is not directed only toward present preservation. It reaches toward a future with higher beings, with whom humans enter commerce and about whom they inquire. Nature would not have given this concern in vain to the best animal; it is a sign that another good after death is reserved for humans.

The soul would not practically know, inquire into, or desire goods that do not belong to it. If it knows immortal goods and immortality, those goods must in some way fit it. Nature does not give a hand or foot in vain; still less would it give intelligence and appetite for divine goods in vain.

Universal religion proves the same. Humans bind themselves to God, despise present life, fast, flagellate themselves, rise at night to contemplate and sing God, and burn with love for higher beings. Religion is found among all nations. Nations differ not in having religion, but in rite and understanding of divine things.

The Averroist-Aristotelian reduction of religion to useful superstition is dismissed as stupid. Religious people are not only crude commoners but the wisest and best humans. Miracles, prophecy, and colloquy with higher beings show religion's truth. False religions do not make religion false, just as bad physicians do not abolish medicine.

The standard of evidence is testimony, not Aristotelian possibility. Recent experience has refuted things earlier philosophers deemed impossible, such as the other hemisphere and inhabitable equatorial regions. So the Aristotelian judgment of impossibility cannot rule out miracle, prophecy, or angelic commerce.

## Note

Religion now becomes evidence inside the immortality proof. Campanella's argument is not merely sociological universality; religion is treated as an appetite and practice that would be vain if no post-mortem good corresponded to it. The anti-Aristotelian Columbus/antipodes pattern reappears as a rule for not letting inherited philosophy veto testimony.
