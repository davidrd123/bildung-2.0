## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] ad deficientias et deformitates actuum; quorum solummodo est permissio et ordinatio. Ergo peccavit Adam, et peccant homines, Deo permittente et ordinante peccatum ad sui gloriam in punitione iusta, eius potentiam manifestante; non autem faciente, nisi actum Physicum substratum peccatis moralibus. Alii vero et hunc actum permitti solum putant, et non fieri a Deo immediate. Alii etiam mortes sequutas ex primo peccato permitti volunt, non fieri: ergo multo minus [suffocationes?][?] hominum in mari, et a Daemone, et clades, et pestes.

Ad quos sequitur quod neque casus in arte et natura a Deo sint, nisi defectu secundarum causarum, a Deo non perpetrato. Et cum Christus dixit quod nec passer cadit absque voluntate Patris in laqueum, hi exponunt absque scientia Patris.

Alii dicunt quod quamvis mors sit sequuta ex peccato, non tamen contra Naturam, quae impediebatur ab originali iustitia ne fieret; qua sublata per peccatum, facta est mors ex elementorum naturali dissidio. Et sic omnes mortes, et hominum et brutorum, et elementorum pugnas volunt fieri secundum Naturam. Etenim corruptiones esse propter generationes; et mortem pecudis ob vitam lupi, et a Deo Physice concurrente in generativis, destructivis, Physicis et Moralibus fieri et gubernari, defectus autem solos permitti.

Nos autem probavimus in secunda Parte divinam providentiam, praedestinationem, et concursum ad omnia. Et quoniam quaedam solummodo ordinantur a Deo ut non Entia et nihilitates adnexas Physicis et Moralibus; Entia vero fieri et ordinari, omnesque Entitates. Neque ad supernaturalia efficaciam auxilii divini in nobis esse a nobis nisi meritorie ex primo auxilio aliquando, cum sumus nostri compotes, sed a Deo semper esse effective, et in nobis et infantibus, prout in Theologicis demonstravimus.

Et quomodo ad mortes physicas concurrit Deus, ut ordinator et actor cum causis agentibus; cum passivis vero tantummodo ut ordinator. Neque enim quicquam pati Deus, aut nihilitatis rerum fieri particeps, ob sui infinitam potentiam habet. Quod autem a Deo ordinentur, manifestat mirificus usus horum defectuum et ipsius nihili, quo res distinguuntur et constant in suis existentiis. Quod vero propter melius permittantur quae videntur casualia praeter intentionem fieri, manifestat eventus, a gubernatore institutus.

Nam et monstra et florum casus mirabilem habent usum, tam ad potestativi quam ad cognoscitivi quam ad volitivi exercitium, et occasiones, et distinctiones, et varietatem rerum ex eis deductam, ut dictum est in secunda Parte. In artibus quoque valet casus olei in vestimentum, ut simus diligentiores et antidoti investigatores, ac per hoc rerum Naturae ad quid valeat doctiores, et ad reprehensionem mutuam, et mutationem cogitationum, actionum, et naturarum alterationem, et ingeniorum. Similiter peccata moralia ad huiusmodi multa valent in Repub., ut [semitonia?][?] ad pulcherrimam varietatem, et commendationem libertatis nostrae et iustitiae humanae ac divinae, ad studium contra mala; et, ut dicebat Lactantius, neque virtus subsisteret sublatis malis. Potius autem dicerem, nec declarabitur. Nam divus Thomas etiam mundum meliorem futurum fuisse sine malis culpae Moralibus, non autem sine Physicis, nempe corruptione et pugna.

Mala enim physica sunt ordinabilia ad maius vel aequale bonum; et ideo permissa volitaque per accidens, quoniam sunt contra bonum particulare tantum. Mala autem moralia, cum sint contra Deum bonum universale, non sunt ordinabilia ad maius vel aeque bonum vel minus; et ideo non sunt volita a Deo per se nec per accidens. Sed permissa physice, permissione cadente super conditione hominis in libertate, ut faciat quidquid vult, etiam quae lege in omni Deus vetat; non autem permittit unquam legaliter, permissione cadente super actus malos ut sic.

Quod autem deinde vertantur in bonum aliquod vel minus, hoc fit a Deo miraculose benignissimo ac potentissimo, non ab ordinabilitate mali. Item, prosunt Deo mirificante nobis mala iterum. Neque enim si ista putaremus non esse mala, investigaremus virtute et virtutum parente Deum. Peccata etiam contra Deum non indicant divinam imbecillitatem ad cohibendum ea, exque suis operibus eradicandum, sed admonent nos nostrae libertatis, et investigationem Dei fontis bonorum pariunt, et unde sint mala ac bona, et tandem misericordias Dei ad tolerandum et purgandum crimina et remedia. Unde S. Greg.: O certe necessarium, canit, Adae peccatum; nihil enim nobis nasci profuit nisi redimi profuisset. O felix culpa, quae talem ac tantum meruit habere redemptorem.

Unde videmus mundorum ad invicem copulationem per hos defectus etiam fieri. Egrediuntur enim Mentes ab Archetypo ad Mathematicum et corporalem mundum, et ingrediuntur ex corporali in mentalem ad Archetypum per plures vias, quarum occasiones sunt isti defectus. Nec enim indigeret gubernatore civitas si in sua institutione prima perpetuo perseveraret; neque navis magistro et nautis, si ad destinatum portum per se iret, neque a ventis et undis, neque a propria stupiditate vel imperitia timeret. Ratio ergo gubernationis ad finem est ex rerum deficientia; similiter etiam copulationis nobiliorum cum vilioribus rebus. Indigent enim haec illis, ut serventur ad finem; illae vero his vel ad defectus proprios refarciendos, ut homo cibo, vel ad gloriam et beneficentiam effundendam, ut orator auditoribus et dives pauperibus, at Diabolus cultoribus ad falsam gloriam. Aequalibus vero ut simul conserventur ex coexistentia.

De praescientia, providentia, praedestinatione, electione, reprobatione, gubernatione, communes notitia.

Praescire est antequam sint res videre, vel in suis causis, uti Astrologus Eclypsim et politicus bellum, vel etiam in sua simul existentia, ut Deus; cuius praescire non est vere ante scire nisi ad sensum nostrum, cum in Deo non sint ante nec post, sed supra scire. Etenim aeternitatis cacumen habet unicum intuitum ad praeterita et futura ut praesentia, sicut dictum est in 2. P. Praescire autem infallibilitatem quidem, sed nullam in rebus ponit necessitatem, neque Entitatem, sed positam ponendam videt. Est enim opus solius cognoscitivi; neque velle seu amare ponit, neque praevelle, sed visam positam vel ponendam praesupponit, neque posse ponit aliquid-

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] to deficiencies and deformities of acts, of which there is only permission and ordering. Therefore Adam sinned, and human beings sin, with God permitting and ordering sin toward his own glory in just punishment, manifesting his power, but not making [sin], except for the Physical act underlying moral sins. Others, however, think that even this act is only permitted and is not made immediately by God. Others also want deaths following from the first sin to be permitted, not made; therefore much less [suffocations?][?] of human beings at sea, and from a Demon, and disasters, and plagues.

For these people it follows that cases in art and nature are not from God, except by a defect of secondary causes not perpetrated by God. And when Christ said that not even a sparrow falls into a snare without the will of the Father, they explain it as without the Father's knowledge.

Others say that although death followed from sin, it was not against Nature, which had been impeded by original justice from happening; when this was removed through sin, death came about from the natural strife of the elements. And thus they want all deaths, of human beings and beasts, and the battles of the elements, to happen according to Nature. For corruptions exist for the sake of generations; and the death of a sheep for the life of a wolf, and with God physically concurring in generative, destructive, physical, and moral things, are made and governed, but defects alone are permitted.

But we proved in the second Part divine providence, predestination, and concurrence toward all things. And since certain things are only ordered by God as non-Beings and nothingnesses annexed to physical and moral things, while Beings and all Entities are made and ordered. Nor is the efficacy of divine aid in us toward supernatural things from us except meritoriously from the first aid at some time, when we are in possession of ourselves; but it is always from God effectively, both in us and in infants, as we demonstrated in the theological writings.

And [we showed] how God concurs toward physical deaths as orderer and actor with active causes, but with passive causes only as orderer. For God does not have it in himself to suffer anything or to become a participant in the nothingness of things, because of his infinite power. But that they are ordered by God is manifested by the marvelous use of these defects and of nothing itself, by which things are distinguished and stand in their own existences. And that the things that seem to happen casually beyond intention are permitted for the sake of something better is manifested by the event instituted by the governor.

For monsters too and the fall of flowers have a marvelous use, both for the exercise of the potestative, the cognitive, and the volitive, and for occasions, distinctions, and the variety of things drawn from them, as was said in the second Part. In the arts too the fall of oil onto clothing has value, so that we may be more diligent and investigators of an antidote, and through this more learned about the things of Nature and what each is useful for, and for mutual correction, and the alteration of thoughts, actions, natures, and talents. Similarly moral sins are useful for many such things in the Republic, like [semitones?][?] for the most beautiful variety, and for commending our freedom and human and divine justice, for zeal against evils; and, as Lactantius used to say, virtue would not subsist if evils were removed. I would rather say: nor would it be declared. For St. Thomas too [held] that the world would have been better without moral evils of fault, but not without physical evils, namely corruption and strife.

For physical evils are orderable to a greater or equal good; and therefore they are permitted and willed accidentally, since they are against only a particular good. But moral evils, since they are against God, the universal good, are not orderable to a greater or equally good or lesser [good]; and therefore they are not willed by God through themselves or accidentally. Rather, they are permitted physically, with permission falling upon the condition of the human being in freedom, so that he may do whatever he wishes, even those things that God forbids in every law; but he never permits legally, with permission falling upon evil acts as such.

But that afterward they are turned toward some good, or lesser [evil], this happens by God most miraculously benign and powerful, not from the orderability of evil. Likewise, evils are useful to us again when God makes them marvelous. For if we did not think these things to be evils, we would not seek God through virtue and as the parent of virtues. Sins against God also do not indicate divine weakness in restraining them and eradicating them from his works, but warn us of our freedom, and produce the investigation of God as the source of goods, and [the question] whence evils and goods come; and finally [they disclose] the mercies of God for tolerating and purging crimes and remedies. Hence St. Gregory: "O certainly necessary," he sings, "sin of Adam; for it would have profited us nothing to be born unless it had profited us to be redeemed. O happy fault, which merited to have such and so great a redeemer."

Hence we see that the coupling of the worlds to one another also happens through these defects. For Minds go out from the Archetype to the Mathematical and corporeal world, and enter from the corporeal into the mental toward the Archetype by many ways, whose occasions are these defects. For a city would not need a governor if it persevered forever in its first institution; nor would a ship [need] master and sailors if it went by itself to the destined port and feared neither winds and waves nor its own stupidity or lack of skill. Therefore the reason for governance toward an end is from the deficiency of things; likewise also [the reason] for the coupling of nobler things with baser things. For these need those, so that they may be preserved toward the end; but those need these either to repair their own defects, as the human being [needs] food, or to pour out glory and beneficence, as an orator [needs] hearers and a rich person [needs] the poor, while the Devil [needs] worshipers for false glory. But equals [need one another] so that they may be conserved together from coexistence.

Common notes on foreknowledge, providence, predestination, election, reprobation, governance.

To foreknow is to see things before they are, either in their causes, as an Astrologer [foresees] an eclipse and a politician [foresees] war, or even in their simultaneous existence, as God does; for his foreknowing is not truly knowing before except according to our sense, since in God there is no before or after, but knowing above. For the summit of eternity has one single intuition toward past and future as present, as was said in Part 2. But foreknowing does indeed posit infallibility, but it posits no necessity in things, nor Entity; rather, it sees [the thing] posited or to-be-posited. For it is the work of the cognitive alone; it does not posit willing or loving, nor pre-willing, but presupposes the thing seen as posited or to-be-posited, nor does it posit the ability to do anything-

## Note

This page gives the strongest Liber XV reason for opening the gap before continuing Liber XVI: governance is generated by deficiency. Defects, accidents, physical evil, moral evil, and even the fallen ink-drop on the page become occasions by which higher and lower worlds are coupled. The page also starts Campanella's technical distinction between foreknowledge and necessity: foreknowledge is cognitive and infallible, but it does not itself posit the entity or necessity of what is foreknown.
