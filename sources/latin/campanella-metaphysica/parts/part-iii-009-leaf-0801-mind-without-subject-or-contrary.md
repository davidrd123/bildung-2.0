# Part III 009: Mind Without Subject or Contrary

## Latin

Quod autem subiecto careat patet, quoniam subsistit spiritus qui est anima brutorum. Mens autem nobilior est spiritu et calore; ergo multo magis ipsa subsistit. Unde sanctus Ambrosius probat animam non esse formam corporis contra Aristotelem; idem etiam probarunt veteres theologi, Iustinus, Chrysostomus, Augustinus, Gregorius, Origenes, et alii.

Quamvis in Clementinis decretum sit animam esse formam corporis, decretum illud non contra doctores suos protulit Ecclesia nec contra Mosen, qui spiraculum vitae organizato formatoque corpori animam introducit, sed contra Averroistas tenentes esse unicam animam cunctis hominibus adsistentem ab extra. Secundum sensum, non secundum verba, interpretandum est.

Forma nulla corporis extenditur extra subiectum suum, a quo possidetur; sed mens extenditur extra corpus et extra mundum corporeum. Ergo non est a corpore et elementatione, quorum est mortalitas, sed ab infinito immortali principio. Sicut lux in aere est forma aeris, sed pendens a sole, non ab aere.

Ipsa anima cognoscit contraria, ut calorem et frigus, lucem et tenebras, et unum per aliud. Iudicat enim anima de frigore et calore quod sint contraria, quoniam patitur ab eis contrario modo; sed iudicium illud non est contrarium, quoniam spectat ad primalitatem Deo proximam, in quo non est contrarietas.

Mens spiritui inexistit ut lux in aere: quo abeunte ipsa non abit nec corrumpitur, tamen afficitur cum spiritu et iudicat. Extra corpus alio modo ipsam iudicaturam puto, prout Thomas docet; tunc contraria visa in ideis videntur non esse contraria.

Potestas mentis immortalitatem indicat, quando nullis doloribus nullisque malis corpus et spiritum destruentibus cedit, tortores spernit, mortem non timet, in sui perennitate confidentiam abditam spem reponens. Hoc martyres, philosophi, et mulierculae gentiles comprobarunt.

Secundo probatur immortalitas ex eo quod intelligit in infinitum et appetit in infinitum. Operatio infinita indicat virtutem infinitam, non quidem entitate, ergo duratione. Intelligere et appetere contracta non sunt; ideo operatio secundum primalitates recte indicat infinitatem durationis in tempore aeterno.

Praeterea anima intelligit ideas universales aeternas; ergo est similis illis in immortalitate. Non sistit intelligentiam in universali sicut bruta, quae noscunt herbam in universali ex similitudine aliarum herbarum, sed intelligit ideam herbae unde omnes originem habent.

## English

Campanella argues that the mind lacks a corrupting subject. Even the spirit that is the soul of beasts subsists; the human mind, nobler than spirit and heat, must subsist still more. Church language about the soul as form of the body should be read against Averroists, not against Ambrose, Augustine, Chrysostom, Origen, Moses, and the older theological line that the soul does not depend on body.

A bodily form does not extend beyond the subject that possesses it. But the mind extends beyond its body and even beyond the corporeal world; therefore it does not arise simply from body and elements, whose condition is mortality. It is like light in air: a form of the air while present there, but depending on the sun rather than on the air.

The mind knows contraries without being contrary in its judging act. The corporeal spirit may be affected by heat and cold in opposite ways, but the judgment that they are opposites belongs to the active primality-near power where there is no contrariety. Outside the body, Campanella thinks the soul will judge differently: contraries seen in ideas are no longer contrary in the same way.

The mind's power appears in endurance. Martyrs, philosophers, and even pagan women despise torture and death by a hidden confidence in their own permanence. Its immortality is also shown by infinite understanding and infinite appetite: not infinite entity all at once, as in eternity, but infinite duration through successive knowing and desiring. Finally, it knows not only the animal universal but the eternal idea from which universals derive.

## Note

This leaf refines the Caput IV criterion. The mind lacks subject because it subsists more strongly than animal spirit; it lacks contrary because judgment of contraries is not itself contrary. Campanella's proof is not pure introspection: theology, martyrdom, infinite appetite, and knowledge of ideas are all folded into one metaphysical argument.
