## Latin

Quod autem subiecto careat patet, quoniam subsistit spiritus, qui est anima brutorum. Mens autem nobilior est spiritu et calore; ergo multo magis ipsa subsistit. Unde Sanctus Ambrosius De Isaac probat animam non esse formam corporis contra Aristotelem. Idem etiam probarunt veteres theologi, Iustinus, Chrysostomus, Augustinus, Gregorius, Origenes, et alii.

Et quamvis in Clementinis decretum sit animam esse formam corporis, decretum illud non contra doctores suos protulit Ecclesia, nec contra Mosen, qui spiraculum vitae organizato formatoque corpori animam, non ut formam, introducit; sed contra Averroistas, tenentes esse unicam animam cunctis hominibus adsistentem ab extra. Et secundum sensum, non secundum verba, interpretandum est, ut docet Sanctus Thomas. Item forma ibi accipitur communi modo pro eo quod est in alio, sive dependeat ab eo, sive non. Theologi namque omnes docent animam a corpore non dependere, neque possideri, et propriam subsistentiam habere; neque eo corrupto corrumpi. Haec autem omnia contingunt formis veris, tam artificiatis quam naturalibus, teste Natura et Aristotele.

Praeterea forma nulla corporis extenditur extra subiectum suum, a quo possidetur; sed mens extenditur extra corpus et extra mundum corporeum. Ergo non a corpore et elementatione, quorum est mortalitas, sed ab infinito immortali principio. Ergo licet sic forma, non pendet a subiecto, nec possidetur; sicut lux in aere est forma aeris, sed pendens a sole, non ab aere.

Praeterea ipsa anima cognoscit contraria, ut calorem et frigus, lucem et tenebras, et unum per aliud; et videntur in ea non esse contraria. Et licet ostenderimus in spiritu animali contraria esse, et contrario modo afficere ipsum, alioquin non cognoscerentur esse contraria, hoc tamen est in parte passiva eius et receptiva, non autem in iudicante activa. Iudicat enim anima de frigore et de calore quod sint contraria, quoniam patitur ab eis contrario modo; sed tamen iudicium illud non est contrarium, quoniam spectat ad primalitatem Deo proximam, in quo non est contrarietas, ut ostensum est in prima parte, quae quidem orta est inter similia idem appetentia insufficiens obiectum[?].

At quomodo a pluribus contrariis simul afficiatur mens contrario modo - alioquin non essent agnita ut contraria - difficile scimus. Sed declaravimus spiritum corporeum pati a contrariis in diversis sui partibus contrario modo, et tunc iudicare contraria. Quoniam autem mens spiritui inexistit, ut lux in aere, quo abeunte ipsa non abit nec corrumpitur, tamen afficitur cum spiritu et iudicat. Idcirco extra corpus alio modo ipsam iudicaturam puto, prout Divus Thomas docet. Nam tunc contraria visa in ideis videntur non esse contraria: contrarietas enim in eis postea accidit ob cupidinem occupandi eandem materiam; et in suis causis radicaliter omnia esse unum, et distinctionem esse a gradibus participationis idearum.

Et ad hanc notitiam Telesius non elevat mentem, quippe qui sensibilia tantummodo tractat, prout sunt, et origines et fines rerum non investigat. Idcirco putat non nisi a corporeo spiritu contraria percipi simul, quod Aphrodisiensem torsit.

Item potestas activa mentis eius immortalitatem indicat, quando nullis doloribus nullisque malis corpus et spiritum destruentibus cedit, tortores spernit, mortem non timet, in sui perennitate confidentia abdita spem reponens. Et hoc martyres, et philosophi, et mulierculae comprobarunt gentiles.

Secundo probatur mentis humanae immortalitas ex eo quod intelligit in infinitum et appetit in infinitum, ut probatum est in praecedenti sermone. Operatio autem infinita indicat virtutem infinitam, non quidem entitate, ergo duratione. Et quamvis calor calefaciat in infinitum, tamen haec actio est contracta ad calefacere. Intelligere autem et appetere contractae non sunt; unde et calor, qui est contractus, potest corrumpi ab alio, sed non per se a se ipso deficere, cum sit proles infinitae causae. Anima vero, quia non est contracta, caret contrario; idcirco eius operatio secundum primalitates indicat recte eius infinitatem. Et quia hoc intelligere et appetere non est simul, sed quasi syllogisticum post aliud, idcirco non infinitatem entitatis seu aeternitatis, quae est omnia simul, sed durationis in tempore aeterno indicant successive.

Nam praeterea non modo ambit cogitando et appetendo omnia corpora, unde probatum est quod non sit corporea, quia nulla pars est maior toto; sed etiam ambit omnia tempora praeterita et futura, semper anterius et ulterius cogitando, et facit universale, quod ubique et semper est. Ergo non temporalis nec localis res, quia pars esset maior toto.

Praeterea anima intelligit ideas universales aeternas; ergo est similis illis in immortalitate. Ubi nota, quod Peripateticorum ratio sumpta ex eo quod universale est effectus et non causa, et inexistit mortalibus et non subsistit, et dicitur aeternum non positive, sed negative, quia secundum quod universale non repugnat ei sempiternitas. Universale vero ideale est causa, et divinitatis aeternae participatio, quae est semper in mente prima. Idcirco ex huius ratione recte concluditur animae immortalitas.

Huius autem notio ex illius notione pendet, quoniam anima nostra non sistit intelligentiam in illo, sicut bruta, quae noscunt herbam in universali ex similitudine aliarum herbarum; sed adhuc intelligit ideam herbae, unde omnes originem habent. Et velit nolit Aristoteles, illam intelligit. Nam in artificiatis est palam. Ergo et in naturalibus, et quicunque Deum auctorem mundi norunt, illas asserunt.

Praeterea homo non modo ea quae ad praesentem spectant vitam intelligit, sed superos et inferos et Deum; et anxie disquirit, utrum sint et quid sint; eorumque societatem appetit. Natura autem non dedit animalibus sapientiam ultra quam necesse est praesenti vitae. Unde leporibus et cervis timidis dedit agilitatem ad fugam; leonibus et apris fortitudinem; araneae sagacitatem ad texendas telas in captionem muscarum; aquilae acumen, ut a longe contempletur escam; lupo auditum validum, ut praevideat in nocte ubi sit pecus et custos, et quomodo venetur escam. Et tandem nullius scientia se extendit ultra conservationem vitae praesentis. Unde et prophetia alcyonum extenditur ad tempestates maris, quoniam ibi nidificant; pecudum et boum ad aereas, ut praeparent sibi stabulum, aut esca repleant ventrem, ne succumbant cum tempestate.

## English

But that it lacks a subject is clear, since the spirit which is the soul of beasts subsists. But mind is nobler than spirit and heat; therefore it subsists much more. Hence Saint Ambrose, in On Isaac, proves against Aristotle that the soul is not the form of the body. The old theologians also proved the same: Justin, Chrysostom, Augustine, Gregory, Origen, and others.

And although it was decreed in the Clementines that the soul is the form of the body, the Church did not put forward that decree against its own doctors, nor against Moses, who introduces the breath of life, the soul, into an organized and formed body, not as form, but against the Averroists who hold that there is one soul assisting all human beings from outside. And it must be interpreted according to the sense, not according to the words, as Saint Thomas teaches. Likewise, form is taken there in a common way for that which is in another, whether it depends on it or not. For all theologians teach that the soul does not depend on the body, is not possessed by it, and has its own subsistence, nor is it corrupted when the body is corrupted. But all these things happen to true forms, both artificial and natural, with Nature and Aristotle as witness.

Furthermore, no form of a body extends outside its own subject, by which it is possessed; but mind extends outside the body and outside the corporeal world. Therefore it is not from body and elementation, to which mortality belongs, but from an infinite immortal principle. Therefore, even if it is form in this way, it does not depend on the subject and is not possessed by it, just as light in air is the form of the air, but depends on the sun, not on the air.

Furthermore the soul itself knows contraries, such as heat and cold, light and darkness, and one through the other; and in it they seem not to be contraries. And although we have shown that contraries are in the animal spirit, and affect it in a contrary way - otherwise they would not be known to be contraries - nevertheless this is in its passive and receptive part, not in the active judging part. For the soul judges concerning cold and heat that they are contraries, because it suffers from them in a contrary way; yet that judgment is not contrary, because it looks to the primality nearest to God, in whom there is no contrariety, as was shown in the first part, which indeed arose among like things seeking the same insufficient object[?].

But how the mind is affected by several contraries at once in a contrary way - otherwise they would not be recognized as contraries - we know with difficulty. Yet we declared that the corporeal spirit suffers from contraries in diverse parts of itself in a contrary way, and then judges contraries. But since mind in-exists in spirit as light in air, when the spirit goes away the mind does not go away nor is corrupted; nevertheless it is affected with spirit and judges. Therefore I think that outside the body it will judge in another way, as Saint Thomas teaches. For then contraries seen in ideas seem not to be contraries: contrariety afterwards happens in them because of the desire to occupy the same matter; and in their causes all things are radically one, and distinction comes from the degrees of participation in ideas.

And Telesio does not raise the mind to this knowledge, since he treats only sensible things as they are and does not investigate the origins and ends of things. Therefore he thinks that contraries are perceived together only by the corporeal spirit, which tormented Aphrodisias.

Likewise the active power of the mind indicates its immortality when it yields to no pains and no evils destroying body and spirit, despises torturers, does not fear death, and places hope in a hidden confidence in its own permanence. Martyrs, philosophers, and pagan women have proved this.

Second, the immortality of the human mind is proved from the fact that it understands into infinity and desires into infinity, as was proved in the preceding discourse. But an infinite operation indicates an infinite power, not indeed in entity, therefore in duration. And although heat heats into infinity, nevertheless this action is contracted to heating. But understanding and desiring are not contracted; hence heat, which is contracted, can be corrupted by another, but cannot fail from itself through itself, since it is the offspring of an infinite cause. But the soul, because it is not contracted, lacks a contrary; therefore its operation according to the primalities rightly indicates its infinity. And because this understanding and desiring is not simultaneous, but almost syllogistic, one thing after another, therefore they indicate not infinity of entity or of eternity, which is all things at once, but infinity of duration in eternal time, successively.

For furthermore it not only surrounds all bodies by thinking and desiring, whence it has been proved that it is not corporeal, because no part is greater than the whole; it also surrounds all past and future times, always thinking earlier and further, and makes the universal, which is everywhere and always. Therefore it is not a temporal or local thing, because the part would be greater than the whole.

Furthermore the soul understands eternal universal ideas; therefore it is similar to them in immortality. Here note that the argument of the Peripatetics, taken from the fact that the universal is an effect and not a cause, and in-exists in mortal things and does not subsist, and is called eternal not positively but negatively, because insofar as it is universal, sempiternity does not conflict with it. But the ideal universal is a cause and a participation in eternal divinity, which is always in the first mind. Therefore from the reason of this universal the immortality of the soul is rightly concluded.

But knowledge of this depends on knowledge of that, because our soul does not stop its intelligence in that [animal universal], as beasts do, which know grass in universal from the likeness of other grasses; but it further understands the idea of grass, from which all grasses have their origin. And whether Aristotle wants it or not, it understands that idea. For in artificial things this is plain. Therefore also in natural things, and whoever knows God as author of the world asserts those ideas.

Furthermore the human being understands not only the things which regard the present life, but higher beings, lower beings, and God; and anxiously inquires whether they are and what they are; and desires their society. But nature did not give animals wisdom beyond what is necessary for present life. Hence to timid hares and deer it gave agility for flight; to lions and boars strength; to the spider sagacity for weaving webs to catch flies; to the eagle sharpness, so that it may look at food from afar; to the wolf strong hearing, so that in the night it may foresee where the flock and the guard are, and how it may hunt food. And finally the science of none extends beyond the conservation of present life. Hence the prophecy of halcyons extends to storms of the sea, because they nest there; that of sheep and oxen to storms of the air, so that they may prepare stable for themselves, or fill their belly with food, lest they succumb with the storm.
