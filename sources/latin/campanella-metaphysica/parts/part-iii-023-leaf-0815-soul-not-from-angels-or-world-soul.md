# Part III 023: Soul Not from Angels or World-Soul

Source: JP2 leaf `0815`, Part III, Liber XIV, Caput VI conclusion and Caput VII opening. Printed page not collated.

## Latin

Si mens nudae materiae unitur, ad quid praefuit spiritus et laboriosa organizatio? Si ut mens aliam faciat ex ea, quid oportuit eam recte factam corrumpi? Cur mens, si subito tam divinum opus facit et praecedens delet, non etiam subito fractum crus reficit?

Homo generat hominem, quia corpus et spiritum sentientem adeo producit quod tandem Deus perficit opus eius, non autem destruit. In rana ex non rana, ubi nulla est motio generantis, formatio fit iuxta ideam ab anima mundi, caloris opera utente, sicut in seminibus.

Homo non est imperfectior brutis: generat corpus et spiritum sicut bruta, et insuper disponit ad susceptionem mentis a Deo, qui nonnisi perfectissimo opificio eam immittit. Deus cooperatur naturae, non peccato: enti, non nihilo, communicat; ens facit, non defectum.

Animam esse partem hominis dicimus, et in seminis potentia receptiva, non autem activa contineri. Agit enim seminalis spiritus opificium capax mentis a Deo infundendae. Partes corporeae et animales sunt in semine actu explicandae in opus; mens autem excedit naturam et a Deo expectatur.

Spiritus humanus efformat organa aptanda humanae menti. Mens licet dominatum gerat, non tamen nisi politicum, quoniam advenit operi facto et non ipsa facit; venit pugnatura hic, alibi vero regnatura. Non semper organa ut ipsa vellet habet.

Viva dicimus semina vita animali, non mentali, et ab ea corpus organizari iuxta ideam quam administrant virtutes angelicae sub anima mundi. Deus vero quod perfectissimum est et ab eis dari non potest adiungit, ipsam videlicet mentem. Mens neque forma neque formatio est, sed ens divinum, non nisi creatori tribuendum opus.

**Appendix: quod anima non sit ab angelis neque ab anima mundi.**

Angelus aut anima mundi non producunt nostram animam: ex nihilo non possunt; ex seipsis facerent similes sibi, et sic anima esset angelus, non mens inclusa ad animandum corpus. Anima mundi non facit animam per decisionem, quia non est partibilis, neque per sui diffusionem ut calor calorem, quia non habet contrarium cuius gratia velit multiplicari neque corruptibilitatem quare velit instaurari.

Anima nostra non intelligeret supra animam mundi neque ad Deum intuitu et religione elevaretur, si ab anima mundi esset. Religio immediate tendit in Deum et tradita est nobis a Deo, non ab anima mundi neque ab angelis nisi ut instrumentis Dei. Ergo anima ipsa fit a Deo.

## English

Campanella rejects the idea that the divine mind suddenly destroys the prior bodily organization and makes a new one. If mind could do that, why would the labor of spirit and organization have preceded it? Why would it not also repair a broken leg instantly?

The parent generates a human by producing body and sensing spirit; God then completes, not destroys, that work by adding mind. Seeds are alive with animal life, not mental life. The seminal spirit organizes a body capable of receiving mind; corporeal and animal parts are in seed to be unfolded, but mind exceeds nature and must be awaited from God.

Human spirit forms organs suited to human mind, but the mind does not always have the organs it would choose. It rules politically, not absolutely: it arrives at a work already made and comes to fight here, to reign elsewhere. Formation belongs to spirit, world-soul, angelic powers, and ultimately God; the inhabitant soul does not build its own house.

Nor is the human soul produced by angels or by the world-soul. Angels cannot create from nothing; if they made souls from themselves, souls would be angels. The world-soul is not divisible and has no need to multiply itself like heat. If our soul came from world-soul, it would not understand above world-soul or rise immediately to God in religion. Religion's immediate tendency to God proves the soul is from God.

## Note

This file settles the generation question in a layered way: parent produces body and sensing spirit; world-soul and angels administer formation; God gives mind. Campanella's political-rule image is useful: mind is sovereign, but within a body whose organs it did not simply choose or build.
