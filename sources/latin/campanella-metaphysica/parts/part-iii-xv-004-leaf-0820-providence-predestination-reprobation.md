## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] in re, sed in ipso potestativo, activo et passivo. Actio autem ponit in re, quoniam effundit extra se quod intus erat, vel multiplicat. Omnes autem primalitates concurrunt ad actionem: voluntas ut inclinans ad illam, scientia ut dirigens, potestas ut imperans et simul subinde exequens. Actio ergo est propter finem proprium vel alienum edere ponereque in existentia simpliciter, ut cum calor calorem producit; vel in existentia secundum quid, ut cum reponit lapidem de pariete in Plateam homo: occasionem dat ut aliquis existentiae modus novus accedat.

Sicut cum praecipit mulieri vir ut se lavet, [ad quam?][?] actionem complures actus et actiones et passiones comperiuntur, et manuum, et aquae, et faciei, et aeris intermixti. Multa enim sunt media ad actionem finalem, et qui haec novit et ordinavit dicitur providere. Sed profecto providentia praeponit voluntatem finis cum scientia directiva ad finem; deinde sequitur mediorum apparatus.

Igitur providentia addit supra praescientiam finis et rerum quae possunt ad finem valere, dispositionem et ordinationem mediorum. Qui enim vult scribere, providet sibi chartam, et pennam aptat, et atramentum conficit, et atramentarium in quo servetur; et adhuc plures actiones et artes requiruntur, quas civitatis providentia portat gubernationis ergo.

Praedestinatio autem est rei alicuius ad finem, quam cognovit quis aptam illi fini, vel facit aptam, impulsus ac directio simul. Quam Theologi restringunt ad directionem hominum et Angelorum in vitam aeternam Supernaturalem, licet sit ad omnia praescripta. Nec formaliter est pars providentiae, ut quidam dicunt, sed extensive, quatenus providentia, quae parat media, complectitur prius praedestinationem; vel forsitan praedestinatio voluntatis consequentis praescientiam eventorum disponit aliter de fine quam prius, ut post culpam Adae praedestinatio aliter se habet ac prius; et hoc ex providentia secundaria etiam.

Reprobatio vero est a fine ad quem praecognoscitur [sponte ineptum?][?] quid, praecisio derelictioque; et opponitur electioni, non praedestinationi. Utraque enim meritum et demeritum praesupponit praevisum; praedestinatio autem prima non praesupponit merita, sed facit res ad finem. Unde ante peccatum Adae omnes praedestinati erant; post peccatum vero facta est electio et reprobatio, ut diximus in Theol., prout se quisque, auxiliante Deo, mundavit vel non mundavit per remedium divinitus datum, ut fiat vas utile cooperando gratiae Dei certam faceret suam vocationem.

Sed praedestinatio paternalis praecedens praevisionem meritorum et demeritorum semper est ad bonum obiectum voluntatis primarium: ut qui praedestinat merces ad nundinas propter lucrum, et filios ad haereditatem. Sed sequens praevisionem non est ad bonum primarium, ut qui merces proicit in mare propter vitam, et filios ad furcam ob salutem Reipublicae, quod antecedente voluntate nolebat, sed consequenti praevisionem incorrigibilitatis vult.

Prima voluntas et praedestinatio est potior simpliciter in intentione, quoniam ad bonum totale. Secunda vero in executione, et ad partiale. Quae primitus est secundum quid, ut notat S. Tho. Epist. Rom., Chrysost. Hom. 1 ad Ephes., Damas. 1 Fid. Orth. 29.

Ergo scientia est omnium rerum, sive bonarum sive malarum, praesentium, absentium, praeteritarum et futurarum, finis, et eorum quae ad finem bonorum et malorum conferentium et impedientium.

Praescientia vero est respectu futurorum tantum, sed eorumdem quorum scientia. Et quidem in Deo est idem quod scientia, quoniam nihil futurum est illi; sed respectu causarum, in quibus simul cognoscuntur, dicuntur praesciri, et respectu nostri. Providentia vero dicitur futurorum cognitio, finis scilicet et eorum quae ad finem, cum praeparatione mediorum, non autem cum infallibili eventu: hoc enim ad gubernationem spectat, ut divus Thom. in quaestionib. disp.

Praedestinatio, quando sortitura est effectum, quem nostra iuvant merita, videtur addere certam electionem voluntatis cum praeparatione mediorum et gubernationem, donec ad finem accedat praedestinata res, sive bonum sive malum. Sed profecto nunquam ad malum, quo malum, res ulla ab ulla causa praedestinatur. Non enim finalis causa ulla est mala, ad cuius malitiam agens respiciat dirigatque, licet admixtum habeat malum. Et hoc solum cum res non est propter se, neque propter Deum, sed tanquam medium ad bonum aliorum. Ergo Deus sapientissimus et optimus nunquam praedestinat ad malum, uti nos; malum enim est defectus boni et deviatio a fine praescripto a causa agente; quae deviatio de se non habet causam agentem, sed deficientem; ergo nec finalem, cum sit defectus a fine. Licet Deus faciat ut sit occasio ad aliud bonum, deinde per miraculum in Moralibus.

Gubernatio autem est per media certa ad finem certum deducere. Prout voluntas antecedens praevisionem et voluntas consequens, quarum nulla est ad malum ut malum, una cum sapientiae consilio deliberat. Ergo antecedens Praedestinatio non habet causam neque occasionem extra praedestinantem; electio vero et reprobatio a mediis vel a fine utique habet occasionem modo praescitam extra in his quae libere ducuntur ad finem.

Res viliores esse gratia meliorum ab his gubernari, et corpus gratia anima. Ergo mundum corporalem gratia mentalis; mentem autem Archetypi, a quo habent omnes colligationem et gubernationem, et ad quem sunt. Articulus III.

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] in the thing, but in its own potestative, active, and passive [principle]. Action, however, posits [something] in the thing, since it pours outside itself what was within, or multiplies [it]. But all the primalities concur toward action: will as inclining toward it, science as directing, power as commanding and at the same time then executing. Therefore action is, for the sake of a proper or alien end, to bring forth and place in existence simply, as when heat produces heat; or in existence qualifiedly, as when a human being replaces a stone from the wall into the Square: he gives occasion for some new mode of existence to accede.

As when a husband orders a woman to wash herself, [toward which?][?] action many acts and actions and passions are found together, of hands, water, face, and intermixed air. For there are many means toward the final action, and the one who knows and orders these is said to provide. But in fact providence places ahead the will of the end together with directive science toward the end; then the apparatus of means follows.

Therefore providence adds, beyond foreknowledge of the end and of the things that can have force toward the end, the disposition and ordering of means. For someone who wants to write provides paper for himself, prepares the pen, makes ink, and [prepares] the inkwell in which it is to be preserved; and still more actions and arts are required, which the providence of the city carries for the sake of governance.

Predestination, however, is at once the impulse and direction of some thing toward an end, which someone has known to be apt for that end, or makes apt. The Theologians restrict it to the direction of human beings and Angels into supernatural eternal life, although it is prescribed for all things. Nor is it formally a part of providence, as some say, but extensively, insofar as providence, which prepares means, first embraces predestination; or perhaps the predestination of consequent will, following foreknowledge of events, disposes otherwise concerning the end than before, as after Adam's fault predestination has itself otherwise than before; and this too comes from secondary providence.

Reprobation, however, is a cutting-off and abandonment from the end to which something is foreknown as [spontaneously unfit?][?]; and it is opposed to election, not to predestination. For both election and reprobation presuppose foreseen merit and demerit; but first predestination does not presuppose merits, but makes things for the end. Hence before Adam's sin all were predestined; after sin, however, election and reprobation were made, as we said in Theology, according as each person, with God aiding, cleansed or did not cleanse himself through the remedy given divinely, so that he might become a useful vessel and, by cooperating with the grace of God, make his calling certain.

But paternal predestination preceding forevision of merits and demerits is always toward the good primary object of the will: as one who predestines merchandise to the market for the sake of profit, and sons to an inheritance. But [predestination] following forevision is not toward the primary good, as one who throws merchandise into the sea for the sake of life, and sons to the gallows for the safety of the Republic, which he did not will by antecedent will, but wills by consequent [will] following forevision of incorrigibility.

The first will and predestination is simply stronger in intention, since it is toward the total good. The second, however, [is stronger] in execution, and toward the partial [good]. The first is primarily so in a qualified respect, as St. Thomas notes on Romans, Chrysostom in Homily 1 on Ephesians, and Damascene in *De fide orthodoxa* 29.

Therefore science is of all things, whether good or evil, present, absent, past, and future, of the end, and of those things contributing to and impeding the end of good and evil things.

Foreknowledge, however, is only with respect to future things, but of the same things as science. And indeed in God it is the same as science, since nothing is future for him; but with respect to causes, in which things are known together, and with respect to us, they are said to be foreknown. Providence, however, is said to be cognition of future things, namely of the end and of the things toward the end, with preparation of means, but not with infallible event; for this belongs to governance, as St. Thomas [says] in the disputed questions.

Predestination, when it is going to obtain an effect that our merits help, seems to add a certain election of will with preparation of means and governance, until the predestined thing, whether good or evil, reaches the end. But in fact no thing is ever predestined by any cause toward evil as evil. For no final cause is evil, toward whose malice the agent looks and directs, although it may have evil mixed in. And this is only when the thing is not for its own sake, nor for the sake of God, but as a means to the good of others. Therefore God, most wise and best, never predestines to evil as we do; for evil is a defect of good and a deviation from the end prescribed by the acting cause. This deviation of itself does not have an acting cause, but a deficient one; therefore neither [does it have] a final [cause], since it is a defect from the end. Yet God makes it be an occasion toward another good, and then by miracle in moral matters.

Governance, however, is to lead through certain means to a certain end. Just as antecedent will before forevision and consequent will, neither of which is toward evil as evil, deliberates together with the counsel of wisdom. Therefore antecedent Predestination has no cause or occasion outside the one predestining; election and reprobation, however, certainly have occasion from the means or from the end, in a foreknown mode, outside [the predestiner], in those things that are freely led toward an end.

That baser things exist for the sake of better things and are governed by them, and the body for the sake of the soul. Therefore the corporeal world [exists] for the sake of the mental; but the mind [exists] for the sake of the Archetype, from which all have binding-together and governance, and toward which they are. ## Note

This page gives Campanella's technical ladder: science, foreknowledge, providence, predestination, election/reprobation, governance. It also keeps the primality vocabulary active in action itself: will inclines, science directs, power commands and executes. The opening of Article III begins the hierarchy of worlds: baser things for better things, body for soul, corporeal world for mental world, mind for Archetype.
