## Opening Finding

Part III's human-soul book immediately reopens the hidden self-knowledge chain from leaves 85 and 402, but in anthropological form. The soul asks after itself because it has gone out from the mental world into the corporeal world: it knows itself by a secret and hidden cognition, yet supervening objects mute and bury that self-notion. The method of inquiry therefore starts indirectly, through operations, not because self-knowledge is absent, but because embodied life has displaced it.

## Corrected Latin

**Liber Decimus Quartus. In quo de Anima Humana.**

Cur seipsam quaeritat anima; et quidem ex operationibus videtur esse spiritus corporeus, tenuis, calidus, lucidus, habitans in corpore sicut instrumento et domo a se vivificata, et non sicut forma, ut putat Aristoteles; et utrum in homine sit alia pars divinior anima.

**Caput I. Articulus I.**

Anima humana, quae de Deo, et primalitatibus constitutivis, et de angelis, et de mundi substantia, ortu, et interitu hucusque disseruit, quaerit nunc de seipsa anxie, quid sit, unde sit, ad quid sit, immortalisne an corruptibilis; ut quem inter entia locum sortita sit intelligat, atque ad proprium cuncta dirigat finem.

Videtur profecto seipsam ignorare, quae seipsam quaerit; et videtur a seipsa abesse, quae non in se, sed in alio se quaeritat. Et quidem ita est. Namque, ut diximus in I Parte, seipsam novit cognitione quadam secreta abditaque, quoniam supervenientibus obiectis multis, a quibus patitur et de quibus iudicat, sui notionem in ea sopiri, occultari, et deiici oportet. Et quoniam alia intelligendo sit illa alia, idcirco quaerit se in aliis, quoniam a seipsa quasi alienata et absens est, e mundo mentali in mundum corporalem egrediendo.

Ergo sicut novit alia per passiones illatas, sic se cognoscere studet; nam innatum esse non nisi cum ad se revertetur recte pernoscet. Utinam saltem aliorum hominum animas intueri liceret, et non essent corpore occlusae sic opaco, quod per ipsum nihil translucet. Et profecto si translucerent, neque manifestae nobis essent, quoniam corpora spectare assueti, non corporea capere nequimus, nisi ad mundum mentalem et lumen divini solis convertamus intuitum.

Quapropter operationes ad intuitum conferamus exteriorem, unde fortasse operans substantia innotescat. Videntur quidem animata ab inanimatis differre sensu, motu, et operatione. Corpus enim abscedente anima non movetur, neque sentit, neque operatur opera vitae, ut nutritionem, augmentationem, generationem, conservationem. Haec ergo omnia anima faciebat vel cum corpore.

In rebus corporeis cognoscimus ignem per se moveri, et a calore omnia crassa attenuari, motuque donari, neque moveri quae non attenuata sunt. Ignem quoque nutriri, sic convertere in sui substantiam quaecunque occupat. Ignem quoque generare ignem sibi similem, se se amplificare, ac diffundere, et augere; item fugere a frigore, cum est invalidus, et persequi cum potens; appetere sui conservationem et contrarii destructionem; et tandem sentire contrarium, a quo fugit, vel quod persequitur ad necem, et simile caelum, ad quod se se continuo movet, ubi tenuitatem, ut praedam, nactus est, victoriosus evolat.

Unde recte existimavimus spiritum tenuem, calidum, mobilem, sensitivum, suae conservationis avidum esse animam cunctorum corporum animatorum, et plantarum et animalium, ut probatum est in Physica et in II Parte huius operis, ex decretis naturae et Scripturae sacrae. Omnia entia sensu, potestate, et appetitu, constitutione metaphysica, praedita esse probavimus.

Et ipsam animam non esse harmoniam, contra Aristoxenum; neque complexionem, contra Galenum; neque actum complexionis, contra Alexandrum; neque formam corporis, contra Aristotelem ostendimus. Neque enim animal ex tot partibus difformibus specie, operatione, virtute, figura, et proprietate diversis compositum, ab unica forma informari difforme ens potest. Idcirco enim difforme.

Verum quia decompositum ens est, et omnes illae formae componuntur in unum ens ad finem unum, sicut in artificiatis partes horologii variae ad unum horologium et operationem, propterea superaddimus formam idealem unam, quae dicitur totius; et animae et corporis et partium est formalis idealitas.

Ostendimus quoque animam non esse in corpore ut formam, quandoquidem una forma non idealis difforme ens non continet, sed tanquam habitator in domo et nauta in navi proprio instrumento. Nam abeunte anima, remanet corpus in sua forma palam et figura, sicut nauta abeunte navis. Verum marcescit corpus, quia deest gubernator causa agens intrinseca coniunctaque.

Nonne cadaver est navis absque homine, ventis et undis, et scopulis, et muribus, et perforationibus, et fractionibus obnoxia? Hoc plus habebat corpus animalis, quod ab anima propter unionem substantialem, non accidentalem modo, etiam nutriebatur eius calor exhalans per cibos ingestus, ne, dum alius abit, nullus remaneat fovens.

Erat enim sicuti molendinum potius, quod abeunte molitore deficit molitio granorum et molendini artificium. Item videmus obturatis nervis perdi sensum et motum, eo quod anima tenuis substantia est per nervos discurrens; quibus obstructis nequit ad exteriora interlabi.

Item, cum sensus non sit aequalis in toto corpore, manifeste non est forma aequalis, ut in aqua quae est tota aqua; sed alibi aliter sentit pro organorum commoditate. Item, cum attente aliquid intuemur, non audimus, vel aegre, eos qui nos vocant; et cum musica delectat spiritum in auribus, totus confluit ad caput, et remanet visus destitutus: non enim vident oculi. Et aliquando dormitat ceteris membris destitutis; et cum per unum oculum intueri volumus in foramen, alterum claudimus, quia totum spiritum in unum colligimus.

Ipse quoque somnus, et deliquia, et cicutae comestio, et vapores crassi subito spiritum ad interiora revocantes, declarant eum non esse formam corporis. Neque enim visus absque sua forma vidente remaneret, neque manus absque sensu tactus; sed esse spiritum tenuem, mobilem ad omnia munera, habitantem in cerebro, et per genus nervosum discurrentem, et vivificantem totum corpus.

Idcirco plantae sectis corticibus circum circa atque nervis suppositis moriuntur, non autem si vel dimidium truncetur stipitis. Haec autem alibi pluribus extensa sunt. Quapropter, cum eadem hominibus et ceteris animantibus eveniant, inquirimus utrum in homine habitet substantia aliqua divinior quam quae in brutis, et utrum sit illa immortalis.

## Working Translation

**Book Fourteen, on the Human Soul.**

Why the soul seeks itself; and how, from its operations, it seems to be a corporeal spirit, thin, hot, and bright, dwelling in the body as in an instrument and a house vivified by itself, and not as a form, as Aristotle thinks; and whether in the human being there is another, more divine part of soul.

The human soul, which up to now has discussed God, the constitutive primalities, angels, and the substance, origin, and destruction of the world, now asks anxiously about itself: what it is, where it comes from, what it is for, whether it is immortal or corruptible, so that it may understand what place it has obtained among beings and direct all things to its proper end.

It certainly seems to be ignorant of itself, since it seeks itself. It seems to be absent from itself, since it seeks itself not in itself but in something else. And in a way this is so. As we said in Part I, it knows itself by a certain secret and hidden cognition, because when many supervening objects arrive, from which it suffers and about which it judges, its notion of itself must be lulled, hidden, and cast down. And because in understanding other things it becomes those other things, it therefore seeks itself in others: by going out from the mental world into the corporeal world, it is as if alienated from itself and absent.

So just as it knows other things through introduced passions, it tries to know itself in the same way. But it will rightly know its inborn being only when it returns to itself. If only it were possible to gaze on the souls of other human beings, and they were not enclosed in a body so opaque that nothing shines through it. And even if they did shine through, they would not be manifest to us, because we are accustomed to looking at bodies and cannot grasp non-corporeal things unless we turn our gaze toward the mental world and the light of the divine sun.

Therefore let us bring its operations before outward inspection, so that perhaps the operating substance may become known. Animate things seem to differ from inanimate things by sense, motion, and operation. When the soul departs, the body does not move, sense, or perform the works of life, such as nutrition, growth, generation, and preservation. Therefore the soul, or the soul with the body, was doing all these things.

Among corporeal things, we know that fire moves by itself; that by heat it thins all dense things and gives them motion; that things not thinned are not moved. Fire is nourished too, converting into its own substance whatever it occupies. It generates fire like itself, amplifies itself, diffuses itself, and increases. It flees from cold when it is weak, pursues cold when it is powerful, desires its own preservation and the destruction of its contrary, and finally senses the contrary from which it flees or which it pursues to death, and the similar heaven toward which it continually moves. When it has seized thinness as prey, it flies upward victorious.

From this we rightly judged that a thin, hot, mobile, sensing spirit, eager for its own preservation, is the soul of all animated bodies, of plants and animals. This was proved in the Physics and in Part II of this work, from the decrees of nature and sacred Scripture: all beings are furnished with sense, power, and appetite by metaphysical constitution.

We also showed that the soul is not harmony, against Aristoxenus; nor a temperament, against Galen; nor the act of a temperament, against Alexander; nor the form of the body, against Aristotle. An animal composed from so many parts differing in species, operation, power, figure, and property cannot be informed as a difform being by one form; that is precisely why it is difform.

But because it is a decomposite being, and all those forms are composed into one being for one end, as the varied parts of a clock are composed into one clock and one operation, we add over them one ideal form, which is called the form of the whole. The formal ideality belongs to soul, body, and parts.

We also showed that the soul is not in the body as a form, since one non-ideal form does not contain a difform being, but rather as an inhabitant in a house and a sailor in a ship, its own instrument. When the soul goes away, the body plainly remains in its form and figure, just as the ship remains when the sailor goes away. But the body withers, because the governor is missing: the intrinsic and conjoined acting cause.

Is not the corpse a ship without a human being, exposed to winds, waves, rocks, mice, holes, and breaks? The animal body had this additional feature: because of a substantial, not merely accidental, union with the soul, it was nourished by the soul's heat as it exhaled through ingested foods, so that while one heat departed, no heat would be left to foster it.

It was more like a mill: when the miller goes away, the grinding of grain and the artifice of the mill fail. We also see that when nerves are blocked, sense and motion are lost, because the soul is a thin substance running through the nerves; when these are blocked, it cannot slip through to exterior things.

Again, since sense is not equal throughout the whole body, the soul is plainly not an equal form, as water is wholly water. It senses differently in different places, according to the suitability of the organs. When we look attentively at something, we do not hear, or scarcely hear, those who call us; and when music delights the spirit in the ears, the whole spirit flows to the head and sight is left deserted, for the eyes do not see. Sometimes it sleeps while the other members are abandoned; and when we want to look through a hole with one eye, we close the other, because we gather the whole spirit into one.

Sleep itself, fainting, eating hemlock, and thick vapors suddenly recalling the spirit inward declare that it is not the form of the body. Sight would not remain without its seeing form, nor the hand without the sense of touch. Rather, the soul is a thin spirit, mobile for all tasks, dwelling in the brain, running through the nervous kind, and vivifying the whole body.

For this reason plants die when their bark is cut all the way around and the nerves underneath are cut, but not if even half the trunk is cut off. These things have been treated more fully elsewhere. Therefore, since the same things happen to humans and to the other animals, we ask whether in the human being there dwells some substance more divine than what is in the beasts, and whether that substance is immortal.

## Notes

- This is the Part III version of the hidden-self-knowledge chain. Leaf 85 has `notitia innata et abdita`; leaf 402 has `intellectione abdita per suam essentiam`; leaf 791 now has `cognitione quadam secreta abditaque`. The recurrence is not merely lexical. Each passage uses hidden self-knowledge to explain why object-directed cognition can look like self-loss without abolishing the soul's inner self-presence.
- The line `alia intelligendo, sit illa alia` restates the becoming-other formula in the very passage that explains why the soul seeks itself. This keeps the leaf-402 distinction alive: exterior cognition makes the knower become other things, while self-knowledge is recovered by return to itself.
- The soul-body relation is deliberately non-Aristotelian: not harmony, not temperament, not act of temperament, not body-form. Campanella's positive language is mixed: thin/hot/mobile/sensitive spirit; inhabitant in a house; sailor in a ship; miller in a mill; intrinsic and conjoined acting cause.
- The passage makes `sensus rerum` central to the soul book. It claims that all beings have sense, power, and appetite by metaphysical constitution, and it points to Part II and the separate `De sensu rerum` for proof.
- `forma idealis` is doing unifying work where a single substantial form cannot: many difform bodily forms are composed into one being and one operation by an ideal form of the whole.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `spiritus corporeus, tenuis, calidus, lucidus` is rendered as "corporeal spirit, thin, hot, and bright." `Lucidus` may need "luminous" or "clear" depending on whether Campanella is stressing light, transparency, or cognitive clarity in later Liber XIV.

Conceptual: `forma idealis` and `formalis idealitas` are left as "ideal form" and "formal ideality." Do not prematurely identify this with the Part XIII archetypal ideas; the local function here is to explain unity of a difform living body.

Syntactic: `Hoc plus habebat corpus animalis...` is awkward in the OCR and translation. The working sense is that the animal body has something more than a corpse: substantial union with soul lets the body's heat be nourished through food.

Edition: The normalized text remains ABBYY-derived and has not been page-image collated. The opening heading and the main argument are stable enough for working substrate, but several small readings should be revisited if this becomes encounter-grade.
