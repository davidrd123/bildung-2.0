## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] in Archetypo infinita, quae ipsi apprehendunt, non tamen comprehendunt. Discurrunt ergo ad infinitum ex his quae intuentur. Intuitus enim ipsorum non potest ad infinita extendi, sicuti neque intuitus noster ad totum coelum, et ostreae ad totum mare.

Omnis autem intuitus in Deum non est beatificus supernaturaliter nisi sit per auxilium primi ordinis, elevans ad ingressum gaudiorum Domini. Beat tamen absque tanto auxilio naturaliter, sicuti maximum bonum. Experimur enim quod omne bonum, etiam parvum, apprehensum ut bonum, aliquod gaudium parit, quoniam a fonte vitae, unde egreditur fluvius voluptatis, teste David, stillicidium aliquod habet.

In hac autem visione et assistentia, omnia norunt quaecunque caeteri Angeli et infimi, multa et plus innumerabiliter. At simul ordinant; proptereaque purgant, illuminant, et perficiunt Angelos sequentis Hierarchiae, et ad operandum in exteriori Mundo rite movent. Et comparantur in Repub. statui Religiosorum, qui contemplantur agenda et docent. Qui vero sunt in secunda Hierarchia comparantur statui Ducum et Regum, qui praecipiunt de agendis et cum subditis simul agunt. Qui vero sunt in tertia, similes sunt militibus et opificibus et custodibus civitatum.

Non enim fit aliquid in Mundo exteriori, nisi ad exemplar interioris, sicut dixit Dominus ad Moysen. Unde neque nos, qui Mundos repraesentamus, facimus aliquid extrinsecus quod intus in corpore nostro non prius nat, sicuti saepe in Physicis declaratum est. Ratio autem ordinis inter omnia est, ut infimum supremi attingat supremum infimi, et e converso.

Non est autem nostrum dicere quod in mundo hoc Angelico operetur Mundus Archetypus, cum longe et ab extrinsecis ianuis id olfaciamus. Nec quid operentur ipsi vitalibus actibus erga Archetypum Angeli. Nec enim oculus vidit, nec auris audivit, neque in cor hominis ascendit, quae cum illis facit Deus supernaturaliter. Nec quae secundum rationem dantis et accipientis physice. Nec puto eos qui semel gustaverunt posse amittere tantum bonum, ut putat Plato in Phaedro: neque enim ignorantia ibi esse potest, neque quid melius optandum in his, qui beantur supra naturam ipsorum.

Qui autem dicuntur peccasse, non supra naturam adhuc beati erant; sed bonum quod habebant sibi adscribendo, et quasi a se volendo, vel propriis viribus, quamvis a Deo datis, Pelagiano more, quod acquirere supernaturaliter poterant se consecuturos affirmando, cadere meruerunt, dum equi, id est concupiscibilis et irascibilis, non probe currum Rationis ferrent, opinabilium pastu depravati.

Retinent autem et nunc superbiam illam. Unde et divinum sibi affectant cultum procurantque, ut Magi omnes norunt, cum tamen non ignorent sese nihil habere quod non acceperint. Et hoc Plato probat cum a Iove sequendo aversos fuisse aurigas curruum scribit. Theologi autem plurimi putant non cecidisse Angelos de prima Hierarchia. Cum enim assistant, nihil habeant extrorsum tam spectabile in quod inclinarent, averterenturque a Deo. Dei enim visio et amor omnia vera et omnia bona in se habet. Nulla etiam aversio ab incommutabili bono fieri potest, nisi praecedat ad bonum minus conversio, ut etiam Plato probat.

Dum per conversionem ad opinabilia ex intuitu verorum, vel per equorum turbationem, qui sunt ira et cupiditas, frangi alas sequentium in Paradiso Iovem et cadere propter hoc in Mundum corporalem docet. Qui autem de illis primis cecidisse volunt Angelos, quibus favet Iob, qui principium viarum Domini vocat Leviathan, nisi contendamus vias non quasque, sed ad exteriorem Mundum post Angelicum ibi intelligi, ii dicere habent, vel cum Lactantio, quod inviderunt Verbo divinitatem et primatum; quod nobis impossibile videtur, invidia enim non erigitur ad valde excedentem et infinitam virtutem. Vel quod hominis in Verbo unionem, quemadmodum Bernard coniecturat, vel sapientiam imponendi nomina rebus datam homini, clam ipsis, ut fabulatur Mahometus; proptereaque iussi sint hominem adorare; quod recusantes, cecidere. Quae narratio est erronea: nec enim Deus ita illudit suis ministris; neque quod praestare non poterant erat in eis culpabile; neque propterea homini subiiciendi erant.

Vel quod ad minus bonum, cum essent assistentes, converti poterant, quod esset occasio aversionis a Deo, nisi bonum proprium propriaque natura, quam plus quam decet et supra Deum amando, aversionem incurrerunt. Proprium autem bonum erat natura gloriosis gradibus primalitatum praedita, et superioritas super Angelos subsequentium Hierarchiarum; non tamen haec amatur supra Deum summum bonum amore innato, cum perfectissime noscatur. Ergo opinione decepti sic se amavere, ut caderent, ut Plato monet. Ergo non cadent qui perfecte et non opinabiliter cuncta norunt beatificati.

Quis autem enarrabit quantis distat numeris et gradibus naturaliter a divinitate? Et an quantum distant Entia nostra a nihilo? Videmus enim species rerum et genera secundum accessum et recessum a summo Ente distingui. Et sicuti sunt gradus in corporalibus, quorum primum, infimumque obtinet tellus; secundum lapis; tertium Planta; quartum animal; quintum homo; et inter animalia sunt gradus a lende per spongiam, per ostream, per Echninum, per lumbricum, per cimicem, per colubrum, per murem, per bufonem, per felem, per canem, per asinum, per equum, per Camelum, per Elephantum, per Aquilam, et caetera usque ad hominem. In cuius specie sunt gradus et quasi innumeri, sicut et in speciebus aliorum generum. Ita quoque in Angelis esse oportet.

Quapropter quaerendum, quisnam ipsorum propinquissimus Deo, et quantum distat ab eo, ut possit affectare aliquo iure deitatem? Profecto nullus est finis graduum inter supremum Angelum atque Deum. Neque enim res ulla apud nos nascens a sole genita soli videtur propinqua aliquo apparenti gradu, excepto igne. Qui tamen soli comparatus est nihil. Quis autem numerabit quantum inter finitum et infinitum intersit? Puto igitur quod propinquiores sunt res omnes nihilo quam Enti summo Deo: et enim motus est prope nihil, et atomum corpusculum. At Angelus nullus est prope Deum nisi respective; et quamvis nihilum ab Ente distat sine fine, tamen cum secunda Entia ex infinito nihilo et finita Entitate constent, propinquiora sunt nihilo, et ideo caduca omnia. Studio minus amabilia quam bonum infinitum Deus, ubi anima non desipit, extra eius intuitum posita.

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] infinite things in the Archetype, which they apprehend, though they do not comprehend them. Therefore they discourse toward infinity from the things they intuit. For their intuition cannot extend to infinite things, just as neither can our intuition [extend] to the whole heaven, nor an oyster's to the whole sea.

But not every intuition into God is supernaturally beatific unless it is through the aid of the first order, elevating to entrance into the joys of the Lord. Yet it naturally beatifies without such great aid, as the greatest good. For we experience that every good, even a small one, apprehended as good, produces some joy, since it has some drop from the fountain of life, from which the river of pleasure goes out, as David witnesses.

But in this vision and assistance, they know all things that the other Angels, even the lowest, [know], and many things beyond, innumerably. Yet at the same time they order; and therefore they purge, illuminate, and perfect the Angels of the following Hierarchy, and move them rightly toward operating in the exterior World. And in the Republic they are compared to the state of Religious, who contemplate the things to be done and teach. Those who are in the second Hierarchy, however, are compared to the state of Leaders and Kings, who command concerning the things to be done and at the same time act with subjects. Those who are in the third are similar to soldiers, workers, and guardians of cities.

For nothing happens in the exterior World except according to the exemplar of the interior, as the Lord said to Moses. Hence neither do we, who represent the Worlds, do anything externally that is not first born within in our body, as has often been declared in the Physical writings. But the reason of order among all things is that the lowest thing of the highest touches the highest thing of the lowest, and conversely.

But it is not ours to say what the Archetypal World does in this Angelic world, since we smell it from far away and from the exterior doors. Nor [is it ours to say] what the Angels themselves do by vital acts toward the Archetype. For eye has not seen, nor ear heard, nor has it ascended into the heart of man what things God does with them supernaturally. Nor [can we say] what things [occur] physically according to the ratio of giver and receiver. Nor do I think that those who have once tasted can lose so great a good, as Plato thinks in the *Phaedrus*: for ignorance cannot be there, nor [can there be] anything better to be chosen in those who are beatified above their own nature.

But those who are said to have sinned were not yet blessed above nature; rather, by ascribing to themselves the good that they had, and by willing it as if from themselves, or by affirming that they would obtain by their own powers, although given by God, in Pelagian fashion, what they could acquire supernaturally, they deserved to fall, while the horses, that is, the concupiscible and irascible [powers], did not carry the chariot of Reason properly, having been depraved by pasture of opinable things.

They also now retain that pride. Hence they also seek divine worship for themselves and arrange it, as all Magi know, although they are not ignorant that they have nothing that they have not received. And Plato proves this when he writes that the charioteers of the chariots were turned away from following Jove. But very many Theologians think that Angels did not fall from the first Hierarchy. For since they assist, they have nothing outward so worthy of sight toward which they would incline and be turned away from God. For the vision and love of God has all true things and all goods in itself. No turning-away from the incommutable good can happen unless a turning toward a lesser good precedes, as Plato also proves.

He teaches this when he says that through conversion toward opinable things from intuition of true things, or through the disturbance of the horses, which are anger and desire, the wings of those following Jove in Paradise are broken and for this reason they fall into the corporeal World. But those who want Angels to have fallen from those first [orders], whom Job favors when he calls Leviathan the beginning of the ways of the Lord, unless we contend that not all ways, but the ways to the exterior World after the Angelic [world], are understood there, have to say either, with Lactantius, that they envied the Word's divinity and primacy; which seems impossible to us, for envy is not raised toward a power so exceedingly surpassing and infinite. Or [they say] that [they envied] the union of the human being in the Word, as Bernard conjectures, or the wisdom of imposing names on things given to the human being, secretly from them, as Muhammad fabulizes; and that therefore they were ordered to adore the human being; refusing this, they fell. This narration is erroneous: for God does not mock his ministers in this way; nor was what they could not perform culpable in them; nor for that reason were they to be subjected to the human being.

Or [one may say] that, since they were assisting, they could be converted toward a lesser good, which would be an occasion of aversion from God, unless by loving their own good and their own nature more than is fitting and above God they incurred aversion. But their own good was a nature endowed with glorious grades of primalities, and superiority over the Angels of the following Hierarchies. Yet this is not loved above God, the highest good, by innate love, when it is known most perfectly. Therefore, deceived by opinion, they loved themselves in such a way that they fell, as Plato warns. Therefore those who know all things perfectly and not opinionably, being beatified, will not fall.

But who will recount by how many numbers and grades [an angel] naturally differs from divinity? And whether our Beings are as far from [divinity] as they are from nothing? For we see species and genera of things distinguished according to approach to and recession from the highest Being. And just as there are grades in corporeal things, of which earth holds the first and lowest; stone the second; Plant the third; animal the fourth; human being the fifth; and among animals there are grades from the lende[?] through the sponge, oyster, echinus, worm, bedbug, snake, mouse, toad, cat, dog, ass, horse, camel, elephant, eagle, and the rest up to the human being. In the human species there are also almost innumerable grades, just as in the species of other genera. So too there must be [grades] among Angels.

For this reason one must ask: which of them is nearest to God, and how far does he stand from him, so that he could claim deity by some right? In fact there is no end of grades between the highest Angel and God. Nor does any thing born among us, generated by the sun, seem near to the sun by any apparent grade, except fire. Yet fire, compared to the sun, is nothing. But who will number how much lies between finite and infinite? I think, therefore, that all things are nearer to nothing than to the highest Being, God; for motion is near to nothing, and so is a tiny atomic body. But no Angel is near to God except relatively; and although nothing is infinitely distant from Being, nevertheless, since secondary Beings consist from infinite nothing and finite Entity, they are nearer to nothing, and therefore all are liable to fall. They are to be studied as less lovable than the infinite good, God, when the soul, placed outside his intuition, is not foolish.

## Note

The angelology keeps returning to the same governance logic: assisting angels contemplate and order, ministering angels execute. The page also turns Plato's chariot myth into a doctrine of angelic fall: fall requires opinionable cognition, self-ascription of received good, and conversion toward a lesser good. The final scale claim is stark: even the highest angel is nearer to nothing than to infinite God.
