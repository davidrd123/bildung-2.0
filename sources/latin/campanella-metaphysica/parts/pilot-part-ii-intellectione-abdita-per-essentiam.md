## Opening Finding

This is the direct Part II locus behind Cassirer's `intellectione abdita per suam essentiam` citation. It confirms leaf 85's hidden self-knowledge doctrine, but in a sharper metaphysical register: self-intellection is not a passion, not an introduced/reflexive act, and not a becoming-other. A thing knows itself through its own essence because it already is itself; what is does not need to become.

The passage therefore limits any broad reading of Campanella's cognition as simple dissolution into the object. External understanding is passive and alienating: to understand exterior things is to suffer from them and become them, and such alien intellection can impede self-intellection. But essential self-intellection is original and hidden; in creatures it represents divine cognition only finitely, impedibly, and non-causally, while in God intellect, intellection, and the one understanding are always one and infinite.

## Corrected Latin

Ad postremam respondeo, quod unaquaeque res intelligit seipsam intellectione abdita per suam essentiam: quoniam intelligere exteriora est pati ab illis, & fieri illa: intelligens enim fit ipsum intelligibile: ut autem intelligat se intellectus, non indiget pati a se, nec fieri ipsemet; est enim: quod autem est, non fit: ergo seipsum novit per essentiam, & notio & intellectio est sua essentia: & verum est in Angelo, & in anima idem esse intellectum, intellectionem, & intelligentem sui ipsius primordiali innata, non autem illata & reflexa.

At aliarum rerum nequaquam: cum enim anima res extra se intelligit, patitur, & passio non est passum, quoniam dicit actum agentis: at cum sit ipsum agens, sentit seipsum factum sic, & non veluti prius erat, quoniam abiicit sui intellectionem, & perdit si mutatur omnino in aliud: at si mutatur parum, impeditur intellectio sui propter alienam.

Hinc est, quod omnia entia, vi cognoscitiva praedita, seipsa quasi non videntur nosse: quoniam semper intelligunt extranea obiecta; & quasi a seipsis alienantur: unde factum est, ut putemus, animam non intelligere seipsam nisi reflexe, quatenus intelligit, se se intelligere alia, seque tanquam aliam in extraneorum intellectione percipit. Et quidem talem intellectionem reflexam, & intellectionem aliorum entium, concedo esse accidens in cunctis creaturis, quoniam ab extra sit, & intellectus ea intelligit, quatenus intelligit se mutatum, & factum illa. At intellectione essentiali, & originali sui ipsius, nihil differre intellectum intelligentem, & intellectionem, firmiter assero cum Aug.

Et quia Deus non intelligit alia a se nisi intelligendo seipsum, non autem e contra, sicut creaturis evenit, & Deus nihil intelligit nisi seipsum per se, & non per accidens, & reflexe, sequitur, quod in eo semper & omnium intellectio, & intellectus, & intelligens sunt unum. Insuper & infinitum; quoniam intelligibile suum non est limitatae intellectionis, nec passionem addit ullam, nec terminum.

Repraesentant creaturae Deum intelligentem in hoc, quo seipsas intelligunt directe per essentiam; sed talis intellectio non pertingit ad Divinam, tum quia est sui tantum, tum quia finiti obiecti, & limitata, tum quia impedibilis ab externorum intellectione, tum quia indiget, & cogitur aliena intelligere, ut ab eis vel cum eis, vel ex eis se se conservet intelligens essentia: Tum quia intellectio sui non causat alia, nec se: intellectio vero aliorum non est causa illorum, sed causatum.

Deus autem intelligendo alia, causat illa, & intelligendo se, causat se, si ita loqui licet, more Graecorum Theologorum: nam potius dicendum est, communicat se sibi, & generat sui ideam verbum, & veritatem. O Magne Deus, nunc intelligo, & quasi tango, quod prius cogitare nullo modo poteram, sed voce tantum proferebam inani; sicut psittacus suum chere sub vetere magistro.

## Working Translation

To the final [argument] I answer that each thing understands itself by a hidden intellection through its own essence, because to understand exterior things is to suffer from them and become them. For the one understanding becomes the intelligible itself. But in order for the intellect to understand itself, it does not need to suffer from itself, nor to become itself; for it is. And what is does not come to be. Therefore it knows itself through its essence, and notion and intellection are its essence. And it is true in the angel and in the soul that the thing understood, the intellection, and the one understanding itself are the same in the primordial innate [intellection], but not in the introduced and reflexive [intellection].

But this is by no means so with other things. For when the soul understands things outside itself, it suffers, and the passion is not the thing suffered, since it names the act of the agent. But when it is the agent itself, it senses itself as having been made thus, and not as it was before, because it casts away its intellection of itself and loses it if it is changed wholly into another. But if it is changed only a little, its intellection of itself is impeded by the alien one.

Hence it is that all beings endowed with cognitive power seem, as it were, not to know themselves, because they are always understanding foreign objects and are, as it were, alienated from themselves. From this it has come about that we think the soul does not understand itself except reflexively, insofar as it understands that it understands other things and perceives itself as another in the intellection of foreign things. And indeed I concede that such reflexive intellection, and the intellection of other beings, is an accident in all creatures, since it comes from outside; and the intellect understands these things insofar as it understands itself as changed and made into them. But in the essential and original intellection of itself, I firmly assert with Augustine that the intellect, the one understanding, and the intellection do not differ at all.

And because God does not understand things other than himself except by understanding himself, and not conversely, as happens with creatures, and because God understands nothing except himself through himself, and not accidentally and reflexively, it follows that in him the intellection of all things, the intellect, and the one understanding are always one. Furthermore, [they are] infinite, because his intelligible is not of a limited intellection, nor does it add any passion or limit.

Creatures represent God as understanding in this respect: that they understand themselves directly through essence. But such intellection does not reach the divine [intellection], first because it is only of itself, then because it is of a finite object and limited, then because it can be impeded by the understanding of external things, then because it needs and is compelled to understand alien things, so that as an understanding essence it may preserve itself by them, with them, or from them. Then, because self-intellection does not cause other things, nor itself; but intellection of other things is not the cause of those things, but caused.

God, however, by understanding other things causes them; and by understanding himself he causes himself, if it is permitted to speak this way, in the manner of the Greek theologians. Rather, it should be said that he communicates himself to himself and generates his idea, Word, and truth. O great God, now I understand, and as it were touch, what before I could in no way think, but uttered only with an empty voice, like a parrot saying its `chere` under an old master.

## Notes

- Cassirer quotes only the opening proof: every thing understands itself by hidden intellection through its essence; external knowing is suffering and becoming; self-knowing does not need to suffer from or become itself because it already is.
- This directly confirms the leaf-85 structure but changes its weight. Leaf 85 says every being first knows itself and that added object-knowledge causes apparent self-forgetting. Leaf 402 says the same thing in Part II as an essence/operation thesis: essential self-intellection is not accidental, introduced, or reflexive.
- The decisive distinction is not sense versus intellect, but essential/original self-intellection versus introduced/reflexive/alien intellection. External intellection can be real cognition, but it is cognition through alteration by another.
- `intelligens enim fit ipsum intelligibile` gives the general becoming formula in a Part II metaphysical setting. The phrase supports encounter 001's `Cognoscere enim est fieri rem cognitam`, but leaf 402 adds that this does not describe essential self-knowledge.
- Creaturely self-intellection represents divine cognition, but only under limitation. It is finite, impedible by external intellection, dependent on alien things for self-preservation, and non-causal. Divine intellection alone causes what it knows.
- The closing prayer is methodologically useful: Campanella registers this point as something only now understood and almost touched, not as a routine scholastic distinction. The parrot comparison marks a transition from empty inherited formula to perceived doctrine.

## Translation Risk

Textual: page-image check confirmed the main Cassirer sentence and page boundary, but the whole page is not fully collated. The most delicate words are `primordiali innata`, `limitatae intellectionis`, `se se conservet intelligens essentia`, and `chere`.

Lexical: `notio` is left as "notion" in the working translation because it sits beside `intellectio` and seems to name the knowing-content or knowledge-state rather than a modern "concept." Revisit against nearby Part II usage.

Syntactic: `passio non est passum, quoniam dicit actum agentis` is scholastic and compressed. The translation keeps the awkward distinction between passion, the thing undergone, and the agent's act rather than smoothing it into "passivity is caused by the object."

Contextual: this slice opens the Cassirer-cited response, not all of Articulus IX. The preceding arguments and the larger Caput VI sequence may refine how `actio`, `operatio`, and `passio` should be rendered.
