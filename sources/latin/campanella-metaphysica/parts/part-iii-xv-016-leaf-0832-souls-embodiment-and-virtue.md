## Latin

Opiniones Origenis et Platonis et Procli, et Virgilii, de animarum ex Mundo mentali in corpora Mundi [situabilis?][?] immissione, esse improbabiles, Mercurii vero proximiorem veritati; et quid nos ex dogmate Christiano addiderimus.

**ARTICULUS V.**

De Angelis, Daemonibus, et Heroibus, et animabus, nihil certe dicere valemus per scientiam nostram praeter illa quae revelata sunt nobis, nisi prius propter quid animae in corpora descendunt examinatum fuerit. Exclusimus autem Origenis dogma ex Platone, quod animae ante Mundi corporei creationem in Mundo Mentali peccaverunt: proptereaque detrusae sunt in corpora, quasi in carceres; unde post diuturnos labores atque saecula, purgatis per vices plurimas maculis, ad mentalem mundum revertantur. Hoc enim neque Mundi corporalis fabrica admittit: esset enim aliquando annihilandus cum purgationes animarum defecerint. Quae autem fecit Deus perseverant in perpetuum: in meliorem autem statum redigentur. Sed nulli usui corpora erunt, si solummodo carceres sunt, consumpta malitia. Quam rectius a Deo permitti putat Trismegistus ad exercitium virtutis animorum.

Multo autem magis condemnatur error eorum, qui ex beatitudine summa in fruitione Deitatis cadere animos opinantur, tanquam fastidio aut oblivione boni tam praesentis capiantur. Praeterea si in poenam tantummodo animae corporantur ad vindictam, cum hoc ignorent ipsae nec recordentur ob id sese pati, non erit vera vindicta, neque admonitio ad meliorem procurandam migrationem.

Proclus in Alcibiade putat animas pulchritudinis sequaces in corpora descendere, tanquam illorum specie delectatas; aliquando dicit tanquam provisuras. Dii enim superiores amant inferiora tanquam illorum benefactores; inferiores vero amant superiores tanquam ex conversationis officio ad id unde illis bene est. Igitur animae, cum sint inter Divos et Daemones versantes, descendunt in corpora inferiora tanquam provisurae, curamque mortalium suscepturae. Hoc autem Dii faciunt non se miscendi. Ergo nec descendendo, sicuti animae generabilibus; unde aliquando ex munere Divum habent ut divine gubernent, quasi a corporibus per ecstasim segregatae; alioquin corporearum imaginum dominio delectatae, aspectu formarum circa sensibilia [moechantur?][?].

Sed profecto, cum Plato, et ipse Proclus, descensum animae in corpus doceat seiungere illam a divinis, a quibus Intelligentia, Potestate, et puritate replebatur; coniungereque materialibus, generationi et corruptioni, a quibus ignorantia, errore, et sui oblivione imbuitur; et sicut erat per se nobilis in primo statu, ita in corpore fieri quasi per aliud, nec tamen proficit scientia in absolutione corporis, sed expeditur ad reminiscendum quae scierat: miror itaque quod pulchritudinem inferiorum anteponat superiorum formositati. Profecto si anima provisura mortalibus descenderet, in sui oblivionem non caderet, et rerum ignorantiam. Non enim per casum in errorem, sed per elevationem ad scientiam providentiae aliorum accingimur. Et cum infantes multi moriantur ante hoc munus, nequaquam propter hoc venisse videntur. Praeterea providentia exercetur cum profectu et gaudio providentis. Mirum etiam quod Virgilius et Platonici asserunt animas reverti in corpora cum biberint in fluvio oblivionis malorum in corporibus aliis perpessorum. Non enim deterioritatis cupido quemquam capit, neque cum felix est anima obliviscitur utilis scientiae ad declinanda mala.

Quapropter melius Mercurius videtur docere, quod videlicet immittit Deus ipse animas non antecreatas, sed cum corporantur; et hoc ad specimen Mundi corporei et sui ipsius gloriam ex huius Mundi mirabilis cognitione, quae animam ad Deum ex his simulachris elevat. Nos autem addimus ob pugnam et gloriae adeptionem, ut probatis malis ad bona ardentius afficiatur. Quae autem Plato in Phaedro dixit de fractione alarum ex equorum errore superius explosa sunt: quoniam non habent veritatem in beatis.

Mentes humanas, ut meliorent corpora, seque ipsas Deo per Virtutem proximas faciant, corporari: hoc tamen praestare se non posse absque divino auxilio; ideoque custodiam Angelorum eis adhibitam, ut partem eiusdem auxilii fatorum liberantis nexibus.

## English

That the opinions of Origen, Plato, Proclus, and Virgil, concerning the sending of souls from the mental World into the bodies of the [situable?][?] World, are improbable, but Mercury's nearer to the truth; and what we have added from Christian doctrine.

**ARTICLE V.**

Concerning Angels, Demons, Heroes, and souls, we certainly cannot say anything by our own science except those things that have been revealed to us, unless it has first been examined why souls descend into bodies. But we excluded the doctrine of Origen from Plato, that souls sinned in the Mental World before the creation of the corporeal World, and for that reason were thrust down into bodies as if into prisons; whence, after long labors and ages, their stains having been purged through very many turns, they would return to the mental world. For the fabric of the corporeal World does not admit this: for it would someday have to be annihilated when the purgations of souls had ceased. But the things that God made persevere forever; they will be brought back into a better state. Bodies, however, will be of no use if they are only prisons, once wickedness has been consumed. Trismegistus thinks much more rightly that this is permitted by God for the exercise of the virtue of souls.

But much more is the error of those condemned who think that souls fall from highest beatitude in the fruition of Deity, as if they were seized by disgust or forgetfulness of so present a good. Besides, if souls are embodied only for punishment, for vengeance, since they themselves are ignorant of this and do not remember that they suffer on account of it, it will not be true vengeance, nor an admonition for procuring a better migration.

Proclus in Alcibiades thinks that souls following beauty descend into bodies, as if delighted by their appearance; sometimes he says as if they were going to provide for them. For higher Gods love inferior things as their benefactors; inferior things love superior things as from the office of conversation toward that whence it is well with them. Therefore souls, since they move among Divinities and Demons, descend into lower bodies as if going to provide for them and going to undertake care of mortal things. But the Gods do this without mixing themselves. Therefore they do not do it by descending, as souls [do] into generable things; whence sometimes by the gift of the Divinities they have it that they govern divinely, as if separated from bodies by ecstasy. Otherwise, delighted by the dominion of corporeal images, they [commit adultery?][?] around sensible things by the sight of forms.

But certainly, since Plato, and Proclus himself, teach that the descent of the soul into body separates it from divine things, by which it had been filled with Intelligence, Power, and purity, and joins it to material things, generation and corruption, by which it is imbued with ignorance, error, and forgetfulness of itself; and since, just as it was noble through itself in the first state, so in the body it becomes as if through another, and yet it does not advance in science at the release of the body, but is freed to remember what it had known: I therefore wonder that he puts the beauty of inferior things before the formosity of superior things. Certainly if the soul descended to provide for mortal things, it would not fall into forgetfulness of itself and ignorance of things. For we are girded not by chance into error, but by elevation into the science of providence for others. And since many infants die before this office, they in no way seem to have come for this reason. Besides, providence is exercised with the progress and joy of the one providing. It is also strange that Virgil and the Platonists assert that souls return into bodies when they have drunk in the river of forgetfulness of the evils suffered in other bodies. For desire of deterioration seizes no one, nor, when the soul is happy, does it forget useful science for avoiding evils.

Therefore Mercury seems to teach better: namely, that God himself sends in souls not created before, but when they are embodied; and this for the display of the corporeal World and for his own glory from the knowledge of this marvelous World, which lifts the soul to God from these images. But we add: on account of battle and the attainment of glory, so that, after evils have been tested, it may be affected more ardently toward goods. But the things Plato said in the Phaedrus concerning the breaking of wings from the error of the horses have been exploded above, because they do not have truth in the blessed.

That human minds are embodied so that they may improve bodies and make themselves nearer to God through Virtue; nevertheless that they cannot provide this for themselves without divine aid; and therefore that the custody of Angels is applied to them as part of the same aid freeing them from the bonds of fates.

## Note

This part rejects the preexistent-soul and punitive-descent accounts while keeping Mercury's embodiment account as nearer to Christian doctrine. The new Campanellan addition is agonistic: souls are embodied for battle and glory, so that tested evils sharpen affection toward goods. The leaf opens Caput IV on embodiment and angelic custody, then breaks mid-word at `de-`.
