[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] suae et bonae[?]. Ergo dedit etiam illis virtutem continendi corpora in suo officio et iustitia. Iustum enim quod pulchrum; pulchrum est boni apparentia. Bonum autem deteriora melioribus subesse; ergo corpus spiritui, spiritum menti, mentemque Deo. Sic enim naturae melius servantur. Bonum enim est conservativum, et iustum est quod aequatur illi qui est conservatio, ut in politicis docuimus. Igitur hanc iustitiam perdidit animus cum Deo subditus esse noluit, a quo servatur. Ergo non fabula est peccatum Angelorum et Adae in posteros traductum, sed ex arcanis naturae relucens veritas, ut dictum est prius.

Ergo dedit Deus menti humanae ut imperare corpori et animalibus et cunctis rebus se deterioribus possit, imperio tamen quod Dei imperium non impediat. Si autem imperaret homo sideribus ut in terram descenderent, et elementis ut de suo loco migrarent, divinae ordinationi omnia servanti officeret, sicuti si suo pedi iuberet facere officium oculorum, et manui pedis, et iecur in locum cordis migrare. Imperium ergo rationale et castum habet homo super res cunctas; et omnis superior super inferiorem. Amittit vero quando in suo ordine et dotibus a Deo datis non permanet. Ergo cecidit homo ab hoc ordine; alioquin non tanta mala ipsum apprehendissent, nec corporis passionibus obediret ita turpiter, ut homo asino et eques equo, contra iustitiam et pulchritudinem eius Epiphaniam.

Nec tribuendum hoc commune malum elementorum pugnae, ut alii docent, aut ordini rerum, ut Avicenna putat. Nam iuxta nostrum dogma non constat homo ex contrariis licet ex oppositis. Item Mens habitans corpus est immortalis et vivacissima, ita ut possit moderari corpus ne ipsam laedat. Natura enim negat Deum subiecisse melius deteriori; et Avicenna ipse docet elementa subdita esse Animabus rationalibus. Et temperantia producit vitam, ut Plato in Charmide docet, et experientia Patrum et Heremitarum; intemperantes vero cito moriuntur. Ergo animi culpa facit corpus morbosum, et cognoscit hoc, et non abstinet.

Igitur cum homo etiam agnoscat iustum et pulchrum et bonum, et tamen non quod vult bonum illud agat, nec quod materia vult sed quod inhabitans in eo peccatum, consequens est ut agnoscat se indigere superiori auxilio. Ergo quemadmodum trahitur ad inferiora in dissimilitudinem, alienationem ad corporeum mundum per sensualitatis corporei spiritus illecebras et furias, et aggravans corpus, et per hominum pravorum et daemonum suggestiones, et oblatas occasiones; sic etiam ad superiora, unde originem habet, per reversionem sui ad se suamque originem curari oportet ab oppositis melioribusque causis, videlicet per illecebras divinarum pulchritudinum oblatarum a Mundi gubernatore, per gratiam Autoris rerum, per suggestiones Angelorum et doctrinas bonorum hominum.

Et quidem maiora oportet esse auxilia quam depravantes causae. Ergo et quae potestativum roborent, et cognoscitivum illuminent, et volitivum purgent sanctificentque, ut possimus fugere omnia mala et sectari omnia bona. Hoc enim spectat ad gubernationis rationem.

**Hominem optimum vivendi genus, quo divinum regrediatur in mundum, non posse tenere nisi lege servetur positiva, naturali; quae cum insufficienter in officio teneret hominem, virtutibus opus fuisse. Quibus nec sufficientibus, additam religionem, quae erat non quae non erat[?]. Nec religionem optimam esse, nisi Deus ipse doctrina et exemplo nobis condescendendo affulgeret, et doctores legum et virtutum et Religionis per sapientiam Dei ipsa revelata consummari[?] probari. Articulus II.**

Cognoscit homo sese indigere legibus, id est regulis iustitiae, per quas doceretur quae sectanda sunt et quae declinanda, citra rationem[?] bonorum et malorum quae in Mundo situal[?] congeruntur; ac cogeretur contra sensualitates suas vitam degere. Neque invenit[?] nisi post fabricae corporalis absolutionem, cum a malis laedi, a multis servari coexistentibus, et quae promittunt salutem mala esse expertus est, et quae dolores afferunt quandoque bona.

Igitur per philosophiam res cunctas expertus, scientias et artes adinvenit, quibus res prius nollet et postea ad sui utilitatem tractaret; et in ipsis tractandis et agendis regulas posuit, quales rerum universitas ostendit rationabilissima, per nostram connaturalem rationem agnitas. Inventum est enim quod Deus lege aeterna, quae verbum Dei est essentialiter, cuncta regit deducitque suos ad fines, fini magno congruentes, et cum videntur contra finem suum supremum accidere, ab eo non declinare.

A lege aeterna derivatam agnoscimus legem naturalem, qua res omnes proprios in fines per certa media deducuntur, qui est conservatio totius per conservationem partium. Ex lege naturali emanavit per modum conclusionis lex gentium, omnibus necessaria ad omnes, ut de contractibus, de passionibus[?], de legatis; et per modum specificationis positiva lex Nationibus singulis, praesentibus occurrens necessitatibus et usibus, idcircoque illis cessantibus non duratura, sicut ius naturae et ius gentium durant semper.

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] his own and good[?]. Therefore he also gave them the power of containing bodies in their office and justice. For the just is what is beautiful; the beautiful is the appearance of the good. But good is that lower things be subject to higher: therefore body to spirit, spirit to mind, and mind to God. For in this way natures are better preserved. For the good is preservative, and the just is what is equated with that which is preservation, as we taught in politics. Therefore the soul lost this justice when it refused to be subject to God, by whom it is preserved. Therefore the sin of Angels and Adam handed down to descendants is not a fable, but a truth shining from the secrets of nature, as was said before.

Therefore God gave to the human mind that it can command the body, animals, and all things worse than itself, yet with an empire that does not impede God's empire. But if the human being commanded the stars to descend to earth, and the elements to migrate from their place, he would obstruct the divine ordering that preserves all things, just as if he ordered his foot to perform the office of the eyes, the hand [to perform the office] of the foot, and the liver to migrate into the place of the heart. Therefore the human being has rational and chaste empire over all things, and every superior over the inferior. But he loses it when he does not remain in his own order and in the gifts given by God. Therefore the human being fell from this order; otherwise such great evils would not have seized him, nor would he obey the passions of the body so shamefully, like a human being [obeying] an ass and a rider [obeying] a horse, against justice and the appearance of its beauty.

Nor should this common evil be attributed to the battle of the elements, as others teach, or to the order of things, as Avicenna thinks. For according to our doctrine the human being does not consist of contraries, although [he consists] of opposites. Likewise the Mind inhabiting the body is immortal and most lively, so that it can moderate the body lest the body harm it. For Nature denies that God subjected the better to the worse; and Avicenna himself teaches that the elements are subject to rational Souls. And temperance produces life, as Plato teaches in the Charmides, and the experience of the Fathers and Hermits [teaches]; but the intemperate die quickly. Therefore fault of the soul makes the body diseased, and it recognizes this, and does not abstain.

Therefore since the human being also recognizes the just and beautiful and good, and nevertheless does not do that good which he wills, nor what matter wills but what the sin inhabiting in him [wills], it follows that he recognizes that he needs higher aid. Therefore just as he is drawn downward into dissimilarity, alienation toward the corporeal world through the allurements and rages of the sensuality of the corporeal spirit, and the aggravating body, and through the suggestions of depraved human beings and demons, and offered occasions; so also he must be cured toward higher things, whence he has his origin, through the return of himself to himself and to his origin, by opposite and better causes, namely through the allurements of divine beauties offered by the governor of the World, through the grace of the Author of things, through the suggestions of Angels and teachings of good human beings.

And indeed the helps must be greater than the depraving causes. Therefore [there must be helps] that strengthen the power-part, illuminate the cognitive part, and purge and sanctify the volitive part, so that we can flee all evils and pursue all goods. For this belongs to the reason of governance.

**That the human being cannot hold the best kind of living, by which the divine may return into the world, unless he is preserved by positive and natural law; and since this held the human being insufficiently in office, virtues were needed. And since these too were insufficient, religion was added, which was what was, not what was not[?]. Nor is religion best unless God himself, by teaching and example, shone upon us by descending, and the teachers of laws and virtues and Religion are proved to be consummated by the revealed wisdom of God itself[?]. Article II.**

The human being recognizes that he needs laws, that is, rules of justice, through which he would be taught what things are to be pursued and what avoided, apart from the reason[?] of goods and evils which are heaped up in the World in situation[?]; and he would be compelled to live his life against his sensualities. Nor does he find[?] [this] except after the completion of the bodily fabric, when he has experienced that he is harmed by evils, preserved by many coexisting things, and that things promising health are evil, and things bringing pains are sometimes good.

Therefore, having experienced all things through philosophy, he discovered sciences and arts, by which he would handle for his utility things that before he did not want and afterward [wanted]; and in handling and doing them he set down rules, such as the universality of things showed to be most reasonable, recognized through our connatural reason. For it was found that God, by eternal law, which is essentially the Word of God, rules all things and leads his own to their ends, ends congruent with the great end, and when they seem to happen contrary to their own supreme end, they do not decline from it.

We recognize natural law as derived from eternal law, by which all things are led to their proper ends through certain means, which is the preservation of the whole through preservation of the parts. From natural law, by way of conclusion, there emanated the law of nations, necessary for all toward all, as concerning contracts, concerning passions[?], concerning legates; and, by way of specification, positive law for individual Nations, meeting present necessities and uses, and therefore not going to endure when these cease, just as natural right and the law of nations always endure.

Both God and the human being sometimes establish this positive law, which teaches one to reduce all accidental things to the norm of nature; about these we spoke more amply in Theology. But indeed we learned by experience that these laws are not sufficient for living well in the corporeal World, with a life ordered to the life of the mental world. ## Note

This corrected file keeps the fall argument tied to metaphysical order: body to spirit, spirit to mind, mind to God. Article II then stages a sequence from law to virtue to religion, because natural, gentile, and positive law cannot by themselves order bodily life toward the mental world.
