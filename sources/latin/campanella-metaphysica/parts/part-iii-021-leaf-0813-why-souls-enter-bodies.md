## Latin

[Fragmentum continuans folium praecedens:] calorem ex sui diffusione. Tum quia vel animae erant beatae vel miserae ante corporis clausuram. Si beatae, numquam cupivissent corporis carcerem. Si miserae, quaerenda est miseriarum causa, et non invenitur nisi semper novas fingamus opiniones. Si vero, inquit Origenes, quia peccarunt, utique stultum est putare eas potuisse peccare si Deo fruebantur, cuius boni summi amor numquam excidit, ut etiam Origenes alibi concedit. Si vero, ut dicitur in Phaedro, ex veri intuitu ad opinabilia transeunt ut cadant, impossibile videtur. In Deo enim omnia vera. Et qui eo fruitur, exterioribus non allicitur. Nec, si erant in quodam statu naturali et sicut Angeli sic etiam animae peccarunt, ut Ficinus interpretatur, tunc enim aut Deus immittit eas in corpora tanquam in poenas aeternas aut purgatorias. Non in aeternas, quia corpus moritur et ipsae animae, licet deceptae, cupiunt in corpore vivere; neque in purgatorias, quoniam corpus est occasio peccandi magis, ut omnes fatemur. Quomodo autem Deus

purgat et lavat luto maculas mentium? Si peccandi finem imponere voluit illis, irrationabiliter ad locum peccati detrudit. Si vero in poenam, oportuisset non tollere illis memoriam delitiarum praeteritarum et status inclyti quem perdiderunt, tum quia poena non est in eo qui nescit quod bonum amiserit, tum quia non cavebunt peccata, cum nesciant ob peccatum se exilio et carcere puniri. Contra hanc sententiam in nostra Theologia diximus, et sunt rationes multas a Cyrillo positae contra Origenem ex auctoritatibus sanctarum Scripturarum. Quapropter rationabilis sententia est quod animae a Deo creentur et simul corporibus immittantur. Non quidem ex Dei substantia, ut putat Stoicus et Manichaeus, quoniam Deus partibilis non est; neque inscius et mutabilis, ut anima humana; neque ex alia materia, cum sit incorporea, nec ex materia mentali ut aliquando suspicatur Augustinus, haec enim impartibilis est. Ergo ex nihilo, ut dictum est prius et mox disputabimus.

**Quaestiones iterum, cur anima immortalis uniatur corpori, et utrum anima producat animam cum semine, sicut spiritus producit spiritum et corpus, ut putat Apollinaris et Nyssenus. Caput VI. Articulus I.**

Sed quaeritur: si naturaliter anima unitur corpori, ergo non est Deus eius causa, sed Natura. Si violenter, ergo homo est violenter homo. Si voluntarie, ergo anima amat corpus ut bonum, in quo tamen multa incurrit mala. Et remanet quaestio cur nobilissimum Ens ignobilissimo uniatur.

Item, si anima indiget spiritu et sensoriis ad cognitionem rerum, cur separatur a corpore? Et quomodo post separationem cognoscit? Si vero non indiget spiritu et sensoriis ad cognitionem rerum, cur unitur ei cum detrimento? Cur, sicut spiritus facit spiritum, non et anima animam per traductionem, ut sentit Apollinaris? Alioquin homo non generaret hominem, sed materiam, quae est ingenerabilis, praesertim secundum eos qui unam modo formam in homine ponunt, quae sit corporeitas, elementaritas, sensitiva, vegetativa, et intellectiva simul. Nam putant, corruptis prioribus formis, semper quae supervenit continere in se illarum formalitates.

Item, compositum generatur. Ergo anima et corpus ab homine generante; alioquin non esset genitor, et imperfectior esset ceteris brutis generantibus materiam, formam, et animam. Item, ut ait Apollinaris, Deus in caestibus et adulteriis cooperaretur immittens animam in tali coitu. Item Gregorius Nyssenus pluribus ostendit animam ab anima oriri ut corpus a corpore. Nam ait imperfecta esset operatio Dei tantum animam facientis et hominis solum corpus.

Item, anima est pars hominis. Ergo continetur in semine. Nam in grano continetur spiritus, spica, aristae[?], internodia, folia, et cetera, quae visuntur eo germinante. Nec aliunde suscipit vim ea producendi. Item simul crescunt et manifestantur vires animae et corporis per operationes paulatim perfectas, ut etiam Albertus Magnus sentit. Ergo habent eandem originem.

Item, organa corporis fiunt conformia animae affectibus; unde alia anima alia habet organa. Diximus enim timidum leporem organa habere ad fugiendum, quoniam anima eius iuxta affectum suum sibi talia paravit; leonis vero animam parasse organa ad pugnandum propter audaciam; avis ad volandum propter levitatem et tenuitatem spiritus; ciconiam collum et rostrum longum ad capiendas serpentes quibus vescitur. Anima ergo fabricatur corpora prout opus sibi sunt. Ergo ipsa est in semine humano, ut asserit Albertus, quae figuram rectam et manus fabricatura. Ergo non creatur, sed traducitur cum semine; alioquin non esset causa efficiens corporis sui, ut ceterae animae, neque illo posset uti. Et quidem Aristoteles saepe animam corporis causam efficientem ponit; sic Telesius, Galenus, Albertus, et omnes Physiologi.

Item, si corpus est propter animam, oportet illam prius esse et ad eius voluntatem hoc formari, ut probat Plato. Hoc autem falsum est, si anima infunditur iam formato corpore. Item semina viva sunt, ergo habent animam. Si autem unica est hominis anima, ergo cum semine traducitur; nec Deus singulis momentis creat novas animas ad libitum cuiusque coeuntis.

## English

[Fragment continuing the preceding leaf:] heat from its diffusion of itself. Then because souls were either blessed or miserable before the enclosure of the body. If they were blessed, they would never have desired the prison of the body. If miserable, the cause of their miseries must be sought, and it is not found unless we keep inventing new opinions. But if, as Origen says, [they were miserable] because they sinned, it is surely foolish to think they could have sinned if they enjoyed God, from whose supreme good love never falls away, as even Origen elsewhere concedes. But if, as is said in the Phaedrus, they pass from intuition of truth to matters of opinion so that they fall, this seems impossible. For in God all things are true. And one who enjoys him is not enticed by exterior things. Nor [is it solved] if they were in some natural state and, as Angels [sinned], so also souls sinned, as Ficino interprets; for then either God sends them into bodies as eternal punishments or as purgatorial ones. Not as eternal, because the body dies and the souls themselves, though deceived, desire to live in the body; nor as purgatorial, because the body is more an occasion of sinning, as we all confess. But how does God

purge and wash stains of minds with mud? If he wished to impose an end of sinning on them, he irrationally thrust them down into the place of sin. But if [he sent them] as punishment, he ought not to have taken from them the memory of their past delights and the illustrious state they lost, both because there is no punishment in one who does not know what good he has lost, and because they will not beware sins, since they do not know that they are punished with exile and prison because of sin. Against this opinion we spoke in our Theology, and there are many arguments set down by Cyril against Origen from the authorities of the Holy Scriptures. Therefore the reasonable opinion is that souls are created by God and sent into bodies at the same time. Not, indeed, from God's substance, as the Stoic and Manichaean think, because God is not divisible; nor unknowing and mutable, as the human soul is; nor from another matter, since it is incorporeal, nor from mental matter, as Augustine at one time suspects, for this is indivisible. Therefore [they are created] from nothing, as was said before and as we will soon dispute.

**Questions again: why the immortal soul is united to the body, and whether the soul produces soul with seed, just as spirit produces spirit and body, as Apollinaris and Nyssenus think. Chapter VI. Article I.**

But it is asked: if soul is naturally united to body, then God is not its cause, but Nature. If violently, then the human being is human violently. If voluntarily, then soul loves body as a good, though in it it incurs many evils. And the question remains why the noblest Being is united to the ignoblest.

Likewise, if soul needs spirit and sensories for cognition of things, why is it separated from body? And how does it know after separation? But if it does not need spirit and sensories for cognition of things, why is it united to it with harm? Why, just as spirit makes spirit, does soul not also make soul by traduction, as Apollinaris thinks? Otherwise the human being would not generate a human being, but matter, which is ingenerable, especially according to those who posit only one form in the human being, which is corporeity, elementarity, sensitivity, vegetativity, and intellectivity all together. For they think that, when the prior forms have been corrupted, the one that supervenes always contains their formalities in itself.

Likewise, the composite is generated. Therefore soul and body [come] from the human being generating; otherwise he would not be a parent, and he would be more imperfect than the other beasts, which generate matter, form, and soul. Likewise, as Apollinaris says, God would cooperate in incest and adulteries by sending a soul into such intercourse. Likewise Gregory of Nyssa shows by many arguments that soul arises from soul as body from body. For he says that the operation would be imperfect if God made only the soul and the human being only the body.

Likewise, soul is part of the human being. Therefore it is contained in the seed. For in a grain are contained spirit, ear, awns[?], joints, leaves, and the rest, which are seen when it germinates. Nor does it receive from elsewhere the power of producing them. Likewise the powers of soul and body grow and are manifested together through gradually perfected operations, as Albertus Magnus also thinks. Therefore they have the same origin.

Likewise, the organs of the body are made conformable to the affections of the soul; hence one soul has one set of organs, another soul another. For we have said that the timid hare has organs for fleeing, because its soul prepared such things for itself according to its affection; but the soul of the lion prepared organs for fighting because of boldness; the soul of the bird [prepared organs] for flying because of lightness and subtlety of spirit; the stork [prepared] a long neck and beak for catching the serpents on which it feeds. Therefore soul fabricates bodies as they are useful for itself. Therefore it is itself in human seed, as Albert asserts, about to fabricate the upright figure and hands. Therefore it is not created, but traduced with seed; otherwise it would not be the efficient cause of its body, as the other souls are, nor could it use that body. And indeed Aristotle often makes soul the efficient cause of the body; so too Telesio, Galen, Albert, and all the Physiologists.

Likewise, if the body exists for the soul, the soul must exist first and this body be formed according to its will, as Plato proves. But this is false if the soul is infused into an already formed body. Likewise seeds are alive; therefore they have soul. But if the human being has a single soul, therefore it is traduced with the seed; nor does God at every moment create new souls according to the pleasure of each person having intercourse.

## Note

This corrected file closes the preexistence dispute and opens Caput VI as a numbered problem set. Campanella rejects the prison-of-body account as a natural explanation, then lets the biological objections press hard: generation, seed, organs, and parental causality all seem to demand soul-traducianism before Article II answers.
