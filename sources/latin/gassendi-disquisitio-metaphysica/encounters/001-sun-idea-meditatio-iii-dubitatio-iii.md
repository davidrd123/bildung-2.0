# 001 - The Vast Idea Of The Sun

## Trigger

Cassirer, *Das Erkenntnisproblem*, Vol. 2, Part 011, footnote 1:

`Disquisitio Metaphysica seu Dubitationes et Instantiae adversus Ren. Cartesii Metaphysicam. In Meditat. III Dubitatio III. (Opera III, 294.)`

Cassirer uses this passage to show that Gassendi explains the astronomical idea of the sun as an enlargement of sensory material, but cannot explain the mind's active power of enlargement.

## Local Context

Gassendi titles the section:

```text
De adventitia Idearum origine, ex iis, quae caeco, & surdo desunt comprobata: & de Idea unica Solis, quae sensu habita, ratione ampliatur.
```

That is, he is explicitly arguing for the adventitious origin of ideas from what is lacking to the born-blind and the deaf, and for one idea of the sun, received by sense and enlarged by reason. This title already names the line Cassirer later presses: the astronomical idea is not an independent intellectual object but a sensory idea amplified.

## Cassirer Passage

```text
Quod autem mente sola percipiamus vastam illam Ideam Solis, non ea propterea elicitur ex innata quadam notione; sed ea, quae per sensum incurrit, quatenus experientia probat, & ratio illi innixa confirmat res distantes apparere minores seipsis vicinis; tantum ampliatur ipsa vi mentis, quantum constat Solem a nobis distare, exaequarique diametro sua tot illis terrenis semidiametris.
```

Witness variant: the standalone scan reads `diametro sua`; Cassirer's Vol. 2 footnote quotes the Opera text as `diametro suo`.

## Working Translation

As for our perceiving by mind alone that vast idea of the sun, it is not for that reason drawn from some innate notion. Rather, the idea that comes in through sense, insofar as experience proves and reason resting on it confirms that distant things appear smaller than nearby things, is enlarged by the mind's own power by as much as it is established that the sun is distant from us and is equal in its diameter to so many terrestrial semidiameters.

## Surrounding Argument

Gassendi answers Descartes' two-idea example by reducing it to one sensory image under rational enlargement. The two ideas of the sun are not different in kind, but two more-or-less adequate conformities to the same sun, like two images of the same person sent from different distances. The astronomical idea is larger only because experience and reason have taught the mind how to expand the ordinary sensory image.

He then turns the born-blind test against innate ideas: look for this idea in someone born blind. Gassendi says one will not find it colored or luminous, nor round unless taught through touch, nor so large unless reason or authority has already enlarged what was previously accepted.

The closing move is sharper than Cassirer's short quotation alone shows. Even those of us who have often seen the sun and reasoned about its true size still return, when we want a distinct thought of the sun, to the species received through the intervening eye. The mind may stretch the received image, but Gassendi insists that this stretching does not give us a genuinely distinct idea of a vast body.

## Descartes' Reply

Descartes' response names the fault line Cassirer later uses. He says Gassendi proves nothing about the two ideas of the sun, because he takes both as one merely because both refer to one sun. More importantly, when Gassendi denies that the idea gathered from astronomical reasons is an idea, he restricts the name `idea` to images painted in phantasy:

```text
cum illam quam ex rationibus Astronomicis colligimus negas esse ideam, nomen ideae ad solas imagines in phantasia depictas, contra id quod expresse assumpsi, restringis.
```

Working translation:

When you deny that the one we gather from astronomical reasons is an idea, you restrict the name of idea to images alone, depicted in phantasy, contrary to what I expressly assumed.

## Notes

- Gassendi's denial targets the inference from "mind alone" to "innate notion." The idea is not innate; it is a sensory idea amplified.
- The amplification is not raw sense alone. `experientia` proves, and `ratio` confirms while leaning on experience.
- Cassirer's pressure falls on `ipsa vi mentis`: Gassendi needs a power of mind to enlarge the sensory idea, but his theory still explains consciousness as reaction to external stimulus.
- The passage therefore grounds the Part 011 finding that experience becomes the problem to be explained, not the solution.
- Descartes' reply confirms that the dispute is not only over the sun, but over the scope of `idea`: Gassendi restricts it to image/species/phantasy, while Descartes needs it broad enough for the intellectual object worked out by thought.
