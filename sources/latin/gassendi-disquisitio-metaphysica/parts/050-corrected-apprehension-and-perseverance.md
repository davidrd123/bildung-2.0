## Opening Finding

Gassendi returns from theology to natural light and gives Descartes a direct answer about how we avoid persevering in error. Right judgment and right willing require corrected apprehensions or notions: they must conform to things both as they are in themselves and as they stand in relation to another. Commanding or forcing intellect and will is useless while a contrary truthlike or better-seeming presentation remains. Even moral failure is reinterpreted through cognition: every sinner is ignorant because passion darkens apprehensions and makes a nearer lesser good appear clearer and more forceful than a distant greater good. The will can ask for examination, but only after intellect has already judged that examining first notions is desirable. Thus the will's turn away from a false judgment is not self-determination without intellect; it is determined by the same intellect through a different, reflexive judgment.

## Working Translation

VII.

To return to what I had begun, and so that I do not run out into supernatural matters but remain within the bounds of natural light, this also seems to need adding: for intellect to judge rightly and will to will rightly, nothing else seems to have to be cared for except that the apprehensions or notions we have of things be conformed to the things themselves, both according to what they are in themselves and according to what they are in relation to another. For unless this happens, command will be given in vain, or force applied in vain, either to intellect, so that it is persuaded of something when it sees something more truthlike, or to will, so that it prefers something when something better is shown.

And indeed, since the matter seems clear about intellect, it also seems no less clear about will. Namely, this seems excellently admitted in the common saying: every sinner is ignorant. For although that other saying is also well worn, "I see and approve the better; I follow the worse," nevertheless no one sins without some passion darkening the apprehensions or notions and disturbing judgment.

Thus virtue and honor may indeed be approved, but meanwhile pleasure, profit, honor, injury, or anything else is more present, appears more clearly, moves more vehemently, and for that reason is approved more and sweeps one away more than virtue, honor, reward, and the other things which, while passion is vigorous, are darkened and either are not attended to, or are not believed with certainty, or appear far away. Nor is return to them despaired of, while in the meantime what is at hand does not seem something to be omitted, and punishment, if any is proposed, is either not thought of or is hoped to be avoidable.

From this it follows that while we sin, we follow the things that for the time appear better, and we are ignorant for this reason: the species of the true or of the greater good is seen only as if from a distance and through a cloud, while the species of the counterfeit or lesser good shows itself as if close at hand and in the sun itself.

Therefore care must be taken not only that we have genuine notions of the true good, but also that passions not creep in and make darknesses, because of which, when intellect judges the worse thing to be better, will is borne toward it.

Finally, I add that the first notions or apprehensions we have of things occupy intellect in such a way that as long as they persist, so long the judgment that follows from them persists; and if they persisted through the whole of life, the same judgment would be held through the whole of life. And as it had happened at the beginning that the judgment was true or false, so it would persevere thereafter.

But because it happens that, either by chance, or by new study, or by admonition, or in some other way, the notions first held are so changed that the following ones are better, we therefore judge that we should not cling to the first ones in such a way that we do not examine whether other notions can be had, to which the first may perhaps be inferior, and therefore whether they should be put away so that they make room for sounder ones.

Once this judgment, or quasi-prejudgment, has been made, the will seeks that this examination be done and commands intellect to gird itself for doing it. And this, finally, is the very caution which I said was in our power: if perhaps we have fallen into error, we need not persevere in error.

Yet will does not determine itself to seeking or commanding this without the work of intellect. For unless intellect had shown it to be something to be sought and worthy of execution, will would never have stretched toward it.

Hence, when you ask: if intellect once determined will to put forward, or approve, a false judgment, by what is will determined when it first begins to guard against persevering in error? It is clear that it is determined by the same intellect; not indeed by the same direct judgment that is proposed for examination, but by another, reflexive one, by which intellect, taught from previous observations, judges it fitting that the judgment be examined.

## Notes

- Gassendi narrows the problem back to natural light after the theological digression about grace and supreme good.
- Correct judgment and correct willing both depend on apprehensions or notions being conformed to things as true in themselves and good in relation to another.
- Force applied to intellect or will cannot override what appears more truthlike or better.
- The moral maxim `omnis peccans ignorans est` becomes part of the error theory. Sin depends on passion obscuring apprehensions and disturbing judgment.
- The Ovidian contrary maxim, seeing the better but following the worse, is absorbed into the same account: passion makes the lesser or counterfeit good more present and brighter than the greater good.
- Avoiding perseverance in error is not will self-moving apart from intellect. It follows a reflexive intellectual judgment that first notions should be examined and perhaps replaced by sounder ones.
