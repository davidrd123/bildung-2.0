## Opening Finding

Gassendi clarifies that he is not denying the doctrine at issue but exposing the weakness of Descartes' reasoning. Descartes' charge that Gassendi attributes creator-level perfection to creatures simply restates the disputed point: whether creatures persevere dependently through continuous divine influx. Gassendi also rejects the claim that divine positive action toward non-being would be a creaturely imperfection. If God can draw from nothing and concur with human destructive actions, Descartes still owes an argument for why positive divine destruction would be an imperfection rather than an exercise of divine power.

## Working Translation

To return to your Response: when I said that there is in you a force, not one which produces you anew, but one sufficient to secure that you persevere unless a corrupting cause supervenes, you object that I do not notice that I attribute to the creature the perfection of the Creator, namely that it perseveres independently of another. But since I know what role I am playing, namely not that of attacking what the thing is, but of showing the weakness of your reasoning, you do not seem sufficiently to notice what role you are sustaining, when in place of a genuine response you allege plainly nothing other than an inconvenience, and indeed an inconvenience of such a kind that it is the very thing in question. For is this not exactly what is being asked, and what you undertook to defend: that you are conserved dependently on God the Creator, or persevere in being?

You go on that I also attribute to the Creator the imperfection of the creature, namely that by a positive action he must tend toward non-being if he ever wishes to bring it about that we cease to be. But again, I do not attribute, and may I perish rather than attribute, any imperfection to the Creator, from whom is every best given thing and every perfect gift; in whom I live, move, and am; to whom belong blessing, glory, wisdom, thanksgiving, honor, virtue, and strength forever. It is to your reasoning that I again attribute this imperfection, that you allege nothing other than an inconvenience, and indeed the very thing that is in question. Namely, the question is whether God preserves things by continuous influx, so that, when the influx is withdrawn, they cannot be preserved but cease to be.

I pass over that you also ought to have shown why it should be turned into an imperfection if God destroys something not only by the mere negation of concurrence, but also by positive action. For does he not have a force of acting by which, just as he draws forth from nothing, he also thrusts down into nothing? Or if he concurs with a human being so that the human drives a sword into the throat of a bull, will he himself not be able, without the human being, to drive the sword into the throat? Will there be an imperfection in God if he can by himself and by positive action slaughter a bull, or, as you would say, tend toward non-being, that is, toward the death of the bull? But of course you wanted to show how much practice you had in Scholastic distinctions.

## Notes

- Gassendi distinguishes doctrine from proof: he is showing the weakness of Descartes' reasoning, not denying every form of divine conservation.
- The charge of creaturely independence is treated as question-begging because the question is precisely whether creatures persevere only through continuous divine influx.
- Descartes' alleged inconvenience is not an answer; it is the disputed thesis under another name.
- Gassendi refuses to attribute imperfection to God and redirects the imperfection to Descartes' argument.
- `Negatio concursus` is contrasted with `positiva actio`: Descartes still must show why divine destruction by positive action would be an imperfection.
- The bull example presses divine concurrence into a vivid causal test: if God concurs in mediated destruction, why would immediate positive destruction be impossible or imperfect?
