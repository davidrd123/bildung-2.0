## Opening Finding

Gassendi identifies the root paralogism in Descartes' transition from material essences to divine existence. The major premise can only mean: what is clearly and distinctly understood to belong to a thing's true and immutable nature insofar as that nature is in the idea may be truly affirmed of that thing insofar as it is in the idea. To step from the idea inside intellect to the thing itself outside intellect changes the supposition, treating the same term in two states. Gassendi reinforces this with dialectical sophisms, then with the golden mountain. In the ideal state, it is just as true and immutable that a golden mountain consists of gold as that a rational animal is furnished with reason. The difference appears only after leaving the prison of intellect for the theater of nature, where experience supplies the criterion.

## Working Translation

Did I not therefore rightly add that, just as in enumerating the perfections or properties of a Triangle you had not counted existence, and therefore had not concluded that the Triangle exists, so in enumerating the perfections or properties of God you should not have placed existence among them in order to conclude that God exists, unless you wanted to beg the principle?

Plainly this was the root of the paralogism, going all the way from the transition itself, from the prior part of the Meditation on the essence of material things to the latter part on the existence of God.

For the transition was this: "Now if, from this alone, that I can draw out from my thought the Idea of some thing, it follows that all those things which I clearly and distinctly perceive to pertain to that thing really pertain to it, cannot an argument also be had from this by which God's existence is proved?"

Then your reasoning was the one that you expressed somewhere in this form:

What we clearly and distinctly understand to pertain to the true and immutable nature, essence, or form of some thing can be affirmed of that thing with truth.

But after we have investigated carefully enough what God is, we clearly and distinctly understand that it pertains to his true and immutable nature that he exists.

Therefore we can then affirm truly of God that he exists.

But since the words of that transition, "from this alone, that I can draw from myself the Idea of some thing, it follows," and so on, show that both in the transition and in the Proposition you use "thing" and "true and immutable nature" not as something you have known outside the intellect, outside your concept, or in itself, but only as something you have within the intellect, or in the Idea, by force of thought, it is clear that the sense of your Proposition can be no other than if it were stated in this way:

What we clearly and distinctly understand to pertain to the true and immutable nature of some thing insofar as it is in the Idea can be affirmed of that thing with truth insofar as it is in the Idea.

For if, from what you observe in an Idea drawn out from your thought, born or made within your intellect and consisting there, you want to make a step to what the thing is, or ought to be, in itself, or in the very nature of things and outside your intellect, the paralogism is evident: the supposition has been changed, or the thing has been taken in a different state.

Sophisms of this kind are heard in the schools of dialecticians: Animal is the genus of human being; a human being is an animal; therefore a human being is the genus of human being. And: Horse is predicated of many; Bucephalus is a horse; therefore Bucephalus is predicated of many, and so on.

Animal and Horse, that is, are now being taken as they are in the intellect and borrow what they are said to be from its operation, and now as they are in themselves and do not depend on the operation of the intellect.

Nor should you say that your Ideas represent things not only as they are in Ideas, or within the intellect, but also as they are in themselves and outside the intellect itself. For this very thing is in question. And since absolutely anyone can say the same with equal right, you see what would have to be admitted from so much disagreement among ideas.

You will say that you limit yourself to clear and distinct knowledge. But the question also is what that clear and distinct knowledge is, to which plainly no falsity underlies, as was discussed above. And no one among those who think opposite things fails to think that he has knowledge as clear and distinct as yours; so that, by the same right by which you prefer yours, any one of them may prefer his own.

I add that when someone has in the intellect the Idea of a golden mountain, he clearly and distinctly understands it to belong to the true and immutable nature, essence, or form of a golden mountain that it consist of gold.

For it is no less true and immutable that a golden mountain must consist of gold than that a rational animal is furnished with reason.

Then will it be permitted to make a step from the nature that is represented by the Idea and is within the intellect to the nature that is in reality and outside the intellect?

Surely only this can be said: what we clearly and distinctly understand to pertain to the true and immutable nature of a golden mountain, insofar as it is in the Idea, can be affirmed with truth of the golden mountain insofar as it is in the Idea.

Nor should you say that there is a difference between a golden mountain and a rational animal. There is indeed a difference after you have gone out from the ideal state into the real state, or from the prison of the intellect into the theater of nature: you see rational animals everywhere, but a golden mountain nowhere.

But as long as you remain in the ideal state, there is no difference of ideas; nor do you have a Criterion by which you can judge whether such an Idea is conformable to a thing existing outside the intellect or not. One must wait until, in the real state, experience becomes the criterion.

You will say that the Idea of a golden mountain does not contain a true and immutable nature, but only a fictitious one, composed by the intellect, because you can divide it not only by abstraction but also by a clear and distinct operation so as to understand mountain without gold.

But in the same way you will be able to divide and understand animal without reason, since, just as there are mountains that are not golden, so there are animals that are not rational.

## Notes

- Gassendi locates the paralogism in the transition from the Meditation's account of material essence to the proof of divine existence.
- Descartes' major premise is restricted to the thing or nature as it is in the idea; it does not license a step to the thing outside intellect.
- The fallacy is a changed supposition: one term is taken now in the intellect's operation, now in the thing itself.
- The dialectical examples make the structure explicit: "animal" and "horse" can be taken as logical predicables or as real things.
- The appeal to ideas representing extra-mental things is rejected as the very point in dispute.
- Clear and distinct knowledge does not solve the problem unless a criterion distinguishes genuine clarity from rival claims to clarity.
- The golden mountain shows the ideal-state limit: in the idea, its gold-content is as true and immutable as rationality is for rational animal.
- The difference between golden mountain and rational animal appears only in the real state, where experience becomes the criterion.
- The passage reinforces the Cassirer pressure: Gassendi makes experience adjudicate conformity between idea and extra-mental thing.
