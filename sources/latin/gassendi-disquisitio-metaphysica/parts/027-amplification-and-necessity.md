## Opening Finding

Gassendi closes the Dubitatio IX exchange by rejecting Descartes' claim that the mind's power to amplify human perfections beyond the human proves divine origin. Amplification does not imply necessary existence. If the mind amplifies human jumping into cosmic flight from Atlas to Caucasus, from Earth to Moon, and through the stars, it does not follow that such a human, another such nature, or God exists as the flyer. One could amplify again to a nature present everywhere and needing no motion; that inference still depends on the unproved principle that whatever we understand and conceive by amplifying exists in reality.

## Working Translation

VI. As for what I say afterward about the amplification of human perfections up to the degree at which they cannot fall within human nature, I am glad that it seemed to you to be an argument for demonstrating God's existence. But I do not therefore see the necessity of that very argument which you do.

For suppose that we so amplify in the mind the perfection or power of jumping which exists in a human being that we conceive him leaping from Atlas, or rather flying, into Caucasus; and from Earth into the orbit of the Moon; and then running through the globes of the stars, in order to discover what goes on there, or how all things stand in them. And although, recognizing that such a perfection, namely one amplified in this way, does not fall within human nature, we nevertheless judge someone happy if it should happen to him, I do not see why it should therefore be admitted that such a perfection necessarily exists, or that, if not a human being, at least another nature, at least God himself, is given who flies in that way.

For one may again amplify in such a way that such a nature is in all those orbs and everywhere, and so has no need of flight or motion in order to travel and know all things. The consequence depends on the same principle that would have to be proved: that all things which we understand and conceive by amplifying exist in reality.

You go on that it is this very force of amplifying all human perfections to the point that they are known to be more than human which you urge and contend would not have been in us unless we had been made by God. But the question is not whether you urge and contend, but whether you prove and demonstrate. As for your conclusion that you certainly do not wonder that it does not appear to me that you have demonstrated this most evidently, because you have not so far noticed that I have correctly perceived any of your reasons, I congratulate you on the happiness that comes to you from so great an absence of wonder.

## Notes

- Gassendi accepts that amplification can generate a beyond-human concept, but denies that this gives necessary existence.
- The jumping/flying example makes amplification deliberately concrete and absurd: enlarged human power does not prove an actual cosmic flyer.
- A further omnipresence amplification shows the same principle at work; the mind can enlarge away the need for motion without thereby proving reality.
- The hidden principle is explicit: Descartes would need to prove that whatever we understand and conceive through amplification exists in reality.
- The closing sarcasm keeps the focus on demonstration rather than assertion, urging, or rhetorical confidence.
