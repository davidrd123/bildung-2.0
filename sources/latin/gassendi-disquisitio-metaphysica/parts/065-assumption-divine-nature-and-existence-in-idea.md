## Opening Finding

Gassendi now turns from Descartes' major proposition to the minor assumption: after investigating what God is, we clearly and distinctly understand that existence belongs to God's true and immutable nature. Gassendi says the investigation has not inspected God in himself, but only the idea of God inside the intellect. The assumption therefore can only say that existence belongs to divine nature insofar as that nature is in the idea. Describing the God one conceives is not demonstrating that God exists. The opponent can press the point as a repeated begging of the question: whether God exists is the controversy; listing existence among divine perfections, appealing to a supremely perfect being that cannot be thought without existence, or distinguishing possible/contingent existence from actual/necessary existence all assume what must be proved. Gassendi closes with the compressed diagnosis: Descartes counted existence among perfections, confused existence in the idea with existence in the thing itself, and assumed existence in order to prove existence.

## Working Translation

From this it is clear what must be said about your Assumption. For first, when you say, "After we have investigated carefully enough what God is, we clearly and distinctly understand," and so on, your meditations show that you have not investigated by looking into God himself, whom you have not seen as he is in himself, but only into the Idea which you judged yourself to have of him within the intellect.

Therefore whatever you understand to pertain to his nature, you do not understand as pertaining insofar as the nature is in itself, but insofar as it is in your Idea. From this it follows that the assumption could not have been expressed in any other way than this:

But after we have investigated carefully enough what God is by looking into his Idea, we clearly and distinctly understand that it pertains to his true and immutable nature that he exists, insofar as it is in the Idea.

And that you are indeed assuming the divine nature not insofar as it is in itself, but insofar as it is in the Idea, is shown even by this: when you are going to prove God's existence, you take the examination of the Idea, or the explored Idea, as your argument. And because the Idea contains a description of the divine nature, you describe such a God as you have an Idea of, so that it may be clear what you want to be understood by the name of God.

For it is asked in vain whether some thing exists or not unless it is first made clear what sort of thing is being conceived.

Therefore, when you try to prove to the one whom you imagine denying God's existence that God exists, and you describe to him what sort of God is conceived by you, you are plainly describing to him such a God as he conceives to exist in your Idea, not as he really exists in himself. For he is waiting for you to prove that such a God exists in himself as you describe existing in the Idea.

Hence, if you press the assumption so that you contend that the existence of the divine nature conceived in your Idea must be taken for the existence of the divine nature in itself, then he will be able to argue that the sophism is manifest. And he will confine you to the assumption already stated, just as he would confine you if, concerning the golden mountain, you assumed anything other than this:

But after we have investigated carefully enough what a golden mountain is by looking into its Idea, we clearly and distinctly understand that it pertains to its true and immutable nature that it consist of gold, insofar as it is in the Idea.

And so he would object in this way: What, I ask, is this form of proving? It is disputed between us whether God exists, yet you assume that it pertains to his nature that he exists? And is this not the very question? Is not this very thing to be proved, that it pertains to his nature that he exists?

Perhaps you will say that this remains proved from the description you made. But is describing what sort of thing you conceive demonstrating that the thing itself exists?

Or do you prove it by enumerating all God's perfections: eternity, immensity, supreme power, wisdom, existence, and so on? But what else are you doing than placing existence among the perfections in order to prove that existence is existence, that is, the same thing by itself?

Or do you prove it because a supremely perfect Being cannot be understood without existence? But is this not the very controversy: whether there is a supremely perfect Being such as is understood by the name of God? Must not the existence of a supremely perfect Being be proved by some other reason than by supposing that a supremely perfect Being is given, which is wholly to beg the principle?

Or do you prove it because no existence except possible and contingent existence belongs to other things, while actual and necessary existence belongs to God himself? But is this not the very question: whether existence belongs to God, whatever that existence may finally be? For if someone denies it universally, he denies actual and necessary existence too.

And so he would indeed press nothing else against you than that you beg the principle. For me it is enough to have marked that the paralogism was born from this: that you treated Existence as among the perfections; that you confused Existence in the Idea with Existence in the thing itself; that you assumed Existence in order to prove Existence.

As for the rest, the things I set out in the Doubt seem to me, at least, to stand so firm that, after considering your response, I think it superfluous to add anything. Readers need only be warned to compare the doubt with the response and judge what should be thought of those words of yours: "Therefore you have not weakened the force of my argument in the least in any respect, and you remain always deceived by that sophism which you say could so easily be solved by me"; and, "Finally I pass over the rest, because while you say that I explain nothing, you yourself explain nothing, and prove nothing except only that you can prove nothing."

## Notes

- Gassendi turns to Descartes' minor assumption: that after investigating what God is, we clearly and distinctly understand existence to belong to God's true and immutable nature.
- The investigation is restricted to the idea of God inside intellect, not God in himself.
- The assumption therefore only licenses existence as belonging to divine nature insofar as that nature is in the idea.
- Describing what one means by "God" is necessary before asking whether God exists, but it is not itself a demonstration of existence.
- The golden mountain parallel returns: its gold-content belongs to its nature in the idea, but that does not prove a golden mountain in itself.
- Enumerating existence among divine perfections proves only "existence is existence."
- Appealing to a supremely perfect being that cannot be thought without existence assumes the existence of such a being.
- Distinguishing possible/contingent existence from actual/necessary existence does not help if the opponent denies existence to God altogether.
- The closing diagnosis has three counts: existence among perfections; existence in idea confused with existence in reality; existence assumed to prove existence.
