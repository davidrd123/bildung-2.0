## Opening Finding

Gassendi challenges Descartes' distinction between an idea being enlarged and merely made more distinct. The acarus comparison does the main work: before the microscope, the idea of a mite was a whitish point without parts; after the microscope, it becomes an articulated body with head, tail, legs, back, and other parts. If that idea is not only clearer but larger, why not say the same of the idea of divine essence when new perfections are discovered? Descartes cannot answer that all perfections "had to be contained" in the prior idea without begging the question, because an idea measures not the thing itself but the knowledge one has of the thing. The telescope, near/far vision, sketch/finished painting, and seal/wax analogies all press the same point: if something new becomes represented in the idea, then something has been added to the idea, even if the object itself had contained it all along.

## Working Translation

Again, then, what sort of thing did you understand your idea to be? No doubt something like the idea of a mite; for you make it indivisible, as the mite is with respect to the eye. And I do not seem to compare it with a mite ineptly. For just as the image or idea of a mite, depicted in your eye, brain, or intellect, represented to you twenty-five years ago only a whitish little point, without any distinction of parts, but now, after the microscope was invented and after you have used it, represents to you a rather large little body, with its own head, tail, legs, back, and other parts; so the idea of God which you first had represented to you an indivisible essence, but afterward it was made more distinct and explicit when you were able to discover perfections which had not yet been noticed.

Further, just as after you inspected the mite with a microscope its idea was made larger, why should the idea of the divine essence not also have been made larger? Because, you say, all the perfections had to have been contained in that same idea which was held before. See how true it is, what I said just now, that you equate the idea with the essence, as if the essence contained nothing which the idea also did not contain. It is as if you could either prove that they are in the idea, or, by saying that they ought to be there, or are contained in the idea because it is supposed true, you did not insinuate a manifest begging of the question.

Do you really say in the same way that the special ideas of the parts of the mite, which were detected by the microscope, had to have been contained in the idea which was held before the microscope? I think you will not say this. For the nature of the convexity of glass is better known to you than that you would think the species of the mite does not increase through the bending and interposition of the little rays arriving from those parts of the mite which, because of the unevenness of its little surface, were looking elsewhere and were not painting special images inserted among the others in the eye. Therefore there is also no reason for you to think that those things which you did not see in God through the prior idea were in the idea itself just as they were in God. For if they had been in the idea, you would certainly have seen them in God; and this is because an idea is the measure not of the thing, but of the knowledge which is had of the thing.

But if you are reluctant to admit the comparison with the mite, use the comparison with the moon, or the sun, or whatever object you like before and after the invention of the telescope: exactly the same result will follow. Or if you prefer to use the naked eye, without glass interposed, use the comparison with a thing seen now from far off, now nearby; the same result will likewise follow. For although above, when the sun was under discussion, you rejected what I said about the truth of a double idea, you nevertheless seemed afterward to approve it; and now too you seem plainly not about to reject it, since you suppose that an idea which was previously less distinct and explicit was nevertheless true.

Therefore, when you first see a person from a hundred paces and do not sufficiently recognize the face because of a less distinct species, and afterward see the person from ten paces and recognize the face more distinctly because of a more distinct species, the species or idea which you have of the nearer person is in you larger just as it is more distinct than the one you have of the more distant person. The same idea of the nearer person has more parts which were not contained in the other, because of the rays of parts which were looking somewhere other than directly into the eye.

But I see a comparison which perhaps will be more pleasing. You seem to want the prior idea to be like a rough sketch, or linear strokes by which a painter marks out on a panel the image to be made, and the later idea to be like a perfected image, adorned, say, with living colors. Yet although the rough image is not enlarged according to its outline, it is nevertheless enlarged by the number and condition of colors, and by the distinction and quality of parts. And however much you may say that it is not enlarged, you still cannot say that nothing is added to it; and therefore the same must be said by you about the idea of God.

And if perhaps you say that in the same unchanged image more things can be known more distinctly and explicitly when there has been greater light or the eye has come nearer, then you will change the supposition and will now compare the image with an object to which nothing new has come, and not with an idea to which something is added. Finally, because the comparison of an image impressed by a seal especially pleases you, consider this: if the whole seal had in the beginning been impressed on the wax, and to the proper depth, nothing at all would be lacking to keep the image from being, from the beginning, as complete, distinct, and explicit as it can be. In the same way, if the idea of God was impressed whole from the beginning, and as much as it can be, nowhere afterward could it be made more complete, more distinct, or more explicit. But if you say that it is made so, you must admit that the same thing happens to it as to the image impressed in wax, when from the beginning either the whole seal is not driven in, or is not driven into the depth according to the whole variety of projection and hollow; and therefore something can be added to it. But why do I linger over these things, since it is clear that, in whatever way it happens, something can be added each day to the knowledge, and therefore to the idea, which you have of God?

## Notes

- The acarus comparison extends the Part 034 point against point-like indivisibility into an optical and experimental register.
- `Idea mensura est non rei, sed notitiae`: the idea measures knowledge of the thing, not the thing itself. This blocks Descartes' claim that everything in the divine essence must already be in the true idea.
- Microscope, telescope, near/far viewing, sketch/finished painting, and seal/wax all show increased distinctness as addition to the idea.
- Gassendi recovers the earlier sun dispute: two ideas can both be true while differing in distinctness and content.
- The seal analogy turns Descartes' own favored image back against him. If the whole seal were fully impressed at the start, later distinctness would be impossible; if later distinctness occurs, the first impression was incomplete.
