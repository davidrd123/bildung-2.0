## Opening Finding

Gassendi refuses Descartes' attempt to refer all final-cause evidence back to efficient causes. If Descartes only means that praise drawn from the uses of things should be referred to the maker, Gassendi says that was already his point. If Descartes means that physics should investigate only efficient causes and abandon final causes, then he has cut off the very route by which the world's maker is recognized. Use and end are the same here: one cannot admire God from the use of parts without admiring him as author of the end. Bare inspection of the world might leave open an eternal world, self-governance, or chance formation; final order is the step by which the efficient cause is recognized. The bridge analogy gives the model: a forest-dweller may first mistake a bridge for a natural rock opening, but once he sees travelers crossing and the ordered stonework fitted to that use, he infers an artisan, intention, and power. Gassendi then argues that many divine ends are manifest in anatomy, while proximate efficient causes remain obscure. Descartes' claim that every efficient cause is easier to know than God's end is reversed: general causes are easy to recite, but particular proximate causes make philosophers fall silent or stammer.

## Working Translation

To add something further about the things you draw out more fully in the response: you object, "Whatever I next bring forward for a final cause must be referred to the efficient cause." But how do you take this expression, "must be referred"? For if the sense is that the admiration, praise, and glory elicited from reasoning about the ends of things, and from perceiving the uses of each thing as so fitting, should be referred to the maker who arranged everything so wisely and providently, you certainly say nothing that was not said throughout the whole doubt. And that this is your sense seems able to be gathered from the words that immediately follow. For you say: "Thus it is fitting, from the use of parts in plants, animals, and so on, to admire God the maker, and from inspection of his works to know and glorify the craftsman."

But if the sense is that one should devote oneself only to the exploration and contemplation of efficient causes, while dismissing the investigation and consideration of final causes, this is certainly the very thing I denied, at least when God is at issue. For I clearly said that this could be done on another occasion. That this latter is your sense is declared both by the state of the question and by what follows in your text. For when you said that it is fitting from the use of parts to admire God, and so on, you go on: "but not to divine for what end he made each thing." Here I want to note how you think human beings can admire God from the use of parts and yet not divine for what end he made the parts. For here use and end are the same; and no one can admire the author of the use who does not admire the author of the end. See, then, whether you act rightly when you dismiss the final cause in this way.

For from bare inspection of the sun, moon, the other parts of the world, and indeed the whole world, it is not thereby understood that there has been a maker, or cause, of the world. Someone could suspect that the world has always been such, and that the ancestors saw no other and descendants will see no other; or certainly that it has come together by chance from the principles of which it consists, so that it stood in this arrangement rather than another. Therefore in this way not only will final cause be banished, but the efficient cause too will be removed from the world. But from the investigation of ends, not only is the final cause itself understood to exist; this is also the very step toward recognizing the efficient cause, which in acting proposed such an end to itself.

It is just as if someone raised in forests and then going out saw a bridge, and at first noticed nothing but an opening through which the water of the river passed. He could suspect it to be something like what he had observed in a certain torrent, where he had seen two rocks touching one another with their tops and, with their bases separated, enclosing a little channel. He would reason nothing more than that either they had always stood so, or that, falling from the mountains on each side, they had thus met one another. But when he also saw travelers crossing over it with great saving and convenience, and therefore approached more closely to inspect the work, and considered the very wide arch, skilfully made and consisting of many rows of stones cut, trimmed, fitted together, and set in order, and in just such a plan that, considering the use, they could not have been arranged more skilfully, then surely he would recognize that the bridge could neither have been made by chance nor be unmade, but must have been made by an artisan who both attended to the end and wisely devised the arrangement of the work, and had enough power to carry out what he set up.

From this it follows that it is not only pious in ethics, as you say, to consider what end we can conjecture God proposed to himself in governing the universe. It is also not inept in physics, whatever you say, but indeed entirely apt and praiseworthy to consider this, insofar as it is absolutely necessary if we want to recognize that the universe is governed by God and, what is further required, that God is the cause of the universe. For when the universe has been looked at, the thought or persuasion does not immediately arise that it is the effect of some founding or directing cause. And since the thought can arise that the universe is unmade and governed by itself, or that it was made by chance and is administered by the same chance, how finally will the persuasion arise either that the world was really made by some cause, or is ruled and administered by some governor, unless we observe the wisdom by which all things in it are arranged? And since wisdom consists in this, that nothing be done rashly or without a proposed end, how will we understand that there is a cause which acted on account of the end which it still preserves?

As for your saying that it cannot be imagined that some of God's ends are more in the open than others, because all are hidden in the same way in the abyss of his inscrutable wisdom: this seems wholly false, unless, when some ends of things are very evident, we refuse to admit that God is the cause of the formation of such things, but refer everything to chance, and say that things are not arranged this way on account of uses and ends, but that, when things had been arranged this way by mere fortune, uses or ends were also discovered by mere fortune. For it is indeed true that some of God's ends are entirely unknown to us and hidden in the abyss of his inscrutable wisdom, namely the sort I acknowledged, which he himself either willed to be hidden or forbade to be investigated. But it is equally not true that God made none in the open, such as those innumerable ones that can be attended to even in the single human body.

For what? Can we therefore not say that when God formed a mouth in the human being, he had regard to this: that the human being would draw in and send out air through it, and would take in food for nourishment? And when at birth he willed it open and meanwhile prepared milk, the breast, and the nipple, which would be received by the mouth and sucked, did he not do this for feeding the infant? And when he made the esophagus hollow and expanded the stomach, did he not do this so that food would be transmitted through the one and worked up in the other? And when he so formed the other vessels, did he not do it partly for the conveyance of waste and partly of nourishment? And when he formed hard bones and distinguished the joints, did he not attend to the firmness of the body and to carrying out various bendings and motions? And when he created muscles and implanted tendons in the more solid parts, did he not want there to be organs by which parts could be moved? And when he gave motion to the heart and communicated it to the arteries, did he not give it for fostering and distributing heat, and for receiving, preparing, and transmitting arterial blood through the whole body? And when he led nerves from the brain through the whole body, did he not lead them so that they would become organs of sense and motion? And when he fashioned eyes and added eyelids and eyelashes, did he not fashion them so that we would see and so that the eyes would be preserved?

But why do I pursue individual things, when individual things are infinite? From these we seem able to assert without rashness that God had regard to these or those ends of this kind.

As I contemplated these things, nothing failed to carry me into admiration. But I say that there are especially three things which have always caused immense wonder. First, the umbilical vessels, so aptly formed, gathered, drawn out, implanted, and distributing nutritive, serous, and spirituous fluid; with their obstruction following at birth, and the more convenient opening, reception, and distribution of other parts, and so on. Second, the valves of the heart, so aptly prepared in substance, form, number, and position for receiving and sending out toward each chamber and through vessels also so aptly arranged; and therefore alternately closing and opening the very mouths of the vessels. Third, the tendons drawing the inner joints of the fingers into the palm, so aptly perforated for the passage of others. These things, and other things no less wonderful in reality, show us, whether we wish it or not, what end the author of nature had in forming them in this way. Therefore, when you compare causes, you seem to seize without reason on what I added about the difficulty of understanding efficient causes, as if every one of them were much easier to know than God's end.

But when you go on to say that someone thinks he knows the very causes which I propose as examples of difficulty, how much you promise not only about yourself but about others too! Certainly, as far as I am concerned, I refuse your pledge, since I frankly confess that I do not think so at all. I could also stand as pledge for quite a few others whom I know to have such candor that, although they have spent their whole life investigating causes of this sort, they nevertheless admit that these causes are plainly unknown to them. But, to say nothing about the rest, do you then think that you know causes of this sort? Do you think you can state them? That is, to say it again: do you understand and can you explain what agent forms and places in the way we observe those valves which are set at the mouths of the vessels in the chambers of the heart? what condition it has, or from where it borrows the matter from which it fashions them? how it applies itself to acting? what organs it uses, or how it uses them? what it needs in order to complete them with that temperament, consistency, cohesion, flexibility, size, figure, and position? O you, not a human being, not a hero, not a demigod, but altogether some god! But please see that you have not persuaded yourself of more than is fair about yourself.

For if you say that you know the agent of this sort is God, first you will say nothing that every pious person does not say; nor is there any reason for you to labor so hard in physics, since anyone from the common people knows how to say the same. Next, even if you know that God is the maker of all things, visible and invisible, you still do not know his nature, nor how he is within the womb, how he acts there, how or what matter he chooses, how he uses, forms, and tempers it, and so on. Third, since it seems manifest that God does not do all things by himself alone, but admits secondary and particular causes, let it be that you know God is the agent who forms and places the valves, and so on. But you know only the first and universal cause, about which there is no question, since no one doubts it and one can answer all questions equally with one word, or with no discrimination. The question is whether you know the secondary and particular cause which forms and places the valves, and so on. To know this, plainly, is worth the effort.

Further, if you say that you know it, and therefore answer the difficulties I proposed, o what a great Apollo you will be! But again, please see that, since among secondary causes some are general and remote, others singular and near, you do not take the former for the latter, since it is not the former but the latter that are being sought. For since there is no philosophical school which, once it has devised principles of things, does not try to derive from them all the effects of nature, there is none that does not discourse on general and remote causes. But when singular and proximate causes are demanded, the kind worth knowing, there is none that does not fall silent or chatter most foolishly. Some indeed bring forward more plausible things than others and seem to proceed somewhat farther. Nevertheless not even they can pass beyond probability, and they cannot help stopping short of insuperable intervals from the special and proximate cause.

So I do not deny that you, with your skill, can say certain things that are more plausible, more special, more proximate. But to say things by which you set the proposed difficulties in the open, and do not rather leave them within insuperable darkness: this is what consciousness of human weakness does not allow one to be persuaded of. And you may say that in physics everything ought to rest on the firmest reasons. Yet one must add: so far and as far as it is permitted to a human being. For with respect to generalities, everyone advances in the geometrical manner: nothing is made except from matter; nothing is made without motion and an agent, and so on. But when one must say specifically what matter, what motion, what agent, and what the other things are, everyone slips and stammers like infants. Hence, as to your saying that there is no cause that is not much easier to know than God's end, I ask that this be judged by comparison of any of those special and proximate causes with any end from those I drew out a little earlier concerning the parts of the body. Everyone will recognize what must be thought.

## Notes

- Gassendi splits Descartes' phrase `ad efficientem esse referenda`: if praise from final order is referred to the maker, Gassendi agrees; if final causes are dismissed from physics, he rejects it.
- `Usus` and `finis` are identified. To admire God from the use of parts is already to admire him as author of the end.
- Final causes become the route to efficient cause, not a rival to it. Without perceived end-order, the world can still be imagined as eternal, self-governed, or chance-assembled.
- The bridge analogy gives a compact epistemic model: ordered use turns an apparently natural structure into evidence of an artisan, intention, design, and power.
- Gassendi grants hidden divine ends, but denies that all ends are equally hidden. Many bodily ends are manifest enough to be named without rashness.
- Efficient causes divide into first/universal and secondary/particular/proximate causes. Naming God as first cause is too general for physics; the hard work is specifying the proximate natural agent.
- Physics can demand firm reasons only within human limits. General principles are easy; specific matter, motion, and agent force philosophers into probability or silence.
