## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' charge that he is looking for knots in a rush by pressing the alleged negative idea of nothing and participation in non-being. If an idea has no object, it is an image of no thing; if non-being is participated in, it seems to be divided so that part of us is something and part nothing. Gassendi does not yet dwell on these puzzles, because the more important point is that Descartes evaded the actual objection. The issue was not whether Descartes literally wrote that God's works are imperfect, but whether Descartes' own account makes the fallible faculty of judgment a divine work that appears imperfect. Gassendi then reconstructs Descartes' clear-and-distinct route from the idea of God to God's existence and exposes the circle again: Descartes has not yet proved clear and distinct cognition non-fallacious, because that proof depends on God as non-deceptive; yet he assumes its truth in order to prove God and God's authorship of that very cognition.

## Working Translation

Whether, when you make yourself a rush and think yourself without knots, you are not at least flexible enough to be tied into knots, and even to be counted among those which some Latins called scirpean and the Greeks called riddles, let others judge. I certainly seek nothing other than level ground, that is, perspicuity. Magnificent words do not move me, which for the most part neither those who hear them nor those who utter them understand.

You speak of an idea of nothing; you speak of a negative idea. I testify that I pass over this, as if it could not be understood that there is an idea and yet no object; that there is an image and yet an image of no thing; that something has the nature of an idea, whose office is to represent something, and yet is something negative, or represents nothing. You take this badly. You say that we participate in non-being. I pass over this too, as if it could not be understood how non-being is so divided that we take a part from it; how among the parts of ourselves one is something, another nothing. You do not put up with this either. You say that our participating in non-being means nothing other than that we are not the supreme being, or that very many things are lacking to us. But I say that it is not grasped why we should not rather be said to be only what we are, instead of participating in what we are not, what is lacking to us, and so on. Yet I declare that I do not want to insist on these things. You call this looking for knots in a rush. So then, in your sense be knotless, and strengthen yourself as well as you can.

Meanwhile, do you yourself require a knot, or not, when you say that I plainly attributed to you something you had nowhere written or thought, namely that you see some works of God as not wholly complete? For I said plainly nothing less than that you either wrote or thought this. I only said historically that you see some works of God as not wholly complete, which you yourself also immediately prove when you say that at least as parts they can seem imperfect. Whether, clinging to this fictitious knot and meanwhile leaving the true point untouched, you can seem to have done enough, those who pay attention will say for themselves.

For when I had objected that your idea of nothing, and your distinction of participation from non-being, do not prevent its being said that God could have given the human being a faculty of judgment, if not infinite, at least immune from error; and when, after your response that it is not strange if some things were made by God whose reason you do not understand, I had insisted that it is nevertheless strange how you could assert that there is in you a true idea representing God as omniscient, omnipotent, and wholly good, and yet see some of his works as not wholly complete, such as the faculty of judgment under discussion, which is liable to error, as an argument that he either did not know, or could not, or from some envy did not wish to create more perfect things: you passed over all this, and judged that one little word alone should be stuck on.

You were a little more cautious around what I noted about that review of yours, where, after you had premised that you now convert thought without any difficulty from imaginable things to things only intelligible and separated from all matter, you added:

"And indeed I have a much more distinct idea of mind, insofar as it is a thinking thing, not extended in length, breadth, and depth, nor having anything else from body, than I have an idea of any corporeal thing. And when I attend to the fact that I doubt, or that I am an incomplete and dependent thing, so clear and distinct an idea of an independent and complete being, that is, God, occurs to me; and from this alone, that such an idea is in me, or that I exist having that idea, I conclude so manifestly that God exists, and that my whole existence depends on him at each moment, that I am confident nothing more evident, nothing more certain can be known by human ingenuity."

For since you supposed that the road had thereby been leveled for proceeding further, I testified that I did not want to delay things, nor to insist continuously that you ought to have demonstrated everything more firmly; but that it nevertheless had to be kept in memory what had been conceded to you and what had not, so that you would not think a prejudice had been made. You paid no real attention to this, and did not think one must consider establishing matters more firmly. But since the things that are answered by you afterwards suppose that these things were perfectly shown by you, I therefore select from the quoted words this reasoning of yours:

If there is in me a clear and distinct idea of an independent and complete being, that is, God, and I therefore exist having that idea, then I manifestly conclude that I exist, that my whole existence depends on him at each moment, and that nothing can be recognized by human ingenuity more evidently and more certainly.

But there is in me a clear and distinct idea of an independent being, and so on.

Therefore I manifestly conclude that God exists, that my whole existence depends on him at each moment, and that nothing can be recognized by human ingenuity more evidently and more certainly.

That is to say: since you have not yet proved, but are going to prove below, that clear and distinct cognition is not fallacious because God exists and because the author of that cognition existing in you is not deceptive, nevertheless in this place you suppose it truthful in order to prove that God exists and is the author of your whole existence, and indeed of the cognition itself existing in you. As for the rest, nothing needs to be added to what was objected in the previous Meditation. I add only on account of the words you immediately attach:

"First of all, I recognize that it cannot happen that he ever deceive me. For in every fraud or deception some imperfection is found. And although to be able to deceive seems to be some argument of sharpness or power, without doubt to will to deceive testifies to malice or weakness, and therefore does not fall in God."

I add, I say, that here you only repeat, and hold as the principle of following reasonings, what was observed near the final words of the previous Meditation that you had not demonstrated, when you reasoned as follows:

If God is subject to no defect, he cannot be deceptive.

But God is subject to no defect.

Therefore God cannot be deceptive.

## Notes

- The `scirpus` exchange turns Descartes' anti-quibbling proverb back against him: Gassendi says he wants plainness and perspicuity, not impressive words.
- The negative idea puzzle is sharpened into a representational problem: an idea should represent something, but a negative idea of nothing seems to have no object.
- Participation in non-being is treated as unintelligible division. If non-being is participated in, Gassendi asks how the self can be partly something and partly nothing.
- Descartes' reply to the imperfect-work objection is treated as evasion. The disputed work is the fallible faculty of judgment, and that problem was left untouched.
- The Cartesian circle returns in its clearest Meditation IV form: clear and distinct cognition is used to prove God before God's non-deceptive authorship has secured that cognition.
- Divine non-deception is not newly proved here; it repeats the undemonstrated premise that God is subject to no defect.
