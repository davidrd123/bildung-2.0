## Opening Finding

Gassendi closes by lowering the authority of his own objections. Descartes need not care about them, because Gassendi's judgment is not weighty enough to matter much to him. The key analogy is taste: liking a food that others dislike does not prove one's palate more perfect, and liking an opinion that others reject does not prove one has found the truer view. Gassendi therefore leaves Descartes free to treat the whole set of objections as worthless, asking only that his friendly affection and veneration be recognized. The polemical run ends with an explicit repair clause: if anything was said too inconsiderately, Descartes should strike it through and take friendship as the higher priority.

## Working Translation

These are the things, excellent sir, that occurred to me to be noted concerning your Meditations. I repeat that there is no reason for you yourself to trouble over them, because my judgment is not of such value that it ought to have even that little weight with you.

For when some food is pleasing to my palate and I see that it displeases others, I do not defend my taste as more perfect than another's. Likewise, when an opinion pleases my mind and does not please others, I am far from maintaining that I have fallen upon the truer one. I rather think that it was truly said that each person abounds in his own sense; and I hold it almost as unfair to want everyone to be of the same opinion as to want everyone to have the same taste.

I say this so that you may think yourself, as far as I am concerned, free to make nothing of all these things that I have judged, and to give them no place at all. It will be more than enough if you recognize my ready affection toward you and do not count as nothing my veneration for your virtue.

Perhaps something has been put forward too inconsiderately, since nothing is easier when one is disagreeing. If that occurs, I wholly devote it to deletion: draw a line through it, and take it this way, that nothing was more important to me than to deserve and keep your friendship safe and sound.

Farewell. I was writing at Paris, on the day after the Ides of May, in the year of salvation 1641.

## Notes

- Gassendi explicitly refuses to make his own judgment authoritative for Descartes.
- The taste analogy softens the preceding polemics without retracting the objections.
- `suo quemque sensu abundare` is used as a pluralizing close: each person abounds in his own sense.
- Descartes is given permission to treat the whole set of objections as negligible.
- The close asks that friendly affection and veneration remain visible even through disagreement.
- Any inconsiderate phrasing is offered for deletion in advance.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `flocci-facere` is rendered "make nothing of" rather than "value at a straw" to keep the English idiom natural while preserving the contemptuous low-value sense.

Textual: `tuer[er]` regularizes an OCR-damaged form in `sartam tectamque tuer[er] amicitiam tuam`. The idiom and syntax are clear, but the bracket marks a witness repair.

Contextual: The conciliatory conclusion should not be read as a collapse of the objections. It is a social and philosophical close, not an argumentative retraction.
