## Opening Finding

Gassendi now reconstructs Descartes' Sixth Meditation proof as a disturbed syllogism. The causal premise about what God can make is placed awkwardly; the Second Meditation claim about mind as thinking thing is dragged in as if it belonged to the proof; and the conclusion depends on Descartes' own report of what he clearly and distinctly perceives. Gassendi's methodological objection repeats the private-demonstration problem: if Descartes only narrates what persuaded him in silent reasoning, others need not take the same thing as demonstrated. He then returns to the older Second Meditation syllogism: what Descartes considers under distinct concepts is treated as really distinct. The appeal to divine power does not rescue the proof. If the power is absolute, Descartes has left natural demonstration for supernatural theology; if it is ordinary, it adds nothing beyond the natural separability or inseparability of the things themselves.

## Working Translation

You proceed, namely: "Here, however, I have disputed and demonstrated that very thing."

But what your dispute and demonstration were can be understood from your context. For since from the beginning up to this Sixth Meditation you seemed to be setting up a dispute on this matter, because of that principle concerning God's power which was posited and quoted a little earlier, and since afterward you interposed the things mentioned about imagination and sense, then finally you wanted to weave the demonstration in this place. But how? Certainly in a form that seems extremely disordered.

For you began in this way: "Since I know that all things which I clearly and distinctly understand can be made by God such as I understand them." This was a causal proposition and, as it were, an antecedent, from which you deduced the consequent: "It is enough that I can clearly and distinctly understand one thing without another in order for me to be certain that one is different from the other." But it seemed rather that this should be inferred: it is enough that you can understand two things to be different, which would be done by clearly and distinctly understanding one without the other, in order for you to be certain that God can make them different.

Then follows: "Because it can at least be placed apart by God, and it does not matter by what power this happens for it to be judged different." This proposition again is causal, and either should have been placed before together with the prior one, or that prior one should have been placed after together with this one, lest the consequent be, as it were, strangled between two causal propositions. Or at least a double antecedent and double consequent should have been distinguished, so that it could be understood that those things are called different by you which can be placed apart either by divine or by any other power.

You next infer in this way: "And therefore from this very fact that I know I exist, and meanwhile notice that plainly nothing else belongs to my nature or essence except this alone, that I am a thinking thing, I rightly conclude that my essence consists in this one thing, that I am a thinking thing." But since you set up a complete enthymeme here, how do you deduce it from the preceding things? Evidently you wanted to draw it from the Second Meditation, so that it would become something like a member of the general proposition in your demonstrative syllogism; yet it should have been transferred into the assumption, so that the assumption itself, having two parts, one about mind and the other about body, would produce the proof of the first.

You proceed: "And although perhaps, or rather, as I will later say, certainly, I have a body which is very closely joined to me." This, however, is like the preparation for the assumption, which is contained in these words: "Nevertheless, because on the one side I have a clear and distinct idea of myself insofar as I am only a thinking thing, not extended; and on the other side a distinct idea of body insofar as it is only an extended thing, not thinking." Then finally follows the conclusion, contained in these words: "It is certain that I am really distinct from my body and can exist without it." And that, indeed, is your demonstration. So that its strength may be better known, the syllogism seems capable of being set out in this way:

In order for me to be certain that, of two things, one is different from the other, it is enough that I can clearly and distinctly understand one without the other, since I know that all things which I clearly and distinctly understand can be made by God such as I understand them, and one of those things can at least be placed apart by God, nor does it matter by what power this happens for it to be judged different.

But although perhaps, or rather, as I will later say, certainly, I have a body which is very closely joined to me, nevertheless on one side I have a clear and distinct idea of myself insofar as I am only a thinking thing, not extended, because from this very fact that I know I exist, and meanwhile notice that plainly nothing else belongs to my nature or essence except this alone, that I am a thinking thing, I rightly conclude that my essence consists in this one thing, that I am a thinking thing; and on the other side I have a distinct idea of body insofar as it is only an extended thing, not thinking.

Therefore it is certain that I am really distinct from my body and can exist without it.

If anything is lacking in the form, if the same words are not found in the proposition, in the assumption, and in the conclusion, accuse no one other than yourself.

As for the matter itself, first this must be repeated: since you carry out this thing which you call a demonstration within yourself; since you speak only about yourself; since you yourself promise yourself about yourself, there is indeed no reason to begrudge you if, when you testify that all things are understood by you so clearly and distinctly, you also testify that all things are demonstrated to you so clearly and distinctly. But since this is so, there is still no reason for you to be upset unless the rest of humanity thinks the same things have been demonstrated to themselves, since you are not addressing them in order to teach them, but only recounting what happens to you while you reason silently. Certainly when things are to be demonstrated to others, it seems one must proceed by another method.

For when you propose yourself as an example for others to imitate, this is to show them what principles compelled you to persuasion, not what principles can compel others; and to demand that if some principles do not seem to have enough strength from themselves, they receive it from the example of your persuasion, so that a prejudice may arise from your authority.

A legitimate method is one that is instituted toward another person and is not ashamed to set out its principles simply and as they are. For just as truth is lovable through itself and does not seek cosmetic coloring, so the demonstration by which it is recognized is preferable the simpler it is and the more stripped of circumlocutions.

Next, because you are not content to have made the case to yourself by your method, but contend that the same case ought to be made to the rest of mortals who use the same reasoning with you, one would indeed have to repeat that you should therefore have reasoned more solidly and used principles of the sort required for genuine demonstration. But there is no need to repeat what has been brought forward up to this point, so that the weakness of the principles on which your reasonings rested would be evident.

Hence there seems no need now to linger over all the things that the demonstration proposed by you contains. It is enough to touch on what was sent forward from the Second Meditation into this place. For there too we noted that you are led away by this reasoning:

Those things are really distinct which I consider by distinct concepts.

But I consider thinking substance and corporeal substance by distinct concepts.

Therefore thinking substance and corporeal substance are really distinct from one another.

This reasoning does not differ from the one in this place. For although in the proposition of the present argument you use the word "diversity," and in the conclusion the word "distinction," you will answer that you want to signify the same thing by each word. And although you apply this proof to the proposition, "Since I know that all things which I clearly and distinctly understand can be made by God such as I understand them," and so on, it nevertheless seems superfluous for the undertaking.

For the power of God to which you flee is either absolute, by which God does something above the order of nature, of the sort we are accustomed to call miracles, or ordinary, by which God acts according to the order of nature, cooperating and concurring as universal cause with any singular causes.

And if you flee to absolute power, you act outside the undertaking, since, having promised demonstration, that is, natural reasoning, you run back to a supernatural argument, and act not as a philosopher but as a theologian. Nor is there any reason for you to anticipate by saying that it does not matter by what power one thing is placed apart from another for it to be judged different. For it indeed does not matter by what power it happens for it to be judged different; but it certainly matters by what power it happens for it to be demonstrated. For if it happens by supernatural power, it will fall under supernatural estimation or faith; but it will not fall under demonstration or natural persuasion.

But if you flee to ordinary power, you make the matter useless for the undertaking, since God's ordinary power contrives nothing unusual and leaves all things as they are in themselves: if separate, separate; if separable, separable; if inseparable, inseparable. Nor can it be taken up as an argument for separability or inseparability any more than natural power itself can be taken up, according to which one thing is separable or inseparable from another. From this it follows that when two causes concur toward the effect of natural separation, namely God's ordinary and general cause and the natural and particular cause of the thing, and when the former is measured by, determined by, and made known through the latter, it follows, I say, that if the natural cause is known, recourse to the ordinary cause is pointless, since it contains nothing further and whatever is manifested is manifested through the natural cause alone. If the natural cause is unknown, recourse to the ordinary cause is still pointless, since it too is unknown, and it cannot be said what sort it is or to what it extends.

## Notes

- Gassendi reconstructs the proof as a syllogism whose form is disordered by causal propositions, transferred material from the Second Meditation, and mismatched terms.
- Descartes' private clear-and-distinct experience may explain what persuaded Descartes, but it does not by itself demonstrate the matter to others.
- Method must expose principles simply and publicly rather than borrowing force from the author's authority.
- The Sixth Meditation argument is said to repeat the Second Meditation structure: distinct concepts are treated as real distinction.
- The divine-power premise is superfluous unless it can function within natural demonstration.
- Absolute divine power belongs to supernatural faith or theology, not philosophical demonstration.
- Ordinary divine power adds nothing beyond the natural separability or inseparability of the things themselves.

## Translation Risk

Structural: Gassendi intentionally turns Descartes' argument into an awkward syllogism; some roughness in the translation is retained so the critique of form remains visible.

Lexical: `existimatio` is rendered as "estimation" in the supernatural-power passage. It carries the sense of judgment or belief and is paired with supernatural faith rather than natural demonstration.
