## Opening Finding

Gassendi clarifies that he is not denying, as a matter of religious faith, that humans are made in God's image. He is asking how Descartes can demonstrate such an image by natural reason. Descartes' reply that an image need imitate only in some respects is too weak unless those respects are enough for the prototype to be recognized through the image. No one needs Apelles' bones or flesh in a painted image, but a single hair-tip is not enough to count as an image of Apelles either. Applied to God, the human mind need not be totally conformed to God, but it must represent enough for God to be discerned. Gassendi denies that imperfect human thinking can represent perfect divine thinking in any adequate proportion; it represents God less than the tip of one hair represents Alexander. He then rejects Descartes' charge of bad faith, saying he reported Descartes' words and drew a contradiction from them rather than claiming that incompleteness and dependence themselves were likenesses.

## Working Translation

Elsewhere you ask whether, when I deny that we have been made in the image of God, and say that God is human-shaped, and collect the respects in which human nature differs from divine nature, I am sharper in this than if I denied that certain panels by Apelles had been made in the likeness of Alexander and then said that Alexander was like a panel, while panels are composed of wood and pigments and not of bones and flesh, as Alexander was. But, O sharpest of men (for I sensed well enough at the beginning that this had to be returned to you), I am not, indeed, especially sharp; yet I am sharp enough to recognize that you ought not deal with me so unfairly, as though whatever I object is therefore my own view. I am not the one who denies that we have been made in the image of God; and it is well that in the Dubitation itself too I testified that this must be believed by religious faith. Rather, I am the one who remembered that you had promised a demonstration, and that I had asked how such an image could be understood according to natural reason.

But after my dullness, let us consider your sharpness. Here, first of all, the question was not whether God had made the human being in the image of another thing, as you here make Apelles paint a panel not in his own likeness but in Alexander's. Yet this is nothing; let us suppose that Apelles painted not Alexander but himself, as when you said that a sculptor carves a statue like himself. Ought he not to represent on the panel his forehead, eyes, nose, cheeks, other parts, and generally his form, so that a judgment could be made from the panel about him? And just as it could be said of the panel that it was Apelles-shaped, so it could be said of Apelles that he was panel-shaped; that is, between prototype and copy there would be the relation called conformity, by reason of which one of the related terms would be reciprocally conformed to the other.

Why then, if God created the human being in his own image and impressed his form on him, could it not be inferred from natural light itself that the human being is God-shaped and God human-shaped, to indicate the conformity by which God as prototype and the human being as copy, or image of God, are mutually conformed? Is this the same as requiring bones and flesh in a panel, when the issue is an image, which clearly need not be conformed to the prototype, or to the thing of which it is the image, according to all perfections, even interior ones? You yourself acknowledge this. But when you add that it is not of the nature of an image to be the same in all respects as the thing of which it is the image, but only to imitate it in some respects, you certainly ought to have added that it is nevertheless of the nature of an image to be the same at least to that extent, and to imitate or represent only so much of the thing, as is sufficient for the thing to be recognized and distinguished through it.

Certainly, when I ask Apelles to paint an image either of Alexander or of himself, I do not require him also to provide bones and flesh. Yet I rightly require him to paint more than the very tip of his own hair, or Alexander's, on the panel. For however much the hair at the very end can be said to be the same as, or in some respect like, Apelles or Alexander, it is clear that this does not suffice for it to be counted as the image of either. From this it follows that although I do not require complete conformity with God himself in the human being or the human mind in order for it to be God's image, I still seem able to require enough conformity to suffice for God to be recognized. For if it is so tiny that what is represented of God through the human being, or his mind, and whatever is in it, has less proportion to God than the point of a hair has to Apelles or Alexander, it seems clear that the human being, or his mind, is not the image of God.

Hence, when in your text it follows, "And it is clear that that most perfect power of thinking which we understand to be in God is represented through that less perfect one which is in us," it is certainly clear that it is represented far less, indeed incomparably, indeed infinitely less, than the whole Alexander is represented by the point of a single hair.

Further, you exclaim that I reported your words in bad faith, pretending that you said a likeness is perceived by you in the fact that you are an incomplete and dependent thing, when on the contrary you had brought these forward as an argument of dissimilarity, lest it be thought that you wished to equal the human being to God, for you said, and so on. How, I ask, did I report them in bad faith, when I reported them without omitting or adding even one letter? How much more badly you report my words, when what I said by drawing a contradiction from the sense of yours was, "You perceive, you say, a likeness, while you understand yourself to be an incomplete, dependent thing," and you report this as if I had said, "You perceive, you say, a likeness in the fact that you are an incomplete and dependent thing." Everyone sees how far apart these are. But I am weary of quibbling, especially since it is clear that I meant nothing other than that two things are most unlike when one is most incomplete and most dependent, and the other most complete and most independent; which, with your usual artifice, you judged should be concealed.

## Notes

- Gassendi distinguishes faith-claim from natural demonstration: he grants image-of-God by religious faith but denies that Descartes has demonstrated it by natural reason.
- The prototype/copy relation makes conformity reciprocal in some respect; hence the provocative `Deiformis` / `hominiformis` inference.
- Descartes' "some respects" account of image is insufficient unless the represented respects let the thing be recognized and distinguished.
- The hair-tip example gives the threshold test: minimal shared feature is not enough for image-likeness.
- Human thinking represents divine thinking less adequately than a hair-tip represents Alexander, so it cannot carry Descartes' claimed divine image.
- The final exchange is about quotation and contradiction: Gassendi says he drew the contradiction from Descartes' sequence, not that dependence itself was a likeness.
