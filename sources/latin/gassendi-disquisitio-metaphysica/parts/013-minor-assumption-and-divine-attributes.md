## Opening Finding

Gassendi now breaks the minor premise apart attribute by attribute. The idea of God does not appear as a self-contained inner possession; it is formed through words, meanings, and social instruction before Descartes begins the proof. The supposed clear idea of an infinite substance becomes apophatic and negative: divine substance is not properly substance but `supersubstantia`; infinity and eternity name the absence of an encountered limit rather than a positive comprehension; omniscience, omnipotence, independence, and creation would require grasping what the finite mind cannot grasp. The word `tanta`, "so great," would be acceptable only if Descartes meant a confused, obscure, human idea supported by parents, teachers, priests, society, and the visible universe.

## Working Translation

Furthermore, to say something also about the minor, or assumption: first you say that you have an idea which represents to you God as a certain infinite substance, and so on. But when do you have it? Before you have plainly heard anything about God, anything about substance, anything about infinity? Certainly before you set yourself to this reasoning, you received from others both words and the meanings of words, according to which notions or ideas of the things signified were formed in your mind, stuck there, and still stick there now. Nor do you remember any notions or ideas representing the things signified by those words which you had in your mind before you heard others speaking about God, substance, and infinity.

Second, you say that you understand a certain substance. And indeed you do well to say "certain," for what sort of thing it is you neither understand nor can understand or explain by speaking. For not even corporeal substance, and not even the substance of the wax itself, however many ways you have turned it about, do you know as it really is; rather, you imagine it, or, since that pleases you, understand it, only in a certain confused and obscure way.

How, then, would you understand distinctly and clearly what sort of thing that substance is, which is so truly said to have made darkness its hiding place and to inhabit light, but nevertheless inaccessible light? Again, you do well to say "certain," as something not at all such as all the substances which are understood by us, or are thought to be understood by us: something which does not stand under any accident, which plainly subsists under no thing, which is not properly substance, which is rather super-substance, which is something far more excellent than whatever we understand as substance, and which finally is something we cannot understand.

Third, you say that you understand it as infinite. But this very thing is what has been utterly denied to you, since it belongs only to an infinite intellect to understand an infinite thing, because it alone is as intellective as the thing is intelligible. And although we use the word infinite, we do not on that account understand that whole wide extension by which the thing spreads out according to itself. Rather, once we have advanced some way in understanding and recognize that we are not permitted to advance further, even if we have not seen a limit there, then, as if returned into ourselves, we call that infinite whose limit we have not recognized. Therefore by this word we signify not what we understand about the thing, but rather what we do not understand.

It is the same as if someone entering a cave tried by sight to penetrate the whole thickness of a mountain. Having advanced some way, and being unable to see anything further, he will call the mountain impenetrable by sight, and by this word he will signify not what he has seen, but rather what he has not seen. But much has already been said about this, and certain things are also to be said shortly after, where your answer will be weighed.

Fourth, you say that you understand it as eternal. But this too is denied. For eternity is nothing other than infinity of duration, and just as infinity, whether according to place or according to perfection, cannot be understood, so neither can infinity according to duration. We say this: that an eternal thing lacks beginning and end. But for that reason we do not understand the whole breadth of duration. Rather, having advanced some way according to prior duration and some way according to subsequent duration, we stop, go back, and, with neither beginning nor end recognized, call the thing eternal, that is, something which has neither beginning nor end. Therefore by this name too we say not what we understand but what we do not understand.

Fifth, you say that you understand it as independent. But how can you do this unless you understand infinity and eternity, so that it appears to you that it depends neither anywhere, nor on any thing, nor at any time?

Sixth, you say that you understand it as supremely intelligent, or omniscient. But how can you do that unless you are as intelligent as God himself? For if you understand only part of what he understands, you understand neither the supremely intelligent nor the omniscient.

Seventh, you say that you understand it as supreme, or omnipotent. But how can you, unless you understand however many effects it ever produced, will produce, could produce, can produce, and will be able to produce forever? For if you understand only part of effects of this sort, you certainly do not understand the supreme, or omnipotent.

Eighth, you say that you understand it as creator both of yourself and of all the rest which exist, if any exist. But how can you understand this, first in your own case, if you know only about yourself that you think, that you are, and that you are a thinking thing? Have you proved that you were created? Do you remember that you at some time did not exist? Do you remember the moment at which you passed from not-being to being? Or how you passed over? Or by what acting cause you passed over? Whether you were made from some matter, or from nothing, or from parents, or together with parents, or without parents?

How can you understand this also of others, if you think all other things false, if you do not know whether anything was created or not created? When you have seen nothing being created, can you understand what it is to be created, what it is for something to have been created, what it is for all things to have been created? Were you there at the time when all things were created, where they were created, and did you perceive how they were created? Or if you were not there, if you did not perceive it, do you really understand what you say that you understand?

Ninth, therefore, as for your saying that the objective reality of the idea by which you understand God is so great that it could not have, and so on: this would indeed be admitted if you said that you understand God as infinite, eternal substance, and so on, in a confused, obscure, human, or rather very human, that is, most imperfect, way. But because you boast more than once that you understand clearly, distinctly, and not far from the way angels do, or those who no longer see through a mirror and in an enigma, but face to face, as Sacred Scripture says, it happens that the word "so great" is not admitted in your sense.

In the same way, when, tenth, you say that it is so great that it could not have proceeded from you, this would indeed be admitted if you understood the idea to be of such a kind that you could not have had it in yourself without parents, teachers, priests, and other human beings with whom you have lived and from whom you have often heard God called infinite, eternal, omnipotent, creator of heaven and earth. Or, if you imagine that you were such as we saw the first human beings imagined by the pagans, you could not have had the same idea without the existence and revolutions of the stars, without the adornment, order, and perfection of the universe and its parts. But because you understand "so great" in such a way that not even in this manner, that is, with external things shining before your mind, could it have proceeded from you, it is therefore not admitted.

## Notes

- Gassendi returns to the social genealogy of divine ideas. Descartes' idea of God arrives through words, meanings, parents, teachers, priests, and common speech before it can be treated as inner evidence.
- The repeated pressure on `quandam` matters. Descartes' "a certain substance" becomes a confession that divine substance is not clearly understood as a substance at all.
- The passage becomes explicitly apophatic: God is not properly substance but `supersubstantia`, and infinity or eternity names the limit we fail to find, not a positive object we comprehend.
- The cave/mountain analogy gives a compact model for Gassendi's account of infinity-language: the word marks failure of penetration, not comprehension of the whole.
- Independence, omniscience, omnipotence, and creatorhood each fail under the same criterion. To understand them clearly would require a finite mind to grasp infinite duration, all divine knowledge, all possible effects, and creation itself.
- The final test of `tanta` is important. Gassendi would admit a very human, confused, externally scaffolded idea of God; he denies only Descartes' claim that this idea cannot proceed from external things illuminating the mind.
