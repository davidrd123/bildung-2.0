## Opening Finding

Gassendi does not fully contest the last confirmations of Meditation VI here. He summarizes Descartes' remaining conclusions: body, bodily faculties, other bodies sending species into the senses, pleasure and pain as sources of pursuit and flight, the everyday reliability of the senses for bodily utility, waking memory as a mark against dreams, and divine non-deception. He then approves the final admission that human life is exposed to errors and that our natural weakness must be acknowledged. Descartes' response is sharply dismissive: Gassendi contradicts him in nothing, but says enough to show that length of speech should not be mistaken for a multitude of reasons.

## Working Translation

I pass over the remaining things, which you pursue fully and elegantly in order to show that there is something besides yourself and God. For you deduce that your body and your corporeal faculties exist, and likewise other bodies, which send their species into your senses and into yourself and produce passions of pleasure and pain, from which there are in you pursuit and flight.

From these you finally gather this fruit: since all the senses, concerning things that pertain to the body's advantage, indicate truth much more often than falsehood, you therefore infer that you should no longer fear that the things daily presented to you by the senses are false.

You say the same consequently about dreams: because they are not joined by memory with all the remaining actions of life in the same way as the things that occur while awake, you therefore determine that true things occur to you not in dreams but while awake. And from this, you say, that God is not deceptive, it follows entirely that in such matters you are not deceived. As you say this very piously, so when you conclude at the end that human life is exposed to errors and that the weakness of our nature must be acknowledged, you do altogether very well.

Response:

Here you contradict me in nothing, and nevertheless say quite enough for the reader to recognize from the prolixity of your words that the multitude of your reasons is not to be estimated.

## Notes

- Gassendi marks this as a passing-over rather than a new sustained objection.
- The sensory argument is summarized around utility: the senses are said to indicate truth more often than falsehood in matters concerning bodily convenience.
- Dreams are handled through memory-continuity with waking life.
- Divine non-deception is accepted in this summary as part of Descartes' concluding reassurance.
- Gassendi explicitly praises the final acknowledgment of error-prone human life and natural weakness.
- Descartes' brief response treats the entire passage as non-contradictory verbosity.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `fructus` is rendered "fruit" because Gassendi is naming the argumentative yield of the preceding deductions. "Result" would be smoother but less close.

Contextual: Gassendi's praise of the final conclusion should not be overread as acceptance of the whole Sixth Meditation proof. The following `Instantia` immediately contests Descartes' use of this non-contradiction.
