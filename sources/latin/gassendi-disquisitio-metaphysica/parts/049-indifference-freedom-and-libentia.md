## Opening Finding

Gassendi accepts that there is indifference in us, but denies that Descartes has thereby proved a will able to move without intellectual determination. He widens `indifferentia` beyond freedom: tables, water, and the sun can be indifferent to different outcomes without being free. Intellect also has indifference in its operations, though not freedom; will receives the name freedom only because its indifference is appetitive. Even there, will's flexibility is rooted in intellect's flexibility. As intellect leaves one appearance of truth only for something more truthlike, will leaves one apparent good only for something appearing better. The case of God as supreme good then forces a distinction between liberty and `libentia`: the blessed will clings necessarily, not by coercion, but by fullest consent and glad inclination. For Gassendi, Descartes' theology of grace can be saved only by calling this more willing or glad, not by making it proof that freedom survives without indifference.

## Working Translation

VI.

What I said about indifference seems so manifest that I appear able to appeal to experience with better right than you do. Indeed, I have almost no doubt that you too experience these things; but perhaps your reputation is more at stake than to let you acknowledge or confess them. So rejoice in your fortune as you please. I do not envy you that, nor whatever trust you can earn among whatever people.

It is enough for me, so far as I am concerned, that I am neither someone who has denied all indifference nor someone who has professed that there is no freedom in me. And so far as you are concerned, it is enough that you flourish your pen according to your own genius, while transferring the word freedom no less than the word will to the judging intellect.

Therefore, around the point where you argue that I experienced freedom when I admitted that we can guard not so much against erring as against persevering in error, what is needed is not so much a response as an objection drawn from the very explanation of the freedom and indifference for judging that is in us.

And so, although the matter remains almost explained from what came before, I nevertheless want to add this here: indifference seems to extend more widely than freedom. For indifference exists not only in the soul but also in other things. A board is said to be indifferent to this or that color; water to this or that flavor; the sun to producing this or that plant; and similarly with the rest. It is therefore less surprising if, although will is properly the seat of freedom because of the indifference it has, intellect nevertheless also has its own indifference, however much it is not called free on that account.

The intellect itself, namely, has this indifference: with respect to the first operation, it apprehends something or does not apprehend it; or it apprehends in such a way that it can, or could have, apprehended the opposite. With respect to the second or third operation, it renders judgment about some thing or does not render it; or it renders one judgment in such a way that it can, or could have, rendered the opposite.

But since whatever intellect apprehends is being, or at least appears to be being, and since consequently it cannot judge that what is, or appears, is not what it is, insofar as it appears as being, or that what is not, or does not appear, is what it is, insofar as it does not appear as being, the result is this: although intellect is indifferent in the way explained, it is nevertheless not indifferent to rendering a true or false judgment except insofar as what is false appears true to it.

Hence even if it is deceived, it nevertheless does not think itself deceived, nor will it think so as long as the apprehension of what appears remains the same. So if you want the judgment to be changed, the apprehension must be changed. The case is like an eye to which a stick, otherwise straight, is presented partly in air and partly in water. It sees the stick as bent, although it is not bent in itself, because it is informed by that species of it; and it will always see it as bent unless the species is changed by taking the stick out of the water.

I add again that the indifference which is in will takes on the name freedom insofar as will can will or not will something, or can so will one thing that it can also will the opposite. Yet the root of this indifference is located in the indifference that is in intellect, insofar as according to the flexibility of intellect there is flexibility of will.

For this is why, just as intellect is not indifferent toward true and false except insofar as the false takes on the species of the true, so will is not indifferent toward good and evil except insofar as evil has the species of good. And just as intellect cannot change a judgment, even one about a thing that is not true, unless the apprehension of the true by which it is veiled is changed, so appetite cannot be changed, even one about a thing that is not good, unless the apprehension of the good by which it is covered is changed.

Indeed, the same thing is true and good: true with respect to itself, good with respect to another; and each either actually or only apparently. Therefore intellect apprehends not only truth, by which the thing is in itself, or is thought to be, but also goodness, by which it is related, or is thought to be related, to another. And for that reason, as it is apprehended as good, so it is judged by intellect; and as it is judged by intellect, so it is sought by will.

From this it follows that, just as when we have wandered from what is actually true there remain countless degrees of what is like the true, some greater and some lesser, according to which something can be called like, more like, or most like the true, so when we have turned aside from what is really good, there remain countless degrees of what is like the good, with such variety that something can be called like, more like, or most like the good. And just as intellect never leaves one truthlike thing except for something more truthlike, so will never leaves something seen as good except on account of something seen as better.

And just as the indifference of intellect consists in this, that it is not so determined to seize on something as truthlike that it cannot seize on something more truthlike, so the indifference of will consists in this, that it is not so determined to something seen as good that it cannot be borne toward another thing seen as better.

And just as intellect, when by clear cognition it has attained the supreme good, namely God thrice-greatest, as the blessed minds in heaven have, is no longer indifferent so as to depart from it, because it can thereafter understand nothing truer, so will, when by the same cognition it has attained the supreme good, namely that same God thrice-greatest, is no longer indifferent, or, what is the same, is no longer free to be deflected from him, because it can thereafter will or love nothing better.

But it must be noted that the necessity by which will clings to the supreme good is not the kind that sounds of compulsion, violence, rigidity, and therefore resistance, trouble, desperation, or anything similar. Rather, it is understood with the fullest consent, willing delight, and satisfaction; it is therefore maximally spontaneous, most sweet, most desired. And so, although many call it both liberty and supreme liberty, it seems that it could better be called `libentia`, or supreme `libentia`, if one may speak that way.

Hence when you declare that there is no need to be able to be borne toward either side in order to be free, but on the contrary that the more you incline toward one, whether because you evidently understand in it the reason of the true and the good, or because God so disposes the innermost parts of your thought, the more freely you choose it; and that neither divine grace nor natural cognition ever diminishes freedom, but rather increases and strengthens it, all this seems true only to the extent that you are called free as if willing gladly, and choose more freely as if more gladly, and do not have diminished freedom as if you do not have diminished `libentia`.

Plainly, there seems to be no notion of freedom if you remove indifference. Meanwhile `libentia` itself is understood to be greater the greater and more perfectly known the good is. And when either God's grace and sweet disposition, or natural cognition, acts on our will, because this happens with respect to the true or to some greater good, the will's willing delight is increased and strengthened. Meanwhile freedom itself should not be said so much to be diminished as perfected, insofar as the inclination is toward the true rather than toward the apparent, toward the greater rather than toward the lesser good. These points, at any rate, are because of what you touched on from theology.

## Notes

- Gassendi does not deny indifference outright. He denies Descartes' inference from experienced indifference to a will moving independently of intellect.
- `Indifferentia` is broader than `libertas`: non-free things can be indifferent to different forms, flavors, or effects.
- Intellect has operational indifference in apprehending or not apprehending, judging or not judging, and judging this rather than the opposite.
- That indifference is still constrained by appearance. Intellect cannot judge false as false; it judges false only insofar as the false appears true.
- The bent-stick analogy makes the correction of judgment depend on changing the presented species or apprehension.
- Will's indifference is rooted in intellect's flexibility. The will leaves an apparent good only for something presented as better.
- Gassendi's `libentia` is an important coined pressure point. The blessed adherence to God is necessary but not coerced; it is fullest consent or glad inclination, not freedom in the indifferent sense.
- Descartes' theological claim about grace increasing freedom is accepted only by translating freedom into `libentia`, not by preserving freedom as indifference.
