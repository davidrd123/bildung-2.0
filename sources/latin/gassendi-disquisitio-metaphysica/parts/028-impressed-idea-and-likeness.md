## Opening Finding

Gassendi reopens image-causality through Descartes' claim that the idea of God is innate, like a mark impressed by an artisan on his work. Gassendi first denies that the idea is fixed and inalterable: it may be partly drawn from the senses, partly fashioned, and progressively improved as one learns more from other humans, angels, or God. He then presses the mark analogy itself. If the divine idea is an impressed mark, what is its mode of impression, its form, and its criterion of recognition? If the mark is not distinct from the work, is the mind itself the idea, the mark, and the subject of impression? Finally, creation does not imply likeness. A generated child may share nature with a parent, but a created work is not like its maker in that way; a house is not like the mason.

## Working Translation

Dubitatio X.

On the mode of impression of the idea of God in the mind, as of a mark impressed by an artisan, whose likeness the mind bears.

It remains, you say, that I examine by what account I received this idea from God. For I did not draw it from the senses; nor was it fashioned by me, since I plainly can subtract nothing from it and add nothing to it. Therefore it remains that it is innate in me, just as the idea of myself is also innate in me.

But in truth it has already been said several times how you could have partly drawn it from the senses and partly fashioned it. As for your saying that you can add nothing to it or subtract nothing from it, consider that at the beginning you did not have it equally perfect. Consider that there can be human beings, or angels, or other natures more learned than you, by whom you may be advised of some things about God which you do not yet know. Consider that at least God could so teach you, and perfect you with such clarity, whether in this life or another, that whatever you now know of him could be reckoned nothing. Whatever finally the case may be, consider that from the perfections of created things an ascent can be made to knowledge of the perfections of God; and just as those perfections are not all known in one moment, and more and more can be discovered day by day, so the idea of God can be had, not perfect in one moment, but more and more perfect day by day.

You go on: "And indeed it is not strange that God, in creating me, placed that idea in me so that it would be like the mark of an artisan impressed on his work. Nor is it necessary that this mark be some thing different from the work itself. From this alone, that God created me, it is highly credible that I was in some way made to his image and likeness, and that this likeness, in which the idea of God is contained, is perceived by me through the same faculty by which I am perceived by myself. That is, when I understand myself, I understand not only that I am an incomplete thing, dependent on another, and aspiring indefinitely toward greater or better things, but at the same time I also understand that the one on whom I depend has all those greater things in himself, not indefinitely and only potentially, but in reality infinitely, and thus that he is God."

Now all of this is certainly specious, and I do not object that it is not true, but I would still ask from where it is proved. To pass over what has already been said: if the idea of God is in you as a mark of an artisan impressed on his work, what is the mode of the impression? What is the form of that mark? By what mode do you discern it? If it is not something other than the work, or other than the thing itself, are you yourself therefore the idea? Are you yourself nothing other than a mode of thought? Are you yourself both the impressed mark and the subject of the impression?

"It is credible," you say, "that you were made to the image and likeness of God." It is certainly credible by religious faith; but by natural reason, how can it be understood, unless you make God human-shaped? And in what can this likeness consist? When you are dust and ashes, can you presume that you are similar to that eternal, incorporeal, immense, most perfect, most glorious, and, what is chief, most invisible and most incomprehensible nature? Do you know that nature face to face, so that by comparing it with yourself you can assert conformity? You say it is credible from the fact that he created you; but from that it is rather incredible. For a work is not like the worker unless it is generated by him through communication of nature. But you were not generated by God in this way; you are not his offspring or a participant in his nature. You were only created, that is, made by him according to an idea, so that you cannot say you are more like him than a house is like a mason. And this is even assuming your creation by God, which you have not yet proved.

You say that you perceive the likeness when you understand yourself to be an incomplete thing, dependent, and aspiring toward greater and better things. But why should this not rather be an argument of dissimilarity, since God, on the opposite side, is most complete, most independent, and most sufficient to himself, since he is greatest and best? I pass over that when you understand yourself as dependent, you do not therefore immediately understand that what you depend on is something other than parents; or if you understand something else, there is no cause why you should think yourself similar to it. I pass over too that it is strange why the rest of human beings or minds do not understand the same thing as you, especially since there is no reason not to believe that God impressed the idea of himself in them just as in you. Indeed this one point argues that the idea is not impressed by God: if it were so, then it would have been impressed on everyone, and all at once as one and the same idea; and all human beings would conceive God in a similar form and species, attribute the same things to him, and think exactly the same about him, whereas the opposite is very well known. But already too much on these matters.

## Notes

- Descartes' innate-idea claim is answered by gradual formation: the idea can be drawn from senses, fashioned, corrected, and made more perfect over time.
- Gassendi turns the artisan-mark analogy into a request for mechanism and discernibility: mode of impression, form of mark, and how the mark is recognized.
- If the mark is not distinct from the work, the mind becomes at once idea, mark, and subject of impression, collapsing the analogy.
- `Imago et similitudo Dei` is conceded as credible by faith but not by natural reason unless God is made anthropomorphic.
- Creation does not entail likeness. Similarity follows from generation by communication of nature, not from making according to an idea; the house is not like the mason.
- Self-awareness of incompleteness, dependence, and aspiration indicates dissimilarity from God rather than likeness.
- If God impressed the same idea in all minds, there should be broad agreement in divine conception; known disagreement argues against a common divine imprint.
