## Opening Finding

Gassendi takes Descartes' proof from the idea of God and turns it into a parallel proof for infinitely many worlds. If the mind can form the idea of infinite substance by amplifying finite substance, then ancient philosophers could form ideas of an infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles by amplifying this visible space, this one world, and a few principles. The argument extends Part 005's `ampliatio`: more dilation does not mean more real content. Descartes replies by making incomprehensibility internal to the formal nature of the infinite, so a true human idea of the infinite need not comprehend it exhaustively.

## Working Translation

Dubitatio VII.

On the chief demonstration for God's existence from the preceding apparatus; in imitation of which it may be fashioned that there really exist infinitely many worlds, of which an idea was had by the ancient philosophers.

You conclude: "Therefore there remains only the idea of God, in which it must be considered whether there is anything which could not have proceeded from me myself. By the name God I understand a certain substance, infinite, independent, supremely intelligent, supremely powerful, and by which both I myself and everything else, if anything else exists, whatever exists, was created. All these things are indeed such that the more diligently I attend, the less they seem able to have proceeded from me alone; and therefore from what was said before it must be concluded that God necessarily exists."

This, of course, is where you were heading. As much as I embrace the conclusion, I do not see how you conclude in this way. You say that those things which you understand concerning God are of such a kind that they could not have proceeded from you alone; by this you intend that they must have proceeded from God himself. But first, nothing is truer than that they did not proceed from you alone, or that you did not have the understanding of them from yourself, or through yourself alone. For they proceeded and were had from things, from parents, from teachers, from doctors, from the society of human beings in which you lived.

"But I am mind alone," you will say, "I admit nothing outside me; not even ears with which I heard, nor human beings who spoke with me." You can say these things. But would you say them unless you heard us with ears, and unless there were human beings from whom you received words? Let us speak seriously and say in good faith: those words which you utter concerning God, do you not have them from the society of human beings with whom you have lived? And since you have the words from them, do you not also have the notions underlying and designated by the words? Therefore they may not be from you alone, but they do not for that reason seem to be from God; they seem to be from elsewhere.

Next, what is there in them which, after first receiving an occasion from things, you could not then have from yourself? Do you for that reason grasp something above human grasp? Certainly if you understood God as he is, there would be reason for you to think you had been taught by God. But all these things which you attribute to God are nothing other than certain perfections observed in human beings and other things, which the human mind can understand, collect, and amplify, as has already been said several times.

You say that an idea of substance can come from you because you are substance, but that the idea of infinite substance cannot because you are not infinite. But for that reason there is no idea of infinite substance in you except in name only, and insofar as human beings are said to comprehend the infinite, which in truth is not to comprehend it. Therefore such an idea need not proceed from infinite substance; it can be made by composing and amplifying, as has already been said.

Unless, when the ancient philosophers, from this comprehended visible space, this one world, and these few principles, had their ideas in themselves in such a way that by amplifying them they formed ideas of an infinite universe, of infinite worlds, and of infinite principles, you are going to say that they did not form such ideas by the force of their own mind, but that those ideas proceeded into the mind from an infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles.

As for your defense that you perceive the infinite through a true idea: look, if that idea were true, it would represent the infinite as it is, and therefore you would perceive what is chief in it and what is now at issue, namely infinity. But your thought always terminates at something finite, and you say only "infinite" because you do not perceive what is beyond your perception. Thus you may not wrongly be judged to perceive the infinite through negation of the finite.

Nor is it enough to say that you perceive more reality in infinite substance than in finite substance. For you would have to perceive infinite reality, which you do not do. Indeed, in truth you do not perceive more, because you only amplify finite reality and then imagine that there is more reality in what has been dilated than in the same thing when it is contracted. Unless you also want those philosophers to have perceived more reality, which really existed, when they conceived many worlds than when they had one world in mind.

From this, by the way, I note the cause why our mind is confused the more it amplifies some species or idea. It seems to be because it draws such a species from its site, removes from it the distinction of parts, and so attenuates the whole until it finally becomes vanishing. Not to mention that the mind is similarly confused for the opposite reason when it contracts an idea too much.

You say that it is no obstacle that you do not apprehend the infinite, or everything in it, but that it is enough that you understand certain few things, so that you may be said to have a true and maximally clear and distinct idea. But no: you do not have a true idea of the infinite, but only of the finite, if you do not comprehend the infinite but only the finite. You can be said at most to know a part of the infinite, but not therefore the infinite itself. So someone who has never left a subterranean cave can indeed be said to know a part of the world, but not therefore the world itself; and he would turn out foolish if he judged the idea of so narrow a part to be the true and genuine idea of the whole world.

"But," you say, "it belongs to the nature of the infinite that it not be comprehended by you, who are finite." I grant it. But it does not belong to the nature of a true idea of an infinite thing that it represent only the smallest part of it, or rather no part at all, because there is no proportion between it and the whole. You say it is enough that you understand these few things clearly perceived by you. Yes, just as it is enough to see the tip of the hair of a person whose genuine idea you want to have. Would it not be an excellent portrait of me if a painter painted one of my hairs, or only the tip of it? Yet the proportion of all things we know about the infinite, or about God, to God is less, not just by far or by the greatest distance, but infinitely less, than the proportion of one of my hairs, or its tip, to my whole self.

In one word, these things prove nothing about God that they do not also prove about those infinite worlds; and all the more so, insofar as those worlds could have been understood more clearly from this one clearly perceived world than God, or infinite being, from your substance, what sort of thing it is not yet established to you.

Responsio.

Here you have nothing which you have not already said before and which has not been rejected by me. I will note only one thing concerning the idea of the infinite, which you say cannot be true unless I comprehend the infinite, and that at most I can be said to know a part of the infinite, and indeed a tiny part, which represents the infinite no better than the likeness of a little hair represents an entire human being.

I warn, on the contrary, that if I comprehend something, it plainly conflicts with that thing's being infinite. For the idea of the infinite, in order to be true, must in no way be comprehended, because incomprehensibility itself is contained in the formal notion of the infinite. And nevertheless it is manifest that the idea we have of the infinite does not represent merely some part of it, but truly the whole infinite, in the way it ought to be represented by a human idea; although without doubt another idea, much more perfect, that is, more accurate and distinct, can be had by God or by some other intelligent nature more perfect than the human.

This is the same reason why we do not doubt that someone ignorant of geometry has an idea of the whole triangle when he understands it to be a figure contained by three lines, although many other things can be known by geometers about the same triangle and noticed in its idea which are unknown to him. For just as it is enough to understand a figure contained by three lines in order to have the idea of the whole triangle, so too it is enough to understand a thing contained by no limits in order to have a true and integral idea of the whole infinite.

## Notes

- Gassendi accepts God's existence as a conclusion but rejects Descartes' route to it. The dispute is not atheism versus proof; it is whether this proof bears what Descartes loads onto it.
- The social genealogy of divine vocabulary returns: words and notions of God come through parents, teachers, doctors, and society before they can be used by the isolated mind.
- `Ampliatio` now receives a direct infinite-worlds test. If amplification of finite substance proves infinite substance, then amplification of this world could prove infinite worlds.
- Gassendi's account of amplification is also an account of confusion: enlarging an idea pulls it from its site, removes part-distinction, and attenuates it until it fades.
- Descartes' response gives the clean counter-formula: incomprehensibility belongs to the formal nature of the infinite, so a true idea of the infinite must not comprehend it exhaustively.
- The triangle analogy is Descartes' answer to Gassendi's hair-portrait analogy. A partial or non-expert grasp may still be an idea of the whole, not merely of a part.
