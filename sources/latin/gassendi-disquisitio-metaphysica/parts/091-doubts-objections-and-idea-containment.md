# Part 091: Doubts, Objections, And Idea Containment

Source status: preferred KB / ProQuest page-image witness. The span contains the first part of Gassendi's `Instantia` after Descartes' `Responsio V`, stopping before Gassendi's optical-species addendum beginning `Et, ut hoc verbum adjiciam`.

## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' meta-objection before returning to mind-body union. A doubt against a conclusion is already an objection against the reason, because a convincing reason would remove doubt about the conclusion. Descartes' appeal to the imagination/intellection distinction also does not help unless intellection gives clear knowledge of what mind is, not merely a verbal assurance that something beyond imagination exists. The `toto genere diversa` answer then repeats the very question at issue: whether mind is a subtle body or wholly incorporeal, and how an unextended thing could be most closely united or quasi-mixed with extended body. Finally, Descartes' earth-magnitude reductio is turned back: Gassendi did not mean that known things are physically inside the head, but that idea or species must contain its object representatively or objectively, and the unresolved difficulty is how such a representation can contain parts while lacking parts itself.

## Working Translation

You say well that what I have here concerning the union of mind with body is similar to the preceding points. For in truth, just as the preceding points were of the sort to which you did not give satisfaction, but only opposed either what was in question or your own authority, so all the points I have here are of this sort: points to which you do not give satisfaction, and to which you oppose either the very question alone or your authority alone.

You say that I nowhere object against your reasons, but only propose doubts which seem to me to follow from your conclusions. But grant that I object nothing except doubts: the doubts themselves are still objections. And if you do not call them objections, perhaps because they have not been set out in scholastic form, they are nevertheless of the sort that it is worth the labor to resolve. As for your saying that they are directed at the conclusions and not at your reasons, know that a conclusion cannot be doubted unless the cause of the doubt, if not even the fault, lies in the reason. Where the reason is convincing, the conclusion cannot be doubted. Therefore I could neither doubt nor object against the conclusions without having objected against your reasons; and since readers must be allowed to judge whether nothing has been objected by me against what you considered reasons, surely if anything was objected because the conclusions were not well proved, it was objected there.

You say the doubts arise only because I want to recall to the examination of imagination things that by their nature do not fall under imagination. But the doubts really arise because I weigh the force of the reasons, and do not easily or rashly assent to everyone who says he is bringing reasons. If, from the fact that you distinguish and interpret imagination and intellection in your own way, you think it is lawful for you to build whatever you like on top of it, we are not the sort who think that, so long as the mind feels a scruple sticking in it, nothing is to be worked through or chewed beforehand, but whatever it finally is must be swallowed down immediately.

You say, therefore, that the nature of mind is not known by imagination, but nevertheless is known clearly and distinctly by intellection. It would matter little to us whether we knew by intellection or by imagination, provided that we knew clearly and distinctly. But since we find that we are not helped by that distinction, and that from intellection taken in your way only this is to be gathered, that there is something besides what we imagine, not that it is clearly and distinctly known what that something is, as has often been repeated, we therefore do not allow words to be given to us in place of the thing. We object the doubts which remain for us, such as they remain, if there are any who want those very doubts objected to themselves, as you wanted through Mersenne to have a principle set up for me, and so on.

You go on: when I compare the mixture of mind and body with the mixture of two bodies, it is enough for you to answer that no comparison should be established between such things, because they are different in the whole genus. How well I predicted that you would answer or oppose nothing except the question itself or your authority. The question is whether mind and body are different in the whole genus or not, that is, whether mind is a subtle body or is wholly incorporeal. You defend that they are different in the whole genus, or that mind is wholly incorporeal.

And since you had seemed to yourself to reason in this way: what is thinking and unextended can be joined most closely and, as it were, mixed with body, so that it composes one thing with it and feels hunger, thirst, and pain, and the senses of hunger, thirst, and pain are nothing other than certain confused modes of thinking arising from its union and quasi-mixture with body; but mind is a thinking and unextended thing; therefore mind can be joined most closely and quasi-mixed with body, compose one thing with it, feel hunger, thirst, and pain, and have those confused modes from the union and quasi-mixture of mind with body.

Since, I say, you had judged that an unextended thing and an extended thing, that is, things different in the whole genus, can be most closely joined, mixed, and so forth, I objected, or if you prefer, I doubted, how that could happen. I asked you to prove how a thing no larger than a point could be united, joined, confused, and quasi-mixed with the parts from which an extended thing is composed. If mind were unextended, no larger than a point, and lacking parts, how could there be its mixture or quasi-mixture with body, when, although body has parts that can be mixed, mind has none, nor can it be said or understood what of itself it mixes or joins with the various parts, since it does not even have anything it could apply to one single part?

When you ought to have explained the mode of mixture or quasi-mixture and loosened the knot of the difficulty, what was the explanation, what was the solution? You said nothing except that I ought not establish a comparison with the mixture of bodies, because they are different in the whole genus. But was this not the very question: how things different in the whole genus from bodies could be mixed in the manner of bodies, or even with bodies themselves? And do you think you have satisfied the issue by that little word, so that no place for doubting remains? Should you not at least have said what sort of mixture or quasi-mixture is to be understood on the side of mind? How can mind be understood as present to body, that is, to many parts, since no body exists or is understood without parts? Since mind is unextended, it cannot be present even to one part, however small, nor even to the smallest physical point, since that is extended, but at most to one mathematical point, which is neither extended nor body.

Because of that word `quasi-mixture`, there had indeed come to my mind the Ciceronian passage: "What a quasi-body and quasi-blood is, I cannot understand"; "Nor can you, Velleius, though you do not want to admit it"; and, "Now what do you understand by that quasi-body and quasi-blood?" I not only admit but readily allow that you know these things better than I do. But once they have been said, what is there that Velleius can understand and Cotta cannot? Thus I understand what body is and what blood is; what quasi-body and quasi-blood are I understand in no way at all. You do not hide it from me as Pythagoras did from outsiders, nor do you deliberately speak obscurely like Heraclitus; but, let it be clear between us, you yourself do not understand it, and so on.

Those lines, I say, had come to mind; yet I did not think they had to be objected and pressed. I trusted that you would at least in some way give an account of your words and unfold them, or by an honest confession profess that you did not know. But neither course pleased you. Nor did it please you to declare how it can happen that mind, if it is incorporeal, unextended, incapable of dissolution of the continuum, of transmutation, and therefore of alteration, can nevertheless be touched by body and feel pain brought on by it, and in general how the corporeal communicates with the incorporeal.

These matters certainly ought not to have been passed over. Nor should evasions have been taken in the place of explanation, by which you silently slipped past, throwing back something that did not belong to the present doubt, but rather to one of the preceding ones, where the question was how a corporeal species could be received in an incorporeal mind so that it might understand body.

You add, namely, that parts are not to be imagined in mind from the fact that mind understands parts in body. But however that may be, what do you throw back? You go on: where do I get the claim that everything mind understands must be in mind? Certainly, if this were so, when mind understands the magnitude of the earth, it would also have that magnitude in itself, and thus would not only be extended, but even greater in extension than the earth. A sharp reply, because the containing thing must be larger than the contained. First, is the difficulty thereby solved by you? You allege, as they say, an inconvenience. Suppose I do not know how to avoid it, since you ask where I get it. Will my ignorance help you, so that you are not therefore still obliged to remove the scruple of the doubt?

Next, I did not precisely oppose that parts must be imagined in mind because mind understands parts in body. Let that stand as it may, since, assuming that it understands body through a species or idea of body, I asked how such a species, since it represents parts and therefore cannot lack parts, can be received in a subject entirely lacking parts. You accordingly ask where I get the claim that everything mind understands must be in mind. How wittily you ask this, as though from my words you had to seize an opportunity to pretend that I think a whole mountain, while it is understood by mind, is really or by itself in the intellect, in the brain, or inside the head, so that the skull contains it and therefore must be larger than the mountain.

Was the question not about the mode in which known things are in the knower, and about the common admission that nothing is in the intellect unless it was first in sense? And since the matter is usually explained through an idea or species by which what is understood is represented, was the question not only about the mode in which such an idea or species, representatively containing the object of intellection, exists in the understanding mind? Why then do you ask where I get the claim that everything mind understands must be in mind? I get it from the same place as the others, and also from you, who admit that we understand through ideas or species, by which things are representatively or objectively in the intellect and therefore in mind.

Therefore when you say that if this were so, when mind understands the magnitude of the earth it would also have that magnitude in itself, I will answer and admit just what must be answered and admitted by you too: that mind has in itself objectively, or through an idea and species, the magnitude of the earth. But when you go on that mind would therefore not only be extended but even greater in extension than the earth, I will either say that you infer badly, since from an ideal or, as they call it, intentional mode of existing you argue to a real mode of existing, that is, the mode in which things exist in themselves and outside the knower; or at least I will concede that, in the mode by which you say mind contains not only the idea of the earth, but also the idea of the whole world, or that its power of understanding is not limited to the idea of the earth's magnitude but extends even to the idea of the magnitude of the world, in that sense mind is not only extended but also greater in extension than the earth, since it contains not only the idea of the earth but also the idea of the world itself, which is greater than the earth.

And indeed, when I have answered in that way, I will think that you have been satisfied. But there will be no reason for you to think that I have therefore been satisfied. For even if the idea or species is the thinnest and tiniest thing, it should not for that reason be admitted as unextended and wholly without parts. The same difficulty always remains: how does an idea represent parts and yet itself lack parts?

## Notes

- Gassendi rejects Descartes' procedural demotion of objections into mere doubts. If a conclusion remains doubtful, the reason has not convinced.
- The imagination/intellection distinction is treated as a verbal stopgap unless intellection discloses what mind is clearly and distinctly.
- The `toto genere diversa` answer is made question-begging: the disputed question is exactly whether mind is wholly incorporeal or a subtle body.
- Gassendi reconstructs Descartes' union claim as a syllogism in which thinking plus non-extension is supposed to license close union, quasi-mixture, and confused pain/hunger/thirst modes.
- The earth-magnitude reductio is answered by distinguishing representative, objective, intentional presence from real containment.
- Even after that distinction, Gassendi keeps the species problem alive: representation of parts seems still to require some part-structure in the representative idea or species.

## Translation Risk

Edition/OCR: The phrase after `per Mersennum voluisse mihi con-` is damaged by line and margin noise in the OCR. I have normalized it as `constituisse principium, &c.` from the visible continuation, but this should be checked against a cleaner image or critical edition before citation.

Lexical: `objective` is kept as "objectively" in the Scholastic/Cartesian sense of being present as object-content in the intellect, not in the modern sense of impartiality.

Contextual: Gassendi's concession that mind is "extended" by containing the idea of the world is argumentative and conditional. It should not be detached from the representative/intentional versus real-existence distinction.
