## Opening Finding

Gassendi says Descartes has assumed, not proved, that geometrical ideas in us are not drawn from singulars. Calling Gassendi's proposals false, or dismissing them as tied to Democritus and Epicurus, does not compel assent. If geometrical figures are considered as limits under which substance is contained, those limits are singular and physical as the boundaries of singular substances; they become geometrical only when intellect considers them separately. Descartes' tiny true lines are unproved, and the microscope example cuts against him: if sensible straight lines become irregular under magnification, there is no path from eye to Descartes' swarm of mathematical linelets. The infant triangle case is answered by instruction and abstraction: geometry comes through teachers, books, invention, or reasoning from sensed figures while ignoring width and depth. The innate-idea thesis is reduced to an innate faculty for knowing things; actual ideas arise through seen or described singulars and become more general only by repeated comparison and abstraction.

## Working Translation

Nevertheless, you did not answer the things I myself brought forward, and which follow from what was disputed about the faculty in the intellect for composing, dividing, subtracting, deducing, and, in one word, variously handling the ideas which it has once drawn from singular things.

III. You merely assumed that you would force me to confess that the ideas of the triangle and of the other geometrical figures which are in us are not drawn from singulars. You assumed it, I say, but did not accomplish it. Nothing would have been more pleasant to me than to be compelled by demonstration, or at least to be moved by some force of likelihood.

For merely saying that the things I propose are false, or that the things I call false are true, on the ground that they do not agree with the opinion I have about the nature of things, nor with the one Democritus and Epicurus have, certainly does not compel me. With them I have nothing in common here; with you, by contrast, concerning the opinion you have about the corporeal nature of things, or the essence of material things, I conjecture I have very much in common. This, I say, does not compel; nor does it overturn the explanation by which I declared how propositions that are said to be of eternal truth can come about or be admitted.

Nor does this compel: that, after admitting there are not in the world substances having length without breadth, or breadth without depth, you add that geometrical figures are not considered as substances, but as limits under which substance is contained. For the limits themselves under which any substance is contained are singular. Since in reality they are nothing other than the bounded singular substance itself, in that respect they are something physical. You yourself show that it depends on the consideration of intellect that they are viewed separately, and that in this respect they are something geometrical or mathematical.

Again, it does not compel that you say you do not concede that the ideas of those figures slipped in through the senses. This is precisely the controversy, and the matter depends neither on your concession, nor on mine, nor on anyone else's concession or non-concession.

Meanwhile, when you admit that everyone commonly persuades himself that they slipped in through sense, you admit that everyone commonly recognizes that the ideas of those figures are not innate in them. Yet you claim to force me against everyone's recognition and persuasion.

What, further, is this thing you say, that such figures as are considered by geometers can undoubtedly be given in the world? How, after all, do you conceive that a line can be given which is length without breadth? You will not say, I suppose, that it is without substance. But if it is with substance, is not such a substance a physical thing? Is it not corporeal? Is it not a long body? Is it not indeed extremely slender, but nevertheless of some thickness? Is it not with parts disposed not only lengthwise but also breadthwise? And therefore with some breadth? And therefore other than, or not such as, the line considered by geometers?

You say that none are given around us unless perhaps they are so tiny that they in no way touch our senses. But let them not touch; it would be enough if you proved that they are given, or how they can cohere. Is it without body, or with a body lacking breadth and depth? You say they are composed for the most part from straight lines. Who indicated this? How do you know it? How do you grasp it? How do you prove that they are so composed?

You say this escapes sense, and you bring the example of a material straight line which, though very fine, is nevertheless observed through a microscope to be extremely uneven. But since this observation is most true about sensible lines, what hope is there that the multitude of those little mathematical lines of yours will ever appear to the eye? What hope is there that you will prove them straight? These things, then, also do not compel.

You go on: when we once, in infancy, looked at a triangular figure drawn on paper, that figure could not teach us how the true triangle, as considered by geometers, is to be conceived. But because we were not geometers in infancy, and only became such either by instruction from teachers or books, or by invention, study, and our own labor, therefore we received the triangle as consisting of lines without breadth only from teachers, Euclidean books, or others. Or, by reasoning in various ways both about the triangle and about other figures, which either ran into sense or were formed by us from those that had run in, by composition, subtraction, alteration, and so on, we gathered it by reasoning.

We could then notice only that our reasonings proceeded precisely and squared with practice itself when we supposed lines without breadth and surfaces without thickness; that is, when we considered length but did not attend to whatever breadth, or however much breadth, might be joined to it, and so on.

Therefore the cause of that triangle whose sides, or extreme lines, we have tried to conceive as lacking breadth was not simply the sight of a triangle seen in infancy. Rather, partly sight first, and partly other things, with instruction or meditation also applied, supplied the cause. Here, indeed, the triangle on paper is understood to contain the geometrical triangle in a quite different way than Mercury is contained in rough wood. For Mercury can really be carved out of the wood; but that indivisible triangle cannot exist except by consideration and supposition.

You go on: because the idea of the true triangle was already in us, and could be conceived more easily by our mind than the more composite figure of the painted triangle, therefore, when that composite figure was seen, we apprehended not that figure itself, but rather the true triangle. But because these things, and others, are said elegantly and Platonically, are they therefore proved elegantly and truly?

Show a child the figure you call composite, a triangle he has not seen before. He will indeed impress the idea or image of it in his mind. And if he has seen another, or has understood one as possible, he will also form a general idea of triangle, just as from one or more seen or understood human beings he forms a general idea of a human being. But if you ask him what other idea he feels excited in his mind, he will say anything rather than that idea of yours, namely one consisting of lines entirely lacking all breadth, as though he remembered having had it beforehand. He will say this, I mean, unless afterward you, or someone else, or the reading of Euclid, or an explanation of reasoning has suggested the thought that answers your question.

The example of a human face depicted on paper from little lines does not help. For that delineation is made only to represent a thing that exists outside intellect, and from which an image has passed into the intellect from elsewhere. But a triangle is representative only of itself; and it is plainly necessary that it first transmit an image of itself into the intellect before, by representing itself, it excites an image of itself in the intellect. Likewise, for it to excite the idea of a geometrical triangle, it is necessary that such an idea have first been impressed either by someone teaching or by reasoning suggesting it.

In one word, all those things said either by you or by others about ideas of things innate in the mind show nothing other than that there is in us an innate faculty for knowing things. For if the ideas you people imagine were of such a kind that, by trying to attend to them, we could attain even the smallest cognition of a thing, what you assert about them would become probable. But now they plainly help not at all. The things themselves outside intellect must be attended to entirely; so that whether ideas are posited or removed, it comes to the same thing, provided there is in us a cognitive power for things.

I know, surely, or can know, some animal never seen by you. What use to you is the innate idea of it that you say you have? Gaze at it as much as you wish. I am certain you will never attain knowledge of it until it has been shown to you or described by someone. But when it has been presented to your eyes, you will draw an image of it through your eyes; or, if you hear it described, through your ears themselves. Then at last you will recognize that there is an idea of it in you. But besides that idea, how will you feel that another previously possessed idea is excited in your mind? How will you prove it, if you set aside the boasting of words?

That no such prior and true idea is excited is clear from this: when one single individual of one genus of animals has been seen, we experience no other idea of it being had or excited in us than one drawn from that sort of individual. And when afterward we see another, and more individuals of the same genus, then we vary the special idea and, in place of one, have two or more. If, after having those, we also have a general idea, it will plainly be the one we draw out from singulars, casting aside differences and retaining what will be common to all.

And this too is evidence that such an idea is not innate but made by us from singulars seen: it is perfected day by day, and always becomes so much more general the more things we know and the more noticed differences we cut away. If it were innate, or at least excited, after the sight of one singular individual, it would exist altogether perfect and would not need ever-new excitations, which is what you say about the excitation of the true triangle from the sight of one paper and composite triangle.

## Notes

- Gassendi says Descartes has assumed rather than demonstrated that geometrical ideas are not drawn from singulars.
- The charge that Gassendi's geometry is false because it does not fit his physical opinion, or Democritus and Epicurus, does no argumentative work.
- Geometrical limits are singular physical boundaries when considered as boundaries of singular substances; they become mathematical only by a separate consideration of intellect.
- Descartes' possible true geometrical lines are unproved. The microscope example makes sensible straight lines less, not more, supportive of true mathematical straightness.
- The childhood triangle does not awaken a ready-made true triangle. Geometry arrives through teachers, Euclid, study, invention, or reasoning from sensed figures by composition, subtraction, and alteration.
- Breadthless lines and depthless surfaces are practical suppositions: reasoning works when we consider length and surface while not attending to attached breadth or thickness.
- The Mercury-in-wood analogy fails because Mercury can be carved out of wood, whereas an indivisible geometrical triangle exists only by consideration and supposition.
- The face/ink-line analogy fails because a face is an external thing already imaged from elsewhere; the geometrical triangle represents only itself and needs prior instruction or reasoning.
- Innate ideas reduce to an innate faculty or cognitive power for knowing things. The alleged inner ideas do no work unless attending to them yields cognition.
- General ideas are formed and refined from singulars: repeated individuals vary the special idea, and a general idea emerges by discarding differences and retaining what is common.
