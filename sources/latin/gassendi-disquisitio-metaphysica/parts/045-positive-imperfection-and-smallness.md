## Opening Finding

Gassendi attacks Descartes' reply by separating defect from smallness. An erring power is not imperfect simply as a power, but as erring or liable to error. Descartes' "negation of greater perfection" makes the defect look like a lesser perfection, while Gassendi insists that the issue is not why the faculty is limited but why it is defective in the work assigned to it. The lame-person and torn-coat analogies clarify the point: the complaint is not that a lame person lacks the speed of a deer, or that a child's coat is not adult-sized, but that the walking is unsteady and the coat is torn. Gassendi then defends his republic analogy and rejects Descartes' body-covered-with-eyes analogy. The proper comparison is not a body without eyes everywhere, but two persons, one seeing clearly and one seeing badly. The part closes by reconstructing Descartes' argument as a weak syllogism: a faculty can have defect insofar as it participates in non-being yet have nothing imperfect insofar as it is created by the supreme being; therefore the human judging faculty is perfect. Gassendi says the hidden question remains why God, who could have created a finite faculty perfect in its own kind, created one imperfect as he willed.

## Working Translation

I ask you in good faith: can there be any positive imperfection, if it is taken precisely as imperfection, or insofar as it is privation? Can any imperfection be negative, if it is taken precisely as a subject and insofar as it has a real nature? I ask again: have I ever taken imperfection in the former way as though I had taken it in the latter, that is, have I treated privation as a subject? Have I ever said that an erring power is imperfect insofar as it is a power, and not rather insofar as it is erring or liable to error? What, then, are you correcting here? Or are you not rather taking things backwards?

For when I say that it is an imperfection that we are liable to error, you say that this is rather a negation of greater perfection. Thus you suppose that the defect in us is some perfection, even if not as great a perfection as there can be. Nor can you say that the question concerns the judging faculty as such; for we are dealing with it precisely insofar as it is liable to error.

I have therefore always insisted on why such a power is imperfect, not on why it is small. It is as if I had complained of a lame person because he had a staggering faculty of walking, not because it was slight in comparison with a deer's; or of a freezing child because he had a torn garment, not because it was small in relation to a larger man. But you have taken it as though the defect in the lame person, or in his faculty, had to be explained not by the negation of straight walking but by the negation of a deer's speed; or the defect in the child, or in his garment, not by the negation of wholeness, but by the negation of the size an adult requires.

From this you cautiously avoided the difficulty and flew into a comparison which could not have been more apt. What in it does not fit? A prince is compared with God, a republic with the world, a citizen with a human being, will with intellect, goodness with truth, wickedness with error. Is anything missing? But, you say, the wickedness of citizens, when referred to the republic, is something positive; whereas a human being's being liable to error, or not having all perfections, is not something positive when referred to the good of the universe. Immortal God, how you muddle everything! For after saying that wickedness is something positive, you should have said that error is not something positive. Or, if you were going to speak of a human being liable to error insofar as he does not have all perfections, you should have spoken in the same way of a citizen liable to wickedness insofar as he does not have them.

Likewise, if you were going to say that an erroneous human being is referred to the good of the universe, you should have said that a bad citizen is referred to the good of the republic; or, if you were going to mark a disparity, you should have shown what good comes to the universe from human beings liable to error. But you preferred to escape by that phrase, both so that it would not be known what you meant and so that it would not appear that nothing can be said about the wickedness of citizens in a republic which cannot also be said about the error of human beings in the world. In reality each is something privative, and you can in no way call wickedness something positive by which it would not also be permissible to call error something positive.

You continue that the comparison can be better made between someone who would want the whole human body covered with eyes so that it might appear more elegant, because no part of it seems more beautiful than the eye, and someone who thinks that no creatures in the world ought to have been liable to error, that is, that none ought to have been less than wholly perfect. But no one has eyes who does not see that you ought to have set up the comparison with two human beings, one bleary-eyed, the other seeing clearly. For one may complain of a human being not because he has only two eyes, but because, having two, he sees imperfectly.

For it has very often been repeated that the difficulty is not why a human being has a limited faculty of judging, but why, having such a faculty, he judges imperfectly. And if a human being were considered deformed because he is not eyed over his whole body as much as he is considered imperfect because he is liable to error, then perhaps that comparison of yours could be set up. But that the matter is otherwise is shown by this alone: it does not even occur to anyone to desire a whole body like the imagined head of Argus, whereas there is no one who does not wish to be made immune from error.

You say that I suppose God destines us to wicked works and gives us imperfections. But I neither suppose nor assert this. I only set before you difficulties which occur and can be raised by anyone, by pressing those distinctions which you borrowed from the Scholastics and ought to have defended in their manner, not by thinking it enough to say that the things objected were false.

Indeed, I did not even mention that God destines us to wicked works, since I remember that the question here concerns only error opposed to truth, not depravity opposed to virtue. But what I myself proposed in the comparison about a prince, you yourself took as concerning God compared with a prince, and you tried to twist difficulties made against your reasoning toward the subject matter, about which there is no controversy, so that I could seem to be fighting not so much with you as with truth itself, which is far from the case.

You continue that this too is false: that God gave a human being an unequal, entangled, and uncertain judging faculty for those few things which he wished to be judged by a human being. But I ask reasonable people whether it was enough to say "false" and meanwhile to give no reason by which what we experience concerning the imperfection of the faculty is proved false. There is nothing surprising here, because here too the difficulty striking your reasoning finally had to be concealed.

For it is indeed true that the works of God are perfect. But because, when considering the mind or the faculty of judging, it seemed imperfect as being liable to error, and for that reason not a work of God, the inquiry was how it could be judged perfect so as to be a work of God. Therefore you reasoned in this way:

That faculty is perfect which, although it has a defect insofar as it participates in non-being and so is not infinite and lacks very many things, nevertheless has nothing in itself by which it is imperfect insofar as it was created by the supreme being.

But the faculty of judging which is in a human being, although it has a defect, that is, is deceived or led into error, insofar as it participates in non-being and so is not infinite and lacks very many things, nevertheless has nothing in itself by which it is imperfect, that is, is deceived or led into error, insofar as it was created by the supreme being.

Therefore the faculty of judging which is in a human being is perfect.

And you yourself saw clearly enough the weakness of the reasoning, since afterwards you moved every stone so that you could stabilize it. But it was too laborious a business. I continually insisted that the question is not why God did not make the faculty infinite, that is, perfect in every kind and absolute, but why, when making it finite, he did not make it at least perfect in its own kind, especially since you admit that there is no doubt God could have created one such that it would never be deceived.

For although he was not bound to create another faculty, and although you cannot give a reason why he created such a one, nevertheless this does not prevent its seeming that he created it not perfect as he could, but imperfect as he wished, in the same way as the craftsman of whom I spoke makes a key not perfect such as he could make it, but imperfect such as he wants it. And just as the will of the craftsman does not prevent the key from being imperfect, so neither does the will of God seem to prevent the faculty of judging from being imperfect. Therefore, because you nevertheless reasoned that it was perfect, I set up that comparison of the craftsman, which there is no need to pursue here, nor the other things clear in the context of the doubt.

## Notes

- Gassendi's central distinction is defect versus smallness: the problem is not limited power, but erring power.
- Descartes' `negation of greater perfection` answer is treated as a category shift that turns defect into a lesser perfection.
- The lame-person and torn-coat analogies clarify the false comparison: lack of deer-speed or adult size is not the same as unsteady walking or a ripped garment.
- Gassendi defends the republic analogy by aligning prince/God, republic/world, citizen/human, will/intellect, goodness/truth, and wickedness/error.
- The body-covered-with-eyes analogy fails because no one wants to be an all-eyed Argus, while everyone wants immunity from error.
- The reconstructed syllogism isolates the unresolved premise: a defect can be present by participation in non-being while the faculty remains perfect as created by God.
- The craftsman/key analogy returns as a direct challenge to divine-voluntarist escape: willing an imperfect key does not make it perfect.
