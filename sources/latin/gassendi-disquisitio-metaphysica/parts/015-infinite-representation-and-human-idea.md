## Opening Finding

Gassendi accepts Descartes' strongest concession with both arms: a true idea of the infinite must not comprehend the infinite. He then argues that Descartes immediately breaks consistency by shifting from comprehension, which belongs to the intellect, to representation, which belongs to the idea. If the human idea represents the whole infinite, then nothing of the infinite is hidden from us; if nothing is hidden, we comprehend it; but Descartes has just said that what is comprehended cannot be infinite. The triangle analogy fails because a triangle is not greater than the idea by which it is grasped, while infinite being is infinitely greater than the idea by which a human mind perceives it.

## Working Translation

And from these things, finally, it appears whether that prize-winning demonstration of yours for proving God's existence is of such a kind that you had to call upon, so to speak, the aid of God and of human beings, by appealing to the sacred Faculty so that it would sanction it and prescribe that it be held not only as equal to geometrical demonstrations in certainty and evidence, but even as surpassing them, and as such that no path lies open to human wit by which a better one could ever be discovered.

Now, by way of a final addition, I will add something to what you warn about the comparison I set up concerning someone who takes the likeness of the tip of a hair for the likeness of a whole human being.

When you say that it plainly conflicts, if you comprehend something, that what you comprehend should be infinite, I receive that with both arms; and likewise what follows: that the idea of the infinite, in order to be true, must in no way be comprehended. Indeed nothing truer could have been said, provided it is not extended to the idea which God has of himself.

But when you go on, "And nevertheless it is manifest that the idea we have of the infinite does not represent only some part of it, but truly the whole infinite," here I now demand consistency. First, you ought to have said that the idea we have of the infinite either is comprehended or is not comprehended, so that you could infer whether it is true or not true. Yet you diverted the reasoning, making a transition from comprehension, which is on the side of the intellect, to representation, which is on the side of the idea.

Next, to ask what you passed over: that idea we have of infinite being, is it comprehended by us or not comprehended? For if we comprehend it in any way, then by your account it is not true. If we do not comprehend it, then neither do we comprehend what it represents, since an idea is recognized only from the representation of the thing of which it is the idea, and judgment is made about the formal reality of the known thing from the objective reality of the idea.

Furthermore, if we do not comprehend the thing represented by the idea, how do we know that it is represented by the idea? How do we know that it is represented whole? I add: if the idea which is in us represents the whole infinite to us, then nothing of the infinite is hidden from us. For if even the smallest particle were hidden from us, it would not truly represent the whole. But if nothing of it is hidden from us, then we comprehend the whole infinite. For what else is it for us to comprehend something than to know it in such a way that nothing of it is hidden from us? But according to you, it conflicts that what we comprehend should be infinite. Therefore it conflicts that what is infinite should be infinite.

You say that the idea of infinite being represents the whole in the way in which it ought to be represented by a human idea. Very well; but the question remains whether such a human idea is true and really represents the thing. For you yourself comprehend what is represented to you by a human idea; otherwise you would neither speak about it, nor assert it, nor assume it as something to be demonstrated to others. But you said that what is comprehended is not infinite. Therefore what is represented to you by a human idea is not infinite. Therefore that idea is not true, even from this: that you said the idea of the infinite, in order to be true, must in no way be comprehended.

You go on: "Although without doubt another idea, much more perfect, that is, more accurate and distinct, can be had by God or by another intelligent nature more perfect than the human." But how cautious you are, since even when speaking of the idea which God has of himself, you say that it is more perfect than the one you have of him, not infinitely, but only much more perfect; otherwise, if someone pressed that there is no proportion between infinite and finite, you would be forced to admit that your idea is not true.

However that may be, do you remember what you said when I earlier distinguished two ideas of the sun, and two species of the same human being seen from far away and near, each similar and true, although one more so and the other less so? You objected that I was doing the same as if I said that true and false do not differ when they are affirmed of the same subject. Allow this now to be turned back against you. For in truth, when you attribute to infinite being both your own, or a human, idea and the idea of God himself, you do the same as if you said that true and false, when affirmed of the same subject, do not differ.

Why should I not allow it? Certainly, when I view the sun from this place on earth, and Eudoxus, if he gains his wish, views it from a place near the sun, we will both see truly. The species, or image, of the sun represented in the eye and intellect of each of us will be true insofar as it will represent something of the sun and will represent nothing that is not in the sun, although what I see and understand from the sun is almost incomparably less than what Eudoxus sees and understands.

But can the same comparison be set up between you and God as between me clinging to the earth and Eudoxus brought near the sun? Can a foot, or some other common measure, not be assigned which, when repeated, would measure both the distance between me and him and the magnitude apparent to him compared with what will appear to me? But none at all can be assigned which, repeated, would equal your intellect to the divine intellect, or the idea you have of God to the one God has of himself. By what reason, then, if my idea of the sun is false with respect to Eudoxus' idea, will yours be true with respect to the divine?

And how fitting would that likeness of yours about the triangle be if, just as that untrained person grasps the whole nature of the triangle while leaving to the geometers properties unknown to himself, so you grasped the whole nature of infinite being while there remained for God properties to be known which are unknown to you?

And how can you say that just as it is enough to understand a figure contained by three lines in order to have the idea of the whole triangle, so too it is enough to understand a thing contained by no limits in order to have a true and integral idea of the whole infinite? Plainly, that figure is not greater than the idea by which it is perceived. But infinite being is not only simply greater, but infinitely greater than the idea by which you perceive it.

Therefore it is indeed true that you understand a figure contained by three lines, for you understand how many lines the figure has and how it has them disposed. But it is false that you understand a thing contained by no limits, because you do not understand whatever it has, nor how it has it; you understand only what it does not have, namely limits.

But when you say that a true and integral idea of the whole infinite is had, I again demand consistency, or how this coheres with what you judged, that the idea of the infinite, in order to be true, must in no way be comprehended, since to have a true and integral idea is nothing other than for it to be comprehended. How also does it cohere with what you said, that it conflicts for what is comprehended to be infinite, since an idea of the whole infinite cannot be had unless the infinite is comprehended? For whoever says "whole" excludes nothing, or leaves nothing incomprehended.

## Notes

- Gassendi accepts Descartes' incomprehensibility premise and turns it against the claim that the human idea represents the whole infinite.
- The hinge is `comprehensio` versus `repraesentatio`: Descartes shifts from the intellect's act to the idea's representational content, but Gassendi insists the two cannot be separated when the truth of the idea is at stake.
- `Totum infinitum` becomes the pressure phrase. To represent the whole is to leave nothing hidden; to leave nothing hidden is to comprehend; but Descartes says the infinite cannot be comprehended.
- The sun/Eudoxus comparison revisits the original Cassirer sun anchor. Gassendi allows degrees of true representation where there is some common measure; he denies that the human-divine gap has any such measure.
- The triangle analogy fails because the triangle is not greater than the idea by which it is perceived, while infinite being is infinitely greater than a human idea.
