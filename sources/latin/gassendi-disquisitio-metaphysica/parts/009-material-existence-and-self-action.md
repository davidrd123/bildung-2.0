## Opening Finding

Gassendi refuses Descartes' demand for a proof of material existence at the level of ordinary certainty: doubting the world's existence is not rigor but foolishness. He then returns to the scope of `idea`. Descartes says idea means everything thought; Gassendi replies that whatever is thought must stand before the mind under at least a mental image, and this is exactly what Descartes is trying to avoid because such an image looks corporeal. The second half presses `nothing acts on itself`: action and passion require distinct termini, and Descartes' spinning-top example is treated as successive part-to-part impulse, not self-action.

## Working Translation

Instantia.

How much you would like me to make myself foolish, if, after your fashion, I were to doubt that there is anything besides me in the world, and if, for proving that anything exists, I were to seek or bring forward reasons other than evidence. You may dream, be deceived, be deluded as you wish; take care in whatever way you please not to think that you are living with us, speaking to us, writing to us, and so on. I continue to act in good faith, because I find so many things in the world, or nature of things, that are obscure and worthy of investigation that I think it too childish and ridiculous to turn things so open into doubt and to waste time proving their existence.

And say that I do nothing but ask questions; say that I appeal to prejudged opinions; say that I had no reason; say that it would have been better if I had kept silent. You will nevertheless not force from me the thought that one must play the fool.

To the matter itself, rather. You say that whatever I dispute here about ideas needs no response. But the device is already clear: when you have nothing to answer, you say that nothing is worthy of response. You add the reason: because I restrict the name `idea` to images painted in phantasy, while you extend it to everything that is thought. How carefully you avoid the rock. After saying that I restrict the name of idea to images painted in phantasy, which I did not do, you very shrewdly took care not to add that you extend it to images painted in the mind, which you knew I had not excluded and about which there was an equal, or rather the same, difficulty. Instead, sidelong, you added that you extend it to everything that is thought.

But I ask you: whatever is thought, is it not the object of thought? And is that object not set before, and as it were present to, the thinking mind? And is it not set before and present under some image, if not a phantastic one, then at least a mental one? And will that image not be an idea? And reciprocally, will that idea not be an image existing in the mind? And did you yourself not also say, as has been noted, that only those things which are thought as images are properly ideas?

Prove, then, which images among all images I did not admit to be ideas, which I reduced to phantasy rather than to mind, or which I did not extend to everything that is thought. But this is precisely what troubles you: since nothing can be set before or present to the thinking mind except as an image, you see that such an image is always a corporeal species, and therefore that the thing thought is not exhibited to the mind except under a corporeal species. Yet you labored to persuade us that incorporeal substances are not exhibited to us in this way, so that you could infer that knowledge of them does not proceed from or depend on knowledge of corporeal substances.

Next you ask in passing by what argument I prove that nothing acts on itself, as though I were not accustomed to using arguments. But it seemed superfluous to me to add an argument where it was so manifest that action and passion are the same as motion. Since every motion has a terminus from which and a terminus to which, as they say, action itself is from the agent as from the terminus from which, and passion is in the patient as in the terminus to which. Agent and patient are therefore distinct; otherwise there would not be two termini, and for that reason no motion.

Hence I was content to bring forward the example of the hand. And so that you would not quibble that the hand acts on itself insofar as the end of the finger strikes the palm, although then the whole hand does not act on itself but one part of the hand acts on another, I used the example of the fingertip, which no longer strikes itself, and of the heel, which, however it can be driven into another part of the same body, cannot be driven into itself.

But because I did not say everything, because I did not say what was superfluous, you seized an occasion, as if from something unexpected, to give credit to yourself when saying that I bring forward no reasons, use no arguments, and object nothing worthy of response. As for me, others may judge; they will never attribute so little to me that I will not think more than enough has been attributed. But as for you, since in this place you blame me for not having used an argument when I said that nothing acts on itself, and from the opposite side you contend that something can act on itself, by what argument, I ask, do you refute what I proposed? By what argument do you prove what you assert? By none at all, certainly. It is strange that you use no arguments where you complain that I used none.

You say that it is easy to respond to the examples I brought forward. But you responded to none of those mentioned, and said only something about the example of the eye, which I myself had brought forward only to anticipate a response. You passed over the objection by which I proposed that the emission of a species from the eye into the mirror is not the return from the mirror into the eye, so as to show that in the emission the eye is agent and the mirror patient, but in the return the mirror is agent and the eye patient. Instead, you returned to the mind, which, since it knows other things, also knows itself, although this very thing is in question and has been proved by no argument from you.

Then, because that answer seemed hardly suitable, you brought forward not an argument but only an example among corporeal things. For you say, "When a top turns itself in a circle, is that rotation not an action which it exercises on itself?" Immortal God! As if it were not a hand or whip which, driven in a circle, impressed this spinning motion. As if the case were not the same as that of an arrow flying through the air. And even if perhaps you will say that the arrow has that motion not from the bow or hand but from itself, will the same thing for that reason act on itself?

Certainly one can say that part acts on part, that is, each following part, from the nock driven by the string all the way to the tip, acts on any preceding part. But it does not follow that the same part acts on itself, or that the whole arrow acts on the whole arrow. Otherwise there would be action as much from the tip to the nock as from the nock to the tip, and so the arrow would not advance more than it would retreat, and would therefore be immobile.

Thus, too, the reason the top turns in a circle is both the mode of impression and its shape, by which the parts are arranged not directly, as in the arrow, but circularly, and so propel themselves indeed in a circle, but in order, or successively. Thus if one first part is marked, after the impression has arrived in order at the last part, it is impelled by that last part as contiguous to it, and so several revolutions are completed. Yet for this reason neither does the same part act on itself, nor the whole top on the whole top, nor is the rotation an action which the top exercises on itself, except in the way in which, when a man strikes his thigh with his hand, you would call this blow an action which the man exercises on himself, and therefore conclude that the same thing acts on itself.

You say afterward that it should be noted that you did not affirm that ideas of material things are deduced from the mind, as I not very honestly pretend here. But tell me, are you not the very one who calls yourself mind, or speaks and has spoken in the name of mind? And were these not your words: "As for the ideas of corporeal things, nothing occurs in them that is so great that it does not seem it could have proceeded from me myself"? And after making the distinction: "Of those things which are clear and distinct in the ideas of corporeal things, I seem to have been able to borrow some from the idea of myself, namely substance, duration, number, and whatever else there is of this kind. For when I think that a stone is a substance, or a thing which is apt to exist by itself, and that I too am a substance, although I conceive myself as a thinking and not extended thing, and the stone as an extended and not thinking thing, so that there is the greatest diversity between the two concepts, nevertheless they seem to agree in the notion of substance."

Tell me then, what did I invent? What did I say not in sufficiently good faith? Was it that I said you affirm, when you only said it seems? But this is the very thing you invent, since you attribute that word to me. My words were these: "I pause only over ideas of corporeal things, because there is no small difficulty in how, from the idea of yourself alone, while you act as incorporeal and consider yourself as such, you can deduce them from yourself." There is no mention of affirmation; I only said that I pause, whether you were affirming or saying that it seems to you.

But grant that I interpreted that `it seems` as though you seriously affirmed it. Was this not permitted because of the consequence deduced by you? For immediately afterward you held it as seriously and affirmatively established, saying: "Therefore there remains only the idea of God, in which it must be considered whether there is anything that could not have proceeded from myself."

You say that you later expressly showed that ideas of material things often come from bodies, and that through this the existence of bodies is proved. But this does not solve the difficulty so long as you keep yourself in that state in which nothing comes to you from bodies; in which, if anything remains that once came from bodies, you judge it false; in which you contend that you borrow knowledge of yourself and of God not from any knowledge of bodies or external things, but plainly from yourself, or from the things that are in you, with external things entirely excluded.

You say that here you only set out that there is not so much reality found in them that, from the principle that nothing is in the effect which has not preexisted formally or eminently in the cause, it must be concluded that they could not have proceeded from the mind alone. And you add that I in no way attacked this. But first, you make chimeras and then want us to attack them? You imagine yourself in the state of not knowing whether any human beings existed before you, or whether there is anything at all outside you; of knowing only yourself, that is, your mind and the nature of mind, since you are supposedly mind alone, entirely clearly and distinctly; of knowing clearly and distinctly that there is incorporeal substance; and although you have never seen a stone or any body at all, or what you have seen is false, nevertheless of thinking that a stone is a substance and agrees with mind in the notion of substance, and similar things. Unless these are not chimeras, and are worthy not only of refutation but not even of mention?

Second, this was your device: to say that I had not attacked it when you noticed that you could not respond to the difficulties I proposed to you in attacking it. I do not repeat them, but I only ask the patient reader to consider whether you should not have responded to the things I objected, beginning with the words, "For if you know only incorporeal substance, how can it happen," and so on, at the end of which I testified that I did not want to linger over them. For the principal difficulty about the intended conclusion remained to be urged immediately.

On that occasion, since I was hurrying, I had also testified just before that I did not want to press, although I surely could have at greater length, the things you had said about the ideas of God, angels, animals, and other things, such as extension, substance, and color. You had spoken about these as if you had never seen, heard, or known in any other way except falsely anything about the things represented by them; and meanwhile you divided ideas in such a way that you judged some could have proceeded from yourself, others be received from things existing outside you, and others proceed from nothing, namely false ideas. This is also the reason why I do not press now, being content to set out the reasoning on which you relied when you explained those things, and about which suspicion was already raised before.

If someone, placing himself in a state of ignorance through affected abstraction, is persuaded that it is easy for him to recognize innate ideas, or those which he would have in a state of ignorance through pure negation, then it is easy for him to discern ideas either had from himself, or received from a thing existing outside him, or even proceeding from nothing.

But I, placing myself in a state of ignorance through affected abstraction, am persuaded that it is easy for me to recognize innate ideas, or those which I would have in a state of ignorance through pure negation.

Therefore it is easy for me to discern ideas either had from myself, or received from a thing existing outside me, or even proceeding from nothing.

## Notes

- Gassendi refuses to turn obvious material existence into a proof problem. For him, the world contains enough genuinely obscure questions without making ordinary existence doubtful.
- The dispute over `idea` sharpens again. Descartes says idea means whatever is thought; Gassendi answers that every object of thought still appears under some mental image, and that this is the point Descartes is avoiding.
- `Imago in mente` becomes the pressure point between phantasy-image and intellectual object. If the image is always a corporeal species, then incorporeal substance cannot be displayed as Descartes needs.
- The self-action dispute is framed through motion: action and passion require distinct termini. Gassendi's denial is not merely an example but a small physics of action.
- Descartes' spinning top is answered by part-to-part succession. A whole does not act on itself; at most one part impels another in a circular order.
- Gassendi keeps attacking the staged Cartesian solitude as `affectata abstractio`: a state of ignorance generated by method, then used as if it could certify innate, adventitious, and fictive ideas.
