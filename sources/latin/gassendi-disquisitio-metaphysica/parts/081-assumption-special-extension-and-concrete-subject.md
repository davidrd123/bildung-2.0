## Opening Finding

Gassendi applies the criterion critique to Descartes' assumption. The question is not about thinking thing and extended thing in general, since God or an angel can be called thinking, while iron or stone can be called extended. The question is specifically about the human mind, human thinking, and the special extension that may belong to that same subject. Descartes therefore cannot win by saying universally that no thinking thing is extended and no extended thing thinks; that universal is denied precisely because the species is disputed. The general notion of body is only an abstraction from what is common to bodies, while complete notions of special bodies must include both the common features and the specific differences. If mind is the disputed subtle body, then conceiving thought and extension apart is incomplete in the same way as conceiving motion apart from figure in the same globe. Gassendi says Descartes has shifted from concrete same-subject cases to abstract or cross-subject examples.

## Working Translation

Now, since it seemed that more had to be said about the assumption, the matter seems plainly settled from what was disputed especially in the Second Meditation. It remains only to apply a few things. Therefore, since the question is about the thinking thing and the extended thing, namely whether they are two complete and different substances, or rather one substance with two different properties, I suppose the difficulty is not about a thinking thing or an extended thing universally. For God and an angel are thinking things, iron and stone are extended things; and yet the dispute here is not whether God and an angel can also be called extended things, nor whether iron and stone are thinking things.

The difficulty is therefore specifically about the human mind, and consequently about special thinking and special extension, which either belongs properly to it or is thought to belong properly to it. You yourself certainly defined the matter when, bringing forward the example of formal distinction, you judged that motion and figure ought to be taken not universally, as if the motion of a globe could be understood on one side and the figure of a cube on the other, but specially, and with respect to the same body, for example a globe, in which there is a special motion, say rolling, and a special figure, say roundness.

Therefore it is also well understood that thinking and extension should not be taken here universally, as if the thinking of mind could be understood on one side and the extension of the grosser body, or the body consisting of members, on the other. They should be taken specially, and with respect to the same substance, for example the mind, in which, as was said, there is special thinking, say human thinking, and there is, or is defended to be, special extension, say that of a subtler body.

Hence, although in your proposition you said that you were certain that everything you clearly and distinctly understand can be made by God just as you understand it, and although the matter should not be conceded as a principle of demonstration, as is understood from what has already been said, though it can be admitted in its own way, you then assume that you clearly and distinctly understand mind as a thinking thing, not extended, and body as an extended thing, not thinking. This very thing is what is denied to you, and what it is worth the effort to demonstrate, insofar as the question concerns special thinking and special extension, that is, as taken with respect to the same subtle body.

And you will indeed say that special thinking and special extension are contained under the general, and that when you understand universally that no thinking thing is extended and no extended thing is thinking, you exclude special extension from a thinking mind and special thinking from a subtle extended body. But since this is denied to you concerning the species, you see with how much right it is denied to you concerning the genus. For how will the claim that no thinking thing is extended be conceded to you by someone who defends that the thinking mind is extended? Or that no extended body is thinking by someone who will defend that a subtle extended body is thinking? There is certainly no way for you to prove that no extended thing is thinking except by induction through all bodies; but when, as you carry out this induction, he has granted the rest to you, and you want to count also that subtle body which he defends as mind, will he not flatly deny it and say that you are proving the same by the same?

How, then, will you make it credible that no extended thing is thinking? By saying that it is repugnant for body, or an extended thing, to elicit thought? But it is the same issue, and this very thing has been objected from the beginning as what has to be proved, namely that it is repugnant to corporeal or extended nature that it think. Nor was it in your power to prove it. Will you say that it remains proved from the fact that you clearly and distinctly understand thinking without thinking extension, and conversely extension without thinking thought? But in the same way you say that you understand very well, or clearly and distinctly, motion without figure and figure without motion, although when motion and figure are taken with respect to the same body, you cannot completely, that is clearly and distinctly, understand what is movable without figure or what is figured without motion.

"This is exactly what I wanted to say," you say. For after giving the example and applying it to the justice and mercy of God, you added these words: "And indeed I completely understand what body is, by thinking only that it is extended, figured, movable, and so on; and conversely I understand mind to be a complete thinking thing, which doubts, understands, wills, and so on, although I deny that there is in it anything of those things contained in the idea of body; which absolutely could not happen unless there were a real distinction between mind and body."

Yet I ask you, of what body are you speaking when you say that you understand it completely? If you understand something other than this subtle body, about which the dispute is whether it is at once extended and thinking, you are beside the question. For although it is granted that the general notion of body is to be extended, figured, movable, but not thinking, nevertheless that notion is only an abstraction containing what is common to all bodies. Yet this does not prevent there from being complete notions of special bodies, which must include both that common element and the special differences.

Thus body universally is understood only as extended, figured, movable, and so on, but not also as animate or sentient. But this does not prevent the fact that, in order to understand that special body called animal, we must further understand it as animate and sentient. In the same way, I will say that you can indeed understand body universally as only extended, figured, and so on, not also as thinking; but this does not prevent the fact that when the issue concerns the subtle body proper to mind, that body too ought to be understood as thinking.

Hence, if you are speaking of this very body, since the controversy is only about it, you see how it is denied that it is completely understood when you understand it only as extended, figured, movable, and so on. Since to understand something completely or adequately is to understand whatever is in the thing understood, you are said by that very fact to understand such a body incompletely or inadequately, insofar as you do not understand the thinking that is in it. The same has to be said proportionally about mind, which you say you understand as a complete thing, since the fact that you do not understand everything that is in it proves your understanding to be inadequate and incomplete, given that beyond the fact that it doubts, understands, wills, and so on, it can be thought to be extended.

And so, to press your example at the same time: although motion, namely corporeal motion, cannot exist without figure, and nevertheless figure can exist without motion, still insofar as motion and figure are taken in the same body while it is actually being moved, as in a globe while it is rolling, they are plainly inseparable. That is, there cannot be such a rolling without such a roundness, nor such a roundness without such a rolling, because when they are understood separately, they are understood incompletely; and when they are understood completely, that is with the globe itself, then roundness is not understood without the body in which rolling occurs, nor rolling without the same body in which roundness occurs.

In the same way, neither divine justice nor divine mercy can exist one without the other, because if they were conceived separately, they would be conceived incompletely; and when they are conceived completely, they are conceived with one and the same God. Similarly, neither can those two properties of the triangle be understood except with the same triangle or triangular body, because when they are understood separately, they are understood incompletely, and when they are understood completely, they are understood only with that same triangle or triangular body.

In the same way now, although thinking, understand human thinking, cannot exist without extension, and nevertheless extension can exist without thinking, still insofar as they are taken in the same actually existing mind, they are plainly inseparable. That is, there cannot be such thinking without such extension, nor such extension without such thinking, because when they are understood separately, they are understood incompletely; and when they are understood completely, that is with the mind itself, then thinking is not understood without the substance in which extension is found, nor extension without the same substance in which thinking is found.

Do you see here the fitting and legitimate application? Do you apply it otherwise? Evidently you argue from abstracts to concretes. You proceed just as if you were taking motion and figure not with respect to the same globe, but with respect to a globe and an arrow at the same time, in one of which you understood roundness and rolling, and in the other length and straight projection, and then inferred that globe and arrow are really distinct. That, to be sure, is to depart from the supposition of motion and figure taken in the same body.

## Notes

- Gassendi narrows the issue from thinking/extension in general to human thinking and the alleged special extension of the same mind.
- Descartes cannot apply a universal rule unless he first wins the disputed species: whether the subtle body counted as mind can think.
- The appeal to induction over all bodies fails at the disputed case because the opponent denies that the subtle body can be counted as non-thinking.
- The general notion of body is an abstraction containing what is common to all bodies; complete notions of special bodies must include special differences.
- The animal example makes the logic concrete: body in general need not include sentience, but the complete notion of animal body does.
- Gassendi retools Descartes' motion/figure example: in a single rolling globe, motion and figure are inseparable when understood completely with the subject.
- The same-subject rule is then applied to human thinking and extension in the actually existing mind.
- Descartes' error is to argue from abstracted predicates or cross-subject examples to concrete real distinction.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `specialis` and `speciatim` are kept close to "special" and "specifically" because the passage turns on species-level dispute against a merely universal or generic notion.

Textual: the phrase `quae debeant etiam tam illud, quam specialia discrimina includere` was checked against the page image but should be rechecked before close citation; the sense is that complete notions of special bodies include both the common element and the specific differences.
