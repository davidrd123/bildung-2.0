## Opening Finding

Gassendi shifts from imagination to sense by refusing the claim that the senses always deceive. He does not deny that deception occurs, but relocates falsity away from passive sense and into judgment or mind. Sense reports appearances as they must appear from their causes; error enters when judgment fails to notice distance, conditions, or other causes that make things appear confusedly, smaller, or otherwise than they are nearby. The question is therefore not whether some deception exists, but whether deception is so universal that no sensory truth can ever be established. The tower, phantom limb, dreaming, fallacies, immersed stick, and geometrical demonstration examples all press the same distinction: appearances as appearances remain indubitable, even when external-object judgments need correction. Descartes replies that this relies on prejudice if it assumes no falsity where none has yet been detected, and that Gassendi retreats to the Second Meditation path when he says only that things appear as they appear. The live issue, Descartes insists, was the truth of things placed outside us.

## Working Translation

DUBITATIO II.
On sense as not always deceptive.

You next discuss sense, and first you very well set out an enumeration of the things that had become known through the senses and had been believed true by you, with nature alone as judge and guide. You then report experiments that so weakened the trust placed in the senses that they drove you to the point where we saw you received around the First Meditation.

At this point, however, I do not intend to bring a lawsuit over the truth of the senses. For although deception or falsity is not in sense, which behaves merely passively and reports only the things that appear, and that necessarily appear as they do from their causes, but rather in judgment or in the mind, which does not act circumspectly enough and does not notice that things far away, from these or other causes, appear more confused and smaller than they are when near, and so on for the rest: nevertheless, wherever deception is, it cannot be denied that there is some deception. The only difficulty is whether it is always this way, so that the truth of some thing perceived by sense can never be established.

There is no need to gather obvious examples. I say only, in answer to the things you bring forward, or rather object, that it seems altogether established that when we both look at and touch a tower nearby, we are certain that it is square, though when farther away we had occasion to judge it round, or at least to doubt whether it was square, round, or of another figure.

So too that sensation of pain which still appears to be in the foot or hand after those members have been cut off can sometimes deceive, namely in those whose members have been cut off, and this because the sensory spirits are accustomed to be carried into them and to express sensation in them. Nevertheless those who are intact are so certain that they feel pain in the foot or hand in which they seem to be pricked that they cannot doubt it.

So too, since we are awake and asleep by turns as long as we live, there is indeed deception through sleep, because things that are not present seem to appear present. Nevertheless we do not always sleep, nor, when we are really awake, can we doubt whether we are awake or rather asleep.

So too, although we can think that we are exposed to nature's deceptions, even in things that have seemed most true, nevertheless we also think that we are capable of truth by nature. And just as we are sometimes deceived, as by an undetected sophism or by a stick half-immersed in water, so sometimes we understand truth, as in a geometrical demonstration or in a stick drawn out of the water, so that we plainly cannot doubt the truth of either.

And even if it is permitted to doubt the rest, at least it is not permitted to doubt this: that things appear such as they appear. Nor can it fail to be most true that they appear such. And the fact that reason dissuades us from many things toward which nature impels us does not at least remove the truth of what appears, to phainomenon. Yet there is no need to inquire here whether reason resists the impulse of sense only in the way the right hand supports the left when it is slipping from weariness, or in some other way.

RESPONSE.

In this way you manifestly show that you rely only on prejudices and never put them off, since you want us to suspect no falsity in those things in which we have never detected falsity. And therefore, when we look at and touch a tower nearby, you want us to be certain that it is square if it appears square; and when we are really awake, you want us unable to doubt whether we are awake or asleep, and the like.

For you have no reason to think that all the things in which there can be error have already long ago been noticed by you. And it could easily be proved that you are sometimes deceived in the very things you thus admit to be certain.

But when you return to the point that at least it is not permitted to doubt that things appear such as they appear, you return to the path, and I asserted this very thing in the Second Meditation. But here the question was about the truth of things placed outside us, about which you have brought forward nothing true.

## Notes

- Gassendi distinguishes passive sense from judgment or mind.
- Sense reports appearances as they appear from their causes; falsity enters through insufficiently circumspect judgment.
- The issue is not whether deception happens, but whether deception is so universal that sensory truth can never be established.
- Near inspection and touch can correct distant tower appearances.
- Phantom-limb pain can deceive amputees, but does not make intact bodily pain doubtful.
- Dreaming deceives, but not all waking is dreamlike; when truly awake, Gassendi denies that we can doubt waking.
- Nature can expose us to fallacy while still making us capable of truth.
- Appearances as appearances remain indubitable: things appear as they appear.
- Descartes accepts that appearance-as-appearance returns to the Second Meditation path, but says the disputed issue is external-object truth.
