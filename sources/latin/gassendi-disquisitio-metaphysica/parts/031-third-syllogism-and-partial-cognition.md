## Opening Finding

Gassendi reconstructs Descartes' third move as the argument from having the idea of God to God's existence. The important pressure point is Descartes' concession that divine perfections are not comprehended but only touched by thought. Gassendi turns that into a limit on the idea: if each known perfection is known only partially, and if only a finite number of perfections is known, then the mind has not truly grasped an idea of infinite being. The claim that God is subject to no defect may be true, but it has not been demonstrated from such partial cognition.

## Working Translation

The third is:

If I recognize that it cannot happen that I exist as a nature such as I am, namely having the idea of God in me, unless God also truly exists, God, I say, that same one whose idea is in me, that is, having all those perfections (which I cannot comprehend, but can touch by thought in some manner or other) and subject to no defects at all, it follows that God exists.

But I recognize that it cannot happen that I exist as a nature such as I am, namely having the idea of God, and so on.

Therefore God exists.

What strength this demonstration too has is sufficiently clear from what has already been said. Here I note only two things. First, when you say that you do not comprehend, but only somehow touch by thought, the perfections which are in God, it becomes manifest that from each one of those perfections which you know, you know only something, not the whole; and therefore you do not know it as infinite. And since the number of those perfections which you know is finite (for you will not enumerate new and new ones for all eternity), you know only something not from all, but only from some; and therefore you know nothing infinite in God, and consequently do not truly have the idea of infinite being.

Second, when you add, "and subject to no defects at all," that indeed is true. Yet it cannot be assumed as if demonstrated by you, since even the perfections which you have known about God, and which you thought you had demonstrated from your idea, you yourself have not comprehended, but only somehow touched, so that you do not know how far they extend. And since you have known not all, but only some, you do not know how far more and more perfections are present in God. Thus you cannot show that the divine perfections are of such a kind that they exclude every imperfection, and that God is therefore subject to no defects at all.

## Notes

- Gassendi treats the third syllogism as already weakened by the earlier critiques of the idea of the infinite and causal proof.
- `Attingere cogitatione` becomes a methodological concession: to touch by thought is not to possess a whole or infinite comprehension.
- The argument is quantitative and qualitative at once. Descartes knows only part of each known perfection, and only a finite subset of divine perfections.
- Gassendi does not deny that God lacks defects; he denies that Descartes has demonstrated defectlessness from the finite, partial idea available to the meditator.
- The move renews the Part 015 pressure on `totum infinitum`: the whole infinite cannot be represented by an idea whose content is partial and finitely enumerated.
