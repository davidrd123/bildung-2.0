## Opening Finding

Gassendi turns from the ontological proof to Descartes' claim that all scientific certainty and truth depend on knowing the true God and God's non-deception. Descartes' recalled triangle demonstration becomes the test: while attending to the proof, assent is forced; after turning away, he says doubt can return unless God is known. Gassendi finds it hard to believe that Descartes was less certain of geometrical demonstrations before the proof of God than afterward. Geometry appears more evident than the theological premises because many dispute God's existence, creation, and divine attributes, while no one denies the demonstrations. He presses atheist and pagan mathematicians as counterexamples and asks whether ordinary believers ground the Pythagorean theorem in divine veracity rather than in demonstration. Descartes answers by opposing the skeptics, who did doubt geometrical demonstrations and would not have done so if they had known God as they should; priority and certainty are measured not by majority opinion, but by what appears prior, more evident, and more certain to those who know both things properly.

## Working Translation

DUBITATIO III.

On the certainty and truth of every cognition, insofar as it is asserted to depend on the clear and evident knowledge that God exists and that he is truthful.

Finally you declare that the certainty and truth of all science depend so much on the one cognition of the true God that, without this, no certainty or true science can be had.

For, you say, "When I consider the nature of the Triangle, it appears most evidently to me, since I am imbued with the principles of Geometry, that its three angles are equal to two right angles. Nor can I fail to believe this to be true as long as I attend to its demonstration. But as soon as I turn the point of my mind away from it, however much I still remember that I perceived it most clearly, it can easily happen that I doubt whether it is true, if I am ignorant of God. For I could persuade myself that I have been made by nature such that I sometimes err in those things which I think I perceive most evidently, especially since I remember having held many things as true and certain which afterwards, led by other reasons, I judged to be false. But after I perceived that God exists, because at the same time I understood that all other things depend on him and that he is not deceitful, and from this gathered that all those things which I clearly and distinctly perceive are necessarily true, then even if I no longer attend to the reasons on account of which I judged this thing to be true, provided that I remember having perceived it clearly and distinctly, no contrary reason can be brought forward which would drive me to doubt; rather I have true and certain science of this. Nor only of this, but also of all the remaining things which I remember having once demonstrated, such as geometrical things and the like."

To these things, excellent sir, since I grant that you speak seriously, nothing else can be said except that you seem likely to have difficulty getting anyone to believe that, before the time when you reasoned the preceding things about God, you were less certain of those geometrical demonstrations than you were afterward.

For those demonstrations seem to have such evidence and certainty that by themselves they force assent, and once perceived they no longer allow the intellect to waver. Thus you would snare even that evil Genius just as easily as when, although God was not yet known, you so confidently asserted that you could not be deceived about that proposition or inference: I think; therefore I exist.

Moreover, although it is true, as nothing is truer, that God exists, that he is the author of all things, and that he is not deceitful, nevertheless these seem less evident than those geometrical demonstrations, even from this argument: many dispute God's existence, the creation of things, and other things about God, while no one will deny these demonstrations. Who, then, is there whom you will persuade that the latter borrow their evidence and certainty from the former?

And who will grasp that Diagoras, Theodorus, or any similar atheists cannot be made altogether certain of demonstrations of this kind?

And how many believers are there who, if asked why they are certain that in a right triangle the square of the base is equal to the squares of the legs, would answer, "Because I know that God exists, that God cannot deceive, and that he is the author of this thing as of all other things," rather than answer, "Because I know it and am persuaded by an indubitable demonstration"?

How much more would Pythagoras, Plato, Archimedes, Euclid, and the other mathematicians answer this way, none of whom seems to think of God in order to become most certain of demonstrations.

Still, because perhaps you will vouch not for others but only for yourself, and because this is pious from another direction, there is certainly no reason for me to contend.

RESPONSE.

Against the things you have here about Diagoras, Theodorus, Pythagoras, and others, I oppose the Skeptics, who doubted even geometrical demonstrations themselves, and I affirm that they would not have done this if they had known God as they should.

Nor is it rightly proved that one thing is better known than another from the fact that it seems true to more people, but only from the fact that, to those who know both as they should, it appears prior in cognition, more evident, and more certain.

## Notes

- The dispute turns the earlier clear-and-distinct circle into a memory and science problem.
- Descartes distinguishes attending to a demonstration from remembering that one clearly and distinctly perceived it.
- Gassendi treats geometrical demonstration as self-compelling and compares it to the `ego cogito; itaque exsisto` certainty before God is known.
- The theological premises seem less evident than geometry because God's existence, creation, and attributes are disputed, while geometrical demonstrations are not.
- Diagoras and Theodorus stand in for atheists who can still be certain of mathematics.
- Pythagoras, Plato, Archimedes, and Euclid stand in for mathematicians who do not ground demonstration in explicit knowledge of God.
- Descartes responds by invoking the skeptics and by rejecting majority acceptance as the measure of what is better known.
- The next `Instantia` should press whether skeptics' doubt affects apparent, life-useful, or mathematical things in the way Descartes needs.
