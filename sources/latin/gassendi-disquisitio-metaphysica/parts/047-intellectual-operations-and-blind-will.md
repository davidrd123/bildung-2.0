## Opening Finding

Gassendi now says he conceded too much when he let Descartes' will/intellect vocabulary pass. To avoid "error in words" while discussing error, he restores the common distinction: the mind has a cognitive faculty, intellect, and an appetitive faculty, will. Everything cognitive belongs to intellect, including simple apprehension, judgment/enunciation, and reasoning; everything appetitive belongs to will, including willing, not-willing, pursuit, avoidance, command, prohibition, love, hatred, and the passions. This lets him reroute judgment and reasoning back under intellect. The examples then show the order of operations: intellect first apprehends a body, movement, sweetness, poison, or relief from misery; then it connects apprehensions in judgment and syllogism; only afterward does will pursue, avoid, command attention, or abstain. The core maxim is that will is a blind power unless intellect carries the torch.

## Working Translation

I had not badly suspected that I was granting too much when, at the beginning of the doubt, so as not to get stuck on words, I said that I did not wish to dispute why, contrary to all authors and to the common use of words, you not only take intellect for the faculty that simply apprehends, but also confuse will with the judging faculty. For from this there remained an occasion for you to play by equivocation, your equivocation of course. Those who saw it complained that I had been too easy and had wished too much to please when, speaking with you and using your phrase, I said that will itself judges.

Therefore, so that while dealing with error we at least avoid error in words, I follow that very familiar distinction by which people commonly distinguish, in the mind or human soul, besides the moving faculty, those two celebrated faculties, the cognitive and the appetitive, which they call by their proper names intellect and will. Accordingly it is clear that whatever belongs to cognition in the mind is suitably referred to intellect, and whatever belongs to appetite to will. Thus not only simple apprehension belongs to intellect, by which we conceive something without affirmation or negation, but also judgment or enunciation, by which we affirm or deny, and reasoning itself, or the judgment by which, from one thing posited, we infer another. To will, however, belongs choice, or willing and not-willing, pursuit and avoidance, and likewise love, hatred, and the other passions which are of the moral kind, just as the former are of the intellectual kind.

The issue here is not whether there is a real, modal, or other distinction, as they call it, between intellect and will. It is enough that, since people have devised names to avoid confusion, by which various concepts concerning the same thing are expressed according to its various observed operations, we should imitate this all the more here, since great confusion would arise if we did not. Therefore, since you attribute to intellect only that first operation, that is, simple apprehension, and assign the remaining two, that is, judgment, both enunciative and illative as they call it, to will, it will be entirely preferable to leave not only the first but also the second and third to intellect, while granting and reserving its own operations to will. You yourself also seem to have done this more suitably when, in the Third Meditation, you openly said that no falsity is to be feared in will or affects, and therefore only judgments remain, in which you must take care not to be deceived.

However you have taken this, or wish to take it hereafter, see what progression people commonly establish as genuine among the operations. Someone sees, for example, some body from a mile away. The intellect indeed apprehends it, but pronounces nothing about it, namely whether it is inanimate or animate. He looks more attentively and observes that it changes place or moves forward, and consequently apprehends motion or progression. Thus two simple apprehensions are carried out, one of the body and another of progression. Immediately judgment follows, or the second operation, by which the intellect connects those two first and simple apprehensions and so judges or pronounces: that body is moving forward.

But it does not stop there. Because from notions already held it has formed others within itself, namely that judgment, or that other second operation, "what moves forward is an animal," it repeats that one, adds the recent one, and connects them so that from both it elicits the third in this way: what moves forward is an animal; that body moves forward; therefore that body is an animal. This is reasoning, or the third operation of the intellect, which is itself also judgment, but dependent on another and more composite.

But if, to someone looking more attentively and for many days, that body seems motionless, then, once the body is apprehended, he will also apprehend immobility or remaining in the same place. From these two first operations he will form this second one: that body is immobile. Then, repeating another already held proposition, he will reason in this way: what is immobile, or what remains unmoved for so long, is not an animal; that body is immobile; therefore it is not an animal. This is now the third operation. The only difference is that in the first way judgment is made by affirming, while in the second by denying. Up to this point there is no commerce with will.

I take another example. The intellect apprehends an apple; it apprehends sweetness; then, connecting both, it pronounces: the apple is sweet. And because it has judged from elsewhere that what is sweet is to be sought, it reasons: the sweet is to be sought; the apple is sweet; therefore it is to be sought. Or, when it has looked attentively and observed poison, it will apprehend the apple and apprehend poison, and joining both will pronounce: the apple is poisoned. Then, taking up the prejudged proposition, it will reason in this way: what is poisoned is to be avoided; this apple is poisoned; therefore it is to be avoided.

Again, up to this point, will has nothing in common with either judgment of either kind. Indeed, there will be nothing in common if we take the matter in a third way: if, after the apple has been apprehended, sweetness is apprehended in it on one side and poison on the other, and then intellect pronounces: this apple is at once sweet and poisoned, or sweet indeed, but poisoned. Then, taking up the prejudged proposition, it reasons: what is sweet indeed but nevertheless poisoned is to be rejected; this apple is sweet indeed but nevertheless poisoned; therefore it is to be rejected.

What is more, it could also be taken in a fourth way, with will contributing nothing: once the apple and the apple's poison have been apprehended, liberation from intolerable miseries is apprehended, and the intellect pronounces: this poisoned apple can liberate from intolerable miseries. Then from a prior judgment it reasons: what can liberate from intolerable miseries, even though it is poisoned, is to be sought; this apple can do that; therefore it is to be sought.

Therefore will itself, or the action of will, is posterior to all these: seeking, aversion, or willing and not-willing; and accordingly choice, neglect, command, prohibition, pursuit, abstinence, and everything that follows, according as intellect has judged or rendered judgment, not only by pure enunciation but also by reasoning. For will does not in reality seek the apple unless intellect has judged it sweet; nor does it refuse or reject it unless intellect has judged it poisoned and to be rejected. Nor does it prefer to abstain from the poison despite the lure of sweetness unless intellect has judged the apple to be at once sweet and poisoned, and judged it better to abstain from the sweet because of the poison than to pursue the poison because of the sweet. Nor does it choose the poison rather than neglect it unless intellect has first judged that liberation from miseries is joined with this poison, and that the lesser evil is to be sought, or has the character of a good, because it frees from a greater evil.

In one word, that common opinion is altogether excellent which maintains that will is a blind power, or one which tends toward nothing and flees nothing unless intellect carries the torch before it. Nor is it an obstacle that we sometimes will the operations of intellect, or that intellect judges because we want it to judge. For that volition depends on another preceding judgment, and the judgment that follows is not such because we will it, but because the simple apprehensions are such.

To resume the earlier example: after someone has seen that body a thousand paces away, intellect at first sight apprehends nothing but body. But since it already knows that every body is either animate or inanimate, and since it lacks a mark or character by which to discern which this body is, it suspends judgment on the matter. Meanwhile, because from elsewhere it apprehends cognition that is prepared by attention, it therefore pronounces or judges: this body can fall into cognition, or become known to one who attends. Then reasoning or judgment follows from another prejudged proposition in this way: that attention by which unknown things are known is to be sought, or is useful and good; but from attention to the motion or rest of this body, what is unknown about it can be known, namely whether it is animate or inanimate; therefore such attention is to be sought, or, if you prefer, is useful and good.

Once this judgment has been posited, will is present, and in reality seeks or wills the useful and good thing shown to it. It commands attention and the judgment to be made according as the thing has been observed by attending. I add this because will itself does not prescribe specifically and distinctly what sort this future judgment will be. That is unknown; nor is it sought except under a general description and confusedly. Rather the judgment will be such as the character of motion or immobility will show to the one attending. I do not add how much attention is needed so that the character be proper and stripped of fallacies, so that a true judgment may be rendered. For it is enough for the present purpose that the judgment will be such as the character perceived by simple apprehension has been.

For example, although the character for rendering this judgment, "the body is moved," is change of place, nevertheless because change of place can appear in the same way whether the body itself is really moved or the eye of the one seeing is moved, the eye must be at rest if the judgment is to be true. But if the eye is moved while the body is at rest, since intellect ought to foresee this, and if it does not the judgment will be false, nevertheless the judgment will be such as necessarily follows from that alone which appears and is apprehended. And so for the rest.

## Notes

- Gassendi withdraws his earlier concession to Descartes' vocabulary: calling will a judging faculty creates equivocation.
- The common distinction assigns cognition to intellect and appetite to will.
- Simple apprehension, enunciative judgment, and illative judgment or reasoning all belong to intellect.
- Will receives willing, not-willing, pursuit, avoidance, command, prohibition, love, hatred, and the passions.
- The body-at-a-distance example shows the three intellectual operations: apprehension, judgment, and reasoning.
- The apple examples multiply Descartes' own poisoned-apple case but keep all judgment and inference on the side of intellect.
- Will is posterior to judgment: it seeks, avoids, commands, or abstains only after intellect has judged something sweet, poisoned, useful, or liberating.
- The `blind power` formula is the part's center: will tends toward or flees nothing unless intellect carries the torch.
- When we will attention itself, that volition still depends on a prior intellectual judgment that attention is useful for knowing.
