## Opening Finding

Gassendi turns the prior critique into a parody proof. If Descartes can argue from mind precisely conceived as substance or thinking thing to mind's incorporeality, an opponent can mirror the same form and argue from mind precisely conceived as substance or extended thing to the denial of incorporeality. Both proofs depend on the same move from what is intellectually separated to what is really separated. Gassendi says the opponent's version is, if anything, better, because it refuses to be led away by the fallacious principle that intellectual separation induces real separation.

## Working Translation

From these things it is now possible to understand how exquisitely you have disputed and demonstrated in the Sixth Meditation whether mind is in truth different from every body. For that this follows from the fact that you made credible in the Second Meditation that mind can be understood as an existing substance, even if you understood nothing to exist which was a thin or subtle body, is clear from what has now been said to be far from true.

Hence I do not repeat that, once it has been conceded to you that mind can be understood precisely as an existing substance, and also precisely as a substance or thinking thing, it is nevertheless not conceded for that reason that mind is nothing other than what is precisely understood. Rather, I repeat only that the consequence is universally faulty if anyone argues from the ideal state to the real one, and from what is cut off in thought to what is connected in the thing itself.

From this, namely, it is gathered that from that principle of yours you can no more dispute and demonstrate that mind is incorporeal or unextended, and therefore can exist without body, than someone else will dispute and demonstrate that it is corporeal or extended, and therefore cannot exist without body. Let us test this briefly.

You will say: "I understand mind as substance without understanding that it is corporeal; for I understand it precisely." He will say: "I understand mind as substance without understanding that it is incorporeal; for I understand it precisely." You go on: "Therefore mind is not corporeal." He goes on: "Therefore mind is not incorporeal." And then, since each of you will deny the other's consequence, you will prove yours: "Things of which I clearly and distinctly understand one without the other are different in reality." He will prove his by the same words: "Things of which I understand one without the other," and so on.

You assume: "But I clearly and distinctly understand mind, namely as substance, without the corporeal degree." He will assume: "But I clearly and distinctly understand mind, namely as substance, without the incorporeal degree." You will infer: "Therefore mind and the corporeal degree are different in reality, or, what is the same, mind is not corporeal." He will infer: "Therefore mind and the incorporeal degree are different in reality, or, what is the same, mind is not incorporeal." You will laugh at his demonstration; he too will laugh at yours; and because of an equal paralogism, you will be altogether equal.

What is more, if you say, "I understand mind precisely as a thinking thing," and he says, "I understand mind precisely as an extended thing," and you add, "Therefore nothing else belongs to the nature of mind except only this, that it is a thinking thing," will he not add with better reason, "But for that reason it does not follow that nothing else belongs to the nature of mind except this, that it is an extended thing"?

And when you infer, "Therefore this does not belong to it, that it is also an extended thing," will he not infer with better reason, "Therefore this can belong to it, that it is also a thinking thing"? And when you sub-infer, "Therefore I have a clear and distinct idea of myself insofar as I am only a thinking thing, not extended," will he not say with better reason, "Therefore I have a clear and distinct idea of myself, that I am at once an extended and thinking thing"?

And when you subsume, "But I have from elsewhere a clear and distinct idea of the body closely joined to me insofar as it is only an extended thing, not thinking," will he not say with better reason, "But I have from elsewhere a clear and distinct idea of the body which I myself am, insofar as it is an extended thing and also thinking"?

And when you conclude, "Therefore it is certain that I am really distinct from my body and can exist without it," will he not say with better reason, "Therefore it is certain that I am not really distinct from the body which I myself am, nor can I exist without it"? Will he not, I say, do so with better reason, insofar as, since each of you has supposed that you understand precisely, he will not let himself be led away, as you do, by that fallacious principle, when from intellectual separation you bring in real separation. But this is too much.

## Notes

- Gassendi compresses the prior argument into a direct attack on the Sixth Meditation demonstration.
- The basic fault is arguing from the ideal state to the real state, or from what thought separates to what is really connected.
- The parody proof mirrors Descartes' form: mind can be understood as substance without the corporeal degree, so mind is not corporeal; mind can be understood as substance without the incorporeal degree, so mind is not incorporeal.
- Both demonstrations become laughable because each rests on the same invalid consequence.
- The sharper version contrasts mind as thinking thing with mind as extended thing, then asks why precision under one description should exclude what is not currently considered.
- Gassendi's opponent is said to reason better because he does not accept the fallacious move from intellectual separation to real separation.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `gradus corporeus` and `gradus incorporeus` are translated literally as "corporeal degree" and "incorporeal degree." The phrase seems to mark the corporeal/incorporeal determination abstracted from mind as substance; keep it visible rather than smoothing it too quickly.

Structural: `status idealis` and `status realis` continue the earlier ideal-state/real-state vocabulary from the ontological-proof section, now redeployed against the mind-body proof.
