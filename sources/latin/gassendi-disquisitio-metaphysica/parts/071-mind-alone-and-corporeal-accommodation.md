## Opening Finding

Gassendi turns Descartes' claim that the mind "uses itself alone" into an unproved premise. Descartes says that in intellection the mind turns toward itself or an idea within itself, while in imagination it turns toward body or a corporeal idea; therefore imagination is not required for the essence of mind. Gassendi reconstructs this as a syllogism and says the critical premise has not been proved: this is precisely the place where Descartes must show that the mind can turn to itself, or to a non-corporeal idea, with no imagination intervening. The passage then gives Gassendi's most careful qualification of his image theory. He does not say that God, angel, and mind are corporeal things. He says that, because divine and immaterial natures exceed his capacity, he can think them only under some corporeal form accommodated to human weakness, while judging the reality itself to be ineffably purer, more beautiful, and more perfect than the idea that represents it. The result is not crude reduction of the divine to body, but an admitted gap between the object and the corporeal or quasi-corporeal form under which finite intellect must think it.

## Working Translation

II.

You say that in intellection the mind uses itself alone, while in imagination it contemplates a corporeal form.

And because from the objections I had made it followed that the mind therefore cannot use intellection alone, which is to use itself alone, in knowing corporeal things such as the triangle, pentagon, and the other figures, since there can be no ideas of those things unless they are corporeal, and the mind cannot attend to them except as corporeal forms, therefore you add: "And although geometrical figures are altogether corporeal, nevertheless the ideas by which they are understood, when they do not fall under imagination, should not on that account be thought corporeal."

But when you say this, how do you prove it? Certainly not at all, although this is the point where, as they say, "Here is Rhodes, here is the leap." It is enough that you reasoned as follows:

If, when the mind understands, it turns itself toward itself or toward one of the ideas that are in it, and, when it imagines, it turns itself toward body or a corporeal idea, and if therefore it can understand something without imagination, it follows that the power of imagining is not required for the essence of mind.

But when the mind understands, it turns itself toward itself or toward one of the ideas that are in it; and when it imagines, it turns itself toward body or a corporeal idea; and therefore it can understand something without imagination.

Therefore the power of imagining is not required for the essence of mind.

But by what argument have you proved that the mind can either turn itself into itself in that way, or attend to some idea that is not corporeal or quasi-corporeal, so that no imagination intervenes? How have you proved that intellection and imagination are not therefore one action, to which this double name is assigned? Or that intellection is not clear and distinct when imagination is clear and distinct, and confused and obscure when otherwise? Or that even in those things which we perceive universally, abstractly, and in some way by consequence, or, as you would say, understand, some image or corporeal form, and thus, by your account, imagination, does not intervene?

But this matter has been pressed more than enough above, both concerning corporeal things, as in the case of the twofold image of the sun, and concerning incorporeal things, as in the case of the substance of mind and the nature of God.

Since, however, you show yourself especially gracious here in these words, "And finally, O flesh, it is worthy of you alone to judge that the ideas of God, angel, and the human mind are corporeal or quasi-corporeal," and so on, you certainly do something worthy of your mind. You consider it to have such lofty flight and such sharp and steady sight that, like an eagle, it does not blink but bears the splendor of divine things with full gaze: so that it contemplates God, not perceiving him under some corporeal form and as if in a riddle, but through a plainly incorporeal species, and as he is according to you; and in the same way the angel, and in the same way the human mind.

As for me, I frankly confess that I do nothing but crawl, and that I am far from comparing my mind even with the very eyes of an owl turned toward the midday sun. That is, when I try to understand divine things, a certain deepest and insuperable darkness takes hold of me; and I am not able to understand God, angel, or mind except under some corporeal form accommodated to my weakness.

Not, certainly, because I therefore think that God is this form, the angel that one, and mind another. Rather, since I have fixed it for myself that things of this kind, which I have received either by hearing and faith or gathered by reasoning to exist, have a nature of which I am wholly incapable of perceiving, I judge that this nature is not at all such as what is presented to my intellect whenever I hear it named, think of it within myself, or want to express it to others.

But while I do not say that it is such as I think it, or such as the idea presented to my intellect represents it, since it is ineffably and inconceivably purer, more beautiful, and more perfect, nevertheless I am always of such weakness that I cannot think it under any other form than the one exhibited to me through an idea of this kind.

And perhaps you too experience nothing other than I do, since perhaps I too understand no less than you that the divine nature is eternal, infinite, omnipotent, omniscient, and so on, and the other natures in the same proportion. But you yourself do not equally want to confess that nothing presents itself to your mind except some corporeal or quasi-corporeal something when you think God, or the nature to which it belongs to be eternal, infinite, omnipotent, omniscient, and the other things that are attributed to him.

No doubt you would think your reputation finished if someone believed you to be the sort of person who tried to imagine a thing not imaginable and fashioned no idea except a corporeal one, to which you falsely seemed to assign the name of God or mind. However you stand, enjoy your good fortune.

## Notes

- Gassendi reconstructs Descartes' reply as a syllogism and attacks the unproved premise.
- The crucial missing proof is that mind can turn to itself, or to a non-corporeal idea, without imagination intervening.
- Gassendi again suggests that intellection and imagination may be one action under two names.
- Clear intellection may depend on clear imagination; obscure imagination may yield obscure intellection.
- Even universal, abstract, consequence-based cognition may still involve an image or corporeal form.
- The "O flesh" exchange is reframed: Gassendi does not identify God, angel, or mind with corporeal forms.
- Corporeal or quasi-corporeal forms are accommodations to finite weakness, not adequate representations of divine or immaterial natures.
- Gassendi preserves excess: the reality thought is purer, more beautiful, and more perfect than the idea under which it is represented.
- The final jab suggests Descartes may share the same dependence on quasi-corporeal presentation but refuses to confess it.

## Translation Risk

Lexical/contextual: `species` is rendered as "species" in Descartes' imagined incorporeal access to God. It could also be "form" or "appearance," but leaving the Latin loanword visible keeps pressure on the species/image vocabulary.
