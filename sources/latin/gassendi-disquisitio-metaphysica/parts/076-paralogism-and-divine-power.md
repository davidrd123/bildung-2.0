## Opening Finding

Gassendi begins the rejoinder by reversing Descartes' accusation. Descartes says Gassendi confuses what can be understood with what really is; Gassendi answers that this confusion is exactly the paralogism he has been exposing all along. The disputed move is from separable concepts to real separation: from thinking thing understood without extension and extended thing understood without thought, Descartes infers mind really unextended. Gassendi repeats the triangle-property counterexample and says Descartes hides the difficulty by treating earlier claims as already demonstrated. The deeper target is the divine-power principle: Descartes assumes that God can make whatever he clearly and distinctly perceives. Gassendi denies that this principle has been demonstrated and says that if its rule is Descartes' own private perception, then it may persuade Descartes but is not a public demonstration for others.

## Working Translation

INSTANTIA.

We must begin here from the final words, in which you say that I testify that I have understood none of these things, by confusing the question of what can be understood with the question of what really is. For truly your confidence is worthy of wonder, since you display the paralogism that you wish had been hidden, and say that I confuse the things I have constantly complained that you confused.

For it has been objected very persistently that this is indeed a paralogism: that the things understood by separate concepts are therefore separate in reality. And nothing has been repeated more often than that you have confused, or treated as the same, those things which can be understood separately with those which are really separate.

For what is the question? Is it not whether mind is at once thinking and extended? I imagine myself doubting, and for the sake of argument I defend the affirmative side. You, intending to prove the negative, argue that a thinking thing can be understood without extension being thought, just as an extended thing can be understood without thought being understood. Therefore, a thinking thing is really without extension, or mind, which is a thinking thing, is in reality not extended.

I answer that it is a paralogism, since whatever things we can understand by distinct or separate concepts are not on that account distinct or separate, or if you prefer, separable, in reality. And I bring forward the example of two properties of a triangle which, though understood separately, are nevertheless not separable in reality either from one another or from the triangle. You insist, and want being able to be understood and really being to be the same. I persist that they are different. You confuse; I distinguish. And yet here are your words, that I understand nothing because I confuse the question of what can be understood with the question of what really is. Please, which of us is doing this?

But clearly you thought this should be passed over with a haughty brow, so that by applying, as it were, a cosmetic, the difficulty you did not dare touch would be hidden. You thought you could slip away like this when, to resume the previous words, you merely said that you did not linger over the things that I had tediously repeated often, namely that you had not proved certain things which you nevertheless demonstrated.

As if, once you had said that in advance, no one would ask where, or by what argument, you had demonstrated not only that God exists, but also to what things God's power extends, so that you could show that God can make whatever you can understand. I indeed recognize that this is tedious, but tedious for you, namely for whom to have said something once is to have demonstrated it; who, impatient of contradiction, think no one should fail to acquiesce immediately when you assert something, and that anything you have proved in whatever way should be taken as demonstrated.

There is no need here to repeat the weakness of the arguments by which you so confidently think you demonstrated that God exists. I only observe that at the beginning of this Sixth Meditation you seized the occasion to mix in these words: "For there is no doubt that God is capable of effecting all the things I am capable of perceiving; and I have never judged that anything cannot be made by him except because it conflicted with being distinctly perceived by me."

But to what end? Evidently so that afterward it would be understood as a principle demonstrated and no longer needing demonstration, when in this article you said, "I know that all things which I clearly and distinctly understand can be made by God such as I understand them." And yet please tell me how you demonstrated it. For neither had you previously said anything about that matter, nor do you have anything more in that place. Do you not use that saying as a principle by which to prove the possible existence of material things? For the causal particle "for" points to this. You do not derive it as the conclusion of a demonstration you have made.

What, then, is tedious about the objection that it ought to have been proved to what things God's power extends, and demonstrated that he can make whatever is distinctly perceived by you? Or perhaps will you say that the latter part of the saying demonstrates the former? If you say this, although each part is a mere statement, let the reasoning then be put together in this form:

If I judge that something cannot be made by God because it conflicts with being distinctly perceived by me, God is capable of effecting all the things that I am capable of perceiving in this way.

But I judge that something cannot be made by God because it conflicts with being distinctly perceived by me.

Therefore God is capable of effecting all the things that I am capable of perceiving in this way.

Please, then, is your judgment and your perception the rule of what God can or cannot do, and therefore to be held as an unshaken principle to be used by anyone who wants to demonstrate that God can or cannot do something? Or will you say that everyone, in your fashion, ought to consult his own perception and listen to his own judgment? If you do, then you send us back to a rule more than Lesbian, and you will declare that your reasons are indeed demonstrations for you yourself, but not for others. And certainly, since the things discussed here about divine power exceed the light of nature, you will be far from admitting that perception and judgment which pagan philosophers, led by the light of nature and capable of grasping demonstration, will testify that they have. But more on this matter later.

## Notes

- Gassendi treats the separability proof as a paralogism from separate concepts to real separation.
- The triangle-property example returns as a countercase to separate conceivability.
- Gassendi reverses Descartes' accusation: Descartes is the one confusing what can be understood with what really is.
- Descartes' divine-power principle is treated as inserted early so it can later function as already demonstrated.
- Gassendi says Descartes has not demonstrated the scope of God's power.
- If the rule is Descartes' own distinct perception, the result is private assurance, not public demonstration.
- The phrase "rule more than Lesbian" marks a flexible or self-adjusting criterion, not a fixed public standard.
