## Opening Finding

Gassendi targets the main separability proof of Meditation VI: because Descartes can clearly and distinctly understand himself as thinking and non-extended, and body as extended and non-thinking, he infers real distinction and divine separability. Gassendi first attacks the underlying principle by comparing it to triangle properties that can be conceived separately without being separately instantiated. He then narrows the real issue. If Descartes means only that mind is not the gross body of limbs, there is no difficulty. The unresolved question is whether mind might itself be a subtle body, diffused through the gross body or seated in some part of it. Gassendi grants a liberal agent-intellect and separable-intellect framing rather than pressing a stricter soul-as-form account, but says Descartes has still not proved pure incorporeality. Meditation II asserted that mind is not wind, fire, vapor, or breath; here, Gassendi says, the promised proof still has not arrived.

## Working Translation

DUBITATIO III.

On the principal demonstration for the mind's existence separately from body, from the fact that the mind is a thinking thing and body an extended thing.

You consequently come to the main undertaking, but with a kind of light skirmish. For you proceed: "Now, however, after I begin to know myself and the author of my origin, I do not think that all the things I seem to have from the senses should be rashly admitted, but neither should all of them be called into doubt." This is good, although without doubt you had thought the same thing before.

Then follows: "And first, since I know that all things which I clearly and distinctly understand can be made by God such as I understand them, it is enough that I can clearly and distinctly understand one thing without another in order for me to be certain that one is different from the other, because it can at least be placed apart by God; and it does not matter by what power this happens, for it to be judged different."

To this nothing else needs to be said except that you are proving the clear from the obscure, lest you complain that there is some obscurity in the inference. And indeed I do not dwell on this, that one must first prove that God exists and to what things his power extends in order to show that he can make whatever you can understand. I ask only: do you not clearly and distinctly understand in the triangle that property by which the greater sides are subtended by the greater angles, separately from another property by which the three angles together are held equal to two right angles? And do you therefore admit that God can separate that property from this one and place it apart, so that the triangle has this property and not that one, or that this property also exists apart from the triangle?

But, so that I do not delay you here, since this separation does little for the matter, you add: "And therefore from this very fact that I know I exist, and meanwhile notice that plainly nothing else belongs to my nature or essence except this alone, that I am a thinking thing, I rightly conclude that my essence consists in this one thing, that I am a thinking thing."

I would delay here, but it is enough either to repeat what was said around the Second Meditation or to wait for what you want to infer.

Finally, then, you say: "And although perhaps, or rather, as I will soon say, certainly, I have a body which is very closely joined to me, nevertheless because on the one side I have a clear and distinct idea of myself insofar as I am only a thinking thing, not an extended thing, and on the other side a distinct idea of body insofar as it is only an extended thing, not thinking, it is certain that I am really distinct from my body and can exist without it."

So this is where you were hastening? Therefore, since the hinge of the difficulty turns especially here, we must stop a little, so that it may be clear how you establish it.

Here the first issue is the distinction between you and body. But what body do you mean? Namely this gross body consisting of members, about which without doubt these words are said: "I have a body joined to me," and, "it is certain that I am distinct from my body," and so on.

But, O Mind, there is no difficulty about this body. There would be if I objected, with many philosophers, that you are an entelechy, perfection, act, form, species, and, to speak in the common way, a mode of the body. For they do not acknowledge you as distinct and separable from that body any more than a figure or some other mode is; and this whether you are the whole soul, or are besides also nous dynamei, nous pathetikos, possible or passible intellect, as they say.

But I prefer to deal with you more liberally, namely by considering you as noun poietikon, agent intellect, indeed also as choriston, separable, although by a different account than theirs. For since they held that intellect to be common to all human beings, or rather to all things, and to furnish understanding to the possible intellect in precisely the same way and by the same necessity as light furnishes sight to the eye, whence they were accustomed to compare it to sunlight and therefore to regard it as coming from outside, I instead consider you, since you too want this, as a certain special intellect that rules in the body.

But I repeat that the difficulty is not whether you are separable from this body or not. Hence I also hinted a little earlier that it was not necessary to run back to God's power, by which the things that you understand separately are separable. Rather, the difficulty concerns the body which you yourself may be, as if you could be a subtle body diffused within that gross one or having its seat in some part of it.

Moreover, you have not yet made good the claim that you are something purely incorporeal. And when in the Second Meditation you declared that you were not wind, not fire, not vapor, not breath, you were indeed warned that this had been declared without proof. You said that you were not disputing those matters in that place. But afterward you did not dispute them, nor did you prove by any argument that you are not a body of this kind. There was hope that you would supply that proof here. And yet if you dispute anything, if you prove anything, you dispute and prove that you are not this gross body, about which, as I have already said, there is no difficulty.

## Notes

- Gassendi attacks the inference from separate clear and distinct conception to real separability.
- The triangle-property counterexample tests whether what can be understood separately can therefore be separated by God or exist apart from the subject.
- The dispute turns on which `body` Descartes has excluded.
- If Descartes means the gross body of limbs, Gassendi grants that this is not the hard question.
- The hard question is whether mind may itself be a subtle body within the gross body.
- Gassendi declines to press the stricter soul-as-form position and instead grants a more liberal agent-intellect or separable-intellect frame.
- Even under that concession, Descartes has not proved that mind is purely incorporeal.
- Meditation II's denial that mind is wind, fire, vapor, or breath remains, for Gassendi, an assertion without proof.
