## Opening Finding

Gassendi's image criterion now becomes a general epistemic rule. Where we do not have a proper image, we do not have a proper idea; we have inference, analogy, or a confused construction from accidents. This carries him from the sun example to substance and then to God: substance is known only through accidents, and the idea of God is assembled and enlarged from finite perfections rather than represented as God is.

## Working Translation

... something that is perceived through some idea. The example has already been suggested. Two people occur to us, one with an uncovered face, the other masked. We perceive the face of the first through a true idea or image; that of the second through consequence from the fact that we recognize in him the other signs of human form. Hence we understand that in the first there is indeed a human face, but in the second there ought to be one. And as we know the face of the first distinctly and as it is in itself, so we know that of the second only confusedly and from analogy to the face of other human beings, in whom we have observed forehead, nose, chin, eyes, cheeks, mouth, and the rest, to which this one ought to have similar features.

The same must be said of the surface and the bottom of the sea, of which the one is understood through an idea, the other through consequence. The same applies to all other things, and also to those whose species we have either too diminished or too enlarged, so that they must be referred back to the true ones. Thus when two towers, equal to each other, occur to us, one nearby and the other far away, we will indeed perceive each through an idea; but because the idea of the nearer one will be genuine, that of the more distant one diminished, it will happen that we perceive the more distant as equal to the nearer not through a true idea, but through consequence, after account has been taken of distance, and through enlargement of the species as it is referred to the genuine one.

DUBITATION IV.

On the idea of substance as not proper, but through accidents and after their manner; and on the idea of God as drawn from things or from their perfections.

Next, recognizing the inequality and diversity of ideas, you say: "Without doubt, those that exhibit substance to me are something greater and, so to speak, contain more objective reality in themselves than those that represent only modes or accidents. And again, the one by which I understand a certain supreme God, eternal, infinite, omnipotent, creator of all things that exist apart from him, plainly has more objective reality in itself than those by which finite substances are exhibited."

You are now advancing with a large step. For that reason, you must be stopped a little. I do not, indeed, get stuck over what you call objective reality. It is enough that, since people commonly say that external things are subjectively or formally in themselves, but objectively or ideally in the intellect, you seem to mean nothing other than that the idea ought to conform to the thing of which it is the idea, so that representatively it contains nothing that is not by that very fact in the thing itself, and that it represents so much more reality as the represented thing has more reality in itself.

You certainly immediately distinguish objective reality from formal reality, which, as I interpret it, is the idea itself, not as representing, but as some entity. But it is established that either the idea or its objective reality is not to be measured according to the whole formal reality of the thing, or what the thing itself has in itself, but only according to that part of which the intellect has received knowledge; or, what is the same, according to the knowledge that the intellect has of the thing.

Thus, for example, there will be said to be in you a perfect idea of the person whom you have looked over attentively, often, and from every side. But the idea of one whom you have seen in passing, once, and only in part, will certainly be imperfect. But if you have seen not the person himself, but a mask drawn over the face and garments covering the body on every side, then either it must be said that there is no idea of him in you, or, if there is some idea, that it is most imperfect and supremely confused.

From this I say that there is indeed a distinct and genuine idea of the accidents, but of the substance lying hidden beneath them only a confused and at most fabricated one. Hence, when you say that there is more objective reality in the idea of substance than in the idea of accident, first it must be denied that there is a true idea or representation of substance, and therefore any objective reality of it. Then, if some is conceded, it must also be denied that it is greater than what is in the ideas of accidents, since whatever reality of this kind it has, it has from the ideas of those accidents under which, or after whose manner, we said above that substance is conceived, declaring that it cannot be conceived except as something extended, figured, and colored.

As for what you add about the idea of God, I ask you: since it is not yet established for you whether God exists, how do you know that God is represented by his idea as supreme, eternal, infinite, omnipotent, creator of all? Is it not from a preconceived knowledge about God, insofar as you have heard those attributes enunciated of God? For if you had heard nothing of this kind up to now, would you describe him in this way? You will say that this is now brought forward only as an example, without anything yet being defined. So be it; but see to it that later you do not take this as already prejudged.

You say that there is more objective reality in the idea of an infinite God than in the idea of a finite thing. But first, since the human intellect is not capable of conceiving infinity, it neither has nor looks toward an idea representative of an infinite thing. And so whoever speaks of something infinite attributes to a thing he does not grasp a name he does not understand. For just as the thing is extended beyond every grasp of his, so the negation of limit attributed to that extension is not understood by one whose understanding is always confined by some limit.

Next, since all the greatest perfections are customarily attributed to God, they all seem to be drawn from things we commonly admire in ourselves, such as long duration, power, knowledge, goodness, blessedness, and so on. Enlarging these as much as we can, we pronounce God eternal, omnipotent, omniscient, best, most blessed, and so on. But the idea that represents all these things does not for that reason have more objective reality than finite things taken together have, from whose ideas that idea has been put together and then enlarged in the way already mentioned. For whoever says "eternal" does not for that reason embrace in his mind the whole extension of that duration that never began and will never end; nor does whoever says "omnipotent" embrace the whole multitude of possible effects, and so with the rest.

Finally, who can be said to have a genuine idea of God, that is, one that represents God as he is? What a tiny thing God would be if he were not other and did not have other things than these little things of ours, however many they are. Should we not judge that the proportion of perfections between God and a human being is less than between an elephant and the tiny animalcule of a mite under its skin? If, then, someone, from the observed perfections of a mite, formed in himself an idea that he called an idea of an elephant and asserted to be genuine, would he not be held utterly foolish? And will someone congratulate himself if, from the perfections seen in a human being, he forms an idea that he maintains both to be of God and to be genuine?

I ask also: those few perfections that we discover to be in us, how do we recognize them to be in God? And once we have recognized them, what sort of essence of God will it be permissible to imagine on that basis? God is in fact above every grasp by infinite intervals; and when our mind is compared to the contemplation of him, it not only grows dark, but is nothing. Therefore there is no reason for us to say that there is any genuine idea that represents God. It is quite enough if, from analogy with these things of ours, we elicit and form some kind of idea for our use, one that does not exceed human grasp and does not contain any reality that we do not perceive in other things or on the occasion of other things.

## Notes

- The mask, sea-bottom, and distant-tower examples clarify the rule from Part 002: inference can tell us what must be there, but it does not give the same kind of cognition as a true idea/image.
- `consecutio` and `analogia` now become working terms. They name legitimate but image-poor knowing.
- The title of Dubitatio IV makes Gassendi's next move explicit: substance has no proper idea; it is conceived through accidents and after their manner.
- `realitas objectiva` is not rejected as vocabulary, but Gassendi restricts its measure to what the intellect has actually received or knows of the thing.
- The idea of substance is demoted to a confused or fabricated construction from accidents. The idea of God is likewise assembled from finite perfections and enlarged by analogy.
- This gives a second major pressure point after the sun: where Descartes wants the idea to carry more objective reality than finite ideas, Gassendi asks what image or representable content the intellect actually has.
