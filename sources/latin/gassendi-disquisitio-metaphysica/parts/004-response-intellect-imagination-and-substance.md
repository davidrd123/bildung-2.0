## Opening Finding

Descartes answers by accusing Gassendi of confusing intellection with imagination. Gassendi turns that accusation into the main issue: if ideas are in the intellect and ideas are images, then what distinguishes intellectual perception from imagination? His answer forces substance back into the same rule as the sun and God: we know it by consequence from accidents, not by a proper clear idea.

## Working Translation

REPLY.

You do the same thing here when you deny that there is a true idea of substance, namely because substance is perceived not by imagination but by intellect alone. But I protested long ago, my dear Flesh, that I have nothing to do with those who want to use only their imagination and not their intellect.

But when you say that the idea of substance has no reality that it does not have from the ideas of those accidents under which, or after whose manner, it is conceived, you prove that you in fact have no distinct idea, because substance can never be conceived after the manner of accidents, nor can it borrow its reality from them. On the contrary, accidents are commonly conceived by philosophers after the manner of substances whenever they are said to be real. For no reality, that is, no entity more than modal, can be attributed to accidents that is not drawn from the idea of substance.

Moreover, when you say that the idea of God is had only from the fact that we have heard certain attributes uttered about God, I would like you to add from where, then, the first human beings from whom we heard these things had that same idea of God. If they had it from themselves, why can we not also have the same from ourselves? If from God revealing it, then God exists.

And when you add that whoever speaks of something infinite attributes to a thing he does not grasp a name he does not understand, you do not distinguish an understanding conformed to the measure of our talent, such as each of us sufficiently experiences himself to have of the infinite, from an adequate concept of things, such as no one has, not only of the infinite but perhaps not even of any other thing, however small.

Nor is it true that the infinite is understood through negation of end or limitation, since, on the contrary, every limitation contains negation of the infinite. Nor is it true that the idea representing all those perfections that we attribute to God does not have more objective reality than finite things have. For you yourself admit that those perfections are enlarged by our intellect so that they may be attributed to God. Do you therefore think that things enlarged in this way are not for that reason greater than those not enlarged? And from where can there be a faculty of enlarging all created perfections, that is, of conceiving something greater or ampler than they are, unless from the fact that the idea of a greater thing, namely God, is in us?

Finally, it is not true that God would be very small if he were not greater than he is understood by us; for he is understood to be infinite, and nothing can be greater than the infinite. But you confuse intellection with imagination and pretend that we imagine God after the manner of some very large human being, as if someone who had never seen an elephant imagined it after the manner of a very large mite-animalcule, which I admit with you would be utterly foolish.

REJOINDER.

How gracious you are: although you present yourself as pure mind, and whatever you protested long ago, you nevertheless deign to have some business with me, who am Flesh. Is it because you think that some intellect still remains in me by which I may perceive this grace of yours? So it seems. Otherwise why would you want to be regarded as silly, sharpening your stylus against mere flesh? But if so, on what basis do you argue that I do not want to use intellect, but only imagination? Did I not press you enough to hand over a criterion by which I could recognize that I am using intellection that is not imagination, or imagination that is not intellection? And if you knew one, why did you refuse to hand it over?

Do you want imagination to be when we perceive some thing in such a way that we seem to look upon a certain image of it facing us, but intellection when we perceive a thing and yet have no image, as it were, facing us? First, then, either you must deny that there is intellection of bodies, or that bodies are perceived by intellect, since they cannot be perceived without some image, as it were, facing us; or, if you admit it, you must admit that the same action is simultaneously imagination and intellection.

And certainly, how can it be denied that this is a function of intellect when carefully, studiously, deliberately, and for the end that the greatest cognition possible may be had, we apply ourselves and attend to some present body, something we use with the eyes and handle with the hands, so that we have a genuine image of it facing the mind? How, I ask, can this be denied unless it is also denied that the intellect can reason or deduce anything about bodies, since it can do this only about things known by it, and bodies cannot be known except through images of themselves?

Next, since you want ideas to be in the intellect and the intellect to know through them the things of which they are ideas, are there not also images in the intellect, and does the intellect not know through images? For what else is the idea of a thing than the image of that thing itself? Certainly an idea is not an idea unless it represents the thing, and an image is not an image unless it represents the thing. But why linger over this, since you too have asserted it, as has already been pressed home? What difference, then, will there be between imagination, by which things are perceived through images, and intellection, by which things are perceived through images?

Will you add that the imaginative faculty does not reason, but the intellect does reason? But in whatever way you deny that the imaginative faculty reasons, I will deny that the intellect imagines; and I will add that reason alone reasons, so that each faculty performs the function cognate with its name, and thus imagination alone is attributed to the imaginative faculty, intellection to intellect, and reasoning to reason.

But if you think that the same faculty of intellect that simply understands things can also reason and deduce one thing from another, why not think that the same faculty can imagine, and all the more because simply understanding things seems to be nothing other than simply imagining things?

Will you say that there are only corporeal images in the imaginative faculty, but incorporeal ones in the intellect? But if we both imagine and understand some body, by what difference at last will you discern its image existing in the intellect from the same image existing in the imaginative faculty? Tell me honestly: can you recognize that there are two images of it in you, and distinguish when you perceive the body by one and when by the other?

Here I do not press how you think that image can be called incorporeal which, insofar as it represents, must itself consist of various parts, colors, figures, and the like, about which more opportunely elsewhere. I say only that if there are images in the intellect, how can they represent anything otherwise than those you admit dwell in the imaginative faculty? Certainly none of them represents a thing as if in a point, but with some kind of diffusion or extension. And let the mind set aside matter and corporeality as much as it wants, or rather as much as it can; nevertheless in that image there will always remain some diffusion or extension. But diffusion or extension is proper to a corporeal image, the kind you yourself concede dwells in the imaginative faculty.

You will say that I am too fleshly because I do not experience any image of a thing facing me that is neither in a point nor has any extension. But since I confess my dullness, I have occasion to complain of your sharpness, because you have not handed over your mode of experiencing, though asked and urged. Among other things, I asked you how you hold yourself, or the mind, as object of contemplation, and how, defining it as thinking substance, you experience yourself facing yourself in some other way than as a certain thin, pure, transparent breeze, and so on. But you passed over this in silence. And now, since you say that you have an idea or image of mind, ought you not say how this idea is an image, and what general definition of image could be given so that it can be applied to it, so that it represents mind as existing neither in a point nor in some extension? But let us deal with what is at issue, for immediately you will again provide occasion to ask the same thing.

You say that substance is perceived not by imagination but by intellect alone. I do not deny that it is perceived by intellect, for it cannot be conceived as something distinct from accidents without abstraction and deduction from consequence, which is proper to intellect. But I maintain that it is also at the same time perceived by imagination, whether the imaginative faculty is a distinct faculty that concurs with intellect, or rather is one and the same faculty in the sense just set out.

The reason is that the intellect cannot understand substance from changes of accidents without apprehending it; and it cannot apprehend it except under some idea or form. And because such an idea is an image, and apprehension through an image is imagination, it follows that the intellect cannot understand substance except by imagining. You who want substance to be perceived by intellect alone and not also by imagination, say how this happens. Say what intellection is other than perception of an idea. Say what imagination is other than perception of an image. And since idea is image, what else is intellection than imagination?

Whether the idea of substance is genuine, clear, and distinct is another question. You contend that it is; I deny that it is. And since you neither wish nor are able to say what sort of thing you conceive it to be, but only flare up at me, I say in good faith and explain how I conceive it. Namely, I say that we perceive substance only by consequence, and thus only under an abstract, imperceptible, conjectural idea.

For since accidents are, as it were, the garments of substance, we reason from changes of accidents that there is a subject common to them and now underlying these, now those. And when the garments are changed, we understand that there is a body that now puts on these clothes, now those. And just as, although we understand that there is some body under the clothes, we nevertheless do not have a true idea of it, since we do not know, for example, whether it is white or dark, smooth or hairy, clean or stained, and so on, but conceive it only confusedly and generally after the manner of others, so although beneath extension, figure, color, taste, softness, and other accidents we understand that there lies, or ought to be, some subject that we name substance, we nevertheless do not for that reason have a genuine idea of it, nor can we assert what sort of thing it is. We conceive it only confusedly and under a certain general species of subjects.

Thus, although we try to set aside in the mind extension, figure, color, and the other accidents, we nevertheless still experience some I-know-not-what facing us: indefinitely extended, confusedly figured, obscurely colored, and so on, as had been objected. Such, then, I say, is the idea or image under which we conceive substance. And it will be very strange if you, when you cannot divine what sort of body that is which you have never seen except covered in clothes, can divine and set forth what sort of thing substance is, which has never appeared to you except veiled by its accidents.

Hence, because I said that the idea of substance has no reality that it does not have from the ideas of those accidents under which, or after whose manner, it is conceived, you argue that I prove I have no truly distinct idea. But while I confess that I truly have none distinct, and honestly profess the sort I do have, I am still not so credulous as to be persuaded that you have a more distinct one. I am not moved by the raised eyebrow with which you want this believed, and you can prove in no other way that you truly have a distinct one than by explaining what sort of thing it is, a task easier for you in proportion as you want the idea to be more distinct.

"Substance," you say, "can never be conceived after the manner of accidents, nor borrow its reality from them." You pass from the concrete to the abstract. I do not say that substance is or is conceived as naked extension, naked figure, naked color, but as something extended, figured, colored. And so when you say that substance can never be conceived after the manner of accidents, one may reply, on the contrary, that substance can never be conceived except after the manner of accidents, namely concrete accidents, so that the sense is that it is always conceived as a certain being that is somehow extended, somehow figured, somehow colored. It falls to you to declare what other distinct and clear conception of it there is.

What you say, that substance does not borrow its reality from accidents, is true. But it is false that it does not borrow from them that knowledge of itself which it has in our minds, since we can know or conjecture nothing about it except through the intervention of accidents and their changes. Whether and how philosophers commonly conceive accidents after the manner of substances, or call them real, I do not dispute here, since it is enough for me that they are, after the manner of substances, by divine power, as I am taught by faith in the Eucharistic mystery. I also do not dispute whether they are only modal entities, as I once defended, nor could this be recalled here except inopportunely. It is enough for me that, since the question is now about ideas of substance and accidents, I think and say about them what reason and intelligence, or common sense as they call it, does not reject.

Whether you do the same when you say that no reality can be attributed to accidents that is not drawn from the idea of substance, as though knowledge of accidents depended on knowledge of substance, and not rather knowledge of substance on knowledge of accidents, others may judge. The comparison of objective reality that you attribute to each idea will be discussed not much later.

## Notes

- Descartes' response is explicit: Gassendi confuses intellection with imagination. This makes the faculty distinction the center of the exchange.
- Gassendi asks for a criterion distinguishing intellection from imagination. Without that criterion, ideas in the intellect still look like images.
- `Idea = imago` is sharpened again: an idea represents; an image represents; if both are representational, Gassendi demands an account of the difference.
- `ratio` is separated from both imagination and intellect as the faculty that reasons. This is not a denial of reasoning but a refusal to let reasoning convert image-less inference into clear idea.
- Substance is known through `abstractio`, `deductio`, and `consecutio`, but even then under an abstract, imperceptible, conjectural idea.
- The garment analogy becomes the main image for substance: accidents are the clothes; substance is the unknown body beneath them.
- The Eucharistic aside matters but should not dominate this tranche. Gassendi brackets the scholastic/metaphysical dispute over accidents' modal entity and returns to what common sense does not reject.
