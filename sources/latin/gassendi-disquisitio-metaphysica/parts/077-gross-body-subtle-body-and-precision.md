## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' claim that the proof concerned every body by pressing the wording and context of Meditation VI. The "body closely joined to me" is the gross body of limbs, the body of sensation, pain, hunger, thirst, and mixture with mind, not an unspecified subtle body. Even if Descartes refuses that reading, Gassendi asks which subtle body is meant: air, wind, spirit, breath, vapor, fire, or something similar. If mind is closely joined to such a subtle body and distinct from the gross body, the real question remains whether mind is that subtle thinking body or something separable from it. Gassendi then attacks Descartes' appeal to the Second Meditation by distinguishing precision from exclusion. Mind may be considered precisely as thinking, or even precisely as substance, without this proving that it is only thinking or only incorporeal. Just as considering a human being as rational does not prove he is not animal, considering mind as substance leaves the corporeal/incorporeal difference unaddressed.

## Working Translation

For look: you linger here especially over what I said, that in this place, while dealing with the distinction between mind and body, you understand this gross body, or the body consisting of members. For you ask by what argument I prove that you dealt with gross body rather than subtle body. Yet there is no need to ask, since the argument is plainly those very words of yours, in which under the name of your mind, or soul, you speak in this way: "And although perhaps, or rather, as I will later say, certainly, I have a body which is very closely joined to me; nevertheless because on one side," and so on, and immediately, "it is certain that I am really distinct from my body and can exist without it."

But, you say, you do not see why these words do not fit subtle body just as much as gross body, nor do you think anyone besides me sees it. It is astonishing to say how cautiously you conduct yourself, since you neither affirm nor deny that you are speaking here about gross body, and say only that neither you nor anyone else besides me sees why you speak of gross body rather than subtle body. As for you, I do not contend, since even if you recognized it a thousand times, you still would not once confess it. But as for others, I tell no one to attend who does not see the same thing I do.

And certainly, since the difficulty is whether mind or soul can exist without body, and therefore whether it perseveres immortal when the body dies, who would see any other body understood here than this gross body consisting of members? For even when many of the Fathers thought that soul or mind was a subtle body, they still asked whether it survived immortal when separated from body, namely from this gross body consisting of members. The reason is that whether it is in itself corporeal or incorporeal, the question is whether it is distinguished from this gross body in such a way that when the body is dissolved, it remains undissolved.

Indeed I also acknowledge that the learned man who examined the passage before me saw the same thing when he said: "So far concerning the real distinction of our mind from body. But since the distinguished author has undertaken to demonstrate the immortality of souls, one may rightly ask whether it follows evidently from that separation. For in the principles of common philosophy it by no means follows, since they commonly hold that the souls of beasts are distinct from their bodies, and yet perish with them."

Surely here both the human body and the bodies of beasts compared with it are understood rather as these gross bodies consisting of members than as any subtle bodies. You too, when you replied in this way: "Nevertheless I do not deny that that close conjunction of mind with body, which we continually experience by the senses, is the reason why we do not notice its real distinction from it without attentive meditation," did you not interpret this as the gross body, in which the senses are, rather than a subtle one?

But to return to the passage itself, I pass over first that you understand in it the same body about which a little earlier you had said: "Not without reason did I judge that the body which I called mine by a special right belonged to me more than others," and so on. That was not a subtle body, of which there was no mention, but a gross body consisting of members, in which, namely, there are sense, pain, hunger, and so on.

I note only those words, "perhaps, or rather, as I will later say, certainly, I have a body which is very closely joined to me," and so on, as making clear what you are speaking about. For if you spoke of a subtle body rather than a gross one, or at least equally of both, show even one place where afterward you said that you certainly have a body very closely joined to you, which is to be understood of a subtle body either rather than, or equally with, a gross body; and I will bring forward various places in which it is either only, or rather, about the gross body.

Namely, you later say: "There is nothing which this nature teaches me more expressly than that I have a body, which is ill when I feel pain, which needs food or drink when I suffer hunger or thirst," and immediately, that "I am not merely present to my body as a sailor is present to a ship, but am most closely joined to it and as if mixed with it, so that I compose one thing with it; otherwise, when the body is hurt, I, who am nothing other than a thinking thing, would not feel pain on that account," and so on. And a little later: "It is certain that my body, or rather I as a whole, insofar as I am composed of body and mind, can be affected by various advantages and disadvantages from surrounding bodies." And again: "For in this complex many things are contained which pertain to mind alone, such as that I perceive what has been done cannot be undone, and all the rest that are known by natural light; and also many things which pertain to body alone, such as that it tends downward, and the like."

Since in that place, then, you promised that you would later say that you certainly have a body very closely joined to you, and since the body which you later said you certainly have is nothing other than this gross body consisting of members, as is manifestly understood from the words quoted, did I not rightly say that if you proved or concluded anything, you prove and conclude it about this gross body consisting of members?

But let it be so. Suppose you did not understand only, or chiefly, this gross body consisting of members. What kind of body, then, did you understand either only, or chiefly, or if not chiefly, at least equally, when you said that you, or your mind, certainly have a very closely joined body, from which body you prove and conclude that mind is so distinct that it can exist without it? Surely you must say that it is air, or wind, or spirit, or breath, or vapor, or fire, or something of this kind.

Therefore mind and this subtle body are something very closely connected and distinct from this gross body consisting of members. Therefore that connected thing is of the sort I objected that mind or soul could be conceived to be, so that accordingly it could be called a body, a substance, a thinking thing. And therefore the question remains whether mind is this very subtle body which is capable of thinking, or whether it is something distinct and separable from it.

Since you defend this latter point, I objected that you had not made the case you had promised. You now answer that in the Second Meditation you made the case that mind can be understood as an existing substance, even if we understand nothing to exist which is wind, or fire, or vapor, or breath, or any other body, however subtle and thin. But first it is false that you made the case for this. For there was not even this, that you are an existing substance, but only precisely that you are a thinking thing; and I also noted in its own place, and repeated in this very passage, that this was stated by you simply and without proof.

And indeed I granted that mind can be understood, or considered, precisely as a thinking thing. But from the beginning it was not granted that for this reason you are not some thin air infused into these members, not wind, not fire, not vapor, and so on. For it is one thing to be regarded precisely as such, and another to be only that which is understood through precision. A human being, for example, can be regarded precisely as rational; but because in that state of precision made by the intellect he is understood only as rational, it would be a faulty consequence if one inferred that he is not an animal, just as if, when the same human being is regarded precisely as an animal, one inferred that he is not rational.

Next, there can be those who grant you that mind is a thinking thing and yet do not therefore grant that it is a substance, whether thinking or existing. For they will be able to maintain that such a thing is a faculty, which can be regarded precisely as a faculty, and as thinking, without the substance or condition of the substance in which it inheres being thought.

But even if it is granted both that it is a substance and that it is understood as a substance, and as an existing substance, and, to add this too, as existing through itself, this is still a precision, or this state of precision, in which no other grade of soul is understood than that of substance. Therefore, just as when a human being is regarded as an animal, or as a sentient body, the precision or state is one in which neither the rational nor the irrational grade is attended to, so when mind is regarded as substance, or as existing through itself, the precision or state is one in which neither the corporeal nor the incorporeal grade is attended to.

And therefore, when you say that mind can be understood as substance even if we understand nothing to exist which is body, this can indeed be granted. But you too must grant that mind can be understood as substance even if we understand nothing to exist which is incorporeal. For the reasoning is equal. Otherwise, as is clear, it would not be precision, but consideration of the genus with one or the other difference, contrary to the supposition.

And you surely acknowledge that you made precision from both grades, that is, not only the corporeal but also the incorporeal, in these words: "Whether in truth it was different from every body, I said that I was not disputing there." And so from the whole Second Meditation nothing else is had than that mind or soul can be understood precisely as substance, or as a thing existing through itself. And this was as it were the principle which, while you digressed to other matters, you then allowed as if to sleep, until at last in the Sixth Meditation you awakened it.

## Notes

- Gassendi argues that Descartes' own examples identify the disputed body as the gross body of sensation, pain, hunger, thirst, bodily need, and mind-body mixture.
- The immortality problem itself normally concerns survival after separation from the gross body, even for those who think soul is some subtle body.
- If Descartes insists that a subtle body is also meant, Gassendi asks which one: air, wind, spirit, breath, vapor, fire, or something similar.
- The subtle-body alternative is not defeated by saying mind is closely joined to body; it becomes the precise unresolved question.
- Gassendi distinguishes considering something precisely as X from proving that it is only X.
- The rational/animal example blocks the inference from "considered as rational" to "not animal."
- Mind considered as substance leaves corporeal and incorporeal differences equally unattended.
- The Second Meditation therefore supplies only a precision of mind as thinking thing or substance, not proof of incorporeality.
