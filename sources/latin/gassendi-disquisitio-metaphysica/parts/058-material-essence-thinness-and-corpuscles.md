## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' coherence complaint by distinguishing local title-work from whole-system repetition. His phrase "and this is all you have" meant the amount offered under the Fifth Meditation's title on material essence, not that Descartes had literally said nothing elsewhere. If every special question required recounting everything before and most of what follows, no one could ever finish any topic without perpetual tautology and confusion. Gassendi then returns to the substantive charge: Descartes' material-essence account gives only quantity, parts, figure, position, motion, and rest, things even "bleary-eyed people and barbers" know. It does not show that knowing these exhausts the beautiful and admirable things in nature. The real palm, Gassendi says, belongs to those who try to explain material nature, power, property, and action through magnitude, figure, motion, position, and the few other features of corpuscles or principles.

## Working Translation

INSTANTIA.

Here I now recognize why you bear it badly that individual particles of your Meditations are investigated. It is certainly not because I thought every single one had to be investigated, since I left very many untouched, but because in this place I seemed, as it were, to disturb the sacred rites of Vesta.

And yet I did not criticize anything. I only said: "And this is all you have about the essence of material things." But you, thinking it was being hinted that nothing worthy of the title had been written, grew hot, and, as you said, were forced to warn me, and so on.

As for me, to tell the truth, when the title of the Meditation raised a large hope about uncovering and explaining the essence of material things, I eagerly wanted to know what you would write about it. I hoped that the principles, causes, powers, and other things disputed among physicists and nevertheless still unknown would be made clear. But when I noticed that you had handed down nothing other than what I reported, I certainly stopped, and could not help wondering at it for a while.

You infer this from that phrase, "And this is all you have about the essence of material things," and, not wanting to seem to write meagerly and coldly, you warn that I did not attend enough to the coherence of what you wrote. You go on to say that you think this coherence is such that everything preceding a given thing, and the greatest part of what follows it, contributes to the proof of that thing, so that in good faith I cannot report how much you have about some question unless I also review everything you wrote about the rest.

Since I receive the warning with the mind it deserves, allow me in turn to warn you.

First, you seem not to have paid enough attention to the genuine meaning of the word "all." Neither in that place, nor in that phrase, nor in my sense was it a word of exclusion, but of quantity. So if something was said by you earlier, later, or elsewhere, my phrase does not imply that it was not said. It means that what you began to say there about that argument was continued only up to that point, and that the argument was changed afterward.

Second, you act too cleverly when, after a special title has been proposed under which you treat a special matter, you think that, once the treatment is finished and the title has now been changed, I cannot say that the argument of the earlier title ends there unless I search out, heap up, and review whatever you could have written about the same matter on another occasion and under other titles.

Third, you do not wish quite justly that I give you room to complain of tautology and perpetual confusion, if, since you want everything preceding and the greatest part of what follows to belong to the proof of each special matter you treat, I cannot free myself from any thing or special matter and declare it finished unless I review everything in each case and everywhere repeat both everything preceding and the greatest part of what follows.

Fourth, it was your job to review, if not everything, at least the chief headings of those things which you complain were passed over by me, so that you might make it clear that I undeservedly hinted that the harvest which can be reaped from this field around the essence of material things is most barren, even though, as from a field specially assigned to this seed, the chief yield should have been expected from it; undeservedly, I say, with you showing that a very rich abundance of sheaves could be gathered from elsewhere.

But I am lingering too much here. It is enough to warn each reader that, after weighing not only what you have here under the proper title, but whatever you also have in the preceding and following parts, he should attend to how much more instructed than before he has become about the essence of material things.

For that all material things are furnished with quantity and parts, with figure and position of parts, with motion or rest of the whole or of the parts, and with other things of this kind, not even bleary-eyed people and barbers are ignorant. Nor do you show from elsewhere that those who know these things are ignorant of nothing beautiful and admirable in the nature of things; that they have penetrated most deeply into the innermost recesses of nature; that this very thing which they know is the very essence of each thing; that other philosophers labor in vain when they desire something more, not because there is difficulty in finding it, but because there is nothing besides this in the nature of things.

Finally, you do not show that the palm should be awarded to those who have tried to explain every nature, power or property, and action of material things from magnitude, figure, motion, position, and a few other things, whether joined or added, of the corpuscles or principles from which each material thing is composed. These verses look to this:

> Concourses, motions, order, position, figure
> make fires, and by changed order change
> nature, and so on.

## Notes

- Gassendi says `tantum` meant "this much here," not "nothing anywhere else."
- He treats Descartes' appeal to whole-work coherence as methodologically unworkable if every special topic requires repeating everything before and much after.
- The Fifth Meditation title promised an account of material essence, but Descartes delivered only quantity, parts, figure, position, motion, rest, and similar mathematical features.
- Gassendi does not deny those features; he denies that they exhaust the essence, powers, properties, and actions of material things.
- The sarcastic "bleary-eyed people and barbers" line marks the thinness of a merely generic account of material things.
- Corpuscular explanation is named as the richer physical route: magnitude, figure, motion, position, and related features of corpuscles or principles.
