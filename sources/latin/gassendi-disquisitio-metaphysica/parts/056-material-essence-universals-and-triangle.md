## Opening Finding

Gassendi opens the Fifth Meditation exchange by attacking the status of eternal, immutable essences and the triangle example. Descartes' triangle is supposed to have a determinate nature independent of the mind, even if no such figure exists outside thought. Gassendi answers that making essences eternal apart from God is hard to accept: if essence is the chief thing in things, then God's production of existence would add little more than clothing a man. Universal human nature is formed by intellect from singular similar natures such as Plato and Socrates; it is not an entity existing before singulars and before abstraction. The same holds for the mental triangle. It is a rule for judging what deserves the name triangle, formed by intellect after seeing material triangles; material triangles do not borrow their properties from an ideal triangle. Geometrical ideas still trace back to sensory surfaces, and the supposedly exact triangle of breadthless lines, depthless area, and partless points is flagged as a further false nature, though Gassendi postpones that digression.

## Working Translation

IN MEDITATIONEM V.

On the essence of material things, and again on God, that he exists.

DUBITATIO I.

On the essence of material things known from quantity alone, figure, and so on; and on the eternal and immutable nature attributed to ideas and universals.

Concerning the fifth Meditation, you first say that you distinctly imagine quantity, that is, extension in length, breadth, and depth; likewise number, figure, position, motion, and duration. From all these, of which you say you have ideas, you select figure, and from figures the triangle. About it you have this:

"Even if perhaps such a figure exists nowhere at all outside my thought, and never has existed, there is nevertheless certainly some determinate nature of it which was not fashioned by me and does not depend on my mind. This is clear from the fact that various properties can be demonstrated about that triangle: namely, that its three angles are equal to two right angles, that the greatest side subtends the greatest angle, and similar things, which, whether I wish or not, I now clearly recognize, even if I had in no way thought of them before when I imagined the triangle, and therefore they were not fashioned by me."

And this is all you have about the essence of material things, for the few things you add belong to the same point. I do not want to get stuck here. I only hint that it seems hard to posit some immutable and eternal nature besides God thrice-greatest.

You will say that you bring forward nothing other than what they say in the Schools: that the natures or essences of things are eternal, and that propositions of everlasting truth are made about them. But this is just as hard, and it cannot otherwise be grasped that there is a human nature when there is no human being, or that a rose is called a flower when there is not even a rose.

They say that it is one thing to speak of the essence of things and another to speak of their existence, and that the existence of things is not from eternity, but nevertheless the essence is. Yet since essence is the chief thing in things, what great thing does God do when he produces existence? Plainly, he does no more than when a tailor clothes a man with a garment.

And yet who will defend that the essence of a human being which is in Plato is eternal and independent of God? As universal, they will say? But in Plato there is nothing except what is singular. Intellect is accustomed, from having seen the similar natures of Plato, Socrates, and the rest of human beings, to abstract a certain common concept in which all agree, and which can therefore be counted as the universal nature or essence of a human being, insofar as it is understood to agree with every human being. But that it was universal before Plato and the others existed, and before intellect abstracted, certainly cannot be explained.

You will say: even when a human being does not exist, and therefore from eternity, is this proposition not true, "a human being is an animal"? But it plainly seems not to be, except in this sense: whenever there is a human being, it will be an animal. For although there seems to be a difference between those two propositions, "a human being is" and "a human being is an animal," because existence is more expressly signified by the former and essence by the latter, nevertheless essence is not excluded from the former, nor existence from the latter. When it is said "a human being is," a human animal is understood; and when it is said "a human being is an animal," a human being is understood while it exists.

Moreover, since this proposition, "a human being is an animal," is not of greater necessity than this one, "Plato is a human being," the latter too will therefore be of everlasting truth, and the singular essence of Plato will be no less independent of God than the universal essence of a human being. And there are similar consequences which I am tired of pursuing.

Yet I add that when it is said that a human being is of such a nature that it cannot be without being an animal, one should not therefore imagine such a nature to be something, or to be somewhere, outside intellect. The sense is only this: in order for something to be a human being, it must be similar to those other things to which, because of their mutual similarity, the same denomination of human being has been attributed. I mean the similarity of singular natures, from which intellect received an occasion for forming a concept, or idea, or form of a common nature, from which whatever is going to be a human being ought not dissent.

Hence I say the same about your triangle, or its nature. The mental triangle is indeed like a rule by which you test whether something deserves to be called a triangle. But for that reason it should not be said that such a triangle is some real thing and a true nature outside intellect. Intellect alone, after seeing material triangles, formed that nature and made it common, just as was said about human nature.

Therefore it should not be thought that properties demonstrated about material triangles agree with them because they borrow those properties from an ideal triangle. Rather, the material triangles have them in themselves, and the ideal triangle has them only insofar as intellect, after inspecting the material ones, attributes the same properties to it, later to give them back in demonstration.

It is the same way in which the properties of human nature are not in Plato and Socrates as if they received them from a universal nature. Rather, the universal nature has them because intellect attributed them to it after observing them in Plato, Socrates, and the rest, later to give them back to those singulars when reasoning requires it. For it is known that intellect, from seeing Plato, Socrates, and all other rational beings, collected this universal proposition, "every human being is rational," and then, when it wants to prove that Plato is rational, introduces that proposition as a principle in a syllogism.

And you indeed say, O Mind, that you have the idea of a triangle, and would have had it even if you had never seen any triangular figure in bodies, just as you have ideas of many other figures which have never run into your senses. But if, as I was saying above, you had until now been deprived of all the functions of the senses, so that you had never either seen or touched various surfaces or the extremities of bodies, do you think you could have had or formed in yourself the idea of a triangle or of another figure?

Do you now have many ideas in yourself which did not slip into you through the senses? Of course you easily have them, because from those which did slip in you fashion those others and form various shapes in the ways explained above. Something would also have to be said here about that false nature of the triangle, by which it is supposed to consist of lines lacking breadth, to contain an area lacking depth, and to be bounded by three points lacking all parts. But we would wander too far.

## Notes

- Gassendi treats Descartes' Fifth Meditation opening as resting on the triangle's supposed determinate nature independent of mind.
- Eternal essence apart from God is treated as metaphysically costly: if essence is the chief thing, existence becomes a mere garment added afterward.
- Universal human nature is abstracted from singulars such as Plato and Socrates; it is not prior to singulars and intellect.
- The proposition "a human being is an animal" is treated as implicitly existential or hypothetical: whenever a human being exists, it is an animal.
- The mental triangle is a rule of recognition, not a real nature outside intellect.
- Material triangles do not borrow properties from an ideal triangle. Intellect abstracts the properties from material triangles, attributes them to the ideal, then gives them back in demonstration.
- Geometrical ideas remain dependent on sensory surfaces and bodily extremities, even when recombined into figures never directly sensed.
- Gassendi flags, but postpones, the fiction of breadthless lines, depthless areas, and partless points.
