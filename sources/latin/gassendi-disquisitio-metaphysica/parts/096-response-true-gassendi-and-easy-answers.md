## Opening Finding

Descartes answers the conciliatory conclusion by splitting the polemical object from Gassendi's person. An "alien mind" had argued with "flesh," but in the conclusion Descartes recognizes the true Gassendi: excellent philosopher, candid soul, upright life, friend to be cultivated. He asks Gassendi not to take his philosophical liberty in refuting the objections badly, and then repeats the hard judgment: in such a long and careful dissertation, Gassendi has brought no reason against Descartes' reasons or conclusions to which Descartes could not easily respond.

## Working Translation

But an alien mind was discoursing with flesh, and, as was fitting, it disagreed with it in many matters. Yet now in the conclusion I recognize the true Gassendi; and I look up to him as a most excellent philosopher, embrace him as a man celebrated for candor of soul and integrity of life, and will always try by whatever services I can to deserve his friendship.

And so I ask that it not be burdensome to him that I used philosophical liberty in refuting his objections, just as whatever is contained in them was indeed very welcome to me. And among other things I rejoiced that from a man of such great name, in a dissertation so long and so accurately written, no reason was brought forward that attacked my reasons, and none even against my conclusions to which it would not have been quite easy for me to respond.

## Notes

- Descartes separates Gassendi's person from the argumentative target through the mind/flesh contrast.
- The conclusion lets Descartes recognize the "true Gassendi" beneath the polemic.
- Friendship, candor, and integrity are explicitly restored.
- The praise is immediately joined to the unchanged methodological verdict: no reason has been brought that overthrows Descartes' reasons or conclusions.
- The phrase `libertas Philosophica` frames the preceding sharpness as permissible freedom in philosophical refutation.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `aliena mens cum carne` is deliberately odd. I keep "alien mind" and "flesh" rather than smoothing to "another mind" and "body" because the contrast sets up the final answer's objection to being divided into mind and flesh.

Contextual: Descartes' praise is real, but not a concession on the substance of the objections. The response preserves friendship while renewing the claim that the objections have been easy to answer.
