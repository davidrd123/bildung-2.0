## Opening Finding

Gassendi turns Descartes' response on indivisible essences against Descartes' earlier claim that substance is manifested by its various attributes, and that the more attributes we know, the more perfectly we understand its nature. If essence and idea are strictly indivisible, then even the least attribute must contain the whole essence and the whole idea, making later attributes unnecessary. If it does not, then essence has breadth among attributes, and the idea can receive additions. Gassendi then pushes the stronger consequence: if ideas represent essence strictly, Descartes has no idea of the sun, moon, stars, earth, water, stones, plants, animals, humans, or anything in the world, because he does not know their inner essences. The workable account is instead that ideas are of things insofar as we know them: distinctly of accidents, properties, effects, and circumstances; confusedly of the hidden essence beneath them.

## Working Translation

Now, to speak about the Response, you object that I did not attend to what philosophers commonly say. But first, did you attend to what you yourself said? Your words, certainly, near the end of the response to the objections against the preceding Meditation, were these: "As for me, I have never thought that anything else is required to make a substance manifest except its various attributes, so that the more attributes of some substance we know, the more perfectly we understand its nature." What, then, do the philosophers now say? They say, you claim, that the essences of things are indivisible; for an idea represents an essence, and if anything is added to or taken from it, it at once becomes the idea of another thing. Do you now approve this or not? Plainly you do approve it, for the whole force of your response lies in this.

But if an essence, or nature, is indivisible, then wherever even the very least of it is, there the whole of it is altogether, and there is nothing more; for what is indivisible has no parts. If the idea too is of this sort, so that nothing can be added to it or taken from it, then wherever even the very least of the idea is, there the whole idea is, and there is nothing in the thing which it does not represent. For if there were something further which it could represent, then something would be added to it, and it would become the idea of another thing.

Now, since you admit that not one only, but more and more attributes of some substance, nature, or essence can be known in such a way that the more have been known, the more perfectly the thing is understood, I ask: when you first enter upon cognition and know one single attribute of the essence, do you think the whole essence of the thing is contained in it or not? Do you think the idea which you have of that thing is the whole idea of the thing or not? If you think so, then it is false that there are more attributes, and false that through more attributes you understand the thing more perfectly. If you do not think so, then the essence of the thing does not consist in something indivisible, but has a breadth of attributes, and even a certain distinction of attributes among themselves, insofar as one can be known while the others are not known. Therefore the idea of the thing itself is not such that nothing can be added to it. Rather, it is capable of addition to just the extent that attributes known later can be represented by the idea just as much as the one known first.

Still, whether you attended to these things or not, as far as the philosophers are concerned I am not asking here to what extent one should admit, or not admit, what they say, that the essences of things are indivisible; nor whether they know the essences of the things to which they apply this. As far as you are concerned, when you say that an idea represents the essence of a thing, I seem able to infer, not badly, that if there are some things whose essence you do not know, you do not have an idea of them.

So I ask you: do you know the essence of the sun, the moon, or another star? You will not say, I think, that you know it. For what do you know of them beyond magnitude, figure, motion, distance, light, whiteness, heat, the power of generating, fostering, moving, and things of that sort? But these are only adjuncts or accidents. The essence itself, the nature, or the inner substance which underlies them, is wholly hidden from you. And although you may assert that in knowing these attributes you understand the nature of the stars, you will assert it to yourself alone, prove it to yourself alone, applaud yourself alone; though having nothing more than others, you alone will honor yourself above the rest. Certainly I think there is no one who, when you know only these things, thinks that you know the essence of the stars. Therefore, by your own account, you also do not have an idea of the stars.

But if you do not have one of the stars, then of what things will you have one? Of earth? Of water? Of some meteor, stone, mineral, plant, animal, human being? Of any body at all, or any thing existing in the universe? Certainly you know the essence of none of them. Whatever you can bring forward that you know about them is, in the same way, nothing other than adjuncts or accidents, properties and effects, all except the essence itself. Therefore you have no idea at all of any thing which is in the world. How, then, can it be that you yourself also speak about the things which are in the world, and therefore understand them, unless you have ideas of them?

Of course you do have ideas, but not of the sort you yourself describe. Ideas are only of things insofar as we know them. And because by distinct cognition we know their accidents, but not their essence, which we only suspect or conceive as hidden confusedly beneath them, the distinct idea is therefore of those accidents themselves, not of the essence, except insofar as it is only confusedly included under them. From this it follows that we have a clearer and more exact idea of some thing the more clearly and exactly, and the more, we know its accidents.

For since we experience ideas in our mind as images of things, and images are not of the essence but of its accidents, it follows that, just as an image of some person expressed on a panel is more perfect the more elaborated the symmetry, arrangement, and representation of more parts has been, and the more carefully each of the individual marks in the individual parts has been exhibited, so the idea of each thing will become more perfect the more of its accidents, or as it were more of its circumstances, it represents in better order, more expressly, more to the life.

## Notes

- Gassendi makes Descartes' earlier attribute-account of substance collide with the new indivisible-essence reply.
- The dilemma is direct: either one attribute contains the whole essence and idea, or the idea admits later additions as more attributes are known.
- The sun returns, now not as sensory versus astronomical idea but as an example of a thing whose inner essence is unknown while many accidents are distinctly known.
- Gassendi's positive account is epistemic rather than essentialist: ideas are of things `quatenus illas novimus`.
- The picture analogy keeps the image-theory of ideas alive. A better image represents more accidents, parts, marks, and circumstances in a more lifelike order.
