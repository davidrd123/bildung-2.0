## Opening Finding

Gassendi now grants for the sake of argument that Descartes may use divine power and asks for the missing criterion: if some things are distinct but cannot be separated by any power, how does Descartes distinguish separables from inseparables? The answer Descartes gives is the same clear-and-distinct separability rule. Gassendi restates his triangle-property objection and notes that Descartes had not answered it directly. He then reconstructs the available Cartesian replies from related cases: formal or modal distinction applies among incomplete entities conceived by abstraction; real distinction applies among complete entities each understood as being through itself. On this basis Descartes can say triangle properties are not complete things or substances, and are clearly distinguishable only formally, not completely separable from the triangle or triangular body. Part 079 therefore sets up the next critique: the complete-entity restriction may already assume what the mind-body proof has to prove.

## Working Translation

Nevertheless, even if it is granted that, when you were about to demonstrate, it was permitted for you to use either kind of God's power as an argument, come now: since theologians admit that there are some things distinct from one another which nevertheless cannot be separated and placed apart by either power, what criterion do you use to distinguish the separable from the inseparable? "In order for me to be certain," you say, "that one thing is different from another, it is enough that I can clearly and distinctly understand one without the other."

For this reason I had opposed the two properties of a triangle, one of which can be clearly and distinctly understood without the other, although it is not different in such a way that, according to your interpretation, it can be placed apart from it. You made no answer to this.

And indeed that example of Justice and Mercy in God had come to mind, which I later noticed had been objected by a learned theologian. But deliberately I had thought one should abstain from a theological example, lest, since although you were acting theologically you wanted to seem to act philosophically, you could complain that you were being drawn outside philosophical boundaries. And I did the same thing that I later found another had also done, when he used two properties of a triangle inscribed in a circle, namely being right-angled and having a base whose square is equal to the squares of the legs.

But since you replied something to each, and since the reply seems able to be adapted to my example, come, what did you say to the first? Namely, you first acknowledged a twofold distinction: one formal, or modal, which you judged to exist between incomplete entities, in which it is enough that one be distinctly and separately conceived from another by an abstraction of the intellect conceiving the thing inadequately; the other real, which you judged to exist between complete entities, which are so distinctly and separately conceived that each is understood as a being through itself and different from every other.

Then, by bringing in an example, you added: "Thus between the figure and motion of the same body there is a formal distinction, and I can very well understand motion without figure and figure without motion, and each by abstracting from body. Yet I cannot completely understand motion without a thing in which there is motion, nor figure without a thing in which there is figure; nor finally can I imagine motion to be in a thing in which figure cannot be, or figure in a thing incapable of motion."

Third, therefore, you interpreted the example of the objection in this way: "Nor in the same way do I understand Justice without a just one, or Mercy without a merciful one; nor is it permissible to imagine that the same one who is just is not merciful." Finally you said that the same does not hold for mind and body, but more on this later.

What did you say to the second? These are your words. First: "Although perhaps triangle can be taken concretely for a substance having a triangular figure, certainly the property of having the square of the base equal to the squares of the sides is not a substance; and therefore each of these two cannot be understood as a complete thing, nor indeed can it be called a thing in the sense in which I said it is enough that I can understand one thing, namely a complete thing, without another," and so on.

Second: "Although we can clearly and distinctly understand that a triangle in a semicircle is right-angled without noticing that the square of its base is equal to the squares of the sides, nevertheless we cannot so clearly understand a triangle in which the square of the base is equal to the squares of the sides without at the same time noticing that it is right-angled."

Third: "Although the concept of a triangle inscribed in a semicircle can be had such that the equality between the square of the base and the squares of the sides is not contained in it, nevertheless it cannot be had such that no proportion between the square of the base and the squares of the sides is understood to pertain to this triangle. And therefore, so long as it is unknown what that proportion is, nothing can be denied of it except what we clearly understand not to pertain to it; and this can never be understood of the proportion of equality."

From these things I recognize what you could have answered to my example: namely, that those two properties are not two things, or substances, or two complete entities of the sort you understood in the proposition; and therefore that one can indeed be understood without the other very well, or clearly and distinctly, but not completely without the body in which the triangle is. And since there is therefore only a formal distinction between them, it is no wonder if neither can exist without the other, and without the triangle itself or the triangular body.

## Notes

- Gassendi asks for the criterion separating separables from inseparables once divine power is admitted.
- The triangle-property counterexample returns as the key test case.
- Gassendi notes that he avoided a theological justice/mercy example because Descartes wanted to appear to be arguing philosophically.
- Descartes' reply distinguishes formal/modal distinction among incomplete entities from real distinction among complete entities.
- The motion/figure example shows abstraction from body without complete separability.
- The triangle-in-semicircle reply denies that the relevant properties are complete things or substances.
- Gassendi grants the shape of the possible Cartesian answer in order to attack it in the next part.

## Translation Risk

Textual: the passage summarizes Descartes' replies to other objectors. The OCR is serviceable but not clean; quoted material should be checked before citation.

Lexical: `ens completum` and `ens incompletum` are kept as "complete entity" and "incomplete entity" rather than smoothed into "independent/dependent thing," because the exact Cartesian distinction is under pressure.
