## Opening Finding

Gassendi closes the exchange by rejecting Descartes' analogy between disagreement about God and unequal recognition of properties in a triangle. First, Gassendi says he never conceded that God impressed the idea of himself on other people; his argument was conditional: if God impressed it on Descartes, there is no reason not to think he impressed it on others too. Yet histories and voyage reports describe individuals and whole peoples with no knowledge or suspicion of God. If later knowledge arises through hearing or teaching, that points to an adventitious idea, not an innate impressed one. Second, among those who do have some knowledge of God, disagreement concerns the core nature, not only unnoticed properties: corporeal or incorporeal, one or many, infinite or finite, providential or ignorant, passionless or subject to passions. The triangle case is not parallel because everyone who knows a triangle accepts its basic nature as a three-line figure. Finally, if God himself impressed one idea of his one simple nature into all minds, it should be uniform, as the same seal produces the same figure in many waxes.

## Working Translation

Finally, as to your saying that it is strange why the rest of human beings do not understand the same thing about God as you do, since he has impressed the idea of himself in them as much as in you: you say this is the same as if I wondered why, although everyone knows the idea of a triangle, not everyone nevertheless notices equally many things in it, and perhaps some reason falsely about it. But how cleverly, in your usual manner, you avoid the rock, or rather the series of rocks!

First, I did not say what you say, that God impressed the idea of himself on the rest of human beings; that would be to concede completely the thing disputed by you. Rather I said that there is no reason why it should not be believed that God impressed the idea of himself on them just as on you, that is, if indeed he impressed it on you. Next, when I asked why they do not understand the same thing you do, this marked not only those who understand something different from you, but also those who understand nothing. For histories and reports of voyages to the New World recount not only individual people, as there have always been some, but even whole nations, as was also formerly reported concerning some islands, who for centuries past had no knowledge or suspicion of God.

What I wanted to suggest from this was that an idea has not been impressed by God on all human beings; and since there is no greater reason about these than about those, it has been impressed on no one, not even on you. Thus, if you and I and others have an idea of God in the mind, it seems adventitious, and chiefly from hearing, by which name God wished to bless us, who, as Scripture says, has not done so with every nation, nor manifested his judgments to them. But how even the pagans, without an impression of the idea, were once able to arrive at knowledge of God has been explained above, since this too belonged to divine grace and generosity, as did the fact that they had an intellect of such capacity that it could arrive there.

Therefore you have said nothing about those in whom there is no knowledge of God, although this should not have been passed over under the pretext of your comparison. And although perhaps you are going to say about them either that they do not attend sufficiently to themselves, or that they indeed have the idea of God dormant, but one which can be awakened by the slightest hearing or some other occasion, this is divination and conjecture, not that most evident and most certain demonstration promised and expected from you. It is mere divination, I say, especially since whatever knowledge they have afterward is of such a kind that it can equally be obtained even if you suppose no idea was previously possessed. They will experience an idea not necessarily such as yours is, but such as those people suggest who teach them something about God, whoever they are and however they teach it; although these people, advanced to adult age, sound in body and mind, awake and reasoning, do not seem less suited to recognize such an idea, or the God represented by it, than you enclosed in the womb or sunk in lethargy.

As for those who hold different opinions, the case is certainly not the same with their ideas as with the idea of a triangle. For among all those who have knowledge of a triangle, there is no one who does not grasp and admit that the nature of a triangle consists, on your own terms, in being a figure enclosed by three lines, whatever may be the case about properties which someone may not have noticed, or may even not have noticed well enough. But among those who have knowledge of God, is there no one who disagrees about his nature? Look: some make him corporeal, others incorporeal; among the former, some give him a human form, others another form; among the latter, some make him the soul of the world, others want him to be a separate substance. Some think he is one, the rest multiple; these infinite, those finite; these mobile, others immobile; some of immense power, some of limited power; some eternal, some born; some the author of the universe, some the universe or a part of it; some knowing and provident, some ignorant of many things and doing his own business; some free from passions, some subject to passions.

To be brief: describe God's nature however you like; describe your idea of the divine essence as conforming however you like; you will bring forward absolutely nothing that is universally admitted, whose opposite does not appear to most people. Is the case, then, the same with the idea of God as with the idea of a triangle?

I add that the force of the argument lay in this: if God himself impressed the idea of himself on all human beings, he would impress it uniformly, since he himself is one and not multiple, simple and not varied, and since he would impress it for this purpose, that he be recognized, and would not wish to impress something alien as his own, lest he deceive anyone. Thus, just as from the impression of the same seal on a hundred, a thousand, or innumerable waxes, the figure is always the same, and the expression of the seal and of its lineaments is the same, whatever color the wax may be, white, black, red, or another color, so plainly from an impression of the divine essence made on all minds, there ought to be the same idea or image, and as it were figure, expression, and representation of the divine essence and its perfections, whatever diversity you could establish among minds. For I do not think you are going to say further that the body obscures and changes it by adding, subtracting, transposing, and transforming what is incorporeal; or if you say it, you will say it gratuitously. This, I say, was the argument. You, however, thought it a great thing if, hiding under your triangle, you could slip away and silently avoid the difficulty of the consequence.

## Notes

- Gassendi distinguishes the conditional argument from concession: he does not grant universal divine impression, but says Descartes would owe it if he claims it for himself.
- Reports of people and whole nations without God-knowledge count against a universally impressed idea.
- Dormant-idea explanations are dismissed as conjecture, not demonstration.
- Later God-knowledge through hearing or teaching supports an adventitious route.
- The triangle analogy fails because disagreement about God concerns the basic nature, not only unnoticed properties.
- The seal analogy returns: a single divine impression should yield a uniform idea across minds, just as one seal yields the same figure across many waxes.
