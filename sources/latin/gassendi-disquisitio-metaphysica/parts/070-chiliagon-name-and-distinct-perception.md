## Opening Finding

Gassendi replies that Descartes has passed over the real difficulty. The pure/mixed mathematics point has been touched only in passing, and the intellect/imagination objection has not been answered at its hinge. If Descartes grants that idea is image, then the idea of the chiliagon must either represent the figure's angles distinctly, in which case it belongs to imagination too, or fail to represent them, in which case it is not an image. Gassendi accepts a middle result that Descartes resists: the chiliagon is an image and representation, but a confused one, so its perception should be called both intellection and imagination. The demand then shifts to definition: distinct perception must mean knowing or cognitively embracing the parts one by one; otherwise a universal, lumped grasp is not distinct but confused. Descartes' clear understanding of the chiliagon is reduced to clear understanding of the word or definition "figure of a thousand angles," not clear understanding of the figure itself. If that counts as clear understanding of the whole, Gassendi argues, then anything nameable could be said to be clearly understood without being imagined.

## Working Translation

INSTANTIA.

I.

As for what I had touched on in passing concerning material things, insofar as you had said that they were the object of pure mathematics, and I had said that they were the object of mixed, not pure, mathematics: whether you have answered anything, and have not rather, like the dog in Egypt, only touched something, so that by using a referral here you could seem elsewhere to have fully solved the difficulty, let attentive readers judge.

As for the distinction of imagination and intellection, nothing needs to be added to the things proposed in the doubt, which you have skillfully passed over.

For as to what you say, that we understand the chiliagon most distinctly when we cannot imagine it, and that the idea by which it is understood is not corporeal because it does not fall into imagination, I leave that for others to weigh. I only note that here too room has been made for that objection on whose occasion I was asking other things above: how can there be said to be an idea of some thing, and yet the idea not be an image of that thing?

For that an idea is an image, not only the rest of humanity but you too have already admitted. But how is the idea of the chiliagon, which falls into intellect and not into imagination, an image of the chiliagon? For if it distinctly represents the angles of the figure as the idea of the triangle represents them, then it also belongs to imagination, which nevertheless you do not concede. If it does not represent them, then it is not an image, since it belongs to the definition of an image that it represent the thing as it is.

For my part, I do not deny that it is an image and represents the chiliagon. But it seems to me both an image and a confused representation, and therefore I call its perception not only intellection but also imagination. And although I do not deny that the intellect, from other distinctly perceived figures, infers by necessary consequence and demonstrates some things about the chiliagon, still I do not acknowledge that it understands the very figure of the chiliagon any more than it imagines it.

But you want all things to be perceived most distinctly and apart from imagination, although you certainly cannot say in what this most distinct distinction consists. For to perceive distinctly, is it not to know the individual parts one by one, or to embrace them in cognition? Therefore, unless you know the individual parts of the chiliagon, or all its angles one by one, or have embraced them in cognition, by what reasoning do you say that you perceive the chiliagon distinctly?

You will object that I am requiring a perception that is had through imagination. But I am not requiring whether it be had through imagination or through another faculty, provided that it be had. I require only that you define what you call distinct perception and apply it to the chiliagon; I am ready at once to give up if the definition is apt and aptly applied. But unless you do this, and unless from your definition the perception appears to be only universal, and, so to speak, lumped together, not one by one and through the individual parts, how will you prove that the perception is distinct and not rather confused?

Hence it seems plainly that you should not have passed over what had been objected concerning the progress of perception from the triangle through the tetragon, pentagon, hexagon, and the rest, up to the chiliagon or myriagon: namely, that you should explain when imagination ceases and intellection alone remains, and explain that this is not a progress of the same faculty according to more and less, but that there are, as you assert, two plainly different modes of operation. You should also explain how intellection joined with imagination would not be more distinct than when it becomes separate and is by itself.

Certainly, since both imagination and intellection concur in the perception of the triangle, while only intellection concurs in the perception of the chiliagon, of what value at last is the distinctness by which the chiliagon is perceived compared with that by which the triangle is perceived? And since there is so much clarity in the perception of the triangle and so much obscurity in the perception of the chiliagon, and since that clarity is wholly from imagination while this obscurity pertains to intellect alone, how much more valuable will the perception be that occurs by imagination or with imagination, compared with the one that occurs by intellect alone?

You say that, if you want to think about the chiliagon, you understand just as well that it is a figure consisting of a thousand sides as you understand that the triangle is a figure consisting of three. But this is plainly nothing other than understanding what is signified by the name "chiliagon." For you embrace the thing itself in the mind in no other way than by that representation so confused that, if the number of angles designated by the word itself were set aside, it could be of the myriagon just as much as of the chiliagon.

Therefore what you say, that many things can be demonstrated about it most distinctly and clearly, is true only to this extent: that the sense is that those things can be demonstrated of a figure whose name you understand most distinctly and clearly to signify a thousand angles, although you do not understand the very figure itself in yourself except confusedly and obscurely.

And what you say, that in reality you clearly understand the whole at once although you cannot imagine the whole at once, can be nothing other than that you clearly understand the word signifying a figure of a thousand angles, even if you do not clearly understand the figure itself. I ask you, then, what is this, to understand the whole clearly? Is not the whole what consists of parts? And can the whole become clear when its parts are obscure? And can the parts not be obscure when they are not discerned one by one? You understand the whole clearly, although you do not imagine the whole? What remains except for you to say that you clearly understand the whole earth, the whole of nature, the whole world, and finally anything at all that can be named, although you do not imagine that whole? And perhaps indeed you will not deny that you understand all things clearly. But if you want us to believe you, first make us understand what it is to understand clearly.

## Notes

- Gassendi treats Descartes' reply on pure mathematics as a referral rather than a solution.
- The core issue is whether an idea can be an idea of a thing without being an image of that thing.
- If the chiliagon idea distinctly represents the figure's angles, Gassendi says it belongs to imagination; if it does not, it is not an image.
- Gassendi accepts the chiliagon idea as an image, but only as a confused image and representation.
- Distinct perception is tested by part-by-part grasp: knowing individual parts or all angles one by one.
- A universal or lumped perception is not distinct unless Descartes can define distinct perception otherwise and apply it to the chiliagon.
- The triangle-to-chiliagon continuum returns as the missed objection: Descartes has not shown where imagination ceases and intellection alone remains.
- The clarity of the chiliagon is reduced to clarity of name or definition, not clarity of the figure itself.
- If name-based clarity suffices, anything nameable could be called clearly understood without being imagined.
