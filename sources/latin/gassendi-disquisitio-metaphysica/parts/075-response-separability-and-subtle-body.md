## Opening Finding

Descartes answers Gassendi's subtle-body objection by denying the boundary Gassendi imposed. The proof, he says, was never merely about the gross body of limbs; it concerned any body, however subtle. He refuses to reargue what he regards as already demonstrated, and says unsupported assertions require only denial. The key counterclaim is that the Second Meditation already made good that mind can be understood as an existing substance even when nothing bodily is understood to exist, whether wind, fire, vapor, breath, or any other thin and subtle body. The present Meditation, Descartes says, has now demonstrated the real difference. He closes by turning Gassendi's charge back on him: it is Gassendi who confuses the question of what can be understood with the question of what really is.

## Working Translation

RESPONSE.

I do not linger here over the things that you have tediously repeated often: that I have not proved certain things which I nevertheless demonstrated; that I dealt only with gross body, although I dealt with any body, even the most subtle, and such things. For what else should be opposed to assertions of that sort, supported by no reason, except denial?

Yet in passing I would like to know by what argument you prove that I dealt with gross body rather than subtle body. Is it because I said, "I have a body joined to me," and, "it is certain that I am distinct from my body"? I do not see why these words would not suit subtle body just as much as gross body, nor do I think anyone besides you sees it.

Besides, in the Second Meditation I made good that mind can be understood as an existing substance, even if we understand nothing to exist that is wind, or fire, or vapor, or breath, or any other body, however subtle and thin. Whether in truth it was different from every body, I said I was not disputing there; here, however, I have disputed and demonstrated that very thing. But by confusing the question about what can be understood with the question about what really is, you testify that you understood none of these things.

## Notes

- Descartes denies that the proof concerns only the gross body.
- The intended scope is any body, even the most subtle and thin.
- The Second Meditation is treated as sufficient for understanding mind as an existing substance without understanding any body to exist.
- The Sixth Meditation is treated as the place where real distinction from every body has now been demonstrated.
- Descartes reverses Gassendi's accusation: Gassendi is said to confuse intelligibility with real being.
