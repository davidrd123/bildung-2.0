# Part 020: Instantia IX, Fieri/Esse, And Sunlight

Source status: preferred KB / ProQuest page-image witness. The span opens Gassendi's `Instantia` after Dubitatio IX and stops before his critique of Descartes' appeal to the nature of time.

## Opening Finding

Gassendi denies only the force of Descartes' inference, not the conclusion that there is a continuous first-cause influx. He treats Descartes' `secundum fieri` / `secundum esse` distinction as Scholastic renaming of the twofold cause-effect account already introduced by Gassendi, then presses the unproved premise: before God can be assigned as conserving cause in being, Descartes must first demonstrate creation and God as creator. The sunlight analogy fails because the light that seems to persist is not one numerically identical thing but continually new light, like water in a stream. That model cannot explain the conservation of created things that remain the same individual, especially the partless mind.

## Working Translation

Instantia.

Why is it that, when I deny nothing except the force of your reasoning, you attack so invidiously, as if I also denied the conclusion itself? I am not someone who denies the continuous influx of the first cause into us. I do not deny whatever the sounder metaphysicians and theologians think about God. Indeed, when you say all metaphysicians, I could oppose to you not only almost all the ancients, but even some among the Scholastic doctors whom you call all. Rather, I deny only that inference by which you think you have established the consequent.

Thus, when you resume the twofold kind of cause and effect which I brought forward and explained, and call it, with the names changed, according to becoming and according to being, you are indeed acting Scholastically. Nor do I fail to recognize that you are an outstanding Scholastic, although you did not recognize me as such when you judged me to be using not so much philosophical reasons as certain rhetorical arts. But how badly the heads of the Schools would think they were being treated, if they did not reinforce more firmly the things you took from them in this particular part of the Meditation, however much you say that you do not remember whether there were any human beings before you.

You say that the sun is the cause of light, and that God is the cause of created things not only according to becoming but also according to being; and therefore that he must always flow into the effect in the same way, in order to conserve it. But first, have you demonstrated that things have been created, and that God is their creator, or cause in becoming, so that, with those same things now persevering, you may assign God as conserver, or cause in being? For if someone denies that God first creates, he will certainly deny that he creates consequently; and all the more because the first production of a thing can be understood, but the consequent, or continued, production cannot. For when we see a stone, we understand that it could have been made; but we by no means understand that it is being made continuously.

For just as, in order that something be made, it must not be, so, in order that it be made continuously, it must continuously not be. But how can what is found continuously to be be said continuously not to be? Likewise, how can that be continuously made which plainly remains the same and individual, immutably? Therefore there seems to be no cause except in becoming, because a cause is properly that from which a thing is made, and when it is made. So, since what perseveres in being is not made, but is supposed to have been made, it is not considered properly to have a cause in being, but to have had a cause why it existed. Thus when something is sometimes said to have a cause of its being, the cause understood is one that supplied being, or made it, not one that thereafter supplies or makes it.

And although you say that giving being to some thing is equivalent to preventing being from being taken away from it, it is plainly not equivalent physically, but only morally, as they say. For if someone turns aside a weapon directed at another person's head, or blocks a destroyer of buildings, he does not do the things which a father or a craftsman does.

Next, the example of the sun does nothing to prove the conservation of one and the same numerically, or singular and individual, thing. For the sun is not only not the conserver, or cause in being, of light; it even seems, as cause in becoming, to be in a condition far worse than any craftsman. For the light of the sun, which seems to persevere in the air, is not one and the same in number, but continually new and new, in the way that water which seems to persevere in a stream is not the same individual water, but continually different and different. So it is not without reason that the sun is called a fountain of light, insofar as, just as a fountain continually supplies new water, so too the sun supplies new light, whether light is a certain substantial efflux, or a certain emanating quality, or energy, or continuous motion.

Therefore, so far is the sun from conserving light once produced that it rather allows that light to vanish and perish, and substitutes in its place another light distinct in number. Hence it can indeed be called a cause in becoming, but in no way a cause in being. It is therefore like, not a craftsman setting up a house which thereafter stands, but a craftsman, if there were such a one, by whom a house is no sooner built than it collapses, and who at the same moment begins another, and then, as that one collapses again, another that will fall in no very different way.

But here we are dealing with created things that persevere as the same in number and as individuals. And although corporeal things, and especially animate ones, are involved as if in a continuous flux of parts and substitution of new parts, still at least the substance of the parts themselves, or matter, remains incorrupt and without flux or substitution. And the question here is especially about the mind, which you make lacking in parts, and which, I suppose, you are not going to say is made one thing and another continuously, like light, rather than persevering as the same in number and individual, like a stone or house.

## Notes

- Gassendi does not deny first-cause influx or the orthodox conclusion that God exists; he denies Descartes' proof route.
- `Secundum fieri / secundum esse` is treated as Scholastic vocabulary Descartes has borrowed, not as a demonstrated distinction that can carry the proof.
- The cause-in-being claim depends on a prior demonstration of creation. Without that, conservation cannot be equated with continued production.
- Continuous making is unintelligible for one and the same individual thing: what is made must not be, but what is continuously conserved is found continuously to be.
- Preventing destruction is only morally, not physically, equivalent to giving being.
- The sunlight analogy fails on numerical identity. The sun supplies serially new light, as a fountain supplies serially new water; this is not conservation of the same individual light.
- The real pressure point is the partless mind. Descartes cannot plausibly say that a new mind is made at every moment in the way new light is supplied.
