## Opening Finding

Gassendi opens the Fourth Meditation exchange by declining to reargue everything Descartes has just treated as demonstrated, but he warns that earlier concessions should not be turned into prejudice. The first pressure point is error. Descartes excuses the fallible faculty of judgment by saying the finite creature participates in nothingness; Gassendi answers that, even if God did not give humans an infinite faculty, he could have given them a finite judgment that assents only to what it clearly perceives and withholds judgment where it does not know. The second pressure point is final causes. Gassendi grants that final causes might be rejected from physics in another context, but when God is at issue he treats the use and fittedness of organs, especially the valves of the heart, as a primary natural-light route to divine wisdom, providence, power, and existence. Descartes replies by reducing the idea of nothing to a negative idea, assigning final causes to efficient causes in physics, and claiming that the mind would have had the same ideas of God and self without the senses, only purer and clearer.

## Working Translation

As to the fourth Meditation: at the beginning you review what you think has been demonstrated above, and by what things you suppose the way has been made for proceeding further. For my part, so that I do not delay things, I will not immediately insist that you ought to have demonstrated those things more firmly. It will be enough if you remember what was conceded and what was not, so that the matter is not drawn into prejudice.

You reason accordingly that God cannot deceive you; and in order to excuse the fallible faculty, or the one liable to error, which you have from him, you throw the blame onto nothingness. You say that some idea of it faces you, that you participate in it, and you assume yourself to be a middle thing between it and God. Fine reasoning indeed. But, to pass over the fact that it cannot be explained how an idea of nothing is had, or what sort it is, how we participate from nothing, and other things of this sort, I note only that this distinction does not remove the fact that God could have given the human being a faculty of judgment immune from error. For although he had not given an infinite one, he could still have given one of such a kind that it would not assent to error, so that what it did know, it would perceive clearly, and what it did not know, it would not pronounce to be this way rather than another.

When I object this to you, you judge that it is not strange if some things are done by God whose reason you do not understand. And this is right. But it is nevertheless strange that there is in you a true idea representing God as omniscient, omnipotent, and wholly good, and that you nonetheless see some of his works as not wholly complete. Thus, since he at least could have made more perfect things and yet did not make them, this seems to be an argument that he either did not know, or could not, or would not; and at least that he was imperfect in this respect: if, knowing and able, he was unwilling, then he preferred imperfection to perfection.

As for your rejecting the use of final causes from physical consideration, on another occasion perhaps you could have done so rightly. But when God is at issue, one must surely fear that you are rejecting the chief argument by which divine wisdom, providence, power, and therefore existence can be established by natural light. For, to pass over the whole world, the heavens, and its other principal parts, from where or how can you argue better than from the use of parts in plants, animals, humans, and in yourself, or your body, you who bear the likeness of God? We see, surely, that all great men, from anatomical investigation of the human body, not only rise to knowledge of God, but also sing a hymn to him because he has so formed and arranged all parts for their uses that he must be praised altogether for incomparable skill and providence.

You will say that there are physical causes of this sort of form and position, that they should be investigated, and that those people are inept who run back to an end rather than to an agent or to matter. But since no mortal can understand, much less explain, what agent forms and places those valves, which are set at the mouths of the vessels in the chambers of the heart, in the way we observe; what condition it has, or from where it borrows the matter from which it fashions them; how it applies itself to acting; what organs it uses, or how it uses them; what it requires in order to perfect them with that temperament, consistency, cohesion, flexibility, size, figure, and position; since, I say, none of the physicists can perceive and declare these and other things, what prevents him at least from admiring that most excellent use and the ineffable providence which so aptly designed such valves for it? Why should he not be praised if he recognizes from this that some first cause must necessarily be admitted, one which has arranged these and all other things most wisely and most harmoniously for their ends?

You say that it is rashness to investigate God's ends. But although that can be true if the ends meant are those which God himself willed to be hidden, or forbade to be investigated, it is surely not true if the ends are those which he placed as if in the open, which become known without much labor, and which are otherwise of such a kind that great praise is referred to God himself as their author.

Perhaps you will say that the idea of God which is in each person suffices for having a true and genuine knowledge of God and his providence, without looking either to the ends of things or to anything else at all. But not everyone has the good fortune you have, so that they possess that idea from birth so perfectly, or look upon it as an object with such clarity. Therefore those to whom God has not given this great perspicacity should not be envied because, from inspection of works, they can know and glorify the craftsman. Not to mention that this does not prevent that idea from being used, an idea which also seems plainly to be so perfected from cognition of things that, if you want to tell the truth, you owe not a little to this cognition, indeed almost everything.

For I ask you: how far do you think you would have progressed if, from the time you were infused into the body, you had remained there up to now with eyes closed, ears stopped, and had perceived this universe of things, and whatever is outside, by no external sense at all, and meanwhile had spent your life entirely in meditating within yourself and turning and turning your thoughts over again? Speak in good faith, and describe what idea of yourself and of God you think you would have had.

**Response.** What idea of nothing we have, and how we participate in non-being, I have explained sufficiently by calling it negative and by saying that this "nothing" signifies nothing other than that we are not the supreme being, and that very many things are lacking to us. But you look everywhere for knots in a rush. And when you say that I see some works of God as not wholly complete, you plainly attribute to me something I nowhere wrote or thought, but only said that if some things were considered not as they have the character of parts in the world, but as some whole, then they could seem imperfect.

Next, whatever you bring forward for a final cause should be referred to the efficient cause. Thus it is fitting to admire God as maker from the use of parts in plants, animals, and so on, and to know and glorify the craftsman from inspection of his works, but not to divine for what end he made each thing. And although in ethics, where conjectures may often be used, it is sometimes pious to consider what end we can conjecture God proposed to himself in governing the universe, it is certainly inept in physics, where everything must rest on the firmest reasons. Nor can it be imagined that some ends of God are more in the open than others, for all are hidden in the same way in the abyss of his inscrutable wisdom.

Nor should you imagine that no mortal can understand other causes. Each of them is much easier to know than God's end, and the very ones you propose as an example of difficulty, someone thinks he knows. Finally, since you here ask so frankly what ideas I think my mind would have had of God and itself, if, from the time it was infused into the body, it had remained there until now with eyes closed and without any use of the other senses, I answer frankly and candidly that I do not doubt that, provided we suppose it neither hindered by the body in thinking nor helped by it, it would have had the same ideas of God and itself which it now has, except only that it would have had them much purer and clearer. For the senses hinder it in many things, and help it in none in perceiving those ideas. Nothing prevents all human beings from noticing equally that they have those same ideas except that they are too occupied in perceiving the images of corporeal things.

## Notes

- This is the first part in `In Meditationem IV`, shifting from the idea-of-God proof to truth, falsity, error, and divine non-deception.
- Gassendi's objection to the fallible faculty of judgment is not that God had to make it infinite, but that God could have made finite judgment non-assenting where it lacks clear knowledge.
- The idea of nothing is left as a pressure point for the next `Instantia`: Gassendi lets the difficulty stand here, while Descartes calls it a negative idea.
- Final causes become a natural-theological route. Against Descartes' ban on final causes in physics, Gassendi treats visible use, anatomical fittedness, and organic arrangement as evidence of divine providence.
- Descartes' response separates pious ethical conjecture from physical explanation and insists that all divine ends are equally hidden.
- The sense-withdrawal thought experiment directly reverses the Cassirer pressure: Descartes says the mind would have the ideas of God and self more purely without the senses, while Gassendi asks how far a sense-isolated mind could really get.
