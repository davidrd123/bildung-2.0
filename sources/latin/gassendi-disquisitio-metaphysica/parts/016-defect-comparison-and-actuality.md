## Opening Finding

Gassendi takes Descartes' auxiliary proof from defect and comparison and makes it finite. Awareness of doubt, desire, and lack does show that the self is not wholly perfect, but it does not require an idea of infinite being. The comparison can come from the whole universe as more perfect than a part, or from other finite parts and persons that have what one lacks. The same deflation governs actuality: what is actual in an idea is not thereby actual in the represented thing. An architect's idea actually contains a house-plan before the house exists, and the ancient philosophers' idea actually contains infinite worlds without making them actual.

## Working Translation

Dubitatio VIII.

On the subsidiary demonstration from the fact that whoever senses that something is lacking in himself ought to infer that there is a being infinitely more perfect.

You argue elsewhere in this way: "For by what reason would I understand that I doubt, that I desire, that is, that something is lacking to me, and that I am not wholly perfect, if there were in me no idea of a more perfect being, from comparison with which I would recognize my defects?"

But if you doubt about some thing, if you desire some thing, if you recognize that something is lacking to you, what is surprising, since you do not know all things, since you are not all things, since you do not have all things? Do you recognize from this that you are not wholly perfect? This is certainly true, and can be said without envy. Therefore you understand that there is something more perfect than you? Why not, although whatever you desire is not always more perfect than you in every respect. For when you desire bread, the bread is not in every way more perfect than you or than your body, but is more perfect only than that emptiness which is in your stomach.

How, then, do you gather that there is something more perfect than you? Namely insofar as you see the totality of things, which includes you, bread, and the rest. Since the individual parts of the universe have some perfection, and some serve and can benefit others, it is easy to understand that there is more perfection in the whole than in a part; and therefore that you, since you are only a part, ought to recognize something more perfect than you.

In this way, then, there can be in you an idea of a more perfect being, from comparison with which you recognize your defects. Not to mention that other parts can also be more perfect; that you desire what they have; and that, from those same parts compared with yourself, your defects can be recognized. For you could have known a human being who was healthier, stronger, more beautiful, more learned, more moderate, and therefore more perfect. Nor was it difficult for you to conceive his idea, and from comparison with him to understand that there is not in you the degree of health, strength, and other perfections which would be in him.

Afterward you object to yourself: "But perhaps I am something greater than I myself understand, and all those perfections which I attribute to God are in some way potentially in me, even if they have not yet been reduced to act, as can happen if my knowledge is increased more and more to infinity." But you answer: "Granting that my knowledge is increased by degrees, and that many things are in me potentially which are not yet actually there, nevertheless none of these belongs to the idea of God, in which nothing is potential; for this very fact of being increased by degrees is the most certain argument of imperfection."

Now it is indeed true that the things you perceive in the idea are actually in the idea itself. But they are not for that reason actually in the very thing of which it is the idea. An architect, for example, forms for himself an idea of a house, an idea which actually consists of designated walls, floors, roofs, windows, and so on; and nevertheless that house and its parts are not yet actual, but only potential. So that idea of the philosophers actually contains infinite worlds; but you will not say for that reason that those infinite worlds are actual. Therefore whether something is or is not in you potentially, it is enough that your idea or knowledge can be increased and enlarged by degrees; nor should it for that reason be inferred that what is represented or known through it actually exists.

Next, what you acknowledge, that your knowledge will never be infinite, is freely accepted. But you should also acknowledge that there will never be in you a true and genuine idea of God, about whom there always remains more, indeed infinitely more, to be known, than about a human being of whom you have seen only the very tip of a hair. Certainly, even if you have not seen that whole human being, you have nevertheless seen another, from comparison with whom you can make some conjecture about him. But it has never been granted to know anything similar to God and his immensity.

You say that you judge God actually infinite, so that nothing can be added to his perfection. But you are judging what you do not know, and judging only from presumption, as the philosophers judged infinite worlds, infinite principles, and an infinite universe to whose immensity nothing could be added.

As for what you add, that the objective being of the idea does not depend on potential being but on actual being: see how that can be true if what we have just said about the idea of the architect and of the ancient philosophers is true, especially when you remember that ideas of this sort are made up from others which the intellect previously had from causes actually existing.

Responsio.

Here you repeat the same error, when you deny that a true idea of God is had. For although we do not know everything that is in God, nevertheless all those things which we know to be in him are true. But the things you intermix, such as that bread is not more perfect than the one who desires bread, that from the fact that I perceive something to be actually in an idea it is not therefore actually in the thing of which it is the idea, that I judge what I do not know, and similar things, show only that you, O Flesh, rashly wish to attack many things whose sense you do not grasp.

For from the fact that someone desires bread, it is not inferred that bread is more perfect than he is, but only that the one who lacks bread is more imperfect than himself when he does not lack it. And from the fact that something is in an idea, I do not infer that the same thing is in the nature of things, except when no other cause of that idea can be given besides the actually existing thing which it represents. This I have demonstrated to be true not of many worlds, nor of any other thing, but of God alone. Nor do I judge what I do not know, for I brought forward reasons why I judged it, and indeed reasons so firm that you could not attack even the least of them.

## Notes

- Gassendi accepts defect-awareness but denies its infinite conclusion. Lack points to comparison, not immediately to God.
- The whole/part model returns positively: the universe can be more perfect than one of its parts without requiring an idea of infinite perfection.
- Comparison remains finite and empirical. Other humans can disclose defects in health, strength, learning, moderation, and similar perfections.
- `Actu in idea` is separated from `actu in re`. A planned house is actual in the architect's idea without the house actually existing.
- The infinite-worlds example is reused under actuality: the ancient idea can actually contain infinite worlds without those worlds being actual.
- Descartes' response narrows the rule: actual existence follows from idea-content only when no cause can be given except the actually existing represented thing, which he says has been shown only for God.
