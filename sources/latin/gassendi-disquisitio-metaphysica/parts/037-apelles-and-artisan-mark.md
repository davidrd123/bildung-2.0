## Opening Finding

Gassendi says Descartes has shifted the issue. The question was not whether a work can bear a mark of expert workmanship, but whether the idea of God in the mind is an image or likeness of its maker. Apelles' inimitable skill may identify a painting as Apelles' work, but that is not the same as a painting representing Apelles himself. A bat painted by Apelles would show Apelles' skill; it would not be Apelles' image. The same distinction applies to creatures: any creature can signal divine workmanship by its perfection, but the disputed case concerns the mind as image and likeness of God. Gassendi therefore defends the comparison with fabrication rather than parental generation: a human is not of the same nature as God, but can be conformed to God's eternal idea, just as a house is unlike the builder's nature but like the idea conceived by the builder.

## Working Translation

When I asked from where you proved that the idea of God is in us like the mark of an artisan impressed on his work, and what the mode of the impression was, or what the form of that mark was, you objected that this was just as sharp as if, when you detected so much artistry in some panel that you judged it could have been painted by Apelles alone, and said that this inimitable artistry was like a certain mark which Apelles impressed on all his panels so that they might be distinguished from others, I were to ask what the form of that mark was, or what the mode of impression was; and you added that I would certainly seem worthy of laughter rather than of a response. Come then, let us try which one is to be laughed at and who carries the point.

The question concerns an idea, or image, which represents the thing as it is. Let Apelles, then, take up a panel on which you say he is going to make a picture in his own image and likeness, that is, one which will represent Apelles himself, just as he is, most perfectly; and yet on that panel he paints only a bat. I, for my part, will recognize no image or likeness of the sort that had been promised. You will recognize such a likeness that you will judge no egg more like an egg than the bat will be like Apelles. Then, because perhaps you will fear the laughter of those whose spleen is saucy, I think you will protest and reply that you did not mean to say the bat is like Apelles, but that the artistry by which it was completed is a very certain mark by which Apelles may be recognized.

But here the issue is not any mark whatever, but a mark which is an image, which bears likeness, and which would have existed if Apelles had painted himself and not a bat. For Apelles could indeed have been recognized by many other marks, by an inscription, a seal, and others; but he ought to have been recognized by representation of his person.

Do you want to recognize this better? Apelles is being compared here with God, insofar as, just as God created various things, so Apelles painted various panels. Any creature indeed proclaims and marks its author, insofar as it is of such a sort that it could not have been drawn out from nothing, formed, fitted together, and receive the perfection it has by any hand other than God's; just as all panels proclaim and indicate the chief painter, insofar as they have been elaborated with such art that they could not have been completed by any hand other than Apelles'. But the comparison in this place should not have been set up around the perfection of the works, or the expert's mark, which is nothing other than the perfection itself (for in this way each creature and each panel contains a mark of this kind), but around representation by image. Thus, since the question is not about any creature whatever, but about the mind alone, insofar as God created it in his image and likeness, you too, speaking of Apelles, should have assumed not just any panel, but only one he painted in order to display in it his own image and likeness.

You did not do this. Instead, you thought you would play your usual trick and escape the difficulty if you led both the question and the comparison away from their correct state. When you were asked what the mode of impression was, and what the form of the mark, or idea, or image impressed on the work was, you artfully evaded, as if you had been asked about a mark which was not an idea or image, but only indicated the skill of the artisan.

The same applies to what you say a little later: that I prefer to compare creation by God with the operation of a craftsman rather than with generation by a parent, and that this is done without any reason. But everyone sees the best reason: through generation by a parent, the child has the same nature as the father and is like the father according to all his parts, even internal ones; but through creation by God, the human being in no way has the same nature as God, nor is conformed to him according to everything, even the essential attributes. By contrast, just as a house constructed by the operation of a builder is of a nature different from the builder, and yet is plainly like the idea which the builder conceived in his mind, so through creation by God the human being is of a nature different from God, and yet is wholly conformed to the idea which God had from eternity.

This does not stand in the way of what theologians teach elsewhere about the account of God's image expressed in the human being because of the faculties of the soul, by comparison with the trace expressed in other things. But, you say, I did not say there is as great a likeness between us and God as there is between children and parents. And why would you have said it, since there can be only one divine Son, who is the splendor of the Father's glory and the figure of his substance? Nor, you say, is there always no likeness between the work of a craftsman and the craftsman himself, as is clear when a sculptor carves a statue like himself. Here now recognize that this is the sort of example you should have used, not a panel in which there would be no image or likeness, but only a mark of skill. In that case you would have had to answer the questions, and you would not have sought an escape by seizing the mere pretext of laughter.

## Notes

- Gassendi separates `nota peritiae` from `imago` or `similitudo`: a work can indicate its maker's skill without representing the maker's person.
- The bat example exposes the slippage in Descartes' Apelles analogy. A bat painted by Apelles is a mark of Apelles' skill, not an image of Apelles.
- The disputed mark must be an image-mark, because the question concerns the mind as created in God's image and likeness.
- The craftsman comparison is defended against the parental-generation comparison because created humans do not share God's nature as children share a parent's nature.
- The house/builder example lets Gassendi preserve created conformity to God's idea without conceding shared divine nature.
