## Opening Finding

Gassendi finishes the Fourth Meditation exchange by testing the proposed method directly. Descartes' sign that one has surveyed everything and found no ground of doubt fails because both the deceived and the non-deceived survey as much as they can and think nothing has been omitted. Later experience often reveals what earlier survey missed. Nor do the four rules from Descartes' method solve the problem: do not admit what is not clearly perceived, divide difficulties, proceed from simpler to more composite, and enumerate fully. Gassendi says each rule may be familiar, but none gives the needed higher-order recognition: how to know that clarity is nondeceptive, that the division is sufficient, that the simple and complex have been correctly ordered, or that enumeration has omitted nothing.

## Working Translation

Come now: have you, up to this point, handed down this method? You say that you warned that all things must be surveyed, and that when nothing has been found that produces doubt, then the cognition will be so clear and distinct that it is without doubt true; since if it were not true, it would follow that God is a deceiver.

But concerning God, thrice-greatest, I ask more sparingly and more reverently. Since he permits malice to exist in the world, he can permit error to exist too, and that because of ends which you yourself cannot deny are unsearchable.

As to your warning, as was already said above, it is neither new nor proper to you. And the question always remains, since both those who are deceived and those who are not deceived survey all things as much as they can and think all things have been surveyed: by what method can they be helped, so that they are made certain that nothing has been passed over, and that nothing will ever occur which proves that something was passed over? For experience teaches that with the passage of time very many things have occurred, despite a survey by which nothing was thought to have been passed over.

You will say that if something occurs, or can occur, it is a sign that the cognition is not clear and distinct. Let that be so. But meanwhile it is nevertheless thought to be clear and distinct; and after the most diligent survey nothing is thought to have been passed over, and nothing is thought able to occur which would prove that something had been passed over. Therefore a method still remains to be handed down by which it may be recognized that nothing has been passed over and that nothing will occur which proves that something was passed over.

Perhaps you will say that it is contained in those four rules which you handed down in your discourse on method. But the first, which prescribes that nothing should be admitted which is not perceived by clear and distinct cognition, is not new, and also does not prescribe what is required: namely, how we may recognize cognition to be so clear and distinct that it does not deceive us and should be admitted.

The second, which prescribes that any difficulty should be divided into as many parts as are necessary so that it may be solved better, is not new either. Nor does it prescribe what is required: namely, how we may recognize that the parts made are sufficient, and how each particular part, and the whole universally, is to be solved.

The third, which prescribes that one must begin from the simpler and better known things and ascend as if by degrees to knowledge of the more composite, is not new either. Nor does it prescribe what is required: namely, how we may recognize the simpler and better known things, and the order and dependence of the more composite and more obscure things.

The fourth, which prescribes that induction, or enumeration of parts, should be made everywhere so general and careful that we are certain nothing is omitted, is not new either. Nor does it prescribe what is required: namely, how we may recognize that we have so carefully run through and enumerated all things that we are made certain nothing has been omitted. But enough about these matters.

## Notes

- Gassendi refuses to make God's non-deception do all the work. God permits evil, so by inscrutable ends he could also permit error.
- The proposed survey test fails at the level of recognition: deceived and non-deceived subjects can both think they have surveyed everything.
- Later experience is evidence that earlier exhaustive-looking surveys often missed something.
- Gassendi turns Descartes' own four rules into a regress of criteria. Each rule needs a way to recognize that it has been correctly satisfied.
- The clear-and-distinct rule still needs a method for recognizing nondeceptive clarity.
- The division rule still needs a method for recognizing sufficient parts and adequate solution.
- The simple-to-composite rule still needs a method for recognizing the simple, the known, the order, and dependence.
- The enumeration rule still needs a method for recognizing that nothing has been omitted.
