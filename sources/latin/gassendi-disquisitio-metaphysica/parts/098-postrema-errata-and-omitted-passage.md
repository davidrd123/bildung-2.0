## Opening Finding

The final page should be handled as apparatus rather than as a normal objection-response unit. Most of it is a page-and-line correction list. The philosophically relevant element is an omitted passage keyed to page 95, `Dubitatio I`, which reinforces an earlier mind-knowledge point: different cognitive powers or acts correspond to different known attributes, and someone can know one attribute, such as hardness, without knowing another, such as whiteness. The example of one born blind returns the sensory-differentiation logic. The omitted passage then supports the claim that our mind is most known in nature because more attributes are known in it than in any other thing, while a later correction distinguishes the thought of seeing/touching from bodily seeing/touching, as dreams show.

## Working Translation / Handling

This page is a final correction sheet. It first gives corrections to the printer's letter to the reader and then lists many page-and-line corrections through the work. It also notes an omitted passage for page 95, in `Dubitatio I`, to be supplied from the index prefixed to the work.

The omitted passage is legible enough to summarize: one act or power of knowing whiteness is not the same as another act or power of knowing hardness or liquefaction. Someone can know hardness without therefore knowing whiteness, for example someone born blind; and similarly for the rest. From this it is clearly inferred that no thing has as many known attributes as our mind, because many more are known in it than in any other thing.

A later correction clarifies that if only mind is admitted in the self, it does not follow that bodily seeing or touching are operations of mind. The intended point concerns only the thought of seeing and touching, which people experience every day in dreams.
