## Opening Finding

Gassendi concedes the harmless version of the sub-assumption: in God there is not merely as much formal reality as there is objective reality in the idea, but infinitely more, however `realitas formalis` is understood. The contested step is `in solo Deo`. Descartes still has to prove that the idea contains something that cannot be drawn from worldly things perceived, heard, thought, compared, joined, and amplified by the native force of the mind. Gassendi then names the infinite-substance argument a `paralogismus`: Descartes mistakes the negative word `infinitum` for a positive comprehension of infinite amplitude and ignores the same inference when ancient philosophers apply it to an infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles.

## Working Translation

From these things it is clear on what foundation the conclusion of that prosyllogism of yours rests, and with what strength the thing built upon it, as it were, stands. But to add something also about the sub-assumption: when you say that in God there is at minimum as much formal reality as there is objectively in the idea, this must not only be admitted, but it must also be added that there is infinitely more, whatever finally you understand by formal reality.

For if you understand divine substance, what is that which is set before your mind from it in comparison with its immensity, which is not set before you and which escapes both you and every created intellect? If you understand representability, what is that which is represented to your mind from God compared with what is represented to the blessed minds in the empyrean heaven? Indeed, what is that which is represented to the blessed minds compared with what is represented to God himself? If finally you understand anything else, it is plainly the same.

But when you add "in God alone," as if your idea contained something which the very things in the world, perceived by sight or hearing, thought by the mind, and by the mind's native force compared, joined, and amplified, do not contain: that very thing is what is denied to you, what has been denied, what needed to be proved.

I do not repeat what has several times been said in the Doubts about the amplification of perfections which, observed in created things, are referred to God. We remove all imperfections as far as is permitted by the weakness of the intellect, and especially set aside all finitude or limitation, namely by promoting the perfections as much as we can toward infinity. So we may promote some observed magnitude, duration, knowledge, power, happiness, and so on, of some created thing, but without ever attaining with the mind that total and truly infinite magnitude, duration, knowledge, power, happiness, and the rest, or being able to say that we attain anything more than something finite, created, amplified to some degree, and such as it really is not in God, since it is there in a mode inthinkably more eminent.

I add here that that reasoning of yours, expressed in the text in these words, "There would not be in me an idea of infinite substance, since I am finite, unless it proceeded from some substance which was really infinite," made up part, or the chief occasion, of the paralogism. That is, you thought you truly understood the whole infinity, or positive amplitude, of infinite substance, and you were unwilling to notice that the negative word "infinite" deceives you, and that your understanding is not thereby extended into immensity and without any end.

Hence I indeed made clear to you the fallacy of the consequence, by bringing forward the example from the ancient philosophers who thought an infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles. But you almost closed your eyes and dissembled the consequence drawn from that example, so that you would not seem either to recognize or to confess the paralogism.

Your reasoning had been of this sort:

If there is in me an idea of infinite substance, such an idea cannot be placed in me except by infinite substance itself, which therefore must necessarily exist.

But there is in me an idea of infinite substance, namely of infinite, eternal God, and so on.

Therefore such an idea cannot be placed in me except by infinite substance itself, or God, who therefore must necessarily exist.

But I had objected that that consequence of the major proposition is fallacious, since the ancient philosophers could with equal right have reasoned thus:

If there is in us an idea of infinite substance, such an idea cannot be placed in us except by that substance itself, which therefore must necessarily exist.

But there is in us an idea of infinite substance, namely of an infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles.

Therefore such an idea cannot be placed in us except by infinite substance itself, that is, by the infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles, which therefore must necessarily exist.

Should this not have had to be solved, if you did not think it was a paralogism? And indeed you seemed to throw in a little word almost in passing into the following doubt; but that will appear there.

Here I pass over the weakness of that consequence of yours from your own principles. For since you have admitted that there can be false ideas in you and that they do not require a positive cause besides you, why, if there is in you an idea of infinite substance, could it not be false and require no positive cause besides you, just as the idea of the ancient philosophers should be judged to have existed?

And you yourself do indeed anticipate the objection in these words: "Nor can it be said that this idea of God is perhaps materially false and therefore can be from nothing, as I noticed a little earlier about the ideas of heat and cold and similar things. For on the contrary, since it is maximally clear and distinct, and contains more objective reality than any other, none is in itself more true, nor is there any in which less suspicion of falsity is found."

Nevertheless, how do you prove that it is maximally clear and distinct, unless you prove that you are one of the blessed minds which are in heaven? How do you prove that it contains more reality than any other idea that can be taken, collected, and amplified from created things, indeed specifically more than that idea of the ancient philosophers? How do you prove that none is in itself more true, and that there is none in which less suspicion of falsity is found, unless you think the same license is allowed to those ancient philosophers?

You go on: "This idea of a supremely perfect and infinite being is, I say, maximally true. For although perhaps it can be imagined that such a being does not exist, still it cannot be imagined that its idea exhibits nothing real to me, as I said before about the idea of cold." The philosophers would have said the same.

It follows: "It is also maximally clear and distinct; for whatever I clearly and distinctly perceive which is real and true and imports some perfection is wholly contained in it." They too would have said the same, while you meanwhile here assume as a principle, concerning the reality and truth of clear and distinct perception, what will have to be proved by you below from the very thing you now wish to prove. I leave aside that small phrase, "and whatever imports some perfection is wholly contained in it," from which below you will set up a certain special demonstration, as if separated from this one.

## Notes

- Gassendi grants the excess of formal reality in God but refuses the exclusivity claim. `In solo Deo` is the burden, not the bare claim that God exceeds the idea.
- The positive alternative to innateness is now fuller: worldly things are perceived, heard, thought, compared, joined, and amplified by the mind's native force.
- `Infinitum` is again a negative word. The paralogism begins when Descartes mistakes this negative sign for positive comprehension of infinite amplitude.
- The ancient-philosopher parody becomes explicit syllogistic counterproof: the same structure would prove infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles.
- Gassendi uses Descartes' own allowance for false ideas and material falsity against the God-idea proof. If false ideas need no positive external cause, the infinite-substance idea still needs a separate defense.
- The Cartesian circle returns inside the sub-assumption. Clear and distinct perception is again assumed in order to prove the God whose veracity is supposed to secure it.
