## Opening Finding

Descartes defends the innate idea by appealing to indivisible essences. The idea of God does not increase when new divine perfections are noticed; it becomes more distinct and explicit, like the idea of a triangle when new properties are discovered. Against Gassendi's gradual amplification account, Descartes says the idea of God is grasped all at once as infinite being incapable of amplification. He also recasts the artisan's mark through Apelles: the inimitable artistry by which Apelles' paintings are known is a mark without needing a separate mark-shape. Likeness need not match in all respects, and the imperfect thinking power in us can image the perfect thinking power understood in God.

## Working Translation

Responsio.

When you criticize what I said, that nothing can be added to the idea of God and nothing subtracted from it, you do not seem to have attended to what philosophers commonly say: that the essences of things are indivisible. For an idea represents the essence of a thing; if anything is added to it or subtracted from it, it immediately becomes the idea of another thing. Thus Pandora is fashioned, and thus all false gods are fashioned by those who do not rightly conceive the true God. But after the idea of the true God has once been conceived, although new perfections can be discovered in him which had not yet been noticed, his idea is not thereby increased, but only made more distinct and explicit, because all those perfections ought to have been contained in that same idea which was had before, since it is supposed to have been true. Nor is the idea of a triangle increased when various properties in it which were previously unknown are noticed. For, so that you may know, the idea of God is not formed by us successively from amplified perfections of creatures, but all at once from this, that we touch with the mind an infinite being incapable of all amplification.

When you ask from where I prove that the idea of God is in us as the mark of an artisan impressed on his work, and what the mode of impression is, or what the form of that mark is, it is the same as if, observing such artistry in a painting that I judged it could have been painted by Apelles alone, and saying that this inimitable artistry is like a certain mark which Apelles impressed on all his paintings so that they may be recognized from others, you then asked what the form of that mark is, or what the mode of impression is. Surely you would seem worthy of laughter rather than response.

What of when you go on: if it is not something other than the work, then you yourself are the idea; you yourself are nothing other than a mode of thought; you yourself are both the impressed mark and the subject of the impression. Is this equally sharp as if, when I had said that the artistry by which Apelles' paintings are recognized from others is not something different from the paintings themselves, you urged: therefore those paintings are nothing other than artistry, and consist of no matter; therefore they are only a mode of painting, and so on?

What of when, in order to deny that we were made to the image of God, you say, therefore God is human-shaped, and collect those respects in which human nature differs from divine nature? Are you sharper in this than if, in order to deny that certain paintings of Apelles had been made in the likeness of Alexander, you said: therefore Alexander was like a painting, while the paintings were composed of wood and pigments, not of bones and flesh as Alexander was? It is not of the nature of an image that it be the same in all things as the thing of which it is the image, but only that it imitate it in some respects. And it is clear that the most perfect power of thinking which we understand to be in God is represented by that less perfect power which is in us.

But when you prefer to compare God's creation with the operation of a craftsman rather than with the generation of a parent, you do this without any reason. For although those three modes of acting are different in their whole kind, nevertheless it is closer to argue from natural production to divine production than from artificial production. Yet I did not say that there is as much likeness between us and God as there is between children and parents. Nor is there always no likeness between the work of a craftsman and the craftsman himself, as is clear when a sculptor carves a statue like himself.

How badly, and in what bad faith, you report my words when you pretend that I said likeness is perceived by me in the fact that I am an incomplete and dependent thing, when on the contrary I brought those things forward as an argument of dissimilarity, lest I be thought to want to make human beings equal to God. For I said that I perceive not only that in these things I am inferior to God, and meanwhile aspiring toward greater things, but also that those greater things are in God, to which greater things there is something similar in me, since I dare to aspire to them. Finally, when you say it is strange why the rest of human beings do not understand the same thing about God as I do, since he impressed the idea of himself in them as much as in me, it is the same as if you wondered that, although everyone knows the idea of a triangle, nevertheless not everyone notices equally many things in it, and perhaps some reason falsely about it.

## Notes

- Descartes answers gradual correction with indivisible essence: new discoveries make a true idea clearer, not larger.
- The triangle analogy returns: properties can be newly noticed without increasing the idea's essence.
- Against Gassendi's amplification account, Descartes says the idea of God is grasped all at once as infinite being incapable of amplification.
- The artisan mark is reinterpreted as recognizable style or inimitable artistry, not a separate physical mark.
- The Apelles/Alexander comparison narrows image-likeness: an image need imitate only in some respects, not share material or nature.
- Descartes rejects the house/mason comparison as weaker than natural generation for divine production, while still denying parent-child-level likeness.
- Human disagreement about God is answered by the triangle model: shared idea does not entail equal recognition of its properties or correct reasoning about it.
