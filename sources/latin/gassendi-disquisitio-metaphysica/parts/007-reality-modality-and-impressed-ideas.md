## Opening Finding

Gassendi uses `Instantia I` to make Descartes' proof depend on a vocabulary audit. If an idea is a mode of an operation, not a substance, then calling its representational side `realitas objectiva` is, for Gassendi, misleading: `modalitas`, likeness, representation, or representational being would be clearer. He then reconstructs Descartes' causal proof through the image of a seal impressed in wax. The cause contains representability; the impressed figure contains representativeness. The whole Cartesian apparatus is translated back into image-causality, and the large metaphysical proof is made to rest on a small effect: not the world, not substance, not even the mind's operation as a whole, but the objective side of one mental mode.

## Working Translation

Instantia I.

You are mistaken if you think that I objected anything to you out of a desire to contradict, or so that I might seem to contradict. I acted as a friend, because I did not distrust that you would receive it as a friend. That is, I proposed doubts about which I thought you would want to be warned. Hence I weighed the principles, and I wanted to test whether I sufficiently understood the things you had said obscurely. Accordingly, I admitted things that were either true or no obstacle, so that I would stop only at what was false or doubtful. This is how I conducted myself especially in this place.

But you received it as those who quarrel in the schools usually do: you looked harshly at me, who approached as a friend, as though I were an enemy; and when I was obligingly granting something, you wanted to make it appear as though you had extorted it from someone resisting by Herculean force.

Come then, what is it in which you boast that I did not contradict you and concluded with you? Namely, I concluded that there must be at least as much formal reality in the cause of the idea as there is objective reality in the idea. But when I concluded this, I concluded it according to the preceding exposition. And when you say that I had in no way contradicted you earlier, you immediately contradict yourself, since you say that you dissent strongly from what I had premised, that the saying should be understood of material cause rather than efficient cause, and so on. I certainly recognize what you mean, but let me first note this.

When God's existence was to be proved from certain works whose cause would be recognized, you could indeed devise no argument other than one drawn from the dependence of the effect on the cause. Yet you abandoned the royal road, which Scripture indicates and all the wise follow, namely contemplation of the admirable universe. Through some path of dream, deception, and delusion, I do not know what, you withdrew yourself from the universe itself, namely by judging it, and whatever was besides you, to be false, and at the same time whatever might be deduced from it and besides you to be false.

You therefore looked in yourself for some effect, such that if you could recognize no cause of it except God, you would prove from it that God exists. And because the very idea of God presented itself, it seemed good to spend the whole work on it. Yet you saw well enough that you had it from hearing, meditation, and continual conversation with human beings. For even if you had wished, you could not have completely stripped off the ideas of things previously seen, heard, and formed by meditation. Nevertheless, you wished to hold yourself as if you had no ideas at all from things, and as if you were free to inquire whether you had them from somewhere other than things, since you were supposing those things not to exist.

Then, because an idea of this sort seemed to be of marvelous thinness for sustaining such a bulk and such a weight, you judged that it had to be reinforced by that distinction of twofold reality, formal and objective. You so boasted in the invention of this distinction that you judged Troy could be stormed by it alone, as by a wooden horse.

But to say nothing here of the royal road abandoned, or of that delusory path, or of the false supposition that you think you did not receive ideas from things, or of the incapacity to demonstrate that your authority, when reporting something about yourself, should be held as the principle of a demonstration: I ask about your distinction, from which, if you are going to prove anything, you will prove it not to others but to yourself. What, I ask, do you want understood by the name `reality`?

If you had said `entity`, you would have said the same thing, it seems, and would have accommodated a worn term, at least in the schools, to your meaning. But you preferred to taste of nothing too common. What, then, is reality? It is an abstract word, whose concrete is real. But what is it that you call real? Real is a derived word, whose primitive is thing. But what is a thing? How I fear that you have used a word you cannot describe. But let that pass.

Is not a thing, for you, only substance, since whatever else there is is not a thing but a mode of a thing? Therefore only what is substantial is real, and only substantiality will be reality. But now, for you, is an idea a substance? You plainly say that it is not. You plainly say that it is a mode, and indeed you say it is not a mode of substance but of operation, which is itself a mode. Therefore an idea, for you, is not a thing but only a mode. Therefore what is ideal can indeed be called modal, but not real; and ideality itself will indeed be modality, but it will not be able to be called reality. Therefore you do not well distinguish a twofold reality in an idea, which does not have any reality at all.

Will you say of the idea what you said universally of accident, that its whole reality, or modal entity, is drawn from the idea of substance? You should have said from substance. But since an idea can be considered either insofar as it is an idea, or representative of an object, or insofar as it coheres with the mind, or operating substance, together with the operation of which it is a mode, it can indeed be said to have reality in the latter way. Thus at most it will have what you call formal reality. But in the former way it will have none, and so you will not rightly call it objective reality. Will you say that modality can be called reality analogically? But why abandon the proper and fitting name of modality and use the improper and unfitting name of reality?

In one word, since you were not concerned with the idea's formal reality, but only objective reality was at heart, why was it necessary both to pour darkness over things by that distinction and to obtrude that word `objective reality`, in place of which you could have said likeness, representation, representative being, or something similar, so that the matter would be stated more plainly?

To say what I perceive: since you do not want ideas, or species and images of things, to flow into us in such a way that they retain something of formal reality from the substance of those things, and since you therefore do not want the image called an idea to arise, come to be, or exist in the mind by way of painting, or as an image is painted on a panel by the application of colors, it remains that you must think that the image called an idea arises, comes to be, or exists in the mind by way of impression, or as an image is impressed in wax by the application of a seal.

You will immediately say that I am too fleshly, because I conceive mental things after the fashion of corporeal things. But, my good man, I think this is the difference between you and me: although we both conceive in the same way, I explain the mode in good faith and do not try to create the impression that I conceive a subtler mode; you do not acknowledge the same mode, and although you reveal no subtler one, you still want to seem to have a subtler one.

Here, certainly, unless I am badly mistaken, you have no other imagining than that of a figure impressed by a seal in a soft thing. Everything you say conforms to this imagining. Yet you do not want to confess it, or even mention the likeness you have in mind, lest you be too easily convicted of understanding mental things after the fashion of corporeal ones.

Certainly even as regards the idea of God, you do not seem able to have it as an impressed mark, to conceive the impressing cause, or to understand the preservation of the impressed mark, made by an unceasing impression from the impressing cause, in any mode more mental and Seraphic than St. Bonaventure did when he used the likeness of a mark or figure impressed in water by a seal, such that the moment the seal ceases to impress, the mark or figure utterly vanishes.

However it may be, since our whole philosophy, which is our weakness, consists in comparing things, for we cannot derive an unknown thing from another known thing without referring it to something similar and better known, you should not wonder if I, conscious of weakness, philosophize by comparison and judge that the things conceived by me cannot be better explained to others than if I openly set out for them the very comparisons or likenesses that I myself use.

Hence whether I attain your mind or do not attain it, I will at least imagine that you conceive the idea as a figure impressed in wax, and the cause of the idea as the seal by which the figure is impressed. You contend that the cause of the idea transfers none of its actual or formal reality into the idea. But neither does a seal transfer anything into the impressed figure, whatever finally you call actual or formal reality, whether substance, or rather the very representable form itself, such as in the seal is the external form, or figure and likeness representable through the impressed figure.

You say that an idea is of such a nature that from itself it requires no other formal reality besides that which it borrows from the thought of which it is a mode. But such too is the nature of a figure, that it requires no other reality than that of the wax itself, or of the substance of which it is a mode.

You add that the idea's containing this or that objective reality rather than another must surely have this from some cause in which there is at minimum as much formal reality as the idea itself contains objectively. So too, that a figure has this or that likeness or representation rather than another must surely come from some seal in which there is at minimum as much representability as the figure itself contains, if I may speak so, of representativeness.

Of course the seal can contain many other representable things besides, as when it has been applied to the wax only in part. But the figure can have nothing representative of something which it does not have from the seal, and which is not at least in the seal as representable. You add that just as the objective mode of being belongs to ideas from their own nature, so the formal mode of being belongs to the causes of ideas from their nature. Likewise, just as the representative mode of being belongs to the figure from its nature, so the representable mode of being belongs to the seal from its nature.

It follows, you say, that although perhaps one idea can be born from another, nevertheless there is not here a progress to infinity, but one must finally come to some first idea whose cause is like an archetype, in which all the reality is formally contained that is in the idea only objectively. So too, although one figure can be born from another, as when shaped and hardened wax is impressed on softer matter, nevertheless there is not a process to infinity, but one must finally come to some first figure whose cause, or seal from which it was made, is like an archetype, in which all the likeness is contained representably that is in the figure only representatively.

You conclude that by the natural light it is clear to you that ideas in you are like certain images, which indeed can easily fall short of the perfection of the things from which they are drawn, but cannot contain anything greater or more perfect. But by the natural light it is clear that a figure is like an image, which can indeed easily fall short of the perfection of the seal from which it is made, as if the wax is granular, otherwise impure, or not soft enough, but cannot contain, or represent from the seal, anything greater or more perfect.

What, then, is the point of all these things? There is no other end than that we understand what you had in view, or could have had in view, when you thrust upon us that objective reality of yours. If instead it had seemed good to say likeness, representation, or something similar, you would have said the same thing, and you would have said it more clearly.

Let us now see with what apparatus and effort you move the machine. "It is manifest by the natural light," you say, "that there must be at minimum as much in the efficient and total cause as in the effect of that cause. For from where, I ask, could the effect assume its reality except from the cause? And how could the cause give it that reality unless it had it? Hence it follows that neither can something come from nothing, nor can what is more perfect, that is, what contains more reality in itself, come from what is less perfect."

Who would not expect here an effect as great as the whole universe, namely to demonstrate the perfection of the divine cause which placed so many and such great perfections in the universe? Yet for the effect you take not the whole world, not the sun, not the earth, not a human being, not a mind, not any substance, indeed not even the operation of any substance, and so not even of the mind, but only a bare and simple mode of the operation of the mind itself; and not even that whole mode, insofar as it has formal being, but only insofar as it has objective being. You have mixed in the word `reality` so as to prepare the way for attributing that name to the idea taken objectively.

But enough has already been said about the name, and a little later something will be said about the thing itself. Since it would have been enough to say, "There must be at minimum as much in the thing imprinting an image of itself as in the impressed image itself," it nevertheless pleased you to approach what you were aiming at by a longer circuit and from a more universal saying. I did not reject that saying universally, since when it is properly taken it seems very true; but as it was proposed by you, I opposed difficulties, which you, with your usual skill, avoided.

You had said that it is manifest by the natural light that there is at minimum as much in the efficient cause as in the effect. To show that this is not manifest, I objected that you should have spoken of material cause rather than efficient cause, and I explained the matter by example in the effects of art and nature. You then said nothing to this, but said only that you strongly dissent from me because the perfection of form can never be understood to preexist in the material cause, but only in the efficient cause.

But, to pass over how steadily you prepare refuges for yourself from the very manner of speaking, since you do not say `form`, about which, as part of the effect, whether substance, accident, mode, or whatever it finally is, one especially had to speak, but `perfection of form`, a phrase from which you can invent anything at all: to pass this over, should you not have added an answer, if not to other points, at least to the objection about the production of insects by the sun?

Should you not have explained how either the form or the soul of a frog preexists in the sun? And if not the soul, then at least the perfection of this soul: not an extrinsic perfection, of course, lest you flee to something which, since it is outside the soul, is nothing of the soul, so far is it from being its perfection, but an entirely intrinsic perfection, one which is essentially the soul itself, or at least something not distinguished from it by more than a modal distinction and by consideration.

Should you not have explained, I say, how this is in the sun rather than in matter or material principles, such as I do not deny there are, for example corpuscles of light continually arriving from the sun, which by their various mixing, addition, subtraction, transposition, and so on, seem able to display now this form, now that, now another, or that perfection? This is the same way that when a statue, a house, or anything else is made by art, the whole form and perfection of it is understood to have preexisted in matter, insofar as it was necessary only to subtract, add, transform, and so on, so that there is plainly nothing in the effect which could not previously have been shown in the matter itself.

I add that I admitted, out of abundance, the common way of speaking by which matter is called a cause, since, properly speaking, only the efficient cause is a cause. Thus cause and efficient are synonyms, and each word signifies the principle from which, while matter is nothing other than matter and signifies the principle out of which. I note this to suggest how easily we are deceived about things themselves unless we have been sufficiently attentive to words.

In this place, certainly, everyone says that the effect preexists in the cause. And yet, first, the effect does not preexist, because if it preexisted, it would exist, although it is supposed not to exist. Second, it neither preexists nor ever exists in the cause; it only exists, or comes to be, from the cause, and at the time when it comes to be. Beforehand, it is said only to have been able to come to be; afterward, to have been made by the cause. Third, it does not preexist or exist even in the matter itself, but only exists, or comes to be, from matter, and at the time when it exists or comes to be. Beforehand or afterward, it is only said that it could have come to be and was made from matter. Fourth, therefore, one cannot say that something of the effect preexisted in the matter, but rather that it was the matter itself, since matter was not the subject of itself, but its own entity.

I add again that I did not say without reason that although sometimes the effect has something from the matter of the efficient cause, it depends on that cause in this respect not as on an efficient cause but as on a material principle. For I meant that when the efficient cause is such that it cannot cut off anything from itself, then plainly nothing that is in the effect preexisted in the efficient cause. In this way the effect of art stands with respect to the craftsman, and the effect of nature must stand with respect to an immaterial cause.

From this it follows that such effects can be said, above others, to be not so much in the cause as from the cause. And if the consideration is transferred to the time before production, they should be said not so much to have been previously in the cause as to have been able to be, or to come to be, from the cause.

Certainly, to say that the effect was in the active power of the cause can be approved only in the sense that the productive power of it was in the cause, while otherwise nothing of the effect was either in the power of the cause or in the cause. And what is said to have been there formally or eminently must be taken in this sense, and the interpretation set out in the Dubitatio must be added.

To say also that it was in the intellect of the cause because of an idea can be admitted only in this sense: that the idea for making it was in the intellect of the cause. Otherwise the effect is neither the idea itself nor anything at all that existed in the intellect, but something plainly outside it.

However it may be, see how I do not object to concluding exactly the same thing that you do, in repeated form:

There must be at minimum as much formal reality, or representable likeness, in the cause, or in the thing impressing an image of itself, for example in the seal, as there is objective reality, or representative likeness, in its effect, or image, for example in the figure of wax impressed by the seal.

But there is a cause impressing in the mind an idea, or image, of itself; and the idea itself, or image impressed in the mind, is the effect of that cause.

Therefore there must be at minimum as much formal reality, or representable likeness, in the cause of the idea, as there is objective reality, or representative likeness, in the idea itself.

## Notes

- Gassendi turns `realitas` into an etymological and ontological problem. If `reale` comes from `res`, and if only substance is strictly `res`, then an idea as a mode cannot strictly have reality.
- The suggested replacement terms are not decorative. `Modalitas`, `similitudo`, `repraesentatio`, and `esse repraesentativum` are Gassendi's attempt to make the Cartesian term say what he thinks it actually means.
- The seal/wax analogy is the strongest positive model so far for idea-causality. Ideas are impressed figures; causes are seals; objective reality is representativeness; formal reality is representability.
- Gassendi is not simply rejecting analogy. He openly says all human philosophy works through comparisons because unknown things are referred to known likenesses. The criticism is that Descartes uses corporeal-image models while refusing to own them.
- The proof of God is again rerouted toward the universe. Gassendi says Descartes abandoned the royal road of contemplating the world and instead made the proof depend on a very thin effect: the objective side of a mental mode.
- The final restatement concedes the Cartesian structure only after translating it into representational terms. This keeps the formal/objective distinction under protest rather than accepting it as Descartes means it.
