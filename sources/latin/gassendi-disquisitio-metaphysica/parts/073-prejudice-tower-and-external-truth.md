## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' charge of prejudice by clarifying his own argumentative role and epistemic posture. He was not presenting dogmas against Descartes' dogmas, but friendly objections to the Meditations; in natural things he tries to shed prejudices without leaping from one prejudice into another. His positive standard is not possession of truth itself, which he says he is unequal to extracting, but disciplined pursuit of what seems true or more truthlike, with readiness to abandon even probability when a stronger one appears. He then reconstructs Descartes' implied claim as a monopoly: only the one who withdraws mind from the senses by the Second Meditation route can shed prejudices, become certain, know whether bodies exist, and know how sense perception works. Against that, Gassendi returns to the tower. Admitting that we are sometimes deceived does not force the claim that we are always deceived. Near inspection, touch, measured faces, and counted angles can remove the distance-based cause of error and yield sufficient evidence that the tower is square. Dream, deceiving God, and evil-genius arguments do not defeat this manifest case; for Gassendi they are unsupported conjectures or misplaced supernatural worries. If Descartes reaches the same certainty only after immense labors, Gassendi counts himself happier for having it from the start. The close returns the external-truth challenge: if Descartes says Gassendi brought nothing true about things outside us, what truer thing has Descartes brought?

## Working Translation

INSTANTIA.

How easily you conceive anger! How easily you impose on me! I had expected this least of all, and I was amazed after I said that you had very well set out the enumeration of the things that had become known through the senses, and so on, and added only that a difficulty remained, which I set out in my usual way. I was amazed, I say, that you had occasion to rise up so unexpectedly. But evidently it is not enough to commend something in you unless at the same time everything is raised all the way to heaven; and it is sacrilege if someone disapproves what has once been approved by you.

You say that here I have shown that I rely only on prejudices and never put them off? Yet neither here nor in all the doubts have I shown either that I rely or do not rely on prejudices, or that I put them off or do not put them off, since I was not engaged in giving you an account of what I thought or did not think. I was only engaged in this: because, with a friend interceding, you had wanted to know whether anything could be objected against your Meditations, I would set out amicably the things that seemed to me could be objected.

But you, not attending to the role I was playing, think that I wanted nothing other than to oppose my doctrines to yours, as if in argument no one ever objects except what he really and with conviction thinks. If you knew me, you would know that in natural matters I am someone who not only tries to put off prejudices, but also carefully guards against coming out of one only to cast himself, in your fashion, into another.

For, as far as the powers of my mind allow, I weigh everything so that I may try to detect what in such darkness seems like the true, or, among many likenesses, what appears more like the true; since both the custom of life and the mind, prone to assent, seem not to allow us to avoid pursuing at least probability.

But since I never pronounce on truth itself, feeling myself unequal to extracting it, I am certainly someone who holds not even any probability so fixed that I am not most ready to abandon it if another appears that seems of greater moment. Hence it happens that if I am ever asked to give an opinion about some matter, I either refuse, or testify that I do not go beyond probability; or if I do not do this, at least I imply enough that those who know me think it has been testified.

But these things are by the way, so that you may understand what role I hold for myself. Now, to resume the role that you wanted me to assume by objecting, I first recognize that you always persist in that reasoning of yours:

Only the one who withdraws the mind from the senses as one should, that is, who counts all things perceived by the senses as false, who thinks he is dreaming, who believes he is deceived by God and mocked by a malicious Genius, so that he next notices that he exists, that he is precisely a thinking thing, that he has the idea of God, that God therefore exists, and that God, since he is the author of clear and distinct cognition, is not deceptive; only that one, I say, puts off prejudices, becomes certain of some thing, knows whether there is some body in the world, and recognizes whether and how something is perceived by sense.

But I alone have accomplished those things. For although many have already said that the mind must be withdrawn from the senses in order to understand metaphysical things, nevertheless no one, so far as I know, had shown by what method this can be done. But the true way, and in my judgment the only way to accomplish this, is contained in my Second Meditation, and so on.

Therefore I alone have put off prejudices; I become certain of some thing; I know whether there is some body in the world; I recognize whether and how something is perceived by sense.

Next I ask by what argument you are led to say that I have shown here that I rely only on prejudices and never put them off. You say that I manifestly show this when I want us to suspect no falsity in those things in which we have never detected falsity. And yet where do I want this? Is not the question about the knowledge that is acquired by the senses? But did I assert or in any way suppose that we have never detected falsity in this knowledge? And were these not my words? Nevertheless, wherever deception is, it cannot be denied that there is some deception; the only difficulty is whether there is always some deception, so that the truth of some thing perceived by the senses can never be established. What, then, are you attributing to me?

But I said that it seems altogether established that when we look at and touch a tower nearby, we are certain that it is square, though when farther away we had occasion to judge it round, or at least to doubt whether it was square, round, or of another figure. I did say this, certainly. But did I therefore say that we have never detected error in sense? I said that we are certain it is square when we look at and touch it nearby. I did indeed say this. And what forbade it? Since I admitted that we are sometimes deceived, was it not permitted to admit that sometimes we are not deceived? Or rather should it have been asserted that we are always deceived? Is that not just as defective as asserting that we are never deceived?

You press that I have no reason to think that all the things in which error can occur have already long ago been noticed by me, and that it can easily be proved that I am sometimes deceived in the things that I thus admit to be certain. But I neither thought nor said that all things, or all in every case, had been noticed by me. Rather I supposed this: that there are cases in which the evidence is so great that I notice plainly nothing that would make the judgment uncertain.

For example, when I look at a tower nearby, and discern its four faces all around, partly with the naked eye and partly with a rule applied, and count the angles, I think enough has been noticed for me to be persuaded that it is square rather than round. But what certainty, you will say, is there that all the things on account of which there can be error have been noticed? Rather, I say, what certainty can there be that all things have not been noticed, since the fact that I am sometimes deceived when I am far away is removed by nearness?

But can it easily be proved that I am sometimes deceived when I admit as certain that a tower seen and touched in this way is square? Go on then, prove it. It is possible that I am dreaming: this, of course, is one of your arguments. But if I were dreaming, neither you nor any mortal could prove to me that I am deceived. On the other hand, I have so much evidence that I am not sleeping that I would deservedly be considered foolish if I required some easier proof, and did not leave it to you alone to dream, if perhaps you think you are sleeping when you bring these things forward.

It is possible that God deceives me: for this is your other argument. But besides the fact that the mind shudders to hear it, as certain as I am that God exists, so certain am I that he is not a deceiver. Therefore, just as it ought not to come into mind, so it does not even come into mind that I can be deceived by God so as to be made uncertain about the shape of the tower.

It is possible that I am mocked by a malicious Genius: for this is the third argument. But since this is mere soothsaying, against which so much evidence fights, and since, if a Demon mocks us, that happens only in matters that pertain to the supernatural state of the soul, I am far from casting away so manifest a cognition on account of a groundless conjecture.

To speak briefly, these arguments by which you think it can easily be proved that I am sometimes deceived in those things that I thus admit to be certain seem to me such that to propose them is nothing other, according to that proverb of yours, than to look for a knot in a rush. Certainly, if you yourself are still moved by them, so that when you are near a tower you are uncertain about its shape, be however you like.

If you say that you are indeed certain, but only because you solved the arguments after you noticed that you are, that you are a thinking thing, that you have the idea of God, and so on, then I, who before those arguments of yours were proposed did not doubt that I am, that I think, that I have the idea of God, and so on, seem to myself happier in this respect: since we both have the same certainty, I have it spontaneously and from the beginning, while you have it only after immense labors.

I had added that at least it is not permitted to doubt that things appear such as they appear. And you objected that I return to the path because you asserted this very thing in the Second Meditation. But to me, at least, it seems that I had no need to return to the path, since I had not left the path. I understand the path, however, not as the one you indicate from your Second Meditation, but the one I had already worn down in the first doubt on the Third Meditation, and which you yourself feared to enter, both because of the reason brought forward there and perhaps also so that you would not seem to imitate the Skeptics, who nevertheless hand down and follow a more perfect method for putting off prejudice.

As for your adding that the question was about the truth of things placed outside us, and that I brought forward nothing true about it: please tell me, what truer thing have you brought forward about that same matter?

## Notes

- Gassendi rejects Descartes' prejudice charge by distinguishing his role as friendly objector from personal doctrinal assertion.
- His methodological self-description is probabilistic: he searches for what seems true or more truthlike in darkness, without claiming truth itself.
- Probability remains revisable; he is ready to abandon any probability when a stronger one appears.
- Gassendi reconstructs Descartes' route as an exclusive claim that only the Second Meditation procedure removes prejudices and grounds sensory knowledge.
- He denies saying that sensory falsity has never been detected; he only denies that it follows that sense always deceives.
- The tower case becomes evidence under correction: nearness, touch, measured faces, and counted angles remove the distance-based cause of error.
- Dreaming, a deceiving God, and malicious Genius are rejected as unable to overturn manifest tower evidence.
- If Descartes needs immense labors to regain the same certainty, Gassendi claims the more economical route.
- The appearance-as-appearance path is claimed by Gassendi as already his, not as a return to Descartes' Second Meditation.
- The final question reverses Descartes' external-truth charge: what has Descartes brought that is truer?

## Translation Risk

Lexical/contextual: `verisimilitudo` is rendered as "probability" in the methodological paragraphs. It also means truthlikeness, and here both senses matter: Gassendi is not doing mathematical probability but disciplined adherence to what appears more like the true.
