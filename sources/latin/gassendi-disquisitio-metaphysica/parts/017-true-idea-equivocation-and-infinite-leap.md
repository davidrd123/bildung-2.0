## Opening Finding

Gassendi says Descartes' response turns on equivocation. A "true idea" can mean a genuine and adequate idea representing God as he is, or only an idea representing something true that we may affirm of God according to our measure. Gassendi says the dispute concerns the first sense, while Descartes answers in the second. The rest of the `Instantia` keeps forcing this distinction: finite comparison can yield an idea of something more perfect, but not an idea of infinite being; what is actual in the idea does not become actual in nature; and Descartes' appeal to objective being again collapses into petition of principle or circle.

## Working Translation

Instantia.

How excellently you play by equivocation. For since the phrase "true idea" can be taken for a genuine and adequate idea, that is, one representing God as he is and as great as he is in himself, or for one representing something true which it is permitted for us to affirm of God according to the measure of our knowledge, it is clear that we are dealing, and that the difficulty between us concerns only, a true idea according to the first signification. But you answer about a true idea taken according to the second, and you invent and say that I repeat an error from which I was very far from even dreaming.

How subtly, too, you then slip away and conceal the force of the argument, or the way in which I objected that there can be in you an idea of a being more perfect than you without there therefore being in you an idea of infinite being. For when you wanted to show that the perception of the infinite is in some way prior in you to the perception of the finite, that is, of God prior to yourself, you had added as cause those words, "For by what reason would I understand that I doubt," and so on. From these you seemed to have reasoned in this way:

If there is in me an idea of a more perfect being, from comparison with which I understand my defects, then there is in me an idea of infinite being.

But there is in me an idea of a more perfect being, from comparison with which I understand my defects, since I understand that I doubt, that I desire, that is, that something is lacking to me and that I am not wholly perfect.

Therefore there is in me an idea of infinite being.

I admitted that you really doubt, that you desire, that something is lacking to you, that you are not wholly perfect, that there is something more perfect than you, and that you understand this. And because, on account of that consequence, "There is in me an idea of a more perfect being; therefore there is an idea of infinite being," and accordingly, "There is some being more perfect than I; therefore that being is infinite," you seemed to be making far too extravagant a transition to another genus, as if there were no intermediate degrees between you and infinite being, and as if you were one step below Jove, I therefore showed that you can understand a being more perfect than you without its being infinite.

First, this can be done by considering the universe, of which you are only a part, since it is otherwise very well known that the whole is more perfect than the part. Then, by turning the mind to another part of the universe in which perfections lacking in you are found, such as a human being healthier, stronger, more beautiful, more learned, more moderate, and so on.

When you ought to have defended your consequence, and sensed that you could not, you decided that ink had to be poured out, saying that I intermix certain things which show that, although I am flesh, I nevertheless want to attack many things whose sense I do not grasp. At this point I cannot sniff out what moved your bile, unless perhaps it was that, when you had said that you doubt, I took it as said seriously and admitted that you both do not know all things and could have known a human being who was more learned, more moderate, and in other respects more perfect than you. If this is not true, rejoice in your good fortune.

It is strange, since I had inserted those words, "although whatever you desire is not always more perfect than you in every respect; for when you desire bread," and so on, only to forestall a quibble which I had sensed could be seized upon. Yet you stuck only on that. For look, you found something to plead: that from someone's desiring bread, it is not inferred that the bread is more perfect than he is, but only that the person who lacks bread is more imperfect than himself when he does not lack it. Yet even here you say nothing which I did not really say. I expressly said that bread is not more perfect than you when you desire it; and when you say that you, lacking bread, are more imperfect than you not lacking or having bread, you say concretely nothing other than what I said abstractly, by comparing bread with emptiness or need of bread.

The main point is that no transition to another genus should have been made; or else it should have been shown coherently how, from the fact that you are now more imperfect without bread and now more perfect with bread, it follows that there is in you an idea of an infinitely perfect being. For you can indeed gather that if someone does not suffer hunger, he is more perfect than you; if someone does not doubt about things doubtful to you, he is more perfect than you; if someone has what you lack, he is more perfect than you. And you can imagine for yourself the idea of a human being who is not pressed by hunger as you are, who does not hesitate about uncertain things, and who feels that nothing is lacking to him. But you cannot gather that such an idea is the idea of infinite or infinitely perfect being; nor is it more permissible to infer that the human being represented by that idea really exists than to infer that a giant, chimera, and other such things whose ideas you can imagine really exist.

Further, because you had reasoned in this way:

That thing exists in whose idea nothing is potential, but everything actual.

But in the idea of God nothing is potential, but everything actual.

Therefore God exists.

I objected that you reason badly from things which are actual in the idea to things which are actual in the nature of things. And because you could distinguish the actual and potential in such a way that whatever was represented by the idea was indeed actually represented, but was understood either as existing only potentially or as also actually existing in the nature of things, I therefore brought forward a double example to show that the consequence has no force in either way. One was the architect, whose idea actually represents a house as existing only potentially. The other was the ancient philosophers, whose idea actually represented an infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles as actually existing.

But you certainly sensed the force. For in truth, if from the fact that the idea represents to you infinite being, in which nothing is potential but everything actual, you infer that it exists, nothing prevents those philosophers, from the fact that their idea represents to them an infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles, from inferring that an infinite universe, infinite worlds, and infinite principles exist.

What, then, did you answer to this? Namely this: "From the fact that something is in an idea, I do not infer that the same thing is in the nature of things, except when no other cause of that idea can be given besides the actually existing thing which it represents; and this I have demonstrated to be true not of many worlds nor of any other thing except God alone."

But first, it is not true that you have demonstrated that no other thing can be the cause of the idea you have of an infinite thing besides the actually existing thing itself, as has been declared up to this point. Nor is it true that you have demonstrated that what you concluded about God cannot be concluded about many worlds. For the whole reason why you proved that God exists was his infinity represented by your idea, with that principle assumed, that there is at minimum as much formal reality in the cause of the idea as there is objective reality in the idea. But the same reason fights for the ancient philosophers: namely the infinity of the universe, worlds, and principles, with the same principle assumed and the same form of argument.

Does the evidence of the paralogism not therefore stand from this? And it is otherwise great evidence from the manifest petition of principle, or circle, by which you prove that infinite being actually exists because it is represented in the idea as actual, and on the other hand that infinite being is represented in the idea as actual because it actually exists.

Furthermore, when you had reasoned in this way:

The one whose knowledge cannot be so increased that it becomes actually infinite and not always capable of increase can nevertheless judge that there is a being so actually infinite that nothing can be added to its perfection.

But my knowledge cannot be so increased that it becomes actually infinite and not always capable of increase.

Therefore I can nevertheless judge that there is a being so actually infinite that nothing can be added to its perfection.

I had suggested that you should recognize that these things fight with themselves. For since knowledge of some object always becomes greater the more is known about the object, it follows that if knowledge cannot so increase that it becomes infinite, then infinite things about the object also cannot be known. Thus the one whose knowledge it is cannot judge that the object is actually infinite. And since he knows only finite things, and because the capacity for increase means many unknown things always remain, he cannot judge what the object lacks or does not lack; so far is he from being able to judge that nothing is lacking to it. To be able to do that, he would have to know everything that is in it, and that it is repugnant for there to be something which is not in it.

I had suggested, I say, that for this reason you ought to recognize that there will never be in you a true and genuine idea of God, since about him there would always remain more, indeed infinitely more, to be known than about a human being whose very tip of a hair you had seen.

You said nothing to this. But because I had added that, when you judge God actually infinite in such a way that nothing can be added to his perfection, you judge what you do not know and judge only from presumption, as the philosophers judged infinite worlds, and so on, you received this angrily in this way: "Nor do I judge what I do not know; for I brought forward reasons why I judged it, and indeed reasons so firm that you could not attack even the least of them." How true that is, and how far it satisfies, readers may judge.

I only warn that when you had finally reasoned in this way:

What is in the objective being of an idea actually exists, because the objective being of an idea cannot be produced by merely potential being, which properly speaking is nothing, but only by actual or formal being.

But in the objective being of my idea there is God, or infinite being.

Therefore God, or infinite being, actually exists.

I had also suggested that you see how much firmness that principle, "the objective being of an idea," and so on, had on account of what I had already objected about the idea of the architect and of the ancient philosophers. For they would necessarily infer that infinite worlds exist if the objective being of the idea they had of them could be produced not only from their potential being but only from actual, or formal, being. I warn, I say, that I had suggested this, and although something undoubtedly should have been answered, nevertheless you answered nothing.

## Notes

- `Vera idea` now explicitly splits into two senses: genuine/adequate representation of God as he is, versus a limited true predication according to the measure of human cognition.
- Gassendi identifies Descartes' move from more-perfect to infinite as a transition to another genus. Finite comparative superiority does not erase intermediate degrees.
- The bread example is clarified: it was not meant to make bread more perfect than a person, but to block the move from local lack to infinite perfection.
- `Actu in idea / actu in re` is extended. Representation can be actual while the represented thing is understood as merely potential, as with the architect's house.
- The infinite-worlds parody returns under objective being: if objective being must come from actual/formal being, ancient philosophers can infer actual infinite worlds from their idea.
- Gassendi restates the circle: Descartes proves actual infinite being from its representation as actual, and its representation as actual from its actual existence.
