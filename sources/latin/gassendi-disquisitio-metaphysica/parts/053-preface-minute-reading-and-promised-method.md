## Opening Finding

Gassendi turns Descartes' appeal to the preface back against him. He remembers the preface, but says it raised a vain hope: nothing in the Meditations delivered the promised method. He also rejects Descartes' complaint against piecemeal examination. If the fruit of the Meditations depends on both the whole body and the individual members, then the individual members must be examined, especially when Mersenne asked for a judgment on them. The complaint about brevity is sharpened: Gassendi did not object that Descartes failed to compress many useful things into few words, but that he stretched a few useless things into many words. Finally he asks where the method was actually handed down, whether in the earlier removal of prejudices or in the Fourth Meditation's conclusion that clear and distinct perception is true because it has God as author. That question sets up the next part's circle charge.

## Working Translation

INSTANTIA.

I certainly remember reading that little preface of yours. But I also remember that I was fed with a hope, emptier than can be said, since afterward I recognized nothing in all the Meditations like what you had made me hope for there.

I also remember that you disliked those who would weigh the Meditations piece by piece. But I did not therefore think one should abstain from weighing them piece by piece, lest, if some headings were passed over, I could suspect that they were the very ones whose not having been understood kept me from gathering the expected fruit.

For as to having only certain headings selected according to your wishes, and disputed in the manner in which a few theses are usually disputed in the Schools from a large number, this did not seem enough to me for rendering the judgment Mersenne wanted to have and promised would be very agreeable to you. And he at least acted in good faith, since, as I now see, he had already read these words in the responses you sent to him: "But because I know how difficult it will be even for those who attend and seriously seek the truth to look at the whole body of my Meditations and at the same time distinguish their individual members, which two things together I think must be done if the full fruit is to be taken from them," and so on.

But what would you like me to say about you, since you do not approve of those who try to distinguish the individual members, and since, when difficulties occur in the individual members, they set those difficulties before you frankly, privately, and thinking they are doing something agreeable to you?

For when you say that they do not care to comprehend the sequence and connection of your reasons, I know, as far as I am concerned, that this is false. If they are not sufficiently comprehended otherwise, the weakness of my intellect may indeed be the cause; but the fault seems also to be yours, since you have made the matter not only very difficult, as you say, but even most difficult, and, so to speak, almost a cross, even for those attending most carefully and seeking the truth most eagerly. Every reader I have encountered has testified to this, although the matter could no doubt have been explained with the easiest ease, as far as you knew and tried to say.

Hence, when you add that you do not at all trust that those who labor so little to strip away prejudices that they complain because you did not speak simply and in few words will easily perceive the method you handed down, since this complaint still touches and troubles you, I refer back to what was said about it in its own place around the First Meditation. Here I add only this: the complaint was instituted not because you did not contract many useful things into few words, but because you drew out a few useless things into many words.

So even now, hand down the method as copiously as you wish, provided only that it is useful and agrees with your promise; someone at least will kiss it.

You are offended because I said that we are still waiting for it. But why should you not bear it, if it is true that you have not yet handed it down? For when you say that it was handed down by you accurately enough, saying so is certainly not sufficient unless you declare in what place or when this was provided.

Do you indicate a place when you say that those who do not labor to strip away prejudices will not perceive it, and so on? Certainly around the Third Meditation you also answered that this was accurately provided by you where you first removed all prejudices, and so on. But how little it was provided by you in that place was made manifest by pressing the point.

Or, because of those words "as has already been said," do you indicate what was concluded in this Fourth Meditation? Certainly you seem to have wanted to embrace the principal aim, and something like an epitome of the method, in these words: "Whenever I so contain the will in rendering judgments that it extends only to those things which are clearly and distinctly exhibited to it by the intellect, it plainly cannot happen that I err; because every clear and distinct perception is something, and therefore cannot be from nothing, but necessarily has God as author; God, I say, that supremely perfect being whom it is repugnant to suppose deceptive. Therefore it is without doubt true."

## Notes

- Gassendi says the preface promised a method that the Meditations did not deliver.
- He defends minute examination because Descartes himself requires grasping both the whole body of the Meditations and their individual members.
- Mersenne's request matters procedurally: Gassendi is rendering the kind of judgment he understood Mersenne to have solicited.
- The complaint about Descartes' style is not a demand for oversimplified brevity. It is the sharper charge that a few useless matters have been expanded into many words.
- Gassendi asks for a concrete location of the method: where and when was it actually handed down?
- The Fourth Meditation formula is identified as a possible "epitome" of the method, but only so it can be tested next for circularity.
