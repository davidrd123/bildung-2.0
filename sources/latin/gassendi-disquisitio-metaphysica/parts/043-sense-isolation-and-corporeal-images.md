## Opening Finding

Gassendi closes the first Fourth Meditation `Instantia` by answering Descartes' claim that a mind deprived of all sense-use would still have the same ideas of God and self, only purer and clearer. Gassendi says this is consistent with Descartes' earlier claims about the mind in the womb or in lethargy, but it begs the question. Descartes assumes precisely what is disputed: that the mind can think and know God and itself from itself once bodily impediment is removed. Gassendi then runs a dilemma. If the body with closed senses would present no impediment, the mind would be as well off as in separation, and opening the senses would make it worse. If there is some impediment, either it is greater than now, so the ideas would not be purer, or less than now, so the sense-deprived state would still be preferable and God could be blamed for drawing us out into sensory life. Against Descartes' claim that senses do not help at all in perceiving God and mind, Gassendi says corporeal images can mislead if taken literally, but they also serve as occasions and steps by which the mind ascends from visible perfections to divine perfections.

## Working Translation

As for what you finally say about the idea of God and of your mind, which you would already have if, from the moment the mind itself was infused into the body, and from birth onward, you had remained with eyes closed, ears stopped, and had perceived this universe of things, and whatever is outside, by no external sense at all, and meanwhile had spent your whole life meditating within yourself, turning and turning things over again; as for your saying, I repeat, that you do not doubt you would have had the same ideas you now have, indeed much purer and clearer: you certainly speak consistently with yourself, looking at what was mentioned above about the mind in the womb and in lethargy. But how plausibly, or by what reason other than begging the question, you say this, let others judge as they wish.

Certainly, when you assume that in that case the mind ought neither to be helped nor hindered in thinking by the body, you suppose that from itself, with the impediment removed, it can think and know God and itself just as well, and more purely and clearly, than it now thinks or knows. But that is what is in question.

Grant, then, that the body was most temperate, for nothing else could be supposed. I ask: would there have been some impediment, or none at all? If none, then your mind in your body would have been just as it is in a state of separation, and you could have wished nothing worse for yourself than that your eyes be opened, your ears be opened, and then that you be granted to smell, taste, and touch something. But what else is this than that whoever granted you sight, hearing, and the other senses deserved badly of you?

If there were some impediment, then it would have been either greater than it is now, after you have known the heaven and the other things that are in the universe, or less. If greater, then you would not have had purer and clearer ideas. If less, then the objection returns: that that state would have been more desirable for you than this one, and that whoever placed you in this state would have deserved badly of you. To this is added that God cannot blame us if we do not know him as we should from his works. For anyone could answer God: why then did you lead me out of my mother's womb, or why did you not immediately blind me and take away all the senses entirely, since in that way I could have known you better, as I should?

Certainly, when you say not only that the senses hinder the mind in many things, but also that they help in nothing toward perceiving the ideas of God and of mind, you seem to accuse all divine and human wisdom, which urges us to use sight so that from the seen perfections of things we may rise toward understanding God's perfections. For example, when we look at the magnitude of the universe, the beauty of the sun, and the wisdom of an architect, we try to understand how much greater, more beautiful, and wiser the one who founded the world, the sun, and the architect must be.

Hence, when you add that nothing prevents all human beings from noticing equally that they have the same ideas except that they are too occupied in perceiving images of corporeal things, people can indeed be blamed if they are so occupied with those images that they think God is such as he is represented through corporeal images. But it nevertheless cannot be denied that corporeal images help them to some extent, since they are an occasion and become, as it were, steps by which they rise toward understanding God, so far as their powers allow.

And please, if corporeal images are an obstacle in someone who has contemplated the universe of things, say someone endowed with sight, let us ask a blind person to express to us what sort of idea of God he has. Will he be able to describe God better because he will have no images of the sun, of the rainbow, and of other corporeal things? But we should not linger over these matters.

## Notes

- Gassendi treats Descartes' sense-deprivation answer as internally consistent but question-begging.
- The disputed assumption is whether the mind can know God and itself from itself once bodily impediment is removed.
- The dilemma makes sensory life look like a divine disservice if Descartes' claim is true: open eyes and ears would make cognition worse, not better.
- The argument turns Cassirer-relevant: sensation and corporeal images are not merely obstacles; they are occasions and steps for ascent from visible perfections to divine perfections.
- Corporeal images can mislead if taken literally as representations of God, but they still have positive epistemic use.
- The blind-person test reappears in a new register: lack of images of sun, rainbow, and other bodies does not improve the idea of God.
