## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' dismissal by saying the passage was intentionally a passing-over, not a failed contradiction. He had chosen not to press the remaining issues, partly from fatigue and partly because several things deserved praise. But he still marks one reason as contestable: the waking/dreaming certainty argument appears to depend on knowing, after all the preceding reasoning, that God exists and is not deceptive. Gassendi then attacks Descartes' rhetoric. Descartes attached the passed-over article to the other doubts so he could claim Gassendi contradicted nothing and could turn Gassendi's length against him. Gassendi replies that in this passage he neither used prolixity nor proposed a reason, but only praised what Descartes had pursued fully and elegantly.

## Working Translation

Immortal gods, what an occasion you seize for putting together this little closing flourish. You taunt me that here I contradict you in nothing. And did I not myself testify that I wanted here to pass over contradiction? Not, indeed, because many things that should be contradicted and noted did not occur to me; among the rest, that reasoning seemed to be your first and highest Muse:

    Only then could I be certain that I am awake and not dreaming,
    when, after all the preceding reasonings, I was going to know that God exists
    and is not deceptive;

    But only now, after all the preceding reasonings, do I know that God exists
    and is not deceptive;

    Therefore only now am I certain that I am awake and not dreaming.

But I judged that nothing further should be contested, partly because I was already worn out, partly because in this place especially there occurred certain things that deserved commendation. And so, contradicting you no further, I only approved the things you had said. You, paying no heed to my passing-over, treating the approval as nothing, pretend that I wanted to contradict and yet could not, since, as you say, "Here you contradict me in nothing, and nevertheless say quite enough."

This is why, when you were distinguishing the articles of the doubts, you attached this one, which ought to have been set apart, to the others, so that you would have a basis from which to taunt me that in it I had doubted nothing, or, as you say, contradicted nothing. Yet I wonder that, if you wanted that article to have the same nature as the others, you did not recognize how brief it was compared with the preceding ones. And why did you say about it what you did not say about the others, that I had said quite enough in it? Was it not so that an occasion would be made for you to add these words: "so that the reader may recognize from the prolixity of your words that the multitude of your reasons is not to be estimated"?

For my part, since I am not one who thinks either the multitude or the magnitude of my reasons should be greatly esteemed by the reader, I nevertheless observe that you say this in a place where I neither used prolixity of words nor wanted to propose any reason, but simply to state that you had pursued the matter fully and elegantly, and so forth. Therefore, if I lacked a reason, at least my disposition should not have been disapproved.

Or did you perhaps act in this way because you thought the reader would fly over the other things and, lingering only at the end, would bring judgment from there about the whole series of doubts? Or because, since I had not followed the other points with equal praise, you could not bear that these alone were praised, as though the reader might suspect that not everything was equally praiseworthy? Or because, when I said that you acted very piously, you thought the reader might pause over whether you were concluding from the principles of faith rather than demonstrating from the principles of nature? Or because, when I added that you did very well when you said that human life is exposed to errors and that the weakness of our nature must be acknowledged, you feared that the reader might think it was said either seriously or about you, as if you were lacking anything concerning matters demonstrated by you?

However it may have been, I will never envy you your opinion of yourself, nor will I therefore cease to follow my own genius in writing, however much you may disapprove prolixity in it. You can certainly say, on the occasion of what I have pursued thus far, that I am very wordy. You can complain that not even a crumb of salt has been sprinkled through all those words. Yet I am neither troubled nor sorry, both because I have not defrauded my own genius and because you alone will not be the judge of this matter.

## Notes

- Gassendi reframes the prior `Praeteritio`: non-contradiction there was deliberate, not failure.
- The waking/dreaming certainty argument is reconstructed as depending on the prior proof that God exists and is not deceptive.
- Descartes' brief response is treated as a rhetorical trap: attach the passed-over article to the doubts, then cite it as evidence that Gassendi has no objections.
- Gassendi distinguishes this closing praise from the earlier objections: he says he was not proposing reasons here.
- The passage is unusually self-reflexive about style, prolixity, reader judgment, and the author's own `genius`.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `Camoena` is rendered in the note as "Muse" because Gassendi is mock-elevating Descartes' waking/dreaming inference as a chief poetic inspiration. The tone is sarcastic.

Textual: `excep[i]ssem` regularizes a damaged OCR form in the sentence about human life being exposed to errors. The sense is secure, but the exact spelling should be checked before citation.

Contextual: Gassendi's "I will never envy you your opinion of yourself" is pointed, but it still belongs to a civil closing exchange. The following `Conclusio` immediately softens the polemical tone.
