## Opening Finding

Gassendi now states the circle in the form needed for the missing-method question. Descartes becomes certain that clear and distinct cognition is true only after proving that God exists, authors all things including clear and distinct cognition, and is not deceptive. But the proof that God exists and is not deceptive rests on a clear and distinct cognition of God that is already treated as true and non-fallacious. Even granting that God is author of genuine clear and distinct cognition, the practical criterion remains missing: many people think they have clear and distinct cognition and are deceived. Saying that they did not really have it only restates the distinction; it does not give the method by which the subject recognizes whether apparent clarity is genuine.

## Working Translation

Moreover, it has already been noted in the first objections both to this Meditation and to the previous one what circle you were using so that you could finally arrive at this point. Now, so that the matter may at least be indicated, I observe that from what you had proposed from the very beginning of the Third Meditation, you reason in this way:

Only then was I going to be certain that clear and distinct cognition is without doubt true, when it had been proved by me that God exists, and that he is the author of all things, and therefore also of clear and distinct cognition, and that he is not a deceiver.

But now I have proved that God exists, and is the author of all things, and therefore also of clear and distinct cognition, and that he is not a deceiver.

Therefore only now am I certain that clear and distinct cognition is without doubt true.

As for the proposition and the conclusion, both the words just now quoted from the end of that Meditation and those reported in their place from the beginning of the previous one abundantly show that you prove clear and distinct cognition to be true, or non-fallacious, because God exists, because he is its author, and because he is not a deceiver.

But as for the assumption, the things recalled in the remainder of the previous Meditation and at the beginning of this one also show that you prove God to exist, to be author, and not to be a deceiver because the clear and distinct cognition of him is true, not fallacious. Above all, these words look in that direction: "And the longer and more carefully I examine all these things, the more clearly and distinctly I know that they are true." And afterward: "It is enough that I understand and judge this very thing, that all those things which I clearly perceive and know to import some perfection, and perhaps also countless others that I do not know, are in God either formally or eminently, so that the idea I have of him is of all the things that are in me the most true and the most clear and distinct."

I ask, therefore, whether you do not commit a circle, as they say, fall into a diallelus, and beg the principle or the question, when you prove one thing by another which itself cannot be proved except from the supposition of the first.

But even if no one doubts that God is the author of clear and distinct cognition, do we not see that there are many people who think they have clear and distinct cognition and nevertheless are deceived? You say that they do not really have such cognition; otherwise they would not be deceived. Nor do they clearly and distinctly know something unless they know what sort of thing it is, since in fact they do not even know it.

Yet because nevertheless those people think they have it when they do not, just as others think they have it when they do; and because both groups hold it as certain that God exists, is author, and is not a deceiver, does there not still remain a method to be handed down, by which the latter may recognize that they have clear and distinct cognition in such a way that they are not deceived, and the former that they do not have it in such a way that they are deceived?

## Notes

- Gassendi recasts the Cartesian circle in syllogistic form.
- Clear and distinct cognition is secured by God as author and non-deceiver.
- God as author and non-deceiver is proved from clear and distinct cognition of God.
- The circle matters here because `Dubitatio IV` asks for a criterion, not merely a doctrine.
- Even if God authors genuine clear cognition, the subject still needs a way to distinguish genuine clarity from apparent clarity.
- Descartes' answer that the deceived do not really have clear and distinct cognition is treated as circularly diagnostic unless a method of recognition is supplied.
