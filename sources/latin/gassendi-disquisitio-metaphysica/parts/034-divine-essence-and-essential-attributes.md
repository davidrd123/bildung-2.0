## Opening Finding

Gassendi applies the previous argument directly to Descartes' claim that nothing can be added to or taken from the idea of God. Descartes says he attended to the indivisible divine essence, not to a growable list of perfections. Gassendi answers first with scale: Descartes does not know the essence of the sun or even the smallest mite, yet claims perfect enough knowledge of God's essence that nothing can be added. He then rejects a point-like model of divine essence. If divine essence is infinite, the notion of infinity carries some notion of extension, while extension cannot be reconciled with strict indivisibility. The more charitable account is that indivisible essence means an inseparable nexus of essential attributes. But then an idea of that essence should represent the attributes, not a bare nexus; so it can have varied representational parts. Even theologians who call divine attributes essential still distinguish attributes from the essence to which they are attributed. Our knowledge of divine attributes comes through effects and a posteriori; if Descartes had the essence itself, he would be happier than Moses. Even if the attributes are granted to be the divine essence, Descartes knows them only finitely, analogically, confusedly, obscurely, and "in a riddle."

## Working Translation

As for the idea in question: because you had said that you have such an idea of God that you could neither take anything from it nor add anything to it, I objected with various ways in which you could add something to it. To escape this, you first suggest that you attended to God's essence, and that since it is indivisible, the idea likewise had to be indivisible; therefore nothing could be added to it or taken from it without its at once becoming another idea, the idea of another thing. Then, to be sure, you do not deny that new perfections not noticed by you can be discovered; but you deny that the idea is increased (that is, that something is added to it; for although you twist the word or conceal it, this is still what is in question), and say only that it is made more distinct, because it contained them before.

But first I am very much amazed that you do not know the essence not only of the sun, but even of the smallest mite, and yet think that you know the essence of God so perfectly. Then those people are fools who ask for days, multiply them, and never make an end of investigating what God is, because the more they consider him, the more obscure they think the matter becomes. No doubt, if they imitate you, in one moment they will have so clear a knowledge of God's essence that nothing can be added to it. Again, fools are those who, from effects, actions, properties, and perfections, as from certain little streams sought out in company, try to be led to the outermost shore of that immense ocean. No doubt, if they follow you, they will not arrive at the shore from the little streams; they will immediately measure the ocean in one step, and proceed through the little streams afterward.

Next, when you take the divine essence as indivisible after the model of other essences, you do not for that reason have it as a point. For since you take it as infinite, you conceive some extension in it, since nothing can be more finite than that thing which is conceived without any extension. And although you will answer that you conceive the divine essence not indeed as a point, but still not on that account as something extended, you will not thereby explain how you conceive it as at once infinite and indivisible, since the notion of infinity is not without the notion of extension, and the notion of extension cannot be together with the notion of indivisibility.

Rather, then, just as those philosophers who commonly say, for example, that the essence of a human being is indivisible do not mean that it lacks a certain extension or certain parts, for they say that genus and difference are such parts, say animal and rational, or physically speaking, body and rational soul; but contend only that the bond of these parts is so simple and indivisible that without it a human being neither exists nor is understood, and therefore no such part, either taken separately or joined and understood with some other, is a human being; just as, I say, they take it in this way, so you too, I think, do not want God to lack a certain amplitude and variety of essential attributes, which are infinite both in perfection and in number. You want only that all these are so inseparably connected with one another that without them, or without any of them, God can neither be nor be understood.

Moreover, since the idea which we think we have of the essence of a human being ought to represent not precisely the indivisible bond itself of the parts, or of the essential attributes, but rather the parts themselves, or the attributes indivisibly connected, and since therefore the idea of a human essence is not indivisible as if it were like a point and lacked parts, in exactly the same way an idea representing God's essence, since it ought to represent not precisely that quasi-bond of God's essential attributes, but rather God's attributes themselves, indivisibly joined, or most simply and, so to speak, most identically united, gives us no reason to think this idea indivisible like a point, and as if lacking various representational parts for various attributes.

Besides this, I ask you: what are these essential attributes of God? No doubt they are those you enumerated when you said that God is an eternal, infinite, independent, omnipotent, supremely intelligent substance, creator of all things and conserver of the same. But first, although theologians call these and the other attributes of the same kind essential, they do this, first, because when they distinguish in God one essence and three persons, they also distinguish the attributes which belong to God as one according to essence from those which belong to him as triune according to persons. From this it follows that the former can be counted essential, the latter personal. Second, they do it so that they may signify more expressly the simplicity and unalterable integrity of the divine essence; for if God were said to have certain accidental attributes, he could be understood to have certain accidents which would both make a certain composition with the essence and be able to be present in him or absent from him. Third, therefore, they do it so that we may understand that any one of these attributes belongs to God with as much necessity as, indeed with greater necessity than, genus and difference belong to a human being; and therefore that whatever is in God is both wholly inseparable from God and is God himself.

But in truth these points do not prevent them from recognizing, besides the attributes themselves, an essence to which those attributes are attributed (and certainly they are attributes, because they are attributed to something). Hence they also ask and conclude how they are distinguished both among themselves and from the essence. Elsewhere, too, they express these attributes with concrete words, so that when, for example, they say "omnipotent," we understand not only omnipotence itself, which is an attribute, but also the essence to which omnipotence is attributed.

I touch on these things to suggest that besides the cognition of the attributes, which become known to us in some way through effects and a posteriori, as they say, there still remains the cognition of the essence. If you had that, you would be happier than Moses himself, who, although he spoke with God so many times, nevertheless never saw anything except his back parts. Grant next that these attributes are God's very essence: what, I ask, do you know about them? Or what fraction of them do you know? Certainly you know nothing about them except finitely, analogically, confusedly, obscurely, in a riddle; and moreover you know so tiny a part of them that whatever you know is not even like a little drop, or the point of a drop, in relation to the whole ocean.

And nevertheless you say that you have such an idea of the divine essence that nothing can be added to it. But evidently you have distinguished nothing between idea and essence, and have thought there is no difference between a thing's being in itself and its being known by someone, since you judged that there is nothing in God which was not in your idea.

## Notes

- Gassendi intensifies the Part 033 critique: ignorance of creaturely essence makes Descartes' claim to a complete divine idea especially implausible.
- The ocean/rivulet image defends the slow a posteriori route through effects, actions, properties, and perfections against Descartes' immediate claim to divine essence.
- `Indivisible` is reinterpreted as an inseparable nexus of essential attributes, not as point-like absence of parts.
- Essential attributes remain attributes; even theological usage does not erase the distinction between attributes and the essence to which they are attributed.
- The final charge is methodological: Descartes has confused idea with essence and thing-in-itself with thing-as-known.
