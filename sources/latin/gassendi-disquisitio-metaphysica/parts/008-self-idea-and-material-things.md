## Opening Finding

Gassendi now attacks Descartes' inventory of ideas at its supposed easiest point: the mind's idea of itself. On Gassendi's species model, cognition requires a thing to act on a knowing faculty by sending or impressing its species. A faculty cannot do this to itself any more than an eye sees itself directly. Self-knowledge would require a mirror and therefore reflection, not direct self-presence. From there Gassendi denies that ideas of corporeal things can be deduced from an incorporeal self-idea: a common notion such as "apt to exist" presupposes that both particular terms are already understood.

## Working Translation

Dubitatio VI.

On the mind's not having from itself an idea of itself, since it does not seem able to understand itself any more than the eye can see itself; nor having from itself an idea of God, of angels, and especially of corporeal things.

You infer that if there is in you an idea whose objective reality is so great that you have contained it neither eminently nor formally, and therefore could not have been its cause, then at last it follows necessarily that something exists in the world besides you, since otherwise you lack an argument by which you may be rendered certain that anything exists.

But from what has already been said, you are not the cause of the reality of ideas. Rather, the very things represented by ideas are, insofar as they emit images of themselves into you, as into a mirror, although from them you may sometimes take occasion to paint chimeras. But whether you yourself are the cause or not, are you for that reason uncertain that there is something besides you in the world? Good words, please. Whatever may be the case about ideas, there is no need for us to seek arguments for that.

You next run through the ideas that are in you, and besides the idea of yourself, you count ideas of God, of corporeal and inanimate things, of angels, animals, and human beings. Since you say that there is no difficulty about the idea of yourself, you infer that the ideas of human beings, animals, and angels can be composed from the ideas you have of yourself, God, and corporeal things, and that ideas of corporeal things too could have proceeded from you.

At this point I am moved to wonder how you can be said to have an idea of yourself, and one so fertile that from it you can bring forth so many others, and how the matter can be without difficulty, when in truth you either have no idea of yourself or have one very confused and imperfect, as we already observed on the previous Meditation. And in that Meditation you inferred that nothing could be perceived by you more easily and evidently than yourself. What if, since you do not have and cannot have an idea of yourself, you should be said to be able to perceive anything else more easily and evidently than yourself?

When I consider how it can be that neither sight sees itself nor intellect understands itself, this came to mind: nothing acts on itself. For neither does the hand, or the fingertip on it, strike itself; nor does the foot kick itself. Since, moreover, in order to elicit knowledge of some thing, the thing must act on the knowing faculty, that is, send its species into it or inform it with its species, it seems clear that the faculty itself, since it is not outside itself, cannot transmit that species of itself into itself, and consequently cannot elicit knowledge of itself, or, what is the same, perceive itself.

And why do you think the eye, although it does not see itself in itself, nevertheless sees itself in a mirror? Because there is an interval between the eye and the mirror, and the eye acts on the mirror by sending its species into it, so that the mirror reacts on the eye by sending back its own species to it. Give me, then, a mirror into which the mind itself acts in the same way, and I promise that, once it reflects your species back into yourself, you will at last perceive yourself, not by direct cognition indeed, but by reflected cognition. Until you provide this, there is no hope that you know yourself.

I could also press here how you can be said to have an idea of God, unless perhaps of the sort and in the way we have already described. How can you have an idea of angels, of whom I do not know whether you would ever have thought unless you had heard something? How can you have ideas of animals and other things, of which I am almost certain you would have had no ideas if they had not fallen into the senses, just as you have no idea of innumerable things whose sight or report has never reached you?

But leaving these aside, I admit that ideas of diverse things existing in the mind can be so composed that many forms of other things arise, although the things you enumerate do not seem sufficient for such great diversity, indeed not even for a distinct and determinate idea of any certain thing.

I pause only over ideas of corporeal things, because there is no small difficulty in how, from the idea of yourself alone, while you act as incorporeal and consider yourself as such, you can deduce them from yourself. For if you know only incorporeal substance, how can it happen that you also grasp corporeal substance? Is there some analogy between this and that? You say that they agree in being apt to exist. But that agreement is not understood unless both things that agree are understood first. For you make a common notion, and before it can be formed, the particulars must be understood.

Certainly, if from understood incorporeal substance the intellect can form the idea of corporeal substance, then there is no reason to doubt that a blind person, or someone held from birth in the densest darkness, can form for himself the idea of light and colors. You say that ideas of extension, figure, motion, and the other common sensibles can consequently be had; but of course you say this easily. The wonder is why you do not deduce light, color, and other things with equal ease. But we need not linger over these things.

Responsio.

If you had anything to bring forward for proving the existence of material things, you would undoubtedly have brought it here. But since you merely ask whether my mind is therefore uncertain that anything besides itself exists in the world, and you pretend that there is no need for you to seek arguments for this, and so you appeal only to prejudged opinions, you show much more clearly that you can give no reason for what you affirm than if you had been entirely silent.

Whatever you dispute here about ideas does not need a response, because you restrict the name `idea` to images painted in phantasy, whereas I extend it to everything that is thought. But in passing I would like to ask by what argument you prove that nothing acts on itself. Of course, you are not accustomed to using arguments. You proved this by the example of a finger that does not strike itself, and of an eye that does not see itself in itself but in a mirror. To these it is easy to reply that it is not the eye which sees the mirror any more than itself, but the mind alone which recognizes both the mirror and the eye and itself too.

There can also be other examples in corporeal things, as when a top turns itself in a circle: is that rotation anything other than an action it exercises on itself? Finally, it should be noted that I did not affirm that ideas of material things are deduced from the mind, as you not very honestly pretend here. For later I expressly showed that they often come from bodies, and that through this the existence of bodies is proved. Here, however, I only explained that there is not so much reality found in them that, from the principle that nothing is in the effect which has not preexisted formally or eminently in the cause, it must be concluded that they could not have proceeded from the mind alone. This you in no way refute.

## Notes

- Gassendi applies the species model to self-knowledge. If cognition requires a thing to send or impress its species into a faculty, then the mind cannot directly know itself because it is not outside itself.
- The eye/mirror analogy gives a new form of mediated cognition: self-knowledge would be reflected cognition, not immediate self-presence.
- The passage sharpens the anti-innatist line beyond God. Ideas of angels, animals, and unknown things depend on hearing, sense, or report; otherwise the mind lacks them.
- Gassendi accepts compositional imagination in principle, but denies that Descartes' inventory can generate determinate ideas of corporeal things from an incorporeal self.
- The argument from common notions is important: "apt to exist" cannot mediate between incorporeal and corporeal substance unless both particulars are already understood.
- Descartes' response keeps the old fault line open. He says `idea` extends to everything thought, not just images in phantasy, and denies that he claimed material ideas are deduced from the mind.
