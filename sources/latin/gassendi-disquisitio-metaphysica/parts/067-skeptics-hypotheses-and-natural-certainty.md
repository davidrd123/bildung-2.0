## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' appeal to skeptics by narrowing what skeptical doubt touched. Skeptics did not seriously attack apparent things, life-useful things, or plain geometrical results; they attacked hidden, uncertain, ostentatious claims and the dogmatists' arrogance. In astronomy, they could accept eclipses and planetary configurations while resisting hypotheses treated as genuine natural mechanisms. In geometry, they could accept triangle and Pythagorean results while disputing hypothetical modes of demonstration, such as indivisible points, lines, and surfaces. Gassendi then rejects Descartes' "better known" reply: if knowledge is said with respect to the knower, what is evident to everyone is properly better known than divine matters many find obscure. Divine nature may be more necessary in itself, but natural certainty follows evidence, so mathematical certainty does not depend on theological certainty. Finally, the `ego cogito; igitur exsisto` becomes the foundation rather than the thing founded: Descartes had its certainty before knowledge of God, and built the divine proof on it, so later divine knowledge cannot retroactively ground it.

## Working Translation

INSTANTIA.

Nothing needs to be added here to the things proposed while doubting, except because of what you object about the Skeptics.

For, to pass over the fact that the uncertainty of the Skeptics did not prevent the dogmatists from being certain, indeed most certain, of their dogmas, and especially of the geometrical demonstrations I named, so that it does no good to oppose the Skeptics to them; to pass over that, I say, I state first that the Skeptics neither doubted nor argued against the things that appear or are useful for life, but only against hidden, uncertain, empty things prepared for display.

And if sometimes they seemed to say something against appearances or life-useful things, they testified that they did this not because they wanted to overturn them, but because it was worthwhile to beat back the arrogance of dogmatic philosophers, who, though they were dim-sighted in the most manifest things of all, boasted that they were sharpest-sighted in the most hidden things.

I say further that they counted whatever was proved by geometrical demonstrations among apparent things, so that they did not doubt the things themselves, but attacked only the mode of demonstrating in which the geometers gloried.

For just as in astronomy they held eclipses and the various configurations of the planets among apparent things, and nevertheless spoke against hypotheses, not insofar as those hypotheses contributed to foreknowing eclipses and the rest, but insofar as they were held as so settled that each person took the hypotheses he defended as genuine and natural, although through opposite hypotheses, which could not be true at the same time, the same appearances were preserved and foreknown:

So in geometry they altogether held among apparent things both that a Triangle has three angles equal to two right angles and that the square of the hypotenuse is equal to the squares of the legs, and the rest of this kind. For these are in nature and need only understanding of the words and attention.

Indeed to demonstrate is nothing other than to command what should be attended to, as when someone looking at a face and not detecting a mark is ordered to inspect more closely and attend to the part in which the mark is.

But they raised doubts about the mode of demonstrating through hypotheses, not insofar as these contributed to the proposed attention and detection, but insofar as they were held as settled and natural, as if the supposed things were plainly such as they were supposed to be, such as the indivisibility of point, line, and surface, which we touched on a little earlier.

Hence you should not see them as pressing against Demonstrations or against plain, natural principles, not hypothetical or suppositional ones, but only against the hypotheses themselves. About these they first asked whether anything should be accepted from hypothesis. And then, although they seemed to dispute against appearances, demonstrated things, or demonstrations themselves, nevertheless their words were that they were doing this not because they wanted to overturn those things, but because they were refuting the arrogance and boasting of the dogmatists. Therefore they should not even be opposed to Pythagoras, Plato, or others, as if they seriously doubted the things they opposed to the Dogmatists.

As for what you add, that it is not rightly proved that one thing is better known than another from the fact that it seems true to more people, but only from the fact that, to those who know both as they should, it appears prior in cognition, more evident, and more certain: I do not see what could have less to do with the question.

For, to pass over the remaining difficulty about those who know both, and, as you say, know both as they should, I say again, as they should, since trust is not immediately to be given to everyone who says he knows as he should: in what way do you prove from this that geometrical demonstrations borrow evidence and certainty from the things said about God's existence, the creation of Things, and similar matters, when the latter are inevident and uncertain to many, while the former are evident and certain not only to many, but even to all, that is, both to others and to those for whom divine things are inevident and uncertain?

And how, therefore, is what no one is ignorant of not better known than what most people are ignorant of?

For although what most people are ignorant of can, as commonly happens, be called better known in itself, this nevertheless signifies nothing other than that there is a greater necessity of existing in it. But since a known thing is said with respect to the knower, it is plainly evident that what becomes known to more people, although it may be less necessary and is called only better known with respect to us, is nevertheless what is properly called better known.

Hence the divine nature itself certainly has every mode of necessity of existing, and no geometrical thing should be compared with it regarding this necessity. But as far as clear and evident knowledge is concerned, geometrical things seem able to be considered better known. And since natural certainty depends on evidence, the certainty of geometrical things does not seem to depend on the certainty of divine things.

Imagine, certainly, a man who is both persuaded that God exists and holds geometrical things demonstratively; and nevertheless the same unhappiness happens to him that happened to the aforementioned Diagoras, when from pious he became impious. Do you think that, when he casts away his persuasion about God's existence, he will also cast away his persuasion about mathematical demonstrations? Unless you think so, you plainly see that this persuasion is independent from that one.

Not to mention what pleased Pico of Mirandola: not that this carries help from that, but rather that it brings harm to it, since one of his celebrated propositions was that nothing is more harmful to the Theologian than to know the Elements of Euclid.

Therefore I could press many things against that reasoning of yours:

Only then could I become certain of some thing, when I could notice that it had been perceived by clear and distinct cognition which had God, who existed and was truthful, as author.

But now I can notice that some thing has been perceived by me by clear and distinct cognition which has God, who exists and is truthful, as author.

Therefore only now can I become certain of some thing.

But the weakness of that is clear both from the things disputed in the preceding Meditation about clear and evident cognition, and from the things we touched on around the Third Meditation about the certainty of your proposition, "I think." It could also have been pressed about your conclusion, "Therefore I am," which somewhere you call the foundation, as it were, of your whole edifice.

I repeat only this: since the certainty of this statement of yours, "I think, therefore I exist," was obtained in you from clear and distinct cognition before knowledge of God himself, and was for you the foundation on which you built whatever you reasoned about God and clear and distinct cognition, therefore, besides the things already pressed about begging the principle around clear and distinct cognition, two things follow from this.

One is that you at least are not made certain of this statement of yours, "I think, therefore I am," by force of the reasoning just deduced. For you had this certainty before you knew God and before you had argued that he exists, is truthful, and is author. Nor can you say that you were confirmed in it afterward, since the foundation is what provides firmness to the thing founded, not the thing founded to the foundation itself.

The other is that, since that because of which each thing is such is itself more such, you should refer the certainty received by the rest of things not to God as some intermediate thing, but to that statement of yours as what is first and as the foundation, on the certainty of which the certainty of the intermediate thing itself also depends.

## Notes

- Gassendi denies that skeptics seriously doubted appearances, life-useful things, or plain geometrical conclusions.
- Skeptical attacks target hidden, uncertain, ostentatious claims and dogmatic arrogance.
- In astronomy, hypotheses can preserve and predict appearances without being treated as genuine natural mechanisms.
- In geometry, the demonstrated results count as apparent; the skeptical pressure falls on hypothetical modes of demonstration such as indivisible point, line, and surface.
- Demonstration is cast as attention-direction: it shows where to look, like directing someone to a mark on a face.
- Gassendi distinguishes what is better known in itself from what is better known to us, and gives priority to what is known with respect to the knower.
- Divine nature may be more necessary in itself, but geometry can be more evident and therefore the ground of natural certainty.
- The Diagoras example tests dependence: if a former believer becomes impious, he need not lose mathematical certainty.
- The `ego cogito; igitur exsisto` is treated as Descartes' foundation before God is known, so divine knowledge cannot later ground that foundation.
