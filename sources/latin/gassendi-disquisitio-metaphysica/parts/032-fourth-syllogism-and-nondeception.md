## Opening Finding

Gassendi reconstructs Descartes' fourth move as the inference from divine defectlessness to divine non-deception. The formal argument is brief: fraud and deception depend on some defect; God is subject to no defects; therefore God cannot be deceptive. The critique is strategic rather than doctrinal. Gassendi has just argued that defectlessness has not been demonstrated from Descartes' partial idea of divine perfections, and now notes that Descartes tucks the non-deception conclusion away so that it can later be drawn out as if it were a fully demonstrated principle.

## Working Translation

Finally, the fourth:

If God is not subject to any defects at all, it is clear that he cannot be deceptive, because it is manifest by natural light that every fraud and deception depends on some defect:

But God is subject to no defects at all:

Therefore God cannot be deceptive.

No doubt this was what you intended when you appended that little clause just noted. But why, out of so many defects which could have been listed, did you choose deception, so that you would mention it at the end? Evidently so that you could put this conclusion, God cannot be deceptive, quietly into the pantry, or as it were into the corner of a cupboard, and then, when there was need, draw it out as if it were a most fully demonstrated principle. We shall see that hereafter.

## Notes

- The fourth syllogism depends on the defectlessness claim left under pressure in Part 031.
- Gassendi does not yet deny the principle that deception implies defect; he attacks its readiness for demonstrative use.
- `Deus fallax esse non potest` is flagged as an argumentative reserve, stored for later deployment.
- The pantry/cupboard image marks a transition from local syllogistic critique to the later problem of how Descartes will secure certainty by divine non-deception.
