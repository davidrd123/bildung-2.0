## Opening Finding

Gassendi attacks the next auxiliary proof by separating persistence from continuous recreation. Some effects, like sunlight, require the present and continuous efficiency of a cause; many generated or made things persist even after their producing cause stops acting or is destroyed. Human dependence may show a producing cause, but not a new creation at each moment. The temporal argument fails because the parts of time are external, successive, and not active causes of production or reproduction. Gassendi also allows an infinite regress in generated causes unless Descartes first proves that the world began, and he explains divine perfections as assembled, amplified, and purified from multiple finite models rather than implanted by one divine cause.

## Working Translation

Dubitatio IX.

On another subsidiary demonstration, from the fact that something could not be made or exist if infinite being did not exist, and that the parts of time are mutually independent.

Next you ask whether, now having an idea of a being more perfect than you, you yourself could exist if no such being existed. And you answer: "From what, then, would I be? From myself, perhaps? Or from parents? Or from any other things less perfect than God?" You then prove that you are not from yourself; but this is not necessary. You also give the reason why you have not always existed; but this too is superfluous, except insofar as you want at the same time to infer that you have a cause of yourself not only as producing but also as conserving.

Namely, from the fact that the time of your life has several parts, you infer that you must be created in each individual part, because of their mutual independence from one another. But see how this can be understood.

There are indeed some effects which, in order to persevere and not fail at any given moment, need the presence and continuous efficiency of the cause through which they first began to be. Such is the light of the sun, although effects of this sort are not so much the same in reality as equivalently, as is said of the water of a river. But we see other effects which persevere not only when the cause they acknowledge is no longer acting, but even, if you like, when it has been corrupted and reduced to nothing. There are so many generated and made things of this kind that it would be tiresome to list them. It is enough that you are one of them, whatever finally your cause may be.

"But the parts of your time do not depend on one another." Here one could press: what thinkable thing has parts more inseparable from one another? Between the parts of what thing is there a more inviolable series and connection? The later parts of what thing could less be turned aside, cohere more, or depend more on the prior parts? But not to press these points, what does this dependence or independence of the parts of time, which are external, successive, and not active, do for your production or reproduction? Surely nothing more than the flow and passage of the parts of water does for the production or reproduction of some rock past which a river flows.

"But from the fact that you were a little while ago, it does not follow that you must now be." I certainly believe it. But not because a cause is required which creates you anew; rather because it is not guaranteed that a cause which could destroy you is absent, or that you do not have in yourself a weakness by which you may eventually fail.

You say, therefore, that it is manifest by natural light that conservation differs from creation only by reason. But how is this manifest, except perhaps in light itself and similar effects? You add that there is no force in a thing by which it will exist a little later, because you are not conscious of such a force, although you are nevertheless a thinking thing. But there is some force in you by which you can think that you too will exist a little later; not necessarily or indubitably, however, because that force of yours, or natural constitution, whatever it is, does not extend so far that it would ward off every corrupting cause, whether internal or external. Therefore you will exist because you have a force, not one which produces you anew, but one which is sufficient to secure your perseverance unless a corrupting cause intervenes.

As for your gathering that you depend on some being diverse from you, you gather well; but not as if you are produced anew by it, rather as having once been produced by it. You go on that such a being is not parents or any other causes whatever. But why should it not be parents, by whom you seem so manifestly to have been produced together with your body, to say nothing about the sun and the other concurrent causes?

"But I," you say, "am a thinking thing and have the idea of God in me." But were not your parents, or their minds, also thinking things and things having the idea of God, so that here that saying should not be pressed, of which we have already spoken, that there must be at minimum as much in the cause as in the effect?

"If," you say, "there is some cause besides God, one can ask whether it is from itself or from another. For if it is from itself, it will be God; if from another, one will ask the same so many times until one arrives at that which is from itself and is God, since one cannot proceed to infinity." But if the cause was parents, that cause could be not from itself but from another, and that again from another, and so on to infinity. Nor will you have proved that this infinite progression is absurd unless you at the same time prove that the world once began, and therefore that there was a first parent who had no parent.

Certainly an infinite progression seems absurd only in causes so connected and subordinated to one another that the lower cannot act without the higher moving it: as when a stone pushes something, itself pushed by a stick which a hand pushes; or when the lowest link of a chain pulls a weight, itself pulled by the higher link, and that by another. In that case one must arrive at one mover which moves first. But in causes so ordered that, when the prior one has been destroyed, the one which depends on it survives and can act, it does not seem equally absurd. Therefore, when you say that it is sufficiently open that no infinite progression is given here, see whether this was so open to Aristotle, who was strongly persuaded that there was no first parent.

You go on that several partial causes did not come together to produce you, from which you received the ideas of the various perfections attributed to God, since those perfections can be found only in one God alone, whose unity or simplicity is a principal perfection. Nevertheless, whether one or several causes of you existed, it is not therefore necessary that they impressed in you the ideas of their perfections which you could unite.

Meanwhile you give occasion to ask why, if there were not several causes of you, at least there could not have been several things whose perfections you admired and then took to be blessed the thing in which they all existed together. You know how the poets describe Pandora. Why could you not, admiring in various human beings some eminent knowledge, wisdom, justice, constancy, power, health, beauty, happiness, longevity, and other things, have composed all these and judged that someone would be worthy of admiration if he had them all together?

Why could you not then increase all perfections by various degrees until that being would seem more worthy of admiration if nothing were lacking to, or could be added to, his knowledge, power, duration, and the rest, and so he were omniscient, omnipotent, eternal, and all the rest? And when you saw that such perfections cannot fall within human nature, why could you not judge that blessed would be the nature to which these belonged? Why not think it worthy of investigation whether there were such a nature or not? Why not be led by certain arguments so that it seemed more fitting that it exists than that it does not? Why not then remove from it corporeity, limitation, and all other things which would connote some imperfection?

In precisely this way many seem to have proceeded, although with various modes and degrees of reasoning: some left God corporeal, some as having human limbs, some not one but multiple, and other things that are too familiar. As for the perfection of unity, nothing prevents all perfections attributed to God from being conceived as intimately joined and inseparable, even if the idea you have of them was not placed in you by him, but drawn by you from things looked over and amplified, as has been said. Thus they certainly depict not only Pandora as a goddess adorned with all gifts and perfections, but also a perfect republic, a perfect orator, and so on.

Finally, from the fact that you exist and have in you the idea of a most perfect being, you conclude that God's existence is most evidently demonstrated. But although the conclusion is true, namely that God exists, it does not appear from what has been said that it has been most evidently demonstrated by you.

## Notes

- Gassendi separates producing cause from conserving cause. Persistence does not automatically mean repeated creation at each moment.
- The sunlight example is restricted. Some effects depend continuously on a present cause, but many made or generated things persist after their producing cause stops acting.
- The parts of time do no causal work. Their dependence or independence is external and successive, like river-water passing a rock.
- Infinite regress is allowed for generated causes unless Descartes proves a first temporal beginning. Gassendi distinguishes subordinated instrumental causes from ordered generative causes.
- Parents, minds, the sun, and concurrent causes remain live candidates for finite production; the idea of God does not by itself remove them.
- Divine perfections can be assembled by admiring multiple finite perfections, composing them, amplifying them, and removing imperfections. Pandora, the perfect republic, and the perfect orator become models for composed ideal totality.
