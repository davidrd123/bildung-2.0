## Opening Finding

Gassendi shifts from the failed sunlight analogy to the temporal premise of Descartes' conservation argument. Descartes treats the nature of time as clear by natural light; Gassendi calls it among the most obscure topics and says the appeal to a self-authorizing mind cannot ground a demonstration. His own tentative account makes time or duration a common, invariant flux: motion measures it but does not cause it to speed up, slow down, or stop. He therefore rejects Descartes' split between abstract time and the duration of the enduring thing, as well as the popular distinction between external and internal time. What can cease at a moment ceases in a common duration shared with other things, not in private separable time-parts.

## Working Translation

You go on to say that this is openly demonstrated from what you explained about the independence of the parts of time. Here, then, is your explanation: "Since the whole time of life can be divided into innumerable parts, each of which in no way depends on the others, from the fact that I existed a little while ago it does not follow that I must now exist, unless some cause as it were creates me again for this moment, that is, conserves me. For it is clear, to one attending to the nature of time, that exactly the same force and action is needed to conserve any thing in the individual moments in which it endures as would be needed to create the same thing anew if it did not yet exist. Thus it is also one of the things manifest by natural light that conservation differs from creation by reason alone."

But at this point, what material, what harvest of paralogisms! For first you suppose that the nature of time is clear, when nothing can be called more obscure. As evidence there can surely serve the innumerable variety of opinions which existed among the ancient philosophers and which still persists. For although you say that you are addressing the Mind, which does not even know whether there were philosophers and is not moved by their authority, nevertheless this Mind, so ignorant of all things outside itself, supposes the nature of time to be so clear that it weaves a demonstration from it. And when I doubt that clarity, because of the obscurity which alone explains the variety of opinions among so many otherwise wise and sharp-sighted philosophers, the Mind gives no proof of this clarity except by thrusting forward its own authority. There is therefore no reason why I, or anyone else, should be moved by its authority or should think that authority itself is the foundation or principle of a demonstration.

And how you will bless us hereafter, how you will oblige the whole nation of the wise, if you make the nature of time clear and open; if you explain whether it is something real or not, how it differs or does not differ from an enduring thing, what is or is not in it, and other similar questions. As far as I am concerned, I frankly confess that I do not know the nature of time. And although I seem to myself to understand in some way what it is, nevertheless, if I wish to explain it, I cannot. That splendid statement of Saint Augustine immediately occurs to me: What, then, is time? If no one asks me, I know; if I wish to explain it to someone asking, I do not know. Indeed I also convince myself that I do not know what time is because I cannot tell others what it is.

It is certainly not enough for what I would like when I say that I conceive time, or duration, as a certain flux which never began, which now perseveres, and which will never cease; which can neither be impeded, nor slowed, nor accelerated. Taken according to its whole amplitude, and insofar as it lacks beginning and end, it can be called eternity, or the duration of God, coexisting with God as a whole because of the immutability of his nature, as a rock coexists with the river flowing past it. But taken according to its parts, it is the duration of things liable to rise and perish, such as the whole world and all the parts of the world, or created things, which, as long as they persevere together, are judged to endure not in many times but in one and the same time.

Finally, because it is successive, human beings have devised a measure for it: motion, namely motion itself as successive, and especially celestial motion. Yet this is without the consequence that, because motion is made swifter or slower, the flux of time is therefore made more hurried or more sluggish. Time has one tenor and continues invariably whether something moves or nothing does; indeed whether the world is made or destroyed, and whether something exists or absolutely nothing exists. This, I say, is not satisfactory enough for what I would like.

But however that may be, this is what I had in view. When you said that time can be divided into innumerable parts, none of which depends in any way on the others, I objected that someone could press: what thinkable thing has parts more inseparable from one another? Between the parts of what thing is there a more inviolable series and connection? The later parts of what thing can be less turned aside, cohere more, or depend more on the prior parts? What is your answer to this? That I wanted to evade the issue by proposing the necessity of sequence which exists between the parts of time considered in the abstract, whereas that is not the question here, but rather time, or the duration of the enduring thing, whose individual moments I do not deny can be separated from neighboring ones, that is, that the enduring thing can cease to be at each individual moment.

But you are plainly mistaken when you think that I was trying to evade rather than to grasp the clarity you had boasted of. You say that I proposed the sequence which exists between the parts of time considered in the abstract. But what do you call abstract time, or time considered abstractly? I recognize only one time, which I will certainly not deny can be called or considered abstract insofar as it does not depend on things, since whether things exist or do not exist, whether they move or rest, it always flows in the same tenor and persists invariantly. But I in no way recognize another time besides that one, which can be called or considered as if in the concrete insofar as it belongs to things, or insofar as things endure in it.

You begin to clarify when you say that the question is not about that time, but about the time or duration of the enduring thing. I ask only what other time of the enduring thing there is besides that one. You have heard the common distinction between external and internal time; but if what I have described is external, what will that internal time be? Let us test it. For example, I am a thing now enduring for fifty years, and I recognize that I endure with the same duration as all my contemporaries. All of us together have so far endured no more than one person has; for from our birth there have not flowed all those myriads of years that could be computed if fifty years were added to themselves as many times as there are now fifty-year-olds. But an internal time that is peculiar to me, and that belongs to me in the concrete besides that external time which you call time in the abstract, I neither recognize nor learn from you.

You say that I do not deny that the individual moments of this time can be separated from neighboring moments, that is, that an enduring thing can cease to be at individual moments. How neatly you act, when you take as conceded the very thing in controversy. I certainly do not even recognize what moments you say I do not deny. For I do indeed confess that an enduring thing can cease to be at individual moments, but namely at moments of that common duration, at the same moments, and not others, at which other things also enduring together can cease to be, and which cannot be separated from one another because of their indissoluble concatenation. If you take it otherwise, why do you not explain it? For that a thing can cease at this moment, and can cease at the following moment, and can cease at another moment, means that the thing has a weak power of enduring. But it does not mean that it has moments, or parts of time, which can cease within the moments or parts of another time. Hence comes that common error by which they say poetically that time devours things. In truth time wears away nothing; physical causes wear away and destroy, if not at one time, at least at another.

## Notes

- Gassendi attacks the temporal premise before the syllogism: the nature of time is not a transparent natural-light datum.
- Ancient disagreement is not appealed to as simple authority but as evidence of obscurity; Descartes' isolated Mind cannot answer by asserting its own authority.
- The Augustine quote becomes methodological evidence: Gassendi can gesture toward time but cannot make its nature clear enough to ground a proof.
- Gassendi's tentative model is a common flux or duration. Motion, especially celestial motion, measures time but does not change the tempo of time itself.
- There is only one time for co-enduring things, not multiplied private durations for each individual thing.
- Descartes begs the question when he turns the power of a thing to cease at any moment into separable internal moments belonging to that thing.
- `Tempus edax rerum` is demoted to poetic speech. Time does not destroy; physical causes destroy at times.
