## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' question about the nature of falsity by refusing to make falsity a positive thing outside intellect. Outside intellect, everything is transcendentally true: each thing is what it is and not another. Falsity belongs properly to intellect, and most properly to judgment, as a lack or privation of conformity between judgment and thing. A thing can be called false only by extrinsic denomination, when it is compared to a judgment that takes it for something else. The false-gold example completes the will/intellect argument: orichalcum is true orichalcum, but because its yellow color presents the same apparent species under which gold is usually hidden, intellect judges gold to be there; will then pursues the "false gold" just as it would true gold, because for intellect the same species appears in both.

## Working Translation

As for what you finally ask, what nature of the false I conceive: there is certainly no reason for you to think that I want the false as such, or falsity, to be something more than privative. For I locate falsity nowhere outside intellect, except in the manner of a denomination which they call extrinsic, since outside intellect nothing is false. Rather, all things are true by the truth called transcendental, by which each thing is what it is and not another.

For orichalcum, for example, is not false gold, but true orichalcum. It is denominated false gold only insofar as intellect thinks it to be gold, although it is not. This is like a wall being called right-hand, not because it is right-handed or has a right-hand part, but because the animal whose right side it is turns its right side toward the wall.

Falsity therefore is properly only in intellect itself; and among its operations, properly in judgment itself. Yet it could also be recognized in simple apprehensions insofar as they do not conform to things and provide judgments with an occasion for falsity.

Therefore when the judgment rendered about a thing conforms to the thing, since it enunciates the thing as such as it is, or enunciates that it is not such as it is not, it is called true. And when it lacks conformity of this kind, or enunciates the thing as such as it is not, or not such as it is, then it is called false. And this is the lack, or privation, of conformity in which I think the nature of the false consists.

But because it happens that this falsity is assigned to the thing, so that the defect of conformity seems to be not so much in the judgment compared with the thing as in the thing compared with the judgment, for that reason the thing itself too is called false by this comparison. And because it is nevertheless not thought false, but thought to be what is judged, for that reason it is said to be apprehended under the species of the true, insofar as it is something true without which it would not be apprehended.

Thus the yellow color of orichalcum is the true color of orichalcum. Yet meanwhile it is a species so similar to that under which gold is usually hidden that it is taken for such a species. And so an occasion is made for intellect to apprehend and judge that gold exists under it, although gold does not. Therefore what is true orichalcum in itself is called false gold in comparison with intellect.

Hence it is no wonder if will is determined by intellect to pursue this false gold just as much as to pursue true gold, since, as far as intellect is concerned, it takes the species of both as the same species of gold, so that, recognizing no difference, it judges gold to be equally under the one and under the other.

## Notes

- Falsity is privative, not a positive external nature.
- Outside intellect, nothing is false in the strict sense; things are transcendentally true as what they are.
- False predication of a thing is an extrinsic denomination, like calling a wall right-hand because of an animal's orientation toward it.
- Falsity belongs properly to judgment as lack or privation of conformity between judgment and thing.
- Simple apprehensions can be implicated only insofar as nonconforming presentations give judgment an occasion for falsity.
- The false-gold example closes the will/intellect problem: will follows intellect's presented species and pursues false gold because intellect has not distinguished its species from true gold.
