## Opening Finding

Gassendi opens a new line of pressure on the idea of body and the idea of self. If the mind is unextended, how can it receive or use an idea or species of an extended body? A corporeal species seems to need parts, extension, figure, position, and variety if it is to represent bodily parts, extension, figure, ordered location, and colors. The self-idea is no safer: knowing that one thinks, or saying that one is not extended, gives only an operation or a negation, not a positive grasp of the substance that operates. Gassendi then attacks the spatial status of mind. If mind is diffused through the whole body, it seems extended; if it is located only in brain or a point, the same problem remains, because physical points have extension and mathematical points cannot receive bodily spirits, sensory routes, or motion. The doubt closes by allowing, for argument, that mind might survive the gross body like scent leaving an apple, while denying that this proves incorporeality.

## Working Translation

On the species of a corporeal thing, which does not seem able to be received in an incorporeal mind; and on mind, which, if it coexists with body or an extended thing, does not seem able to be unextended.

"But," you say, "on one side I have a clear and distinct idea of myself insofar as I am only a thinking thing, not extended; and on the other side a distinct idea of body insofar as it is only an extended thing, not thinking."

Indeed, as for the idea of body first, it does not seem necessary to labor much over it. For if you pronounced this about the idea of body universally, we would have to repeat what we objected: it has to be proved by you that it is repugnant to corporeal nature that it be capable of thought. Otherwise the principle would be begged, since the question raised about you is whether you are not a subtle body, as if thinking were repugnant to body.

But because you pronounce it, and certainly deal only with that gross body from which you contend you are distinct and separable, I do not so much deny that you have an idea of it as deny that you can have one if you are indeed an unextended thing. For I ask you: how do you think a species or idea of a body that is extended can be received in you, an unextended subject?

For whether such a species proceeds from the body, it is without doubt corporeal, has parts outside parts, and is therefore extended; or whether it is impressed from somewhere else, because it must always represent an extended body, it must still have parts and likewise be extended. Otherwise, if it lacks parts, how will it represent parts? If it lacks extension, how will it represent an extended thing? If figure, how a figured thing? If position, how a thing having upper, lower, right, left, and oblique parts? If variety, how various colors, and so on? Therefore the idea does not seem altogether to lack extension. But unless it lacks extension, in what way will you, if you are unextended, be subjected to it? How will you fit it to yourself? How will you use it? How will you experience it as gradually being effaced and finally vanishing?

Next, as for the idea of yourself, nothing needs to be added to what has already been said, especially on the Second Meditation. From that it is proved that you are so far from having a clear and distinct idea of yourself that you seem to have none at all. For although you recognize that you think, you still do not know what kind of thing you are that thinks. Thus, since this operation alone is known, the principal thing remains hidden from you, namely the substance that operates. Hence a comparison occurs: you can be said to be like a blind man who, feeling heat and being told that it is from the sun, thinks he has a clear and distinct idea of the sun insofar as, if he is asked what the sun is, he can answer, "It is a heating thing."

But, you will say, here I add not only that I am a thinking thing, but also that I am a non-extended thing. Nevertheless, to pass over the fact that this is said without proof, although it is still in question, I ask first: do you therefore have a clear and distinct idea of yourself? You say that you are not extended; you say what you are not, not what you are. For having a clear and distinct, or what is the same, true and genuine idea of some thing, is it not necessary to know the thing itself positively and, so to speak, affirmatively? Is it enough to know that it is not some other thing? Will the idea of Bucephalus be clear and distinct, then, if someone at least knows about Bucephalus that he is not a fly?

But not to press this, I ask instead: are you therefore a non-extended thing? Are you not diffused through the body? I do not know what you would answer. For although from the beginning I took you to be only in the brain, I did this more by conjecturing than by fully grasping your opinion. I drew the conjecture from the words that follow later, when you say that you are not affected by all parts of the body, but only by the brain, or even by only one small part of it. Yet I was not fully certain whether you were therefore only in the brain, or part of it, since you can be in the whole body and be affected in only one part, just as we commonly admit that the soul is diffused through the whole body and yet sees only in the eye.

The following words raised a similar doubt: "and although the whole mind seems to be united to the whole body," and so on. In that place, to be sure, you do not assert that you are united to the whole body, but nevertheless you do not deny that you are united to it. However it stands, first let it be, if you please, that you are diffused through the whole body, whether you are the same as the soul or something different. I ask you: are you unextended, you who are stretched from head to heel? You who are made equal to the body? You who have parts corresponding to so many of its parts?

Will you say that you are unextended because you are whole in the whole and whole in any part? If you say this, I ask you: how do you grasp it? Can one thing be at the same time whole in many places? Let faith teach us this about the sacred mystery; here the dispute about you is as about a natural thing, and indeed from natural light. Is it permissible to understand that there are many places and not many things placed? And are not a hundred more than one? And if some thing is whole in one place, can it be in others unless it is outside itself, as place is outside places? Say what you like: at least it will be both obscure and uncertain whether you are whole in any part, rather than in individual parts through individual parts of yourself. And since it is far more evident that nothing can be whole at the same time in many places, it will become still more evident that you are not whole in the individual parts, but only whole in the whole, and therefore diffused through the whole by your parts, and so have extension.

Next, let it be that you are only in the brain, or only in a small part of it. You see that the very same difficulty remains, because however small that part is, it is nevertheless extended, and you will be co-extended with it; therefore you will be extended and will have particles corresponding to its particles. Will you say that you take the part of the brain as a point? Hardly credible, but let it be a point. If it is a physical point, the same difficulty remains, because such a point is extended and does not altogether lack parts. If it is a mathematical point, you know first that it does not exist except in imagination.

But let there be, or rather let there be imagined, a mathematical point in the brain to which you are joined and in which you exist. See how useless the fiction will be. For in order for it to be imagined, it must be imagined in the convergence of nerves, through which all parts informed by the soul transmit into the brain the ideas or species of things perceived by the senses. But first, all the nerves do not come together into a point, either because, since the brain is continued into the spinal marrow, many nerves pass into it along the whole back, or because those that tend into the middle of the head are not found to end in the same place of the brain.

But let us grant that they all converge; nevertheless their convergence cannot be in a mathematical point, because they are bodies, not mathematical lines, so that they could come together in a mathematical point. And even if we grant that they come together, the spirits carried through them will not be able to leave the nerves or enter the nerves, since they are bodies, and body cannot be in a non-place or pass through a non-place, such as a mathematical point is. And although we grant that they can be and pass through it, nevertheless you, existing in a point in which there are no right, left, upper, lower, or other regions, cannot judge from where they arrive or what they report.

I say the same about those which you would have to transmit for sensing, reporting, and moving. I pass over the fact that it cannot be grasped how you impress motion on them if you yourself are in a point, unless you yourself are body, or unless you have a body by which you touch them and at the same time drive them forward. For if you say that they move by themselves and that you only direct their motion, remember that somewhere you denied that body moves by itself, so that it could accordingly be inferred that you are the cause of that motion. Then explain to us how such direction can occur without some exertion of yourself and therefore motion. How can exertion toward some thing and motion of that thing occur without mutual contact of mover and moved? How can contact occur without body, when by natural light it is so clear that nothing can touch or be touched without body?

Yet why do I linger over these things, since it falls to you yourself to prove that you are an unextended thing and therefore incorporeal? Nor indeed, I think, will you draw an argument from the fact that a human being is commonly said to consist of body and soul, as if, since one part is called body, the other ought to be called non-body. For if you did this, you would give occasion for distinguishing in this way: a human being consists of a twofold body, namely a gross and a subtle body, so that when the former retains the common name of body, the name of soul is given to the latter. Moreover, the same will be said of other animals, to which you will not have conceded a mind equal to your own: blessed indeed if, on your authority, they have even a soul.

Hence, therefore, when you conclude that it is certain that you are really distinct from your body, you see that this will indeed be conceded, but it will not thereby be conceded that you are incorporeal rather than a species of very subtle body distinct from that grosser body. You add that you can therefore exist without it. But when it has been conceded that you can exist without that gross body just as scented vapor exists when, leaping from an apple, it is dispersed into the air, what will you have gained from that?

Certainly you would have gained something more than what the philosophers mentioned want, who think that you perish utterly in death, like a figure that so vanishes through a change of surface that afterward it is nothing, or plainly nothing at all. For since you were also some corporeal or subtle substance, you would not be said to vanish utterly in death or to pass plainly into nothing. Rather, you would subsist through your scattered parts, however much, because of the scattering, you would no longer think and would not be called a thinking thing, or mind, or soul.

Yet I always object all these things not because I doubt the conclusion intended by you, but because I distrust the force of the demonstration you have set out.

## Notes

- Gassendi now attacks the received or usable idea of body in an incorporeal mind.
- A bodily species or idea seems to require extension if it is to represent bodily extension, parts, figure, position, and color variety.
- Descartes' self-idea is said to know only the operation of thinking, not the substance that thinks.
- Adding "non-extended" gives only a negation, not a positive idea of what mind is.
- The blind-man/sun analogy returns the theme that knowing an effect or operation does not supply a full idea of the thing producing it.
- If mind is diffused through the whole body, Gassendi argues it seems extended through corresponding parts.
- If mind is only in the brain, the same problem recurs because any physical part is extended.
- If mind is put in a mathematical point, the point cannot handle bodily nerves, spirits, sensory directions, or motor contact.
- The gross-body distinction is conceded for argument, but it does not prove incorporeality; mind might be subtle body separable from gross body.
- Gassendi preserves the intended conclusion while attacking the force of Descartes' natural-light demonstration.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `species` is translated as "species" rather than "form" or "image" because Gassendi pairs it with `idea` and treats it as a representational item that can proceed from body or be impressed.

Technical: `punctum physicum` and `punctum mathematicum` are kept as "physical point" and "mathematical point." The distinction matters because Gassendi grants the mathematical point only as a fiction or imagination.
