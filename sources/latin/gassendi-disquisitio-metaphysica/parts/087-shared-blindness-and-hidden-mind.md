## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' claim that Gassendi knows nothing more about mind. He grants, even embraces, blindness about the nature of mind, but denies that shared blindness can be turned into Descartes' clear knowledge. If no one could see the sun, no one could infer that the sun contains only heat. Before telescopes, no one was entitled to deny Saturn's rings or Jupiter's attendants merely because no instrument exposed them. Likewise, if no one has the eyes, telescope, or key for mind's hidden interior, Descartes cannot infer that nothing is there beyond thought. Gassendi accepts that he knows no more than Descartes about mind, but rejects the stronger charge that he does not know even what Descartes knows.

## Working Translation

Next, you say that no one can argue that the idea of the sun which the blind man forms does not contain all things that can be perceived about the sun, except those who, endowed with sight, additionally recognize its light and figure. "But you know nothing more, and not even that very thing which I know, about mind; so that in this matter you rather can be called blind, while I, with the whole human race, can at most be called dim-sighted."

Very well, excellent man. Indeed, I do not unwillingly acknowledge the difference, and I admit that, as far as the nature of mind is concerned, I am altogether blind. Yet let us examine what this whole point amounts to.

Plainly, your aim is this: since I cannot say what mind has besides thought, you take yourself to know whatever is in mind, and therefore to have clear and distinct cognition of mind. So you do the same thing as a blind man who, speaking with another blind man, would say that he knows whatever is in the sun because he feels its heat, and the other cannot say what else is there. But besides that other blind man there are sighted people who recognize more than he does. Besides me, however, are there no people who recognize more in mind than you do, you with whom the whole human race stands?

I determine nothing about others, and I do not want to presume so badly about the keenness and skill of the whole race, or of absolutely all human beings who have been, are, or will be, that they could not or cannot notice something in mind that at present lies hidden from you. I am only weighing the force of your reasoning. And I first consider: if someone had been born among the Andabatae, or if, as all moles are deprived of eyes, God had willed all human beings to be born blind and yet to feel the heat of the sun, by what right could an Andabata, or any other mortal, assert that there is nothing in the sun besides heat, since in that case there would be no seeing person who could refute him?

Then, since in all past ages, and until scarcely more than thirty years ago, no one had a telescope at hand by which he could refute someone who said that the star of Saturn lacks rings, and the star of Jupiter lacks attendants, would anyone rightly have pronounced that there are no rings on Saturn and no attendants around Jupiter? Again, if with a palace closed someone says that there is nothing in it except the walls he sees, because no one comes along to open it and show how many things are contained in it, would he have asserted this rightly?

Further, if God has willed us to be born in such a way that we lack eyes, or keenness of sight, by which, apart from thought, we may know thoroughly nothing about the substance and the other things that are in mind; if so far there is no one who can make a telescope for this purpose; if there is no doorkeeper present to introduce us and show that there is something other than what is known to you: by what right do you suppose that there is nothing besides? And do you not yourself admit that it cannot be known that God has put nothing more in some thing than what we know about it, unless God himself reveals it? How, then, does that agree with this reasoning of yours?

Certainly, if that were legitimate, mortals would have no reason to complain that anything is unknown to them. For what of the bowels of the earth? There is nothing under this crust, so to speak, of earth, because there is no one who proves that the inner space is full, or says what is in it, or what the earth contains at all besides these things which we see and touch. What likewise of the amplitude of the world? Of all the powers of all herbs? Indeed, what of absolutely everything? Plainly nothing is unknown, because beyond the things that are known no one proves that anything is known, and therefore that anything exists.

But if this is ridiculous, by what right do you boast that you know whatever is in mind because I cannot say what is in it besides what you know? Because I am ignorant, are you therefore learned? Because I am a private man and in straitened circumstances, are you for that reason a king and richer than Croesus? The boast is not unlike. And as for the fact that I know no more than you about mind, I accept it, as is fair. But as for the claim that I do not know even that very thing which you do, that must be judged by others.

For I know mind as a thinking thing, that is, as understanding, doubting, affirming, denying, imagining, willing, sensing, and so on, which you say that you know. And unless I knew this before you knew it, at least I knew it before you said it. But since you testify that you know nothing else about mind, I do not see how you can say that I do not know even that very thing about it which you do, nor why I should be blind rather than you at most dim-sighted, that is, indeed seeing clearly but, out of modesty, calling yourself dim-sighted; and this along with the whole human race, which would also see clearly, or at most also be dim-sighted.

Yet I know people from the human race full of candor, who frankly reject what they are not and do not deny that they are blind concerning the nature of mind and other matters so hidden. So if perhaps we all are half-blind, this distinction can be established: some profess that they are blind, as they are; others imitate Seneca's Harpaste, in refusing to recognize themselves as what they are.

## Notes

- Gassendi grants blindness about mind's nature, but refuses Descartes' move from shared ignorance to Cartesian knowledge.
- The blind-person analogy is widened: if everyone were blind, no one could infer that the sun contains only heat.
- The telescope examples make absence of refutation historically contingent, not evidence of absence.
- Saturn's rings and Jupiter's attendants are used as cases where instrument-lack once blocked discovery.
- The closed palace example gives the same structure: an unopened interior cannot be declared empty because no one has entered.
- Gassendi explicitly invokes Descartes' own standard: only divine revelation could prove God put no more in a thing than we know.
- Knowing mind as thinking, doubting, affirming, denying, imagining, willing, and sensing is conceded as common ground.
- The real dispute is whether that common ground exhausts mind's nature.

## Translation Risk

Historical: `Andabatae` is kept as a proper name. Gassendi uses the proverbial blind-fighting gladiators to imagine an entire human condition without sight.

Historical: `ansulae` and `asseclae` are translated as "rings" and "attendants." The reference is to telescopic discoveries around Saturn and Jupiter, not to unaided visual astronomy.

Historical: Seneca's `Harpaste` is retained as a proper-name allusion: the point is refusal to acknowledge one's own blindness.
