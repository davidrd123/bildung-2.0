## Opening Finding

This first translation tranche makes the book's argumentative texture clearer than Cassirer's footnote alone. Gassendi is not simply citing sense against innate ideas. He is trying to force Descartes' broad `idea` back into a sensory-image model: ideas come from external things, and the scientific idea of the sun is one sensory idea enlarged by reason. Descartes' reply names the dispute directly: Gassendi restricts `idea` to images painted in phantasy.

## Working Translation

DUBITATION III.

On the adventitious origin of ideas, proved from those ideas that are lacking to the born-blind and the deaf; and on the single idea of the sun, which, having been received by sense, is enlarged by reason.

You seem next to turn into doubt not only whether any ideas proceed from external things, but even whether there are any external things at all. And thus you seem to infer that, although there are in you ideas of things that are called external, nevertheless the ideas do not prove that the things exist, since they do not necessarily proceed from those things themselves, but could be from you, or exist in some other way I do not know.

I think this is why you said earlier that you did not first perceive earth, heaven, and stars, but ideas of earth, heaven, and stars, by which there could be deception. And so, if you do not yet believe that earth, heaven, stars, and the rest exist, why, I ask, do you walk upon the earth, or move your body so as to look at the sun? Why do you go to the fire so that you may feel heat? Why to the table, or to food, so that you may satisfy hunger? Why do you move your tongue to speak, or your hand to write these things to us? Certainly such things can be said or thought up subtly; but they do not advance the business at all. And since in fact you do not doubt that these things are outside you, let us proceed seriously and in good faith, and speak of things as they are.

But if, after supposing the existence of external things, you think it has been abundantly demonstrated that we cannot derive from them the ideas we have of them, then you must answer not only the reasons you yourself raise against yourself, but also those that can be raised besides. That is, you take it as if ideas are admitted to come from things because we seem to have been taught so by nature, and because we experience that they do not depend on us or on our will. But even if we say nothing about the reasons or their solutions, this too, among other things, had to be raised and answered: why there is no idea of color in one born blind, or of voice in one born deaf, unless because those external things could not of themselves send any species of themselves into that unhappy person's mind, since the doors were closed from birth and obstacles to their passage were set there continuously.

You then press the example of the sun. Since there are two ideas of it, one drawn from the senses, as when it appears small, and another from astronomical reasons, by which it is conceived as immense, the one, you say, is more similar and truer which is not drawn from the senses but elicited from innate notions, or produced in whatever other way. But in fact those twin ideas of the sun are alike and true, or conformable to the sun, though one more so and the other less so. They are like two ideas of the same person, one sent out from ten paces, another from a hundred or a thousand. They are alike and true, or conformable, but the former more, the latter less, because the one that comes nearer is less worn away, the one that comes from farther away more so, as would be explained if it could be done briefly and if you yourself could sufficiently grasp it.

As for our perceiving by mind alone that vast idea of the sun, it is not for that reason drawn from some innate notion. Rather, the idea that enters through sense, insofar as experience proves and reason resting upon it confirms that distant things appear smaller than nearby things, is enlarged by the mind's own power by as much as it is established that the sun is distant from us and is equal in its diameter to so many terrestrial semidiameters.

And do you want to grasp that nothing of this idea is implanted by nature? Seek it in someone born blind. You will discover, first, that it is not colored or luminous in his mind; then, that it is not round unless someone has warned him and he has previously handled a round body with his hands; finally, that it is not so great unless, moved either by reason or by authority, he has enlarged what was previously accepted.

Yet, to insert this point, I ask you: we ourselves, who have seen the sun so often, who have so often observed its apparent diameter, who have so often reasoned about its true diameter, do we have any image of the sun other than the common one? We do indeed infer by reason that the sun is one hundred sixty and more times larger than the earth. But do we therefore have an idea of so vast a body? We enlarge this image received by the senses as much as we can; we strain the mind as much as we can. But still we make for ourselves nothing but sheer darkness. And whenever we want to have a distinct thought of the sun, the mind must return to the species it received through the intervening eye. It is enough for the mind if it does not deny that the sun is in fact larger, that if the eye were moved nearer it would obtain a larger idea, and yet meanwhile it attends to as much as it has.

REPLY.

Here, in order to overthrow the reasons on account of which I judged that one should doubt the existence of material things, you ask why, then, I walk upon the earth, and so on. In this, the question is plainly begged: for you assume what was to be proved, namely that it is so certain that I walk upon the earth that it cannot be doubted. And when you want to add, to the reasons I raised against myself and refuted, another one, namely why there is no idea of color in one born blind or of voice in one born deaf, you plainly show that you have no reason of any weight. For how do you know that there is no idea of colors in one born blind, since sometimes in us, even if we close our eyes, sensations of light and colors are nevertheless stirred up? And even if what you say is granted, could it not be said with equal right by someone who denies the existence of material things that one born blind has no ideas of colors because his mind is destitute of the faculty for forming them, just as you say that he does not have them because he is deprived of eyes?

What you add about the two ideas of the sun proves nothing. When you take both as one because they refer to one sun, it is the same as if you said that true and false do not differ when they are affirmed of the same subject. And when you deny that the one we gather from astronomical reasons is an idea, you restrict the name `idea` to images alone, painted in phantasy, contrary to what I expressly assumed.

REJOINDER.

I would make myself plainly ridiculous if I assumed that I had to prove to you that you walk upon the earth, see, are warmed, eat, speak, sense, and so on. You said that no one of sound mind had ever seriously doubted those things. If, therefore, you now seriously doubt them, and when I asked that we proceed seriously and in good faith and speak of things as they are, you do not accept the condition, what reason is there for me to press this matter?

## Notes

- `adventitia Idearum origine` now matters as a title term. Gassendi's local burden is the external, non-innate origin of ideas.
- The born-blind / deaf argument is not merely empirical color psychology; it is the gate through which external things send `species` into the mind.
- The sun example is a test of whether rational enlargement changes the kind of idea. Gassendi says no: it is one idea, received by sense and amplified.
- Descartes' reply is valuable because it names the category clash. For Descartes, an idea gathered from astronomical reasons is still an idea; Gassendi's denial narrows `idea` to image and phantasy.
- This supports Cassirer's reading, but it also gives the book its own live problem: Gassendi's anti-skeptical common-sense pressure and his sensory-source theory are doing different jobs at once.
