## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' widened use of `proprietas` by separating physical property from logical attribute. A property is independent of intellectual operation; an attribute depends on the intellect that attributes or predicates. Every property can become an attribute, but not every attribute is a property. If existence is made a special property or attribute under the genus of property, then ordinary predication breaks: "existence" cannot be predicated of thing, substance, property, wisdom, or any coordinate property. Gassendi therefore treats existence not as a special property, but as the most general or transcendental condition applying across all genera and species. The deeper test is removability: remove a property such as sight and the human being remains, though blind; remove existence and the thing is not imperfect, but nothing.

## Working Translation

INSTANTIA.

You do not see of what genus of things I want existence to be. I am not surprised by that, since I do not want it to be of any certain genus. But do you fully see what you are doing meanwhile, when you want it to be of some special genus?

Plainly you seem first to conceive Thing, or, as people commonly say, Being or Something, as the most general genus, which you distribute into two species, or subordinate genera, namely Substance and Property.

Then, dividing the genus of Properties into its species, you take existence as one of them, and meanwhile confuse the words Property and attribute.

To say something first about this very confusion, which seems to have been the cause of the equivocation: it is known that the word Property sounds like something physical and independent of the operation of the intellect, while the word attribute sounds like something logical and dependent on the intellect that attributes, or predicates.

From this it follows that every property can indeed become or be an attribute, but not conversely that every attribute is a Property. This is clear even from the fact that Substance too is an attribute, though it is not a Property, but a genus distinct from it; indeed Thing itself is an attribute, and is a genus more general than substance and property.

Therefore an attribute is plainly something not only transcendent, such as Thing or Being is, but also super-transcendent, insofar as it is predicated of Thing or Being. Yet it is transcendent or super-transcendent precisely insofar as it is something logical and belongs to the intellect, which by its thought goes beyond the whole universe of Things.

For this reason, so that there is no equivocation, you must either abstain from the word Attribute, or, by limiting it to the notion of property, understand attribute as something distinct from Substance and less widely extending than Thing.

So now, when you want Existence to be a special Property or special attribute, the word property can indeed be predicated of Existence, and so can the word Thing itself, insofar as it belongs to every genus and so can be predicated of a species. But the word Existence itself, neither abstractly nor concretely, will be able to be predicated: not of Property universally, because no species is predicated of its genus; not of Thing universally, because what is not predicated of the lower or less general thing is not predicated of the higher or more general thing either.

Again, neither will it be predicated of Substance, because no species is predicated of an opposed genus, nor of that of which the genus to which it belongs is not predicated. Finally, neither will it be predicated of any other property, because no species is predicated of another species that is under the same genus.

Thus, just as we do not say, Animal is human, Living body is human, Plant is human, Horse is human, so neither will we be able to say, Property is existent, or exists; Thing exists; Substance exists; Wisdom, or some other property, exists.

Do you see, then, how many things would follow as not existing from the fact that you want Existence to be of a special genus of things? Do you see why I have not referred it to some special genus of things, but have rather treated it as the most general, or transcendental, genus, which belongs to all genera and species of things in such a way that, unless it can be predicated of them, they themselves neither are things nor can be counted as things?

This, certainly, is why I earlier made under the name of perfection the objection that now occurs to be made under the name of property.

You want to know whether Existence should be held as a property of the thing that exists. Consider whether a thing can be deprived of it, or be understood as deprived of it, in such a way that some thing still remains, or is understood to remain.

You will find that this is so in the case of all properties. When they are removed either in reality or in the mind, the substance whose properties they are is still understood to persist as some thing.

But when existence has been removed in reality, or is understood as removed, I ask whether the thing whose existence it was will persist as something or as nothing. You plainly see that it will become nothing, because what does not exist is nothing.

For even when you suppose it to persist in parts, you also suppose that existence has not been removed in parts. But when you have supposed that no existence remains, then the thing will be plainly nothing.

Therefore I marked from this the difference between some perfection or property, for example sight, and Existence itself.

For a human being to whom sight belongs, if he is deprived of it, does not on that account become nothing, but only blind, that is, imperfect and deprived of sight. But whatever the thing is to which existence belongs, if it is deprived of existence, then it is truly said to be none or nothing, not imperfect or deprived of existence, since what is called imperfect or deprived is still supposed to be something, even without the perfection or property it had.

## Notes

- Gassendi rejects Descartes' broad use of `proprietas` by distinguishing physical property from logical attribute.
- A property is independent of intellect; an attribute depends on attribution or predication by intellect.
- Every property can be an attribute, but not every attribute is a property. Substance and Thing can be attributes without being properties.
- If existence is made a special species under Property, then "existence" cannot be predicated of Property, Thing, Substance, Wisdom, or any coordinate property.
- Gassendi therefore treats existence as most general or transcendental, not as a special genus or property.
- The removability test preserves the Part 061 objection: a property can be removed while the subject remains; remove existence and no subject remains.
- Sight supplies the example. A human being without sight is blind and imperfect; a thing without existence is not imperfect, but nothing.
