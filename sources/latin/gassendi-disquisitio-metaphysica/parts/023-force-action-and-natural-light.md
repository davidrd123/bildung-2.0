## Opening Finding

Gassendi now attacks Descartes' confirmation that the same force and action are needed for conservation as for new creation. The nature of time is still too obscure to make the claim clear; creation has not yet been established; and Descartes does not know what the action of creation is, so he cannot know whether conservation is the same action. The house and chick examples counter the sunlight model. Gassendi also widens the issue: even if a first cause is invoked, Descartes has not proved creation ex nihilo rather than production from eternally preexisting matter or administration of an eternal world. Finally, the claim that creation/conservation is manifest by natural light collapses because philosophers using natural light denied creation from nothing, while Scholastics placed creation and conservation primarily under supernatural light.

## Working Translation

Fifth, from these points it is clear what strength belongs to the confirmation of the whole proposition when you say: "For it is clear, to one attending to the nature of time, that exactly the same force and action is needed to conserve any thing in the individual moments in which it endures as would be needed to create the same thing anew if it did not yet exist." For the nature of time is not clear, such that by attending to it you could make clear a matter of such obscurity. And when you attend to these things, you have not yet established that anything is created; nor do you know what action creation is, so that you could know whether conservation is the same action or a different one, and whether it proceeds from exactly the same force or from another.

And although you can remember the example of light and similar cases, whose weakness has already been explained, the example of the house can occur to mind. A house is certainly not conserved and does not endure at individual moments by the same force by which it was built. Likewise a chick is not conserved and does not endure by the same force and action by which it was generated; and there are countless other examples.

And although you can say that the account is different for the first cause, this very point is in question, and you have not yet proved that such a cause is given. Nor have you proved that its action is creation from nothing rather than production from eternally preexisting matter, or the simple administration of a world existing from eternity. You yourself do not remember whether you were created by that cause, nor when, where, from what, on what occasion, by what force, or how. And even if you remembered, and the matter were clear to you about yourself, you nevertheless could not say the same of any thing whatever, since you would not know the condition and nature of every thing. Finally, to one attending, it seems rather clear that there is less labor in conserving a thing, as I said about the house, than in producing it.

Sixth, when you infer in this way that conservation differs from creation by reason alone and that this is one of the things manifest by natural light, nothing else comes to mind to say except that you are excessively blessed, since all things are so manifest to your natural light. For that the same thing should occur so spontaneously to you, stripped of all antecedent knowledge, which occurred to the most learned and subtle theologians as well as philosophers only after long studies and deep searching into divine and human things, should surely be reckoned a happiness beyond comparison. But tell me, please: would you at least have brought out these words in this way if you had not received them from the Scholastics? And since they refer conservation, just as creation, especially to supernatural light, did you have no scruple about defecting from them and referring it to natural light alone?

And since conservation cannot be manifest by natural light without creation also being manifest, from which it differs by reason alone, did you think you had been persuaded by mere demonstration that creation exists just as conservation does? And if so, how do you think it can happen that something is called manifest by natural light which all philosophers led by the light of nature alone not only did not hold as manifest, but even asserted the opposite, defending that nothing is created, or that nothing comes from nothing? Or did you believe that you were reasoning legitimately in this way?

That is manifest by natural light which was unmanifest to all philosophers using the light of nature alone.

But creation, and conservation consequent on it as differing only by reason, was unmanifest to all philosophers using the light of nature alone.

Therefore creation, and conservation consequent on it, is manifest by natural light.

## Notes

- The fifth objection targets Descartes' confirmation of the premise: the same force and action cannot be inferred from the nature of time because time remains obscure.
- Gassendi insists that Descartes has not yet established creation, nor the action proper to creation, and so cannot compare creation and conservation as actions.
- The house and chick examples return against the sunlight model: ordinary persistence is not powered by the same force as first production.
- The first-cause appeal remains unproved and underdescribed. Gassendi leaves open alternatives to creation ex nihilo: production from eternally preexisting matter or administration of an eternal world.
- The sixth objection attacks the migration from Scholastic/supernatural teaching to natural light. Descartes claims as naturally manifest what philosophers using natural light denied.
- The closing mock syllogism makes the contradiction explicit: what was unmanifest to natural-light philosophers cannot simply be declared manifest by natural light.
