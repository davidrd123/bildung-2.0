## Opening Finding

Gassendi now gives the species argument an optical model. A visible species is not a point: the tower-image in the retina occupies a portion of the retinal surface, and a species serving imagination in the brain must likewise have some parts if it is to count as an image of the tower. Even when the argument is transferred to separated mind, Gassendi holds the minimum claim: a species need not have as many parts as the thing, and a very small representative can stand for a very large object, but it must have some extension if it represents extension and parts. This keeps the pressure from Part 091 alive in a more empirical register.

## Working Translation

And, to add this word, it is first clear concerning the species of sight that it is endowed with parts. For the retina at the back of the eye is a certain concave mirror, in which the image of a tower, for example, is painted in such a way that, through its mediation, the power of sight perceives the tower.

Moreover, this image is not painted on a point of the retina, but on some portion of it. This is clear by experience, from which we also have that the luminous rays, from which the species of the thing does not differ, come together not on the retina itself, but a little before it, that is, within the vitreous humor. Thus, once the crossing has occurred because of passage through the crystalline lens, the rays, somewhat spread out, strike distinct parts of the retina distinctly. And certainly even spectacles themselves, according as they are convex or concave, either draw away or promote the crossing or coming-together, sufficiently show that this kind of spreading is necessary. The matter can be understood very well from comparison with a telescope, through which the rays of the transmitted image, in order to create a picture, must be received not at the very point of coming together but beyond it.

Thus the image occupies a definite portion of the retina, namely as much as the rays coming from the object, for example the tower, reach. And since the appearance of the largest or total object of vision is roughly a hemisphere, which the species fills in the retinal cavity limited by the opening of the pupil, the tower will appear as large as the part it occupies of that quasi-hemisphere, and therefore as large as the portion it obtains in the cavity of the retina through its species. So if the eye recedes so far that the tower, becoming always a smaller and smaller portion of the hemisphere, is finally only like a point of it, and from its species there is only a point in the retinal cavity, and the image itself is not painted as having parts and some extension, then there will not even be an image, and the tower will become wholly invisible.

Next, to transfer the matter into the brain, where you want there to be a species serving imagination: certainly that species, if it is in a point, cannot be or be called an image of the tower, since, to be an image, it must have parts which are representative of the tower's parts. Make it smaller, as much as you wish, than the one that was in the retina, just as in fact the parts of the optic nerve, into which the retina is gathered and which is implanted in the brain, are more contracted; however small it is, it is always necessary that it have parts and some extension.

Finally, to come to mind itself: since at least a separated mind can understand a tower, and therefore be informed by its species, make the species as thin, minute, and tiny as you wish. Nevertheless, because it is going to represent parts and extension, it must have parts and extension, and be at least more extended than a mathematical point.

Nor indeed is it necessary, whether the species belongs to the imaginative faculty or to the intellect, that for this reason it have as many parts as the thing itself has. For even the species of sight itself does not have as many, I do not say of the internal parts, but of the superficial parts, since because of the unevenness of the surface, few of its parts are turned toward the pupil and the back of the retina and express their picture there. From this it follows that among bodies nearer to us and bodies farther away even to the outermost stars, whatever is seen under the same angle, or under the same portion of that already mentioned quasi-hemisphere, appears equally large, because from the more remote bodies, even if they are very large, no more parts are turned toward the eye and imprint their species than from the nearer and smallest ones.

I say this on account of the idea of the magnitude of the earth. For just as that cavity or capacity of the retina is like a physical center in which all the angles are terminated which embrace the magnitude of the sun, moon, and other bodies spread over roughly the whole hemisphere above the eye, and for that reason the retina does not have as much magnitude as the sun, moon, and other bodies have in themselves, so nothing seems to prevent mind also from having extension which, even if it is understood to be far smaller than that cavity of the retina or even than the complex of the optic nerve, is nevertheless still like a center in which the angles of all the things presented to it are terminated, among them the magnitude of the earth, without therefore having as much magnitude as all the objects together, and especially the earth.

For since the sides of any angle analogically embrace a portion of a circle and sphere, whether they have been produced farther or terminated nearer, the largest thing can therefore be represented by the smallest. Yet these things cannot be explained in a few words, and however the matter stands, the difficulty always remains unresolved: how does an image represent parts, if it itself lacks parts?

## Notes

- Gassendi shifts from a general idea/species objection into optical anatomy.
- Retinal representation becomes the first evidence: an image of a tower occupies a portion of retina rather than a point.
- The optic nerve and brain transfer preserve the same minimum: a smaller species may still represent, but not as a wholly unextended point.
- Separated mind is used as the hardest case. If it can understand a tower, Gassendi says some species representing extension and parts must be present even there.
- The part does not require a one-to-one map between object-parts and species-parts. Small species can represent large bodies under angular proportion.
- The residual claim is narrow but persistent: representation of parts seems to require at least some part-structure in the image or species.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `species` is left as "species" because "image," "form," and "representation" each erase part of the argument. Here it ranges from retinal image through brain/imagination species to a possible intellectual species.

Edition/OCR: `latera anguli cujusque` is normalized from a damaged OCR line. The sense is clear enough for this draft, but the exact phrase should be checked before close citation.

Contextual: Gassendi's optical anatomy is doing argumentative work, not serving as a modern physiology claim. It should be read as a pressure model for part-bearing representation.
