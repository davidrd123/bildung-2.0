## Opening Finding

Gassendi presses Descartes' God-dependent eternal essences into a dilemma. If immutable and eternal natures are from themselves, then they are independent of God and God's willing adds nothing. If they are produced by God, then they passed from non-being to being, became effects, and therefore cannot be both non-existing and eternal in the sense Descartes needs. The Jupiter-and-fates analogy fails because Jupiter is imagined before the fates he establishes, while God cannot be before eternal natures if those natures are genuinely eternal. The triangle sharpens the problem: if God could have made triangle-nature otherwise, a triangle need not have three sides and three angles; if he could not, then triangle-nature is necessary from itself rather than from divine choice. Gassendi's alternative is to recognize no true thing besides God unless it is actually created, existing, and singular. Uncreated possibles have no present reality or truth except as future things; universal natures and ideas arise when intellect forms general notions from observed or quasi-possible singulars.

## Working Translation

What I said was this: it seems hard to posit some immutable and eternal nature besides God thrice-greatest. You object that whether I want it to be hard or soft, it is enough for you that it is true, namely because you make it not existing and not independent of God.

So I congratulate you on having a palate to which nothing of this kind is hard, to which everything is as soft as possible and also easiest to swallow. I congratulate you too on the perspicacity by which you profess to recognize natures, that is, true things, which nevertheless are not existing; which are nowhere, neither inside the world nor outside the world; which are not God; and which nevertheless depend on God.

Since no law forbids me to ask how you grasp this, come now: when you say that God willed and disposed that natures should be immutable and eternal, either the natures themselves were from themselves and unproduced, and God only willed and disposed them, just as a builder wills and disposes for a building the stones he finds already made and does not make; or they were produced by God, and God was their author, who, in making them, willed and disposed them.

If the former, then the natures were through themselves and independently of God. God willed and disposed in vain that they be immutable and eternal, since they were already such from themselves. For suppose that God had not willed and had not disposed: would they have been any less such?

If the latter, first, they were not immutable, since they passed from non-being, as they say, to being, or from possibility to act. Second, they were not eternal, because in order to be made they had to be not-being, and so at some time they began to be, which conflicts with an eternal thing. Third, therefore, they were effects, since every production is the production of some effect. Therefore they also existed, since every production terminates in existence. Therefore they were not only immutable and eternal, as you assert, but also existing, which you deny, while you soften for yourself the thing that is hard for me by saying that the question is about a non-existing thing.

From this you surely see how fittingly you take that example about the fates from the poets, and indeed from the Stoics. For when Jupiter established the fates, he is supposed to have existed before the fates, and he made decrees about things which could have been otherwise, or not have been. But God was not before natures, if they were indeed immutable and eternal. If they could not be otherwise than they are, then otherwise they would not be the natures of the things of which they are natures.

And tell me, please: when God deliberated about the nature of the triangle and decided what sort of thing he wanted the triangle to be, could it have happened that he decided otherwise about the triangle? Or could there have been a triangle that did not consist of three sides and three angles?

Since, however, God thrice-greatest is not bound to things established by him as the poets' Jupiter is bound to the fates, but by his absolute power can destroy whatever he has established, I ask you: by what reasoning do you grasp that God could have so disposed concerning the triangle that now, if he absolutely wills, by destroying its nature he can do what he could do from the beginning, namely make the triangle not consist of three sides and three angles?

And do not say that, if God destroys it or decides otherwise, it will not be a triangle but another figure. For when afterward there is a figure consisting of three sides and the same number of angles, it will not cease to be a triangle. It will become manifest that the nature of the triangle is such, not because God willed to make it such rather than otherwise, but because it is such by necessity of itself.

How much more satisfactory, then, it seems to recognize no true thing besides God thrice-greatest unless it is actually created by him, actually existing, and actually singular. Things not yet created and not existing, but merely possible, should be thought to have neither reality nor truth; or, if they are judged to have any, it must be understood as future. Therefore they do not really have reality so much, since they themselves do not exist and therefore do not have anything, as they will one day have it, namely when they will have come to be.

Finally, things, or rather ideas, should not be admitted in any other way than from the operation of intellect. From several singular things observed, or held as quasi-possible, intellect as it were draws out and forms certain general notions. Just as they are drawn from singulars, so they can be attributed to singulars as agreeing with them.

In this way, certainly, the things themselves, or universal natures, can be called dependent on God, insofar as the singulars from which they are formed, and from which they do not in reality differ, depend on God. Moreover, the intellect and its consideration, by which they become universal, also have God as author. They can even be called immutable to the extent that God willed singulars to be constantly of this kind, and eternal to the extent that, if God had willed to produce singulars continuously from eternity, intellect could have drawn such notions from them.

And you, indeed, reasoned in this way:

If there are in me ideas of figures which either do not exist, or of which there can be no suspicion that they slipped in through the senses, and if I can demonstrate various properties concerning those figures, then surely such natures are immutable and eternal, and not drawn from singulars.

But there are in me ideas of such figures, which I could imagine even if they neither existed anywhere in the world outside my thought nor had ever existed there, such as the triangle and others, concerning which there can be no suspicion that they slipped in through the senses; and I can demonstrate various properties both concerning the triangle and concerning these.

Therefore their natures are immutable and eternal, and not drawn from singulars.

## Notes

- Gassendi turns Descartes' God-dependent eternal essences into a production dilemma: either unproduced and independent, or produced and therefore temporal, effected, and existing.
- The phrase "non-existing nature" is treated as a softening word that conceals the harder ontology.
- The Jupiter/fates analogy fails because Jupiter can be imagined before the fates, while eternal natures cannot stand after God in the same temporal or production order without losing eternality.
- The triangle example is sharpened into an absolute-power problem: if God could have made triangle-nature otherwise, the three-sided triangle is not necessary; if he could not, the necessity belongs to the triangle's nature rather than to God's choice.
- Gassendi's alternative is singularist and actualist: a true thing besides God must be created, existing, and singular.
- Possibles have no present reality or truth except as future, not-yet-existing things.
- Universal natures remain dependent on God only through the singulars from which intellect forms them and through the God-authored intellect that universalizes them.
- The final syllogism stores Descartes' argument for the next section: ideas of non-sensed geometrical figures with demonstrable properties are supposed to prove immutable, eternal, non-abstracted natures.
