## Opening Finding

Gassendi turns Descartes' answer about the blind man's idea of the sun into a critique of aspectual clear and distinct cognition. If a blind man has a clear idea of the sun as heating, then a person struck in the dark could claim a clear idea of the striker as a striking thing. This does not answer what the thing is; it only names the predicate by which the thing becomes known. Gassendi applies the same pressure to `res cogitans`: to know mind as thinking thing is, on his account, like knowing the sun as heating thing. It gives the operation, not the nature or substance. Descartes' reply that cognition of a thinking thing extends more widely than cognition of a heating thing also fails, because the issue is not breadth or capacity but fullness relative to the object. A small vessel can be as full as a large one.

## Working Translation

Concerning the idea of the mind itself, and as regards the comparison made with the blind man, "That blind man," you say, "can have a clear and distinct idea of the sun as a heating thing, although he does not have one of the same thing as an illuminating thing." Here I now seem to recognize what you think it is to have clear and distinct cognition of some thing. It is enough, evidently, that it become known to us by any adjunct of itself whatever, and in whatever way, or that it make us have a sense of itself and arouse a concept, for us to be said to have a clear and distinct idea of it, provided it is understood that we have the idea of it as such, or insofar as it becomes known to us.

In this way, then, if someone meets you in thick darkness and silently strikes you, you will say that you have clear and distinct cognition of him, although you have been able neither to see nor to distinguish him. And when you are asked, "Who, then, is that man?" you will answer, "He is a striking thing." And when it is pressed, "Can you say nothing else?" you will reply, "I did not say that I had any cognition of him other than as a striking thing." The same will be furnished in other cases.

But so that we do not run off to other things: you had said that you have a clear and distinct idea of mind. And since it could be asked of you what, then, mind is, you had said, "It is a thinking thing." Then I objected, "Do you know nothing else, or can you tell us nothing else?" You became heated, and when I asked further about the nature of mind, of which you boast that you have an idea and that you deliver clear and distinct cognition, you attacked me, saying that it was worthy of flesh, such as I was, to want to know and to track down by an almost chemical labor the intimate nature or substance of mind, when it is enough to know of it that it is a thing, and indeed a thinking thing.

I added that this was as if a blind man who had felt the heat of the sun were to say that he had a clear and distinct idea of the sun, and therefore profess that he was delivering clear and distinct cognition of the sun's nature, because if he were asked what, then, the sun is, he could answer, "It is a heating thing." And when he were pressed that this does not perform what was promised, he would become heated and say that, for having clear and distinct cognition of the sun and its nature, it is enough that it be called a thing, and indeed a heating thing.

Therefore, since the question was about the idea, not of mind as it is thinking, nor of the sun as it is heating, for no one is ignorant that mind thinks, and not even the blind man is ignorant that the sun heats, but of each as it has a nature, which is contained obscurely and confusedly under the name "thing," you answered nothing about the thing, but touched only what was not in controversy.

Nevertheless, I admit that much. And from the fact that you concede that in the blind man there is only an idea of the sun as it is a heating thing, not likewise as it is an illuminating thing (I add: and as it is a thing having such color, figure, magnitude, position, motion, distance, substance, and whatever things from its inner nature are hidden even from the sharpest-sighted), I infer that you ought in the same way to concede that in you there is only an idea of mind as it is a thinking thing, not likewise as it is a thing having such a substance, nature, and the other points more than once objected.

For to say that those things are to be understood as contained under the name "thing" is to say nothing known. When the blind man says, "The sun is a heating thing," it is the same as if he said, "The sun is something or other that heats; for I know nothing else about it except that it has heat." So when you say, "Mind is a thinking thing," it is the same as if you said, "It is something or other that thinks; for I have perceived nothing else about it except thought."

You say that you are not rightly compared with the blind man, first because, as you say, cognition of a thinking thing extends much more widely than cognition of a heating thing, indeed more widely than whatever we know about any other thing, as was shown in its proper place. Yet the question is not about breadth of extension, but about the clarity and distinction of cognition, insofar as each cognition is bounded by the object of which it is cognition.

For it is certain that objects are not equal, and that either the same thing or just as much is not known about one as about another. Yet cognition of a few things about one object can still be the same, or equally clear, as cognition of many things about another. It is the same as if, regarding two vessels, the question were not capacity but fullness. Although the larger contains more liquid than the smaller, the smaller can nevertheless be as full as the larger. And although the larger is full of wine while the smaller is full of water, nevertheless the water equals the capacity of the smaller just as the wine equals the capacity of the larger.

Hence, although thought extends itself to more things than heating, it is nevertheless always thought, and nothing else than thought, equal to the faculty for knowing thought. In the same way, heating, although it extends itself to not as many things, yet still to innumerable things (namely to however many drops of water, however many stalks, and all other things capable of heating), is nevertheless heating, equal to the faculty for knowing heating. Therefore you stand in the same position, when you do not know mind except as a thinking thing, as the blind man does when he does not know the sun except as a heating thing.

## Notes

- Gassendi treats Descartes' blind-man answer as revealing an aspectual definition of clear and distinct cognition.
- A thing known only through one effect or predicate is known only "as such," not according to its nature or substance.
- The dark-striker example generalizes the problem: being able to say "striking thing" does not mean one knows who or what struck.
- `Res cogitans` is put under the same pressure as `res calefaciens`: both can name an operation without disclosing the thing's nature.
- The disputed object is not mind as thinking or sun as heating, since those predicates are conceded; it is the thing's nature obscurely held under `res`.
- Saying the hidden nature is contained under the word `res` gives no positive cognition.
- Descartes' breadth reply is answered by a fullness/capacity distinction: a narrower cognition can be as complete relative to its object as a broader one.
- Gassendi therefore treats "thinking thing" as no more adequate for mind than "heating thing" is for the sun.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `late-patentia` is translated as "breadth of extension" rather than "wide-openness" because Gassendi is contrasting the range of cognition with its clarity and distinction.

Technical: `capacitas` / `plenitudo` is kept as "capacity" / "fullness." The vessel analogy is a precise counter to Descartes' claim that cognition of a thinking thing ranges more widely than cognition of a heating thing.

Technical: `labore quasi chymico` is rendered "an almost chemical labor." Gassendi is preserving Descartes' polemical jab while turning it back against the adequacy of the `res cogitans` answer.
