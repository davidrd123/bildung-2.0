## Opening Finding

Gassendi's rejoinder does not retreat from image-language after Descartes' objection. It doubles down. He treats the born-blind case as the obvious empirical control Descartes evades, then argues that Descartes' own earlier definition gave the name `idea` properly only to images or likenesses of things. The pressure point is not merely whether the sun idea is innate; it is whether intellectual inference can count as an idea when no representable image answers to it.

## Working Translation

REJOINDER.

I would make myself plainly ridiculous if I assumed that I had to prove to you that you walk upon the earth, see, are warmed, eat, speak, sense, and so on. You said that no one of sound mind had ever seriously doubted those things. If, therefore, you now seriously doubt them, and when I asked that we proceed seriously and in good faith and speak of things as they are, you do not accept the condition, what reason is there for me to press this matter?

You say that I have no reason of any weight for asking why there is no idea of color in one born blind, or of voice in one born deaf. Come then: what reason of your own, and of what weight, do you bring against it? "For how do you know," you ask, "that there is no idea of colors in one born blind, since sometimes in us, even if we close our eyes, sensations of light and colors are nevertheless stirred up?" Brilliant indeed.

The question had been whether, since we have ideas of things that are called external, for example of color or of voice, it could be proved that we borrow such ideas from the things themselves. You had denied this, and after bringing two arguments against yourself, you had solved them. I objected that those alone should not have been brought and solved, but especially the one drawn from comparison between us and someone born blind or deaf.

For since there is in us an idea of color that is not in the blind person, and an idea of voice that is not in the deaf person, I argued that the cause is this: we, by God's kindness, have open eyes by which we have received colors, and open ears by which we have received sounds; they have neither open eyes nor open ears by which they ever have received or could have received them.

At this point, when you ought to have solved this and declared for what other cause ideas of colors and voices are in us but are not in the blind and deaf, if we could have borrowed them from elsewhere than from the things themselves, you slip away by asking how I know that there is no idea of colors in one born blind. But meanwhile, could anything more charming have been demanded by you? You think I could not answer as Aristotle once did, when someone asked why beautiful things delight us: "That is a blind man's question."

"But sometimes," you say, "in us, although we close our eyes, sensations of light and colors are nevertheless stirred up." Yes, indeed, some dim sensation of light or of brighter colors is sometimes stirred up. But this is because we are sighted and have translucent eyelids, so that they are in us like a veil that is not completely opaque. Is the same thing stirred up in those whose eyes have been pierced or dug out, and to whom those blind from birth should be considered equivalent, at least as regards deprivation of the faculty of seeing?

There was once a man blind from birth who studied philosophy under the same professor as I did. I remember more than once placing before him various colors both in sunlight and in shadow, and ordering him to test whether there was any difference; he never recognized any at all. If you do not believe me, try the same thing. And even if you refer judgment about colors to the blind person, you will at least recognize, I think, that if you, with the faculty of seeing that you possess, had remained with closed eyes up to this point, there would not be so many ideas of colors and images of things in you as there now are, after you have had them open and attentive so often and for so long.

I add that, even if I grant that the same sensation of light and colors occurs in the blind person as is sometimes stirred up in us with our eyes closed, the difficulty would still always have to be solved: from where would he receive this dim idea of light and colors unless he received it from external things? And how does it happen that he does not have it clear and distinct as we do, unless we have it from those same things, inasmuch as, when their species glide toward us, we had open doors, and they did not?

Here is another kind of subtlety: you say that someone who denies the existence of material things can say just as well that one born blind has no idea of colors because his mind lacks the faculty of forming them, as I say that he does not have them because he is deprived of eyes. As though someone who denies the existence of material things, and so even denies that he is a human being, should be listened to. Or as though he penetrates into the mind of the blind person so as to know which faculties are lacking or not lacking in him, just as I inspect his face and discern which organs are or are not in it. Or finally, as though he could say with enough probability, "This blind person does not see because his mind lacks the faculty of seeing, not because he lacks eyes, and although he had eyes in the best condition, he still would not see"; while I can say, "This blind person does not see because he lacks eyes, not because he lacks the faculty of seeing, which, though he had it in the most perfect degree, he still would not see while lacking eyes."

From this it is clear how solid that reasoning is:

Whoever is not moved by two certain reasons to think that the ideas he has of external things are drawn from the things themselves ought not admit that they are drawn from the things themselves.

But I am not moved by two certain reasons to think that the ideas I have of external things are drawn from the things themselves.

Therefore I ought not admit that they are drawn from the things themselves.

There is no unlike subtlety when you say that, in taking those two ideas of the sun, which refer to one sun, as one, I do the same as if I said that true and false do not differ when they are affirmed of the same subject. As though I had said either that the idea of the sun, insofar as it appears a foot wide, is so true that when by reason we deduce that the sun is equal to so many terrestrial semidiameters, the idea enlarged according to our capacity is false. Or as though it were not at the same time true both that the sun appears a foot wide and that it is of that immense magnitude that is shown by reasoning. Or as though there were danger that dialecticians would catch me affirming true and not-true of the same subject in respect to the same consideration, and so would show that there is a contradiction in me.

But the subtlest subtlety is this, my dear fellow: in your usual way you say that what I add about the two ideas of the sun proves nothing, and under these words you evade the very head of the difficulty. For since the question was whether the idea of the sun was innate, or elicited from innate notions, I sent you to someone born blind, so that you might test what kind of idea of the sun he would have from himself: one that would seem able to be neither luminous, nor colored, nor even round, nor representative of the sun as large as it is, unless he had received something by report. But you, with great cleverness and equal foresight, took care not to seem to have crashed upon this rock.

You then complain that I restrict the name of idea to images alone painted in phantasy, contrary to what you expressly assumed. At this point, first, you do not blame me for restricting the name of idea to images. And why exactly would you do so, since not only is this the notion of all who speak about ideas, that they take them as images of things, but you too, earlier, when dividing thoughts, spoke in this way: "Some of these are like images, to which alone the name idea properly belongs, as when I think of a human being, or a chimera, or the sky, or an angel, or God. Others, however, have certain other forms besides; as when I will, when I fear, when I affirm, when I deny, I always indeed apprehend some thing as the subject of my thought, but I also embrace something more than the likeness of that thing. Of these, some are called volitions or affects, others judgments."

I report these things so that you may remember that you properly take ideas for certain images or likenesses of things; but the acts themselves of affirming, denying, fearing, willing, or whatever is found in them beyond the images or likenesses of things that are apprehended when they are affirmed, denied, and so on, are not to be judged ideas; or at least, if they are judged such, this is done improperly.

So, secondly, you blame me for restricting the name of idea to images painted in phantasy. But I restrict it neither to those painted in phantasy nor to those painted in the intellect. Rather, I extend it to all of them, whatever they are, however they are, and wherever they can be experienced in us. What then is it that you blame, or what is the difference between you and me? Plainly this: I retain the general notion of idea, just now approved by you, namely that it is an image; but you, quickly forgetting it, transfer the name to that which is not an image and which you cannot say represents what kind of thing to you.

And because you take it badly when it is objected to you, or asked of you, what sort of thing you therefore conceive, of which you say that you have an idea and of which I do not experience myself to have an image, you object that I ask for ideas or images such as are painted in phantasy, not such as are in the intellect. Then, because it seems utterly absurd that the images that are in phantasy, such as the sun of a foot-wide diameter, should be so clear and distinct, while the images that are in the intellect, such as the sun more than one hundred sixty times greater than the earth, should be so obscure and confused, you come very close to denying that they are images, while nevertheless judging them to be true and properly so-called ideas.

From here, secure that they are perceived by you clearly and distinctly, you burst into insults, as though you alone use intellect while I remain in phantasy or imagination alone. Readers may judge that, however, when they have gone through what I said in the dubitation about the idea and the apparent and true magnitude of the sun, remembering what I touched on in the preceding Meditation. I repeat only this: it is one thing for us to perceive something through a true idea or image, another through a necessary consequence from some antecedent supposition. In the former way we conceive that a thing is such-and-such; in the latter, that it ought to be something such. And just as in the former way we understand the thing distinctly and as it is in itself, so in the latter way we understand it only confusedly and analogically, that is, by referring it to something.

## Notes

- Gassendi's polemical posture matters. He treats Descartes' doubt about walking, seeing, eating, speaking, and writing as a condition he refuses to grant for serious discussion.
- The born-blind test shifts from anecdote to method: sensory organs are the open doors through which species reach the mind.
- The passage gives `species` a concrete function: external things send species; the mind receives ideas/images through open sensory routes.
- Gassendi's quotation of Descartes is central. He reads Meditation III as giving proper use of `idea` only to image-like thoughts; volitions, affects, and judgments are ideas only improperly, if at all.
- The key distinction at the close is `true idea/image` versus `necessary consequence`. For Gassendi, astronomical reasoning yields a conclusion that the sun must be such-and-such; it does not give a distinct idea of the sun as it is.
- This is stronger than generic empiricism. Gassendi is not denying reason; he is denying that reason's necessary inference becomes an image or idea in the proper sense.
