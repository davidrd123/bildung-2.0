## Opening Finding

Descartes answers the species objection by refusing the premise that a corporeal species is received in the mind. Pure intellection of corporeal and incorporeal things occurs without a corporeal species; imagination does need a corporeal species, but as a body to which mind applies itself, not as something received in mind. The blind-man/sun example is reversed: the blind man may have a clear and distinct idea of the sun as heating thing without having it as illuminating thing, while Descartes says Gassendi knows no more about mind than he does. The non-extension claim is also narrowed. It is not meant to explain what mind is, but to deny an error, just as one may deny that Bucephalus is music. Mind's union with the whole body and its power to move body do not, for Descartes, imply that mind is extended or corporeal.

## Working Translation

Here you ask how I think that, in me, an unextended subject, there can be received a species or idea of body, which is extended. I answer that no corporeal species is received in the mind, but that pure intellection of both corporeal and incorporeal things occurs without any corporeal species. For imagination, however, which can exist only concerning corporeal things, there is indeed need of a species that is a true body, and to which the mind applies itself, but not one that is received in the mind.

As for what you say about the idea of the sun, which a blind man elicits from its heat alone, it is easily refuted. For that blind man can have a clear and distinct idea of the sun as a heating thing, although he does not have one of the same thing as an illuminating thing. Nor do you rightly compare me to that blind man: first, because cognition of a thinking thing extends much more widely than cognition of a heating thing, indeed even more widely than whatever we know about any other thing, as was shown in its proper place; and then because no one can argue that the idea of the sun which the blind man forms does not contain all things that can be perceived about the sun except those who, endowed with sight, recognize its light and figure besides. But you know not only nothing more about mind, but not even that very thing which I know. Hence in this matter you rather can be called blind, and I at most dim-sighted along with the whole human race.

Nor indeed did I add that the mind is not extended in order to explain what it is, but only in order to warn that those err who think it is extended. In the same way, if some people affirmed that Bucephalus is music, it would not be pointless for others to deny this of him. And surely in the points you add here, to prove that mind is extended because it uses body, which is extended, you seem to me to reason no better than if, from the fact that Bucephalus neighs or lows, and so gives forth sounds that can be referred to music, you were to conclude of Bucephalus that he is music.

For although mind is united to the whole body, it does not follow from this that mind is extended through the body, because it does not belong to the ratio of mind that it be extended, but only that it think. Nor does it understand extension through an extended species existing in itself, although it imagines extension by turning itself to the corporeal species which is extended, as has already been said. Finally, it is not necessary that mind itself be body, even if it has the power of moving body.

## Notes

- Descartes rejects Gassendi's received-species premise directly: no corporeal species is received in mind.
- Pure intellection is separated from imagination by the need for a corporeal species.
- Imagination still needs a corporeal species, but Descartes locates the species as a true body to which mind applies itself, not as something in mind.
- The blind-man/sun analogy is constrained by aspect: a clear idea as heating need not be a clear idea as illuminating.
- Descartes answers Gassendi's negative-self-idea objection by saying non-extension is a denial of error, not a positive definition of mind.
- The Bucephalus/music analogy blocks the inference from using or producing something classifiable under a domain to belonging to that domain.
- Whole-body union and causal power over body do not, for Descartes, entail extension or corporeality of mind.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `species quae sit verum corpus` is translated as "a species that is a true body." Descartes' point is not that the species becomes a mental image-body, but that imagination requires an extended corporeal item outside the mind to which mind applies itself.

Lexical: `Bucephalum esse Musicam` / `quod sit Musica` is left as "Bucephalus is music." The category mismatch is intentional in Descartes' analogy, though English makes it sound especially odd.

Technical: `ad summum lusciosus` is rendered "at most dim-sighted." The point is comparative: Descartes concedes limited human sight about mind, while denying that Gassendi has superior access.
