## Opening Finding

Descartes answers in two moves. First, Gassendi has not attended to the coherence of the Meditations: one cannot report what Descartes has on a question without recounting the preceding and following argument. Second, immutable essences and mathematical truths are not independent existing things beside God. They depend on God's will and disposition, but because God so willed and disposed them, they are immutable and eternal. Against Gassendi's abstraction account, Descartes argues that geometrical essences cannot be drawn from singulars, since Gassendi himself denies that true mathematical points, lines, surfaces, and triangles exist among sensed bodies. A drawn triangle did not teach us the true geometrical triangle; it only occasioned recognition of an idea already in the mind, just as ink lines evoke the idea of a human face only because the face is known from elsewhere.

## Working Translation

RESPONSIO.

Because here, after quoting a few of my words, you add that this is all I have about the proposed question, I am forced to warn you that you have not paid sufficient attention to the coherence of what I wrote. For I think it is such that everything preceding a given thing, and the greatest part of what follows it, contributes to the proof of that thing. So in good faith you cannot report how much I have about some question unless you also review everything I wrote about the rest.

As for your saying that it seems hard to you to posit something immutable and eternal besides God, it would rightly seem so if the question were about an existing thing, or even if I posited something so immutable that its immutability did not depend on God. But just as the poets imagine that the fates were indeed established by Jupiter, but that after they had been established, he bound himself to preserve them, so I do not think that the essences of things, and those mathematical truths which can be known about them, are independent of God. Nevertheless, because God so willed, because he so disposed, I think they are immutable and eternal. Whether you want this to be hard or soft, it is enough for me that it is true.

The things you next have against the universals of the dialecticians do not touch me, since I do not understand them as they do. But as to essences that are clearly and distinctly known, such as that of the triangle or of any other geometrical figure, I will easily force you to confess that the ideas of them which are in us are not drawn from singulars.

For here you call them false, because of course they do not agree with your preconceived opinion about the nature of things. And a little later you say that the object of pure mathematics, such as point, line, surface, and the indivisibles composed from them and behaving indivisibly, cannot exist in reality. From this it follows that no triangle, and absolutely nothing from those things which are understood to belong to the essence of it or of other geometrical figures, has ever existed, and therefore that these essences are not drawn from any existing things.

But, you say, they are false; that is, in your opinion, because you suppose the nature of things to be such that they do not conform to it. Yet unless you also contend that all geometry is false, you cannot deny that many truths are demonstrated about them, and since these are always the same, they are rightly called immutable and eternal.

That perhaps they do not conform to the nature of things that you suppose, nor even to the one Democritus and Epicurus fashioned from atoms, is only an extrinsic denomination that changes nothing. And nevertheless they no doubt conform to that true nature of things which was established by the true God. This is not because there are substances in the world having length without breadth or breadth without depth, but because geometrical figures are considered not as substances, but as limits under which substance is contained.

Meanwhile I do not concede that the ideas of those figures ever slipped into us through the senses, as almost everyone persuades himself. For although such figures as are considered by geometers can no doubt exist in the world, I deny nevertheless that any are given around us, unless perhaps they are so tiny that they in no way touch our senses. They are composed for the most part from straight lines, yet no part of a line that was really straight has ever moved our senses. When we examine with a flea-glass those lines that seemed most straight to us, we find them plainly irregular and everywhere bent in waves.

And therefore, when in infancy we first looked at a triangular figure drawn on paper, that figure could not teach us how the true triangle, as considered by geometers, was to be conceived, because it was contained in it no otherwise than Mercury in a rough piece of wood.

But because the idea of the true triangle was already in us, and could be conceived by our mind more easily than the more composite figure of the painted triangle, therefore, when that composite figure was seen, we apprehended not that figure itself but rather the true triangle. This is exactly the way that, when we look at paper on which little lines have been drawn in ink so as to represent the face of a human being, the idea excited in us is not so much of those little lines as of a human being. That would not happen at all unless the human face had been known to us from elsewhere, and unless we were more accustomed to think about it than about those little lines, which we often cannot distinguish from one another when they are even a little distant from us.

So indeed we could not recognize the geometrical triangle from the one painted on paper unless our mind had had the idea of it from elsewhere.

## Notes

- Descartes again appeals to the coherence of the whole Meditation against Gassendi's local excerpting.
- Immutable mathematical truths are dependent on God, but once God wills and disposes them, they are eternal and immutable.
- Descartes rejects being tied to dialecticians' universals.
- Gassendi's denial of real mathematical points, lines, and surfaces is turned against him: if no such singulars exist, geometrical ideas cannot be abstracted from them.
- Nonconformity to atomist or Gassendist physics is treated as an extrinsic denomination, not a falsification of geometry.
- Geometrical figures are not substances but limits under which substance is contained.
- Sense experience supplies no truly straight line; magnification shows apparent straightness to be irregular and wavy.
- The painted triangle functions as an occasion for recognizing a prior idea of the true triangle, not as the source of that idea.
- The face/ink-line analogy makes recognition depend on a known form that organizes a rough sensory occasion.
