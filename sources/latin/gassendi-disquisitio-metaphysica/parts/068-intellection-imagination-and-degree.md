## Opening Finding

Gassendi opens the Sixth Meditation by setting aside Descartes' claim that material things can exist as objects of pure mathematics, while noting that material things belong to mixed rather than pure mathematics and that pure mathematical objects such as point, line, and surface cannot exist in reality. His main target is the renewed distinction between intellection and imagination. Against Descartes' triangle/chiliagon case, Gassendi treats both as actions of one and the same cognitive faculty, differing only by degree of distinctness, effort, confusion, and slackening. As figures gain sides, no determinate boundary shows where imagination ends and intellection alone remains. If clear, effortful, sensible attention is called imagination and confused, low-effort attention is called intellection, Descartes only degrades intellection and praises imagination. Gassendi then presses the consequence: if imagining and understanding are one power differing by degree, imagination cannot be excluded from the mind's essence as a distinct power, and the mind cannot turn to itself or to ideas without also turning toward something corporeal or quasi-corporeal.

## Working Translation

IN MEDITATIONEM VI,
which is on the existence of material things and the real distinction of mind from body.

DUBITATIO I.
On the distinction between intellection and imagination.

Concerning the Sixth, I do not linger over what you say at the beginning, that material things, insofar as they are the object of pure mathematics, can exist, although material things are the object of mixed mathematics, not pure mathematics; and the object of pure mathematics, such as point, line, surface, and the indivisibles consisting from them and behaving indivisibly, cannot exist in reality.

I stop only at this, that here again you distinguish imagination from intellection. For, O Mind, these two seem to be actions of one and the same faculty, as we suggested above. And if there is any difference, it seems to be nothing more than according to more and less. See how those things are already proved from this.

You said above that imagining is nothing other than contemplating the figure or image of a corporeal thing. But here you do not deny that understanding is contemplating a triangle, pentagon, chiliagon, myriagon, and the other things of this kind, which are figures of corporeal things.

And indeed you now set up the difference as follows: imagination occurs with a certain application of the cognitive faculty to body, while intellection does not require such application or exertion. So when you simply and without labor perceive the triangle as a figure consisting of three angles, you say that you understand it. And when, not without some exertion of yourself, you hold the figure as if present, inspect it, examine it, and distinctly and one by one recognize and discern its three angles, you say that you imagine it.

Consequently, because you do perceive without labor that the chiliagon is a figure of a thousand angles, yet by applying and exerting yourself you cannot discern them, have them as if present, and distinguish all its angles one by one, but are as confused about it as about the myriagon or any other figure of this kind, you therefore judge that with respect to the chiliagon or myriagon there is intellection, not imagination.

Nevertheless nothing prevents you from extending imagination to the chiliagon just as to the triangle, in the same way as intellection. For you do make some effort to imagine that figure of so many angles in some way, although the multitude of angles is so great that you cannot grasp it distinctly. And besides, you do perceive that the word "chiliagon" signifies a figure of a thousand angles, but this is only the force of the name; for on that account you do not understand a thousand angles in that figure any more than you imagine them.

One must notice that distinctness is lost and confusion acquired by degrees. For you will perceive, imagine, or understand the tetragon more confusedly than the triangle, but more distinctly than the pentagon. Then the pentagon more confusedly than the tetragon, but more distinctly than the hexagon, and so on in sequence, until you have nothing you can set before yourself expressly and, because you can no longer grasp it expressly, for the most part neglect to exert yourself.

Therefore, if you want to call it both imagination and intellection when you know a figure distinctly and with a certain sensible exertion, but intellection alone when you contemplate it only confusedly and with no or very slight exertion, you certainly may. But there will not on that account be any reason for you to construct more than one genus of internal cognition, to which it is only accidental that, according to more and less, you look at some figure distinctly or confusedly, intently or slackly.

And certainly, when we want to run through the heptagon, octagon, and further figures up to the chiliagon or myriagon, and when we attend always and continuously to greater and lesser distinctness or slackening, will we be able to say where, or in which figure, imagination ends and intellection alone remains? Or rather will there not appear one series and tenor of one kind of cognition, whose distinctness and exertion continually and imperceptibly decrease, while confusion and slackening increase?

Consider otherwise whether you are not depressing intellection and raising imagination. For what else are you seeking than mockery for the former and praise for the latter, when you attribute negligence and confusion to the former but diligence and perspicuity to the latter?

You afterwards assert that the power of imagining, insofar as it is distinguished from the power of understanding, is not required for your essence. But how is that so, if it is one and the same power, whose functions differ only according to more and less?

You add that by imagining the mind turns itself toward body, by understanding, toward itself or the idea it has in itself. But why should that be so, if the mind cannot turn itself toward itself or any idea without at the same time turning itself toward something corporeal, or represented by a corporeal idea?

For the triangle, pentagon, chiliagon, myriagon, and the other figures, or their ideas, are altogether corporeal; nor can the mind attend to them by understanding except as corporeal things, or after the manner of corporeal things.

As for the ideas of things believed immaterial, such as God, angel, and the human soul or mind, it is also clear that whatever ideas we have of them are either corporeal or quasi-corporeal, drawn, that is, from the human form and from things otherwise very tenuous, very simple, very insensible, such as air or aether, as we also touched on above.

As for your saying that you infer only probably that some body exists, because you cannot say this seriously, there is no need to linger over it.

## Notes

- Gassendi notes but does not dwell on the pure-mathematics claim: material things belong to mixed mathematics, while pure mathematical objects cannot exist in reality.
- The main argument treats intellection and imagination as one faculty's actions, not two distinct powers.
- The triangle/chiliagon/myriagon case becomes a continuum of more and less distinct, more and less effortful cognition.
- `Vis nominis` marks the chiliagon's thousand angles as nominally grasped, not distinctly intuited.
- Descartes' distinction appears to praise imagination as diligent and perspicuous while lowering intellection into negligence and confusion.
- If imagining and understanding differ only by degree, imagination cannot be removed from the mind's essence as though it were a distinct power.
- Even ideas of things believed immaterial are routed through corporeal or quasi-corporeal representation.

## Translation Risk

Lexical/contextual: `contentio` is rendered as "exertion." It may also carry strain, directed attention, or cognitive application; Descartes' response may clarify whether the dispute is over a degree of effort or a genuinely different mode of operation.
