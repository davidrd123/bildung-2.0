## Opening Finding

Gassendi treats the major premise as a methodological failure before treating it as a technical error. Descartes has abandoned the public road through the visible universe, where God's existence, power, wisdom, goodness, and other attributes are announced by the order of the whole. In its place he uses a private, obscure, enclosed idea that cannot be displayed to others like the stars. The alleged effect is then reduced from a thing to a mode, from a mode to a mode of a mode, and finally to a relation or non-being. Once the idea's formal reality depends only on thought, its inseparable objective reality depends on thought too, making an external cause either superfluous or hidden by self-deception.

## Working Translation

Next, to say something also about the major proposition: first, as was said before, you abandoned the royal, open, clear road by which one arrives at knowledge of God's existence, power, wisdom, goodness, and other attributes. I mean this splendid work of the universe, which by its magnitude, distinction, variety, arrangement, beauty, constancy, and other adjuncts cries out its Author. This whole work, I say, so great, so various, so marvelous, you rejected; whatever had become known to you about it, you held as false. And to prove God's existence, power, and the other attributes, you used only an idea enclosed within your mind, an idea which is obscure even to you and cannot be shown to others as the stars and the other parts of the universe are shown.

An idea, I say, of the sort not everyone has, of the sort you did not always have, of the sort you can fail always to have: a flowing thing, an accidental thing; indeed not a thing, but the mode of a thing, the mode of a mode, a part of a mode.

Second, you used not the whole reality of the idea, but only its objective reality, although it does not even have reality through itself, because it is not a thing, but only modality, because it is only a mode. And certainly, just as a figure impressed in wax is nothing through itself but only the wax itself, insofar as it is depressed here, raised there, wider here, narrower there, so an idea impressed on the mind is nothing through itself, but only the mind itself as it has itself disposed in its own way and in this, that, or another relation.

And just as the likeness or representation of the seal which is in the wax is a mere relation, and therefore not a being, so also the likeness or representation of the foreign thing which is in the mind, and which you call objective reality, will be a mere relation and not a being. Tell me, then, what an impressive effect you used for demonstrating so great a cause.

Third, the objective reality of the idea, as you take it, is inseparable from the formal reality of the same idea. For unless it is formal, it will not be objective; and unless it is objective, it will not be formal. Therefore, from whatever efficient cause the formal reality depends, the objective reality will depend also. But according to you, formal reality depends on no cause other than your thought, or mind. For you expressly teach that the nature of the idea itself is such that it demands from itself no other formal reality than that which it borrows from your thought, of which it is a mode. Therefore objective reality too will depend on your thought alone, or mind.

For this reason, either the hypothesis is superfluous and inept if you seek outside yourself a cause which you know to be in you, or you conceal the truth from yourself, and it is no wonder if you are led to the conclusion with that same truth concealed.

Fourth, what could have been said more obscurely than that there is at minimum as much formal reality in the cause as there is objective reality in the effect? For although you called the formal by another name, the actual, still you did not declare either in what formality consists or in what actuality consists; nor whether the reality of the cause was its substance, or its very representability through the idea, or finally something else, which was necessary for making the proposition evident.

Although you also added that the mode of being objectively in the intellect through the idea, however imperfect it may be, is nevertheless certainly not nothing, you still did not say in what respect it was something, in what respect it deserved to be called a thing, in what respect it had reality, or in what respect it required a cause. This too was surely necessary for the evidence of the proposition.

Again, although you compared objective reality with formal reality, you still did not explain whether both were absolute, whether both were relative, whether one was absolute and the other relative, nor what that was in which formal reality was equal to, or could exceed, objective reality. And with other things of this sort left unexplained, the proposition could not fail to be unevident.

## Notes

- The `via regia` motif returns with more force. Gassendi's preferred proof-route is public and cosmological: the universe can be shown, while Descartes' enclosed idea cannot.
- The private idea is degraded through a sequence: thing, accidental thing, non-thing, mode of a thing, mode of a mode, part of a mode. This extends the earlier `realitas` / `modalitas` pressure.
- The seal/wax model now yields a stronger ontological demotion. Objective reality is not just thin representativeness; as likeness or representation it is a mere relation and therefore `non ens`.
- Gassendi turns Descartes' own account of an idea's formal reality against the proof. If the idea as mode borrows formal reality only from thought, and objective reality is inseparable from formal reality, then the objective reality also depends on thought alone.
- The major premise is also attacked as under-defined. Gassendi asks what formality, actuality, cause-reality, representability, absoluteness, relativity, and excess are supposed to mean before the causal comparison can be evident.
