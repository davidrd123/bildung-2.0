## Opening Finding

Gassendi now names the structure of Descartes' proof and attacks it before touching its premises. The proof is not a public demonstration because it rests on Descartes' private report about what is in his own mind. It also assumes a state Descartes is not actually in: no body, no senses, no external things, and all prior images treated as false. Finally, it uses clear and distinct perception before Descartes has secured the divine veracity needed to guarantee clear and distinct perception. Gassendi explicitly calls this a `diallelus`, or circle.

## Working Translation

Instantia.

Whether I have nothing here that has not already been said, and whether you have refuted everything, those who read will judge. They will judge especially what I touched on here concerning an infinite universe, infinite principles, and worlds, and what, since you could solve it by no reason, you passed over in your usual manner.

So that this may appear better, and at the same time so that the force of your Archimedean demonstration may become clear, it is worth taking up the matter again from what precedes.

You wanted to demonstrate that God exists, a thing otherwise most certain. But you seemed to yourself to prove it by that reasoning or demonstration of which you had declared beforehand that no better one could ever be devised by human wit. Here you conclude it in this way:

If there is in me an idea whose objective reality is so great that it could not have proceeded from me, then it is necessary that it was placed in me by some cause distinct from me, in which there is at minimum as much formal reality as there is objectively in the idea, and which therefore exists in reality.

But there is in me an idea whose objective reality is so great that it could not have proceeded from me, namely the one which represents to me, or through which I understand, God as a certain infinite, eternal, independent, supremely intelligent, supremely powerful substance, and by which both I myself and every other thing, if anything else exists, whatever exists, was created.

Therefore such an idea must be from a cause in which there is at minimum as much formal reality as there is objectively in the idea itself, and which therefore exists in reality.

But in God alone is there at minimum as much formal reality as there is objective reality in the idea itself, for God alone is infinite substance, while anything else, and I too, is finite.

Therefore such an idea is from God alone, and therefore God exists.

This, then, is your demonstration, which you state is not only more evident and more certain than geometrical demonstrations, but even the most evident and the most certain. Whether this is so, it is useful first to attend to the following points generally.

First, you demonstrate only to yourself; you speak only about yourself; you answer only for your own fact. But a genuine demonstration concerns all human beings and is general. If it uses a fact as a principle, it uses not a private fact, or one appearing privately, but a universal fact, or one appearing universally to all. Therefore, although your reasoning may have force with respect to you, it cannot have the force of demonstration among others, but at most the force of authority. According as you have influence among human beings, each person will give such credit as he wants to you when you report it.

Second, your reasoning supposes that you are in a state in which you really are not, and from which, therefore, you can demonstrate nothing as from a foundation already laid down. For you suppose that you dream, are deceived, are deluded. You suppose that you have no body, no hands, no head, no brain. You suppose that everything you have so far seen, heard, smelled, tasted, touched, or thought is not only doubtful but even false, except that you have now noticed that you think, that you are, that you are a thinking thing.

You suppose that you have erased from your thought all images of corporeal things, or, because this is difficult, that you count them as empty and false and worth nothing, and that you address only yourself and look more deeply into yourself. You suppose that in this state of ignorance through affected abstraction of this sort you can pass judgment about other things, whether they are or are not, just as you could do in a state of ignorance through pure negation, as if you had never seen, heard, and so on.

You suppose that the ideas you have of angels, animals, and other bodies do not make you certain that the things represented by them either have existed, or do exist, or that there is anything at all besides you in the world. Finally, you suppose that you are not certain whether you have received anything about God from someone speaking, or from yourself hearing, and whether you have formed the idea you have of him partly by hearing and partly by your own thought. These and other things are what you suppose; since they are certainly false, what certain thing will be built on them? What true thing will be demonstrated?

Third, you can demonstrate nothing until you have propositions which, clearly and distinctly perceived, you are certain are not deceptive. But you cannot yet have them, and you will not have them at all until in the fourth Meditation you establish, as best you can, that pronouncement: whatever I clearly and distinctly perceive is true.

At the beginning of this Meditation you raised a difficulty about this, which you were not going to remove except after God's existence and his veracity had been shown. This is what you finally attempted to accomplish in the fourth Meditation, by teaching that this pronouncement does not deceive because every clear and distinct perception, since it is something, has God as its author, and since God is supremely perfect, it is repugnant for him to be deceptive.

Therefore, since you have not yet proved in this place that God exists and that it is repugnant for him to be deceptive, you cannot have the sort of propositions necessary for demonstrating. Hence it follows that when you use some propositions here, certain of them as clearly and distinctly perceived by you, in order to prove that God exists, you fall into a diallelus, or commit what they call a circle.

## Notes

- Gassendi restates Descartes' proof syllogistically before attacking it. This keeps the criticism aimed at the demonstration's form, not merely its rhetoric.
- The first objection is publicity. A demonstration cannot rest on a private report about one's own idea unless that report has public or universal force.
- The second objection is the false foundation of affected abstraction. Descartes assumes a state of total sensory and bodily exclusion in which he is not actually situated.
- The third objection is the Cartesian circle. Clear and distinct perception is needed to prove God, but God's existence and veracity are needed to guarantee clear and distinct perception.
- The phrasing `diallelus sive circulus` is a major methodological marker and likely deserves cross-text attention if it appears in Cassirer or later notes.
