## Opening Finding

Gassendi turns Descartes' causal axiom sideways. If "nothing is in the effect that is not in the cause" is true, Gassendi says it fits material cause better than efficient cause. The efficient cause does not pour its own being into the effect; it shapes a given material. That shift weakens the move from an idea's objective reality to a formally real cause. The idea's "objective reality" is reduced to representation, likeness, symmetry, relation, or mode, and the Cartesian comparison with the thing's formal reality becomes, for Gassendi, a category mistake that can be conceded only because the idea contains almost nothing in comparison with its cause.

## Working Translation

Dubitatio V.

On that saying, "There is nothing in the effect that is not in the cause," understood of the material cause; and how far it can be true that there must be at least as much formal reality in the cause of the idea as there is objective reality in the idea.

You consequently assume that it is manifest by the natural light that there must be at minimum as much in the efficient and total cause as there is in the effect; and from this you infer that there must be at least as much formal reality in the cause of the idea as there is objective reality in the idea. This is indeed another great step, and one at which we must pause a little.

First, that common saying, that nothing is in the effect which is not in the cause, seems to have to be understood of matter rather than of the efficient cause. For the efficient cause is external and generally of a different nature from the effect. And although the effect is said to have reality from the efficient cause, it has not necessarily a reality which the efficient cause has in itself, but one which it can borrow from elsewhere.

The matter is plainly seen in the effect of art. Although a house has its whole reality from the craftsman, the craftsman gives it a reality not received from himself but from elsewhere. The sun does the same when it changes lower matter in various ways and produces various animals. So too even a parent: although offspring has some matter from the parent, it has that not from the efficient principle, but from the material one.

As for your objection that effects must be contained in the cause either formally or eminently, this proves nothing more than that an effect sometimes has a form similar to the form of its cause, sometimes a dissimilar and indeed more imperfect one, so that the form of the cause stands above it. But it does not follow that for this reason an eminent cause gives something of its own being, or that a cause which contains something formally shares its form with the effect. For although this seems to happen in the generation of living things, which comes from seed, you will not say, I think, that a father, when he generates a son, cuts off and gives him a part of his rational soul.

In one word, the efficient cause contains the effect in no other way than insofar as it can sketch it out and perfect it from some definite matter.

Next, concerning what you infer about objective reality, I use the example of my image, which can be considered either in a mirror before which I place myself, or in a panel which a painter prepares. Just as I myself am the cause of the image in the mirror, insofar as I send a species of myself into the mirror, and the painter is the cause of the image displayed on the panel, so when the idea, or image, of me is in you or in any other intellect, one can ask whether I myself am its cause, insofar as I send a species of myself into the eye and, with the eye intervening, all the way into the intellect itself; or whether some other cause delineates it in the intellect with something like a brush or stylus.

It seems, moreover, that no other cause besides me is required. For although the intellect can later enlarge, diminish, compose, or otherwise handle the idea of me, I myself am nevertheless the primary cause of the whole reality which it has in itself. And what is said here about me is to be understood likewise of any external object.

Now that reality of the idea is distinguished by you into two kinds. Its formal reality, indeed, can be nothing other than that tenuous substance which flowed out from me, was received in the intellect, and was formed into an idea. If you do not want the species proceeding from the object to be a substantial efflux, do whatever you please; you will always lessen the reality.

Its objective reality, however, can be nothing besides the representation or likeness of me which the idea bears, or at least the symmetry by which the parts of the idea are so disposed that they refer to me. However you take it, this seems to be nothing real, since it is only the relation of the parts among themselves and to me, or a mode of formal reality insofar as it has been formed in this way. But no matter: since it pleases you, let it be called objective reality.

This being granted, you would seem to have to compare the formal reality of the idea with my formal reality, or with my substance, and the objective reality of the idea with the symmetry of my parts, or with the delineation and external form. Yet it pleases you instead to compare the objective reality of the idea with my formal reality.

Moreover, whatever may be the case about the explanation of the preceding axiom, it is clear not only that there is in me as much formal reality as there is objective reality in the idea of me, but also that the formal reality of the idea is almost nothing in comparison with my formal reality, or with my whole substance. For that reason it should also be conceded to you that there must be at least as much formal reality in the cause of the idea as there is objective reality in the idea, when the whole of whatever is in the idea is almost nothing in comparison with the cause itself.

Responsio.

Here you say many things so that you seem to me to contradict me, yet you do not contradict me in any way, since you plainly conclude the very same thing that I do. But nevertheless you mix in many things here and there from which I dissent strongly: for example, that the axiom, "Nothing is in the effect which has not preexisted in the cause," should be understood of the material rather than the efficient cause. For the perfection of form can never be understood to preexist in the material cause, but only in the efficient cause. And likewise that the formal reality of the idea is a substance, and things of that sort.

## Notes

- Gassendi's first move is not to deny causality but to relocate the maxim from efficient cause to material cause. Artifacts, sunlight, animals, and generation all show efficient causes working through matter rather than transferring their own being.
- `Formaliter` and `eminenter` become containment-terms under pressure. Gassendi lets them describe likeness or superiority of form, but denies that either requires the efficient cause to partition its own being into the effect.
- The image example is the local bridge from species-theory into Cartesian objective reality. A mirror, a painted panel, and an idea in an intellect are treated as representational arrangements caused by an external object.
- Objective reality is demoted to `repraesentatio`, `similitudo`, `symmetria`, relation, or mode. This is why Gassendi can concede Descartes' formula while making it nearly empty: the idea contains almost nothing compared with the thing.
- Descartes' response marks the next issue cleanly. He rejects both the material-cause reading of the axiom and Gassendi's account of the idea's formal reality as substance.
