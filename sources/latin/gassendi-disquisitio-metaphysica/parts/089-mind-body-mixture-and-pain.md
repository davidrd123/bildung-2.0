## Opening Finding

Gassendi uses Descartes' own account of pain, hunger, and thirst to sharpen the union problem left open in Part 088. Descartes says nature teaches that mind is not merely present to body as a sailor is present to a ship, but is most closely joined and quasi-mixed with body. Gassendi asks what such union can mean if mind is incorporeal, unextended, indivisible, without parts, and less divisible than a point. Pain becomes the test case: if pain involves disruption of parts, alteration, and differentiated reception from foot, arm, or other bodily parts, then an unparted and unalterable mind seems unable to be the subject of such a sensation.

## Working Translation

On the commixture of mind with body, and on the sense of pain; namely, that this does not seem able to occur if mind is unextended or incorporeal.

You next intersperse some things bearing on the same point, on all of which we need not insist. I note this: you say that nature teaches through the sense of pain, hunger, thirst, and so on, that you are not present to the body as a sailor is present to a ship, but that you are most closely joined to it and, as it were, mixed with it, so that you compose one thing with it.

"For otherwise," you say, "when the body is injured, I, who am nothing other than a thinking thing, would not therefore feel pain, but would perceive that injury by pure intellect, as a sailor perceives by sight if something in the ship is broken. And when the body needs food or drink, I would understand this expressly; I would not have confused senses of hunger and thirst. For surely those senses of thirst, hunger, pain, and so on are nothing other than certain confused modes arising from the union and quasi-mixture of mind with body."

These things are indeed well enough, but it remains to be explained how that conjunction and quasi-mixture or confusion can belong to you if you are incorporeal, unextended, and indivisible. For if you are no larger than a point, how are you joined to the whole body, which is of such great magnitude? How even to the brain, or to some small part of it, which, as has been said, however tiny it is, still has magnitude or extension?

If you wholly lack parts, how are you mixed, or as it were mixed, with the particles of this part? There is no mixture without mixable parts on both sides. And if you are entirely discrete, how are you confused with matter itself and how do you compose one thing with it? And since composition, conjunction, or union occurs between some parts, must there not be a proportion between parts of this kind? But what proportion of corporeal with incorporeal can be understood?

Do we grasp how stone and air are so fitted together, for example in pumice, that a genuine composition results from them? And yet there is a greater proportion between stone and air, since air too is body, than between body and a soul or mind that is plainly incorporeal. And must not union occur through intimate contact? But how, as I was saying before, without body? How will what is corporeal lay hold of what is incorporeal so as to keep it joined to itself; or how will the incorporeal lay hold of the corporeal so as to have it reciprocally bound to itself, if there is absolutely nothing in it by which it may be laid hold of, or by which it may lay hold?

Hence, since you confess that you feel pain, I ask how you think yourself capable of the sense of pain if you are incorporeal and unextended. For the affection of pain is understood only from some pulling-apart of parts, when something is interposed that makes a break in continuity. That is, the state of pain is a state contrary to nature. But how can that be contrary to nature, or be affected, which by nature is of one mode, simple, indivisible, and untransmutable?

And since pain either is alteration or does not occur without alteration, how can that be altered which, since it is more indivisible than a point, cannot become other or cease to be as it is without being reduced to nothing? I add also: when pain comes from the foot, from the arm, or from other parts at the same time, must there not be various parts in you in which you receive it variously, lest you feel pain confusedly and as if it were the pain of only one part? In a word, the general difficulty always remains: how can the corporeal communicate with the incorporeal, and what proportion may be established between one and the other?

## Notes

- Gassendi does not reject the sailor-in-ship contrast as a phenomenological claim; he asks how Descartes can make it intelligible under incorporeality and non-extension.
- The Descartes quotation supplies the key terms: close conjunction, quasi-mixture, one composite, and confused modes arising from union.
- Gassendi turns `quasi permixtio` into a parts problem. Mixture requires mixable parts on both sides, while the Cartesian mind is supposed to have none.
- The pumice example gives a physical comparison: stone and air can compose because both are bodies, and even that case is easier than body joined to a plainly incorporeal mind.
- Pain is treated as a bodily-structural event: disruption, break in continuity, alteration, and reception from differentiated parts.
- Multiple local pains from foot, arm, or other parts make the no-parts claim especially unstable, since differentiated reception seems to require differentiated receiving sites.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: I keep `commixtio`, `quasi permixtio`, and `confusio` close as "commixture," "quasi-mixture," and "confusion." The cluster is argumentative here; smoothing all three into "union" would lose Gassendi's pressure.

Lexical: `puncto impartibilius` is rendered literally as "more indivisible than a point." The phrase is deliberately sharp because Gassendi is testing whether such a mind can undergo alteration without becoming nothing.

Contextual: the "sailor in a ship" contrast is Descartes' own Sixth Meditation formulation as quoted by Gassendi. Descartes' response begins on the same printed page, so Part 090 should be read before treating this as Gassendi's final position on union.
