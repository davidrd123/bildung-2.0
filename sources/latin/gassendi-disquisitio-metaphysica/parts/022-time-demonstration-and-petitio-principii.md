## Opening Finding

Gassendi turns Descartes' temporal conservation claim into a formal syllogism and attacks it step by step. The premise is false because time's parts are connected, and Descartes confuses a thing's weak power to cease with separable moments of time. The deduction is heterogeneous because temporal parts do not cause either being or non-being; producing, conserving, and destroying causes do. Even if one grants that prior existence does not entail present existence, nothing forces the stronger conclusion that a cause must recreate the thing. Finally, `rursus creari` assumes prior creation by God, God's existence, and God as creator, precisely what the proof is supposed to demonstrate.

## Working Translation

But now let your demonstration be formed:

If the individual parts of the time of life in no way depend on the others, then from the fact that I existed a little while ago, it does not follow that I must now exist, unless some cause as it were creates me again for this moment, that is, conserves me.

But the individual parts of the time of life in no way depend on the others.

Therefore from the fact that I existed a little while ago, it does not follow that I must now exist, unless some cause as it were creates me again for this moment, that is, conserves me.

First, the condition of the proposition, insofar as you make the assumption from it, is false, because the parts of time are so connected with one another that the later parts depend on the prior parts by a necessary and, so to speak, unimpeded sequence. And although you take the power of ceasing at individual moments for the very moments of time themselves, you take it wrongly, because you confuse the enduring thing with duration. You multiply times as many times as there are enduring things; and in whatever other way you explain yourself, you make time, an obscure thing, more obscure.

Next, when, once that condition is posited, you deduce that it does not follow from the fact that you existed a little while ago that you must now exist, the deduction is plainly heterogeneous. For just as the preceding part of time was not the cause that you existed earlier, but rather the cause which produced you in that time, or did not bring it about that you did not exist, so neither is the following part of time the cause that you exist later or do not exist. Rather, it is the cause which either conserves you or destroys you in that time. From this it follows that, from the fact that you existed before, it does not follow that you must now exist, not because the parts of time are dependent or independent of one another, but because there is some cause which, since it did not destroy you before, can destroy you now.

Hence the weakness of the inference is also apparent from this, that an entirely opposite one could be admitted with equal right. For as you said, from the fact that I existed a little while ago, it does not follow that I must now exist. You could also have said: from the fact that I existed a little while ago, it does not follow that I must now not exist. But this would be, of course, not because of the dependence or independence of the parts of time, but because of the presence of a conserving cause, or because of the absence of a corrupting cause. I could also propose incorruptible things, and especially God, of whom you will not say that from the fact that he existed a little while ago, it does not follow that he must now exist, as if God had this from the dependence or independence of the parts of time with which he coexists, and not rather from the immutability of his nature, by which he cannot cease to be.

Third, although the consequent can be admitted without the antecedent, namely that from the fact that I existed a little while ago it does not follow that I must now exist, nevertheless there is no necessity for the further inference, unless some cause as it were creates me again. For if in place of these words we use others, such as unless there is absent a cause which destroys me, or unless there is a cause which protects me, or unless there is enough strength in me that I still subsist, or similar formulations, all of these will seem to cohere better than that formulation of yours. For they follow spontaneously and plainly, while you deduce the matter from something unexpected, and all the more because, as I said before, it cannot be understood that a thing which continuously is is continuously produced.

Fourth, when you say that you are created again, or as if created, you suppose that you were first created; therefore by God as creator; therefore by God as existing. But with this reasoning of yours you are trying to prove both that God exists, and that he is creator, and that he created you and the other things. Therefore you not only argue from what is not a cause as if it were a cause, but you also beg the question. I will not mention how plainly remarkable it is that the Mind, so ignorant, and so little exercised in reasoning so far, could know so hidden a thing as creation, which so many illustrious philosophers, after so much reasoning about it, held to be wholly unknown.

## Notes

- Gassendi formalizes the time argument before rejecting it. The structure matters because the objections target premise, deduction, and hidden assumption separately.
- The first objection extends Part 021: Descartes turns the power to cease at a moment into the moments of time themselves, thereby multiplying times with enduring things.
- The second objection makes temporal parts causally idle. Causes produce, conserve, or destroy; parts of time do not.
- The opposite inference test weakens the proof: prior existence no more entails present non-existence than it entails present existence.
- The third objection preserves the weak conclusion while rejecting recreation. Absence of a destroyer, presence of a protector, or internal strength are more natural continuations.
- The fourth objection names `petitio principii`: renewed creation assumes first creation, God as creator, and God's existence.
