## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' response by saying that Descartes has evaded the actual question. Gassendi had asked about a species of body: corporeal for sight, perhaps incorporeal or intelligible for intellect. Descartes only denied a corporeal species in mind and left unclear whether the intellect knows body through an incorporeal species or through no species at all. Gassendi then presses the paper example. If the same paper, length, square shape, and whiteness are not merely imagined but understood, then either the intellect uses the same or a very similar species as imagination. The separated mind sharpens the problem: if the corporeal species is only in the brain, the separated mind cannot imagine body, but it still should be able to understand body. If that species can be present to separated mind, why not to embodied mind? The original dilemma returns: an extended species makes its subject extended; an unextended species cannot represent extended, figured, colored body.

## Working Translation

It is truly remarkable how cautiously you respond. I was asking: how could it happen that in the mind, an unextended subject, there could be received a species or idea of body, which is extended? You answer that no corporeal species is received in the mind. But I was asking universally about the species of body, which, since body can be known both by a corporeal faculty, such as sight, and by an incorporeal faculty, such as intellect, seems to have to be known through a species: by sight, indeed, a corporeal one; but by intellect, an incorporeal one. In your cleverness, foreseeing the difficulty, you said that no species is received, not no species of body, but no corporeal species.

And because it could immediately be asked whether, since body is known by intellect, it is at least known through an incorporeal species, you did not answer that. You only said that pure intellection of both corporeal and incorporeal things occurs without any corporeal species. So you have still left it doubtful whether you think intellection of a corporeal thing occurs through an incorporeal species, or rather without any species, whether corporeal or incorporeal.

Why so? Surely because you did not seem able to defend the claim that intellection occurs without an intelligible and incorporeal species. And yet, by admitting an intelligible species of body, you did not seem able to defend that it is incorporeal, because of the difficulties already objected. Therefore you preferred neither to deny nor to assert it, so that if you were pressed from one side, an escape into the other would remain open.

Meanwhile, however, you turned aside by thrusting imagination forward and by admitting that for it, which can exist only concerning corporeal things, there is need of a species that is a true body and to which it applies itself, not one that is received in the mind. The same point also belongs to what you say near the end: that the mind does not understand extension through an extended species existing in itself, although it imagines extension by turning itself to the corporeal species that is extended.

At this point the first question returns: are bodies known not only by imagination, but also by intellection? And since it seems indubitable that bodies are the object not only of the imaginative faculty but also of the intellective faculty, what remains? When I contemplate this paper, for example, and examine its length, width, figure, color, and so on, and therefore not only imagine the paper itself but also understand it, do I not understand it through a species just as I imagine it through a species?

And do I not, by the same species by which I imagine the length of one foot, also understand the length of one foot, and similarly the width, square form, whiteness, and other things? I do not now ask any further for a criterion by which, on this or a similar occasion, I may distinguish intellection from imagination, since that has already been asked for in vain. I ask only this: when I not only imagine but also understand the paper placed before me, insofar as I understand it, is there not really present to the intellect a species of this magnitude, figure, and color? But if one is present, what other species is it than the one present to the imaginative faculty? Surely, if not the same, it is very similar indeed.

Therefore, although imagination is only of corporeal things, nevertheless, since intellection, while it is of incorporeal things, is also of corporeal things, it seems that for intellection, insofar as it is of corporeal things, there is no less need than for imagination of a species that is a true body. Nor can it stand in the way that intellection is an action of the mind, which is incorporeal, since imagination too, according to you, is an action of the same mind. And what you add, that the corporeal species needed for imagination is not received in the mind, seems able to be adapted to intellection by equal right.

Moreover, because you want this species, since it is not received in the mind, to be received in the brain, and imagination, or the imaginative faculty, to be applied to it, it plainly follows that when mind has been separated from body and deprived of brain, imagination will be entirely idle, and mind will no longer be able to imagine any body, because it no longer has a species to which it may apply imagination.

I ask, then: does it at least understand body at that time? To say that it does not understand body is inconsistent first with you yourself, since you want there to occur in the ideas of corporeal things nothing so great that it seems unable to proceed from mind. Then it conflicts with the nature of mind, which through itself is an understanding thing. In so perfect a state, it cannot be either duller than when sunk within darkness, or altogether blind, or completely deprived of that light by which, in the body, it was gazing upon so large a part of its object. It also conflicts with the similar nature of the souls of angels; otherwise it would follow that they do not understand bodies, since angels too are separated substances, and yet are deprived of a brain and of species to which they may apply themselves.

Therefore, if separated mind understands, or at least, what does not seem deniable, can understand bodies, let it understand this same paper. But when it understands the same length, width, figure, and color, will there be present to it some other species than the one mentioned before? If you say another, say what it is. If you say the same, say where it is, since it is no longer in the brain. Surely you must say that it is received in the mind itself. But if in the state of separation it can be received in the mind, why not in the state of conjunction? However it either is or arrives, it is at least certain that the species or idea of this paper can be in the mind; otherwise the mind could not be said to understand the paper.

Once this is the case, the difficulty I objected returns. For such a species is either unextended or extended. If it is extended, then it is corporeal and proves its subject extended. If it is unextended, then it does not have the character of a species or image, since, being unextended, it cannot represent an extended object, and consequently neither a figured nor colored one, and so forth. And I ask you: how do you grasp that the idea of a square thing is present to the mind, while the idea itself by which the thing is apprehended is not apprehended as square? And since separated mind understands through a species similar to the one by which it imagined when joined to the body, how do you grasp that one is extended and the other unextended?

## Notes

- Gassendi charges Descartes with answering the narrower phrase `species corpoream` while avoiding the broader question of a species of body.
- The dilemma is whether intellectual cognition of body uses an incorporeal species or no species at all.
- Gassendi thinks Descartes avoids both horns because an incorporeal species of body is hard to defend, but intellection without any intelligible species is also hard to defend.
- The paper example turns the problem concrete: the same length, width, square form, and whiteness seem to be both imagined and understood.
- The unanswered criterion problem returns: without a distinction between intellection and imagination, Gassendi treats their species as the same or very similar.
- Separated mind removes the brain as a possible location for the corporeal species, while preserving the expectation that mind can understand bodies.
- Angels appear as a parallel case of separated substances that should still understand bodies without brains.
- The original dilemma returns: an extended species proves its subject extended; an unextended species cannot function as an image of extended, figured, colored body.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `species intelligibilis` is translated as "intelligible species" rather than "intellectual species" because Gassendi is naming the object-form through which intellect would know body, not merely the act of intellect.

Technical: `Imaginatio plane ferietur` is rendered "imagination will be entirely idle." The verb carries the sense of being inactive or out of operation in this context.

Technical: `imaginatrix` and `intellectrix` are translated as "imaginative faculty" and "intellective faculty." The terms matter because Gassendi is comparing faculties through their relation to one species of the same corporeal object.
