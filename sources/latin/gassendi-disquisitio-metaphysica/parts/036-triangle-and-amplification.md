## Opening Finding

Gassendi answers Descartes' triangle comparison by denying the parity between God's essence and the three lines of a triangle. Descartes may invoke the triangle only after showing that God's essence and all essential divine attributes are as fully explored and ready-to-hand as the three lines constituting a triangle. Gassendi then presses Descartes' claim that the idea of God is not successively formed from amplified creaturely perfections but grasped all at once as infinite being incapable of amplification. If taken literally, this conflicts with the present chapter's claim that the idea is impressed by God; if not, it simply begs the question. The old error remains: Descartes treats it as easy to think a whole positive infinite being as to utter a negative name, even though neither he nor all humans together can promise complete thought of even the finite world with all its parts and properties.

## Working Translation

As for what you add about the idea of a triangle, nothing needs to be added to what has already been said above until you have made it plain that God's essence, and therefore all his essential attributes, are so thoroughly explored by you, and that you have them so ready and counted out, as you have the three lines from which a triangle is constituted.

As for what you say otherwise, for my instruction (for, as I said, you act as an altogether excellent Teacher, and one worthy of being heard), namely that the idea of God is not formed by us successively from the amplified perfections of creatures, but all at once, from this, that we touch by the mind an infinite being incapable of every amplification: I would try to discover what you mean, if you were not either contradicting yourself, by teaching that the idea of God is formed all at once by us, though in this chapter especially you contend that it is impressed by God; or, if you did not say the very thing you meant as well as you wished, begging the very principle and, in place of a solution, answering nothing to the difficulties except the very thing under dispute.

This is not even to suggest that you are always held by the same hallucination: that it is as easy to think a whole positive being, or its whole capacity, even though lacking an end, as it is easy to pronounce and assign to it a negative name. And this especially since you cannot promise this to yourself about a finite thing, such as the world with all its parts, particles, and their properties; indeed, all humans, however many there are or will be, cannot promise it to themselves.

## Notes

- The triangle analogy remains blocked until Descartes can show divine essence and attributes are as determinate to him as the three lines of a triangle.
- Gassendi catches a tension between two Cartesian descriptions: an idea formed all at once by us versus an idea impressed by God.
- The all-at-once appeal is treated either as self-contradiction or as renewed question-begging.
- The amplification critique returns: naming the infinite negatively is not the same as thinking a whole positive infinite being.
- The finite-world comparison raises the burden. If the total content of the finite world outruns all human thought, the whole positive infinite outruns it still more.
