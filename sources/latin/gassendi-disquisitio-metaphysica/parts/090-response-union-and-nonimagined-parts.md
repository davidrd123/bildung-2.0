## Opening Finding

Descartes answers the pain and mixture objection by refusing Gassendi's comparison class. Mind-body union is not to be compared with the mixture of two bodies, because mind and body differ in the whole genus. The response also repeats the imagination boundary: Gassendi's doubts arise because he tries to submit things that do not fall under imagination to imagination's examination. Parts are not to be imagined in mind simply because mind understands parts in body; otherwise, Descartes says, when mind understands the earth's magnitude it would contain that magnitude and be larger in extension than the earth.

## Working Translation

The things you have here concerning the union of mind with body are similar to the preceding ones. You nowhere object to my reasons, but only propose doubts which seem to you to follow from my conclusions, although in reality they arise only from this: that you want to recall to the examination of imagination things that by their own nature do not fall under imagination.

So here, when you want to compare the mixture of mind and body with the mixture of two bodies, it is enough for me to answer that no comparison should be established between such things, because they are different in the whole genus. Nor are parts to be imagined in mind from the fact that mind understands parts in body. For where do you get the claim that everything mind understands must be in mind itself? Certainly, if this were so, when it understands the magnitude of the earth, it would also have that magnitude in itself, and thus would be not only extended, but even greater in extension than the earth.

## Notes

- Descartes treats Gassendi's objections as doubts against conclusions rather than objections against reasons.
- The central defense is categorical: mind-body union and body-body mixture are `toto genere diversa`.
- The imagination/intellection boundary returns as the response's main lever.
- Descartes rejects a containment principle: what mind understands need not be in mind as a part or magnitude.
- The earth-magnitude example answers Gassendi's part-reception argument by reductio.

## Translation Risk

Lexical: `orbis terrarum` is rendered as "the earth" rather than "the world-circle" or "globe of lands." The point is magnitude, not geography; the Latin should be checked before citation if the exact referent matters.

Contextual: Descartes' "no comparison" answer is intentionally thin here. Gassendi's following `Instantia` begins immediately and challenges whether the response satisfies the doubts or merely repeats the disputed authority.
