## Opening Finding

The final answer refuses Descartes' mind/flesh split. The speaker is one, not double, and the dispute was never about attacking the truth of God or the immortality of souls. It was about the force of the reasoning that Descartes calls demonstration and Gassendi denies to be demonstration. This lets the close restate a central rule of the whole exchange: conclusions considered as true propositions may belong to Gassendi and all the pious too; conclusions considered as deductions from Descartes' principles remain open to attack through the force of the consequences that draw them. The passage then restores gratitude and friendship, while asking Descartes to attribute any roughness to the "philosophical liberty" Descartes himself had modeled.

## Working Translation

I, excellent sir, am the same as before. Namely, I am one, not double; nor do I see how flesh, cut off from mind, reasons or thinks that it reasons. You, who were able to see, were permitted to distinguish and to provide that your mind not contend foolishly and waste its labor while addressing a mindless thing. I, who am not so sharp, was permitted to refer my reasoning, if I used any against you, not to flesh but to mind, if I have any, and therefore to think that your mind differed with my mind, not with flesh.

And if the matter had indeed been between us, as was warned beforehand and sometimes repeated, concerning the truth of the things themselves, for instance God's existence and the immortal nature of souls, then, if such madness, may it be absent, had seized me that I had gathered arguments against that truth itself, you could certainly now conclude that mind had discoursed with flesh and, as was fitting, dissented from it. But because I have constantly testified that nothing more is at issue between us than the force of that reasoning which you maintain to be demonstration and I maintain not to be demonstration, see by what right you call yourself mind and me flesh, that is, a mindless thing.

As for your saying that you rejoice that no reason has been brought forward that attacked your reasons, and none even against your conclusions to which it would not have been very easy for you to respond, there is no reason for me to envy you that joy as far as the reasons are concerned; since each person abounds in his own sense and can rejoice accordingly.

But as far as the conclusions are concerned, insofar as they are propositions about the truth itself, there is certainly no reason for you alone to rejoice in them as though they were yours, since they are mine too, and belong to all the pious and right-thinking; nor did I wish, nor could it have come into my mind, to bring any reason against them. Moreover, if you take the conclusions insofar as they are deduced from your principles, then it has been declared above that nothing else is objected against them except what is brought forward to attack the reasons, that is, the force of the consequences for drawing them out. And here readers must be permitted to judge whether no reason has been brought by me that attacked your reasons, and whether it is the same thing or a different thing to answer easily and to answer adequately.

For the rest, I accept not only gratefully but most gratefully, as is fitting, whatever benevolence and commendation you sprinkle here. Not, indeed, because I recognize myself as the person you say you look up to, but because, when you grant me more than I deserve, this must be referred to the affection by which you wished me to be bound to you. Thus, although my style up to this point has been a little rougher, nothing has for that reason diminished from my inclination to honor and love you.

It has always been clear to me how much you excel among the minds of our age. I have always remembered how highly I valued being able to cultivate friendship with you. And so I would like you to take it this way: if anything was said by me too severely, attribute it to the simplicity by which I thought I could follow the rule prescribed by you. You seemed to call this philosophical liberty; since I did not understand well enough, I set your example before myself.

And although I carefully guarded against throwing back things equal to those you had heaped on me, if nevertheless something perhaps slipped in which you judge should be disapproved, there is reason for you to pardon it as something previously approved by your own deed. For I am one who by nature and study am borne toward gentleness of manners; yet I seemed to see that you wanted to test my patience, thinking that one may safely sport with a bull that has no hay on its horn.

From that it was surely brought about that, though otherwise I would have made no reply, I thought it mattered to remind you, by pressing the point, that one ought not deal in that way with a man who had deserved no ill from you. Hereafter it will be as you yourself prescribe. As far as I am concerned, I testify this frankly: it will never stand through me in the way of your finding me, if you deem it worth so much, most observant and most compliant toward you.

Farewell. Paris, on the Ides of March, 1642.

## Notes

- The mind/flesh split is refused: the speaker remains one, not divided into reasoning mind and mindless flesh.
- The exchange is reframed again as a dispute over demonstrative force, not over the truth of God or the immortality of souls.
- Gassendi distinguishes conclusions as true propositions from conclusions as deductions from Descartes' principles.
- The final reader-judgment point is procedural: readers must decide whether the objections attacked Descartes' reasons and whether an easy answer is the same as an adequate answer.
- Descartes' praise is accepted as benevolence rather than as Gassendi's due.
- The rough style is explained as an imitation of Descartes' own `libertas Philosophica`.
- The close preserves friendship but also insists that Descartes' manner had tested Gassendi's patience.

## Translation Risk

Textual: The final clause in the reader-judgment sentence is OCR-damaged and appears duplicative in the page image. I render the sense as the contrast between answering easily and answering adequately, but the exact Latin should be checked against a cleaner witness before citation.

Lexical: `Tauro, cui foenum in cornu non sit` is preserved in translation as the horn/hay proverb rather than paraphrased away. It marks the claim that Descartes thought Gassendi could be teased without danger.

Contextual: The repeated claims of friendship are real, but they do not erase the methodological objection. The final answer keeps the distinction between truth and demonstration alive until the end.
