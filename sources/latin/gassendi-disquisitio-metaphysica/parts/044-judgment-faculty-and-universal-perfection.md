## Opening Finding

Gassendi returns to the fallible faculty of judgment. Descartes' solution says that a creature which seems imperfect should be considered not as a whole by itself, but as a part of the universe; from that angle its imperfection may belong to the greater perfection of the whole. Gassendi grants the part/whole distinction but denies that it answers the case. The issue is not whether a part is small relative to the whole, but whether the judging faculty, considered as a task-bearing whole in itself, is defective in its own office. A republic with some wicked citizens is not more perfect than one with all good citizens; at most the contrast makes virtue shine accidentally. Likewise, error-liability does not make the universe better by giving error-free parts a contrast. Descartes replies by denying the premise: liability to error is not a positive imperfection but a negation of greater perfection, especially with respect to God. The better analogy, he says, is not wicked citizens in a republic, but someone who wants the whole human body covered with eyes because the eye is the most beautiful part.

## Working Translation

You then offer the solution that a creature which is seen as imperfect ought to be considered not as some whole thing, but as a part of the universe; and in that respect it will be perfect. And the distinction is certainly to be praised. But here we are not dealing with the imperfection of a part insofar as it is a part and is compared with the integrity of the whole. We are dealing with it insofar as it is some whole thing in itself and exercises a special office. And when you refer this to the universe, the difficulty will always remain: whether the universe would in reality have been more perfect if all its parts had existed perfect, than it now is when very many parts exist imperfect.

For that republic will be more perfect in which all the citizens are good than another in which many, or some, are bad. Therefore when you afterwards say that the perfection of the whole universe is somehow greater because some of its parts are liable to error than if all were alike, it is the same as if you were saying that the perfection of the republic is somehow greater because some citizens are bad than because all are good. From this it follows that, just as it seems plainly to have to be the wish of the best prince to have all citizens good, so it seems that it ought to have belonged to the plan of the author of the universe that all its parts be created and be immune from error.

And although you can say that the perfection of those which are immune appears greater from opposition to those which are liable, still that is only accidental, just as the virtue of the good, although it somehow shines out from opposition to those who are vicious, shines out only accidentally. Therefore, just as it is not desirable that some citizens be bad so that the good may become more illustrious, so it seems it should not have been permitted that some parts of the universe be liable to error so that those which were immune might shine more clearly.

You say you have no right to complain that God willed you to sustain in the world a role which was not the first and most perfect of all. But this does not remove the doubt why it was not enough for him to give you a role to sustain which was the least among the perfect roles, and why he had also to give you an imperfect one. For although it is not counted as a fault in a prince that he does not call all citizens to the highest offices, but has some in middle and some in low offices, it would nevertheless be counted as a fault if he not only assigned some to the lowest tasks, but also assigned some to wicked ones.

You say you cannot give a reason by which you could prove that God ought to have given you a greater faculty of knowing than the one he gave; and that however skilled an artisan you understand him to be, you do not therefore think that he ought to have put in each of his works all the perfections which he can put in some. But what I was just saying still holds. You see that the difficulty is not so much why God did not give you a greater faculty of knowing, as why he gave you an erring one; and the dispute is not why the supreme artisan does not wish to give all perfections to all works, but why he also wishes to give imperfections to some.

You say that, even though you are not able to abstain from errors by evident perception of things, you can nevertheless do so by the settled rule by which you firmly propose to assent to nothing which you have not evidently perceived. But however much you can always maintain that attention, is it not always an imperfection not to perceive evidently those things which need to be judged, and to be perpetually exposed to the danger of erring?

You say that error lies in the operation itself insofar as it proceeds from you and is a certain privation, not in the faculty which you received from God, nor even in the operation insofar as it depends on him. But even if there is not error in the faculty received from God in a proximate way, there is nevertheless error in it remotely, insofar as it was created with this imperfection, that it can err. Therefore there is indeed no reason, as you say, to complain of God, who in reality owed you nothing and yet gave you those goods for which you ought to give him thanks. But there is still reason to wonder why he did not give more perfect goods, if he knew, if he could, and if he was not led by envy.

You add that there is no reason to complain that he concurs with you in the act of erring, since all acts are true and good insofar as they depend on God, and it is somehow a greater perfection in you to be able to elicit them than not to be able to do so; and since the privation, in which alone the formal reason of falsity and fault lies, needs no concurrence of God because it is not a thing and is not related to him. But although that distinction is subtle, it still does not plainly satisfy. For even if God does not concur with the privation which is in the act and is falsity and error, he nevertheless concurs with the act without which the privation would not exist. And besides, he himself is the author of the power which is deceived or errs, and therefore, so to speak, of an impotent power. Thus the defect which is in the act seems to have to be referred not so much to that power which is impotent as to the author who made it impotent and, though he could, did not wish to make it powerful or more powerful.

Certainly, just as it is not counted as a fault in a craftsman that, when opening a small chest, he does not make an enormous key, but that, after making a tiny key, he gives it a form unfit or difficult for opening; so it is indeed not God's fault that, in giving a judging faculty to a little human being, he did not give one so great as he would judge sufficient for all things, or very many things, or the greatest things. But it is very strange why, for those few things which he wished to be judged by a human being, he gave a faculty unequal, entangled, and uncertain.

**Response.**

Here you everywhere wrongly take our being liable to errors as a positive imperfection, when it is only, especially with respect to God, a negation of greater perfection in creatures. Nor does the comparison of citizens of a republic with parts of the universe rightly fit. For the wickedness of citizens, when it is referred to the republic, is something positive; but a human being's being liable to error, or not having all perfections, is not something positive when it is referred to the good of the universe.

But the comparison can be better set up between someone who would want the whole human body to be covered with eyes so that it might appear more elegant, because no part of it seems more beautiful than the eye, and someone who thinks that no creatures in the world ought to have been liable to error, that is, that none ought to have been less than wholly perfect. And it is plainly false that you suppose God destines us to wicked works and gives us imperfections and the like. It is also plainly false that God gave a human being an unequal, entangled, and uncertain judging faculty for those few things which he wished to be judged by a human being.

## Notes

- Gassendi accepts the part/whole distinction in principle but says it misses the case of a faculty considered in its own proper office.
- The republic analogy makes error-liability more than mere variety: a whole with all good citizens is better than one that needs bad citizens as contrast.
- The key shift is from magnitude to defect. Gassendi is not asking why the faculty is not infinite or supreme, but why it is errant in the few matters God wants humans to judge.
- Descartes' response turns on `positive imperfection` versus `negation of greater perfection`; Gassendi's next `Instantia` will attack that distinction directly.
- The divine-concurrence distinction is recorded but not accepted. Gassendi says that if God concurs with the act without which the privation would not be, and authored the fallible power, the defect is not fully quarantined from the author.
- The small-key analogy is the sharpest local image: the problem is not that the key is not enormous, but that it is unfit or difficult for opening the few things it is meant to open.
