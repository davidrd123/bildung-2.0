## Opening Finding

Gassendi attacks the Fifth Meditation proof that God's existence belongs to God's essence as the triangle's angle equality belongs to the triangle's essence. The comparison is not just: Descartes compares essence with essence but then compares existence with a property, rather than existence with existence or property with property. If the comparison were made properly, either omnipotence would be compared with the triangle's angle property, or God's existence with the triangle's existence; the latter would not prove necessary divine existence, because triangles do not necessarily exist. Gassendi then denies the key premise that existence is a perfection. Existence is not one perfection among others, but that without which neither a thing nor its perfections are. To include existence in the list of divine perfections is therefore to beg the question. Pegasus supplies the parody: if perfect Pegasus includes wings and existence, perfection-in-kind would prove Pegasus too.

## Working Translation

DUBITATIO II.

On proving God's existence from this: that the supremely perfect being requires existence in the way that the triangle requires the equality of its three angles with two right angles.

You next undertake to demonstrate God's existence, and the force of the argument lies in these words: "To one who attends, it is manifest that existence can no more be separated from the essence of God than from the essence of a triangle the magnitude of its three angles equal to two right angles, or from the idea of a mountain the idea of a valley. So it is no more repugnant to think God, that is, a supremely perfect being, lacking existence, that is, lacking some perfection, than to think a mountain lacking a valley."

In truth one must attend to this: a comparison of this kind seems not quite just. For you do indeed rightly compare essence with essence. But then you do not compare either existence with existence or property with property, but existence with property.

Hence it seems that either you should have said that omnipotence, for example, can no more be separated from God's essence than that equality of the magnitude of the angles can be separated from the essence of a triangle; or certainly that God's existence can no more be separated from his essence than the triangle's existence from the essence of the triangle.

In this way either comparison would have proceeded well, and not only the first but also the second would have been conceded. Yet you would not thereby have proved that God necessarily exists, because neither does the triangle necessarily exist, even though its essence and existence cannot be separated in reality, however much they may be separated in the mind or thought apart, just as the divine essence and existence can also be thought.

Next one must attend to this: you place existence among the divine perfections, yet you do not place it among the perfections of a triangle or of a mountain, though in the same way, and according to each thing's mode, it can be called a perfection.

But surely, neither in God nor in any other thing is existence a perfection. It is rather that without which there are no perfections. For what does not exist has neither perfection nor imperfection. And what exists and has several perfections does not have existence as a singular perfection, one among that number, but as that by which both the thing itself and its perfections exist, and without which neither the thing itself is said to have perfections nor the perfections are said to be had.

Hence existence is not said to exist in a thing after the manner of perfections; nor, if a thing lacks existence, is it called imperfect, or deprived of perfection, so much as nothing. Therefore, just as in enumerating the perfections of a triangle you do not count existence, and hence do not conclude that the triangle exists, so in enumerating the perfections of God you should not have placed existence among them in order to conclude that God exists, unless you wished to beg the principle.

You say that in all other things existence is distinguished from essence, but not in God. But how, I ask, are Plato's existence and Plato's essence distinguished from each other, except by thought alone? Suppose Plato no longer exists: where will his essence be? Moreover, are not essence and existence in God distinguished by thought in the same way?

You object to yourself: perhaps just as, from thinking a mountain with a valley, or a winged horse, it does not follow that either the mountain or such a horse exists, so from the fact that you think God as existing, it does not follow that he exists. And then you argue that a sophism is hidden there. But it was not difficult to solve the sophism you yourself invented, especially by assuming what is so manifestly repugnant, that an existing God does not exist, and by not assuming a human being or a horse in the same way.

But if you had taken God with knowledge, power, or other attributes as you took a mountain with a valley and a horse with wings, then the difficulty would have proceeded, and it would have fallen to you to explain how a sloping mountain or a winged horse can be thought without existing, while a knowing and powerful God cannot be thought without existing.

You say it is not free to think God without existence, that is, a supremely perfect being without the highest perfection, as it is free to imagine a horse with wings or without wings. But nothing need be added except this: just as it is free to think a horse without wings, without thinking existence, which, if it came to the horse, would on your account be a perfection in it, so it is free to think God as having knowledge, power, and the other perfections without thinking existence, which, if he has it, will then belong to consummate perfection.

Therefore, just as from the fact that a horse is thought as having the perfection of wings it is not thereby inferred that it has existence, which, on your account, is the chief of perfections, so neither from the fact that God is thought as having knowledge and the other perfections is his existence thereby inferred. Rather, that existence must finally be proved.

And although you say that existence as well as the other perfections is comprehended in the idea of a supremely perfect being, you say what must be proved and assume the conclusion as a principle. For otherwise I too would say that in the idea of a perfect Pegasus is contained not only the perfection that it has wings, but also that it exists. For as God is thought perfect in every genus of perfection, so Pegasus is thought perfect in its own genus; and nothing seems able to be objected here which, preserving the proportion, could not be used on either side.

You say that, in thinking the triangle, it is not necessary to think that it has three angles equal to two right angles, although this is no less true, as is afterwards evident to one who attends. Likewise, the other perfections of God can indeed be thought without thinking existence, but existence is not for that reason less true when one attends that it is a perfection.

Yet you see what can be said: just as that property is afterwards attended to as being in the triangle because it is proved by demonstration, so, in order for existence to be attended to as being in God, it has to be proved by demonstration. Otherwise I will easily show absolutely anything to be in anything.

You say that, in attributing all perfections to God, you do not act in the same way as if you thought all quadrilateral figures could be inscribed in a circle, since there you are mistaken because you afterwards find that a rhombus cannot be inscribed, whereas here you are not mistaken because you afterwards find that existence belongs to God.

But you seem to act in exactly the same way. Or if you do not, you must show that existence is not repugnant to God, just as it is shown to be repugnant for a rhombus to be inscribed in a circle.

I pass over the rest, which you either do not explain, do not prove, or which are solved from what has already been brought forward, such as: that nothing can be thought except God alone to whose essence existence belongs; that two or more gods of this kind cannot be understood; that such a God has existed from eternity and will remain for eternity; that you perceive many other things in God from which nothing can be taken away or changed; that these things should be inspected more closely and investigated more carefully so that they may be detected and held as certain, and so on.

## Notes

- Gassendi says the proof equivocates in its comparison: essence is compared with essence, but then existence is compared with a property.
- A proper property/property comparison would pair divine omnipotence with the triangle's angle equality.
- A proper existence/existence comparison would pair God's existence with the triangle's existence, which would not prove necessary divine existence because triangles do not necessarily exist.
- Existence is denied the status of a perfection. It is the condition without which things and their perfections are, not one perfection among them.
- If something lacks existence, it is not so much imperfect as nothing.
- Listing existence among God's perfections in order to infer God exists is treated as begging the principle.
- The mountain/valley and winged-horse objections are said to be too easily solved because Descartes builds "existing God" into the case; the real parallel is God with knowledge and power versus mountain with slope or horse with wings.
- Pegasus supplies the parody: a perfect Pegasus could be said to include both wings and existence if perfection-in-kind licenses existential inference.
- The triangle analogy requires demonstration. Angle equality is attended to as true after demonstration; likewise God's existence must be demonstrated rather than smuggled into the idea.
- The rhombus comparison leaves the same burden: Descartes must show existence is not repugnant to God, just as one shows circle-inscription is repugnant to the rhombus.
