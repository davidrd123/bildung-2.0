## Opening Finding

Gassendi begins the `Instantia` by turning Descartes' closing movement of Meditation III into a series of syllogisms. He identifies five steps: a recap proving the idea of God innate, an account of how it was impressed, the argument from that idea to God's existence, the inference that God is not deceptive, and the contemplative pause over God. This part covers the first two. The first syllogism restates the innate-idea proof from non-sensory and non-fabricated origin. The second shows how little the artisan-mark claim adds: if it is not strange that God impressed the idea in creating the mind, then God impressed it; but Gassendi notes that no further examination or reason has been supplied.

## Working Translation

Instantia.

You are acting quite splendidly as Teacher when you remind me what philosophers commonly teach; quite splendidly as Disputant when you escape by so subtle a distinction. I do not seem, as you say, to have attended to what philosophers commonly say. But we will come to that shortly. First it is useful to repeat your lesson, namely the very closing flourish of the Meditation, which is in question.

There are five things which you accomplish in it. For after you said that it remained for you to examine by what account you received the idea of God from God, first you resume, by a kind of recapitulation, the proof that it is innate in you. Second, you show how it was impressed in you. Third, you conclude the argument about God's existence as proved from his idea. Fourth, you infer under that that God is not deceptive. Finally, you say that it pleases you to dwell here for some time in the contemplation of God. To say nothing about this final head, since the others are like a compendium of your whole demonstration and a certain entrance to the things that follow, I choose to express the force of each in syllogistic form.

The first, then, has this form:

If the idea of God which I have was not drawn from the senses, nor fashioned by me, because I plainly can subtract nothing from it and add nothing to it, it remains that it is innate in me, just as the idea of myself is also innate in me.

But the idea of God which I have was not drawn from the senses, nor fashioned by me, because I plainly can subtract nothing from it and add nothing to it.

Therefore it remains that the idea of God which I have is innate in me, just as the idea of myself is innate in me.

What strength this demonstration has is clear both from what has been said so far, and will immediately have to be discussed more fully around that particular point, that nothing can be subtracted from or added to the idea.

The second is of this sort:

If it is not strange that God, in creating me, placed that idea in me so that it would be like the mark of an artisan impressed on his work, and it is also not necessary that this mark be some thing different from the work itself, but from this alone, that God created me, it is highly credible that I was in some way made to his image and likeness, and that this likeness, in which the idea of God is contained, is perceived by me through the same faculty through which I am perceived by myself; that is, when I turn the mind's gaze upon myself, I understand not only that I am an incomplete thing, dependent on another, and indefinitely aspiring toward greater and greater, or better, things, but at the same time I also understand that the one on whom I depend has all those greater things in himself, not indefinitely and only potentially, but in reality infinitely, and is thus God; if, I say, this is not strange, it follows that God, in creating me, placed that idea in me so that it would be like the mark of an artisan impressed on his work, and so on.

But it is not strange that God, in creating me, placed that idea in me so that it would be like the mark of an artisan impressed on his work, and so on.

Therefore God, in creating me, placed that idea in me so that it would be like a mark on his work, and so on.

That is to say, by no other examination and no other reason do you show how you received the idea of God from God himself, or how it was impressed in you.

## Notes

- Gassendi treats the end of Meditation III as a compact sequence of five moves and prepares to test each as a syllogism.
- `Syllogistica forma` is methodological: it exposes whether Descartes has supplied an inference or only repeated a claim.
- The first syllogism concentrates the innate-idea proof on the premise that nothing can be added to or subtracted from the idea.
- The second syllogism turns the artisan-mark proof into a near tautology: if it is not strange that God impressed the idea, then he impressed it.
- Gassendi's immediate point is not yet to answer all details, but to show that the proof supplies no examination beyond the assertion it needs.
