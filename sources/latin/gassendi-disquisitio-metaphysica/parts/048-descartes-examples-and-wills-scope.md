## Opening Finding

Gassendi applies the rebuilt faculty map to Descartes' own examples. If `will` means intellect judging in its second or third operation, then it extends to nothing not already perceived by intellect's first operation. If `will` means appetitive will, then it cannot will error as error: an unknown error is as nothing to will, and a known error would be shunned as bad. Against Descartes' thin-body example, Gassendi denies that he positively judges mind to be a thin body; by faith he holds mind incorporeal, and by natural light he finds the mind's nature obscure. His question is directed at Descartes, who claims clear and distinct natural knowledge of incorporeal mind while the mind in body seems to understand substance only under some corporeal representation. Against the poisoned-apple example, Gassendi says one either judges the poison or does not; in neither case does will outrun intellect. Section V then turns Descartes' concession that we will nothing without understanding something into an attack on the whole proof: willing and understanding are coextensive at the same level, whether distinct or confused. False judgment is not understanding what is not understood; it is understanding something as being in the thing when it is not in the thing.

## Working Translation

To speak now about your response, your words are: "Do you want me here to say briefly what the will can extend itself to that escapes intellect? Namely, to everything in which it happens that we err." But how rightly you say this is clear even from the fact that, since error happens to us either in the second or in the third operation of the mind, if you take will for what we call intellect acting by the second or third operation, it is manifest that it extends to nothing which the same intellect has not perceived by the first operation, since the judgment is just such as the apprehension of the thing is.

But if you take it for what we call will, it is manifest that it is not borne toward error, which it can in no way will, love, command, or pursue, since error is unknown and, with respect to will, is just as if it did not exist. What is more, when error is known, will cannot will or love it at all, since it is something bad. And when it wills and loves a judgment that happens to be erroneous, it does not will and love it as erroneous, but as thought true and shown as such by intellect. For surely if it were thought and shown to be erroneous, then it would not love it but would turn away from it.

You continue, now using will not for the judging faculty but for the appetitive faculty: "Thus, when you judge that the mind is some thin body, you can indeed understand that the mind itself is a mind, or a thinking thing, and likewise that a thin body is an extended thing. But that one and the same thing thinks and is extended, you certainly do not understand; rather, you only want to believe it because you already believed it before and do not gladly depart from your opinion."

But since you declaim so finely, what do you gather from this? Namely, that my will is borne toward something which my intellect does not perceive. But what is that thing? Namely, that the same thing thinks and is extended. Yet if I will that, do I not will it in the same way in which I understand it? For in what other way do I will it conjoined than insofar as it is joined by intellect and shown to will? And if I want to believe it because I already believed it before, then I had already judged before what I now will. But I believed, or judged, by intellect. Therefore will is not on that account borne toward something that escaped intellect.

"But you do not gladly depart from your opinion?" My good man, would that you departed as gladly as I do, if you are preoccupied by anything. I certainly pronounce nothing on this whole question. Since I hold as fixed only what sacred faith prescribes to me, what remains from natural light, which alone is at issue here, is so obscure to me that I am very far from either making or defending a judgment.

For when you say that I judge the mind to be some thin body, I do not judge this, since by faith I hold it to be incorporeal, and, as far as natural light is concerned, I take the matter to be too obscure for me to profess that by it I know the nature of mind. Meanwhile I object to you, who profess to know this nature by natural light. Since you say you know clearly and distinctly that mind is incorporeal, and since it does not appear how, while mind dwells in body, it can understand any substance except under some corporeal species or representation, I ask you under what other species the mind understands itself than that of some thin body.

For the same reason, I do not judge, nor profess to know, whether and how an extended thing understands. Since I plainly do not know how I elicit vision, I confess that I know still far less how I elicit intellection itself. And yet I consequently ask you why intellection could not come from an extended thing, since the substance of mind cannot be understood except as extended or after the manner of an extended thing; nor, if it is something unextended, can it be understood how it elicits this action, and with such variety as is ready to observe.

I object and propose these things, I say, so that I may infer: if you do not understand these matters better than the rest of us, why do you boast of them with such confidence as better understood, indeed as best understood, and hawk them about? And you say that I do not gladly depart from my opinion? Certainly I would not, unless someone arose who put forward something more fitting. But give someone who shows the truth, and everyone will embrace it not only more gladly but even more ardently.

It follows: "Thus, when you judge that an apple which is perhaps poisoned is suitable for you as food, you indeed understand that its odor, color, and such things are pleasant, but not therefore that the apple itself is useful to you as food. Rather, because you will it so, you judge it so." But what do you gather from this too? For either I judged that there was poison in the apple, as with the other features, or I did not judge it, which you do not express clearly enough. If I did judge that poison was present, then I did not will the poison as something unjudged or not rightly perceived. If I did not judge it, then I indeed willed the apple as fragrant, colored, tasty, and nourishing, insofar as I judged it such; but I did not will it as poisoned, because I did not judge it in this way. And you say that I judge this way because I will this way. How much more rightly you would say that I will this way because I judge this way! For I do not judge that the apple is useful as food because I will it; that does not depend on my will. Rather I will it because I judge that it is useful as food.

What is more, you yourself are immediately forced to confess, even unwillingly, that we will nothing about which we do not understand something in some way. This alone, if nothing else, at least hints at the weakness of that reasoning of yours:

If errors arise in someone, this is because his will extends more widely than intellect, and he does not contain it within the same limits, but extends it even to those things which he does not understand.

But errors arise in me.

Therefore they arise from the fact that I have a will extending more widely than intellect, that I do not contain it within the same limits, and that I extend it even to those things which I do not understand.

Clearly, when you now admit that we will nothing about which we do not understand something in some way, you have fully sensed that will does not extend more widely than intellect, nor run out or extend beyond the limits to which intellect reaches. For when you immediately deny that we understand and will equally, you seem not to have noticed sufficiently that these are inseparable: willing the whole and understanding the whole; willing the part and understanding the part.

For if we will some whole, we necessarily understand the whole. Nor is it an obstacle that we do not understand all its parts distinctly, since neither do we will all its parts distinctly. We plainly will as we understand. Thus, if we understand one or several parts distinctly and the rest confusedly, we also will one or several parts distinctly and the rest confusedly. And if we understand all confusedly, we also will all confusedly. Therefore when you add that we can will very many things concerning the same thing and yet know very few, this seems entirely false. It is not in your power to declare what, finally, you will concerning some thing which you do not know to be in the thing, distinctly if distinctly, confusedly if confusedly.

You continue: "But when we judge wrongly, we do not therefore will wrongly, though perhaps we will something wrong." Here you seem to disturb the matter completely. For since you had taken the word will for the judging faculty, or for intellect acting by the second or third operation, I said, imitating you, that will judges wrongly because intellect, or the same faculty acting by the first operation, perceives wrongly. But now you change the hypothesis. You both distinguish volition from judgment and pass from physical wrongness to moral wrongness.

Just as the Scholastics, following Aristotle, teach that acting unjustly and doing an unjust thing are different, insofar as someone can, without an unjust intention, do something that happens to be unjust beyond his will, so you establish that judging wrongly and willing something wrong are different. But with this difference: they do not change the word "acting" and so speak clearly; you change the word "judging" into the word "willing" and so speak obscurely.

What follows is: "Nor do we understand anything wrongly; rather, we are only said to understand wrongly when we judge that we understand something more fully than we really understand it." You do seem to clarify how you take that reasoning of yours: namely, that intellect extends only to those things which are such as they are understood to be, while will, or judgment, extends also to those things which are not such as they are understood to be. But your whole construction appears useless from the fact that the same restriction or expansion has to be made for intellect and will, or for the same intellect simply apprehending and judging.

For just as there are some things which we understand well because we understand them as they are, so those same things are the ones about which we judge well because we judge that they are as they are. And conversely, just as there are some things about which we judge wrongly because we judge them to be what they are not, so those same things are the ones which we understand wrongly because we understand them to be what they are not. From this it follows that the mode of judging follows the mode of understanding, and that we render judgment about no thing about which there has not been understanding: right if the understanding is right, wrong if it is wrong.

And, to pass over the fact that you did not answer what was objected, that judgment does not extend more widely than understanding, but that understanding rather extends more widely than judgment, or, to use your words, intellect more widely than will, I note only that there seems to be an appearance of contradiction here when you say that we judge something to be understood by us more fully than it is really understood. For how can what is not understood be understood, so that what is not understood is judged to be understood?

Do you mean to say that we judge something understood to be in the thing, when it is not in the thing? But it is one thing to be in the thing, another for it to be understood to be in the thing. Whether something is or is not in the thing belongs to the truth or falsity of the judgment. But that it is understood to be in the thing belongs absolutely to rendering judgment about the thing. Therefore whether what is judged is in the thing or is not in it, it is nevertheless understood to be there. Thus, although it is not really there, nevertheless it is really, that is, actually, understood to be there; and our judgment is false not because we understand what we do not understand, but because we understand what is not in the thing.

## Notes

- Gassendi splits Descartes' sentence by the two senses of `will`: if will means judging intellect, it cannot outrun intellect's first apprehension; if it means appetitive will, it cannot will error as error.
- Error is not loved as error. A judgment that happens to be erroneous is willed as true, because intellect presents it as true.
- The thin-body mind example is rejected as a caricature of Gassendi's position. By faith he holds mind incorporeal; by natural light he says the matter is obscure.
- The challenge is turned back on Descartes: if Descartes claims clear and distinct natural knowledge of incorporeal mind, he must say under what non-corporeal representation the embodied mind understands itself.
- The poisoned-apple example again follows judgment. If poison is judged, it is willed as judged; if not judged, the apple is willed only under judged features such as fragrant, colored, tasty, and nourishing.
- Descartes' concession that we will nothing without understanding something becomes evidence against the wider-will argument.
- Gassendi pairs willing and understanding at the same level: whole/confused, part/distinct, or part/confused.
- The closing distinction is important: false judgment is not understanding what is not understood, but understanding something as in the thing when it is not in the thing.
