## Opening Finding

Descartes answers Gassendi's continuum argument by refusing to identify clear understanding with complete imaginative inspection. The chiliagon is not confusedly understood merely because it cannot be imagined all at once: many things can be demonstrated about it distinctly and clearly, and this would be impossible if it were perceived only confusedly or merely by name. The distinction between understanding and imagining is therefore not a difference of more and less, but a difference between two modes of operation. In intellection the mind uses itself alone; in imagination it contemplates a corporeal form. Descartes also draws the line against Gassendi's corporeal account of ideas: geometrical figures may be corporeal, but the ideas by which they are understood need not be corporeal when they do not fall under imagination. His final rebuke names Gassendi as "O flesh" and states the Cartesian counter-thesis sharply: whoever represents God or mind by human form, air, aether, or other subtle bodies tries to imagine what is not imaginable and falsely assigns the name of God or mind to a corporeal idea. In the true idea of mind, only thought and its non-corporeal attributes are contained.

## Working Translation

RESPONSE.

As for your denying that material things, insofar as they are the object of pure mathematics, exist, I have already dealt with that earlier.

But it is false that the intellection of the chiliagon is confused. For many things can be demonstrated about it most distinctly and most clearly, which certainly would not happen if it were perceived only confusedly or, as you say, by name alone. But in reality we clearly understand the whole of it at once, although we cannot imagine the whole of it at once.

From this it is clear that the powers of understanding and imagining do not differ only according to more and less, but as two plainly different modes of operation. For in intellection the mind uses itself alone, while in imagination it contemplates a corporeal form.

And although geometrical figures are altogether corporeal, nevertheless the ideas by which they are understood, when they do not fall under imagination, should not on that account be thought corporeal.

And finally, O flesh, it is worthy of you alone to judge that the ideas of God, angel, and the human mind are corporeal or quasi-corporeal, drawn, that is, from human form and from other very tenuous, very simple, very insensible things, such as air or aether. For whoever represents God or mind to himself in that way is trying to imagine a thing that is not imaginable, and fashions nothing but a corporeal idea, to which he falsely assigns the name of God or mind. For in the true idea of mind only thought is contained, with its attributes, none of which are corporeal.

## Notes

- Descartes refuses Gassendi's claim that the chiliagon is understood only confusedly or by the force of the name.
- Demonstrability is used as evidence of clear intellection: if many things can be demonstrated of the chiliagon, it cannot be merely confused.
- `Totum simul clare intelligimus` separates all-at-once understanding from all-at-once imagining.
- Intellection and imagination are not degrees of one operation but two modes of operation.
- In intellection the mind uses itself alone; in imagination it contemplates corporeal form.
- Descartes accepts that geometrical figures are corporeal but denies that non-imaginative ideas of them must be corporeal.
- The rebuke `o caro` makes the mind/flesh opposition explicit and answers Gassendi's `o Mens` from the prior doubt.
- The true idea of mind contains thought and its non-corporeal attributes, not a corporeal or quasi-corporeal representation.

## Translation Risk

Lexical/contextual: `totum simul clare intelligimus` is rendered as "we clearly understand the whole of it at once." The next `Instantia` will test whether Descartes means the whole figure itself, the definition, or demonstrable properties under a name.
