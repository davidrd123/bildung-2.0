## Opening Finding

Gassendi argues that Descartes' complete-substance restriction exposes the fallacy of the whole proof. The question is not whether two already separate complete substances are really distinct; everyone grants that. The question is whether what Descartes calls two things might instead be one thing conceived under two concepts, as with thinking and extension in a subtle body. By naming them "two things" or "complete substances," Descartes assumes the point under dispute. Gassendi then attacks the supporting distinction between adequate and inadequate cognition. If adequate cognition contains all properties in the known thing, only God has it; all human cognition is inadequate because it abstracts from what remains unknown or unattended. Without revelation or total knowledge of both entities, Descartes cannot exclude an indivisible nexus that would make them inseparable and not really distinct.

## Working Translation

But first, the matter seems worthy of consideration: the fallacy of your proposition, and therefore of your whole demonstration, is brought out so clearly by you yourself. For since the question is not about two separate things, which are manifestly both two and different, is not the difficulty about a thing which you say is not one but two, namely one which, although the items are so closely joined that they appear to be one, nevertheless maintain a distinction between themselves on account of which they are separable, while others say they are not two but one, which, although it is conceived by two different concepts, nevertheless maintains a unity on account of which the items presented by concepts of this kind remain inseparable?

The question is certainly about the nature of mind, namely whether it is corporeal. And although the difficulty is not whether it is this gross body consisting of members, nevertheless it is disputed whether it is a subtle body, which even you do not deny, as follows from what has now been said, is very closely joined to it.

But when you want the thinking thing, which you call mind, and the extended thing, which you call body, although they are so connected that they appear to be one thing, nevertheless to be two things which retain a distinction and can be separated, others want, or rather defend only for the sake of argument in order to test the force of your reasons, that mind is one thing which is extended and thinking; and although it is conceived now as thinking, now as extended, and for that reason seems to be two things, nevertheless it is one, and that which thinks is not separable from that which is extended. And this, indeed, is the hypothesis. But now you speak of the thesis in this way: "It is enough that I can clearly and distinctly understand one thing without another in order for me to be certain that one is different from the other."

What are you doing meanwhile? Clearly you assume as a principle the very thing that is disputed with you, namely that there are two things, when others dispute that they are two and defend that they are one. And if indeed you understood two things in the way theologians take them when they treat distinctions, you could rightly be said to be contained within the question, or, as they commonly say, to remain within the terms. But under the name of one and another, or of two things, you understand two substances, and in no way two properties, two faculties, and so on, as you answered the learned men. Therefore you altogether slip past what has to be proved, by proposing two things or substances to those who deny that there are two and defend that there is one.

What is more, since the difficulty is whether between the items which you call two and they call one there is a formal or a real distinction, and since you contend that it is real, you use the real distinction itself as the middle term or argument, since you do not use the word distinction, but diversity, which you immediately interpret not as the same as distinction in general, but as real distinction specifically, according to which one thing can be placed apart from another.

I pass over the fact that no one ever doubted whether two substances, or things complete in your sense, are really distinguished and can be separated and exist apart from one another. Rather, the dispute has been either about two incomplete substances, one as act and the other as potency, such as they call matter and form; or about substance and accident, as corporeal substance and quantity are; or about two accidents, such as quantity and filling of place.

Therefore you accomplish nothing when from your understanding you prove that two substances, or complete things, are two substances or different things, really distinct, and capable of one being placed apart from the other. Obviously this is confessed among all and disputed by no one against you, nor is there any need to flee with such great effort to the power of God.

The question is whether, when there are two items, do not call them things; call them incomplete entities; call them whatever you like, whether, I say, when there are two items of which one is clearly and distinctly understood without the other, as you do not deny that one property of a triangle can be understood without another, one is therefore really distinguished from the other and can be placed apart from it. And nevertheless you want the question to be only about clear and distinct cognition in two complete substances, as if the matter did not aim at explaining whether being extended and thinking are two properties of the same substance, or constitute two distinct substances; and as if it did not fall to you to prove, not whether complete substances are diverse or really distinct, but whether those items of which one is clearly and distinctly understood without the other are two complete substances.

Next, to say it by the way, you seem to be twisted in vain both to establish a distinction between things that are really and formally distinguished, and to defend the notion you propose for each distinction. For since the business would be finished for you by this one statement, "Only things, or complete substances, one of which I clearly and distinctly understand without the other, are really distinguished," there is no reason for anything more to be asked of you.

And yet look: you seem reduced to no small straits when, among other things, forced to explain adequate and inadequate cognition, you twist yourself in various ways and conclude in this way: "Therefore, when I said that it is not enough that one thing be understood without another by an abstraction of the intellect conceiving the thing inadequately, I did not think it could be inferred from this that adequate cognition is required for real distinction, but only cognition which we would not render inadequate by abstraction of the intellect. For it is very different for some cognition to be plainly adequate, and for it to be adequate to some extent, so that we perceive it is not rendered inadequate by us through abstraction of the intellect."

But if you thought that for real distinction it is not enough that one thing be understood without another by an abstraction of the intellect conceiving the thing inadequately, what reason was there why you did not think it could be inferred that it is therefore required that one thing be understood without another by an abstraction of the intellect conceiving the thing adequately? Is this not a legitimate consequence? And since to conceive adequately is to have adequate cognition, why did you not think adequate cognition was required?

"I thought," you say, "that cognition suffices which we do not render inadequate by abstraction of the intellect." But what is this, that we render cognition inadequate by abstraction of the intellect? What do you call inadequate cognition? Is it not cognition that is not adequate to the known thing, or by which we know not the whole of whatever is in the thing, but only a part? You certainly define adequate cognition as that in which absolutely all properties that are in the known thing are contained, such as God alone knows himself to have.

Therefore, since it is true that there is no thing of which we know more than a part of what is in it, none of our cognition is not inadequate. Therefore none is adequate, or, since you do not want to confess this openly, none that we can know to be adequate unless it is revealed by God, who could have placed more in the thing than is known by us. Therefore we render no cognition that is adequate, or that we can say is adequate, inadequate by abstraction of the intellect.

What, then, do you call our rendering it inadequate by abstraction? Certainly all our cognition is a kind of abstraction, insofar as from all the things God has placed in a thing, and which are knowable, we know only some, and so, as it were, abstract, so that the rest remain unknown; or insofar as from the things we know habitually, we consider one thing actually, and so, as it were, abstract, so that the rest are counted as excluded from consideration.

And so, if none of our cognition is without abstraction; if none is without exclusion of many things which are knowable in the known thing; if none therefore is not made inadequate by abstraction, why do you propose as a special effect that we render cognition inadequate by abstraction of the intellect?

"It is very different," you say, "for cognition to be plainly adequate and for it to be adequate to some extent." But no cognition is adequate if it is not plainly adequate; and if it is said to be only adequate to some extent, it is inadequate in reality. For if it is asked whether a pygmy is adequate to a giant, you will say that he is not indeed plainly adequate, but is nevertheless adequate to some extent, because by his height he rises to half the giant's leg. Yet by the very fact that he is adequate only to the height of a part, he is inadequate in reality.

Therefore it does no good to distinguish in this way, so that you say cognition becomes inadequate from adequate. Nor does it help to add that it ought to be adequate to the point that we perceive it is not rendered inadequate by us through abstraction of the intellect, since there is absolutely none that we do not perceive to be rendered inadequate. If it includes all that we know about the thing, it is rendered inadequate insofar as all the rest that we do not know are excluded from it, and only by God revealing can we perceive what they are or are not. If it includes only part of what we know habitually, it is rendered inadequate insofar as other things are excluded from it which we are not considering at all.

I say now, therefore: if there are two entities, I would not dare, because of you, to say two things, which we see to be unseparated, and the question is whether they are mutually separable or really distinguished, and you affirm this while I deny it, how will you convince me? Will you be able to say that God has placed nothing in each entity that you do not clearly and distinctly perceive? Certainly you will not be able to, unless at the same time you say that God revealed this to you. Will you say that you have cognition of each so adequate that you perceive it is not rendered inadequate by abstraction of the intellect? Certainly not, because of what has already been said. How, then, will you be able to say that there is not something in each which is, as it were, an indivisible nexus, on account of which they are inseparable from one another and not really distinguished? Certainly, until you know all the things that are in each, so that absolutely nothing escapes you, there can always be the suspicion that nothing prevents there being something by which separability and real distinction are removed.

## Notes

- Gassendi says Descartes begs the question by calling the disputed pair two complete substances.
- If two complete substances are already granted, real distinction and separability are not disputed.
- The actual dispute is whether thinking and extension are two properties of one substance or two complete substances.
- The move from "diverse" to "really distinct" imports the desired conclusion as the middle term.
- The complete-substance restriction evades the triangle-property counterexample rather than answering the underlying criterion problem.
- Gassendi attacks Descartes' adequate/inadequate cognition distinction as unstable.
- If adequate cognition contains all properties in the known thing, only God has it, and human cognition remains inadequate.
- Without exhaustive knowledge or revelation, Descartes cannot rule out an indivisible nexus making the two inseparable.
