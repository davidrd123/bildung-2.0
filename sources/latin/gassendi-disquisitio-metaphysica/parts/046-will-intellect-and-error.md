## Opening Finding

The next Fourth Meditation dispute shifts from the created judging faculty to the cause of error. Descartes had explained error through the will, or faculty of judgment, extending more widely than intellect. Gassendi refuses the geometry of that explanation. He lets Descartes' terminology pass for the moment, but argues that will does not range beyond intellect: the will judges nothing the intellect has not first presented. If anything, intellect is wider, since it obscurely apprehends many things on which no judgment, pursuit, or flight follows. Error therefore does not arise because will exceeds intellect, but because intellect apprehends badly and will judges badly after that apprehension. Descartes answers by making the overreach internal to judgment: in error we will or affirm more than we understand. The cases of the thin-body mind and the possibly poisoned apple show that one can grasp a few features while willing or judging much more. The disagreement now turns on whether false judgment belongs to intellect's defective showing, or to will's free assent beyond what is understood.

## Working Translation

You next inquire what the cause of falsity or error is in you. First, I do not here dispute why you call intellect only the faculty of knowing ideas, or of simply apprehending the things themselves without any affirmation or negation, while you call will or free choice the faculty of judging, whose role it is to affirm or deny, assent or dissent. I only ask why will, or freedom of choice, is circumscribed by no limits in your account, while intellect is circumscribed.

For these two faculties certainly seem to extend equally widely, and intellect at least not less widely than will, since the will is borne toward nothing which the intellect has not foreseen. I said "at least not less widely," because intellect even seems to extend more widely than will. For not only is there no willing, choice, judgment, and consequently no selection, pursuit, or flight concerning any thing which we have not apprehended, or whose idea has not been perceived and presented by the intellect; but we also understand many things obscurely concerning which there is no judgment, pursuit, or flight. And the faculty of judging is often so undecided that, with equal weights of reasons or with none existing, no judgment follows, while the intellect meanwhile apprehends the things that remain unjudged.

Further, when you say that you can always understand greater and greater things, and especially the very faculty of intellect, of which you can even form an infinite idea, this very thing argues that intellect is not more limited than will, since it can extend itself all the way to an infinite object. And when you acknowledge that your will is equal to the divine will, not extensively but formally, consider why the same could not also be said of intellect, once you define the formal notion of intellect just as you define the formal notion of will.

But briefly, tell us: to what can the will extend itself that escapes intellect? Error therefore does not seem to arise, as you assert, because the will extends more widely than intellect and extends itself to judging those things which intellect does not perceive. Rather it arises because, although they extend equally widely, intellect perceives something badly and will judges badly. Therefore there is no reason for you to advance will beyond the limits of intellect, since it does not judge concerning things which intellect does not perceive, and it judges wrongly only because intellect perceives wrongly.

The example you bring from the thing itself, concerning the reasoning you made about the existence of things, proceeds rightly enough as far as the judgment about your own existence is concerned. But as far as the other things are concerned, it does not seem rightly assumed, since whatever you say, or rather play at, you do not really doubt. Instead you judge altogether that there is something besides you and distinct from you, since you already understand something besides you and distinct from you.

When you suppose that no reason occurs which persuades one side more than the other, you can suppose that; but at the same time you must suppose that no judgment will follow, and that the will always remains indifferent and does not determine itself to judging until some greater likelihood occurs to the intellect on one side than on the other.

Accordingly, what you say next does not seem at all true: that this indifference extends to things which are not known with enough perspicuity in such a way that, however probable the conjectures drawing you toward one side may be, the mere cognition that they are conjectures can impel you to assent to the contrary. For that cognition, that they are only conjectures, will indeed make you render judgment on the side toward which they draw you with some fear and hesitation; but it will never make you render judgment on the contrary side, unless conjectures occur which are not only equally probable but even more probable.

When you add that you experienced this in these days, when you supposed those things to be false which you had believed to be most true, remember that this was not granted to you. For you cannot truly have felt or persuaded yourself that you had not seen the sun, the earth, human beings, and other things; that you had not heard sounds, had not walked, had not eaten, had not written, had not spoken, using of course the body and its organs; and so on.

In the end, therefore, the form of error seems to consist not so much in the incorrect use of free choice, as you assert, as in the dissonance of the judgment from the thing judged. And this arises because the intellect apprehends that thing otherwise than it is. Therefore the fault seems not so much to belong to choice because it does not judge rightly, as to intellect because it does not show rightly.

For the dependence of choice on intellect seems to be such that if intellect clearly perceives something, or seems to perceive it, choice renders a ratified and determinate judgment, whether the thing is true in itself or is thought to be true. But if intellect perceives obscurely, then choice renders a doubtful and fearful judgment, though for the time being it is held truer than the opposite, whether truth or falsity underlies it in the thing itself. From this it follows that we can take care not so much that we not err, as that we not persevere in error; and that we examine our own judgments not by doing violence to choice, but by applying intellect to clearer cognition, which judgment will always follow.

**Response.**

Do you want me here to say briefly what the will can extend itself to that escapes intellect? Namely, to everything in which it happens that we err. Thus when you judge that the mind is some thin body, you can indeed understand that the mind itself is a mind, that is, a thinking thing; likewise that a thin body is an extended thing. But that one and the same thing thinks and is extended, you certainly do not understand. You only want to believe it because you already believed it before and do not gladly depart from your opinion.

So too, when you judge that an apple which is perhaps poisoned is suitable as food for you, you indeed understand that its smell, color, and such things are pleasant; but you do not therefore understand that the apple itself is useful to you as food. Rather, because you will it so, you judge it so. In this way I do indeed admit that we will nothing about which we do not understand something in some way; but I deny that we understand and will equally. For concerning the same thing we can will very many things and know only very few.

But when we judge wrongly, we do not therefore will wrongly, though perhaps we will something wrong; nor do we understand anything wrongly, but we are only said to understand wrongly when we judge that we understand something more fully than we really understand it.

As for what you afterwards deny about the indifference of the will, although those things are manifest in themselves, I do not wish to undertake proving them before you. Such things are of the kind each person ought to experience within himself rather than be persuaded of by reasons; and you, O Flesh, do not seem to attend to those things the mind does within itself. Therefore do not be free if it does not please you. I, certainly, will rejoice in my freedom, since I both experience it in myself and it is attacked by you with no reason, only bare negations. And perhaps I will deserve greater trust among others because I affirm what I have experienced, and what each person will be able to experience within himself, than you who deny the same thing only because perhaps you have not experienced it.

Although it can also be proved from your words that you have experienced this very thing. For by denying that we can take care not to err, because you do not want the will to be borne toward anything to which it is not determined by intellect, you at the same time concede that we can take care not to persevere in error. But that cannot happen at all without that freedom of the will to move itself without the determination of the intellect into one side or the other, which you denied.

For if the intellect has once determined the will to put forward some false judgment, I ask you, when the will first begins to take care not to persevere in error, by what is it determined to this? If by itself, then it can be borne toward something to which it is not driven by intellect; and this is what you denied, and the only thing in controversy. But if by intellect, then the will itself does not take care. Rather, just as before it was borne toward the false which was proposed to it by intellect, so now it happens by chance to be borne toward the true because intellect proposes the true to it.

But besides, I would like to know what nature of the false you conceive, and in what way you think it can be an object of intellect. For I, who understand nothing else by the false than the privation of the true, am persuaded that it plainly conflicts with itself for intellect to apprehend the false under the aspect of the true. Yet that would be necessary if intellect ever determined will to embrace the false.

## Notes

- Gassendi suspends the terminology dispute for the moment, but the pressure is already visible: Descartes assigns simple apprehension to intellect and affirmation/assent to will or free choice.
- Gassendi denies that will ranges beyond intellect. Will judges only what intellect has already presented, and intellect may even range wider because it apprehends things left unjudged.
- Error is relocated from overextended will to bad apprehension: intellect perceives badly, and will judges badly after it.
- The skeptical exercise is challenged again as performance. Gassendi says Descartes never really persuaded himself he had not seen, heard, walked, eaten, written, or spoken.
- Gassendi defines the form of error as dissonance between judgment and judged thing, arising from intellect apprehending otherwise than the thing is.
- Descartes answers by distinguishing understanding something about a thing from understanding all that one wills or judges about it.
- The poisoned-apple example makes will/judgment overrun sparse cognition: pleasant smell and color do not amount to cognition that the apple is useful food.
- Descartes treats indifference of the will as experiential; the ability not to persevere in error is used as evidence that will can turn without being determined by intellect.
