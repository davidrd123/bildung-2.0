## Opening Finding

The question now shifts from the cause of error to the missing method for recognizing when clear and distinct perception is actually safe. Gassendi grants that one should attend to what one perfectly understands and separate it from what is confused or obscure, but says this is too obvious to need the whole preceding meditation. The real difficulty is not whether clear and distinct cognition would avoid error, but by what art or method one can tell that one's cognition is genuinely clear and distinct, since people often seem to themselves to cognize something as clearly as possible and yet are deceived. Descartes answers by returning to the preface: the fruit of the Meditations will not be great for readers who dispute isolated parts rather than grasping the order and connection of the reasons; and those who have not worked to shed prejudices should not be expected to perceive the method easily.

## Working Translation

DUBITATIO IV.

On the still-desired method by which we may recognize that we perceive something so clearly and distinctly that no danger of erring is present.

You conclude by magnifying the fruit that you can obtain from this meditation, and you prescribe what must be done so that you may attain truth. Namely, you say that you will attain it if only you attend sufficiently to everything that you perfectly understand, and separate those things from the rest that you apprehend more confusedly and obscurely.

Now this is not only true, but also of such a kind that the whole preceding meditation, without which it could have been understood, seems to have been superfluous. Consider, however, excellent man, that the difficulty does not seem to be whether, in order not to be deceived, we must understand something clearly and distinctly. Rather, it is by what art or method we may discern that we have clear and distinct understanding in such a way that it is true and cannot happen that we are deceived.

For at the beginning we objected that we are not rarely deceived, even though we seem to ourselves to cognize something so clearly and distinctly that nothing could be clearer and more distinct. You too objected this to yourself, and yet we are still waiting for that art or method on which one should especially lean.

RESPONSIO.

As to the fruit of these Meditations, I warned sufficiently in the little preface, which I suppose you have read, that it would not be great for those who, not caring to comprehend the sequence and connection of my reasons, will be eager to dispute only about individual parts of them.

And as to the method by which we can distinguish the things that are really perceived clearly from those that are only thought to be perceived clearly, although I believe that I have handed it down accurately enough, as has already been said, I nevertheless do not at all trust that those who labor so little to strip away prejudices that they complain because I have not spoken about them simply and in few words will easily perceive it.

## Notes

- Gassendi accepts the general rule but says it is trivial: attend to what is perfectly understood and separate it from confused apprehension.
- The real problem is criterial, not exhortative. How does one know that a cognition is genuinely clear and distinct rather than merely seeming so?
- This restates the practical edge of the Cartesian circle: one needs secure clear-and-distinct cognition, but must also distinguish it from counterfeit clarity.
- Descartes' answer does not yet give a fresh criterion. It shifts attention to the preface, the order and connection of the reasons, and the labor of removing prejudices.
