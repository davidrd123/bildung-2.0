## Opening Finding

Gassendi closes the fourth `Instantia` by returning Descartes' non-extension answer to the question it was supposed to solve. If non-extension was only a warning against error, then it still does not explain what mind is. If mind is united to the whole body, Descartes cannot answer the extension argument by simply repeating that extension is not in mind's account. Gassendi treats that as the disputed conclusion reintroduced as a principle. The section then recasts Descartes' clear-and-distinct self-knowledge as a private, unshaken principle that can persuade Descartes but cannot function as public demonstration. The unanswered physical questions remain: how an incorporeal, unextended mind touches, impels, and moves body, and where exactly its union with body is supposed to occur.

## Working Translation

Then you fall back on this: you say that you added that mind is a non-extended thing, not in order to explain what it itself was, but only to warn that those are in error who think it extended. Yet you had no more warned against that than you had warned against those who might think it non-thinking; for otherwise, when you were reporting what mind was, or what was represented through its idea, you had said absolutely and in one continuous statement that you have its idea insofar as it is only a thinking thing, not extended.

But obviously this is what pressed you: the objection had been made that by this word you expressed not what mind was, but what it was not. However it stands, I accept this gladly, although I see that you, whether voluntarily or from an error of reading I do not know, compare Bucephalus with Music, while I, both from common sense and from the printer's reading, compared him with a fly.

Let readers see, then, how suitably you say that I reason no better, when I argue that mind is extended if it is coextended with body, than if someone argued that Bucephalus is Music if he neighs, or produces sounds that can be referred to music. For what parity is there, I ask, or what application of likeness? None at all. So you only go on: "Even if mind is united to the whole body, it does not therefore follow that it is extended through the body."

But how do you prove that it does not follow? "Because," you say, "it is not of its account to be extended, but only to think." What a solution! The question is what mind is. You define it as a thinking thing, not extended. The latter clause is disputed with you by those who say that, at least on the soul's account, it is extended.

You say that they are in error. They prove that they are not in error by an argument you yourself do not deny: namely, that mind is united to the whole body. For body is extended insofar as it is not in a point, but is diffused through various parts of place, to which it therefore necessarily has corresponding parts. So mind too, since it is not in a point but is diffused through just as many parts of place, through the very body to the whole of which it is united, and therefore through just as many corresponding parts of its own, will plainly have to be called extended.

Do you really solve this argument? You say that it is not of mind's account to be extended. Is this not the very thing in question? And should the conclusion alone be brought forward as the reason and defense of the conclusion? In fact there is no reason for you to argue, or to think one must argue with you, if, once you have laid down a dogma, you offer nothing in defense of it except the dogma itself, and, whatever is objected, want it to stand unshaken solely because you offer it.

Is it not evidence that this is what you wanted, that, abandoning the royal road of demonstrating, you addressed only yourself, spoke of yourself alone, expressed only your own judgment about yourself, and then judged that this should be held by all mortals as a principle of demonstration? It is no wonder, then, if you have seemed to reason in this way:

That of which I have a clear and distinct idea must be a fixed and unshaken principle.

But I, who am mind, have a clear and distinct idea that I am only a thinking thing, not extended, while my body is an extended thing, not thinking.

Therefore that I, that is mind, am only a thinking thing, not extended, and that my body is an extended thing, not thinking, must be a fixed and unshaken principle.

And consequently:

Whatever I deduce from my fixed and unshaken principle is certain.

But from my fixed and unshaken principle, that I am only a thinking thing, not extended, and that my body is an extended thing, not thinking, I deduce that I am truly distinct from my body and can exist without it.

Therefore it is certain that I am truly distinct from my body and can exist without it.

What should be thought of this reasoning must be judged from what has been said before. I add here only, because of the final words of your response, that you have shown beautifully how much what you yourself say pleases you. I had proposed the difficulty: how can the soul, if it is unextended and incorporeal, touch, impel, and move body? But you replied only with these words: "Nor finally is it necessary that it itself be body, even if it has the power of moving body."

In other words, because you yourself said it, you decided that it must be held fixed and certain, and you thought the readers would be so inattentive that they would not see the difficulty, or would believe that this was done abundantly and sufficiently. I would now answer something; but you did not want to provide an occasion, since not only did you fail to solve the difficulties noted by you, you did not even touch the others, and those the chief ones.

Not to mention specifically that you have still cleverly avoided what ought not to have been concealed concerning the union of mind with body: whether it is united to the whole body, or to the whole brain, or to a sensible part of the brain, or to a physical point, or to a mathematical point. But because, whatever you declared, the difficulties were ready to hand, it is no wonder if you judged it better to avoid and conceal than to enter a labyrinth from which afterward you could not extricate yourself.

## Notes

- Gassendi accepts Descartes' clarification only narrowly: non-extension may warn against an error, but it still does not say positively what mind is.
- The section corrects the Bucephalus comparison. Descartes' `Musica` response is treated as a likely misreading of Gassendi's intended `Musca`, a fly.
- Whole-body union remains the main pressure. If body is extended because it is diffused through places with corresponding parts, then mind united to the whole body seems to inherit the same diffusion unless Descartes gives a further account.
- Saying that extension is not of mind's `ratio` is treated as begging the question, since non-extension is precisely the disputed clause.
- Gassendi reconstructs Descartes' argument as private authority: Descartes' own clear and distinct idea becomes a fixed principle for all mortals without a public demonstration.
- The closing list carries forward the point-location problem from Part 083: whole body, whole brain, sensible brain part, physical point, mathematical point.
- The next `Dubitatio` will start from the same unresolved body-mind union problem, now through pain, hunger, thirst, and quasi-mixture.

## Translation Risk

Edition: `Musica` / `Musca` should be kept as a reported textual correction, not silently retrojected into Descartes' earlier response. Part 084 should continue to record Descartes' printed `Musica` comparison.
