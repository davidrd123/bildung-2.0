## Related Vol. 2 Gassendi Material

Parts 007-011 are the active Cassirer chapter context. Most of the other source pressure in these parts points to Gassendi's *Syntagma*, *Physica*, and later Opera locations rather than directly to the *Disquisitio*. Do not treat this campaign as if it grounds the whole chapter by itself.

Current use:

- Part 007: perception as mediated motion-sign; mostly `Syntagma Philosophiae Epicuri` and physics materials.
- Part 008: sensory truth and judgment; not yet a direct *Disquisitio* anchor.
- Part 009: space and time pressure; not yet a direct *Disquisitio* anchor.
- Part 010: atomistic motion and conceptual alteration of perception; not yet a direct *Disquisitio* anchor.
- Part 011: Descartes comparison and the sun idea; direct *Disquisitio* anchor located. The sequential continuation now also exposes Gassendi's pressure on objective reality, representational image-causality, mental images, reflected self-knowledge, self-action, infinite-world amplification, the causal proof of God, the Cartesian circle, the public cosmological `via regia` opposed to Descartes' private enclosed idea, the social-linguistic origin of divine attributes, the mind's native comparison/combination/amplification of received materials, the return of the sun example as graded true representation under a common measure, the finite comparison model for defect-awareness, equivocation over what counts as a true idea, resistance to turning temporal dependence into continuous recreation, Descartes' `secundum fieri` / `secundum esse` reply, Gassendi's demand that conservation arguments preserve numerical individuality rather than appeal to serial replacement like sunlight, his attack on time/duration as too obscure to serve as a natural-light premise, the formal charge that temporal recreation begs what the proof must establish, the challenge to importing creation/conservation from Scholastic supernatural doctrine into natural-light demonstration, the insistence that continuous-influx claims remain disputed rather than answered by alleging inconvenience, the renewed role of parents as thinking producing causes with the idea of God, the distinction between accidental parent-regress and essential causal subordination, the final rejection that amplified beyond-human perfections entail real existence, the renewed pressure on impressed-image causality through the artisan-mark analogy, Descartes' reply through indivisible essences and Apelles' recognizable artistry, Gassendi's syllogistic reconstruction of the Meditation's closing arguments, the partial-cognition critique of divine perfections under Descartes' `touching by thought` concession, the strategic storage of divine non-deception for later certainty claims, the renewed account of ideas as distinct representations of accidents rather than hidden essences, the contrast between a posteriori approach to divine attributes and Descartes' conflation of idea with essence, the microscope/telescope/seal argument that increased distinctness adds to the idea because ideas measure knowledge rather than things themselves, the renewed rejection of the triangle/all-at-once infinite-being analogy, the separation of workmanship-marks from image-likeness in the Apelles analogy, the prototype/copy threshold for image-likeness under natural demonstration, and the appeal to absence and diversity of God-knowledge against a uniform impressed idea.
- Part 040: the Fourth Meditation opening adds a direct source witness for Cassirer's Vol. 2 judgment/error pressure. Gassendi asks why a finite faculty of judgment created by God had to be liable to error, while Descartes answers through a negative idea of nothing and by treating the senses as hindrances to pure ideas of God and self. The same part also records a physics-side divergence over final causes: Gassendi defends organic use and anatomical fittedness as evidence of providence, while Descartes restricts physics to efficient causes.
- Part 041: the `Instantia` gives the Meditation IV form of the Cartesian circle. Gassendi argues that Descartes assumes clear and distinct cognition as truthful in order to prove God, while that cognition's non-fallaciousness is supposed to be secured only by God as non-deceptive author.
- Part 042: the final-cause exchange complicates the physics contrast. Gassendi does not deny efficient causes; he argues that final order is often the route to recognizing an efficient cause at all, while proximate efficient causes remain obscure once one moves beyond general principles.
- Part 043: the sense-isolation reply gives direct support for the chapter's psychology pressure. Gassendi treats corporeal images as occasions and steps for ascent toward divine perfections, not as either pure obstacles or sufficient origins.
- Part 044: the second Fourth Meditation doubt strengthens the judgment/error contact. Gassendi presses whether error-liability can be treated as mere part/whole variety, privation, or negation of greater perfection once the judging faculty is considered in its own finite office.
- Part 045: Gassendi's `Instantia` makes the judgment/error pressure sharper by distinguishing limitation from defect. Finite scope is not the issue; the issue is why the faculty judges imperfectly within the few matters assigned to it.
- Part 046: the judgment/error contact shifts into the will/intellect problem. Gassendi treats error as dissonance rooted in intellect's bad apprehension, while Descartes says error occurs when will or judgment affirms more than intellect understands.
- Part 047: Gassendi answers the will/intellect problem by remapping the faculties. Judgment and reasoning are intellectual operations; will is a blind appetitive power that follows what intellect presents. This gives Cassirer's judgment/error issue a rival faculty architecture, not just a local objection.
- Part 048: the direct answer to Descartes' examples narrows the error theory further. False judgment is a mismatch between what is understood as being in the thing and what is in the thing, while will remains tied to what intellect has presented, distinctly or confusedly.
- Part 049: the indifference/freedom section makes the same structure modal and practical. Indifference is wider than freedom, intellect has its own indifference under appearances of truth, and will's freedom follows apparent or greater good as presented by intellect. Gassendi's `libentia` preserves necessary adherence to the supreme good without making it evidence for a will independent of cognition.
- Part 050: the non-perseverance answer gives the correction mechanism. Passion can darken apprehensions so a lesser good appears nearer and better, but the turn toward examination is still determined by intellect through a reflexive judgment that first notions may need to yield to sounder ones.
- Part 051: the closing falsity section gives the ontology of error. Outside intellect, things are transcendentally true; falsity is privation of judgment's conformity to the thing, with "false" things named only extrinsically through mistaken presentation.
- Part 052: the fourth doubt turns the error problem into a method problem. Gassendi asks not whether genuine clear and distinct cognition is safe, but how apparent clarity is to be distinguished from genuine clarity before it is trusted.
- Part 053: the opening `Instantia` makes the method problem procedural. Gassendi insists on examining both whole sequence and individual members, and demands a public location where the promised criterion was actually delivered.
- Part 054: the circle is reconstructed as a criterion failure. Descartes secures clear cognition by God and God by clear cognition, while Gassendi adds that apparent clear cognition can mimic genuine clarity unless a further method distinguishes them.
- Part 055: Gassendi extends the criterion failure to method rules themselves. Survey, division, simple-to-complex order, and enumeration all need a further sign that they have been completed without omission or misordering.
- Part 056: the Fifth Meditation opening reconnects the project to Cassirer's abstraction problem. Gassendi treats the mental triangle and universal essences as rules abstracted from singular sensory materials, not autonomous natures outside intellect.
- Part 057: Descartes' response gives the opposing pole for Cassirer: the true geometrical object is not abstracted from sensed singulars but already available to mind and used to recognize rough sensory figures.
- Part 058: Gassendi's reply connects the abstraction dispute back to physics. Quantity, figure, position, and motion are necessary but too thin unless they explain material powers and actions through corpuscles or principles.
- Part 059: the abstraction dispute becomes a modal-ontological dispute. Gassendi rejects non-existing true natures and present reality in possibles, then makes universal natures depend on God only through actual singulars and the intellect that forms general notions from them.
- Part 060: the Fifth Meditation abstraction dispute ends by distinguishing innate faculty from innate ideas. Gassendi grants native cognitive power but makes object-ideas arise from singulars, descriptions, comparison, and progressive abstraction.
- Part 061: the ontological proof shows the same anti-conceptualist pressure in another register. Gassendi denies that existence is an item inside the idea's perfection-list and treats the move from supreme perfection to existence as a petitio principii unless independently demonstrated.
- Part 062: Descartes' response makes the ontological counter-pole explicit: necessary existence belongs to God's essence because God is his own being, while possible existence only perfects created or geometrical ideas.
- Part 063: Gassendi answers by distinguishing physical property from logical attribute and treating existence as transcendental rather than as a special property contained in a concept-list.
- Part 064: the ideal-state/real-state distinction gives the epistemic form of the objection. Conceptual containment in an idea authorizes truth only inside the idea; experience in the real state must adjudicate conformity to extra-mental things.
- Part 065: the minor-premise attack closes the ontological-proof section by denying that description of God's idea demonstrates God, and by separating existence in idea from existence in reality.
- Part 066: the certainty section returns the circle pressure to mathematics. Gassendi resists making geometrical demonstration depend on prior knowledge of God, while Descartes answers through the skeptical doubt of demonstrations.
