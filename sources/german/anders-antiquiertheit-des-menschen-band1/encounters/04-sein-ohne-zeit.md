## Why This Tranche

This is the campaign's worldlessness test.

Anders is not using Beckett as ornament. The opening sections of `Sein ohne Zeit` give him a way to name:

- negative parable as form
- the non-world of modern existence
- continued existence without directed action
- waiting as the misinterpretation of merely staying there

## Current Pressure

This tranche risks looking like a detachable literary essay, but it belongs to the same apparatus-world diagnosis as the other encounters. The point is not Beckett commentary for its own sake; it is a phenomenology of stalled historical life.

The phrase `Sein ohne Zeit` resists easy English because it names both temporal deprivation and world-structural deprivation at once.

## Corrected German (negative parable)

```text
Daß es sich um eine Parabel handelt, darüber sind sich alle Kommentatoren
einig. Aber der Streit um die Auslegung der Parabel ist in vollem Gange,
ohne daß auch nur einer von jenen, die darüber streiten, wer oder was
Godot sei, sich den Kopf darüber zerbrochen hätte, welchem Mechanismus
Parabeln gehorchen. Der Mechanismus heißt "Inversion".

Denn um die Fabel von derjenigen Daseinsform, die Form oder Prinzip nicht
mehr kennt, und in der das Leben nicht mehr weitergeht, zu erzählen,
zerstört er Form und Prinzip der Fabel: die zerstörte, nämlich die nicht
weitergehende Fabel wird nun zur angemessenen Fabel vom nicht
weitergehenden Leben. Will man daher Becketts "Inversion"
zurückübersetzen, so bedeutet seine sinnlose Parabel vom Menschen die
Parabel vom sinnlosen Menschen.
```

## Draft

```text
All the commentators agree that it is a parable. But the dispute over how
to interpret the parable is in full swing, without even one of those who
argue over who or what Godot is having broken his head over the mechanism
that parables obey. That mechanism is called "inversion."

For in order to tell the fable of that mode of existence which no longer
knows form or principle, and in which life no longer goes forward, Beckett
destroys the form and principle of the fable: the destroyed fable, that
is, the one that no longer proceeds, becomes the fitting fable of
life-that-no-longer-proceeds. If one therefore retranslates Beckett's
"inversion," his meaningless parable of man means the parable of
meaningless man.
```

## Corrected German (worldlessness and Nicht-Welt)

```text
Die beiden "Helden" sind also nur noch am Leben, nicht mehr in der Welt.
Und dies ist mit so erbarmungsloser Folgerichtigkeit durchgeführt, daß
andere Darstellungen weltlosen Daseins daneben geradezu tröstlich wirken.

Irgendwie hatten sie also alle noch "Welt": zu viel und darum keine
bestimmte Welt Biberkopf; Welthoffnung K.; eine falsche Welt Kohlhaas.
Angekommen in der Nicht-Welt waren sie noch nicht. Dort angekommen sind
erst Becketts Geschöpfe.

Da es, wo es keine Welt mehr gibt, auch Kollision mit der Welt nicht mehr
geben kann, ist die Möglichkeit des Tragischen verlorengegangen. Oder
richtiger: das Tragische an diesem Dasein besteht darin, daß ihm noch nicht
einmal mehr Tragik vergönnt wird, daß es immer zugleich als Ganzes Farce
ist.
```

## Draft

```text
The two "heroes" are therefore only still alive, no longer in the world.
And this is carried through with such merciless consistency that other
depictions of worldless existence seem almost consoling beside it.

In one way or another they all still had "world": too much of it and
therefore no determinate world in Biberkopf's case; world-hope in K.'s;
a false world in Kohlhaas's. They had not yet arrived in the Nicht-Welt.
It is only Beckett's creatures who have arrived there.

Since where there is no longer any world there can no longer be collision
with the world either, the possibility of the tragic has been lost. Or
rather: the tragic in this existence consists in the fact that not even
tragedy is granted to it any longer, that it is always at once, as a
whole, farce.
```

## Corrected German (waiting as misread existence)

```text
Daß Estragon und Wladimir, die absolut nichts tun, für Millionen tätiger
Menschen repräsentativ sind, wird wohl niemand leugnen können.

Von dieser Art "Leben": vom Menschen, der bleibt, weil er nun einmal da
ist, berichtet also Becketts Stück.

Die Devise, die man allen klassischen Desperadofiguren in den Mund hätte
legen können, hätte gelautet: "Wir haben nichts mehr zu erwarten, also
bleiben wir nicht." Estragon und Wladimir dagegen benützen
"Inversionsformen" dieser Losung: "Wir bleiben", scheinen sie zu sprechen,
"also warten wir." Und: "Wir warten, also haben wir etwas zu erwarten."

Diese Devisen klingen positiver als die ihrer Ahnen. Aber sie klingen nur
so. Denn daß die zwei auf etwas Bestimmtes warteten, davon kann gar keine
Rede sein. Eigentlich warten sie also auf gar nichts. Aber angesichts und
auf Grund ihres täglichen Weiterexistierens ist es ihnen unmöglich, nicht
zu schließen, daß sie warten.

Godot ist nichts als der Titel für die Tatsache, daß Dasein, das sinnlos
weitergeht, sich selbst als "Warten", "etwas Erwarten" mißversteht.
```

## Draft

```text
No one will be able to deny that Estragon and Wladimir, who do absolutely
nothing, are representative of millions of active people.

Beckett's play thus speaks of this kind of "life": of the human being who
remains because he simply happens to be there.

The motto one could have put into the mouths of all classical desperado
figures would have been: "We have nothing more to expect, therefore we do
not remain." Estragon and Wladimir, by contrast, use inversion-forms of
this motto: "We remain," they seem to say, "therefore we wait." And: "We
wait, therefore we have something to expect."

These mottos sound more positive than those of their ancestors. But they
only sound that way. For there can be no question of the two waiting for
something determinate. In truth they are waiting for nothing at all. But in
view of and on the basis of their daily continued existence, it is
impossible for them not to conclude that they are waiting.

Godot is nothing other than the title for the fact that existence which
goes on meaninglessly misunderstands itself as "waiting," as "expecting
something."
```

## Notes

- The Beckett essay is not a decorative detour from the rest of Band I. It gives Anders a way to describe the temporal and worldly condition of the `Massenmensch`.
- `Inversion` is methodological here, not just literary. The broken form is part of what the essay knows.
- `Sein ohne Zeit` should remain German in the running draft for now. `Being without time` is intelligible but too quickly metaphysical; the phrase also names a social and historical mutilation.
- `Nicht-Welt` is stronger than simple `worldlessness`. It marks arrival in a condition beyond merely having the wrong world or too much world.
- The waiting analysis matters because it shows existence misreading its own bare continuation as expectation. That gives the Beckett line a direct link to the apparatus-world and labor material.
- Encounter 04 confirms retrospectively that `Massenmensch` is not confined to the media essay.

## What This Tranche Clarified

- `Sein ohne Zeit` names a real structural condition, not a one-off essay title.
- Worldlessness in Anders is not introspective emptiness but the loss of world as a field of action and collision.
- Waiting in Beckett becomes for Anders a model of how modern existence mistakes persistence for meaning.
- The Beckett essay deepens the same diagnosis already visible in the media and shame encounters.

## Decision for Now

- Keep `Sein ohne Zeit` open and visibly German in the running draft.
- Keep `Nicht-Welt` and `Weltlosigkeit` local to the encounter for now.
- Let `Massenmensch` continue to gather pressure across encounters rather than deciding its English yet.
