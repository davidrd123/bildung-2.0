# Nietzsche, Briefe III.281

**Triggered by:** Escolios epigraph (p. 9 of the original edition)

## The OCR Text

```
Dass es sich hier um die lange Logik einer
ganz bestimmten philosophischen Sensibilität
handelt und nicht um ein Durcheinander
von hundert beliebigen Paradoxien
und Heterodoxien, ich glaube, davon
ist auch meinen wohlwollendsten Lesern
nichts aufgegangen.
```

(OCR had *bestinmiten* for *bestimmten*, *nichtís* for *nichts*.)

## Source

From Nietzsche's letters (*Sämtliche Briefe*, vol. III, p. 281). The specific letter needs to be identified — likely from the period of *Beyond Good and Evil* or *Genealogy of Morals*, where Nietzsche was most frustrated by being read as a mere provocateur.

## Attempt

> That what is at work here is the long logic of a quite specific philosophical sensibility, and not a jumble of a hundred arbitrary paradoxes and heterodoxies — I believe even my most well-disposed readers have failed to grasp this.

## Notes

- *die lange Logik einer ganz bestimmten philosophischen Sensibilität* — "the long logic of a quite specific philosophical sensibility." This is the key phrase. Not *system* — *Sensibilität*. Not *Vernunft* (reason) — *Logik* (logic, but also: coherence, rigor of development). Nietzsche is claiming that his aphorisms have an internal logic, but it's a logic of *sensibility*, not of argument.
- *Durcheinander* — jumble, mess, chaos. What his readers see: disconnected provocations.
- *beliebigen* — arbitrary, random. The paradoxes aren't arbitrary — they're necessary. But even well-disposed readers can't see this.
- *aufgegangen* — dawned on, become apparent to. From *aufgehen*: to rise (like the sun), to open up. The understanding hasn't risen in them.

## Why Gómez Dávila Chose This Epigraph

This is the *Escolios*' self-defense avant la lettre. Gómez Dávila is telling you, through Nietzsche's voice: what follows is not a collection of random provocations. There is a "long logic" of sensibility running through it. You will probably miss it. Even his most sympathetic readers will read individual aphorisms as paradoxes rather than seeing the coherent philosophical sensibility that generates them all. The epigraph is a warning and a dare.

## What German Does Here

*Sensibilität* is not *Empfindlichkeit* (sensitivity as in touchiness) or *Gefühl* (feeling). It's closer to the French *sensibilité* — an entire way of perceiving and responding to the world. English "sensibility" works, but in English it's gone soft (Jane Austen territory). In German it retains its philosophical weight: a mode of apprehension prior to argument.
