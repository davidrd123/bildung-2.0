## Why This Passage

The introduction states the compact thesis: the minimal working semiotic
structure is not a single isolated language or text, but a pair of mutually
non-translatable languages connected by translation. `Три функции текста` then
explains why adequate transfer is only one function and why creative translation
produces new texts.

This is the strongest first test because it directly touches the repo's own
translation-residue practice.

## Close English

From `Вводные замечания`:

```text
1. The transmission of existing information (texts).

2. The creation of new information, that is, the creation of texts not
unambiguously derivable by given algorithms from those already existing, but
possessing a certain degree of unpredictability.

3. Memory: the capacity to store and reproduce information (texts).

The study of semiotic systems created by humanity across its cultural history
led to unexpected conclusions: these same functions, to one degree or another,
also belong to semiotic objects. And if in texts of a communicative character
the function of transmitting information predominates, then in artistic texts
created by art the capacity to generate new messages comes forward. In this
connection it was established that the minimal working semiotic structure is not
one artificially isolated language, or a text in such a language, but a parallel
pair of mutually non-translatable languages that are nevertheless connected by a
translation block. Such a mechanism is the minimal cell for generating new
messages. It is also the minimal unit of such a semiotic object as culture. Thus
culture turns out to be two-in-one, minimally, and at the same time an
indivisibly one minimal working semiotic structure. This approach brought
forward the concept of the semiosphere and emphasized the importance of studying
the semiotics of culture.
```

From `Три функции текста`:

```text
Behind this view of the work of the semiotic mechanism stands the conviction
that its goal is the adequate transmission of some message. The system works
well if the message received by the addressee is fully identical to the one sent
by the addresser, and badly if differences are present between these texts.
These differences are classified as errors, for the avoidance of which special
mechanisms of structure work, redundancy in particular.

This conviction is not groundless: it points to an exceptionally important
function of semiotic structures. Yet one must admit that as soon as we accept
this function as the only one, or even as the main one, we find ourselves before
a series of paradoxes.

Thus it becomes clear that, for a full guarantee of adequacy between transmitted
and received message, an artificial simplified language and artificially
simplified communicants are necessary: with a strictly limited volume of memory
and the complete erasure from the semiotic personality of its cultural baggage.
The mechanism created in this way will be able to serve only a limited circle of
semiotic needs; the universalism proper to natural languages will be alien to it
in principle.

Can one consider that this artificial model should be regarded as the model of
language as such, as its ideal, from which it differs only by imperfection, the
natural result of Nature's unreasonable creation? Artificial languages model not
language as such, but one of its functions: the capacity for adequate
transmission of a message. For, by reaching perfection in the realization of
that function, semiotic structures lose the capacity to serve other functions
proper to them in their natural state.

What, then, are these functions?

Here, first of all, the creative one should be named. Every system realizing the
full set of semiotic possibilities not only transmits ready-made messages, but
also serves as a generator of new ones.
```

```text
What, then, shall we call new messages? First of all, let us agree on what we
will not call by this name. Messages obtained from certain initial messages as a
result of unambiguous transformations, that is, messages that are the fruit of
symmetrical transformations of the initial one, will not be considered new. If a
translation from language L1 of text T1 into language L2 leads to the appearance
of a text T2 such that, by the operation of reverse translation, we obtain the
initial text T1, then we will not consider text T2 new in relation to T1. From
this point of view, the correct solving of mathematical problems does not create
new texts.

The polar opposite of artificial languages is represented by semiotic systems in
which the creative function is strongest. It is obvious that if even the most
mediocre poem is translated into another language, that is, into the language of
another poetic system, the operation of reverse translation will not give the
initial text. The very fact that one and the same poem can be translated many
times by different translators testifies that, instead of exact correspondence
to text T1, in this case a certain space is correlated with it. Any of the texts
filling that space will be a possible interpretation of the initial text. In
place of exact correspondence, one of the possible interpretations; in place of
symmetrical transformation, an asymmetrical one; in place of identity between
the elements composing T1 and T2, their conventional equivalence.
```

```text
The text arising in such cases we will regard as new, and the act of translation
that creates it as creative.

The scheme of artistic translation shows that transmitter and receiver use
different codes, K1 and K2, which intersect but are not identical. In the case of
reverse translation, this will yield not the initial text but some third text
T3. Still closer to the real process of message circulation is the case in which
before the transmitter there stands not one code, but a multiple space of codes,
each of which is a complex hierarchical device and allows the generation of a
certain multiplicity of texts equally corresponding to it. Asymmetrical
directedness and the constant need for choice make translation in this case an
act of generating new information and realize the creative function of both
language and text.

Especially indicative is the situation in which between the codes there exists
not simply difference, but a situation of mutual untranslatability. Translation
is carried out by means of a conventional system of equivalences accepted in the
given culture. The combination of translatability and untranslatability, with
different degrees of each, determines the creative function.

Since meaning in this case turns out to be not only the invariant remainder that
is preserved through diverse transformational operations, but also what changes
in the process, we can state an increment of meaning in the text in the course of
these transformations.

One more feature should be noted. When using artificial languages, or natural
and poetic languages as artificial ones, we separate meaning from language. In
complex operations of meaning-generation, language is inseparable from the
content it expresses.
```

## Residue / Strain Report

- English wants to turn `взаимно-непереводимых` into an absolute claim that
  translation is impossible. The argument needs the opposite: translation works
  because equivalence is partial, conventional, and asymmetrical.
- `minimal working semiotic structure` is better than `minimal semiotic
  structure` because `работающей` carries the operative test. The question is
  not what can be isolated analytically, but what can actually generate messages.
- `двуединая` and `неразложимо-единая` should stay awkward for now. Lotman is
  binding duality and unity at the level of minimal structure; elegant English
  would likely hide the pressure.
- `message`, `text`, `language`, and `code` form a small system. Translating
  them fluently one by one is less important than preserving the way the system
  moves.

## What This Sharpens

The packet confirms that Lotman's useful pressure is not simply "translation
friction is productive." His stronger claim is structural: a single isolated
language can transmit, but the generation of new messages requires a minimal
pair of non-identical semiotic systems linked by translation.

For `bildung-2.0`, that sharpens why close/free translation, glossary residue,
and cross-project contact are not cleanup problems. They are part of the
productive apparatus by which understanding changes.

## What This Does Not License

- No promotion of `semiosphere` into a repo master term.
- No full Lotman campaign.
- No stable glossary decision beyond the checked range of packet 001.
- No broad adoption of parallax translation.
- No claim that every non-equivalence is valuable. Lotman's point requires a
  working translation relation, not mere mismatch.
