## Some Conclusions

Immersed in cultural space, the human being inevitably creates around himself
an organized spatial sphere. This sphere includes, on one side, ideological
representations and semiotic models, and on the other, the recreating activity
of the human being, since the world artificially created by people, the
agricultural, architectural, and technical world, correlates with their
semiotic models. The connection here is mutual. On one side, architectural
structures copy the spatial image of the universe; on the other, this image of
the universe is built by analogy with the humanly created world of cultural
structures.

An important feature of spatial models created by culture is that, unlike
other basic forms of semiotic modeling, they are built not on a verbal-discrete
basis but on an iconic-continuous one. Their foundation is formed by visually
imaginable, iconic texts; verbalization has a secondary character. Such an
image of the universe is easier to dance than to tell, easier to draw, sculpt,
or build than to explicate logically. The work of the right hemisphere of the
brain is primary here. But the first attempts at self-description of this
structure inevitably introduce the verbal level, followed by semantic tension
between continuous and discrete sign pictures of the world.

The spatial picture of the world is multilayered. It includes the mythological
universe, scientific modeling, and everyday "common sense." In the ordinary
person these layers, along with a number of others, form a heterogeneous
mixture that functions as something unified. In the consciousness of the modern
person, Newtonian, Einsteinian, or even post-Einsteinian ideas mix with deeply
mythological images and with the persistent habits of seeing the world in its
everyday outlines. Onto this substrate are layered images created by art or by
more deeply developed scientific conceptions, as well as constant recoding of
spatial images into the language of other models. The result is a complex
semiotic mechanism in constant motion.

The spatial image of the world created by culture stands, as it were, between
the human being and the external reality of Nature, in constant attraction to
both poles. It addresses the human being in the name of the external world,
whose image it declares itself to be. Human historical experience subjects this
image to constant reworking, striving toward adequacy to the world it
represents. But this image is always universal, while the world is given to the
human being in experience only partially. For this reason an unavoidable
contradiction inevitably remains between these two interconnected aspects,
which form the universal plane of content and the plane of expression, with the
inevitably incomplete adequacy of the first as reflected in the second.

The relations between the human being and the spatial image of the world are no
less complex. On one side, this image is created by the human being; on the
other, it actively forms the person immersed in it. A parallel with natural
language is possible here. One can say that the activity moving from the human
being toward the spatial model comes from the collective, while the reverse,
formative direction acts on the personality. But this analogy also has a
parallel with poetic language, which is created by the personality and in the
reverse movement acts on the collective. In the process of spatial modeling, as
in the process of language formation, both directions are active.
