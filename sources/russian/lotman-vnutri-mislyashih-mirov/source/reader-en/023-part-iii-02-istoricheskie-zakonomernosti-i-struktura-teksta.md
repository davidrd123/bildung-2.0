## Historical Regularities And The Structure Of The Text

It was said above that the historian is condemned to deal with texts. This
circumstance decisively affects not only the structure of the historical fact,
but also the way it is understood and the way historical regularities are
imagined. The historian does not observe events. The historian receives
retellings of them in the form of narrative sources. Even when the historian is
also an observer of what is being described, as in the rare cases of Herodotus
or Caesar, the historian still transforms those observations in thought into a
verbal text. What is transmitted is not what was seen, but a reflection on what
was seen, a retelling of the visible.

In earlier times historians sometimes opposed information received from verbal
sources, which seemed ambiguous and in need of interpretation, to the supposedly
indisputable testimony of material monuments, archaeological data, and the world
of iconic images. But from the point of view of semiotics, all kinds of
messages are texts, and all share the consequences that follow from using a
text as mediator. The question of how the use of texts affects historical
knowledge therefore has the most general significance.

The transformation of an event into a text means, first of all, that the event
is retold within the system of one language or another. It is subordinated to a
structural organization that has been given in advance. The event itself may
appear to the viewer or participant as unorganized, chaotic, as organized in a
way that lies outside the field of comprehension, or as a cluster of several
mutually unconnected structures. Once it is retold by means of language, it
inevitably receives structural unity. This unity physically belongs only to the
plane of expression, but it is inevitably transferred to the plane of content.
Thus the very fact of turning an event into a text raises its degree of
organization. More than that, the system of linguistic connections is
inevitably transferred onto the interpretation of connections in the real world.

Roman Jakobson made a subtle observation on this point. Discussing grammatical
universals and near-universals, he pointed out that the order of meaningful
elements, because of its plainly iconic character, has a particularly strong
tendency toward universality. This is why in conditional sentences the normal,
primary, neutral, unmarked order is the one in which the condition precedes the
consequence. Likewise, in a declarative sentence with a nominal subject and
object, the subject tends to come first. This grammatical arrangement reflects a
hierarchy of grammatical notions: the subject of the action designated by the
predicate is perceived as the point of departure, the producer of the action, in
contrast to its end point, the object of the action. Whatever the true rank of
the agent may be, that agent is necessarily advanced to the position of hero of
the message as soon as it assumes the role of subject. In the sentence "the
subordinate obeys the superior," contrary to the table of ranks, attention is
first concentrated on the subordinate as agent and only then passes to the
superior who is obeyed.

Jakobson is in fact speaking here about two different cases. In the first part
of his argument, polemically against Saussure, he emphasizes that linguistic
structures contain an element of iconicity. In grammar, as in extra-linguistic
reality, causes and conditions precede effects and results. In the second part
of the passage, however, the grammatical structure of the text predetermines
the distribution of roles in the extra-textual sphere. In the first case,
reality imposes its structure on language. In the second, language subordinates
reality to its own organization.

An utterance inevitably organizes material in temporal and causal coordinates,
since these belong to the structure of language. The compulsory character of
grammatical organization is felt most sharply within the phrase. At higher
levels of syntagmatic structure, however, the principles of narrative
organization come forward. Narrativity implies the connected character of the
text. Textual connectedness is formed by the repetition of certain structural
elements in successively arranged phrases.

In a study of connected text, E. V. Paducheva gives an example of the violation
of this rule: the speech of a character in Evgeny Schwartz's play *The Dragon*
who is simulating schizophrenia. The burgomaster says that Mueller's shop has
received a fresh batch of cheese; that a girl's best ornament is modesty and a
transparent little dress; that at sunset wild ducks flew over the cradle; and
that Mr. Lancelot is expected at a meeting of the city government. Paducheva
concludes that the description of restrictions on the combinability of units in
a text usually requires an analysis of the internal structure of those units.
Very often, the laws governing their combination can be reduced to the
necessity of repeating some component parts. Formal verse structure depends in
part on repeated similar-sounding syllables; agreement between noun and
adjective depends on identical values of gender, number, and case; the
combinability of phonemes is often reducible to the rule that neighboring
phonemes repeat the same value of some differential feature. The connectedness
of a paragraph is based to a considerable degree on the repetition of identical
semantic elements in neighboring phrases.

The semantic connectedness of narrative segments produces plot. Plot is the law
of the narrative text in the same sense that syntactic connection is the law of
the well-formed phrase. But if a story about reality requires a plot, or
plots, it does not follow that plots are immanently present in reality. The
events of real life fill a certain spatial-temporal continuum; narrative text
draws them out into a linear plot construction.

Thus the historian's need to rely on texts, and the text's need to recount
events according to the laws of linguistic, logical, rhetorical, and narrative
constructions, means that historical reality reaches the researcher in a
form already deformed. To this must be added ideological coding, the highest
hierarchical stage in the construction of a narrative text. This includes
genre, ideological-political, social, religious, philosophical, and other codes.

The desire to overcome these difficulties of historical science to some extent
conditioned the rise of the direction in French historiography that, over the
past half-century, has taken shape as the school of *nouvelle histoire*, the
history of the long duration.

The immediate impulse for this direction was the evident crisis of positivist
political history, which already in the second half of the nineteenth century
had reached a stage of compilation and theoretical poverty. The desire to free
history from the deeds of rulers and the biographies of great people generated
an interest in the life of masses and in anonymous processes. Jacques Le Goff,
listing predecessors of this view of history, recalls Voltaire, Chateaubriand,
Guizot, and Michelet. To this list we might add Leo Tolstoy, who persistently
repeated that genuine history takes place in private life and in mass
unconscious movements, and who never tired of ridiculing apologies for "great
people."

Yet the movement of *nouvelle histoire* had a more serious meaning than the
wish to distance itself from a scientific eclecticism that had already
discredited itself. One of its main impulses was the desire to escape the
enchanted circle described above. This led to a critique of the very concept of
the "historical fact" and to an effort to free history from "historical
personalities." The famous slogan of Lucien Febvre and Marc Bloch is connected
with this: a history of people, not of "Man." The demand to study impersonal,
collective historical impulses that determine the actions of masses unaware of
the forces acting upon them produced the innovative subject matter of this
school and led historians far beyond the usual routine topics of research. One
could recall here the works of Le Goff himself, Jean Delumeau, Michel Vovelle,
Philippe Aries, and, among Italian historians, Carlo Ginzburg.

No less noticeable was the effort to avoid the habitual plot structure of
historical narrative. The aspiration toward a history of the long duration, or,
as Fernand Braudel would put it more boldly, an almost immobile history, and as
Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie would put it still more decisively, an immobile
history, is essentially connected with this. This extreme did not find support.
Nevertheless, the desire to bring history closer to anthropology and to focus
attention on the slowest processes clearly reveals an effort to avoid the
danger of narrative history. In the final account, history becomes like a
geological process that acts on people, but not by means of people.

The direction of the history of the long duration, or of the "long breath," as
it is also called, brought fresh air into historical science and enriched it
with a number of studies that have already become classics.

And still not all of this school's principles can be accepted without
objection. History is not only a conscious process, but neither is it only an
unconscious process. It is the mutual tension between the two. The history of
the long duration developed under the sign of broad scientific synthesis. This
was demonstratively indicated by the title of the journal *Revue de synthese
historique* and by the title of Henri Berr's work of 1921, *L'histoire
traditionnelle et la synthese historique*. Marc Bloch also emphasized the same
point: in a society, whatever that society may be, everything is connected and
mutually dependent - political and social structure, economy, beliefs, and the
most elementary as well as the subtlest manifestations of mentality.

Yet it is striking that the synthesis, conducted largely on the basis of
economics and sociology, completely bypassed linguistics, although it was
precisely in linguistics that the most revolutionary transformations were
taking place at that time. After Jakobson's scholarly contacts with Claude
Levi-Strauss, anthropology and ethnology in Levi-Strauss's work came to rest on
linguistics, which became one of the most significant events in the science of
the ending century. The French historical school, meanwhile, almost did not
notice it. At the same time, French structuralism developed under the sign of
synchronous analysis and did not invade the "foreign" territory of historians.

The parallel with the history of language seems quite appropriate here. The
history of language, at least the languages of written cultures, develops in
the tension between two poles: living oral speech and the written book
tradition. The historian of Russian language B. A. Uspensky writes that the
literary language tends toward stability, while living speech tends toward
change. From this arises an unavoidable distance between literary language and
living speech, forming a constant tension between these poles, something like a
field of force. The degree of rupture between literary language and living
speech is determined by the type of literary language. One can say that the
difference in the character of the evolution of the system - the living
language - and the norm - the book language - comes down to the difference
between continuous and discrete development.

Uspensky further explains that, unlike the evolution of the system, the
evolution of the norm, including the book norm, that is, the norm of literary
language, has not a continuous but a discrete, stepwise character. Thus, if the
history of language can be understood as an objective process in principle
independent of speakers' attitude toward language, the development of literary
language is directly dependent on the changing orientation of the language
bearer.

The history of language is a typical mass and anonymous object, a process of
the long duration. But the history of literary language is a history of
creativity, a process connected with individual activity and with a heightened
degree of unpredictability. If "political history" neglected one side of the
two-in-one historical process, "new history" does the same thing with the
other. Like the history of language, any dynamic process that occurs with human
participation oscillates between two poles. One pole is the pole of continuous
slow changes, characteristic of processes on which human consciousness and will
have no influence, and which are often not even visible to contemporaries
because their periodicity exceeds the span of a generation. The other pole is
conscious human activity, carried out through personal acts of will and
intellect.

It is impossible to tear one side away from the other, just as it is impossible
to tear north from south. Their opposition is a condition of their existence.
More than that, these are tendencies realized at every level. In Byron's
brilliant individuality one can distinguish blocks of anonymous mass processes,
and in the work and personality of any figure of the "mass culture" of European
Byronism in the early nineteenth century one can find elements of creative
unrepeatability. Everything done by people and with the participation of people
cannot fail, in one measure or another, to belong to the anonymous processes of
history, and cannot fail, in one measure or another, to belong to the personal
principle. This is determined by the very essence of the relation between the
human person and culture: the person is at once isomorphic with the universe of
culture and necessarily only a part of it. More than that, in which of these
two possible aspects a given element of history appears depends not on its
immanent essence, but on the position of the historian describing it. Is
Beau Brummell a fact of mass culture, where we would readily place him by
following our habitual contempt for phenomena such as fashion, or is he an
individuality that left its stamp on the anonymous "slow" history of its time?
The simple transfer of a fact from one line of connections to another can make
the traditional appear individual.

The different degree to which conscious human efforts participate in different
levels of a single historical process also touches the different assessment of
the role of chance, on the one hand, and of the creative capacities of the
individual, on the other. The task of "freeing history from great people" may
turn into a history without creativity and a history without thoughts and
freedom: freedom of thought, freedom of will, the possibility of choosing
paths. Along this road, complete antipodes in every other respect, Hegel and
the historians of the "new school" unexpectedly converge.

Hegel's philosophy of history is built on the idea of movement toward freedom
as the goal of the historical process. But the primordial predetermination of
the goal already removes the question of freedom, and this follows clearly from
Hegel's own reasoning. It is not by chance that Hegel was convinced that "the
world of reason and self-conscious will is not given over to chance." There is
a direct connection between the denial of the role of chance in history and
Hegel's idea that the world of reason, realizing itself, represents the final
act of world history, which, having attained self-knowledge, completes its
movement. Spirit, for Hegel, is not something that gives itself over to the
external play of accidents. On the contrary, it is an absolutely determining
principle, not subject to accidents, but using them for its own purposes and
holding them in its power.

This movement of thought logically ends in a picture of the unconditional and
predetermined merging of the particular in the general. Thus the freedom of the
individual is not the choice of an alternative path, but the free merging of
oneself with the necessity of world development.

For Hegel, spirit realizes itself through great figures. For "new history," the
anonymous forces that dominate world development realize themselves through
unconscious mass manifestations. In both cases, historical action is action
deprived of choice.

Is this so?

Leaving aside the question of how the intellectual capacities and moral
responsibility of the human person look from this point of view, let us look at
the matter from another side. Uncertainty is a measure of information. Minimal
information is contained in the choice of one of two equally probable
possibilities. As the reserve of uncertainty is exhausted, the informativeness
of the process declines, falling to zero at the moment when the process becomes
completely redundant, that is, completely predictable.

Imagine a stone flying along a definite trajectory. If we could take into
account all the factors acting on it before it was thrown, and if we could
fully exclude the possibility that new factors would enter during its flight,
we could point to the place where it would fall before the flight began. Thus,
for the purpose of determining at what moment and at what point of its
trajectory the stone would be located, the actual throw would be completely
redundant. But let us change the conditions. Suppose that we cannot take into
account all the factors that will appear as the stone moves along its
trajectory. Then, with each moment, the accuracy of our prediction will grow,
and at the same time the redundancy of the further text will grow as well, if
we consider the curve of the trajectory drawn on a screen by a tracking device
as a text. Redundancy is inversely proportional to information. Information
will fall in proportion to the distance the stone has flown and to the
dwindling number of possible alternatives remaining for its further movement.
Of course, if the stone had the capacity to choose its path at every moment of
movement, the potential informativeness of its flight would remain.

The documentary history of humanity has already lasted thousands of years. If
the historical process had no mechanisms of unpredictability, if it did not
include accidents not as embroidery coloring the surface of a canvas of
constantly acting factors, as Lucien Febvre said in his review of Braudel, but
as an important functioning mechanism, history would long ago have become
redundant, and we would be able to predict its course in advance. As is well
known, this does not happen. Even where the role of an individual person is
negligible and the preceding state of the system is well enough known - for
example, in the history of the economy over the past three centuries, in the
history of separate languages, in the history of technical inventions, or in
the history of everyday customs - prediction is marked not by success but by
brilliant defeats. This is all the more true of the history of creative human
activity, which the *histoire nouvelle* lost together with the cult of great
people.

The reader should not misunderstand me. The works of the *histoire nouvelle*
school are perceived in contemporary historiography as a fresh wind. Reading
Braudel, Le Goff, Delumeau, Vovelle, and others gives the specialist not only
professional but aesthetic pleasure, because the beauty of clear thought is
also beauty. The critique of certain methodological aspects of these studies is
meant only to indicate the necessity of further movement, not to cross out the
path already traveled.

Behind the methodology of this school there can be seen an age-old scientific
psychology built on the conviction that where determinacy ends, science ends.
From the notorious "demon of Laplace" to Einstein's assertion that "God does
not play dice," there stretches the desire to remove chance from the world, or
at least to place it beyond the limits of science. In a special section on the
nature of chance in *Science and Method*, Henri Poincare wrote that we have
become absolute determinists, and even those inclined to preserve free will for
human beings recognize the unlimited rule of determinism in the inorganic
world. Every phenomenon, however insignificant, has its cause, and an infinitely
powerful mind, boundlessly informed about the laws of nature, could foresee it
from the beginning of the ages. One could not gamble with such a mind without
losing one's entire fortune. Although Poincare later notes the real
impossibility of taking numerous factors into account except probabilistically,
the spirit of Laplace's demon does not let him go: if we knew precisely the
laws of nature and the state of the universe at the initial moment, we could
predict the state of the universe at any subsequent moment.

With respect to historical science, special circumstances operate here that
make it necessary for the historian to choose between an unscientific
substitution of biographies of "great people" for history and rigid
determinism. We have already seen how extra-textual reality is deformed when it
is turned into a text, the source for the historian. We have also seen the
paths by which historians try to escape this danger. But there is another
source of deformation of reality, this time not under the hand of the creator
of the source-document, but as the result of the actions of its interpreter,
the historian.

History develops along the vector, or arrow, of time. Its direction is defined
by movement from the past into the present. The historian, however, looks at
the texts being studied from the present into the past. For most writers who
have considered the epistemological side of historical science, the identity of
the prospective and retrospective views has seemed such a self-evident truth
that the question was not even made an object of consideration. It seemed that
the essence of a chain of events did not change whether we looked at them in
the direction of the arrow of time or from the opposite point of view. More
than that, for those who see history as movement toward a definite goal, this
view seems natural. For their opponents, such as the "new history" in its
critique of Arnold Toynbee, it seems a methodically convenient device.

Marc Bloch, by symmetrically titling two chapters of his final book
"Understanding the Present by the Past" and "Understanding the Past by the
Present," seemed to underline the symmetry of time's direction for the
historian. More than that, he believed that the retrospective view allows the
historian to distinguish essential connections from accidental ones. Sometimes,
he wrote, it is useful for historians to begin reading history backwards. In
comparing the past reconstructed by the historian with a film, Bloch uses the
image of a film in which only the last frame remains intact. To reconstruct the
erased features of the other frames, one must first unwind the film in the
direction opposite to that in which it was shot.

One could immediately observe that in such a film all frames except the first
would turn out to be completely predictable, and therefore entirely
superfluous. But that is not even the main point. More important is that the
very essence of the historical process is distorted. History is an asymmetric,
irreversible process. If we use Bloch's image, it is such a strange film that,
when run backwards, it will not lead us to the initial frame.

Here lies the root of the disagreement. For Bloch, and this is a natural
consequence of the retrospective view, the historian must consider the events
of the past as the only possible events. To assess the probability of an event
is to establish how many chances it has of occurring. But are we entitled to
speak of the possibility of a fact in the past? In the absolute sense, Bloch
says, obviously not. One can only guess about the future. The past is a given
in which there is no longer any place for the possible.

There is firm consistency in these statements. The retrospective view
inevitably leads to the conclusion that what really happened was not only the
most probable but the only possible thing. If, however, we proceed from the
idea that a historical event is always the result of the realization of one
alternative among others, and that in history the same conditions do not yet
mean univocal consequences, then other methods of approaching the material will
be required. A different habit of historical approach will be required as well:
the realized paths will appear surrounded by bundles of unrealized
possibilities.

Imagine a film showing the life of a person from birth to old age. Watching it
retrospectively, we would say that this person always had only one possibility
and, by iron regularity, ended as he had to end. The error of such a view would
become obvious if the frames were watched prospectively. Then the film would
become a story of missed possibilities, and a deep disclosure of the essence of
the life would require a number of parallel alternative shots. It is possible
that in one variant the hero dies at sixteen on a barricade, while in another,
at sixty, he writes denunciations of his neighbors to the organs of state
security.

We have already noted that history is an irreversible, nonequilibrium process.

For considering the essence of such processes and understanding what they mean
when applied to history, it is exceptionally important to take into account the
analysis of these phenomena carried out in the work of Ilya Prigogine, who
studied dynamic processes at the chemical, physical, and biological levels. His
work has a deep and revolutionary meaning for scientific thinking as a whole:
it introduces chance phenomena into the sphere of scientific interest and, more
than that, reveals their functional place in the general dynamics of the world.

In considering irreversible processes, Prigogine distinguished different forms
of dynamics. Distinguishing between equilibrium and nonequilibrium structures,
he points out that in spaces close to us, dynamic processes take place in
different ways. The laws of equilibrium possess high generality; they are
universal. The behavior of matter near equilibrium, by contrast, is marked by
repetition. Dynamic processes occurring under equilibrium conditions follow
determined curves. But as one moves away from the entropic points of
equilibrium, movement approaches those critical points at which the predictable
course of processes is disrupted. Prigogine calls them bifurcation points. At
these points the process reaches a moment when univocal prediction of the
future becomes impossible. Further development occurs as the realization of one
of several equally probable alternatives.

What is especially noteworthy for us is the following. At the beginning of
their book, Prigogine and Isabelle Stengers recall the words of Isaiah Berlin,
who saw the difference between the natural sciences and humanistic knowledge in
the antithesis between interest in the repeatable and interest in the unique.
But they then draw attention to the fact that in the transition from
equilibrium conditions to strongly nonequilibrium conditions, we pass from the
repeatable and general to the unique and specific.

In strongly nonequilibrium situations, processes unfold not as a uniform,
determined flow but amid fluctuations. At the moment of bifurcation the system
is in a state in which it is impossible to predict into which next state it
will pass. One can only indicate the set of states into which transition is
possible. At this moment chance may play the decisive role, if by chance we
mean not causelessness, but a phenomenon belonging to another causal series.
Here it is useful to recall W. Ross Ashby's definition. When he says that a
factor is random, he does not mean what the factor is in itself, but what
relation it has to the main system. The successive digits in the decimal
expansion of pi are as determined as digits can be. But a sequence of a
thousand of them may serve as random numbers for agricultural experiments, not
because the digits are random, but because they are probably not connected with
the features of the given set of plots of land. Thus supplementing a choice by
"chance" means supplementing it by the action, or variety, of another system
whose behavior is not connected with the behavior of the main system.

Prigogine and Stengers describe a trajectory of movement through a space in
which equilibrium and nonequilibrium relations are unevenly distributed. As one
moves away from equilibrium, the amplification of a fluctuation that occurs at
the "right moment" leads to the preferential choice of one reaction path from a
series that are a priori equally possible. In strongly nonequilibrium
conditions, processes of self-organization correspond to a subtle interaction
between chance and necessity, fluctuations and deterministic laws. Near
bifurcations, the main role is played by fluctuations and chance elements; in
the intervals between bifurcations, deterministic aspects dominate.

Thus the accidental and the lawful cease to be incompatible concepts. They
appear as two possible states of one and the same object. Moving in a
determined field, it appears as a point in linear development. Entering a
fluctuational space, it appears as a continuum of potential possibilities, with
chance serving as the trigger.

Shedding light on the general theory of dynamic processes, Prigogine's ideas
seem very fruitful when applied to historical movement. They are easily
explicated in connection with the facts of world history and its complex
interweaving of spontaneous, unconscious movements and personally conscious
ones. The alternation of epochs in which determined processes dominate with
periods in which the role of bifurcation points increases explains the
phenomenon, noted above, of maintaining redundancy at a relatively constant
level.

At the same time, it would be an inadmissible simplification to imagine
anonymous processes as the complete rule of determination and individual
activity as the kingdom of chance. First, there are no completely anonymous
elements in history, and no elements determined one hundred percent by personal
efforts. In each of them both principles are easily distinguished. The question
is only their proportion. Second, both tendencies have their periods of calm,
predictable flow and their periods of sharp explosions, when determination and
univocal predictability recede into the background.

What is more important is precisely what constitutes the specificity of
historical development: development whose elements are thinking and willing
units. Leo Szilard as early as 1929 published a work with the declarative title
"On the Decrease of Entropy in a Thermodynamic System by the Intervention of
Intelligent Beings." History is a process that takes place "with the
intervention of a thinking being." This means that at bifurcation points not
only the mechanism of chance enters into action, but also the mechanism of
conscious choice, which becomes a most important objective element of the
historical process.

Understanding this presents the necessity of historical semiotics in a new
light: the analysis of how the human unit that must make a choice represents
the world to itself. In a certain sense this is close to what "new history"
calls mentality. Yet the results of research in this field, and the comparison
of what has been achieved by V. N. Toporov, B. A. Uspensky, Vyacheslav Vsevolod
Ivanov, A. A. Zaliznyak, A. M. Pyatigorsky, and many others in reconstructing
various ethnocultural types of consciousness, convince us that historical
semiotics of culture is the most promising path in this direction.

When the historical process is considered in the direction of the arrow of
time, bifurcation points turn out to be historical moments when the tension of
contradictory structural poles reaches its highest point and the whole system
leaves a state of equilibrium. At these moments the behavior of both individual
people and masses ceases to be automatically predictable. Determination recedes
into the background. Historical movement at such moments should be thought not
as a trajectory, but as a continuum potentially capable of splitting into a
number of variants. These nodes of lowered predictability are moments of
revolution or sharp historical shifts. The choice of the path that is actually
realized depends on a complex of accidental circumstances, but in still greater
measure on the self-consciousness of the actants. It is not by chance that in
such moments word, speech, and propaganda acquire especially important
historical significance.

If, before the choice was made, a situation of uncertainty existed, then after
its realization a fundamentally new situation takes shape. For this new
situation the choice is already necessary, and for further movement it appears
as a given. Accidental before realization, the choice becomes determined after
it. Retrospectivity intensifies determinacy. For further movement, the choice
is the first link of a new regularity.

Prigogine notes that at moments of bifurcation the process acquires an
individual character, approaching the characteristics of the humanities. One
could distinguish a gradation in the intensity of unpredictability: the
intervention of chance, the intervention of a thinking being, the intervention
of creative consciousness. If at one pole of development there stands rigid,
univocal determination, then the other pole is structurally represented by
creative, artistic activity. Neither pole can appear in pure form in a real
process. Yet the fact that a work of art before its creation appears
unpredictable, and after its creation appears as the determined beginning of a
lawful tradition, demonstrates the isomorphism of processes at different
levels.

Let us consider the behavior of an individual person. As a rule, it is realized
according to certain stereotypes that determine the "normal," predictable
course of actions. Yet the number of stereotypes and their set in a given
society is significantly broader than what an individual person realizes. Some
available possibilities are rejected in principle, others turn out to be less
preferred, and still others are regarded as permissible variants. At the moment
when historical, social, and psychological tension reaches the high point at
which a person's picture of the world shifts sharply, usually under conditions
of high emotional tension, the person may abruptly change stereotype, as if
jumping onto another orbit of behavior, one completely unpredictable for that
person under "normal" conditions.

Of course, if we consider the behavior of a crowd at such a moment, we discover
a certain repetition in how many units of people changed their behavior by
choosing an "orbit" that would have been completely unpredictable for them under
other conditions. What is predictable in average numbers for the "crowd" is
unpredictable for the individual person. Kant already observed that marriages,
the births conditioned by them, and deaths, on which human free will seems to
have so much influence, appear to follow no rule by which one could determine
their number mathematically in advance. Yet annual data from large countries
show that these events too occur according to constant laws of nature.

From Kant's observation one might conclude that individual phenomena are
characterized by reduced predictability and differ in this from mass
phenomena. Such an assumption, however, would seem premature. Empirically, the
historian knows how often the behavior of a crowd turns out to be less
predictable than the behavior of an individual person. Theoretically, the very
concept of "individuality," as we have already had occasion to see, is not
primary and self-evident, but depends on the method of coding. Far more
interesting is Prigogine and Stengers's consideration that near bifurcation
points a system tends to shift into a regime of individual behavior. Applied to
Kant's example, one could say that an individual marriage usually takes place
in a setting of emotional tension, as the result of choosing from an
alternative, a free act of will. For a collectivity of the type "population,"
meanwhile, this action realizes a norm. The closer to the norm, the more
predictable the behavior of the system.

There is still another side to this question. Where the next link of events
can be predicted, one can assert that there has been no act of choice from
among equipossible alternatives. But consciousness is always choice. Thus, by
excluding choice, by excluding unpredictability as perceived by an external
observer as chance, we exclude consciousness from the historical process.
Historical regularities differ from all others precisely because they cannot be
understood if the conscious activity of people, including semiotic activity, is
excluded.

Creative thinking is especially revealing in this respect. As we have already
said, creative consciousness is the act by which a text emerges that cannot be
predicted by automatic algorithms. Now one could add that certain aspects
nevertheless always remain predictable - tradition - and if one considers only
these, the process will appear continuous and smooth. Other aspects are
predictable with a certain degree of probability, and still others are
completely unexpected. The degree of their unexpectedness, however, will differ
depending on whether we read the sequence of texts in the direction of time or
against it.

An unexpected text is not an impossible text, but only a least probable, or
sufficiently improbable, one. Yet the low probability of the emergence of, for
example, "Byronic romanticism" without Byron defines the situation only before
Byron's appearance. More than that, in the sphere of culture, the more
unexpected a phenomenon is, the stronger its influence on the cultural
situation after it has been realized. The occurrence of an unexpected event,
the appearance of an unpredictable text, sharply changes the situation for what
follows. The improbable text becomes reality, and subsequent development
proceeds from it as from a fact. Unexpectedness is erased, the originality of
genius turns into the routine of imitators, and Byron is followed by Byronists,
Brummell by the dandies of all Europe.

In retrospective reading, the dramatic discreteness of this process is removed,
and Byron appears as the "first Byronist," the follower of his followers, or,
as this is usually called in studies of cultural history, a "predecessor."

Friedrich Schlegel wrote in his *Fragments*: "The historian is a prophet turned
toward the past." This witty remark gives us occasion to note the difference
between the position of the fortune-teller predicting the future and the
historian "predicting" the past. No fortune-teller or prophet names the future
univocally as something inevitable and the only possible thing. A prediction is
either built on a two-stage conditional principle, such as "if you do this,
then that will happen," or it is deliberately formulated in an indefinite way
that requires further interpretation. In any case, prediction always preserves
a reserve of uncertainty, an inexhaustion of the choice among certain
alternatives. Hence the constant motif in folklore and archaic texts of the
misunderstood prediction.

Thucydides, for example, reports the case of Cylon. Having asked the oracle at
Delphi, Cylon received a prophecy that on the greatest festival of Zeus he
should seize the Athenian Acropolis. During the games at the Peloponnesian
Olympia, Cylon, together with armed men sent by Theagenes and with his own
supporters, seized the Acropolis, intending to become tyrant. He thought that
this was the "greatest festival of Zeus" of which the oracle had spoken, and
that as an Olympic victor he had special reason to interpret the oracle in that
way. Whether the prophecy meant the greatest festival in Attica or somewhere
else, Cylon did not consider, and the oracle did not explain. As a result, his
plan brought him death.

In Rome, a special college of decemvirs was chosen to interpret the prophecies
of the Sibylline books. Cicero, in *On Divination*, remarks that the
interpreters of all these oracles occupy, in relation to those whose
prophecies they interpret, the position grammarians occupy in relation to
poets. Summing up, Cicero writes that Chrysippus filled a whole scroll with
oracles, some false, some fulfilled by chance, some so indefinite and obscure
that their interpreter himself needs an interpreter. To understand the sense of
an oracle, one has to resort to other oracles. Finally, some are so ambiguous
that only dialecticians can sort them out. Thus Croesus received the oracle
that if he crossed the Halys he would destroy a great kingdom. He thought he
was destined to destroy the enemy's kingdom, but he destroyed his own. Cicero
also gives the oracle to Pyrrhus, deliberately formulated so that it can mean
both "I say, Aeacides, you can defeat Rome" and "I say to you, Aeacides, Rome
can defeat you."

The historian who "predicts backward" differs from the fortune-teller by
removing uncertainty. What did not happen *de facto*, for such a historian,
could not have happened. The historical process loses its uncertainty, that is,
it ceases to be informative.

Uncertainty implies an interpreter. Here one parallel comes to mind. Unlike the
scientific text, the artistic text also contains a high degree of uncertainty
and needs an interpreter: a critic, a literary scholar, a connoisseur. The
scientific text, meanwhile, does not need such a mediator. The difference, it
seems, is that the scientific text looks at the world as already constructed,
already made, and therefore looks at it to a certain degree retrospectively,
though of course less so than the historian. The artistic text is an act of
creating a world, a world into which mechanisms of unpredictable
self-development have been built.

Another series of phenomena is connected with this. The very concept of
unpredictability is relative and depends on the greater or lesser probability
of a given event. The nearer one comes to bifurcation points, the more
individual the curve of events becomes. At the same historical moment,
different types of texts possess different degrees of predictability and
unpredictability. Thus in science one observes the simultaneous appearance of
even the most unexpected ideas: Newton and Leibniz worked simultaneously on
integral and differential calculus; Darwin and Wallace independently arrived at
the idea of evolution; Poincare outlined the ideas of relativity theory at the
same time as Einstein; Lobachevsky, Bolyai, Gauss, Schweikart, and Taurinus
independently and simultaneously approached the problem of non-Euclidean
geometry. In the history of the arts such phenomena are not observed.

From this follows another feature. If we suppose that some major inventor died
before managing to make a discovery, we can have no doubt that sooner or later,
and usually within a visibly close time frame, the discovery would be made. In
this sense, the unexpected death of a given scientist does not introduce a
fundamental change into the course of the historical process. But if Dante or
Dostoevsky had died in childhood, without writing their works, those works
would never have been written. This would have meant a change not only in the
path of literature but in the general history of humanity, even though the
degree of genius of Dante and Einstein may be comparable. The fundamental
difference is that the thought of the scientist is separable from the
individual text and is therefore translatable. The thought of the artist is the
text. And a text is created only once.

Thus we can conclude that the necessity of relying on texts places the
historian before an unavoidable double distortion. On the one hand, the
syntagmatic, linear directedness of the text transforms the event by turning it
into a narrative structure. On the other hand, the historian's gaze moves in
the opposite direction. To understand the specificity of the historian's
position, let us imagine the following thought experiment. In one of his
reflections, Flammarion invented a little man, whom he called Lumen, who would
move away from the Earth at a speed exceeding the speed of light. For such an
observer, time would move in the reverse direction; as Poincare noted, history
would flow backward and Waterloo would precede Austerlitz.

Now imagine that Flammarion's Lumen then settled on Earth, enrolled in a
history faculty, submitted to earthly time and to the linear laws of human
speech, and faced the task of writing a historical work on the basis of his
impressions. Historical events in his exposition would undergo two
transformations. The first would correspond to his experience of the reverse
flow of time. The second would be a secondary turn that returned time to its
"normal" earthly succession. Under these conditions, our Lumen would become a
strict determinist.

One more type of deformation imposed by narrative structure on historical text
must still be mentioned. Narrative, especially in its most ancient form, myth,
inevitably imposes on extra-textual reality powerful boundaries that cut
through its continuity: beginning and end. Every text, by definition, has
boundaries. But not all boundaries possess the same modeling weight. One can
distinguish cultures and texts oriented toward the beginning, to which they
give special semiotic significance, from cultures and texts oriented toward the
end. Myths of origin and eschatological myths immediately come to mind. But the
meaning of these orientations is much broader.

It is not only archaic myths "about origins" that see in beginnings the basis
for modeling culture. Let us take examples from medieval texts.

In *The Tale of Igor's Campaign* there is a passage about the companions of
Yaroslav of Chernigov. In the same measure that the mysterious names in this
passage have seemed puzzling and have generated extensive commentary, the
expression "ringing in the ancestral glory" has seemed transparent and not in
need of special explanation. Yet it remains unclear why the deeds of Yaroslav's
companions make the glory of ancestors ring, not their own glory. Why do the
great-grandsons perform the deeds, while what rings is not their new glory but
the old glory of the great-grandfathers, who in their time performed other
historical deeds?

For understanding this place in the text, D. S. Likhachev's deep observation
on the nature of the sense of time in Old Russian culture is exceptionally
important. The past was somewhere ahead, at the beginning of events, whose
series was not correlated with the perceiving subject. "Back" events were
events of the present or future. What was "behind" was the inheritance left by
the dead, the last thing connecting the dead with us. "Forward glory" was the
glory of the remote past, of the first times; "back glory" was the glory of
the latest deeds.

Likhachev pointed to an extraordinarily important aspect of the question. The
linear time of modern cultural consciousness is inseparably connected with the
idea that each event is replaced by the next, while what has gone into the past
ceases to exist. The feature of reality is attributed only to the present time.
The past exists as memory and cause, the present as reality, the future as
effect. Connected with this is the conviction that if only the present
possesses genuine reality, its meaning is disclosed only in the future. Hence
the striving to arrange events into a single moving chain and organize it by
causal relations.

Old Russian consciousness proceeded from other conceptions. The "first" events
lying at the basis of the world order do not pass into the ghostly existence
of memories. They exist eternally in their own reality. Each new event in such
a series is not something separate from its "first" prototype. It is only a
renewal and growth of that eternal, pillar-like event. Every murder of brother
by brother does not constitute some new and separate deed; it is only a renewal
of Cain's sin, which in itself is eternal.

For this reason new and unheard-of crimes are especially grave. From the moment
of their realization they receive being, and each new repetition of them falls
not so much on the sinner who immediately committed them as on the soul of the
first criminal. In the same way, great and glorious deeds only revive the
eternally existing and uniquely real "first glory." They ring it like a bell,
which has real being even when it is silent, while its ringing is not being
but testimony to being. Just as in the cyclical time of myth, events
continuously repeat the primordial order of the eternal cycle and, at the same
time, magical ritual intervention is required in order for each event provided
for in the order to be realized, so heroic deeds of great-grandsons are
necessary in order for the eternal bell of ancestral glory to ring.

Such a type of consciousness turns thought not toward the end, the result, but
toward the beginning, the source.

It will be easier for modern consciousness to imagine such a structure of
thought if we illustrate it with historically closer examples. Gogol felt with
unusual sharpness, both with the mind of a researcher and with the intuition of
an artist, the very essence of the archaic worldview. In "A Terrible
Vengeance," the system of categories that interests us here is reflected with
extraordinary vividness. The villain Petro, having murdered his sworn brother,
commits an unheard-of crime that joins the sins of Cain and Judas and becomes
the initiator of a new and unprecedented evil. His crime does not pass into the
past. Generating a chain of new crimes, it continues to exist in the present
and to grow continuously. This conception is expressed in the image of a corpse
that grows underground with each new crime.

Like a text, which on the page of a book has an atemporal existence and in the
process of reading is realized as already read, being read, or still to be
read, the text of world history for medieval consciousness exists in its
deepest being outside time. Only by being renewed in the deeds of people who
realize it does it acquire temporal sequence. This metaphor had a profound
effect on medieval historiosophical thought. It must also be taken into account
that the very concept of reading was different. It implied not only repeated
and constant return to certain texts, but also the ordered character of those
returns. Analogously, human deeds could be perceived as repetitions of deeds
that had already occurred, or rather as repeated realizations of their deep
prototypes.

Thus two sides are active in such a conception. On the one hand, there is the
initiator. It is the initiator who creates the permanent constructive features
of the world. Having been created once, these "pillar" constructions already
exist outside time, not entering the history of people but located in deeper
layers of being. Hence the attention, typical of Old Russian writing, to the
questions "who began it?" and "where did it come from?" The *Primary
Chronicle*, from its very first line - where the Russian land came from, who
first began to rule in Kiev, and how the Russian land came to be - is permeated
by the idea of beginnings.

The one who performs the "first" deed bears responsibility before God, because
that deed no longer disappears but exists eternally, renewed in later deeds. On
the other hand, there must be a "renewer," the one who by his deeds, good or
evil, seems to wipe the dust from the ancient deeds of the initiator. The role
of such a renewer is exceptionally great. Alongside this figure another type of
person is possible: the one who, in one way or another, weakens the role of the
first deeds, whether bad or good. It is precisely stories about these acts and
actions that interest the medieval narrator first of all, whereas the account
of "first deeds" is typical of the mytho-epic stage. The narrative texts that
arise in this way differ sharply from historical stories by being turned not
toward the results of an action but toward its sources. A highly revealing sign
of a text's orientation here will be the comparative structural weight, the
markedness, of the categories of beginning and end.

Historical narrative and the structure of the novelistic text connected with
it are subordinated to temporal and causal sequence and oriented toward the end
of the text. The main structural meaning of the narration is concentrated
there. The question "How did it end?" characterizes our perception of a
historical event and of a novelistic text in equal measure. Mythological texts
that narrate the act of creation and legendary initiators are oriented toward
the beginning. This is expressed not only in the fact that their main question
is "Where did it come from?" but also in the special markedness of the
beginning of the text and the clearly subordinate role of its end. Medieval
secular narrative texts, including gestes, deeds, and to a certain extent
chronicles, represent an intermediate type. The hero of the narration is a
"historical person," but the meaning of the events is turned toward the source.

An interesting example of a peculiar compromise between these principles is
*The Tale of Woe-Misfortune*, in which the markedness of the category of end is
clearly expressed: the young man's withdrawal into the monastery completes and
forever stops the course of events. Yet the narration is still turned toward
the beginning. It tells of the creation of Adam and Eve and of their original
sin, and the whole plot of the tale is only a "renewal" of the initial sin of
the human being.

*The Tale of Igor's Campaign* is a typical deed. Although the acting persons
belong to the "historical" plane, the entire interpretation of the meaning of
events is turned toward origins. Hence the basic conception of the *Tale*: the
campaign of Igor is a "renewal" of the campaigns of Oleg Gorislavich, who is
interpreted as the initiator of civil strife in Rus. At the same time, the
"present" times of political feuds are perceived by the author of the *Tale* in
relation to the ideal times of the "first princes." The beginning of the text
is characteristically far more marked than its muted end.

The concept of glory in *The Tale of Igor's Campaign* is especially revealing
in this sense. Glory is thought in the *Tale* as something preexisting since
the time of the ancestor-initiators. Glory is always "grandfatherly." It can be
made to fade if it is not renewed by new heroic deeds, but it does not
disappear, continuing its being in a certain atemporal world. The text has many
expressions for unworthy treatment of this ancestral glory: jumping out of
grandfatherly glory, trampling ancient glory, tearing apart Yaroslav's glory.
But the renewal of glory is also possible: ringing in great-grandfatherly
glory.

For the author of the *Tale*, the behavior of Igor and Vsevolod is marked by
the excessiveness of their claims. This gives their deeds, as vassals of the
Grand Prince of Kiev, a negative coloring, and gives them, as knightly heroes,
a peculiar beauty and value. They are not content with honor. They decide to
appropriate glory for themselves. They want, by accomplishing something unheard
of, to create a standard of glory that would eclipse everything before them and
force all descendants to ring in their glory.

Not only glory but other foundations of the world order can sleep, remaining as
if in numbness until the time of renewal. The "lie" awakened in the *Tale* is
something more primordial than the specific Polovtsian khans known by name in
Rus. It is Evil itself, which after Sviatoslav's successful campaigns had
remained numb and which was revived by the action of Igor and Vsevolod.

The union in medieval tales and deeds of belief in the pre-established
structures of the world with the idea of the activity of the individual person
as one who rings the glory of the grandfathers or awakens ancient Falsehood
created the equilibrium between pre-established order and personal freedom and
responsibility that is characteristic of Russian medieval literature of the
pre-Moscow period.

At the same time, certain types of culture are oriented toward eschatology. One
could point here to Aztec mythology, the Ragnarok of Scandinavian mythology,
the apocalyptic beliefs of a number of medieval religious movements, Wagner's
*Twilight of the Gods* and the subsequent eschatology of modernism at the turn
of the twentieth century, as well as the true boom of eschatological moods
after the invention and use of the atomic bomb and the threat of ecological
catastrophe.

In the present connection, however, it is more interesting to attend not to the
content of eschatological ideas and myths, but to the modeling significance of
the end in certain types of texts. It is well known that the beginning of a
text, its "opening," to use the term accepted for folklore, plays the role of a
semiotic indicator. By it the audience determines in which semiotic key the
following material should be perceived. This can be compared with the mechanism
of an organ. An organ has several keyboards or registers. Before a particular
piece is played, a particular register has to be switched on. If the whole set
of codes of a given culture is imagined as a hierarchy of registers, then the
opening of a text will play the role of a switch that activates the particular
register in which the rest of the text should be perceived. Connected with this
is the distinctive effect of a mismatch between the register switched on and
the subsequent text, widely used in contemporary cinema to create "false
expectation," and also in parodies, mock-heroic travesty poems of the
eighteenth century, and so on. When Chernyshevsky, sitting in a cell of the
Peter and Paul Fortress and at the same time on hunger strike, wrote his
social-utopian novel *What Is To Be Done?*, he deceived the prison guards and
the censorship by giving the work the opening of a detective story.

Still more important are the demands that narrative structure makes on the end
of a text. It is well known that the "happy ending" and the "tragic ending" are
among the most important characteristics of a novel or film for the reader.
Marriage, or the hero's achievement of the goal, leaves the audience with an
impression of prosperous completion, while the death of the hero leaves a
tragic one, even if the action of the novel takes place before the birth of
Christ and in real time the characters have long since died anyway. The point
is that a discrete, singled-out text always has its own time, which begins with
the opening and ends with the final word of the text. If the hero is happy and
young in the last phrase of the text, then for the reader that hero is already
forever happy and young. More than that, the text itself becomes a space in
which people are happy and young. This is the narcotic action of the happy
endings of modern Cinderella stories and other genres of mass literature. The
inevitable property of every verbal text, its division into discrete units,
acquires in plot text, through the highlighting of the category of beginning
and end, the property of being a finite model of an infinite world.

When this property of narrative is transferred onto historical material, it has
the deepest disturbing effect, and this effect has a double character. At the
level of the source-text, it is expressed in the fact that the chronicler, the
memoirist, or some other creator of a source-document subordinates the text to
a plot-narrative structure, supplying each "novella" with an end of moralistic,
philosophical, or literary-artistic resonance. When the director Fred Zinnemann
ends the film *A Man for All Seasons* not with the final shot, the execution of
Thomas More, but with the voice of a narrator announcing that Henry VIII and
Cardinal Wolsey both received retribution from fate, we receive this news with
a certain relief, confirming for ourselves that justice is built into the
course of history. The same feeling guides the creators of historical sources
about Thomas More's life. The influence of literary tradition on Thomas
Stapleton, More's biographer, is clear from the very conception: to include the
biography of More in a trilogy about three holy Thomases - Thomas the Apostle,
Thomas Becket, and Thomas More. The anonymous author who hid behind the
pseudonym "Ro. Ba." builds his biography of More according to literary and
hagiographic canons as well. In general, it can be exceptionally difficult to
distinguish a historical source, even one having the character of a legal act,
from a literary text.

The other aspect is more essential: the influence of narrative models on the
consciousness of the historian as scholar. The historian's striving to build
periodizations is connected with the need to retell the continuous material of
reality in the discrete metalanguage of science. In this respect the historian
does not yet differ from any other scholar. What is more specific is when the
historian begins to regard the classification as an internal property of the
extra-textual material and transfers the eschatological experience of
"beginnings" and "ends" from the sphere of descriptions into the sphere of what
is described.

For example, the eschatology of the "end" of the century, the ideas of
Wagner's *Twilight of the Gods*, Nietzsche, and their followers, determined the
catastrophic character of Oswald Spengler's thinking. Literary tradition gave
him a language, and the reality contemporary to the philosopher gave him
convincing material. But from language, the instrument of description,
Spengler constructed the object of description, world history. The first thing
required for this was to cut historical material into pieces by introducing
absolute beginnings and absolute ends. Historical material, of course, is
divided into periods, and its boundaries are marked by bifurcation points that
give impulses to irreversible and unpredictable changes. But even at moments of
the sharpest breaks, which seize significant bundles of historical fibers, they
never embrace all sides of life, except perhaps when a whole people or culture
is physically destroyed. Life is discontinuous at some levels and continuous at
others. When one speaks of a boundary, it is necessary to indicate from whose
point of view.

Spengler, however, speaks of absolute breaks caused by the death of one
cultural spirit and the birth of another. It is precisely the absolute
character of the cutting and the separation of cultures that allows each of
them to be considered as a separate organism, transferring to them the notions
of youth, maturity, old age, and subsequent inevitable death. The same absolute
character of the division requires that all aspects of a culture be born and
die simultaneously, and therefore that they be animated from a single center.
That center, and here the Hegelian tradition is felt, can only be the soul of
the culture.

We see clearly how the narrativity of research thinking affects the
construction of history created by the scholar. The effort to consider world
history as a series of closed cultures with "beginnings" and "ends," though in
a much subtler form than Spengler's, can also be felt in Arnold Toynbee's
grandiose work.

The searches for the "beginning of history," the beginning of civilization,
and in connection with this the appearance of the paradoxical term
"prehistoric civilization," should also be attributed to the influence of
mythological narrative models.

In recent decades archaeology has made a number of discoveries that
significantly move back the lower boundary of our knowledge of the most ancient
period of humanity's cultural development. What seemed to be the beginning
turns out to be the result of a long path. What was considered primitive
reveals depth and complexity. The great historian of Eastern cultures N. I.
Konrad was entirely right when, in a letter to Toynbee, he defended the idea of
the continuity of the development of human culture. Whole civilizations do of
course perish. From ancient Achaean culture, for example, there remain ruins:
the Lion Gate, the palace of Knossos, and a few other things. But did not one
other thing remain from it, something perhaps infinitely more important: the
*Iliad*? The *Iliad* is in no way the beginning of a new literature. It is the
sum of a whole preceding culture, but a sum drawn by a new people who inherited
that culture. The genuine beginning of Greek literature is the primitive poetry
and prose that we find in the "post-Homeric" epoch.

Konrad suggests that perhaps the same approach can be taken to another
mysterious literary monument, the Indian *Ramayana*. While nothing was known of
the existence in India of a culture more ancient than the one we knew, before
the discoveries at Harappa and Mohenjo-daro, there seemed no choice but to
assume an incredibly developed myth-making fantasy, joined to an astonishing
art of artistic expression. But perhaps the matter can be explained more
simply, and purely historically. Perhaps here too there is the same mystery of
history as in the case of the *Iliad*. In any case, the *Ramayana* is not
primitive. It is not a beginning. It is a great cultural world that has gone
into irrevocable distance, but has been transfigured and reborn through those
who destroyed it. In short, it is a case analogous to the Greek *Iliad*.

In light of the data of contemporary archaeology, it is exceptionally risky to
speak of an absolute beginning. The idea once expressed - that if we have a
developed civilization, then we can be sure that before it there was already a
developed civilization - no longer seems paradoxical. Here a parallel suggests
itself with an autocatalytic reaction, in which the presence at the beginning
of the reaction of some amount of the substance that is its final result
accelerates the very process of its formation. Proximity to culture generates a
rapid process of culture-production.

By moving back the boundaries of the emergence of culture - or rather, by
removing the question, since on one side it is inspired by mythology and on the
other we do not have, and apparently never will have, sufficient data for
posing it in a genuinely scientific way - we must train ourselves to think that
the forms of culture known to us are by no means the only possible ones. In
particular, can we suppose the existence of powerful civilizations that
developed outside writing and have therefore disappeared for us?
