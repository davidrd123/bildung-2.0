## Semiosphere And History

Vyacheslav Vsevolod Ivanov

In the book offered to readers, Yuri Mikhailovich Lotman arrives at the ideas
that would especially concern him in the last years of his life. They touch on
the main key questions of the history of culture in general, and of Russian
culture and literature in particular.

The central theoretical problem that occupied Lotman at the end of his life
became explosion in culture and history, and the principled unpredictability of
the events that follow an explosion. Lotman knew that he was writing at an
explosive moment in the history of Russian and world culture. He hurried to
respond to the social changes that stirred him, as they stirred all of us. But
at the same time this whole movement of thought had been prepared by many
years of previous reflection and work on the history of Russian culture.

Lotman's distinction between gradual evolution and explosions represents a
contemporary form of debates around different models of development, debates
that began at the end of the eighteenth century and continued in Darwin's
teaching and in responses against it, including Danilevsky's famous book,
Berg's well-known work on nomogenesis, and later anti-Darwinian works. Lotman
himself had Cuvier's theory of geological catastrophes in mind when he spoke of
changes in the meaning of the concept of "revolution" in connection with
geological catastrophes.

It should be noted that, like Lotman's late works, Cuvier's works are marked
by a connection with the time that produced them. Cuvier was by no means only a
study-bound scholar, although his reaction to events occurring in France was
the polar opposite of Lotman's chivalrous position. According to a little
dictionary of turncoats I saw in the library of one of Lotman's teachers,
B. V. Tomashevsky, Cuvier was nearly a record-holder in the number of timely
transitions from one political faith to another. In contemporary terms, we
would say that he always changed, even ahead of time, along with the general
line. Yet independently of his personal inclination toward compromises,
perhaps dictated above all by the desire to continue his scientific work
without interference, Cuvier in his general scientific conception was a son of
the French Revolution. Development was thought by him first of all as a change
of epochs separated from one another by catastrophes.

In the concrete field of geology and paleontology, which was Cuvier's
scientific domain, contemporary science seems quite recently to have found
irrefutable evidence for the correctness of his model. Long debates over the
causes of the dinosaurs' disappearance ended with the discovery of traces of a
meteorite whose collision with Earth, in a region close to the famous Bermuda
Triangle, completely changed ecological conditions on Earth and led to the
extinction of most of its then inhabitants. Data are accumulating that allow
some scholars to suppose that an analogous catastrophe occurred several tens of
millions of years later as well. In any case, it is already indisputable that
geological and biological evolution on Earth was not only gradual, to use the
terminology of Lotman's works, but also included explosions. These were, from
the intra-terrestrial point of view, completely unpredictable, since they were
connected with the intrusion of cosmic objects.

Since Lotman in his late works returns to the need to create a unifying theory
that would include, alongside the history of society and culture, what used to
be called natural history, it is worth noting that explosion also enters as
the basic event in all contemporary cosmogonic and astrophysical constructions.
The history of the universe begins with an explosion, the Big Bang. According
to the anthropic, or anthropological, principle outlined by the great physicist
Ehrenfest as early as 1917 but developed only in the last decade, the
parameters of the universe after the Big Bang were favorable for the future
evolution that led to the appearance of the human being and the human brain.
One can agree with an idea from one of the recent speeches of the Czech
president and writer Vaclav Havel, who argues that this physical principle
should become a component of the human worldview.

Among the most serious reflections on these themes, one should note three
articles by Academician A. D. Sakharov, written during his exile in Gorky and
published then by P. L. Kapitsa in the *Journal of Experimental and
Theoretical Physics*. In them Sakharov also discusses the hypothesis of the
cyclical development of the universe, essentially returning to the idea of the
Swedish physicist Arrhenius, according to which the universe can arise and
perish. Sakharov allows this same cyclical model for "information processes."
The analogy with Lotman's reasoning is striking. In his final works, Lotman
insists on the cyclical character of natural phenomena, in contrast to the
linear movement realized in the history of culture and in human history in
general. The physicist Andrei Linde, at the end of a book devoted to the
inflationary model of the early development of the universe, also insists on
the need for a synthesis of physics and the science of the human being.

The problems posed in Lotman's book were discussed in the preceding decades
much more often in the works of natural scientists than in those of humanists,
who, with rare exceptions, did not touch the fundamental questions of
diachrony. Exceptions include the ideas of the Formalists that influenced the
formation of Lotman's views, such as Y. N. Tynianov, and especially the ideas
of R. O. Jakobson, who, following Berg in his Prague period and later,
developed the conception of a teleological model of nomogenesis. In the 1960s
and 1970s, not only scholars but also Western writers were impressed by the
book *Chance and Necessity* by the molecular biologist and Nobel laureate
Jacques Monod. For Lotman's book, however, the most important acquaintance was
with the works of another Nobel laureate, Ilya Prigogine, who together with
his collaborators illuminated the nature of explosive processes from the point
of view of the natural sciences. The works of the French mathematician Rene
Thom on catastrophe theory, including its application to semiotics, have a more
strict form.

Lotman was prepared to receive Prigogine's ideas by the period when, from the
beginning of the 1960s, the development of Russian semiotics went hand in hand
with the first experiments in applying information theory to cultural
phenomena. Although information theory arose mainly thanks to the work of the
American engineer and mathematician Claude Shannon, who studied the
transmission of messages through technical communication channels, the range of
its applications turned out to be very broad. This was noticed at once by one
of the theory's creators, the great Russian mathematician A. P. Kolmogorov,
with whom Lotman was in contact during the years when Kolmogorov began his
work on Russian verse.

In Lotman's approach to explosive processes, his interest in their information
characteristics is clearly felt. It is precisely in this that Lotman turns out
to be especially close to Prigogine. At the last lecture Lotman gave at Moscow
University, he set out a general scheme of explosion in culture. When asked
about Prigogine, he answered that he considered Prigogine's works brilliant. It
should be noted that concepts of chaos, important for Prigogine, have recently
come to the center of attention for specialists in mathematics and in those
natural sciences that widely apply the apparatus of strange attractors,
fractals, and other new ideas and methods. These may exert significant
influence on humanistic research as well.

Yuri Mikhailovich Lotman was not only one of the founders of contemporary
semiotics, understood as a general science studying all signs and texts in the
spirit of what Charles Peirce had already dreamed, and of structural-semiotic
literary studies in the Tartu-Moscow variant oriented primarily toward texts
and their study. He was a historian and a historian of culture in both the most
general and the most concrete sense. From his last book, *Conversations on
Russian Culture*, one can form an idea of how extensive and utterly unique his
knowledge in this area was. The remarkable chapter of his commentary on
*Eugene Onegin* devoted to the culture and everyday life of that epoch says the
same thing. I will give only one additional example. Among the concrete
historical-cultural disciplines in which Lotman was an incomparable expert was
"duel studies." Once Lotman took a whole group of his Tartu students to
Leningrad in order to acquaint them in museum collections with the distinctive
features of dueling pistols. Lotman's general writings on the history of
culture contain many precious examples of his deep knowledge of Russian culture
throughout its whole course.

From the middle of the 1980s Lotman became increasingly interested in the
methodology of the study of history. The last of the large seminars at which
Tartu and Moscow scholars again gathered under his aegis in Kaariku at the
Summer School was devoted to the relation between semiotics and history. There
he presented his program of the principled unpredictability of the consequences
of explosive processes.

In the works where this problem is discussed mainly on the material of Russian
history, one sees at the same time Yuri Mikhailovich's desire to use his unique
knowledge for the benefit of all who would like to understand Russia by reason,
despite Tyutchev's warning that a "common yardstick" cannot be applied to it,
and his reluctance to slide onto the path of cheap journalistic predictions.
Lotman tries to formulate certain factors and regularities that are constantly
present in Russian history: the role of the West, attraction toward it and
repulsion from it; the character of the Enlightenment, which turned out to be
different from its Western European variant; the significance of utopia.

The last example shows his insight, hidden away in the same way that his ironic
smile was sometimes hidden in the mustache of the smiling scholar. He advises
us not to hurry with conclusions about disillusionment in utopias. This advice
can already be useful to those specialists in "Sovietology" and
"Kremlinology" whose hasty journalistic conclusions lack the temporal depth of
Lotman's historical knowledge.

Given Lotman's fascination with the concrete history of Russian everyday life
and Russian things, it would seem that he should have become an ally of the
French historical school of the *Annales*, which is discussed in this book. He
recognizes the great merits of scholars such as Jacques Le Goff, who in works
on medieval history revealed the role of gradual changes and everyday life in
history. But unlike some new apologists of this historical school, Lotman would
never have made this aspect of study the only one deserving attention. He is
interested both in concrete historical personalities, to whose biographical
interpretation he devoted individual monographs beginning in his youth, and in
mass movements that lead to explosive unpredictability.

His conception of history, and in particular Russian history, is without
prejudice. He wants to understand what previously occurred without submitting
to the distortions of the past inherent in retrospection, whose unavoidable
presence he constantly emphasizes. What has already happened, for the
historian, seems to cancel the multiple possibilities that existed before the
accidental choice of one of them. The main methodological innovation Lotman
wants to introduce into historical science consists in the need to reconstruct
this multiplicity.

I am not completely certain that possibilities are always equally probable,
even in the most radical explosion. Lotman insisted on this. Let us consider as
an example the situation of summer 1917 in Russia, between the two revolutions.
Pasternak, who gave his book of poems *My Sister Life* the subtitle "Summer
1917," and perhaps wrote part of it later, although it was published several
years afterward, drew in it the atmosphere of chaos and the miracle generated
by it. By the end of that summer it becomes clear that the achieved maximum of
entropy cannot grow further. In the political plane a choice lay ahead that in
any case would limit the freedom that was turning into anarchy. Already here
there is no equiprobability. The probability of preserving freedom - of the
person, of assembly, of speech, of the press - at the previous level had become
negligibly small. What lay ahead was a choice among several possible
unfreedoms. They can be connected with the personalities of General Kornilov
and the Bolshevik leaders Trotsky and Lenin.

The behavior of each of them, in turn, is connected with biographical
informational characteristics. In particular, Lenin's insistence on the
necessity of seizing power quickly may, among other things, have been connected
with his assumptions, not necessarily only subconscious, about the limited
personal lifetime he had in reserve. Each of the social and personal motives
for the behavior of these and other historical figures could influence the
probability of their coming to power. The success of the Kornilov movement and
the Bolshevik coup did not appear equally probable to many people involved in
the events. One can refer here to F. A. Stepun's memoirs, with their
description of Kerensky weeping at the moment when he had to sign the first
order to shoot a deserter.

The determination, and perhaps even the calculation, of the probability of
different events that may occur after an explosion seems one possible direction
for future historical research, in which the methods of probability theory and
information theory will occupy the place due them.

In his historical and historical-literary works, Yuri Mikhailovich Lotman
concentrated attention on characters and types especially characteristic of
Russia. He proposed a synthesis of his historical reflections, his thoughts on
the nature of the text, and the semiosphere. Although his books speak of a
science of the past, they are wholly addressed to the future of science.

## From The Editor

Y. M. Lotman's monograph, written specially for the London publishing house
I. B. Tauris & Co. Ltd. under the title *Universe of the Mind: A Semiotic
Theory of Culture*, translated by Ann Shukman with an introduction by Umberto
Eco, grew out of articles published in the 1960s through the 1980s. The present
edition is based on the manuscript provided by the publisher, with attention to
draft versions of the text preserved in Y. M. Lotman's archive. Corrections in
quotations and minor stylistic editing are not separately noted. The reference
system and the formatting of the bibliography have been changed. All footnotes
marked by asterisks are editorial. L. N. Kiseleva, N. P. Vorobieva,
D. E. Kuzovkin, and instructors of the Department of Russian Literature and the
Department of Foreign Literature at Tartu University took part in preparing the
edition. We are also grateful to the staff of the Rare Books Department of the
State Public Historical Library for their assistance in preparing the
manuscript.

T. E.
