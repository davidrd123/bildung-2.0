## Conclusion

The individual human intellectual apparatus does not have a monopoly on the
work of thought. Semiotic systems, each separately and all together in the
integrating unity of the semiosphere, carry out intellectual operations
synchronously and through the full depth of historical memory. They store,
process, and increase the volume of information.

Thought is inside us, but we are also inside thought, just as language is
something generated by our consciousness and directly dependent on the
mechanisms of the brain, while we too are inside language. If we were not
immersed in language, our brain could not generate it. And conversely, if our
brain were not capable of generating language, we could not be immersed in it.
So too thought is both something born from the human brain and a sphere
surrounding us, outside of which intellectual generation would be impossible.
Finally, the spatial image of the world is both inside and outside us.

We are both part and likeness of an enormous intellectual mechanism. From this
come the considerable difficulties, but also the enormous importance, of
studies of this type. A steadily clearer synthetic character emerges: whether
we study the structure of the artistic text, the work of functional asymmetry
in the cerebral hemispheres, problems of oral speech or the communication of
deaf-mute people, advertising in the modern world, or the system of religious
representations in archaic cultures, we are coming to know different mechanisms
of the single intellectual life of humanity.

We are inside it, but it too, all of it, is inside us. Being simultaneously a
"matryoshka," a participant in an infinite number of dialogues, a likeness of
the whole, and an "other" for others and for oneself, we are both a planet in
an intellectual galaxy and the image of its universe.

The present book is an attempt to pose the question. The creation of a general
and historical semiotics of culture would be the answer.
