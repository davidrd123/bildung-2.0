## Symbolic Spaces

## 3. The House In The Master And Margarita

Among the universal themes of world folklore, an important place belongs to the
opposition between the House, one's own, safe, cultural space protected by
tutelary gods, and the anti-house or "forest house," an alien, demonic space,
a place of temporary death, entry into which is equivalent to a journey into
the afterworld. The archaic models of consciousness connected with this
opposition show great stability and productivity in the later history of
culture.

In Pushkin's poetry of the second half of the 1820s and the 1830s, the theme
of the House becomes an ideological focus, absorbing thoughts about cultural
tradition, history, humanity, and the self-standing of the human being. In
Gogol's work, it receives completed development in the form of an opposition,
on the one hand, between the House and the demonic anti-house, such as the
brothel or the offices of the Petersburg tales, and on the other hand between
homelessness or the Road as highest value and the closed egoism of life in
houses. In Dostoevsky, the mythological archetype merges with the Gogolian
tradition: the hero who inhabits the underground, the coffin-room, already
spaces of death in themselves, must pass through the dead house in order to
rise and be reborn.

This tradition is exceptionally significant for Bulgakov. For him the
symbolism of House and Anti-house becomes one of the organizing principles of
his entire work. The subject of this essay is the function of this motif in
*The Master and Margarita*.

The first thing we learn is that the only hero who passes through the whole
novel from the first page to the last, and who at the end will be called "the
disciple," is the poet Ivan Nikolaevich Ponyrev, writing under the pseudonym
Bezdomny, "Homeless." Yeshua too is introduced into the text in a similar way:
asked where he lives permanently, he answers that he has no permanent dwelling
and travels from city to city. The procurator reduces this to one word:
vagabond. Immediately after this, the text turns to the accusation that Yeshua
intended to destroy the building of the temple, while Ivan's later address
will be the madhouse.

Alongside the theme of homelessness, the theme of the false house immediately
arises. It is realized in several variants, of which the most important is the
communal apartment. The concepts "home" and "communal kitchen" are for
Bulgakov incompatible in principle, and their juxtaposition creates the image
of a phantasmagoric world.

The apartment becomes the concentration of an anomalous world. In its space,
the pranks of infernal forces, the mysticism of bureaucratic fictions, and
everyday squabbling intersect. Just as every casual appeal to the devil in the
novel has a double semantics, functioning both as emotional interjection and
as literal designation, so purely "apartment" conversations usually have a
double semantics with an absurd or infernal underside. Lotman points to the
line that, in the building's housing jargon, means one cannot occupy rooms
formerly occupied by the dead Berlioz, but that also produces the nightmare
image of Koroviev literally sitting on half of a dead man.

The fact that the apartment symbolizes not a place of life but something
directly opposed to it is revealed by the stable connection between the themes
of apartment and death. The word "apartment" first appears in the novel in a
threatening context: after predicting Berlioz's death, Woland answers the
question of where he will stay by saying that he will stay in Berlioz's
apartment. The theme develops in Koroviev's words to Nikanor Ivanovich: the
dead man, after all, no longer has any use for the apartment. Berlioz's uncle
comes to Moscow with the goal of registering himself in the rooms of his dead
nephew. A relative's death becomes a favorable moment that a businesslike
person must not miss.

Apartment number 50 is the place of infernal phenomena, but these began there
long before Woland and his retinue settled in it. The jeweler's apartment had
always been "bad." The "miracles of disappearances" that took place there do
not make it unique in the novel, however. The main property of anti-houses in
the novel is that people do not live in them; they disappear from them, run
away, fly away, or leave and vanish without a trace.

The apartment's irrationality is revealed by the parallel between the famous
fifth dimension, which allows those familiar with it to expand a room to any
desired limits, and the ordinary Muscovite who, without any fifth dimension at
all, converts a three-room apartment into a four-room one, then exchanges it
into two separate apartments, then exchanges one of those again. The irrational
contradiction between the universal pursuit of "space" and the incompatibility
of this concept with life is exposed in Bengalsky's cry after losing his head:
take the apartment, only give back the head. To live "on space" is as unreal
as sitting on half a corpse.

Bulgakov's apartment has an obviously uninhabitable appearance. In the building
into which Ivan runs in pursuit of Woland, the door is opened by a little girl
who immediately disappears somewhere. The entrance hall is enormous, neglected,
weakly lit, and under a high ceiling black with dirt. A bicycle without tires
hangs on the wall, a great iron-bound chest stands nearby, a winter hat lies on
a shelf, and behind a door a radio voice angrily shouts verse. It is here that
Ivan encounters a naked woman in infernal lighting from the coals smoldering
in the heater.

Yet the signs separating the anti-house from the House cannot be reduced only
to the neglect, disorder, and homelessness of communal apartments. Margarita
does not know the horrors of communal life, but she too feels that one cannot
live in the mansion; one can only die there. Similarly, Pontius Pilate hates
Herod's palace. He lives, eats, and sleeps under the columns of the balcony,
unable even during a hurricane to enter the palace. The one time Pilate enters
the palace interior, he meets there with the head of the secret guard. Rooms
are used not for life, but for secret meetings. Afranius and Niza likewise go
inside a house in order to negotiate the price for Judas' murder.

In the stories of poisoners, murderers, and traitors appearing at Satan's ball,
the walls of rooms several times play the darkest role. When news of Berlioz's
death spreads through the building, Nikanor Ivanovich receives dozens of
applications laying claim to the dead man's living space: pleas, threats,
complaints, denunciations. The apartment becomes a synonym for something dark,
and above all for denunciation. Aloisy Mogarych denounced the Master because
he wanted to move into his rooms. The "housing question" acquires the character
of a capacious symbol. Woland summarizes: ordinary people are much the same as
before; only the housing question has spoiled them.

The anti-house in the novel is not represented only by apartments. The fates of
the characters pass through many "houses." Among the chief ones are
Griboedov's House, the madhouse, and the camp building that appears in
Margarita's dream as an infernal place for a living person. Griboedov is
especially important. In it the semantics traditionally invested by cultural
history in the concept "House" undergoes complete travesty. Everything turns
out false, from names on signs to the absurd inscription "Perelygino."

It is significant that judgment is carried out precisely over premises raised
to the degree of symbols. Margarita punishes apartments, while sparing
Latunsky from Woland's retinue; Koroviev and Behemoth burn Griboedov's House.

The infernal nature of pseudo-houses is transferred also to their accumulation,
the city. At the beginning and end of the novel we are shown the houses of the
evening city. Woland looks at upper floors that dazzlingly reflect the broken
sun, leaving Mikhail Alexandrovich forever. Later, the western windows of the
upper floors of the great buildings ignite with that same broken, blinding
sun, and Woland's eye burns like one of those windows, although his back is to
the sunset. The comparison with Woland's eye reveals the sinister meaning of
these burning windows, bringing their gleam close to the repeated reflection of
glowing coals in the novel. In general, shining windows in the novel are a sign
of the anti-world.

Bulgakov opposes the House of the living to the anti-house of the pseudo-living
through a whole set of stable signs, including lighting and sound. From the
anti-house come the sounds of the gramophone, as in the Master's story of the
night when he approached his little basement, now occupied by Aloisy Mogarych,
and heard a gramophone playing in his rooms; or the identical radio sounds in
every apartment. The sign of the House is the sound of the piano. The double
nature of apartment number 50 is revealed, in particular, by alternating sounds
of piano and gramophone.

Using spatial language to express non-spatial concepts, Bulgakov makes the
House a center of spirituality, expressed in the richness of inner culture,
creativity, and love. Spirituality forms a complex hierarchy in Bulgakov. At
the lower level is dead, unspiritual existence. At the highest level is
absolute spirituality. The first needs living space, not a House. The second
does not need a House: Yeshua does not need one, because his earthly life is
an eternal road. Pontius Pilate, in happy dreams, sees himself walking
endlessly along a moonbeam.

Between these poles lies the broad and ambiguous world of life. On its lower
levels we encounter demonic spirituality, cruel games that shake and stir up
the inert world of unspirituality, bring irony and mockery into it, and loosen
it. These wicked amusements awaken those who can be awakened and, in the end,
contribute to the victory of a spirituality higher than themselves. This is the
meaning of the epigraph from Goethe, with its Manichaean tinge: the force that
always wills evil and always works good.

Higher stands art. It has a fully human nature and does not rise to the
absolute: the Master has not earned light. But art is hierarchically higher
than Woland's physically stronger servants or figures such as Afranius, who are
also not without a creative element. In the aspect that interests us, this
greater spirituality appears spatially. Woland's retinue, arriving in Moscow,
settles in an apartment. Afranius and Pilate meet in a palace. The Master needs
a House. The search for the House is one point of view from which the Master's
path can be described.

The Master's path is a wandering.

His story gives clear transitions from one space to another. It begins with the
winning of a hundred thousand and the transformation of a museum worker and
translator into a writer and Master. Having won the money, Ivan's mysterious
guest buys books, abandons his room on Miasnitskaia, and rents from a builder
two rooms in the basement of a small house in a little garden. The Master
recalls this as a golden age: a completely separate little apartment, even a
hallway with a sink, a stove with fire burning in it, and a room filled with
books.

The Master's new dwelling is a "little apartment." What transforms it into a
House is not the sink in the hall, but the intimacy of cultural spirituality.
For Bulgakov, as for Pushkin in the 1830s, culture is inseparable from an
intimate, hidden life. Work on the novel turns the little basement apartment
into a poetic House. Opposed to it is Griboedov's House, where outside the
shy, intimate atmosphere of culture, future authors of *Don Quixote*, *Faust*,
*The Inspector General*, or *Eugene Onegin* are expected to ripen like
pineapples in a greenhouse.

Once the Master gives up creativity, the House turns back into a miserable
basement. He says he has been broken, that he is bored, that he wants to go
back to the basement. Woland summarizes the fall: the man who composed the
story of Pontius Pilate is going to settle near a lamp and live like a beggar.
But the Master nevertheless receives a House.

Margarita shows him his eternal home: silence, sand under her bare feet, a
Venetian window, climbing grapevines reaching to the roof. Having passed
through the temptations of pseudo-houses, the infernal place for a living
person, and the house of sorrow, having been purified by flight, for flight is
the constant companion of departure from the world of apartments, the Master
obtains a world of dear domesticity, of life saturated with culture, with the
spiritual labor of previous generations and the atmosphere of love, a world
from which cruelty has been expelled. In this room there will be those he
loves and who will not disturb him; they will play and sing; candles will burn;
he will fall asleep smiling, wearing his worn and eternal cap.

This partial aspect of the construction of *The Master and Margarita* is
interesting because it allows us to place the novel in the general perspective
of Bulgakov's work. *The White Guard* can be understood as a novel about the
destruction of the domestic world. It begins with the mother's death, with the
poetic description of the native nest, and at the same time with an ominous
prophecy: the walls will fall, the falcon will fly from the white glove, the
light in the bronze lamp will go out. At the opposite end of Bulgakov's work
stands *Theatrical Novel*, in which a homeless writer, living in a beggarly
room that ceases to be a room while he is writing and becomes the cabin of a
flying ship, resurrects the Turbins' House. Out of the white page there appears
a colored, three-dimensional little box in which a light burns and figures
move; with time this camera in the book begins to sound, and the piano is heard.
Then the box grows to the size of the theater's rehearsal stage, and the
heroes recover their lost House.

At the lowest point of this creative curve stands *Zoyka's Apartment*. It is
here that "apartment" acquires the symbolic meaning familiar to us from *The
Master and Margarita*. The novel is at once included in a very deep
literary-mythological tradition and serves as the organic result of its
author's development.

For Bulgakov, the House is an inner, closed space, the bearer of meanings of
safety, cultural harmony, and creativity. Beyond its walls lie destruction,
chaos, and death. The Apartment is chaos that has taken on the appearance of
the House and displaced it from life. The fact that House and Apartment,
especially the communal apartment, appear as antipodes leads to the removal of
the basic everyday feature of a house, namely that it is a dwelling or living
quarters, as insignificant. Only the semiotic features remain. The House
becomes a sign element of cultural space.

Here an important principle of human cultural thinking is laid bare: real
space becomes an iconic image of the semiosphere, a language in which various
non-spatial meanings are expressed, and the semiosphere, in turn, transforms
the real spatial world surrounding the human being in its own image and likeness.
