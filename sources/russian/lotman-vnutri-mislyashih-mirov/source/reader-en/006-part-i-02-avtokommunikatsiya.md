## Autocommunication: "I" And "The Other" As Addressees

### On Two Models Of Communication In The System Of Culture

The organic connection between culture and communication is one of the
foundations of contemporary cultural studies. One consequence of this is the
transfer into the sphere of culture of models and terms borrowed from
communication theory. The application of the basic model developed by Roman
Jakobson made it possible to connect a broad range of problems of language,
art, and, more broadly, culture with the theory of communicative systems. As is
well known, Jakobson's model had the following form:

context,
message,
addresser to addressee,
contact,
code.

The creation of a unified model of communicative situations was a significant
contribution to the semiotic sciences and found a response in many scholarly
works. But the automatic transfer of already existing ideas into the field of
culture creates a number of difficulties. The main one is this: in the mechanism
of culture, communication takes place along at least two channels that are
arranged differently.

We will still have to return to the necessary presence, in the unified mechanism
of culture, of pictorial and verbal links, which can be regarded as two
differently arranged channels for transmitting information. But both of these
channels are described by Jakobson's model and, in that respect, are of the same
type. If we set ourselves the task of constructing a model of culture at a more
abstract level, however, it becomes possible to distinguish two types of
communication channels, only one of which is described by the classical model
used up to now. To do this, we must first distinguish two possible directions in
the transmission of a message. The most typical case is the direction "I - HE,"
where "I" is the subject of transmission, the possessor of information, and "HE"
is the object, the addressee. In this case it is assumed that, before the act of
communication begins, some message is known to "me" and not known to "him."

The dominance of communications of this type in the culture familiar to us hides
another direction in the transmission of communication, which could be
schematically characterized as the direction "I - I." The case in which a
subject transmits a message to himself, that is, to someone to whom it is
already known, appears paradoxical. In fact, however, it is not so rare, and it
plays a considerable role in the general system of culture.

When we speak of the transmission of a message in the "I - I" system, we do not
have in mind, first of all, those cases in which the text performs a mnemonic
function. Here the second "I," the receiver, is functionally equated with a
third person. The difference comes down to the fact that in the "I - HE" system
information moves in space, while in the "I - I" system it moves in time.

We are interested above all in the case where the transmission of information
from "I" to "I" is not accompanied by a break in time and performs not a
mnemonic, but some other cultural function. The communication of already known
information to oneself occurs first of all in those cases where the rank of the
message is thereby raised. Thus, when a young poet reads his own poem in print,
the message remains textually the same as the handwritten text already known to
him. Yet, being translated into a new system of graphic signs that possesses a
different degree of authority in the given culture, it receives a certain
additional significance. Similar are cases in which the truth or falsity of a
message is made dependent on whether it has been spoken in words or only
implied, said or written, written or printed, and so on.

But in a whole series of other cases as well, we have the transmission of a
message from "I" to "I." These are all cases in which a person addresses
himself: in particular, those diary entries made not for the purpose of
remembering certain information, but in order, for example, to clarify the
writer's inner state, a clarification that does not take place without the act
of writing. Addressing texts, speeches, and reflections to oneself is an
essential fact not only of psychology but also of the history of culture.

In what follows, we will try to show that the place of autocommunication in the
system of culture is much more significant than one might have supposed.

How, then, is this strange situation achieved, in which a message transmitted in
the "I - I" system does not become completely redundant, but acquires some
additional new information?

In the "I - HE" system, the framing elements of the model turn out to be
variable: the addresser is replaced by the addressee. The code and the message
are constant. The message and the information contained in it are constant; what
changes is the bearer of the information.

In the "I - I" system, the bearer of information remains the same, but in the
process of communication the message is reformulated and acquires a new meaning.
This happens because an additional, second code is introduced, and the initial
message is recoded in the units of its structure, acquiring the features of a
new message.

The communication scheme in this case looks like this:

context,
shift of context,
message 1 becomes message 2,
code 1 becomes code 2.

If the communicative system "I - HE" provides only the transmission of some
constant volume of information, then in the "I - I" channel its qualitative
transformation takes place, leading to the restructuring of this "I" itself. In
the first case, the addresser transmits a message to another, the addressee, and
himself remains unchanged in the course of the act. In the second, by
transmitting to himself, he internally restructures his own essence, since the
essence of personality can be interpreted as an individual set of socially
significant codes, and here, in the process of the communicative act, that set
changes.

The transmission of a message along the "I - I" channel does not have an
immanent character, since it is conditioned by the intrusion from outside of
certain additional codes and by the presence of external impulses that shift the
contextual situation. A characteristic example is the effect of measured sounds
- the clatter of wheels, rhythmic music - on a person's inner monologue. One
could name a whole series of literary texts that reproduce the dependence of
vivid and unrestrained fantasy on the measured rhythms of horseback riding:
Goethe's "Erlking" and a number of poems in Heine's *Lyrical Intermezzo*; on
the rocking of a ship, as in Tyutchev's "Dream at Sea"; or on the rhythms of
the railway, as in Glinka's "Travelling Song" to words by Kukolnik.

Let us consider Tyutchev's "Dream at Sea" from this point of view.

In the poem, the sea and the storm rock the boat; the speaker, drowsy, gives
himself over to the caprice of the waves. Two infinities are within him, and
they play with him at will. Around him, the rocks sound like cymbals, the winds
call to one another, and the waves sing. He lies deafened in a chaos of sounds,
but above that chaos his dream flies. Painfully bright and magically mute, it
drifts lightly over the thundering darkness. In its feverish rays it unfolds
its world: the earth grows green, the ether shines, labyrinth-gardens, halls,
and pillars appear, and crowds of silent people boil up. He recognizes many
faces unknown to him, sees magical creatures and mysterious birds, strides like
a god along the heights of creation, and the motionless world shines beneath
him. Yet through all his dreams, like a magician's howl, he hears the thunder of
the sea abyss, and into the quiet realm of visions and dreams bursts the foam
of roaring waves.

We are not interested here in the aspect of the poem connected with Tyutchev's
important comparison, or opposition, between the spiritual life of the human
being on the one hand and the sea on the other.

Since the text is evidently based on a real experience - a memory of a four-day
storm in September 1838 during a journey on the Adriatic Sea - it interests us
as a monument of the author's psychological self-observation. Among other
approaches to the text, the legitimacy of such an approach can hardly be denied.

The poem distinguishes two components of the author's mental state: the soundless
dream and the measured roar of the storm. The latter is marked by the unexpected
inclusion of anapestic lines in an amphibrachic text. Lines devoted to the roar
of the storm are marked by anapest, as are two symmetrical verses beginning with
"but," which depict the breakthrough of the dream through the noise of the storm
or the breakthrough of the storm's noise through the dream. The verse devoted to
the philosophical theme of the "double abyss," the "two infinities," which links
the text with other poems by Tyutchev, is marked by the single dactyl.

Just as sharply, the noise of the storm is distinguished against the background
of the soundless world of the dream - "magically mute," populated by "silent"
crowds - by an abundance of sounding characteristics. But precisely these
measured, deafening sounds become the rhythmic background that conditions the
liberation of thought, its flight and brightness.

Let us give another example, from chapter eight of *Eugene Onegin*.

Onegin's eyes are reading, but his thoughts are far away; dreams, desires, and
sorrows crowd deeply into his soul. Between the printed lines he reads, with
spiritual eyes, other lines, and in those he is completely absorbed. They are
secret legends of heartfelt, dark antiquity, unconnected dreams, threats,
rumors, predictions, the living nonsense of a long tale, or the letters of a
young girl.

Gradually he falls into a sleep of senses and thoughts, and before him
Imagination casts its motley pharaoh.

He looks like a poet as he sits alone in the corner; the fire burns before him,
and he hums "Benedetta" or "Idol mio," dropping now a slipper, now a journal
into the fire.

In this case, three external rhythm-forming codes are given: the printed text,
the measured flicker of the fire, and the tune being hummed. It is very
characteristic that the book here does not function as a message. It is being
read without its content being noticed: "his eyes were reading, but his thoughts
were far away." It functions as a stimulator of the development of thought. It
stimulates not by its content, but by the mechanical automaticity of reading.
Onegin "reads without reading," just as he looks at the fire without seeing it
and hums without noticing that he is doing so. All three rhythmic series,
perceived by different organs, have no direct semantic relation to his thoughts,
to the "pharaoh" of his imagination. Yet they are necessary if he is to read
"other lines" with "spiritual eyes." The intrusion of external rhythm organizes
and stimulates the inner monologue.

A third example we would like to cite is the Japanese Buddhist monk
contemplating a stone garden. Such a garden is a comparatively small plot
covered with gravel, with stones arranged on it according to a complex
mathematical rhythm. Contemplation of these complexly arranged stones and gravel
is meant to create a certain attunement that assists introspection.

Various systems of rhythmic series, constructed according to syntagmatically
clearly expressed principles but lacking their own semantic meaning - from
musical repetitions to repeated ornament - can function as external codes under
whose influence the verbal message is restructured. Compare Yu. V. Knorozov's
concept of the relation between information and fascination. But in order for
the system to work, there must be a collision and interaction of two
heterogeneous principles: a message in some semantic language and the intrusion
of a purely syntagmatic additional code. Only from the combination of these
principles is the communicative system formed that can be called the language of
"I - I."

Thus the existence of a special channel of autocommunication can be considered
established. Incidentally, this question has already attracted the attention of
researchers. We find an indication of the existence of a special language,
functionally intended for autocommunication, in Lev Vygotsky, who describes it
under the name "inner speech." There we also find an indication of its
structural features:

"The fundamental difference between inner speech and external speech is the
absence of vocalization.

"Inner speech is mute, silent speech. This is its basic difference. But it is in
this direction, in the sense of a gradual growth of this difference, that the
evolution of egocentric speech takes place. The fact that this feature develops
gradually, that egocentric speech first becomes distinct functionally and
structurally before it becomes distinct with respect to vocalization, only
points to what we have placed at the basis of our hypothesis about the
development of inner speech: namely, that inner speech develops not by an
external weakening of its sounding side, passing from speech to whisper and from
whisper to mute speech, but by functional and structural separation from
external speech, passing from it to egocentric and from egocentric to inner
speech."

Let us try to describe some features of the autocommunicative system.

The first feature distinguishing it from the "I - HE" system will be the
reduction of the words of this language. They will tend to become signs of
words, indexes of signs. In the prison diary of V. K. Kuchelbecker there is a
remarkable entry on this point:

"I have noticed something strange, curious for psychologists and physiologists:
for some time now I have dreamed not of objects, not of events, but of certain
wondrous abbreviations that are related to them as a hieroglyph is to an image,
as a table of contents is to a book. Does this not come from the small number of
objects surrounding me and events happening to me?"

The tendency of the words of the "I - I" language toward reduction appears in
abbreviations, which form the basis of notes written for oneself. In the end,
the words of such a note become indexes that can be deciphered only if one
already knows what is written. Compare academician I. Yu. Krachkovsky's
characterization of the early graphic tradition of the Quran: the absence not
only of short but also of long vowels and diacritical points, making reading
possible only by knowing the text by heart.

A vivid example of communication of this type is found in the famous scene of
Kitty's and Konstantin Levin's explanation in *Anna Karenina*, all the more
interesting because it reproduces the episode of the explanation between Leo
Tolstoy and his fiancee, S. A. Bers. Levin writes only the initial letters of a
sentence. Kitty understands. When he points to one letter and asks what word it
stands for, she says it means "never."

In all these examples, we are dealing with a case in which the reader
understands the text only because he or she knows it in advance. In Tolstoy,
this happens because Kitty and Levin are already spiritually one being; the
fusion of addressee and addresser takes place before our eyes.

Words-indexes formed by such reduction tend toward isorhythm. This is connected
with the main feature of the syntax of this type of speech: it does not form
completed sentences, but strives toward endless chains of rhythmic repetitions.

Most of the examples we cite are not pure communication of the "I - I" type.
They are compromises, arising as a result of the deformation of an ordinary
linguistic text under the influence of its laws. Here two cases of
autocommunication should be distinguished: with a mnemonic function and without
it.

As an example of the first, one can cite Pushkin's well-known note beneath the
fair copy of the poem "Beneath the Blue Sky of Her Native Land." The note is an
abbreviated memorial record of hearing of the death of Riznich on July 25, 1826,
and of hearing of the deaths of Ryleev, Pestel, Muravyov, Kakhovsky, and
Bestuzhev on July 24, 1826.

This note has a clearly mnemonic function, although one should not forget
another function as well. Because in the "I - I" system the connection between
signified and signifier is to a large degree occasional, it turns out to be much
more convenient for secret writing, since it is built according to the formula:
"understandable only to those to whom it is understandable." The encryption of a
text is, as a rule, connected with its translation from the "I - HE" system into
the "I - I" system. Members of a collective using secret writing are in this
case regarded as a single "I"; those from whom the text must be hidden make up a
collective third person.

Yet even here there is an obvious unconscious action that cannot be explained
either by the memorial-mnemonic function or by the secret character of the note.
In the first line the words are shortened to groups of several graphemes, while
in the second a group consists of a single letter. The indexes tend toward equal
extension and rhythm. In the first line, since the preposition tends to merge
with the noun, two groups are formed which, with the phonological parallelism of
"u" and "o" on the one hand and "l" and "m" on the other, reveal features not
only of rhythmic but also of phonological organization. In the second line, the
need, for conspiratorial reasons, to reduce surnames to one letter set another
internal rhythm, and all the other words were reduced to the same degree. It
would be strange and monstrous to suppose that Pushkin constructed this tragic
note with conscious regard for rhythmic or phonological organization. The point
is different: the immanent and unconsciously acting laws of autocommunication
reveal certain structural features that we usually observe in the example of the
poetic text.

These features are still more noticeable in the next example, which lacks both a
mnemonic and a conspiratorial function and presents self-message in a purer
form. This concerns the unconscious notes that Pushkin made while accompanying
the process of reflection, perhaps without even noticing them.

On May 9, 1828, Pushkin wrote the poem "Alas! the language of talkative love,"
dedicated to Anna Alekseevna Olenina, whom he was courting. Nearby appears a
note made of reversed and rearranged forms of her name. Next to it is another
note, in which Pushkin wrote over "Annette" the word "Pushkine." The course of
thought is not hard to reconstruct: Pushkin was thinking of Annette Olenina as a
bride and wife. The text consists of anagrams, with right-to-left reading set as
the rule, of the name and surname of A. A. Olenina, about whom he was thinking
in French.

The mechanism of this note is interesting. First, by being read backward, the
name is transformed into a conventional index. Then a certain rhythm is set by
repetition, and by rearrangement a rhythmic disturbance of the rhythm is
introduced. The verse-like character of such a construction is obvious.

The mechanism for transmitting information in the "I - I" channel may be
represented as follows. A certain message in natural language is introduced.
Then a certain additional code is introduced, a purely formal organization built
in a definite way in syntagmatic terms and either completely freed from semantic
meanings or tending toward such liberation. Between the initial message and the
secondary code there arises a tension under whose influence a tendency appears
to interpret the semantic elements of the text as included in an additional
syntagmatic construction and as receiving new, relational meanings from their
mutual correlation. Yet although the secondary code tends to transform the
primarily meaningful elements into elements freed from general linguistic
semantic connections, this does not happen. General linguistic semantics
remains, but a secondary semantics is laid over it, formed by the shifts that
arise when rhythmic series of various types are built out of meaningful units of
language.

But the semantic transformation of the text is not limited to this. The growth
of syntagmatic connections inside the message mutes the primary semantic
connections, and at a certain level of perception the text can behave as a
complexly built asemantic message. But syntagmatically highly organized
asemantic texts tend to become organizers of our associations. Associative
meanings are attributed to them. Thus, when we look closely at a wallpaper
pattern or listen to non-programmatic music, we attribute certain meanings to
elements of these texts. The more emphasized the syntagmatic organization is,
the more associative and free the semantic connections become. Therefore a text
in the "I - I" channel tends to grow over with individual meanings and receives
the function of organizing the disordered associations that accumulate in the
consciousness of the personality. It restructures the personality included in
the process of autocommunication.

Thus the text carries meanings of three kinds: primary, general linguistic
meanings; secondary meanings arising from the syntagmatic reorganization of the
text and the counterposition of primary units; and third-degree meanings,
arising from the drawing into the message of extra-textual associations of
various levels, from the most general to the most personal.

There is no need to prove that the mechanism we have described can at the same
time be presented as a characterization of the processes lying at the basis of
poetic creation.

But a poetic principle is one thing, and actual poetic texts are another. It
would be an oversimplification to identify the latter with messages transmitted
along the "I - I" channel. A real poetic text is transmitted along two channels
simultaneously, with the exception of experimental texts, glossolalia, texts of
the type of asemantic children's counting rhymes and zaum, and texts in
languages incomprehensible to the audience. It oscillates between meanings
transmitted in the "I - HE" channel and meanings formed in the process of
autocommunication. Depending on its nearness to one or another axis and on the
orientation of the text toward one or another type of transmission, it is
perceived as "verse" or as "prose."

Of course, the orientation of a text toward the primary linguistic message or
toward a complex restructuring of meanings and increase of information does not
in itself mean that it will function as poetry or as prose. Here the correlation
with the general cultural models of these concepts in a given epoch enters into
operation.

Thus we can conclude that the system of human communications can be built in
two ways. In one case we are dealing with certain information given in advance,
which moves from one person to another, and with a code that remains constant
throughout the whole act of communication. In the other, we are dealing with an
increase of information, its transformation and reformulation. What are
introduced are not new messages but new codes, and receiver and sender are
combined in one person. In the process of such autocommunication, the
personality itself is reformulated. Connected with this is a very broad range of
cultural functions, from the feeling of one's separate being, necessary to a
person in certain types of culture, to self-recognition and
autopsychotherapy.

The role of such codes can be played by formal structures of different kinds,
which perform the function of reorganizing meanings more successfully the more
asemantic their own organization is. Such are spatial objects intended for
contemplation, like patterns or architectural ensembles, and temporal ones, like
music.

The matter is more complicated with verbal texts. Since the autocommunicative
character of the connection can be masked, taking the forms of other kinds of
communication - for example, prayer may be understood as communication not with
oneself but with an external, powerful force; rereading, or reading an already
known text, may be understood by analogy with first reading as communication
with the author, and so on - the addressee receiving a verbal text must decide
what has been transmitted to him: a code or a message. Here, to a considerable
degree, the matter will concern the receiver's orientation, since one and the
same text can play the role both of message and of code, or, oscillating between
these poles, both at once.

Two aspects should be distinguished here: the properties of the text that allow
it to be interpreted as a code, and the mode of functioning of the text in which
it is used accordingly.

In the first case, the need to perceive a text not as an ordinary message but as
a certain code model is signaled by the formation of rhythmic series,
repetitions, and additional orderings that are completely unnecessary from the
point of view of communicative links in the "I - HE" system. Rhythm is not a
structural level in the construction of natural languages. It is no accident
that if the poetic functions of phonology, grammar, and syntax find a basis and
analogy in the corresponding non-artistic levels of the text, then no such
parallel can be indicated for meter.

Rhythmic and metrical systems have been transferred not from the communicative
system "I - HE," but from the structure "I - I." The spread of the principle of
repetition to the phonological and other levels of natural language is an
aggression of autocommunication into a linguistic sphere foreign to it.

Functionally, a text is used not as a message but as a code when it does not add
any new information to what we already have, but transforms the self-
understanding of the personality that generates texts and translates already
existing messages into a new system of meanings. If reader M is told that a
certain woman named Anna Karenina threw herself under a train as a result of
unhappy love, and instead of adding this message in her memory to messages she
already possesses, she concludes, "Anna Karenina is me," and revises her
understanding of herself, her relations with certain people, and sometimes even
her behavior, then it is obvious that she is using the text of the novel not as
a message of the same type as all others, but as a certain code in the process
of communication with herself.

This is exactly how Pushkin's Tatiana read novels.

Imagining herself the heroine of her beloved authors - Clarissa, Julie,
Delphine - Tatiana wanders alone in the forest silence with a dangerous book.
In it she seeks and finds her secret ardor, her dreams, the fruits of the
fullness of her heart. She sighs, and, appropriating another's rapture and
another's sorrow, whispers by heart, in forgetfulness, a letter for the beloved
hero. But our hero, whoever he may have been, was certainly no Grandison.

The text of the novel that Tatiana has read becomes a model for reinterpreting
reality. Tatiana has no doubt that Onegin is a novelistic character. What is
unclear to her is only which role he should be identified with:

"Who are you, my guardian angel, or a treacherous tempter?"

In Tatiana's letter to Onegin, it is characteristic that the text breaks into
two parts. In the framing sections, the first two and the last stanzas, where
Tatiana writes as a young lady in love to her neighbor on the estate, she
naturally addresses him with the polite "you." But the middle part, where she
models both herself and him according to novelistic schemes, is built on the
intimate "thou." Since, as Pushkin warned us, the original of the letter was
written in French, where in both cases only the pronoun *vous* could have been
used, the form of address in the central part of the letter is only a sign of
the bookish, non-everyday, coded character of the text.

It is interesting that the romantic Lensky also explains people to himself,
including himself, by identifying them with certain texts. Here too Pushkin
demonstratively uses the same set of cliches: "savior," equivalent to
"guardian," and "corrupter," equivalent to "tempter." Lensky thinks: "I will be
her savior. I will not allow the corrupter..."

It is obvious that in all these cases texts function not as messages in some
language - not for Pushkin, but for Tatiana and Lensky - but as codes that
concentrate information about the very type of language.

We have borrowed examples from literature, but it would be wrong to conclude
from this that poetry is communication in the "I - I" system in pure form. This
principle is carried through in a more consistent form not in art, but in
moralistic and religious texts such as parables, in myth, and in the proverb.
The penetration of repetitions into proverbs is characteristic of the period
when they were not yet perceived primarily aesthetically, but had a much more
essential mnemonic or moral-normative function.

Repetitions of certain constructive, architectural elements in the interior of a
temple force us to perceive its structure as something not connected with
practical construction or technical needs, but, say, as a model of the universe
or of the human personality. Precisely because in this case the interior of the
temple is a code, and not only a text, it is perceived not only aesthetically -
only a text, not the rules of its construction, can be perceived aesthetically -
but religiously, philosophically, theologically, or in some other non-artistic
way.

Art arises neither in the row of texts of the "I - HE" system nor in the row of
texts of the "I - I" system. It uses the presence of both communicative systems
for oscillation in the field of structural tension between them. The aesthetic
effect arises at the moment when the code begins to be used as a message and the
message as a code: that is, when the text switches from one system of
communication to the other while preserving in the consciousness of the audience
its connection with both.

The nature of artistic texts as mobile phenomena, simultaneously connected with
both types of communication, does not exclude the fact that individual genres
are oriented to a greater or lesser degree toward the perception of texts as
messages or as codes. A lyric poem and an essay are, of course, not related in
the same way to one or another system of communication. But apart from the
orientation of genres, at certain moments, for historical, social, and other
epochal reasons, a given literature as a whole - and more broadly, art as a
whole - may be characterized by an orientation toward autocommunication. It is
significant that a negative attitude toward the cliche-text will be a good
working criterion of literature's general orientation toward the message.
Literature oriented toward autocommunication will not merely fail to avoid
cliches; it will show a tendency to turn texts into cliches and to identify the
"high," the "good," and the "true" with the "stable," the "eternal" - that is,
with the cliche.

But moving away from one pole, even in conscious polemic with it, by no means
signifies escaping its structural influence. No matter how much a literary work
imitates the text of a newspaper report, it preserves, for example, such a
typical feature of autocommunicative texts as the possibility of multiple,
repeated reading. Rereading *War and Peace* is a much more natural occupation
than rereading the historical sources used by Tolstoy. At the same time, no
matter how much a verbal artistic text strives - for reasons of polemic or
experiment - to cease being a message, this is impossible, as the whole
experience of art convinces us.

Poetic texts are evidently formed by a peculiar "swinging" of structures: texts
created in the "I - HE" system function as autocommunications, and the reverse
also occurs. Texts become codes; codes become messages. Following the laws of
autocommunication - the division of the text into rhythmic pieces, the reduction
of words to indexes, the weakening of semantic connections and the emphasizing
of syntagmatic ones - the poetic text enters into conflict with the laws of
natural language. Yet its perception as a text in natural language is the
condition without which poetry cannot exist or perform its communicative
function. But the complete victory of the view of poetry as only a message in
natural language would also lead to the loss of its specificity. The high
modeling capacity of poetry is connected with its transformation from message
into code. The poetic text, like a kind of pendulum, swings between the "I - HE"
and "I - I" systems. Rhythm is raised to the level of meanings, and meanings are
folded into rhythm.

The laws of the construction of the artistic text are to a significant degree
the laws of the construction of culture as a whole. This is connected with the
fact that culture itself may be regarded as the sum of messages exchanged by
various addressers - each of whom is, for the addressee, "the other," "he" - and
as one message sent by the collective "I" of humanity to itself. From this point
of view, the culture of humanity is a colossal example of autocommunication.

Simultaneous transmission along two communicative channels is proper not only to
artistic texts. It constitutes a characteristic feature of culture, if culture
is regarded as a single message. In connection with this, one can distinguish
cultures in which the dominant element will be the message transmitted along the
general-language "I - HE" channel and cultures oriented toward
autocommunication.

Since broad layers of information, actually constituting the specificity of a
given personality, can function as "message 1," their restructuring leads to a
change in the structure of personality. It should be noted that if the "I - HE"
scheme of communication implies the transmission of information while preserving
the constancy of its volume, then the "I - I" scheme is oriented toward an
increase of information: the appearance of "message 2" does not destroy
"message 1."

The European culture of modern times is consciously oriented toward the "I - HE"
system. The consumer of culture occupies the position of an ideal addressee: he
receives information from outside. Peter I formulated this relation very
precisely when he said, "I am in the rank of those being taught, and I require
those who teach me." *The Honest Mirror of Youth* instructs young people to see
education as receiving knowledge, desiring to learn from everyone and not merely
looking over things superficially. It should be emphasized that we are speaking
precisely of orientation, since at the level of textual reality every culture
consists of both types of communication. Moreover, the feature noted here is not
specific to the culture of modern times: in different forms it is encountered in
various epochs. The singling out here of European culture from the eighteenth to
the twentieth centuries is necessary because it is precisely this culture that
conditioned our habitual scholarly ideas, in particular the identification of
the act of information with receiving and exchange. Yet by no means all cases
known from cultural history can be explained from these positions.

Let us consider the paradoxical position in which we find ourselves when
studying folklore. It is well known that folklore provides the greatest grounds
for structural parallels with natural languages, and that it is precisely in
folklore that the application of linguistic methods has been accompanied by the
greatest successes. Indeed, here the researcher can establish the presence of a
limited number of elements in the system and rules of combination that are
comparatively easy to formulate. But here one must immediately emphasize a deep
difference: language gives a formal system of expression, but the sphere of
content remains, from the point of view of language as such, maximally free.
Folklore, especially such forms as the fairy tale, makes both spheres maximally
automated. Yet this situation is paradoxical. If a text really were built in
this way, it would be completely redundant. The same could be said of other
kinds of art oriented toward canonical forms, toward the fulfillment rather than
the violation of norms and rules.

The answer evidently lies in the fact that if texts of this type possessed a
definite semantics at the moment of their origin - the semantics of the fairy
tale were evidently set by its relation to ritual - then later these links were
lost, and the texts began to acquire the features of purely syntagmatic
organizations. At the level of natural language they unquestionably possess
semantics, but as phenomena of culture they tend toward pure syntagmatics: that
is, from texts they become "codes 2." This tendency of myth to turn into a
purely syntagmatic, asemantic text - not a message about certain events, but a
scheme for organizing a message - is what Claude Levi-Strauss had in mind when
he spoke of its musical nature.

For the existence of culture as a mechanism organizing a collective personality
with a common memory and collective consciousness, it is evidently necessary to
have paired semiotic systems, with the subsequent possibility of translating
texts.

The same kind of structural pair is formed by communicative systems of the "I -
HE" and "I - I" types. Incidentally, it should be noted that the rule according
to which one member of any culture-forming semiotic pair must be represented by
natural language, or include natural language within itself, seems to be
interpretable as a universal law for earthly cultures.

Real cultures, like artistic texts, are built according to the principle of a
pendulum-like swinging between these systems. Yet the orientation of one or
another type of culture toward autocommunication or toward receiving truth from
outside in the form of messages appears as a dominant tendency. It is expressed
especially sharply in the mythologized image that each culture creates as its
ideological self-portrait. This model of itself affects cultural texts, but it
cannot be identified with them. Sometimes it is a generalization of structural
principles hidden behind textual contradictions; sometimes it is their direct
opposite. In the field of cultural typology, it is possible for a grammar to
arise that is in principle inapplicable to the texts of the language it claims
to describe.

Cultures oriented toward messages have a more mobile, dynamic character. They
tend to increase the number of texts without limit and produce a rapid growth
of knowledge. A classic example may be European culture of the twentieth
century. The reverse side of this type of culture is the sharp division of
society into transmitters and receivers, the emergence of a psychological
orientation toward receiving truth as a ready-made message about someone else's
mental effort, and the growth of social passivity among those who occupy the
position of receivers of the message. It is obvious that the reader of the
modern European novel is more passive than the listener to a fairy tale, who
must still transform the received cliches into texts of his own consciousness;
the theater spectator is more passive than the participant in carnival. The
tendency toward mental consumerism constitutes the dangerous side of a culture
one-sidedly oriented toward receiving information from outside.

Cultures oriented toward autocommunication are capable of developing great
spiritual activity, but often turn out to be much less dynamic than the needs of
human society require.

Historical experience shows that the most viable systems are those in which the
struggle between these structures does not lead to the unconditional victory of
either one.

At present, however, we are still very far from the possibility of forecasting,
with any real grounding, the optimal structures of culture. Before that, we must
first understand and describe their mechanism, at least in its most
characteristic manifestations.
