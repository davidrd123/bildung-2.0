## Mechanisms Of Dialogue

We have said that the elementary act of thinking is translation. Now we can
say that the elementary mechanism of translation is dialogue. Dialogue implies
asymmetry, and asymmetry is expressed, first, in the difference between the
semiotic structures, or languages, of the participants in dialogue, and second,
in the alternating direction of messages. From this second point it follows
that the participants in dialogue alternately move from the position of
"transmission" to the position of "reception," and that transmission therefore
takes place in discrete portions, with intervals between them.

If dialogue is meaningless without semiotic difference, then with an exclusive
and absolute difference it is impossible. Asymmetry implies a level of
invariance.

But the possibility of dialogue requires still another condition: the mutual
interest of the participants in the situation in the message, and their ability
to overcome inevitable semiotic barriers. Thus John Newson, studying the
dialogic situation that arises in communication between a nursing mother and an
infant, notes, in a way that sounds somewhat unexpected in texts of this kind,
love as a necessary condition of dialogue, the mutual attraction of its
participants. One may observe that the choice of object in this case is
exceptionally successful for understanding the general mechanisms of dialogue.
Within an organism, dialogue as a form of sign exchange is impossible: other
forms of contact dominate there. But between units that are completely without
a common language, dialogue is impossible as well. The relation mother-infant
represents, in this sense, an ideal experimental cell: the participants in
dialogue have already ceased to be one being, but as if they have not yet
completely ceased to be one. Here we encounter in pure form the fact that the
need for dialogue, the dialogic situation, precedes real dialogue and even the
existence of a language for it.

Something else is still more interesting. In order to work out a common
language, each participant in the situation strives to move into the "other's"
language. The mother pronounces sounds that reproduce the infant's cooing. But
what is more striking is that the facial expressions of the infant, when filmed
and then watched in slow motion, show that the child too imitates the mother's
facial expression, that is, tries to move into her language. It is also curious
that in dialogue of this kind a strict sequence is observed in the alternation
of transmission and reception: when one side is carrying out a "message," the
other preserves a pause, and conversely. Thus, for example, and many people
have probably had occasion to observe this, an "exchange of laughter" takes
place between mother and child, that "language of smiles" in which Rousseau saw
the only conversation guaranteed against lying.

It should be kept in mind, however, that discreteness in the process of moving
from transmission to reception arises in practice at the level of description,
when a dialogic situation is fixed by an external observer. Discreteness, the
capacity to issue information in portions, is the law of all dialogic systems.
Yet discreteness at the level of structure may arise where, in its material
realization, there is continuity at different levels of intensity. Thus, for
example, if a real process is carried out in the form of a cyclical alternation
between periods of maximum activity and periods of its maximum lowering, then a
recording instrument, if it does not register indicators below a certain
threshold, will represent the process as discrete. The apparatus of a culture's
self-description behaves in the same way. The development of culture is
cyclical and, like most dynamic processes in nature, is subject to sinusoidal
oscillations. But in a culture's self-consciousness, the periods of least
activity are usually registered as interruptions.

These considerations make sense when we examine certain aspects of cultural
history. If we isolate from the history of world culture some single row, such
as "the history of English literature" or "the history of the Russian novel,"
we obtain a chronologically extended continuous line in which periods of
intensity are replaced by relative lulls. But as soon as one sees immanent
development as one part in a dialogue, it becomes clear that periods of
so-called "decline" are often times of pause in the dialogue, filled with the
intensive reception of information, after which periods of transmission follow.

Relations among units of all levels, from genres to national cultures, are
built in this way. One can distinguish the following scheme. The relative
inertia of one or another structure is brought out of a state of rest by a
stream of texts that arrive from structures connected with it by certain
relations and themselves in a state of excitation. A stage of passive
saturation follows. A language is assimilated; texts are adapted. At the same
time, the generator of texts is, as a rule, located in the nuclear structure of
the semiosphere, and the receiver is located on the periphery. When saturation
reaches a certain threshold, the internal mechanisms of text-generation in the
receiving structure are set in motion. From a passive state it passes into a
state of excitation and itself begins vigorously to emit new texts, bombarding
other structures with them, including its own "exciter." This process can be
described as a change of center and periphery. At the same time, and this is
very important, an increase of energy takes place: the system that has entered
a state of activity emits much more energy than its exciter, and spreads its
influence over a significantly broader region. From this follows the
progressive universalism of cultural systems.

Let us explain this with some examples. Italy, stunned from the fifth century
onward by the invasions of the Germans, Alaric, Radagaisus, Genseric, then the
Huns, then the Goths of Odoacer, the Ostrogoths of Theodoric, the Byzantines,
Lombards, Franks, Arabs and Normans from the south, and Magyars from the
north, turned into a geographical concept and seemed to have lost its cultural
life. Meanwhile, within the cultural space outlined by the broad boundaries of
the millennia-old Mediterranean civilizations, and drawing into itself Germanic,
Slavic, and Arab peoples, new "hot points" of civilization were arising. Among
these one should include Provençal culture of the 11th and 12th centuries.

For a certain period, Italy appears as a "receiver" of texts. In poetry this is
above all the lyric poetry of the Provençal troubadours. Along with it comes a
fashion for courtly behavior and for the Provençal language. In Italy, canzoni
and sonnets begin to be created in Provençal, and, what is especially
characteristic, grammars of the Provençal language are written. One of the two
old Provençal grammars, *Razos de trobar*, was compiled in Italy around this
time and especially for Italians.

After the studies of Ramón Menéndez Pidal, which significantly strengthened
the "Andalusian" theory of the origin of Provençal poetry, it becomes obvious
that the role of Provence as a catalyst of Renaissance poetry in Italy is
symmetrical in relation to the role of Andalusia in the "provocation" of the
lyric poetry of the Aquitanian troubadours. Warning against exaggerating the
role of the Arab-Andalusian impulse and pointing to the significance of local
folkloric and Roman-antique traditions, as well as medieval Latin poetry,
Pidal nevertheless concludes that in the conception of courtly love,
Arab-Andalusian poetry preceded the lyric poetry of Provence and other Romance
countries, and served as their model in stanzaic form. From this, in his
account, follows the coincidence of the varieties of stanza with a unison
reprise in Arab-Andalusian and Romance poetry. At the very origins of its
history, in the period of the greatest Spanish-Aquitanian rapprochement,
Provençal lyric poetry experienced Andalusian influence, which resulted in the
conception of service to love, distinct from knightly service because the
latter required reciprocity from the lord toward the vassal. Service to love
meant that the lover meekly gives himself over to the power of his despotic
lady and praises her cruelty. Thanks to Andalusian influence, the troubadours
began to understand love in a new way, as unappeasable desire and sweet
torment. This same influence led them to use the stanza with a unison reprise.
Thus the Provençal song could be born only at the courts of southern France,
but it could not have come into the world without the important influence of
Arab-Andalusian lyric.

Later, Provençal lyric poetry increasingly acquires national traits and
independence, and matures to the level at which it itself turns out to be a
cultural "translator." Other cultural currents are also flowing: epic poetry
penetrates from France; through Sicily, and indirectly through Provence, comes
Spanish-Arab culture. Finally, there is the never-ceasing influence, even
though it sometimes grows faint in Italy, of the antique "soil." The cultural
influence of the Greek-Byzantine tradition does not pass by either. Thus, if
one judges by cultural production, this period may appear, as it often does, to
be a time of decline. But from another side, it is a period of exceptionally
powerful saturation. At the crossroads of numerous cultural streams, the most
ancient and the newest, Italy absorbs diverse flows of texts. In its cultural
space, texts collide with one another and form a whole that cries out with
contradictions.

At the next stage, this inevitably gives rise to a cultural activity completely
unheard of in the history of world civilization. Over the course of several
centuries, Italy turns out to be a real volcano, spewing forth the most diverse
texts and filling the cultural oecumene of the West. The Renaissance and, in
part, the Baroque can be called the "Italian period" of European culture: the
Italian language becomes the language of courts and dandies, of fashion and
diplomacy. It is used in ladies' alcoves and in cardinals' offices. Italy
supplies Europe with painters and actors, bankers, jewelers, lawyers,
cardinals, and favorites of queens. The power of this incursion can be judged
by the force of the anti-Italian moods that flare up in England, Germany, and
France, and by the energy with which an Italianized European culture will try,
in place of one alien face, to acquire many "native" national faces.

What the Renaissance did with Italian culture, the Enlightenment did with
French culture. France absorbed cultural currents from all of Europe, from the
Reformation teachings of Holland, Germany, and Switzerland to the empiricism of
Bacon and Locke and the celestial mechanics of Newton, while also assimilating
the Latinism of the Italian humanists and the mannerism of the Spanish and
Italian Baroque. In the age of the Enlightenment, it made the whole of
enlightened Europe speak its language. Just as in the Renaissance every
cultural current faced a hard choice, to be a supporter or an enemy of
humanism and the cult of antiquity, which was equated with the spirit of
Italian culture, so in the 18th century one could only be an adherent or an
opponent of religious tolerance, the cult of Nature and Reason, and the
destruction of age-old prejudices in the name of the freedom of Man. Paris
became the capital of European thought, and the number of texts that poured
from France into every corner of Europe simply cannot be counted. It is enough
to compare this moment with the pause that came after Madame de Staël's *On
Germany*, when France "switched to reception," discovering English culture from
Shakespeare to Byron, and German culture, Schiller, Goethe, and the romantics,
the whole of "northern Europe," from Kant to Walter Scott.

From the point of view of the "receiving" side, the process of reception is
divided into the following stages.

First, texts arriving from outside preserve the appearance of "foreign" texts.
They are perceived in a foreign language, both in the sense of natural language
and in the broadly semiotic sense. In the receiving culture they occupy the
highest value position in the hierarchy: truth, beauty, divine origin, and so
on are attributed to them. The foreign language becomes a sign of belonging to
"culture," to the elite, to higher dignity. Correspondingly, texts that had
previously existed in the "one's own" language, as well as this language
itself, receive a lower evaluation: untruth, "coarseness," and "lack of
culture" are attributed to them.

Second, both principles, the "imported" texts and "one's own" culture, mutually
restructure one another. Translations, reworkings, and adaptations multiply. At
the same time, the codes imported together with the texts are built into the
metacultural sphere.

If at the first stage the dominant psychological tendency was a break with the
past, the idealization of the "new," that is, of the worldview received from
outside, and the desire to tear oneself away from tradition, so that the "new"
was experienced as saving, then now the dominant pull is toward the restoration
of the interrupted path. "Roots" are sought. The "new" is interpreted as
organically flowing from the old, which is thereby rehabilitated. Ideas of
organic development gain the upper hand.

Third, there appears a striving to separate some higher content of the
assimilated worldview from the concrete national culture in whose texts it was
imported. A conception takes shape that "there" these ideas were realized in an
"untrue," clouded, and distorted form, and that precisely "here," in the bosom
of the culture that received them, they are in their true, "natural"
environment. A dislike for the culture that originally transmitted these texts
is cultivated, and their truly national nature is emphasized.

Fourth, the provoking texts are completely dissolved in the cultural thickness
of the receiving culture, while that culture itself enters a state of
excitation and begins vigorously to generate new texts, based on cultural codes
that in the distant past were stimulated by an external incursion, but have
already been completely transformed through a series of asymmetrical
transformations into a new and original structural model.

Fifth, the receiving culture, into whose space the general center of the
semiosphere has moved, passes into the position of a transmitting culture and
itself becomes the source of a stream of texts directed toward other regions of
the semiosphere, peripheral from its point of view.

Of course, in the real process of cultural contacts this schematically outlined
cycle may be realized far from fully. And, in general, favorable historical,
social, and psychological conditions are needed for its realization. The
process of "infection" must be prepared by a series of external conditions and
must be felt as necessary and desired. Like every dialogue, a situation of
mutual attraction toward contact must precede the contact itself.

In Russian culture these conditions were present, and this makes it possible
to trace the main stages of the cycle with greatest clarity.

Russian culture lived through two periods when it was tuned "to reception,"
absorbing a stream of texts that came to it from outside.

The first cycle falls at the time of the baptism of Rus' in 988. It is
accompanied by a very intensive stream of texts in Greek and in the artificial
common Slavic book language, created by Cyril and Methodius and known as Church
Slavonic, from countries that had already adopted Christianity earlier,
including Bulgaria and Serbia. No later than the 11th century, translation
activity unfolds in Rus'. But in parallel with the assimilation of texts there
develops a desire to separate the Christian faith from Greek influence. This
affects the text of the Kievan chronicle, into which a series of legends is
introduced that are meant to lessen the role of the Greeks in Rus' adoption of
Christianity. After the capture of Constantinople by the Turks, a suspicious
attitude toward the purity of the Greeks' faith is definitively established in
Rus', and Christianity is irrevocably affirmed as the "Russian faith." Along
the way, Russian Christian culture develops with exceptional speed.

Because of a series of historical causes, this cycle did not reach its final
stage. The giant dialogue between Russian and Western cultures, which unfolded
over the 18th to 20th centuries, was realized much more fully.

A new page in the relation of Russian culture to European culture coincided
with the beginning of the 18th century. Without going deeply into the question
of how far the psychological self-evaluation of people of that time coincided
with later evaluations by historians, let us note only that they themselves
firmly and repeatedly called themselves "new people." This self-evaluation had
as it were two sides. At first the "new people" were perceived as the antithesis
of the "old people" of pre-Petrine Rus'. The conflict was thought of as an age
conflict: the new people were young Russian Europeans, supporters of
enlightenment and new customs, while the "old" were defenders of antiquity,
people "rooted in custom," bearers of tradition.

Later, however, the accents shift, and the "new Europeans" are opposed to the
"old Europeans." Young Russian civilization is opposed to old Western European
civilization as one capable of carrying out what the West conceived but did not
carry out. It is significant that the same reorientation of meaning in the word
"new" is also found in Russian texts of the 11th century. When the Kievan
chronicler, describing the baptism of Rus', cites the apostle Paul, "Old things
have passed away, and behold, all things have become new," he obviously
understands by the old, or the "ancient," pagan Rus'. But when in the 11th
century Metropolitan Hilarion, the first Russian metropolitan appointed by
Prince Yaroslav in place of a Greek, compares the Greeks with the Old Testament
and Russian Christians with the New, that is, with the Gospel, it is obvious
that newness is filled with a special meaning: the pupil is not worse than the
teacher, but better.

As with the adoption of Christianity, Peter's reform is accompanied by a sharp
and demonstrative break with tradition. When Peter, insulting the religious
feelings of Muscovites, orders a "sinful" theater building to be constructed on
Red Square, a holy place, he acts in the same way as Saint Vladimir, who ordered
the statue of Perun to be tied to horses' tails.

The exceptionally strong effect of the Enlightenment in general, and of
Rousseau in particular, on Russian culture should be attributed in part to this
same striving for a cardinal break with the past, if one leaves aside social
motivations. Rousseau's decisive denial of history and his opposition to it of
a theory grounded in Nature and its unchanging principles, his denial of the
whole preceding cultural tradition, and his opposition of the spoiled cultural
elite to the moral health of the people formed the basis of Rousseau's
popularity in Russia. The cult of Rousseau, which arose in Russia in the last
third of the 18th century, continued to exist even at a time when in France he
was remembered only by historians.

Because French had become universally widespread in the educated circles of
Russian society, reading the works of French writers was possible in the
original. Translations were a sign of recognition, of inclusion in the Russian
literary situation. But since, after Peter, Western influence was perceived as
connected with civilization, and civilization received a negative evaluation,
the growth of Rousseau's influence paradoxically merged with anti-French moods,
with the struggle against Gallomania and European influence. The attitude
toward France was extraordinarily complex, especially in the period of the
Revolution and the Napoleonic wars, and of the patriotic upsurge connected with
them. Here several tendencies collided: an enthusiastic attitude toward the
ideas of the Revolution; a liberal desire to separate the figures of the
Enlightenment from the Jacobins; and a conservative-nationalist tendency to
declare the French Revolution the inevitable consequence of all French culture.

Against this background, however, one more tendency made itself felt. The
Rousseauist model received the following interpretation: French culture, and
especially its shadow, the French education of the Russian nobility, which had
forgotten the native language, the Orthodox faith, folk clothing, and Russian
culture, is precisely the destructive civilization against which Rousseau
rebels. It is opposed by the natural life of the Russian peasant, the bearer of
healthy morality and natural virtues. The basic structural opposition of the
Enlightenment, "Nature - prejudice," "natural - perverted," began to receive a
specific interpretation. The natural was identified with the national. In the
Russian peasant, the *muzhik*, people saw the "man of Nature"; in the Russian
language, a natural language created by Natura herself. France, by contrast,
came to be seen as the fatherland of all prejudices, the kingdom of fashion and
"fashionable ideas." Russian society infected with Gallomania, a society of
"reborn Slavs," also appears effeminate and contaminated. It is opposed by the
people, in whom direct heirs of the heroic and primitive life of Homeric-Hellenic
culture are seen. Such conceptions would nourish Gnedich, the translator of
the *Iliad* and an admirer of Rousseau and Schiller; they would be characteristic
of Griboedov and of the circle of young writers whom Tynyanov called the "young
archaists"; and finally, they would influence Gogol.

Of course, this is only one of the currents in the general movement of texts
from the West into Russia. In the interval between Pushkin and Chekhov, Russian
culture becomes transmitting, with the work of Tolstoy and Dostoevsky as the
peak, and the stream of texts turns in the opposite direction.

Of course, the picture we have drawn is schematic in the extreme. In reality,
the circulation of texts proceeds continuously in different directions; large
and small streams cross and add together their effects. At the same time, texts
are transmitted not by one center of the semiosphere but by many, and the
semiosphere itself is mobile in its boundaries. Finally, the same processes
also occur at other levels: periods of the aggression of poetry into prose are
replaced by the aggression of prose into poetry; the mutual tension of drama
and the novel, written and oral culture, elite and mass culture, creates
multidirectional currents of texts. In different aspects, the same centers of
the semiosphere can be simultaneously active and "receiving"; the same spaces
of the semiosphere can be centers in some relations and
periphery in others. Attractions provoke repulsions; borrowings provoke
originality.

The space of culture, the semiosphere, is not something that acts along
predetermined and easily calculable paths. It boils like the Sun, and, as on
the Sun, centers of excitation change places within it. Activity flares up now
in unknown depths, now on the surface, and irradiates energy into relatively
calm spheres. The result of this continuous boiling is the release of colossal
energy. But the energy released by the semiosphere is the energy of information,
the energy of Thought.
