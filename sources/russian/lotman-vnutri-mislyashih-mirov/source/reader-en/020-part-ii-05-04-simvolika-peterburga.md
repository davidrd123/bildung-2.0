## Symbolic Spaces

## 4. The Symbolism Of Petersburg

In the system of symbols worked out by the history of culture, the city holds
a special place. Two basic spheres of urban semiotics should be distinguished:
the city as space and the city as name. The second aspect, the city as name,
was examined by Lotman and Boris Uspensky in their work on echoes of the
"Moscow as the Third Rome" conception in the ideology of Peter I, and will not
be considered here.

The city as a closed space can stand in a twofold relation to the surrounding
land. It can be not only isomorphic with the state, but can personify it, can
be the state in a certain ideal sense. Thus Rome-city is at the same time
Rome-world. But the city can also be the state's antithesis. City and country,
*urbs* and *orbis terrarum*, can be perceived as hostile entities. In this
second case one recalls Genesis, where Cain is named as the first builder of a
city and the first to give a city a name.

When the city relates to the surrounding world as a temple located at the
center of a city relates to the city itself, that is, when the city is an
idealized model of the universe, it is as a rule located at the center of the
Earth. More precisely, wherever it is located, it is assigned a central
position and is considered a center. Jerusalem, Rome, and Moscow appear in
different texts precisely as centers of certain worlds. As the ideal
embodiment of its own land, such a city can at the same time appear as the
prototype of the heavenly city and as a shrine for surrounding lands.

But the City can also be located eccentrically in relation to the land
correlated with it, placed outside its limits. Sviatoslav transferred his
capital to Pereiaslavets on the Danube, and Charlemagne transferred his capital
from Ingelheim to Aachen. Such transfers have a palpable semiotic aspect. Above
all, the existential code is sharpened: what exists is declared nonexistent,
and what has yet to appear is declared the only thing truly existing. When
Sviatoslav says that Pereiaslavets on the Danube lies at the center of his
land, he has in mind a state still to be created, while the actually existing
Kievan land is treated as if it did not exist.

At the same time, valuation sharply increases. The existing, marked by the
present and by "one's own," is evaluated negatively, while what is to appear in
the future and what is "alien" receive high axiological value. One can also
note that "concentric" structures tend toward closedness and toward separation
from an environment evaluated as hostile, while eccentric structures tend
toward openness and cultural contacts.

The concentric position of the city in semiotic space is usually connected
with the image of a city on a hill or on hills. Such a city acts as mediator
between earth and heaven; myths of origin gather around it, and gods usually
take part in its foundation. It has a beginning but no end: it is the "eternal
city," *Roma aeterna*.

The eccentric city is located "on the edge" of cultural space: on the seashore,
at the mouth of a river. Here the active opposition is not earth/heaven, but
natural/artificial. This is a city created against Nature and locked in
struggle with it, which gives a double possibility of interpretation: as the
victory of reason over the elements, on one side, and as the perversion of the
natural order, on the other. Around the name of such a city eschatological
myths, prophecies of destruction, and ideas of doom and the triumph of the
elements gather. As a rule this means flood, submersion at the bottom of the
sea. The prophecy of Methodius of Patara gives such a fate to Constantinople,
which persistently plays the role of the "non-eternal Rome."

This version of the eschatological legend entered Petersburg mythology in a
stable form. Not only the plot of flood, supported by periodic floods and
generating a large literature, but also a detail, the tip of the Alexander
Column or the angel of the Peter and Paul Fortress sticking up above the waves
and serving as a mooring place for ships, makes one suppose a direct
reorientation from Constantinople to Petersburg. Sollogub recalled that
Lermontov liked to draw the raging sea with the end of the Alexander Column and
its crowning angel rising above it. Similar motifs appear in poems and tales of
the underwater city.

The eternal struggle between element and culture, built into the idea of the
doomed city, is realized in the Petersburg myth as the antithesis of water and
stone. But this stone is not "natural" or "wild," not unworked rock standing
from time immemorial in its place. It is brought, shaped, and humanized,
cultivated. Petersburg stone is an artifact, not a phenomenon of nature.
Therefore stone, rock, and cliff in the Petersburg myth are endowed not with
their usual traits of immobility, stability, and ability to withstand wind and
water, but with the unnatural trait of movability.

The motif of the moving immovable is only part of the general picture of a
perverse world, in which stone floats over water. Attention is directed
precisely at metamorphosis, the moment when the "normal" world turns into an
"inverted" one. The natural semantics of stone and cliff would be something
like Tyutchev's image of the sea and the cliff: calm, proud, unmoved,
unchanging, contemporary with the universe. In Tyutchev's symbolism the cliff
is Russia, which for him is more an antonym than a synonym of Petersburg.

Petersburg stone is stone on water, on a swamp, stone without support, not
coeval with the universe but laid down by a human being. In the "Petersburg
picture," water and stone change places. Water is eternal. It existed before
stone and will defeat it. Stone is marked by temporariness and spectrality.
Water destroys it.

The situation of an "inverted world," which inscribes this model of the city
in a broad current of European culture from the 16th to the early 18th
century, ultimately connected with the Baroque tradition, in principle
contained the possibility of opposite evaluations by its audience. The inverted
world in the Baroque tradition, intersecting with folk-carnival tradition, can
be perceived as utopia, a Cockaigne, a world turned upside down. But it can
also take on the sinister outlines of the world of Bruegel and Bosch.

At the same time Petersburg is identified with Rome. By the beginning of the
19th century, this idea becomes widespread. The joining in the image of
Petersburg of two archetypes, "eternal Rome" and "non-eternal, doomed Rome,"
or Constantinople, creates the double perspective characteristic of the
cultural interpretation of Petersburg: eternity and doom simultaneously.

Because Petersburg is, by its initial semiotic assignment, inscribed in this
double situation, it can be interpreted at the same time as "paradise," the
utopia of the ideal city of the future, the embodiment of Reason, and as the
sinister masquerade of Antichrist. In both cases there is extreme idealization,
but with opposite signs. The possibility of double reading in the Petersburg
myth is illustrated by one example. In the tradition of Baroque symbolism, the
snake under the hoof of Falconet's rider is a conventional allegory of envy,
hostility, and obstacles raised against Peter by foreign enemies and internal
opponents of reform. But in the context of the prophecies of Methodius of
Patara, well known to the Russian audience, the same image receives another,
ominous reading.

In that context horse, rider, and snake no longer oppose one another, but
together make up details of a sign of the end of the world. The snake, from a
secondary symbol, becomes the chief figure of the group. It is no accident
that in the cultural tradition generated by Falconet's monument, the snake will
be given a role the sculptor probably did not foresee.

The ideal artificial city, created as the realization of a rationalist utopia,
was supposed to be without history, since the rationality of the "regular
state" meant the negation of historically formed structures. This implied
building the city on a new site and, accordingly, destroying everything "old"
if anything old was present there. The idea, under Catherine II, of creating an
ideal city on the site of historical Tver arose after the fire of 1763 had
essentially destroyed the city. From the point of view of the intended utopia,
such a fire could be considered a fortunate circumstance.

But the presence of history is an indispensable condition of a functioning
semiotic system. In this respect, a city created "all at once," by a demiurge's
wave of the hand, having no history and subordinated to a single plan, is in
principle unrealizable.

The city, as a complex semiotic mechanism and generator of culture, can perform
this function only because it is a cauldron of texts and codes, differently
structured and heterogeneous, belonging to different languages and different
levels. It is precisely the principled semiotic polyglotism of any city that
makes it the field of diverse semiotic collisions impossible under other
conditions. By bringing together different national, social, and stylistic
codes and texts, the city carries out diverse hybridizations, recodings, and
semiotic translations, which turn it into a powerful generator of new
information.

The source of such semiotic collisions is not only the synchronic juxtaposition
of heterogeneous semiotic formations, but also diachrony: architectural
structures, urban rites and ceremonies, the very plan of the city, street
names, and thousands of other relics of past epochs act as code programs,
constantly regenerating texts of the historical past. The city is a mechanism
that continually gives birth anew to its past, which can then be juxtaposed
with the present as if synchronically. In this respect the city, like culture,
is a mechanism that resists time.

The rationalist utopian city was deprived of these semiotic reserves. The
consequences would probably have discouraged an 18th-century rationalist
enlightener: the absence of history called forth an intense growth of
mythology. Myth filled the semiotic emptiness, and the situation of the
artificial city proved exceptionally mythogenic.

Petersburg is exceptionally typical in this respect. The history of Petersburg
is inseparable from Petersburg mythology, and the word "mythology" here is not
a metaphor. Long before 19th-century Russian literature, from Pushkin and Gogol
to Dostoevsky, made Petersburg mythology a fact of national culture, the real
history of Petersburg was already permeated with mythological elements. If we
do not identify the history of the city with official departmental history,
reflected in bureaucratic correspondence, but perceive it in connection with
the life of the mass of the population, then the enormous role of rumors, oral
stories about extraordinary events, and specific urban folklore is immediately
striking. The first collector of this folklore was the Secret Chancellery.
Pushkin apparently intended his diary of 1833-1835 to become a kind of archive
of urban rumors; Delvig collected "frightful stories"; Dobrolyubov, in the
manuscript newspaper of his student years, theoretically grounded the role of
the oral element in popular life.

One feature of "Petersburg mythology" is that the feeling of Petersburg's
specificity enters into its self-consciousness. It implies the presence of some
external, non-Petersburg observer. This may be the "view from Europe" or the
"view from Russia," that is, the "view from Moscow." What remains constant is
that the culture constructs the position of an external observer upon itself.
At the same time the opposite point of view is formed: from Petersburg onto
Europe or onto Russia, that is, onto Moscow. Accordingly, Petersburg will be
perceived either as "Asia in Europe" or as "Europe in Russia." Both
interpretations converge in affirming the inorganic, artificial character of
Petersburg culture.

It is important to note that the consciousness of "artificiality" is first a
feature of Petersburg culture's self-evaluation and only later moves beyond
it, becoming the property of concepts alien to it. Connected with this are
features constantly emphasized in the Petersburg "picture of the world," such
as spectrality and theatricality. It might seem that the medieval tradition of
visions and prophecies would have been more organically connected with
traditional Russian Moscow than with "rationalist" and "European" Petersburg.
Yet it is precisely in the Petersburg atmosphere that this tradition receives
its most palpable continuation.

The idea of spectrality is very clearly expressed in the stylized legend of
Petersburg's foundation that Odoevsky placed in the mouth of an old Finn. The
builders lay stone after stone, but the swamp swallows everything. Peter builds
a ship, looks around, and sees no city. Saying that his people know how to do
nothing, he begins raising rock after rock and forging them in the air. Thus he
builds a whole city and lowers it onto the ground. This story of course
characterizes not Finnish folklore, but the conception of Petersburg in the
circle of Petersburg writers of the 1830s close to Pushkin, to which Odoevsky
belonged. A city forged in the air, with no foundation beneath it, had to be
regarded as a spectral, phantasmagoric space.

Study of the material shows that in the oral literature of the Petersburg
salon, a genre that flourished in the first third of the 19th century and
played a significant historical-literary role, a special place belonged to the
telling of frightening and fantastic stories with an obligatory "Petersburg
color." The roots of this genre reach back into the 18th century. It includes
faith in the authenticity of the event, the appearance of Peter I's ghost,
tragic prophecies, and the Bronze Horseman as a characteristic mark of
Petersburg space.

A typical story of this genre is E. G. Levasheva's account of Delvig's
posthumous visit. According to the story, her husband and Delvig once made a
simple salon agreement that whichever of them died first would appear to the
other after death. Years later, Delvig died, and exactly one year after his
death, at midnight, he silently appeared in Levashev's study, sat down in an
armchair, and then left without saying a word.

Lotman is interested in this story as a fact of Petersburg "salon folklore,"
apparently not accidentally connected with Delvig. Delvig cultivated the oral
"frightful" Petersburg tale. It is indicative that Pushkin and Titov's *The
Secluded House on Vasilievsky Island* is tied by definite threads to the
atmosphere of Delvig's circle. The "Petersburg mythology" of Gogol and
Dostoevsky relied on the tradition of oral Petersburg literature, canonized it,
and, alongside the oral tradition of anecdote, introduced it into the world of
high literature.

The whole mass of texts of "oral literature" from the 1820s and 1830s compels
one to perceive Petersburg as a space in which the mysterious and fantastic are
lawful. The Petersburg tale is related to the Christmas tale, but temporal
fantasticness is replaced in it by spatial fantasticness.

Another feature of Petersburg spatiality is its theatricality. The very nature
of Petersburg architecture, the unique consistency of enormous ensembles not
breaking apart, as in cities with long histories, into areas of building from
different periods, creates the feeling of scenery. This strikes both the
foreigner and the Muscovite. The Muscovite considers it a sign of
"Europeanism." The European, accustomed to the neighborhood of Romanesque and
Baroque, Gothic and classicism, and to architectural mixture, observes with
astonishment the distinctive and strange beauty of Petersburg's huge ensembles.
Custine wrote that Peter the Great and his successors understood their capital
as theater.

The theatricality of Petersburg space appears in the clear division between
its "stage" and "backstage" parts, in the constant consciousness of the
presence of a spectator, and, what is especially important, in the replacement
of existence by "as-if existence." The spectator is constantly present, but for
the participants in the staged action the spectator exists "as if"; to notice
the spectator's presence would be to violate the rules of the game. Likewise,
the whole backstage space does not exist from the point of view of the stage.
From the point of view of stage space, only stage being is real; from the point
of view of backstage space, it is play and convention.

The feeling of a spectator-observer who should not be noticed accompanies all
the ritual ceremonies filling the routine of the "military capital." The
soldier as actor is constantly on view, but is separated from those who observe
the parade, inspection, or any other ceremony by a wall transparent only in one
direction: he is seen and exists for the observers, but they are invisible and
do not exist for him. The emperor is no exception. Custine describes Nicholas I
as always posing, always playing the emperor, having many masks but no face: if
one seeks the human being, one always finds the emperor.

The need for an auditorium is a semiotic parallel to what eccentric spatial
position gives geographically. Petersburg has no point of view on itself; it is
forced constantly to construct a spectator. In this sense both Westernizers and
Slavophiles are equally creations of Petersburg culture. It is characteristic
that in Russia a Westernizer may exist who has never been to the West, knows no
languages, and is not even interested in the real West. For the Westernizer,
the West is an ideal point of view, not a cultural-geographical reality. But
this reconstructed "point of view" possesses a higher reality in relation to
the actual life observed from its position. Saltykov-Shchedrin's formula that
they lived physically in Russia, or more specifically in Petersburg, but
spiritually in France did not exclude, but implied, that collision with real
Western life often became tragic and turned the "Westernizer" into a critic of
the West. Conversely, Slavophiles who studied abroad, attended lectures by
Schelling and Hegel, or had to hire university professors to learn to speak
Russian, just as conditionally constructed Rus' for themselves as the necessary
point of view on the real world of post-Petrine Europeanized civilization.

The constant oscillation between the reality of the spectator and the reality
of the stage, with each of these realities appearing illusory from the point of
view of the other, produces the Petersburg effect of theatricality. The second
side of it is the relation between stage space and backstage space. The spatial
antithesis between Nevsky Prospect, together with the whole ceremonial,
palatial part of Petersburg, and Kolomna, Vasilievsky Island, and the outskirts
was interpreted in literature as a mutual relation of nonexistence. Each of the
two Petersburg "stages" had its own myth, realized in stories and anecdotes
and tied to definite localities. There was the Petersburg of Peter the Great,
who plays the role of the patron deity of "his" Petersburg, or as *deus
implicitus* invisibly present in his creation. And there was the Petersburg of
the official, the poor man, the "person outside citizenship of the capital," in
Gogol's phrase. Each of these characters had "his own" streets, districts, and
spaces. A natural result was the emergence of plots in which these two
characters, through extraordinary circumstances, somehow collided.

Lotman gives as an example Krylov's story about being summoned to the head of
the gendarmes after censoring Lachinova's satirical novel *Tricks in the
Caucasus*. The episode is embedded in rumors that the head of the gendarmes had
a chair in his office that lowered the seated person halfway through a trapdoor
so that hidden executioners, not seeing whom they were punishing, could flog
him. Riding past the monument to Peter the Great on St. Isaac's Square,
Dubelt, wrapped in his coat, says as if to himself that this is the man who
ought to be flogged: Peter the Great, for his foolish trick of building
Petersburg on a swamp. Krylov suspects a trap and remains silent. Later, when
Orlov invites him to sit, Krylov perches carefully on the edge of the chair,
terrified that the seat will drop.

Krylov's story is interesting in many respects. Although it is a report by a
participant in a real event, it is already compositionally organized and
halfway to becoming an urban anecdote. The point of the story is that Peter I
and the official meet in it as equals, and the Third Section must choose which
one should be flogged. The question is discussed in the place traditional for
such reflections: Senate Square. At the same time, ritual flogging of the
statue is not merely a form of condemnation of Peter, but a typical pagan
magical action against a deity behaving "incorrectly." In this respect,
Petersburg anecdotes about sacrilegious actions toward the monument to Peter,
like every sacrilege, are a form of worship.

"Petersburg mythology" developed against the background of other, deeper
layers of urban semiotics. Petersburg was conceived as Russia's seaport, a
Russian Amsterdam, and the parallel with Venice was also stable. At the same
time it was to be a "military capital," a residence, the governmental center of
the country, and even, as analysis shows, a New Rome with the imperial claims
that followed from this. Yet all these layers of practical and symbolic
functions contradicted one another and were often incompatible. The foundation
of a city that would functionally replace the Novgorod destroyed by Ivan IV,
restore the cultural balance between two historical centers traditional for
Rus', and restore traditional ties with Western Europe was necessary. Such a
city should have been an economic center and a place of meeting for different
cultural languages. Semiotic polyglotism is the law for a city of this type.
Meanwhile, the ideal of the "military capital" demanded one-planedness and
strict consistency within a single system of semiotics. From this point of
view, every falling away from that system could appear only as a dangerous
violation of order. The first type of city always tends toward the
"incorrectness" and contradictoriness of the artistic text; the second tends
toward the normative "correctness" of a metalanguage.

The struggle between Petersburg as artistic text and Petersburg as metalanguage
fills the whole semiotic history of the city. The ideal model fought for real
embodiment. It is no accident that Petersburg appeared to Custine as a military
camp in which palaces had replaced tents. Yet this tendency met stubborn and
successful resistance: life filled the city with noble mansions in which an
independent private cultural life took place, and with the intelligentsia of
mixed social rank, with its own distinctive cultural tradition rooted in the
spiritual milieu. The transfer of the residence to Petersburg, not at all
necessary for the role of Russian Amsterdam, complicated the contradictions
still more. As capital, symbolic center of Russia, and New Rome, Petersburg had
to be the country's emblem and expression; but as a residence endowed with the
features of anti-Moscow, it could only be Russia's antithesis. The complex
interweaving of "one's own" and "alien" in Petersburg's semiotics left its
mark on the self-evaluation of the entire culture of the period.

As historical development proceeded, Petersburg increasingly moved away from
the intended ideal of the rationalist capital of the "regular state," a city
organized by regulations and without history. It grew over with history,
acquired a complex topocultural structure, supported by the many-estate and
multiethnic composition of its population. The life of the city became
exceptionally complex very quickly. The motley character of Petersburg already
frightened Paul. The city ceased to be an island in the empire, and Paul
decided to make an island within Petersburg. Analogously, Maria Feodorovna
tried to transfer a small piece of cozy Montbeliard to Pavlovsk. By the 1830s,
Petersburg had become a city of cultural-semiotic contrasts, and this served
as soil for an exceptionally intensive intellectual life. By the number of
texts, codes, connections, and associations, and by the volume of cultural
memory accumulated in the historically negligible period of its existence,
Petersburg can rightly be considered a unique object in which semiotic models
were embodied in architectural and geographical reality.
