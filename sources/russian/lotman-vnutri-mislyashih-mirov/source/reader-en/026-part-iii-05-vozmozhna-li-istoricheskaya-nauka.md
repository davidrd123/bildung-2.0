## Is Historical Science Possible, And What Is Its Function In The System Of Culture?

We have considered the difficulties that stand in the way of historical
science as it strives toward its original goal: to restore "how it actually
was." The question naturally arises: is history possible as a science, or does
it represent some entirely different kind of knowledge? This question, as is
well known, is not new. It is enough to recall the doubts that tormented
Benedetto Croce on this point.

Let us permit ourselves the assertion that the difficulties confronting
historical science are distinctive but not unique. They are distinctive because
they are the difficulties of historical science specifically. They are not
unique because they modify certain general scientific methodological problems
of the contemporary stage. In different fields of science, one and the same
problem is becoming actual: the problem of language, the interaction between
the metalanguage of description and the object described.

Science has moved from a naive world, in which habitual modes of perception
and generalization were credited with certainty, and the problem of the
observer's position in relation to the described world worried almost no one,
into a world of relativity. It has moved from a world in which the scholar
considered reality "from the position of truth" into one in which questions of
language concern every science. In essence, the matter is this: science, in
the form in which it took shape after the Renaissance, taking as its foundation
the ideas of Descartes and Newton, proceeded from the assumption that the
scientist is an external observer, looking at the object from outside, and
therefore possesses absolute "objective" knowledge. Modern science in its
various spheres, from nuclear physics to linguistics, sees the scientist as
inside the world being described and as part of that world. But object and
observer are, as a rule, described in different languages.

Consequently, the problem of translation arises as a universal scientific
task. When Plato defined thought as "the dialogue of the soul with itself," he
proceeded from the idea that the conversation is conducted in one language.
Werner Heisenberg formulated the problem in relation to physics: quantum
mechanics set even more serious demands. It became necessary to abandon
objective description of nature in the Newtonian sense, where definite values
are assigned to the basic characteristics of a system, such as position,
velocity, and energy. In its place came the description of situations of
observation, for which only the probabilities of particular results can be
defined. The very words used to describe phenomena at the atomic level thus
became problematic. One could speak of waves or particles while remembering
that the issue is not a dualistic description but a unified one. The meaning of
old words lost some of its clarity.

Historical science is going through this period as well. And as in other areas
of knowledge, it naturally produces a state of shock. As always at the border
of great epochs, the old question arises again: what is truth? The transition
from naive faith in an "absolute point of view" to historical semiotics poses
the problem of translating the language of the source into the language of the
researcher. The meaning of this problem will become clear if we compare it
with the fullest results of the preceding development of the methodology of
historical knowledge. Let us refer to R. G. Collingwood's *The Idea of
History*. Having analyzed the difficulties that arise in historical
interpretation of facts, Collingwood proposes that the historian fully identify
his consciousness with the historical person.

Thus, when a historian of politics or military affairs encounters a description
of certain actions by Julius Caesar, the historian tries to understand them,
that is, to determine what thoughts in Caesar's consciousness caused him to
carry out those actions. This presupposes a mental transfer of oneself into
the situation in which Caesar found himself, and a reproduction in one's own
thinking of what Caesar thought about that situation and about the possible
ways of resolving it.

Collingwood insists on this thought and returns to it repeatedly. Suppose, for
example, that a historian reads the Theodosian Code and has before him an edict
of the emperor. Simply reading the words and being able to translate them is
not yet the same as understanding their historical meaning. In order to assess
it, the historian must imagine the situation the emperor was trying to resolve
and imagine how that situation appeared to the emperor. Then the historian must
put himself in the emperor's place and decide how one should act in such
circumstances. He must establish the possible alternative ways of resolving the
situation and the reasons for choosing one of them. In this way, the historian
must reproduce within himself the whole process of decision-making on this
question. By this path he reproduces in his own consciousness the emperor's
experience, and only to the degree that he succeeds in this does he receive the
historical, and not merely philological, meaning of the edict.

Behind these arguments stands the conviction that the semiotic, and therefore
psychological, world of a twentieth-century Englishman and the world of Caesar
or Theodosius are identical, and that to reincarnate oneself as Caesar or
Theodosius it is enough to possess imagination and intuition refined by work
with sources. Of course these qualities can help the historian greatly. But
they cannot cover over the fact that the concepts of "situation" and "solution"
may diverge very far between the historian and the historian's hero. The
natural decision for a person of one epoch may not fit into the consciousness
of another century even as an abstract possibility. The concept of "alternative
methods" may arise only as the result of describing "the world of Theodosius,"
not from the psychological experience that seems "natural" to the researcher.
It is not an initial axiom but the result of a scientific path.

Collingwood's reasoning remains within the limits of common sense, that true
demiurge of the post-Cartesian world at whose center stood "truth clear as the
sun." Descartes expressed this faith in common sense in the second part of his
*Discourse on Method*. Book learning, at least sciences whose claims are only
probable and lack practical demonstrations, sciences that gradually took shape
and developed from the opinions of different people, are by no means as close
to truth as the simple judgments that naturally take shape in a person of good
sense.

Collingwood proposes to remove the antinomy between "the world of Theodosius"
and "the world of the historian" by fully identifying them. The path of
semiotics is the opposite. It implies the maximum exposure of the differences
in their structures, the description of these differences, and the treatment of
understanding as translation from one language into another. Not the removal of
the researcher from the research, which is practically impossible, but the
awareness of the researcher's presence and the maximum accounting for how this
must affect the description.

Therefore, to the extent that the instrument of semiotic research is
translation, the instrument of historical-cultural study must be typology, with
mandatory accounting for the historian and for the type of culture to which the
historian himself belongs.

A characteristic error can serve as an example. In one of his works,
Levy-Bruhl reports the case of a Kaffir to whom a missionary proposed sending
his son to the missionary school. Wishing to avoid a direct refusal, the Kaffir
answered the missionary: "I will see it in a dream." Levy-Bruhl remarks that
we have here a situation in which every European would answer, "I will think
about it," and concludes that for the Kaffir the dream performs the same
function that thinking performs "for us." In reality, the Kaffir's words
should apparently be translated as "I will consult about it," since the matter
concerns receiving an omen in a dream, the same divination that is a natural
consequence of the desire to receive the most authoritative information, and is
no different from the European phrase: "I will consult my lawyer, or my
doctor, or my computer." If we remove a slight shade of European arrogance,
what remains is a widespread tendency that is by no means the monopoly of
"pre-logical thinking," and that Kant already indicated when he opposed
Enlightenment to immaturity.

The historian and history are located within a single space of human culture,
but in principle they speak different languages, and their relations are
asymmetric. From this follows another function of history in culture. History
is often called the memory of humanity, but this formulation is rarely thought
through. If the function of history is the attempt to "present the past as it
actually was" - an old formula, but one that in essence expresses every
historian's striving - then memory is an instrument of thinking in the present,
although its content is the past. Put another way, the content of memory is the
past, but without it thinking "now" and "here" is impossible. It is the deep
foundation of the actual process of consciousness. If history is the memory of
culture, this means that it is not only a trace of the past, but also an active
mechanism of the present.

If one needs a metaphor for the capacity of memory, perhaps the least suitable
image would be a library with books arranged on shelves, or even a computer
whose memory contains certain data, however large their number may be. Memory
is more likely to be imagined as a generator that reproduces the past anew, a
capacity that, as a result of certain impulses, switches on the generation of a
thinkable reality that consciousness transfers into the past. This capacity is
part of the general process of thinking and is inseparable from it.

The relation between cultural memory and cultural self-reflection is built as a
constant dialogue. Certain texts from chronologically earlier layers are brought
into culture. Interacting with its contemporary mechanisms, they generate an
image of the historical past. This image is then transferred by culture into
the past and, already as an equal participant in dialogue, affects the present.
But in the light of the transformed present, the past too changes its
appearance. This process does not unfold in a vacuum. Both participants in the
dialogue are also partners in other collisions. Both are open to intrusion from
outside by new texts, and texts, as we have already had occasion to emphasize,
always conceal within themselves possibilities for ever new interpretations.
Moreover, this image of the historical past is not anti-scientific, although
it is not scientific. It exists alongside the scientific image of the past as
another reality, and interacts with it too on a dialogic basis.

Just as various forecasts of the future form an unavoidable part of the
universe of culture, culture cannot do without "forecasts of the past."
