## The Alternative Variant: Non-Written Culture Or Culture Before Culture?

One common temptation for anyone reflecting on the history and typology of
cultures and civilizations is to think: "This did not exist, therefore it
could not have existed." Or, to paraphrase: "This is unknown to me, therefore
it is impossible." In practice this means that the chronological layer we can
study through well-preserved written sources - insignificant compared with the
whole unwritten and written history of humanity - is taken as the norm of the
historical process, and the culture of that period as the standard of human
culture.

Let us stop at one example. All culture known to European science is based on
writing. It is impossible for us to imagine a developed non-written culture, or
indeed any developed non-written civilization, because when we try to imagine
either one we involuntarily call up images of cultures and civilizations
already familiar to us. Not long ago, two prominent mathematicians, Postnikov
and Fomenko, expressed the idea that since the global development of writing
became possible only with the invention of paper, the whole "pre-paper" period
of cultural history is one continuous late falsification. There is no point in
disputing this paradoxical assertion here. But it is worth attending to it as
a vivid example of the extrapolation of common sense into unexplored areas. The
habitual is declared the only possible.

The connection between the existence of developed civilization, class society,
division of labor, and the high level of public works, building, irrigation,
and similar techniques conditioned by them, on the one hand, and the existence
of writing, on the other, seems so natural that alternative possibilities are
rejected a priori. Relying on the enormous material actually available to us,
one might recognize this connection as a universal law of culture, if not for
the mysterious phenomenon of the pre-Inca civilizations of South America.

The evidence accumulated by archaeology presents a truly astonishing picture.
Before us lies a thousand-year panorama of a series of civilizations replacing
one another, creating powerful building works and irrigation systems, erecting
cities and huge stone idols, possessing developed crafts - pottery, weaving,
metallurgy - and, more than that, unquestionably creating complex systems of
symbols, yet leaving no traces of writing. This fact remains an unexplained
paradox. The sometimes proposed supposition that writing was destroyed by
incoming conquerors, first by the Incas and then by the Spaniards, does not
seem convincing. Stone monuments, tombs, unplundered burials preserved in their
original state, pottery, and other utensils would have brought down to us some
trace of writing if writing had existed. Historical experience shows that
trace-free destruction on such a scale is beyond the power of any conqueror.
It remains to suppose that writing did not exist.

Let us not bind ourselves in advance with the claim "such a thing is
possible." Rather, let us try to imagine, since we have no other supports, what
such a civilization would have had to be like if it really existed.

Writing is a form of memory. Just as individual consciousness has its own
mechanisms of memory, collective consciousness, discovering the need to fix
something common to the whole collective, creates mechanisms of collective
memory. Writing should be counted among them. But is writing the first and,
more importantly, the only possible form of collective memory? The answer to
this question should be sought from the idea that forms of memory derive from
what is considered worth remembering, and this in turn depends on the structure
and orientation of the given civilization.

Our usual attitude toward memory implies that what is to be remembered, what
is fixed by the mechanisms of collective memory, is exceptional events. These
are events that are unique, or that happened for the first time, or that should
not have happened, or whose realization seemed improbable. Precisely such
events enter chronicles and annals and become the property of newspapers. For a
memory of this type, oriented toward the preservation of excesses and
incidents, writing is necessary. A culture of this kind constantly multiplies
the number of texts. Law becomes overgrown with precedents. Legal acts fix
separate cases: sales, inheritances, decisions of disputes. Each time the judge
is dealing precisely with an individual case. The same law governs artistic
literature. Private correspondence and memoir-diary literature arise as well,
also fixing "cases" and "incidents."

Written consciousness is characterized by attention to causal connections and
to the results of actions. It records not the time at which sowing should
begin, but what the harvest was in a given year. Connected with this is a
heightened attention to time and, as a consequence, the emergence of the idea
of history. One can say that history is one of the by-products of the emergence
of writing.

But let us imagine the possibility of another type of memory: the effort to
preserve information about order, not about its violations; about laws, not
about excesses. Imagine that while observing an athletic contest, we did not
consider it essential who won or what unforeseen circumstances accompanied the
event, but concentrated on something else: preserving for descendants
information about how and when competitions are held. Here the chronicle or
newspaper report does not come to the foreground. Calendar, custom, which fixes
that order, and ritual, which allows all this to be preserved in collective
memory, come forward instead.

A culture oriented not toward multiplying the number of texts, but toward the
repeated reproduction of texts given once and for all, requires a different
organization of collective memory. Writing is not necessary here. Its role will
be performed by mnemonic symbols, both natural and human-made: especially
notable trees, cliffs, stars, and heavenly bodies in general; idols, burial
mounds, architectural structures; and the rituals into which these places and
sanctuaries are included. The connection with ritual, and in general the
sacralization of memory characteristic of such cultures, leads observers raised
in the European tradition to identify these places with sites of religious
worship in the meanings familiar to us. Concentrating on the genuinely present
sacred function, the observer tends not to notice the regulating and governing
function of the complex: mnemonic, or sacred, symbol plus rite.

The actions connected with this complex preserve for the collective the memory
of the deeds, representations, and emotions corresponding to the given
situation. Therefore, if we do not know the rituals and do not take into
account a huge number of calendar and other signs - for example, the length and
direction of the shadow cast by a given tree or structure, or the abundance or
scarcity of leaves or fruit in a given year on a particular sacred tree - we
cannot judge the function of preserved structures. It should also be kept in
mind that if written culture is oriented toward the past, oral culture is
oriented toward the future. For this reason predictions, divinations, and
prophecies play an enormous role in it. Sacred places and sanctuaries are not
only places where rituals preserving the memory of laws and customs are
performed, but also places of divination and prediction. In this respect,
sacrifice is a futurological experiment, because it is always connected with
an appeal to the deity for help in making a choice.

It would be mistaken to think that a civilization of this type lives under
conditions of "information hunger," as if all people's actions were fatally
predetermined by ritual and custom. Such a society simply could not exist.
Members of a "non-written collective" faced the need to choose every hour. But
they made this choice not by referring to history, causal connections, or
expected effectiveness, but, as many non-written peoples do, by turning to
diviners or sorcerers. In essence, the need to "consult" someone - a doctor, a
lawyer, an elder - is a remnant of the same tradition.

Opposed to this tradition stands Kant's ideal of the person who decides for
himself how to think and act. Kant wrote that Enlightenment is the human
being's exit from self-incurred immaturity. Immaturity is the inability to use
one's own understanding without guidance from someone else. It is so
comfortable to be immature. If one has a book that thinks for one, a pastor
whose conscience can replace one's own, or a doctor who prescribes one's way of
life, then there is no need to trouble oneself.

Non-written culture, with its orientation toward omens, divinations, and
oracles, transfers the choice of behavior into an extra-personal realm.
Therefore the ideal person is one who knows how to understand and correctly
interpret portents and, in carrying them out, knows no hesitation, acts openly,
and does not hide intentions. By contrast, a culture oriented toward the human
being's capacity to choose a strategy of behavior independently requires
prudence, caution, circumspection, and concealment, since every event is viewed
as something that has happened "for the first time."

A curious example can be found in Victor Turner's account of divination among
Central African peoples, in particular the Ndembu. Divination is performed by
shaking a basket containing special ritual figurines and evaluating their final
arrangement. Each figurine has a definite symbolic meaning, and whichever one
comes out on top plays a determining role in the prediction of the future.
Turner describes a figurine named Chamutang'a. It depicts a man sitting
crumpled up, supporting his chin with his hands and resting his elbows on his
knees. Chamutang'a means an indecisive, inconstant person. It also means "a
person from whom one does not know what to expect." His reactions are
unnatural. According to informants, he is capricious: now giving gifts, now
being miserly; sometimes laughing immoderately in company for no visible
reason, sometimes not uttering a word. No one can predict when he will fall
into anger and when he will show not the slightest sign of irritation. The
Ndembu like a person's behavior to be predictable. They honor openness and
constancy, and if they feel someone is insincere, they suppose that such a
person is very likely a sorcerer. Here the idea that what is hidden is
potentially dangerous and unfavorable receives new illumination.

It is not difficult to notice, however, that all the main gestural elements of
the Ndembu divinatory figurine Chamutang'a are also present in Rodin's
*Thinker*. The symbolism of supporting the chin with the hand is so stable that
Rodin's statue needs no explanation. This is all the more remarkable because
the sculptor's intention included the representation of the "first" thinker.
Neither the forehead nor the proportions of the figure carry the signs of an
intellectual stereotype. The whole meaning is transmitted only by the pose. It
is also interesting to recall that, according to descriptions, Garrick used the
same gestural stereotypes to create the "Hamlet type," with the correction that
the figure is standing, which makes the basic gesture complex still more
visible. In deep thought, he comes out from behind the scenes, rests his chin
on his right hand, whose elbow is supported by the left hand, and looks aside
and downward toward the ground. Then, taking his right hand away from his chin
while still supporting it with his left hand, he says: "To be or not to be?"

If we consider that Garrick's acting fixed the gestural image of the Hamlet
type that lasted on European stages for about a hundred years, the meaning of
this passage becomes especially significant.

What, then, is common to the Ndembu Chamutang'a, Hamlet, and Rodin's
*Thinker*? The invariant meaning is: a person in a state of choice. But for the
Ndembu, the state of choice means a rejection of custom, of the role affirmed
through the ages. It is connected either with the semantics of violating the
established order, that is, with sorcery, since the Ndembu attribute everything
irregular to hostile sorcery, or with such negative qualities as doubleness and
indecision.

Omens and predictions, by forecasting the future, connected the function of
choice with collective experience, leaving open and decisive action to the
individual person. Baratynsky gives this in poetic form: when a wolf runs out
of the forest onto the hero's path, turning and lifting its bristles, it
prophesies victory, and the hero boldly throws his regiment against the enemy.

A society built on custom and collective experience must inevitably have a
powerful culture of forecasting. This necessarily stimulates observations of
nature, especially of the heavenly bodies, and the theoretical knowledge
connected with them. Certain forms of descriptive geometry can be fully
combined with the non-written character of culture as such, supplemented by
calendar-astronomical oral poetry.

The world of oral memory is saturated with symbols. It may seem paradoxical
that the appearance of writing did not complicate but simplified the semiotic
structure of culture. Mnemonic-sacred symbols represented by material objects,
however, are included not in a verbal text but in the text of ritual. In
addition, they retain a certain freedom in relation to this text. Their
material existence continues outside the rite, and their inclusion in various
and numerous rites gives them broad polysemy. Their very existence implies an
enveloping sphere of oral stories, legends, and songs. As a result, the
syntactic connections between these symbols and different contexts turn out to
be loosened. A verbal text, especially a written one, rests on syntactic
connections. Oral culture weakens them to the limit.

For this reason oral culture may include a large number of symbolic signs of a
lower order, as if on the threshold of writing: amulets, ownership marks,
counting objects, signs of mnemonic "writing." But it reduces to the utmost
their composition into syntactic-grammatical chains. A culture of this type is
not incompatible with objects that allow counting, probably within quite
complex arithmetical operations. Within such a culture there may be a rapid
development of magical signs used in rituals, making use of the simplest
geometrical figures - circle, cross, parallel lines, triangle, and others - as
well as the basic colors. These signs should not be confused with hieroglyphs
and letters. Hieroglyphs and letters tend toward definite semantics and acquire
meaning only in a syntagmatic row, forming chains of signs. The former signs
have blurred and often internally contradictory meanings. They acquire meaning
in relation to ritual and to the oral texts of which they are mnemonic signs.
Their different nature is revealed by comparing a phrase, a chain of linguistic
symbols, with an ornament, a chain of magical-mnemonic and ritual symbols.

The development of ornaments and the absence of inscriptions on sculptural and
architectural monuments are equally characteristic signs of oral culture. A
hieroglyph, a written word or letter, and an idol, burial mound, or sacred
place are, in a certain sense, polar and mutually exclusive phenomena. The
first designate meaning. The second remind one of it. The first are a text or
part of a text, and the text has a homogeneous semiotic nature. The second are
included in the syncretic text of ritual or are mnemonically connected with
oral texts attached to a given place and time.

The antithesis between writing and sculpture is beautifully illustrated by the
biblical episode of the clash between Moses and Aaron: the tablets of the
former, intended to give the people a new mechanism of cultural memory, the
covenant, and the syncretic unity of idol and ritual dance, embodying the old
type of information storage. Moses comes down from the mountain carrying the
two tablets of revelation, written on both sides. The tablets are God's work,
and the writing cut into them is God's writing. Joshua hears the sound of the
people crying out and says there is a sound of war in the camp. Moses replies
that this is neither the shout of victory nor the cry of defeat; he hears the
sound of singing. When Moses comes near the camp and sees the calf and the
dancing, his anger burns. He throws the tablets from his hands and breaks them
under the mountain, then takes the calf they had made and burns it with fire.

Plato's dialogue *Phaedrus* gives very interesting material from the point of
view of the theme that concerns us. Devoted to questions of rhetorical art, it
is closely connected with problems of mnemonics. From the very beginning of the
dialogue, Plato leads Socrates and Phaedrus beyond the city walls of Athens in
order to demonstrate to readers the connection between local places, groves,
hills, and springs and the collective memory embodied in myths. Phaedrus asks
Socrates whether this is the place by the Ilissus where Boreas is said to have
carried off Oreithyia. Socrates replies that according to the tradition, yes.
Phaedrus asks whether it was not from here, because the stream is so lovely,
clear, and transparent that girls would naturally play along its bank.
Socrates answers that the place is lower down the river, two or three stadia
away, where the crossing to the shrine of Agra is located and where there is
also an altar to Boreas.

Further on, Socrates unexpectedly offers his interlocutor the paradoxical
conclusion that writing harms memory. A society based on writing appears to
Socrates as memoryless and abnormal, while a non-written society appears as a
normal structure with firm collective memory. Socrates tells of the divine
Theuth, who revealed the arts to the Egyptian king. When the turn came to
letters, Theuth said that this science would make the Egyptians wiser and more
remembering, since a remedy for memory and wisdom had been found. The king
answered that Theuth, father of letters, had out of love for them assigned them
the opposite of their real effect. They would implant forgetfulness in the
souls of those who learned them, because memory would be deprived of exercise.
People would remember from outside, trusting writing and alien signs, rather
than from within, by themselves. Theuth had found not a remedy for memory but a
reminder. He would give students seeming wisdom, not true wisdom. They would
hear much without instruction and seem very knowledgeable while remaining
mostly ignorant, difficult to associate with, seeming wise instead of wise.

It is revealing that Plato's Socrates connects writing not with the progress of
culture, but with the loss of a high level already attained by non-written
society.

The attachment of oral texts, which crystallize around idols and sacred
places, to a definite place and time manifests itself in the very different
experience of local landscape in written and non-written cultures. Written
culture tends to regard the world created by God or Nature as a Text and seeks
to read the message contained in it. Therefore the main meaning is sought in
the written Text, whether sacred or scientific, and then extrapolated to the
landscape. From this point of view, the meaning of Nature is disclosed only to
the "written" person. This person seeks laws in Nature, not omens. Interest in
omens is regarded as superstition. The future is sought from the past, not on
the basis of divinations and portents.

Non-written culture relates to landscape differently. Since a given sacred
place, sanctuary, or idol is "switched on" into cultural use by ritual,
sacrifice, divination, songs, or dances, and since all these actions are
attached to a definite time, these places, sanctuaries, and idols are connected
with a definite position of the stars, sun, or moon, with cyclical winds or
rains, periodic rises of river water, and so on. Natural phenomena are
perceived as reminding or predictive signs. The fact that the biblical God, in
his covenant with Noah, set the rainbow as a sign, and gave Moses written
tablets, clearly symbolizes a shift in typological orientation toward different
kinds of memory.

It is easy to see that so-called "folk" and "cultured" medicine are oriented
toward two different kinds of consciousness: non-written and written.
Baratynsky needed unusual insight and independent thought, at the dawn of the
age of positivism, to see in superstition and omens not falsehood and savagery,
but fragments of another truth, going back to another type of culture:

Superstition! It is a fragment
of an ancient truth. The temple fell;
and its descendant could not decipher
the language of its ruins.

It is significant that the poet connects superstition here precisely with a
temple, an architectural structure, and not with a "grave inscription in an
incomprehensible language," the image Pushkin found for an incomprehensible
word.

Baratynsky's comparison suggests itself when one reflects on the lost meaning
of the pre-Inca architectural structures of ancient Peru.

The biblical texts cited above draw the picture familiar to us: non-written and
written cultures appear as two stages succeeding one another, the lower and the
higher.

But can we conclude from the fact that in the Eurasian space familiar to us
historical movement proceeded precisely along this path that it could only have
proceeded in this way? The thousand-year existence of non-written cultures in
pre-Columbian America offers convincing testimony to the stability of such a
civilization, and the high cultural indicators it achieved vividly demonstrate
its cultural possibilities. For writing to become necessary, historical
conditions must be unstable, circumstances dynamic and unpredictable, and there
must be a need for varied semiotic translations arising from frequent and
long-lasting contact with an alien ethnic environment. In this respect, the
space between the Balkans and North Africa, the Near and Middle East, the
coasts of the Black and Mediterranean seas, on the one hand, and the plateaus
of Peru, the valleys among the Andes, and the narrow strip of the Peruvian
coast on the other, represent polar opposite historical basins. In the first
case there is a cauldron of constant ethnic mixing, continuous movement, and
collision among different cultural-semiotic structures. In the second there is
centuries-long isolation, the extreme limitation of trade and military contacts
with external cultures, and ideal conditions for the continuity of cultural
tradition. The destruction of isolation, as a rule, is accompanied by the
complete disappearance of one or another ancient Peruvian civilization. The
victory of written civilization in one case and of non-written civilization in
the other seems natural.
