## Symbol - "The Gene Of Plot"

In 1834 Karl Bryullov's painting *The Last Day of Pompeii* was exhibited in
Petersburg. It made a strong impression on Pushkin. He tried to copy certain
details of the painting and at the same time sketched a verse fragment:

Vesuvius opened its maw - smoke poured in a cloud - flame
Spread wide, like a battle banner.
The earth is troubled - from the shaken columns
The idols fall! The people, driven by fear,
Under a rain of stone, under burning ash,
In crowds, old and young, flee from the city.

Comparison of the text with Bryullov's canvas reveals that Pushkin's gaze
slides along the diagonal from the upper right corner to the lower left. This
corresponds to the basic compositional axis of the painting. The artist and art
theorist N. Tarabukin, a student of diagonal compositions, wrote that the
content of a painting built compositionally along this diagonal is often one or
another demonstrative procession. The viewer of the painting in such a case
occupies a place, as it were, among the crowd depicted on the canvas.

Tarabukin's observation is exceptionally exact, and questioning informants fully
confirmed that viewers' attention is as a rule concentrated precisely on the
crowd. Characteristic in this respect is the opinion of the palace commandant
P. P. Martynov, who, according to a contemporary, said while looking at the
painting: "For me the best thing is old Pompey, whom the children are
carrying." Martynov was, in Pushkin's words, a fool and a brute, and his
statement is cited as an anecdotal example of ignorance of Roman history. For
us, however, in this case it is the opinion of a naive, inexperienced viewer,
whose attention was seized by the large figures in the foreground.

The comparison of *The Bronze Horseman* and *The Last Day of Pompeii* allows
one substantial observation about Pushkin's poetics. The poetics of Boileau,
the poetics of the German romantics, and the aesthetics of German classical
philosophy all proceeded from the idea that in the artist's consciousness there
is first a verbally formulable thought, which is then clothed in an image that
serves as its sensuous expression. Even for objective-idealist aesthetics, which
regarded the idea as the highest, superhuman reality, the artist who
unconsciously drew reality objectively gave clear other-being to an idea that
was dark and had not recognized itself. Here too, then, the image was as it
were a package concealing some single correct verbal, that is, rational,
interpretation of it. An approach to Pushkin's work from such positions gives
rise to long-lasting disputes, for example, about what the flood in *The Bronze
Horseman* means and how the image of the monument should be interpreted.

Pushkin's semantic paradigm is formed not by words, but by images-models that
have a syncretic verbal-visual being. Their contradictory nature implies the
possibility not simply of different readings, but of complementary readings, in
Niels Bohr's sense: equally adequate interpretations that at the same time
exclude one another. Moreover, the interpretation of one node of Pushkin's
structure automatically determines the corresponding concretization of the
whole row. For this reason a dispute over one or another understanding of the
symbolic meaning of isolated images in *The Bronze Horseman* is useless.

The first member of the paradigm could be anything that, in the poet's
consciousness at one or another moment, could associate with a spontaneous
catastrophic explosion. The second member differed from it by the differential
features of made-ness, belonging to the world of civilization, as the
"conscious" differs from its opposite, the "unconscious." The third member
differs from the first as the personal differs from the impersonal. The
remaining features can be redistributed in different ways inside the
three-member structure, depending on its concrete historical and plot
interpretation.

Thus in Pushkin's sketch "A Motionless Guard Dozed On The Royal Threshold" we
read:

Was it long ago that the peoples of the world
Celebrated the fall of the Great Idol...

The fallen idol is the feudal order of "old Europe." The image of the element
is interpreted accordingly. Compare the tenth chapter of *Eugene Onegin*:

The Pyrenees shook menacingly -
The volcano of Naples blazed...

One and the same image-model was clothed, with striking stability, in the same
words:

The earth trembled,
The pillars sway...
The earth sways...
The earth trembled - the city shook...

and in variants of "Vesuvius opened its maw":

Austria shook, Naples rose up.

The design of the poem about Alexander I and Napoleon apparently had to include
the triumph of the "idol":

And the Ruler himself marveled at his work.

The image of the iron foot that trampled rebellion outlines, behind Alexander
I, the figure of Falconet's monument to Peter. Yet the appearance of
Napoleon's shade probably implied a forecast of the future triumph of the
element:

To the world eternal freedom
From the darkness of exile he bequeathed.

The possibility of splitting the "idol" into Alexander I, or in general into a
living bearer of the complex imagery of this member of the structure, and the
Bronze Horseman as statue is already outlined in the mysterious, and perhaps
not entirely joking, poem "The Tsar Frowned His Brows":

The tsar frowned his brows
And said: "Yesterday
A storm knocked down
The monument to Peter."

The possibility of this doubling is built into Pushkin's understanding of the
image of the statue as a phenomenon dual in its very nature. "What a giant he
is represented as here! What shoulders! What a Hercules! But the dead man
himself was small and frail; standing on tiptoe here, he could not have reached
his own nose with his hand." The correlation of the members of the paradigm
gave it semantic flexibility, allowing different semantic facets to be
actualized at different stages in the development of Pushkin's thought. Thus if
destructiveness was emphasized in the element, the counter-member could receive
the function of constructiveness; the irrationalism of the "senseless and
merciless" element accentuated the moment of consciousness. At the same time,
in the early 1820s variant the "idols" were passive and the bearer of action
was the "volcano." In the consciousness behind *The Bronze Horseman*, this is a
collision of two forces equal in their possibilities. This is connected with
the activation of the third member: the human personality and its fate in the
struggle of these forces.

It should be emphasized once again that in *The Bronze Horseman* the collision
of images-models is by no means an allegory of some unambiguous sense. It marks
a certain cultural-historical equation that permits any semantic substitution
so long as the relation among the members of the paradigm is preserved. Pushkin
studies the possibilities hidden in the tragically contradictory elements that
make up his paradigm of history. He is not trying to interpret for us, "in
images," some final thought that he has already grasped and that can be
formulated without remainder.

Pushkin's understanding of this conflict of history, one of the most important
for him, will become clearer if we study all the realizations and complex
transformations of this paradigm in all the Pushkin texts known to us. From
this point of view, special significance belongs not only to the image of the
snowstorm that opens the plot conflict of *The Captain's Daughter* - "Well,
master," cried the driver, "trouble: a snowstorm!" - but also to the fact that
Pugachev both appears out of the snowstorm and saves Grinev from the snowstorm.
Accordingly, in the tale he is connected now with the first, "elemental,"
member of the paradigm, now with the third, "human" member. The splitting of
the second member into supplementary, that is, compatible-incompatible,
functions leads in *The Bronze Horseman* to an extraordinary complication of
the image of Peter: Peter of the introduction, Peter in antithesis to the flood,
and Peter in antithesis to Evgeny are entirely different and seemingly
incompatible figures, each of them transforming the whole paradigm accordingly.
Yet they all occupy one and the same structural place in it, forming a
microparadigm and, in this respect, being identified with one another.

Thus what is essential is that the triangle be preserved: the rebellion of the
elements, the statue, and the human being. Further, various interpretations are
possible when these images are projected into the realm of concepts. A purely
mythological projection is possible: water, or fire; worked stone; human being.
The second member, however, can be interpreted historically: culture, ratio,
power, the city, the laws of history. Then the first member will be transformed
into the concepts "nature," "unconscious element," "rebellion," "steppe,"
"elemental resistance to the laws of history." But this same relation can also
be the opposition of "wild freedom" and "dead unfreedom." Equally complex will
be the relations of the first and second members of the paradigm with the
third. In the third may be actualized what Gogol called the "poor wealth" of
the simple person, whose right to life and happiness stands opposed both to the
fury of the unleashed elements and to "boredom, cold, and granite," to "iron
will" and inhuman reason. Yet in this same third member a petty egoism may also
show through, turning Liza from *The Queen of Spades*, in the final result,
into a wind-up doll repeating another's path. None of these possibilities ever
appears in Pushkin as the only one. The paradigm is given in all its
potentially possible manifestations. It is precisely the incompatibility of
these manifestations with one another that gives the images the depth of
unfinishedness, the ability to answer not only the questions of Pushkin's
contemporaries, but also future questions posed by descendants.

The inclusion in the system under study of oppositions from other oppositions
central to Pushkin - living/dead, human/inhuman, mobile/immobile, in the most
various combinations - also multiplies the possibilities of interpretation and
evaluation, introducing an axiological criterion. Besides the "natural"
combination living-moving-human, a perverse one is also possible:
dead-moving-inhuman. Acquiring the image of a "moving dead man," the second
member can receive the feature of irrationality, of blind and inhuman
regularity; then simple "human" ideals belonging to the third member of the
paradigm receive the feature of rationality. Thus one and the same figure, for
example Peter in *The Bronze Horseman*, can appear in one opposition as the
bearer of the rational principle and in another as the bearer of the
irrational. And one or another real historical movement can be placed in the
first and third positions. For Pushkin, this revealed the profound
contradictoriness of real historical forces, which can be "caught" only with
the help of that maximally flexible model which genuine art is able to build.

It is enough to imagine Pushkin at the celebration of the Lyceum anniversary on
October 19, 1828, watching the same Yakovlev - a "clown" who gave a very
lifelike representation of the Petersburg flood - "representing a wax person,"
that is, a moving statue of Peter, in order to understand the possibility of
very complex distributions of comic and tragic within this paradigm.

Pushkin's contrastive-dynamic poetics determined not only the vitality of his
artistic creations, but also the depth of his thought, which still allows us to
see in him not only a genius artist but also one of the greatest thinkers.

The so-called "little tragedies" belong to the peaks of Pushkin's creative work
in the 1830s, and it is no accident that they have repeatedly drawn the
attention of scholars. In the present work our task is not to consider them
comprehensively. Our aim is much more modest: to introduce one more image into
the world of Pushkin's realistic symbolism. Already in the mid-1930s, in the
article "The Statue in Pushkin's Poetic Mythology," R. O. Jakobson wrote that
in the diverse symbolism of a poetic work we find a definite constant
organization, cementing elements that carry unity in the poet's many-sided work
and imprint his works with individuality. These elements introduce the poet's
integral individual mythology into the varied confusion of poetic motifs that
often deviate and slip away.

The "alphabet" of symbols of one or another poet is far from always
individual: it can draw its symbolism from the arsenal of an epoch, a cultural
direction, or a social circle. The symbol is connected with the memory of
culture, and a whole row of symbolic images vertically permeates all of human
history or large areal layers of it. The individuality of an artist is
manifested not only in the creation of new occasional symbols, in the symbolic
reading of the nonsymbolic, but also in the actualization of images of a
symbolic character that are sometimes very archaic. Yet what is most
significant is the system of relations the poet establishes among the
fundamental image-symbols. The field of meanings of symbols is always
polysemous. Only by forming a crystalline lattice of mutual connections do they
create the "poetic world" that constitutes the distinctiveness of the given
artist.

In Pushkin's realist work, the system of symbols forms an exceptionally
dynamic, flexible structure that generates an astonishing wealth of meanings
and, what is especially important, their volumetric multiplanarity, almost
adequate to the multiplanarity of life itself. To describe the whole system of
Pushkin's symbolism would be an impossible task for us, both because of the
scope of the present work and because of the complexity of the problem itself.
The *Little Tragedies*, however, make it possible to introduce several
additional symbols and observe their connection with the structure already
described.

The semantic center of *The Stone Guest*, *Mozart and Salieri*, and *A Feast
During The Plague* is formed by a motif that could be defined as the "fatal
feast." In all three plots the feast is connected with death: a feast with a
guest-statue, a feast-murder, and a feast in a plague-stricken city. In all
cases, moreover, the feast has not only an ominous but also a distorted
character: it is blasphemous and violates some fundamental prohibitions that
ought to remain inviolable for a human being. The motif of the terrible feast
is archaic; it is enough to point to the feast of Atreus. But in Pushkin it
receives a special sense because the symbol of the feast is exceptionally
important for all his work.

In its basic meaning in Pushkin's work, the feast first of all has a positive
sound. The theme of the feast is the theme of friendship:

God help you, my friends,
In the cares of life, of royal service,
And at the feasts of riotous friendship,
And in the sweet mysteries of love.

The feast is the joining of people into a brotherly circle. The festive table,
joy, and brotherhood are steadily associated with freedom. Joy is a sign of
liberty, while boredom and anguish are products of slavery. The triple
connection feast-joy-freedom discloses the first and basic layer of this
symbol:

I love the evening feast,
Where Joy is chairman,
And Freedom, my idol,
Is lawgiver at the table.

From this comes an additional feature: the ring or circle, the circle of
friends, brothers, comrades. This makes the symbol of the feast active not only
in Pushkin's friendly lyric but also in his political lyric: the idea of
struggle, unanimity, and political union is linked with wine, joy, and friendly
disputes. At the same time one must keep in mind that the feast - communion,
the shared tasting of wine and bread - is an ancient symbol of an unbreakable
bond, a sacrificial union. Hence the union of feast and eucharist. At the same
time, the feast is endowed with Bacchic features of revelry, energy spilling
over its bounds.

A distinctive condensation of the basic elements of the feast in Pushkin's
poetry is "The Bacchic Song," which names the theme of joy in its very first
line. Church Slavonic forms for "joy" and "voice" give it a solemn, almost
ritual sound. Solemn majorness permeates the poem. Only the first line contains
an interrogative intonation. It speaks of a moment of silence that has arrived
and includes a word with the semantics of quiet and perhaps sadness:
"fallen silent." The whole rest of the poem is opposed to the first line and
is filled with triumphant, joyful vocabulary. The abundance of exclamatory
intonations is combined with imperative verbal forms. Only the verb in the
first line is given in the indicative. All the remaining verbs - pour, throw,
raise, resound - are imperatives. The formula "long live" as a Church
Slavonicism also expresses the semantics of command.

This gives the text a volitional sound. The basic symbolic meaning of the poem
is the victory of light over darkness. This first of all concerns the
extra-textual situation, the moment when the song is performed. "The Bacchic
Song" is a ritual hymn in honor of the sun, performed at the end of a night
feast, at sunrise.

The theme reveals the symbolism of the feast image step by step: Bacchic joy;
love; wine; a double image of the ring, both the secret rings thrown into round
glasses and the circle of friendly glasses moved together; the muses and
reason; the sun. The comparison of the sun with reason, "the immortal sun of
the mind," its opposition to the lamp of "false wisdom," and the epithet "holy
sun" give the image a maximally generalized and not merely astral sense. The
meaning of the circle as one of the most ancient solar signs is repeated here
as well.

The complexity of the symbol appears in the fact that the feast as embodiment
of friendship does not exclude the combination "the noise of feasts and stormy
disputes." Worship of Reason, even ritual service to it, does not forbid "the
mad playfulness of feasts." Neither reason nor Poetry is hostile to the madness
of feast-day joy.

Another meaning of feast is connected with an excess of some feature: a "feast
of imagination," a "feast of young designs." A third meaning goes back to the
folkloric battle-feast, although before Pushkin it already had a considerable
literary tradition. It is no accident that this meaning is present in early
Lyceum verses. Standing quite apart is the expression in "Andre Chenier": "In
the morning, execution, the people's customary feast." It already seems to
foretell the semantics of the terrible feasts that attract our attention here.

In an article on *A Feast During The Plague* that contains a number of
interesting thoughts but is extremely subjective, I. L. Pankratova and V. E.
Khalizev, proceeding from a conception of the "sinful" young Pushkin and the
mature Pushkin whose dominant feeling is repentance, assign the poetry of
feasts to the sins of the poet's youth. Pushkin's development appears to them
as expressible in a formula according to which in 1835 the words of *The
Wanderer* sounded - "I see the light" - and summed up Pushkin's moral
evolution. Proceeding by such a method, one would have to suppose that the
lines "The earth is motionless. The vaults of heaven are upheld by you,
Creator" express Pushkin's own cosmological views.

An exhaustive analysis of the texts, rather than a subjective and selective
one, leads to another conclusion. The image of the feast in the direct meaning
of this symbol is present throughout Pushkin's work as a positive one. Compare
*The Feast of Peter the Great*, the poem with which Pushkin opened *The
Contemporary*, thereby emphasizing the programmatic significance of this text.
The frightening images of the distorted feast are active precisely against this
background. Their sense is emphasized by the consciousness of their special and
anomalous character. The repentance attributed to Pushkin regarding the ideal
of the feast as such completely distorts the semantic perspective.

The feast is an image that symbolically expresses union, unity, joyful brotherly
fusion. But is union and fusion of life and death possible? It is precisely
these forces that play their parts in the little tragedies. To be sure, these
conflicts are seen in such a generalized form only when they are formulated at
their deepest level. Closer to the plot surface, they play out among the triad
of symbols already familiar to us: element, regularity, human being. Finally,
on the very surface of the plot, they are embodied in concrete historical and
cultural-epochal images. Each of the preceding levels is not automatically and
without remainder expressed in the language of the following one, but plays
with it, expressing itself ambiguously and only to a certain degree in the
principally different language of another level.

In the little tragedies, the basic symbols of Pushkin's artistic world of the
1830s receive a specific interpretation: things, ideas, and people collide.
Moreover, these collisions not only have an extreme character, but take place
in monstrously distorted forms. The world of the little tragedies is a world
shifted out of place, a world at a breaking point. Every phenomenon acquires
features alien to it: the immobile moves, love triumphs on graves, refined
aesthetic feeling leads logically to murder, and feasts turn out to be feasts
of death. Yet precisely the destruction of the norm creates the image of a
necessary, though unrealized, norm.

The need for harmony, the belief in its possibility and naturalness, forms the
semantic background of these tragic stories, which in a strange way leave the
reader with an impression of the deepest artistic health, although they draw
pictures that are diseased and distorted.

In *The Miserly Knight*, things displace people. The unnatural feast in which
the Baron and his chests of gold take part as brothers is lawfully supplemented
by the enmity of father and son and their mutual readiness to kill one another.
Money is animated. It is "servants," "friends," or "masters." But above all it
is gods. It is precisely in the society of these friend-gods that the Baron
decided to "hold a feast for myself today." G. A. Gukovsky noted traits of
knightly psychology in Albert's words about Count Delorge. Yet another thing
also strikes the eye in this utterance: under the power of money, the living
and the nonliving have changed places. Value belongs to what is dead,
artificially made, and subject to exchange, while what is living, natural, and
inalienable appears devalued. All human ties are torn apart; things have taken
the place of people: gold, chests, keys.

In *The Miserly Knight* the Baron's feast takes place, but another feast, at
which Albert would have had to poison his father, is only mentioned. This feast
will take place in *Mozart and Salieri*, linking these two otherwise very
different plays into a single "montage phrase" through a rhyme of situations.

Gukovsky convincingly showed that the conflict of *Mozart and Salieri* is not
the envy of mediocrity toward talent. Salieri is a great composer, devoted to
art and feeling it no worse than Mozart. Faithful to a conception of realism as
the demonstration of the social-historical determination of the person,
Gukovsky sees the sense of Pushkin's play in the triumph of historically
progressive forms of art over those passing into the past. He accordingly
defines Salieri as an artist of the 18th century and Mozart as a romantic. In
Gukovsky's conception there is also a factual vulnerability: what Pushkin knew
about Salieri could not have suggested the image of a classicist artist.
Contrary to the scholar's opinion, the mention of Beaumarchais does not speak
for this, nor still less the reference to *Tarare*, a romantic revolutionary
opera, one of the most popular in the 1790s, in which spirits, genii, and
shades of heroes appeared on stage for the glorification of tyrannicide.
Karamzin called the play "strange."

But the point is not only this. The change of cultural epochs can explain the
birth of envy. But Salieri does not merely envy: he kills.

Pushkin not only shows the psychology of envy but, with astonishing mastery,
draws the mechanism by which murder comes into being. To suppose that
classicism, at the moment of its historical completion, generated a psychology
of murder would be as strange as attributing such views to Pushkin.

Let us trace Salieri's path to murder. Salieri is a born musician: "I was born
with love for art." From early childhood he dedicated himself to music. His
relation to creativity is self-renouncing service. The words of Salieri are
constantly cited:

Having killed the sounds,
I dissected music like a corpse. I tested
Harmony by algebra.

People have become so accustomed to seeing in these lines a declaration of dry
rationalism in art that they do not usually reflect on their actual sense in
the context of the entire monologue. Yet immediately after them come the words:
"Then, already experienced in science, I dared to surrender myself to the bliss
of creative dream." Salieri is not at all an enemy of inspired creativity, not
an enemy of "creative dream." He merely considers, with severe self-limitation,
that the right to inspired creativity is won through long trial and service,
which opens access to the circle of initiated creators. Such a view recalls a
knightly order more than an academy of mathematicians. Neither the idea of
service to art nor the difficult trial of mastering craft was alien to the
theory of romanticism.

Salieri has assimilated a medieval priestly attitude toward art. It is not only
duty and service bound up with self-renunciation, but also a peculiar grace
that descends on the worthy. As grace is higher than the priest, so art is
higher than the artist. It is from this moment that Salieri's fatal movement
toward crime begins. Pushkin takes the noblest of abstract ideas, essentially
the most humanistic one - the idea of art - and shows that, when placed above
the human being and turned into a self-sufficient abstraction, it can become an
instrument of human murder. Having placed human art above the human being,
Salieri can easily take the next step, convincing himself that a person and
that person's life may be sacrificed to this fetish.

Thought torn away from its human content turns into a chain of sophisms. In
speaking of the image of Angelo in Shakespeare's *Measure for Measure*, Pushkin
wrote that Angelo justifies his cruelty by the profound judgment of a statesman
and seduces innocence with powerful, captivating sophisms. Thus thought freezes,
clothes itself in the armor of supposed truths, turns to stone, that is, becomes
dead. Stone rises against the human being; abstraction attempts murder against
life.

The first step toward murder is the assertion that the murderer is only the
executor of someone else's higher will and bears no personal responsibility:
"I am chosen"; "I cannot oppose my lot, my fate." Next comes the most decisive
step: "to kill" is tabooed and replaced by the euphemism "to stop":

I am chosen to stop him.

The hardest thing in a criminal deed is to learn its respectable name, to
master the lexicon of murderers. Even when speaking with himself, Salieri does
not want to name things directly: the matter concerns only an innocent action -
one must "stop." At the same time there is another important move of the
sophism: Mozart is represented as the aggressive side, while the "priests,
servants of music" are merely defending victims. This too is essential in the
sophistry of murder: the victim is represented as a strong and dangerous
attacking enemy, while the murderer is represented as the victim who defends
himself.

Finally comes the decisive question:

What use is it if Mozart lives
And reaches still another height?
Will he thereby raise art?

Mozart's right to life, the right of a human being in general, turns out to be
defined by the "use" he brings to the progress of art. Those called to judge
this question are the "priests, servants of music." If they decide that
Mozart's life is useless for the idea of art, then they have the right to pass
a death sentence on him.

Thus abstract dogma, even noble in its initial premises, when placed above
living and trembling human life, when it regards that life only as a means for
its higher aims, freezes, turns to stone, acquires the traits of an inhuman
"idol," and becomes an instrument of murder.

Mozart's feast at the Golden Lion, his last feast, takes place not in the
company of a musician friend, as Mozart thinks, but of a stone guest. On the
immediate plot level, seated at the table are a life-loving, childlike person -
precisely a person, not a musician - and the one who, a refined connoisseur and
expert in music, says of himself, "I love life little": a human being and a
principle. But the shadow of the fatal feast is already present in the first
conversation between Mozart and Salieri:

Imagine... whom?
Well, me, a little younger:
In love - not too much, just a little -
With a beauty, or with a friend, say with you -
I am merry... Suddenly: a sepulchral vision,
Unexpected darkness or something of the kind...

The appearance of the Commander is foretold. But the sharpness of the situation
lies in the fact that the friend is the Commander. Mozart, believing that genius
and villainy are incompatible, thinks he is feasting with a genius, that is,
with a human being. In fact, however, stone is sitting at table with him, a
"sepulchral vision."

The second mention is Mozart's words, when he feels that the black man who
commissioned the Requiem sits "as a third with us." The third is the name of
Beaumarchais, which falls from Salieri's lips. Whether slander or fact, the
fame of poisoner pursued Beaumarchais in life and stained his name after death.
Thus the symbol of the feast and the motif of the struggle between human being
and stone lead to the decisive moment: murder.

Two worlds collide: Mozart's normal human world, which affirms the link of art
with humanity, play, carelessness, ordinary life, and creative work seeking what
has not yet been found; and Salieri's perverse world, where murder is called
duty, poison is called a "gift of love," and poisoned wine a "cup of
friendship," where a person's life is only a means, truth has already been
found and formulated, and priests stand guard over it.

The factual victory belongs to the second world, the moral victory to the
first. Distortion is the law of Salieri's world, and he uses the unnatural
combination "I feasted with a hated guest" as ordinary and normal. In *The
Stone Guest*, this atmosphere spreads to all the heroes and fills the whole
space of the drama. Mozart's natural, human, domestic childlikeness, the
simple-hearted kindness of his genius, turns in Don Juan into an evil passion
that, for fullness of sensation, requires the presence of death. Don Juan too
is a genius, but he will no longer say that genius and villainy are "two
incompatible things." Mozart is embodied naturalness. He shows that to be
genius is a norm of human existence. Don Juan is a genius who rushes to violate
norms, to destroy any boundaries precisely because they are boundaries.

But if the shift of the author's point of view discloses something new in the
"human" row - an analysis of the personal principle in the human being, with
Mozart revealing the genius in the human, and Don Juan the terrible in the
genius - then the world of "stone" dogmas and principles also appears in a new
light: as the defender of ideas of fidelity, the hearth, and family morality.
The Commander is illuminated differently than the brilliant envier Salieri.
This leads to the fact that in *The Stone Guest* both antagonists are, each in
his own way, frightening and, each in his own way, attractive.

The image of Don Juan doubles. He is a cunning seducer, a calculating cynic, a
"debauchee and scoundrel," as Don Carlos characterizes him. But he is also the
Don Quixote of love, and every word of falsehood is miraculously transformed
in his mouth into a word of sincerity and truth. He believes so completely, at
each moment, in what he is saying that his speeches become true. He is the
enemy of the women who fall in love with him - "How many poor women have you
ruined?" - but he is also their greatest friend. Even when deceiving, he says
to them what they themselves secretly long to hear, and for each of them Don
Juan's love is seduction and ruin, but also triumph and happiness.

Don Juan is "depraved, shameless, godless," as the monk calls him. But his
godlessness is not connected with a morbid thirst for blasphemy. It is
connected with limitless courage, which forbids him to acknowledge the
existence of forces capable of instilling fear in him. He appears with the
words "I fear no one in Madrid" and triumphs over fear in his last moments.
When the Statue tells him to leave Donna Anna, Don Juan refuses and dies with
the name of his earthly love on his lips. Blind, limitless human courage makes
Don Juan's image captivating; the destructiveness of his passions reveals the
terrible side of human personality.

The image of the Commander also doubles, and this distinguishes him from
Salieri. He is the Statue, the embodied principle, the "dead happy man," the
"marble husband." But he is also a human being. This duality is given in Don
Juan's well-known monologue:

What a giant he is represented as here!
What shoulders! What a Hercules!
But the dead man himself was small and frail;
Standing here on tiptoe he could not
Have reached his own nose with his hand.

The contrast between the imposingness of the statuary sign and the physical
frailty of the real Don Alvaro has long since become a textbook example. But
the words "and yet he was proud and brave, and had a stern spirit" restore the
correspondence between the spiritual essence of Donna Anna's dead husband and
the appearance of the Statue. The living Don Alvaro also doubles. He bought
Donna Anna's love: "We were poor. Don Alvaro was rich. My mother ordered me to
give my hand to Don Alvaro" - the situation of Tatyana. But he loved Donna Anna
and would have kept faith with her beyond the grave.

The role of the Statue is also ambiguous. It is the embodied merciless and
inhuman principle of Duty, refusing the human being the right to earthly
happiness. Yet it is also moral judgment and higher retribution against the
violator of ethical norms who brings chaos and destruction into the world.

The Statue has still another aspect. In *Mozart and Salieri*, the third
essential component of Pushkin's symbolism is not represented: the images of
the element. True, the presence of infernal forces is felt in the play, but
only Mozart hears it. Salieri is deaf to everything except the sophisms of
abstract reason. In *The Stone Guest*, the intrusion of otherworldly forces is
one of the basic motifs of the play. Something alien both to the human being and
to the abstract principles of culture, duty, and even morality, something
incomprehensible and nonhuman, intrudes into the fate of Don Juan, who, while
blasphemously laughing at human values, has touched something terrible in whose
existence he did not even believe. The capacious image of the elements allowed
the substitution of such distant meanings as popular rebellion and the invasion
of infernal forces, compare the snowstorm in *The Captain's Daughter* and in
"Demons." In general it made possible the introduction of a component of sense
that is multiplanar and open to many interpretations. Don Juan invited the
"marble husband" to come later to his wife's house, but it was not a dead
jealous man who came to Don Juan; it was someone more frightening. Don Juan
withstood even this encounter.

The fusion of the symbolism of the stone "idol" and the inexpressibly
elemental principle reveals in the first some new facets absent from Salieri's
image. The human being in the Don Juan variant finds himself in a solitude that
discloses both the strength of his character and the inevitability of final
destruction. In *The Bronze Horseman*, the "idol" and the element will be
hostilely opposed. It is precisely the varied combination of initial semantic
groups that allows Pushkin to disclose the many-sided complexity of historical
and social conflicts.

The infernal feast in the presence of death occurs twice in *The Stone Guest*:
it is as it were rehearsed at Laura's, where Don Alvaro is replaced by his
gloomy brother, and at Donna Anna's, where the Commander appears in the form of
the Statue. The cycle of little tragedies ends with a play in which the image
of the feast becomes the sense-organizing center.

*A Feast During The Plague* has attracted less attention from scholars than any
other play of the cycle, apparently in part because of its translated
character. Yet the significance of this work is exceptionally great. Already in
*The Stone Guest*, the action takes place in a special, strange, and terrible
space. Pushkin excluded from all Don Juan plots the obligatory scenes with the
seduction of a peasant girl and other side plots. Scenes in a castle, a village,
and the like fell away. What remained were the cemetery and scenes with dead
people and supernatural apparitions. The cemetery is the place of residence of
both Don Juan and the Commander. From there they arrive in turn at Donna Anna's
house. In *A Feast During The Plague*, the cemetery becomes one of the two basic
elements of space.

*A Feast During The Plague* introduces us into a world of destroyed and
distorted norms. The whole action unfolds as if in a crooked mirror, in a
perverted space. One of the basic spatial symbols of late Pushkin is the Home.
This is one's own, native, and at the same time closed and protected space, the
space of private life, in which the ideal of independence is realized. But it
is also the place where a human being lives an authentic life. Here the
experience of national culture accumulates; here, and this thought will be
taken up by Lev Tolstoy, the authentic rather than imaginary life of the
people, inner rather than external, takes place. This space is the world of
human personality, the world opposed both to the invasion of the elements and
to everything that regards the life of an individual person as a detail
unworthy of attention. It is the "dwelling" of "labors and pure delights," and
the "old little house" at whose threshold Evgeny's corpse was found in *The
Bronze Horseman*. But it is also the center and concentration of world order.
The household gods, the Penates, "counselors of Zeus," are praised as mysterious
powers. Precisely here, in his own Home, the human being learns the "first
science": to honor himself. Pushkin underlined those last words.

The Home is the natural space of the Feast. But *A Feast During The Plague*
takes place in the street. The first stage direction reads: "A street. A table
laid." This combination already forms a "joining of the unjoinable." When a
cart loaded with dead bodies passes by the table with the feasting guests, an
almost surrealist effect arises, recalling the appearance of horses with a cart
in a living room in Bunuel's film *Un Chien Andalou*. Such fractures of
artistic space in *A Feast During The Plague* have deep meaning: the action
takes place in a world gone mad. Homes have been abandoned; people do not live
in them and are afraid to enter them. Domestic life takes place in the street.
The homes of the living are opposed to the homes of the dead, "cold,
underground dwellings." The oxymoronic use of the word "dwelling" for the
grave is characteristic.

There are three semantic centers in the play: the participants in the feast,
whose perverse space is the street turned into a place of feasting; the priest,
whose space is the cemetery; and the Plague, which fills the entire world of
the play.

The Plague appears in the row of Pushkin's images of the elements. One should
note the principled difference between two faces of the elemental principle:
when the element appears in the guise of a human mass, its inexorability is
combined with mercy, as in Arkhip and Pugachev. When the symbol is embodied in
a natural disaster, the plague or the flood, its inexorable mercilessness and
senseless destructiveness come to the fore.

The Plague creates a situation of general death and thereby reveals the deeply
hidden properties of the other participants in the situation.

The human world, thirsting for life, joy, and gladness, answers the Plague by
accepting the challenge. In Wilson's original tragedy, the blasphemous,
anti-divine moment is emphasized much more sharply in the feast, especially in
the behavior of the Young Man. Pushkin muted it, although the priest's phrase
"godless feast, godless madmen!" hints at this shade. The joy of the feast is
rebellion. But this rebellion is directed against God only indirectly; its main
sense is the refusal to recognize the power of the Plague, a rebellion against
Fear. The center of this plot line is the Chairman's song, an apology for
courage. Joy stands against fear of death. A feast during the plague is an act
equivalent to "rapture in battle," to the courage of the seafarer on the
"enraged ocean" or the traveler seized by a hurricane:

All, all that threatens ruin
Conceals for the mortal heart
Inexpressible delights...

The Chairman's "Hymn in Honor of the Plague" has often been compared with
"The Hero," a poem written in the same Boldino period and devoted to the legend
of Napoleon's visit to the plague hospital in Jaffa. In the dialogue of Poet
and Friend, precisely the emperor's mercy - he "coldly shakes the plague by the
hand" in order to strengthen the spirit of the sick soldiers - is declared the
summit of his glory. This comparison has been used to condemn Walsingham. It
has been argued that in Napoleon contempt for danger magnificently merged with
compassion for people branded by the plague, while Walsingham, having composed
the hymn, sees only the pit with its terrible decay and hears only the clear
knock of the funeral cart. Thus these authors inexplicably discern fear of
death in the Chairman's hymn.

In comparing Walsingham and Napoleon from the poem "The Hero," one must keep
in mind that the semantic positions of these two personages are as different
as those of the Bronze Horseman and Evgeny in *The Bronze Horseman*. Napoleon
is a historical figure, "chosen," "on the throne." He is Power, the embodiment
of the idea of statehood, the representative of those forces that, without a
humane principle, become the scourge of humanity:

Leave the hero a heart! What then
Would he be without it? A tyrant...

Walsingham, however, is not a ruler but a human being. He is himself a victim
of the Plague, which has taken from him his mother and wife and may take his
life tomorrow. He should be compared not with Napoleon, but with the soldiers
lying in the hospital. The courage he shows, his bold refusal of the authority
of disease, does not call forth condemnation from Pushkin.

If Walsingham represents personality, the Priest represents a moral principle.
He condemns individualistic rebellion in the name of moral duty. Unlike Don
Juan and the Commander, however, Walsingham and the Priest have a common enemy:
the Plague. *A Feast During The Plague* is the only one of the little tragedies
that not only does not end with the death of one or both antagonists, but ends
with their actual reconciliation: the recognition of each person's right to go
his own way in accordance with his own nature and his own truth. The play is
immersed in an atmosphere of death, but none of its characters dies. Moreover,
a careful comparison of Pushkin's text with the English shows an almost
textual closeness between Pushkin's drama and passages from John Wilson's
dramatic poem *The City of the Plague*. But this only emphasizes the essential
difference: Wilson romantically admires horrors; the monstrous plague city
becomes for him the true picture of the world, and the author's desire to shake
the reader with terrible pictures becomes the central task of his aesthetics.

Behind *A Feast During The Plague*, as behind all Pushkin's little tragedies,
there arises the image of a harmonious norm of life and human relations, the
image of that Feast of love and brotherhood, freedom and mercy, which for
Pushkin constitutes the hidden essence of life.

The study of abnormal conflicts is a path toward deep comprehension of the
norm. Behind disharmony there arises a hidden image of harmony.

* * *

In the examples given, we see how the symbol acts in the role of a condensed
program of the creative process. The further development of the plot is only
the unfolding of certain potentials hidden within it. It is a deep coding
device, a distinctive "textual gene." Yet the fact that one and the same
initial symbol can unfold into different plots, and that the process of such
unfolding has an irreversible and unpredictable character, shows that the
creative process is asymmetrical by its very nature. Using Ilya Prigogine's
terminology, one can define the moment of creative inspiration as a strongly
nonequilibrium situation that excludes unambiguous predictability of
development.
