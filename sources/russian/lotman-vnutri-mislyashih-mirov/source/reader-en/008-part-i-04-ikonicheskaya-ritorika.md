## Iconic Rhetoric

The connection between the phenomenon of art and the doubling of reality has
been noted more than once by aesthetics. In this respect, ancient legends about
the birth of rhyme from echo and of drawing from the tracing of a shadow are
filled with deep meaning. At the same time, the magical function of objects
such as the mirror, which create another world resembling the reflected one but
not identical with it - an "as if" world - is just as significant as the role of
the metaphor of reflection, of mirror-likeness, for art's self-consciousness.

The possibility of doubling is the ontological precondition for transforming the
world of objects into a world of signs. The reflected image of a thing is torn
out of the practical connections natural to it - spatial, contextual, goal-
oriented, and so on - and therefore can easily be included in the modeling
connections of human consciousness. The reflection of a face cannot be included
in the connections natural to the reflected object: one cannot touch it or
caress it. But it can quite well be included in semiotic connections: one can
insult it or use it for magical manipulations. In this respect it is of the
same type as casts and imprints, for example footprints or handprints.

Magical operations, fixed by exceptionally broad ethnographic material from
different cultures, performed on a person's footprint, are usually explained by
the diffuseness of archaic consciousness, which supposedly does not distinguish
part from whole and sees in the imprint of a foot something fundamentally
identical with the person who ran by. One may, however, suggest something
slightly different: it is precisely the fact that the footprint, while being the
person, at the same time obviously is not the person, and that it is switched
off from the whole mass of everyday practical connections, that provokes its
inclusion in a semiotic situation.

Yet in the elementary fact of doubling some object, the semiotic situation is
hidden only as a pure possibility. As a rule, it remains unconscious for naive
consciousness, which is not oriented toward a sign-perception of the world. A
different situation arises when double doubling occurs, the doubling of
doubling. In these cases, the inadequacy of the object and its reflection, and
the transformation of the latter in the process of doubling, come clearly to the
fore. This naturally directs attention to the mechanism of doubling; that is, it
makes the semiotic process not spontaneous, but conscious.

The multiplicity of doubling and the transformation of the reflected image
during this process play a special role in pictorial texts. In verbal texts, the
conditional relation of content to expression, the conventional character of
this relation, is much more obvious. The exposure of this fact is given
relatively easily, and further efforts to create a poetic text are directed
toward overcoming it: poetry fuses the planes of expression and content into a
complex formation of a higher level of organization.

The pictorial arts, and their potential semiotic kernel - the mechanical
reflection of an object on a mirror plane - create the illusion of the identity
of object and image. Thus one more link is added to the process of creating the
artistic sign, or text. First, the sign-conventional nature lying at the basis
of every semiotic fact must be disclosed: the text that naive consciousness
perceives as unconditional must be understood in its sign-conventionality. In
practice this means that, at this stage, traits of the verbal are attributed to
a nonverbal text. Only at the next stage does the secondary iconization of the
text take place, corresponding to the moment in poetry when traits of the
nonverbal, or iconic, are attributed to a verbal text.

The role played in this process, especially in its first stage, by the doubling
of doubling can be seen in the function of the mirror at certain moments in the
development of pictorial art. One can say that for certain moments of painting,
the mirror on the canvas performed a typologically similar role to wordplay in
the poetic text. By revealing the conventionality lying at the basis of the
text, it made the language of art the main object of the audience's attention.
Double doubling, as a rule, is not the lot of the whole canvas, but only of a
definite part of it. In this case, in the zone of secondary doubling, there is a
sharp increase in the measure of conventionality, which exposes the sign nature
of the text as such.

Thus, for example, the pathos of Renaissance art lay in part in the affirmation
of "natural" perspective as the embodiment of a certain constant point of view.
In Velazquez's *Venus at Her Mirror*, however, the introduction of the mirror
makes it possible, within the generally accepted system of perspective, to show
the central figure, Venus, simultaneously from two points of view: the viewer
sees her from behind, and in the mirror sees her face. Point of view is singled
out as an independent structural element that can be separated from the object
given to naive contemplation and presented as a conscious and independent
entity.

In Jan van Eyck's *Portrait of Arnolfini and His Wife*, we encounter the mirror
in the same function: the central figures are visible to us on the canvas from
the front, and in the reflection from behind. Yet the effect is complicated
here, first, by the fact that the image in the mirror is given with distortion:
the spherical surface of the mirror transforms the figures, sharpening attention
to the specificity of reflection. It becomes obvious that every reflection is at
the same time a shift, a deformation that sharpens certain aspects of the
object, on the one hand, and reveals, on the other, the structural nature of the
language into whose space the given object is projected. The spherical and round
surface of the mirror emphasizes the flat and rectangular character of the
figures of the merchant and his wife, who seem as if painted onto flat glass
panes inserted within the illusionistically three-dimensional space of the
room, an illusion created by the detailed and convincing treatment of things.

The system of reflected figures and spatial perspective in the mirror is
directed perpendicular to the plane of the painting and goes beyond its limits.
An effect is created similar to the one Jan Mukarovsky noted for cinema when
analyzing cases in which cinematic sound space extends beyond screen space and
possesses greater volume. Such are, for example, cases in which a carriage is
shown on screen in such a way that the horses, placed along an axis
perpendicular to the screen, do not fall within the frame; the camera is located
where the horses would be. If the sound is edited so as to reproduce the clatter
of hooves, then the axis of sound space is positioned as if perpendicular to the
screen. It is precisely the mirror and the perspective reflected in it that
disclose the contradiction between the flat nature of the canvas and the
volumetric character of the world depicted on it: that is, the nature of the
language of painting.

The combination of mirror and metastructural elements allowed Velazquez, in
*Las Meninas*, to make the very essence of pictorial language and its relation
to the object into the object of visual knowledge. The canvas depicts the
artist at the moment when he himself is putting the image onto the canvas, while
the object of his depiction is visible to the viewer reflected in the mirror
behind him.

In all these cases, as in many others - for example, the spherical mirror that
expands the lateral space of Quentin Massys's *The Money Changer and His Wife* -
the mirror doubles what had already been doubled by the painter's brush. At the
same time, it introduces into the canvas what, by virtue of the specificity of
the accepted pictorial language, would seem to have to remain outside its
limits. In doing so, it separates, as it were, the mode of depiction from what
is depicted. The mode of depiction becomes the object of depiction. The process
of self-consciousness of the nature of language that occurs here vividly
recalls analogous phenomena in the verbal art of the baroque epoch.

The examples given concern particular cases, which taken together also belong
to the problems of the rhetoric of the text.

Rhetoric, one of the most traditional disciplines of the philological cycle, has
now received new life. The need to connect the data of linguistics and the
poetics of the text gave rise to neorhetoric, which in a short time brought an
extensive scholarly literature into being. Without touching on the problems that
arise here in all their fullness, let us single out the aspect we will need in
what follows.

A rhetorical utterance, in the terminology adopted here, is not a simple message
onto which "ornaments" have been laid from above, so that when they are removed
the basic meaning remains. In other words, a rhetorical utterance cannot be
expressed in a non-rhetorical way. Rhetorical structure lies not in the sphere
of expression, but in the sphere of content.

Unlike a non-rhetorical text, as already noted, we will call rhetorical a text
that can be represented as the structural unity of two or several subtexts
encoded by means of different, mutually untranslatable codes. These subtexts may
represent local orderings, so that different parts of the text must be read by
means of different languages. Or they may function as different words,
coextensive along the whole length of the text. In this second case, the text
presupposes a double reading, for example everyday and symbolic. All cases of
the contrapuntal collision, within a single structure, of different semiotic
languages will belong to rhetorical texts.

Characteristic of the rhetoric of the baroque text is the collision, within one
whole, of zones marked by different measures of semioticity. In the collision of
languages, one of them invariably appears as "natural," a non-language, while
the other appears as emphatically artificial. In baroque church wall paintings
in Czech lands, one can encounter the motif of a little angel in a frame. The
peculiarity of the painting consists in the fact that the frame imitates an oval
window, and the seated figure on the "windowsill" lets one little leg hang down,
as if climbing out of the frame. The leg that does not fit inside the
composition is sculptural. It is attached to the drawing as its continuation.
Thus the text is a pictorial-sculptural combination. The background behind the
figure imitates blue sky and appears as a break in the space of the fresco. The
projecting volumetric leg tears this space in another way and in the opposite
direction. The whole text is built on play between real and unreal space and on
the collision of the languages of the arts, one of which appears as a "natural"
property of the object itself, the other as an artificial imitation of it.

The art of classicism demanded unity of style. The baroque shift of local
orderings appeared as barbarism. The whole text, throughout its full length,
had to be organized evenly and coded in a single way. This does not mean,
however, a refusal of rhetorical structure. The rhetorical effect is achieved by
other means: the multilayeredness of the linguistic structure. The most common
case is one in which the object of depiction is first coded theatrically, and
only then by a poetic, lyrical, historical, or pictorial code.

In a number of cases, especially characteristic of historical prose, pastoral
poetry, and painting of the eighteenth century, the text is a direct
reproduction of a corresponding theatrical exposition or stage episode. Depending
on the genre, such a mediating text-code may be a scene from tragedy, comedy,
or ballet. Thus Charles Coypel's canvas *Psyche Abandoned by Cupid* reproduces
a ballet scene in all the conventionality of a spectacle of this genre as
interpreted by the eighteenth century. The secret of such rapprochement should
not be sought in the biography of the painter, who was also active in the
theater, since we find the same regularities in other masters of the same epoch,
including Watteau.

In speaking of the "theatricalization" of painting in certain epochs, one should
not reduce the matter to a superficial metaphor. The question has deep roots in
the very nature of theater, on the one hand, and in the essence of
"intermediate coding," on the other.

The following aspects of this two-in-one problem can be distinguished.

For every act of semiotic awareness, the distinction in surrounding reality
between significant and insignificant elements is essential. Elements that do
not carry meaning, from the point of view of the given modeling system, as if do
not exist. The fact of their real existence recedes into the background in the
face of their irrelevance in the given modeling system. Existing, they as if
cease to exist in the system of culture. The singling out in the surrounding
world of such a layer of culturally relevant phenomena is the initial and
essential act of any semiotic modeling of culture. To carry it out, a certain
primary coding is necessary. It may be realized by identifying life situations
with mythological situations, and real people with characters of myth or ritual.
At different stages of culture, such a mediating code may be etiquette or
ritual - "that exists which has equivalents in ritual" - or historical narrative
- "that has genuine being which will be entered onto the tablets of history."
Theater, however, is especially active in this respect, combining a number of
aspects of the systems named above.

A widespread consequence of the fact that theater served as the intermediate
code between life object and painted canvas was the portrait manner in which the
model was dressed in some theatrical costume. Such are numerous eighteenth-
century female portraits in the costumes of Vestals, Dianas, and Sapphos, and
male portraits a la Titus, Alexander the Great, or Mars. The fact that precisely
theater, and not an indefinite mass of cultural-mythological representations,
functions as the coding mechanism is confirmed by the character of the costumes,
which reproduce stage properties assigned by eighteenth-century theatrical
tradition to one or another character. Such costume stylization means that in
order to identify with a character significant in the given culture and thereby
become worthy of the artist's brush, a real person had to be likened to a
definite known hero of the stage. Such coding has a reverse effect on people's
real behavior in life situations. The examples confirming this are numerous. In
the present connection, it is curious to point to cases in which the stylized
conventional costume of the portrait affects real fashion. In the spread of
antique dress in the Empire style in Petersburg, a decisive role was played by
the portraits of Elisabeth Vigee Le Brun. They proved stronger than government
prohibitions, and Maria Feodorovna appeared at an intimate dinner on March 11,
1801, the last in the life of Paul I, in a forbidden "antique" dress.

Another factor was the set of subjects and the related idea of pictorial theme.
When selecting what, from the point of view of one or another cultural system,
is worthy of becoming an object of depiction, and how this object should be
depicted, what moment or state of it is "pictorial," preliminary coding in the
system of another artistic language, most often theatrical or literary, plays an
important role. An essential moment in the singling out of a "pictorial
situation" is the segmentation of the flow of time in which the given object is
included in its real being. The continuity and uninterruptedness of the temporal
flow in which the object of depiction is immersed is opposed by the singled-out
and stopped moment of depiction. The psychological instrument for realizing this
switch is often the preliminary understanding of life as theater.

Imitating the dynamic continuity of reality, theater at the same time breaks it
into segments, scenes, and thereby singles out, in its continuous flow, integral
discrete units. Within itself, such a unit is thought as immanently closed,
tending toward stoppedness in time. It is no accident that terms such as
"scene," "picture," and "act" cover equally the domains of theater and painting.

Between the non-discrete flow of life and the singling out of discrete
"stopped" moments characteristic of the pictorial arts, theater occupies an
intermediate position. On the one hand, it differs from painting and approaches
life by continuity and movement; on the other, it differs from life and
approaches painting by dividing the flow of action into segments, each of which
at every separate moment tends toward compositional organization within any
synchronous slice of the action. Instead of the continuous flow of extra-artistic
reality, we have something like a series of separate, immanently organized
pictures, with instantaneous transitions from one pictorial solution to another.

The intermediate position of theater between the moving and non-discrete world
of reality and the immobile and discrete world of the pictorial arts determined
the fact of constant exchange of codes, on the one hand between theater and
people's real behavior, and on the other between theater and the pictorial arts.
The consequence is that life and painting, in a whole series of cases, communicate
with one another through the mediation of theater, which here performs the
function of an intermediate code, a translator-code.

The interaction of theater and behavior has as its result the fact that beside
the constantly active tendency in the history of theater to liken stage life to
real life, the opposite tendency is just as constant: to liken real life, or
certain of its spheres, to theater. This latter tendency becomes especially
noticeable in cultures that develop clearly expressed spheres of ritualized
behavior. If in its origins theatrical action goes back to ritual, then in later
historical development the reverse borrowing often takes place: ritual absorbs
the norms of theater. Thus, for example, the court ceremonial of the imperial
court created by Napoleon I was openly oriented not toward continuity with the
royal court etiquette destroyed by the Revolution, but toward the norms by
which seventeenth-century French theater represented the court of the Roman
emperors. Talma took an active part in the development of the etiquette. Ballet
powerfully invaded the sphere of military drill and parade. Theatrical spectacle
seized even such an apparently alien sphere as battle practice. Lermontov
described the feeling of spectators watching a clash in battle "without
bloodthirsty excitement, as at a tragic ballet."

If the epoch of classicism sharply delimited the spheres of ritualized and
practical behavior, romanticism was characterized by the penetration of
theatrical norms of behavior into everyday life. On the one hand, the closed
sphere of "high" state behavior was abolished; on the other, the "middle" sphere
of love and friendship behavior, situations such as "communion with nature," or
solitude "amid the noisy ball," was ritualized.

The emergence of a "theater of everyday behavior" changed the way a person
looked at himself. "Poetic" moments and situations were singled out in life and
declared the only significant, even the only existing, ones. In "non-poetic"
moments a person as if went backstage and, from the point of view of the "play
of life" being acted on stage, as if ceased to exist until the next entrance.
Thus, for example, in the consciousness of a romantic of the epoch of the
Napoleonic wars, battle life is significant and possesses genuine reality - that
is, can become the content of texts of various kinds - only as a chain of
heroic, sublimely tragic, and touching scenes. Precisely for this reason,
Stendhal's and Tolstoy's depictions of war made such an impression on readers:
they moved the stage platform backstage, asserting that genuine being takes
place precisely there, while on the stage there occurs only "as if existence,"
imaginary life.

"Genuine reality" belonged not only to definite situations, but also to stable
sets of roles proper to a given epoch. In order to exist - "to feel ourselves
more strongly," as Lavater wrote to Karamzin - a person had to add sign-being to
his physical being. Lavater had in mind simple doubling: our eye, he wrote, is
not so arranged as to see itself without the mediation of a mirror. In definite
cultural epochs, this is achieved by identifying one's personality with some
typical role significant in the given system.

The choice of role was accompanied by the choice of gesture. A sphere of
"significant movements" - gestures - was singled out, in contrast to purely
everyday movements not connected with any meaning.

The critique of classicism as an "age of pose" by no means signifies a refusal
of gesture. The sphere of the significant is simply shifted: ritualization and
semantic content move into those spheres of behavior that previously were
perceived as completely extra-sign. Simple clothing, careless pose, touching
movement, demonstrative refusal of signness, the subjective denial of gesture -
all become bearers of basic cultural meanings, that is, they turn into gestures.
In Lermontov, "all the movements" of the heroine are at once "full of expression"
and "dear simplicity." "Dear simplicity" means the refusal of gesturality; but
the spiritualized character of these movements, their fullness of meaning,
turns them into gestures of a new type. This could be compared with the refusal
of the system of gesture developed for the stage by Karatygin and the transition
to Mochalov's "sincere" gestures.

Pushkin's lines on Olenina's eyes are revealing in this respect. With the
demonstrative affirmation of "childlike simplicity" as the highest value, a
pictorial-theatrical code is introduced to interpret the meaning of what, without
it, has only physical being. The "angel of Raphael" refers to the *Sistine
Madonna*, known to Pushkin through engravings and perhaps also through literary
descriptions. Lel, the "Slavic Amor," or perhaps simply Amor, refers to the
pictorial and theatrical-ballet tradition.

A triangle is created: the real behavior of a person in the given system of
culture, theater, and the pictorial arts. Inside this triangle there occurs an
intensive exchange of symbolism and means of expression. Theatricality
penetrates everyday life and influences painting; everyday life affects both,
advancing the slogan of "naturalness"; finally, painting and sculpture actively
influence both theater, determining the system of poses and movements, and
extra-artistic reality, raising it to the level of "having meaning."

It is highly essential here that, in passing into another sphere, one or another
meaningful structure preserves a connection with its natural context. Thus arise
the "theatricality" of gesture in painting and in life, the "pictorialness" of
theater or of life itself, and the "naturalness" of stage and canvas. It is
precisely this double belonging to different semiotic systems that creates the
rhetorical situation in which there lies a powerful source for the production of
new meanings.

Rhetoric - the transfer into one semiotic sphere of the structural principles of
another - is possible at the junction of other arts as well. An exceptionally
large role here is played by the whole sum of semiotic processes at the boundary
between word and image. Thus, for example, surrealism in painting, in a certain
sense, can be interpreted as the transfer into the purely pictorial sphere of
verbal metaphor and of purely verbal principles of fantasy. Yet precisely
because the combination of the verbal principle and rhetoric seems natural, it
seemed useful to us to show the possibility of rhetorical construction outside
connection with the word.
