## Symbol In The System Of Culture

The word "symbol" is one of the most polysemous in the system of semiotic
sciences. The expression "symbolic meaning" is widely used as a simple synonym
for sign-character. When the context emphasizes the conventionality of the
relations between content and expression, researchers often speak of a symbolic
function and of symbols. At the same time, Saussure already opposed symbols to
conventional signs, emphasizing the iconic element in the former. Let us recall
that Saussure wrote, in this connection, that scales can be a symbol of justice
because they iconically contain the idea of equilibrium, whereas a cart cannot.

According to another classification, a symbol is defined as a sign whose
meaning is some sign of another series or another language. This definition is
opposed by the tradition of interpreting the symbol as a certain sign
expression of a higher and absolute nonsign essence. In the first case,
symbolic meaning acquires an emphatically rational character and is interpreted
as a means for adequately translating the plane of expression into the plane of
content. In the second, the content of the irrational flickers through the
expression, and the symbol plays the role of a bridge from the rational world
into the mystical world.

It is enough to note that every linguo-semiotic system, whether one actually
given in the history of culture or one describing some significant object, feels
its own incompleteness if it does not provide its own definition of the symbol.
The point is not to describe, in the most exact and complete way, some single
object identical in all cases. The point is the presence in each semiotic
system of a structural position without which the system is not complete:
certain essential functions do not receive realization. The mechanisms that
serve these functions are stubbornly called by the word "symbol," even though
both the nature of the functions and, still more, the nature of the mechanisms
through which they are realized are exceptionally difficult to reduce to any
invariant. Thus one can say that even if we do not know what a symbol is, every
system knows what "its symbol" is and needs it for the work of the semiotic
structure.

In order to make an attempt to define the character of this function, it is
more convenient not to give some universal definition, but to start from the
ideas given to us intuitively by our cultural experience and then try to
generalize them.

The most familiar idea of the symbol is connected with the idea of some content
that, in its turn, serves as the plane of expression for another content, as a
rule one more valuable culturally. At the same time, the symbol should be
distinguished from reminiscence or quotation, because in them the "external"
plane of content-expression is not independent but is a kind of sign-index,
pointing to some more extensive text to which it stands in a metonymic
relation. The symbol, by contrast, both in the plane of expression and in the
plane of content, always represents a certain text: it possesses a certain
single meaning closed in on itself and a clearly expressed boundary that allows
it to be distinctly singled out from the surrounding semiotic context. This last
circumstance seems to us especially essential for the capacity "to be a
symbol."

There is always something archaic in the symbol. Every culture needs a layer of
texts that perform the function of archaism. The condensation of symbols is
usually especially noticeable here. This perception of symbols is not
accidental: their core group really has a deeply archaic nature and goes back
to the pre-literate epoch, when certain signs, as a rule elementary in graphic
terms, represented folded mnemonic programs of texts and plots preserved in the
oral memory of the collective. Symbols have retained the capacity to preserve,
in folded form, exceptionally extensive and significant texts. Yet another,
also archaic, feature is still more interesting for us: the symbol, while
representing a completed text, need not be included in any syntagmatic row; and
if it is included in one, it still preserves its semantic and structural
independence. It is easily separated from its semiotic surroundings and just as
easily enters a new textual environment. This is connected with an essential
feature of the symbol: the symbol never belongs to any single synchronous slice
of culture. It always pierces that slice vertically, coming from the past and
going into the future. The memory of the symbol is always older than the memory
of its nonsymbolic textual surroundings.

Every text of culture is fundamentally heterogeneous. Even in a strictly
synchronous slice, the heterogeneity of the languages of culture forms a
complex polyphony. The widespread idea that, by saying "the epoch of
classicism" or "the epoch of romanticism," we have defined the unity of a
cultural period or at least its dominant tendency is only an illusion generated
by the accepted language of description. The wheels of various mechanisms of
culture move at different speeds. The tempo of the development of natural
language cannot be compared with the tempo, for example, of fashion. The sacred
sphere is always more conservative than the profane. This increases the
internal diversity that is the law of culture's existence. Symbols are among
the most stable elements of the cultural continuum.

As an important mechanism of cultural memory, symbols transfer texts, plot
schemes, and other semiotic formations from one layer of culture to another.
Constant sets of symbols that pierce the diachrony of culture take on, to a
large degree, the function of mechanisms of unity: by carrying out culture's
memory of itself, they prevent it from falling apart into isolated
chronological layers. The unity of the basic set of dominant symbols and the
duration of their cultural life largely determine the national and areal
boundaries of cultures.

The nature of the symbol, considered from this point of view, is dual. On the
one hand, piercing the thickness of cultures, the symbol is realized in its
invariant essence. In this aspect we can observe its repeatability. The symbol
will appear as something heterogeneous to the textual space surrounding it, as a
messenger from other cultural epochs, that is, from other cultures, as a
reminder of the ancient, or "eternal," foundations of culture. On the other
hand, the symbol actively correlates with the cultural context, is transformed
under its influence, and transforms it in turn. Its invariant essence is
realized in variants. It is precisely in the changes undergone by the "eternal"
meaning of the symbol in a given cultural context that this context most
clearly reveals its own changeability.

This last capacity is connected with the fact that the most historically active
symbols are characterized by a certain indeterminacy in the relation between
the text-expression and the text-content. The latter always belongs to a more
multidimensional semantic space. Therefore expression does not fully cover
content, but only hints at it. Whether this is caused by the fact that the
expression is only a brief mnemonic sign of a diffuse text-content, or by the
belonging of the former to the profane, open, and displayed sphere of culture
while the latter belongs to the sacred, esoteric, and secret sphere, or by the
romantic need to "express the inexpressible," is indifferent in the present
case. What matters is only that the semantic potentials of the symbol are
always broader than their given realization: the connections into which the
symbol enters, by means of its expression, with one or another semiotic
environment do not exhaust all its semantic valences. This forms the semantic
reserve with whose help the symbol can enter into unexpected connections,
changing its own essence and deforming the textual environment in unforeseen
ways.

From this point of view it is significant that symbols elementary in their
expression possess greater cultural-semantic capacity than complex ones. The
cross, the circle, the pentagram possess far greater semantic potentials than
"Apollo flaying Marsyas," by virtue of the gap between expression and content,
their nonprojectability onto one another. It is precisely "simple" symbols that
form the symbolic core of a culture, and it is precisely saturation with them
that allows us to judge the symbolizing or desymbolizing orientation of a
culture as a whole.

Connected with this is the set toward a symbolizing or desymbolizing reading of
texts. The first allows one to read as symbols texts, or fragments of texts,
that in their natural context were not designed for such perception. The second
turns symbols into simple messages. What for symbolizing consciousness is a
symbol appears, under the opposite set, as a symptom. If the desymbolizing 20th
century saw in one or another person or literary character a "representative"
of an idea, class, or group, then Blok perceived people and phenomena of
everyday life as symbols of the manifestation of the infinite in the finite.
Compare his reaction to the personality of Klyuev or Stenich, the latter
reflected in his article "Russian Dandies."

The two tendencies are very interestingly mixed in Dostoevsky's artistic
thinking. Dostoevsky, an attentive reader of newspapers and a collector of
reporter's factual material, especially criminal-judicial chronicles, sees in
the scattered mass of newspaper facts visible symptoms of hidden social
diseases. The view of the writer as doctor, as in Lermontov's preface to *A
Hero of Our Time*, as natural scientist, as in Baratynsky's *The Concubine*, or
as sociologist, as in Balzac, turned the writer into a decipherer of symptoms.
Symptomology belongs to the sphere of semiotics; the old name for symptomology
was "medical semiotics." Yet the relations of the "accessible," expression, and
the "inaccessible," content, are constant and unambiguous here, built on the
principle of a "black box." Thus Turgenev in his novels records the symptoms of
social processes with the exactness of a sensitive instrument. Connected with
this is the idea of one or another character as a "representative." To say that
Rudin is a "representative of the 'superfluous people' in Russia" means to
assert that in his person he embodies the basic traits of this group and that
one can judge the group by his character. To say that Stavrogin or Fedka in
*Demons* symbolizes certain phenomena, types, or forces means to assert that
the essence of these forces has to some degree expressed itself in these
heroes, but that in itself it remains not yet fully disclosed and mysterious.
Both approaches constantly collide and interweave in Dostoevsky's
consciousness.

The opposition of symbol and reminiscence is built differently. We have already
pointed to their essential difference. Now it is appropriate to indicate one
more difference: the symbol exists before the given text and independently of
it. It enters the writer's memory from the depths of cultural memory and comes
alive in a new text like a seed that has fallen into new soil. Reminiscence,
reference, and quotation are organic parts of the new text, functional only in
its synchrony. They go from the text into the depth of memory, while the symbol
goes from the depths of memory into the text.

It is therefore no accident that what appears in the creative process as a
symbol, a suggestive mechanism of memory, is realized in readerly perception as
reminiscence, since the processes of creation and perception are directed
oppositely: in the first, the final text is the result; in the second, it is
the starting point. Let us clarify this with an example.

In the plans for Dostoevsky's "poem" *The Emperor*, the design for a novel
about Ivan Antonovich, there are notes about how Mirovich persuades Ivan
Antonovich, who has grown up in isolation and knows none of life's temptations,
to agree to a conspiracy: he "shows him the world, from the attic, the Neva and
so on," shows him God's world, and says, "Everything is yours, if only you want
it. Let us go!" It is obvious that the plot of temptation by the specter of
power was linked in Dostoevsky's consciousness with the symbol: the transfer,
by the tempter, of the tempted one to a high place - a mountain, the roof of
the temple, or in Dostoevsky the attic of a prison tower - and the display of
the world lying at his feet. For Dostoevsky the Gospel symbolism unfolded into
the plot of the novel; for the reader, the plot of the novel was explained by a
Gospel reminiscence.

The opposition of these two aspects is, however, conditional, and in such
complex texts as Dostoevsky's novels it cannot always be carried through.

We have already said that Dostoevsky perceived newspaper chronicles and the
facts of criminal trials both as symptoms and as symbols. Essential aspects of
his artistic and ideological-philosophical thinking are connected with this.
Their meaning can be disclosed through the contrast between Tolstoy's and
Dostoevsky's relation to the word.

Already in the early story "The Wood-Cutting," a principle emerged that
remained characteristic for Tolstoy throughout his work: officers eating
breakfast under fire from the mountaineers, with feigned indifference and inner
tension, hear a cannon shot. The narrator asks lazily where the wine was
bought, while in the depths of his soul two voices speak with equal clarity:
one saying, "Lord, receive my spirit in peace," the other saying, "I hope I
will not duck, but will smile while the cannonball flies past." At that same
moment something terribly unpleasant whistles over his head and the cannonball
slaps down two steps away. Volokhov remarks, with perfect coolness, that if he
were Napoleon or Frederick he would certainly say something witty. The narrator
answers that he has already done so. Volokhov adds that no one will write it
down; the narrator says he will. From behind them Antonov, irritated, spits to
the side and says that the damned thing nearly hit his legs.

If we speak of the features of Tolstoy's word manifested in this passage, we
will have to note its complete conventionality: the relation between expression
and content is conditional. The word can be a means of expressing truth, as in
Antonov's exclamation, or of lying, as it becomes in the officers' speech. The
possibility of separating the plane of expression and joining it to any other
content makes the word a dangerous instrument, a convenient condenser of social
falsehood. Therefore, in matters where the need for truth becomes vitally
necessary, Tolstoy would prefer to do without words altogether. Thus Pierre
Bezukhov's verbal declaration of love to Helene is false, while true love is
explained not in words but in "glances and smiles," or, as with Kitty and
Levin, in cryptograms. Akim's wordless, unintelligible "tae" in *The Power of
Darkness* has truth as its content, while eloquence in Tolstoy is always false.
Truth is the natural order of Nature. Life purified of words, and of social
symbolism, in its natural essence, is truth.

Let us give several examples of narration from Dostoevsky's *The Idiot*. In
one place, "there was obviously something else here," some mental and emotional
"mess," something like an insatiable feeling of contempt that had gone
completely beyond measure, in short something extremely ridiculous and
inadmissible in decent society. Elsewhere, a glance "looked as if it were
posing a riddle." Elsewhere, someone is terrified by certain recent glances and
words. Elsewhere, a character says that another could not love because she was
too proud - no, not proud, that was a mistake, but vain - no, not even that:
self-loving to the point of madness. Elsewhere, "those are very good feelings,
only somehow it all came out wrong; there is illness here and something else!"

These passages, chosen almost at random, belong to the speeches of different
characters and of the narrator himself. Yet all of them are characterized by
one common trait: the words do not name things and ideas, but only hint at
them, at the same time making clear the impossibility of finding an exact name
for them. "And something else" becomes a kind of marker of the whole style,
which is built on endless clarifications and reservations that clarify nothing,
but only demonstrate the impossibility of final clarification. In this
connection one might recall Ippolit's words from *The Idiot*: in every
brilliant or new human thought, or even simply in every serious human thought
born in someone's head, there always remains something that cannot in any way
be transmitted to other people, even if one writes whole volumes and explains
one's thought for thirty-five years. There will always remain something that
will in no way want to come out from under one's skull and will remain with
one forever; with it one will die, perhaps without transmitting to anyone the
most important part of one's idea.

In such an interpretation, this thought, essential for Dostoevsky, receives a
romantic sound, drawing close to the idea of "inexpressibility." Dostoevsky's
relation to the word is complex. On the one hand, in various ways he not only
emphasizes the inadequacy of the word to its meaning, but also constantly
resorts to an inexact, incompetent word, to witnesses who do not understand
what they are witnessing and who give the external appearance of facts an
interpretation known in advance to be inexact. On the other hand, these
inexact and even incorrect words and testimonies cannot be treated as having no
relation to truth and simply crossed out, as can the whole layer of socially
hypocritical utterances in Tolstoy's prose. They constitute an approximation
to truth, they hint at it. Truth glimmers dimly through them. But it only
glimmers through all words, except the Gospel words. In this respect there is
no fundamental difference between the testimony of the competent and the
incompetent, the perceptive and the foolish, since both separation from truth,
inadequacy to it, and the capacity to be a path toward it lie in the very nature
of the human word.

It is not difficult to notice that, in such an understanding, the word acquires
the character not of a conventional sign but of a symbol. Closer to
Dostoevsky's understanding is not Zhukovsky's romantic "The Inexpressible," but
Baratynsky's analytic word:

Alien to explicit meaning,
For me it is a symbol
Of feelings whose expression
I have not found in languages.

The striving to see in a separate fact a deep symbolic meaning is intrinsic to
Dostoevsky's text, although it does not constitute its only organizing
tendency.

Observation of the movement of Dostoevsky's creative designs provides
interesting material. In conceiving some character, Dostoevsky designates it by
a name or marks it with some feature that allows him to bring it closer to one
or another symbol present in his memory. Then he "plays through" various plot
situations, estimating how this symbolic figure might behave in them. The
polysemy of the symbol allows him to vary substantially the openings, middles,
and endgames of the plot situations he analyzes, to which he returns many times
while trying out one or another "move."

Thus, for example, behind the image of Nastasya Filippovna in *The Idiot* there
immediately and openly arises - directly named in the text by Kolya Ivolgin and
indirectly by Totsky - the image of *The Lady of the Camellias*, the "camelia."
Dostoevsky, however, perceives this image as a complex symbol connected with
European culture and, transferring it into the Russian context, observes not
without polemical pathos how a Russian "camelia" will behave. Yet other
symbols, guardians of cultural memory, also played a role in the structure of
Nastasya Filippovna's image. One of them we can reconstruct only
hypothetically. The design and initial work on *The Idiot* belong to the period
of the foreign journey at the end of the 1860s, one of the strongest
impressions of which was the visit to the Dresden picture gallery. Its echoes,
including the mention of Holbein's painting, sound in the final text of the
novel. From A. G. Dostoevskaya's 1867 diary, we know that when she visited the
gallery she first "saw all Rembrandt's paintings" alone, and then, after
walking through the gallery once more with Dostoevsky, "Fedya pointed out the
best works and spoke about art."

It is difficult to suppose that Dostoevsky did not pay attention to
Rembrandt's *Susanna and the Elders*. This painting, like *The Abduction of
Ganymede* hanging in the same hall - "Rembrandt's strange painting," in
Pushkin's words - must have arrested Dostoevsky's attention by its treatment of
a theme that disturbed him: a depraved assault on a child. In his canvas,
Rembrandt departed far from the biblical plot. In chapter 13 of the prophet
Daniel, where the story of Susanna is told, the matter concerns a respectable
married woman, mistress of a house, and the "elders" who encroached on her
virtue were not necessarily old men. Rembrandt, meanwhile, depicted a thin,
pale adolescent girl, deprived of feminine attractiveness and defenseless. He
gave the elders traits of repulsive lust, contrastingly contradicting their
advanced age. Compare the contrast between the lustful heat of the eagle and
the mask of fright and revulsion on the face of Ganymede, depicted not as a
mythological youth but as a child.

The fact that at the beginning of the novel we find Nastasya Filippovna at the
moment when one "elder," Totsky, resells her to another - formally to Ganya, but
with the implication that in fact it is to General Epanchin - and the very story
of Totsky's seduction of someone almost a child make it probable that Nastasya
Filippovna was perceived by Dostoevsky not only as a "camelia" but also as a
"Susanna." She is also the "woman taken in adultery," of whom Christ said,
"Whoever among you is without sin, let him first cast a stone at her," and
refused to condemn her: "I do not condemn you." At the intersection of the
image-symbols "camelia," Susanna, and sinful woman there is born the folded
program that, when plunged into the plot-space of the novel's designs, will
unfold and be transformed in the image of Nastasya Filippovna. Equally probable
is the link between the image of the Brigadier's wife from Fonvizin's play and
General Epanchina as a symbol recorded in memory and its plot unfolding.

The case of Ippolit Terentyev is more complex. This character is multiplanar,
and it is likely that various "symbols of life," that is, symbolically
interpreted facts of reality, were woven into him first of all. One detail,
however, draws attention: Ippolit's wish, before death, to deliver a speech to
the people, and his belief that it is enough for him to "talk with the people
from the window for only a quarter of an hour" and the people will at once
"agree with him in everything" and "follow" him. This detail, which lodged in
Dostoevsky's memory as a capacious symbol, went back to the moment of
Belinsky's death, which so struck his whole circle. Turgenev recalled that just
before his death Belinsky spoke for two hours without stopping, as if to the
Russian people, and often turned to his wife, asking her to remember everything
well and transmit these words faithfully to whom it concerned. Nekrasov also
remembered this: on the day of death Belinsky rose from his bed, the mute
chest found strength again, and the voice thundered; joyfully he cried
"Forward!" and imagined the people and the ringing of Moscow bells, as if he
stood in the square among the people and spoke.

The vivid episode lodged in the writer's memory became symbolized and began to
manifest the typical traits of the behavior of the symbol in culture: to
accumulate and organize new experience around itself, turning into a kind of
condenser of memory, and then to unfold into a certain plot multiplicity that
the author later combined with other plot constructions, making selections. The
initial kinship with Belinsky was almost lost in the process, having undergone
numerous transformations.

It should be kept in mind that the symbol can be expressed in a syncretic
verbal-visual form that, on the one hand, is projected onto the planes of
various texts and, on the other, is transformed under the reverse influence of
texts. Thus, for example, it is easy to notice that in V. Tatlin's Monument to
the Third International (1919-1920), the image of the Tower of Babel from the
painting by Bruegel the Elder is structurally recreated. This connection is not
accidental: the interpretation of revolution as a revolt against God was a
stable and widespread association in the literature and culture of the first
years of the revolution. If in the God-fighting tradition of romanticism the
hero of rebellion became the Demon, to whom the romantics gave traits of
exaggerated individualism, then in the avant-garde literature of the
post-revolutionary years the mass and anonymous character of rebellion was
emphasized. Compare Mayakovsky's *Mystery-Bouffe*. Already in Marx's formula,
very popular in those years, "the proletarians storm heaven," there was a
reference to the myth of the Tower of Babel, subjected to a double inversion:
first, the evaluations of heaven and the earth attacking it were exchanged;
second, the myth of the separation of peoples was replaced by the idea of their
union, that is, the International.

Thus a chain is established: biblical text, Bruegel's paintings, Marx's
utterance, Tatlin's Monument to the Third International. The symbol appears as
a distinct mechanism of collective memory.

Now we can try to outline the place of the symbol among other sign elements.
The symbol differs from the conventional sign by the presence of an iconic
element, a certain likeness between the planes of expression and content. The
difference between iconic signs and symbols can be illustrated by the antithesis
of icon and painting. In a painting, three-dimensional reality is represented
by a two-dimensional image. Yet the incomplete projectability of the plane of
expression onto the plane of content is hidden by an illusionistic effect: the
perceiver is encouraged to believe in full likeness. In the icon, and in the
symbol generally, the nonprojectability of the plane of expression onto the
plane of content enters into the very nature of the communicative functioning
of the sign. Content only flickers through expression, and expression only
hints at content. In this respect one can speak of a merging of icon with
index: expression points to content to the same degree that it depicts it.
Hence the well-known conventionality of the symbolic sign.

Thus the symbol appears as a condenser of all the principles of signness and,
at the same time, leads beyond the limits of signness. It is a mediator between
different spheres of semiosis, and also between semiotic and extrasemiotic
reality. Equally, it is a mediator between the synchrony of the text and the
memory of culture. Its role is the role of a semiotic condenser.

To generalize, one can say that the structure of symbols of one or another
culture forms a system isomorphic and isofunctional with the genetic memory of
the individual.
