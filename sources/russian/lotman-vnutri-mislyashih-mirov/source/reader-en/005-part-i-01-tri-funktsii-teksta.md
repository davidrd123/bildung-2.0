## Three Functions Of The Text

In the system developed by Saussure, which for a long time determined the
direction of semiotic thought, there is an obvious preference for the study of
language rather than speech, for the structure of the code rather than the text.
Speech, and its delimited, articulated form - the text - interest the linguist
only as raw material, as the manifestation of a linguistic structure. Everything
relevant in speech, or in the text, is given in language, or in the code.
Elements present in the text but without correspondence in the code are not
bearers of meaning. This corresponds to Saussure's decisive statement: "One must
from the very beginning take one's stand on the ground of language and regard it
as the norm for all other manifestations of speech activity." To take language
as the norm means to make it the scientific point of reference for determining
what is essential and what is inessential in linguistic activity. Naturally,
everything that has no correspondence in language, or in the code, is "removed"
when the message is deciphered. Once the metal of linguistic structure has been
smelted from the ore of speech, only slag remains. In precisely this sense, a
science of language can do without the analysis of speech.

But behind this scientific position stands a whole complex of almost everyday
ideas about the function of language, ideas that are not directly expressed. If
the linguist-scholar is interested in the structure of language extracted from
the text, then the everyday receiver of information is interested in the content
of the message. In both cases the text appears as something valuable not in
itself, but only as a kind of package from whose depths the object of interest
is extracted.

For the receiver of the message, the following logical sequence seems natural:

Thought, or the content of the message, passes through the coding mechanism of
language into a text. The text then passes through the decoding mechanism of
language, and again becomes thought, or the content of the message.

Of course, one should recall Benveniste's warning. He pointed out that from the
fact that we are not conscious of the linguistic operations we perform, and from
the fact that "we can say whatever we like,"

"there arises the widespread conviction that the process of thought and speech
are two activities different in their very basis, joined only for the practical
purposes of communication, each of them having its own sphere and its own
independent possibilities; language, on this view, gives reason the means for
what is usually called the expression of thought."

And further:

"Of course, language, when it appears in speech, is used to transmit 'what we
want to say.' But the phenomenon that we call 'what we want to say,' or 'what
we have in mind,' or 'our thought,' or by some other name, is the content of
thought. It is very difficult to define it as an independent essence without
resorting to terms such as 'intention' or 'psychic structure,' and so on. This
content takes form only when it is uttered, and only in that way. It is formed
by language and in language."

Still, one can imagine a certain meaning that remains invariant through all
transformations of the text. This meaning can be imagined as a pre-textual
message realized in the text. The "meaning - text" model is built on such a
presumption. In the ideal case, it is assumed that the informational content
does not change either qualitatively or in volume: the receiver decodes the text
and receives the original message. Once again the text appears only as the
"technical packaging" of the message in which the receiver is interested.

Behind this view of the work of the semiotic mechanism stands the conviction
that its goal is the adequate transmission of some message. The system works
"well" if the message received by the addressee is fully identical to the one
sent by the addresser, and "badly" if differences are present between these
texts. These differences are classified as "errors," for the avoidance of which
special mechanisms of structure work, redundancy in particular.

This conviction is not groundless: it points to an exceptionally important
function of semiotic structures. Yet one must admit that as soon as we accept
this function as the only one, or even as the main one, we find ourselves before
a series of paradoxes.

If we see the adequacy of message transmission as the main criterion for judging
the effectiveness of semiotic systems, we will have to admit that all naturally
arising linguistic structures are rather badly arranged. In order for a
sufficiently complex message to be received with absolute identity, conditions
are needed that are practically unattainable in a natural situation. The
addresser and the addressee would have to use completely identical codes; in
effect, in semiotic terms, they would have to be something like a doubled version
of one and the same personality, since a code includes not only a certain
two-dimensional set of rules for enciphering and deciphering the message, but a
multidimensional hierarchy. Even the statement that both participants in
communication use one and the same natural language - English, Russian,
Estonian, and so on - does not ensure identity of code, since unity of
linguistic experience and identity in the volume of memory are also required.
To this must be added unity of ideas about norm, linguistic reference, and
pragmatics. If we add the influence of cultural tradition, that is, the semiotic
memory of culture, and the inevitable individuality with which that tradition is
opened to one or another member of the collective, it becomes obvious that the
codes of sender and receiver can coincide in reality only to a very relative
degree. From this inevitably follows the relative identity of the initial and
received texts. From this point of view, it can indeed seem that natural
language performs its assigned work badly. As for the language of poetry, there
is no need even to speak of it.

Thus it becomes clear that, for a full guarantee of adequacy between transmitted
and received message, an artificial, simplified language and artificially
simplified communicants are necessary: with a strictly limited volume of memory
and the complete erasure from the semiotic personality of its cultural baggage.
The mechanism created in this way will be able to serve only a limited circle of
semiotic needs; the universalism proper to natural languages will be alien to it
in principle.

Can one consider that this artificial model should be regarded as the model of
language as such, as its ideal, from which language differs only by
imperfection, the natural result of Nature's "unreasonable" creation? Artificial
languages model not language as such, but one of its functions: the capacity for
adequate transmission of a message. For, by reaching perfection in the
realization of that function, semiotic structures lose the capacity to serve
other functions proper to them in their natural state.

What, then, are these functions?

Here, first of all, the creative function should be named. Every system that
realizes the full set of semiotic possibilities not only transmits ready-made
messages, but also serves as a generator of new ones.

What, then, shall we call "new messages"? First of all, let us agree on what we
will not call by this name. Messages obtained from certain initial messages as a
result of unambiguous transformations - messages that are the fruit of
symmetrical transformations of the initial message, where launching the
transformation in reverse gives us the original text - will not be considered
new. If a translation from language L1 of text T1 into language L2 leads to the
appearance of a text T2 such that, by the operation of reverse translation, we
obtain the initial text T1, then we will not consider text T2 new in relation to
T1. From this point of view, the correct solving of mathematical problems does
not create new texts. Here one can recall Wittgenstein's proposition that within
the limits of logic nothing new can be said.

The polar opposite of artificial languages is represented by semiotic systems in
which the creative function is strongest. It is obvious that if even the most
mediocre poem is translated into another language, that is, into the language of
another poetic system, the operation of reverse translation will not give the
initial text. The very fact that one and the same poem can be translated many
times by different translators testifies that, instead of exact correspondence
to text T1, in this case a certain space is correlated with it. Any of the texts
filling that space - T1, T2, T3, and so on up to Tn - will be a possible
interpretation of the initial text. In place of exact correspondence, one of the
possible interpretations; in place of symmetrical transformation, an
asymmetrical one; in place of identity between the elements composing T1 and T2,
their conventional equivalence. When French poetry is translated into Russian,
the rendering of French twelve-syllable syllabic verse by Russian six-foot
syllabo-tonic iambic verse is a convention, a tribute to an established
tradition. In principle, however, French syllabics could also be translated by
means of Russian syllabics. The translator is faced with the need to choose. An
even greater uncertainty arises, for example, in the transformation of a novel
into a film.

The text arising in such cases we will regard as new, and the act of translation
that creates it as creative.

The scheme of adequate transmission of a text when an artificial language is
used may be represented as follows: the sender and receiver use one shared code,
K.

The scheme of artistic translation shows that sender and receiver use different
codes, K1 and K2, which intersect but are not identical. In the case of reverse
translation, this will yield not the initial text but some third text, T3. Still
closer to the real process of message circulation is the case in which before
the sender there stands not one code, but a multiple space of codes - k1, k2,
k3, and so on up to kn - each of which is a complex hierarchical device and
allows the generation of a certain multiplicity of texts equally corresponding
to it. Asymmetrical directedness and the constant need for choice make
translation in this case an act of generating new information and realize the
creative function of both language and text.

Especially indicative is the situation in which between the codes there exists
not simply difference, but a situation of mutual untranslatability, as when a
verbal text is translated into an iconic one. Translation is carried out by
means of a conventional system of equivalences accepted in the given culture.
Thus, for example, when a verbal text is rendered by a pictorial one, as in a
painting on a Gospel subject, the space of theme will intersect in the codes,
while the spaces of language and style will be correlated only conventionally
within the limits of the given tradition. The combination of translatability and
untranslatability, with different degrees of each, determines the creative
function.

Since meaning in this case turns out to be not only the invariant remainder that
is preserved through diverse transformational operations, but also what changes
in the process, we can state an increment of the meaning of the text in the
course of these transformations.

One more feature should be noted. When using artificial languages, or natural
and poetic languages as artificial ones - for example, when conveying a Tolstoy
novel by a brief plot annotation - we separate meaning from language. In complex
operations of meaning-generation, language is inseparable from the content it
expresses. In this latter case we have not only a message in a language, but a
message about language, a message in which interest shifts to its language. This
is precisely that orientation of the message toward the code in which Roman
Jakobson saw the basic feature of the artistic text.

In this case, many phenomena are paradoxically displaced. For example, when one
is oriented toward the constancy of the message, the fact that language precedes
the message in that language and is given in advance to both participants in
communication seems so natural that it is not specially mentioned. Even in
complex cases, the receiver first recognizes, by certain signals, which of the
codes known to him has been used to encipher the message, and only then begins
to "read." When the heroes of Jules Verne's novel *In Search of the Castaways*
extracted three fragments of a document from a bottle, they first established
that one of them was written in English, another in German, and the third in
French, and only afterward set about reconstructing the meaning of the damaged
document.

In the second case, the opposite order is possible: first the document is given,
and only then is its language reconstructed. This order is quite ordinary when
we receive into our hands a fragment of a culture distant from us. This may
involve not only verbal texts in unknown languages, but also monuments of art
and material culture torn from their contexts, whose function and meaning the
archaeologist must reconstruct. This case is still more ordinary in the history
of art, since every innovative artistic work is a sui generis work in a language
unknown to the audience, a language that must still be reconstructed and
assimilated by its addressees. The possibility of such "self-teaching" by the
addressee is conditioned, first, by the fact that in any language, even a
maximally individualized one, not everything is individual: levels inevitably
exist that are common to both participants in communication and serve as the
basis for reconstruction. Second, this "individual" and new element inevitably
stands on a certain tradition, the memory of which is actualized in the text.
Finally, third, the language of art is inevitably heterogeneous, and, in moving
as far as possible from the pole of meta-languages and artificial languages, it
paradoxically and necessarily includes elements of reflection on itself, that
is, metalinguistic structures. The experience of the European avant-garde
convincingly testifies that the more individual an artistic language is, the
more space is occupied by authorial reflection directed toward the language and
included in that language's own structure. The text consciously turns itself
into a lesson in language.

Thus the spectrum of texts that fill the space of culture appears to us as
arranged along an axis whose poles are formed by artificial languages on the one
side and artistic languages on the other. The remaining languages are placed at
different points on this axis, inclining now toward one pole, now toward the
other. At the same time, it must be kept in mind that the poles of this axis are
abstractions, unrealizable in actual languages: just as artificial languages are
impossible without some, even rudimentary, synonymy and other "poetic" elements,
so metalinguistic tendencies are inevitable in languages with a demonstrative
tendency toward "pure" poeticity.

It should also be taken into account that the place of a text on the axis named
above is mobile: the reader may evaluate the relation of the "poetic" and the
"informational" in the text differently than the author does. When Aseev writes,
"I would forbid 'the sale of oats and hay'... / For it smells of the murder of
the Father and the Son," and when a peasant who has wandered into town in
Pilnyak reads "commutators, accumulators" as a proverb-like utterance, it is
obvious that such a text - a signboard - in the first case is read as poetic,
and in the second as a proverb. In the first case, the sound side is unlawfully
illuminated; in the second, the syntagmatics is deformed according to the laws
by which paremias are built.

The possibility of choosing one of two positions as the point of reference in
approaching language entails significant consequences. In one case, the
informational point of view, in the narrow sense, will present language as a
machine for transmitting unchanged messages, and poetic language will appear as
a particular and, in general, strange corner of this system. It will be seen
only as natural language with additional restrictions imposed on it and,
therefore, with a significantly narrowed informational capacity.

But another view is also possible, one that has likewise been demonstrated more
than once in linguistics: the creative function will be regarded as a universal
property of language, and poetic language as the most representative
demonstration of language as such. The semiotic models opposed to it will then
turn out to be a particular region of linguistic space.

From this point of view, the historical "dispute" between the brilliant
linguists Saussure and Jakobson is exceptionally interesting.

Saussure clearly understood precisely the first function as the main principle
of language. Hence the clarity of his oppositions, his emphasis on the universal
significance of the principle of conventionality in the relation between
signified and signifier, and so on. Behind him appears the culture of the
twentieth century, with its faith in positive science, its conviction that
understanding is a good and misunderstanding an absolute evil, with universal
literacy and the novels of Zola and the Goncourts. Roman Jakobson was, and
remained, a person of avant-garde culture. His first book, *Newest Russian
Poetry: First Sketch* (1921), appeared as a brilliant prologue to the whole of
his scholarly activity. The language of Khlebnikov and the language of the
Russian futurists were for Jakobson not an anomaly, but the most consistent
realization of the structure of language and one of the most important impulses
for his later phonological investigations. His sensitivity to the aesthetic side
of semiotic systems is connected with his experience of working with artistic
language. This explains the pressure with which he criticizes Saussure,
attacking the principle central to Saussure, the conventionality of the bond
between signifier and signified in the sign. Indeed, the language of the
artistic text acquires secondary features of iconicity, and this is reflected in
the emergence of the question of the "untranslatability" of poetic language. In
the article named above, Jakobson with exceptional subtlety reveals the features
of iconicity inherent in the language of everyday communication: that is, the
presence of a potential artistic principle in language as such. If academician
A. N. Kolmogorov had already shown in the early 1960s that poems cannot be
written in artificial languages, then Roman Jakobson convincingly demonstrated
the potential iconicity, and consequently the presence of an artistic aspect, in
natural languages, thereby confirming Potebnia's thought that the whole sphere
of language belongs to art.

The third function of the text is the function of memory. The text is not only a
generator of new meanings, but also a condenser of cultural memory. The text has
the capacity to preserve the memory of its preceding contexts. Without this,
historical science would be impossible, since the culture - and more broadly,
the picture of life - of preceding epochs reaches us inevitably in fragments. If
the text remained in the consciousness of the perceiver only itself, then the
past would appear to us as a mosaic of unconnected fragments. But for the
perceiver, the text is always a metonymy of a reconstructed integral meaning, a
discrete sign of a non-discrete essence. The sum of the contexts in which a
given text acquires meaningfulness, and which in a certain way are as if
incorporated into it, may be called the memory of the text. This semantic space
created by the text around itself enters into definite relations with cultural
memory, or tradition, deposited in the consciousness of the audience. As a
result, the text once again acquires semiotic life.

Any culture is constantly bombarded by isolated chance texts falling upon it
like a meteor shower. These are not texts included in a definite, coherent
tradition that exerts influence on one or another culture, but precisely
isolated disturbing intrusions. They may be fragments of other civilizations
accidentally dug up from the earth, or texts accidentally carried in from
cultures distant in time or space. If texts did not have their own memory and
could not create a definite semantic aura around themselves, all these
intrusions would remain museum rarities outside the main cultural process. In
fact, they turn out to be important factors provoking the dynamics of culture.
This is connected with the fact that the text, like a seed containing within
itself the program of future development, is not a frozen given, immutably equal
to itself. The internal not-fully-determined character of its structure creates,
under the influence of contacts with new contexts, a reserve for its dynamics.

There is also another aspect to this question. It would seem that a text,
passing through centuries, should wear away and lose the information contained
in it. Yet in those cases in which we are dealing with texts that preserve
cultural activity, they reveal a capacity to accumulate information, that is, a
capacity of memory. Today *Hamlet* is not only Shakespeare's text, but also the
memory of all interpretations of this work and, moreover, the memory of those
historical events outside the text with which Shakespeare's text can call up
associations. We may forget what Shakespeare and his spectators knew, but we
cannot forget what we have learned after them. And this gives the text new
meanings.
