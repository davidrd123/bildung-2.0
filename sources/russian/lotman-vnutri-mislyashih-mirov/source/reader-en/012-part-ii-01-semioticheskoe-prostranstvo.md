## Semiotic Space

Up to this point our reasoning has been built according to the generally
accepted scheme: an individual isolated communicative act was placed at the
foundation, and the relations that arise between addresser and addressee were
studied. With this approach it is assumed that the study of an isolated fact
discloses all the basic traits of semiosis, which can then be extrapolated to
more complex semiotic processes. Such an approach satisfies Descartes' well
known third rule from the *Discourse on Method*: to keep to a definite order of
thinking, beginning with the simplest and most easily known objects and rising
gradually toward knowledge of the most complex. In addition, it answers to a
scientific habit that began in the Enlightenment: to build a "Robinsonade," to
single out an isolated object and then give it the significance of a general
model.

For such isolation to be correct, however, the isolated fact must be capable of
modeling all the properties of the phenomenon to which the conclusions will be
extrapolated. In this case that cannot be said. A device consisting of an
addresser, an addressee, and a single channel linking them will not yet work. In
order to work, it must be immersed in semiotic space. All participants in
communication must already have some experience, must already have habits of
semiosis. Thus semiotic experience must paradoxically precede any semiotic act.

If, by analogy with the biosphere of V. I. Vernadsky, we single out a
semiosphere, it becomes obvious that this semiotic space is not the sum of
separate languages, but the condition of their existence and work. In a certain
respect, it precedes them and constantly interacts with them. In this respect a
language is a function, a condensation of semiotic space, and the boundaries
between them, so clear in the grammatical self-description of language, appear
blurred and full of transitional forms in semiotic reality. Outside the
semiosphere there is neither communication nor language.

Of course, a one-channel structure is also a reality. A self-sufficient
one-channel system is an admissible mechanism for transmitting maximally simple
signals and, in general, for realizing the first function. But it is completely
unsuitable for the task of generating information. It is no accident that such a
system can be imagined as an artificially created construction, while under
natural conditions working systems of an entirely different type arise. The
fact that the dualism of conventional and iconic signs - more exactly, of
conventionality and iconicity, present in different proportions in one or
another sign - is a universal of human culture can be regarded as a vivid
example of the fact that semiotic dualism is the minimal form of organization
of a working semiotic system.

Binarity and asymmetry are obligatory laws in the construction of a real
semiotic system. Binarity, however, should be understood as a principle that is
realized as multiplicity, since each newly formed language is in turn
fragmented on the basis of binarity. A mechanism for multiplying its languages
is "built into" every living culture. Later we will see that a
counter-directed mechanism of language unification works in parallel. Thus, for
example, we constantly witness the quantitative growth of the languages of art.
This is especially noticeable in 20th-century culture and in past cultures
typologically comparable with it. Under conditions in which the main creative
activity shifts into the camp of the audience, the slogan becomes actual: art
is everything we perceive as art.

At the beginning of the 20th century, cinema turned from fairground amusement
into high art. It did not arrive alone, but accompanied by a whole cortege of
traditional and newly invented spectacles. In the 19th century no one would
have seriously considered the circus, fairground shows, folk toys, signboards,
or the cries of street vendors as kinds of art. Having become art, cinema at
once divided into feature and documentary cinema, photographic and animated
cinema, each with its own poetics. At present, another opposition has been
added: cinema and television. True, the broadening of the assortment of the
languages of art is accompanied at the same time by its narrowing: certain arts
practically fall out of the active clip. Thus one should not be surprised if,
under more careful investigation, the diversity of semiotic means inside one or
another culture turns out to be a relatively constant quantity. But something
else is essential: the composition of the languages entering the active cultural
field constantly changes, and the axiological evaluation and hierarchical place
of the elements entering it are subject to even greater change.

At the same time, throughout the whole space of semiosis - from social, age, and
other jargons to fashion - there is also constant renewal of codes. Thus any
individual language turns out to be immersed in a certain semiotic space, and
only by virtue of interaction with this space is it capable of functioning. The
indivisible working mechanism, the unit of semiosis, should be considered not an
individual language but the entire semiotic space belonging to a given culture.
This space is what we define as the semiosphere. Such a name is justified
because, like the biosphere, which is on the one hand the aggregate and organic
unity of living matter, in the definition of the academician V. I. Vernadsky
who introduced the concept, and on the other hand the condition for the
continuing existence of life, the semiosphere is both the result and the
condition of the development of culture.

Vernadsky wrote that all condensations of life are most closely connected with
one another. One cannot exist without another. This link among different living
films and condensations, and their constant character, is an eternal feature of
the mechanism of the earth's crust, manifest in it throughout geological time.
This thought is expressed with particular definiteness in the following
formula: the biosphere has an entirely definite structure that determines,
without exception, everything that occurs within it. The human being, as
observed in nature, like all living organisms and all living matter, is a
definite function of the biosphere in a definite space-time of it.

Already in notes from 1892, Vernadsky pointed to the intellectual activity of
the human being, or humanity, as a continuation of the cosmic conflict between
life and inert matter. The lawful character of the conscious work of national
life, he wrote, led many to deny the influence of personality in history,
although in essence we see throughout history a constant struggle of conscious,
that is, "not natural," ways of life against the unconscious order of dead laws
of nature. In this tension of consciousness lies all the beauty of historical
phenomena, their original position among other natural processes. By this
tension of consciousness, a historical epoch can be evaluated.

The semiosphere is marked by heterogeneity. The languages filling semiotic
space differ in their nature and stand in relation to one another along a
spectrum from complete mutual translatability to equally complete mutual
untranslatability. Heterogeneity is determined by the heterogeneity and
heterofunctionality of languages. Thus, if by way of thought experiment we
imagine a model of semiotic space in which all languages arose at one and the
same moment and under the influence of identical impulses, we would still have
before us not one coding structure but a certain multiplicity of connected yet
different systems.

For example, suppose we build a model of the semiotic structure of European
romanticism, conditionally delimiting its chronological frame. Even inside such
a fully artificial space we will not obtain homogeneity, since different
degrees of iconicity will inevitably create a situation of conditional
correspondence rather than one-to-one semantic translatability. Of course, the
poet-partisan of 1812 Denis Davydov could compare the tactics of partisan war
with romantic poetry when he demanded that the commander of a partisan
detachment not be a methodical man with calculating reason and a cold soul:
this field, filled with poetry, required romantic imagination and passion for
adventure and was not satisfied with dry, prosaic bravery. "It is a stanza of
Byron!" he wrote. Yet one need only look through his historical-tactical study
*An Essay Toward A Theory Of Partisan Action*, equipped with plans and maps, to
be convinced that this beautiful metaphor speaks only of the bringing together
of the incomparable in the contrastive consciousness of a romantic. The fact
that the unity of different languages is established with the help of metaphors
speaks best of all to their principled difference.

But one must also take into account that different languages have different
periods of circulation: clothing fashion changes at a speed incomparable with
the period in which stages of literary language change, and romanticism in
dance is not synchronous with romanticism in architecture. Thus while in some
areas of the semiosphere the poetics of romanticism is being lived through,
others may already have advanced far in a postromantic direction. Consequently,
even this artificial model will not give a homologous picture in a strictly
synchronous slice. It is no accident that, when attempts are made to give a
synthetic picture of romanticism characterizing all the arts, and sometimes
other areas of culture as well, chronology has to be sacrificed decisively. The
same is true of baroque, classicism, and many other "isms."

If, however, we speak not of artificial models but of modeling the real
literary process, or more broadly the real cultural process, then we must
recognize, continuing our example, that romanticism captures only a definite
area of the semiosphere, in which various traditional structures continue to
exist, sometimes reaching back to deep archaism. In addition, no stage of
development is free from collision with texts arriving from outside, from
cultures that previously had been entirely beyond the horizon of the given
semiosphere. These intrusions - sometimes individual texts, sometimes whole
cultural layers - exert varied disturbing effects on the internal structure of
the "picture of the world" of the given culture.

Thus, in any synchronous slice of the semiosphere, different languages and
different stages of their development collide. Some texts turn out to be
immersed in languages that do not correspond to them, and the codes that would
decipher them may be absent altogether. Imagine, as a certain single world
taken in a synchronous slice, a museum hall in which the display cases contain
exhibits from different epochs, inscriptions in known and unknown languages,
deciphering instructions, explanatory texts for the exhibition prepared by
methodologists, diagrams of tour routes, and rules for visitors' behavior. Add
guides and visitors to this hall and imagine all this as a single mechanism -
which, in a certain respect, it is. We will obtain an image of the semiosphere.
At the same time, one must not forget that all elements of the semiosphere are
not in a static relation but in a mobile, dynamic relation, constantly changing
the formulas of their relation to one another. This is especially noticeable in
the traditional moments inherited from past states of culture. The evolution of
culture differs radically from biological evolution, and here the word
"evolution" often performs a poor and disorienting service.

Evolutionary development in biology is connected with the extinction of species
rejected by natural selection. Only what is synchronous with the researcher is
alive. Something analogous occurs in the history of technology, where a tool
displaced from use by technical progress finds refuge only in the museum. It
turns into a dead exhibit. In the history of art, works belonging to epochs of
culture that have receded into the distant past continue actively to
participate in its development as living factors. A work of art may "die" and
be reborn, may become contemporary after having become obsolete, or may even
point prophetically toward the future. What "works" here is not the latest
temporal slice, but the whole thickness of the texts of culture. The stereotype
of literary history built according to an evolutionist principle was created
under the influence of evolutionary conceptions in the natural sciences. As a
result, the synchronous state of literature in a given year is considered to be
the list of works written in that year. Yet if one were to make lists of what
was read in one or another year, the picture would probably be different. It is
difficult to say which list would characterize the synchronous state of culture
more fully. Thus for Pushkin in 1824-1825 the most actual writer was
Shakespeare. Bulgakov experienced Gogol and Cervantes as contemporary writers.
The actuality of Dostoevsky is felt at the end of the 20th century no less
than at the end of the 19th. In essence, everything contained in the actual
memory of culture is included, directly or indirectly, in its synchrony.

The structure of the semiosphere is asymmetrical. This is expressed in the
system of directed currents of internal translations that pierce the whole
thickness of the semiosphere. Translation is the basic mechanism of
consciousness. The expression of a certain essence by means of another language
is the basis for revealing the nature of that essence. And since, in most
cases, the different languages of the semiosphere are semiotically
asymmetrical, that is, they do not have one-to-one semantic correspondences,
the whole semiosphere can be regarded as a generator of information.

Asymmetry appears in the relation between the center of the semiosphere and its
periphery. The center of the semiosphere is formed by the most developed and
structurally organized languages. First of all, this is the natural language of
the given culture. One can say that, if no language, including natural
language, can work without being immersed in the semiosphere, then no
semiosphere, as Emile Benveniste already noted, can exist without natural
language as its organizing axis. The point is that, alongside structurally
organized languages, in the space of the semiosphere there crowd private
languages, languages capable of serving only individual functions of culture,
and language-like, half-formed formations that can be bearers of semiosis if
they are included in a semiotic context. This can be compared with the way a
stone or a strangely bent tree trunk can function as a work of art if it is
regarded as a work of art. The object acquires the function attributed to it.

In order to perceive this whole mass of constructions as bearers of semiotic
meanings, one must possess a "presumption of semioticity": the possibility of
significant structures must be given in the consciousness and semiotic
intuition of the collective. These qualities are developed on the basis of the
use of natural language. Thus, for example, the dependence, in a number of
cases, of the structure of the "family of gods" and other basic elements of the
picture of the world on the grammatical structure of language seems obvious.

The highest form of structural organization of a semiotic system is the stage
of self-description. The process of description itself is the carrying of
structural organization through to completion. Both the stage at which
grammars are created and the codification of customs or legal norms raise the
object described to a new level of organization. Therefore the self-description
of a system is the final stage in the process of its self-organization. At the
same time, the system gains in the degree of structural organization, but loses
the internal reserves of indeterminacy with which its flexibility, its capacity
to increase informational capacity, and its reserve for dynamic development are
connected.

The necessity of the stage of self-description is connected with the threat of
excessive diversity inside the semiosphere: the system may lose unity and
definiteness and "spread apart." Whether we are speaking of linguistic,
political, or cultural aspects, in all cases we encounter similar mechanisms.
Some one area of the semiosphere, as a rule one belonging to its nuclear
structure, creates its grammar in the process of self-description, whether real
or ideal - that depends on whether the description is internally oriented
toward the present or the future. Attempts are then made to spread these norms
to the entire semiosphere. The partial grammar of one cultural dialect becomes
the metalanguage for describing culture as such. Thus the dialect of Florence
becomes, in the Renaissance, the literary language of Italy; the legal norms of
Rome become the laws of the whole empire; and the etiquette of the court of
Louis XIV becomes the etiquette of the courts of all Europe.

A literature of norms and prescriptions arises, in which the later historian
sees a real picture of the actual life of one or another epoch, its semiotic
practice. This illusion is supported by the testimony of contemporaries, who
really are convinced that this is exactly how they act. The contemporary
reasons approximately as follows: "I am a person of culture, that is, a Hellene,
a Roman, a Christian, a knight, an esprit fort, a philosopher of the
Enlightenment, or a genius of the romantic epoch. As a person of culture I
realize behavior prescribed by such-and-such norms. Only what in my behavior
corresponds to these norms can be considered an action. If, through weakness,
illness, inconsistency, and so on, I depart from these norms in some respect,
then this has no significance; it is irrelevant; it simply does not exist." The
list of what in a given system of culture "does not exist," although it
practically occurs, is always an essential typological characteristic of the
accepted system of semiotics.

Thus, for example, the well-known Andreas Capellanus, author of *De amore*,
written between 1175 and 1186, a treatise on the norms of courtly love,
subjects noble love to careful codification and requires of the lover fidelity
to the lady, silence, careful secrecy, chastity, courtesy, and so on. Yet he
calmly permits violence toward a peasant woman, since in this picture of the
world she "as if does not exist." Actions toward her stand outside semiotics;
that is, they are "as if not there."

The picture of the world created in this way will be perceived by
contemporaries as reality. Moreover, it will be their reality to the extent
that they have accepted the laws of the given semiotics. Later generations,
including researchers, reconstructing life from texts, will assimilate the idea
that everyday reality was exactly like this. Meanwhile, the relation of this
metalayer of the semiosphere to the real picture of its semiotic "map," on the
one hand, and to the everyday reality of life lying on the other side of
semiotics, on the other, will be quite complex.

First, if in the nuclear structure where the given self-description was created
it really did represent an idealization of a certain real language, then on the
periphery of the semiosphere the ideal norm contradicted the semiotic reality
lying "under" it rather than flowing from it. If at the center of the
semiosphere the description of texts generated norms, then on the periphery the
norms, actively invading "incorrect" practice, generated the corresponding
"correct" texts. Second, whole layers of cultural phenomena that were marginal
from the point of view of the given metastructure did not correlate at all with
its idealized portrait. They were declared "nonexistent."

Beginning with the work of the cultural-historical school, a favorite genre of
many researchers has been the article titled "An Unknown Poet of the 12th
Century," "On One More Forgotten Writer of the Enlightenment," and the like.
Where does this inexhaustible reserve of the "unknown" and "forgotten" come
from? These are those who in their own epoch fell into the category of the
"nonexistent" and were ignored by scholarship so long as its point of view
coincided with the normative views of the epoch. But the point of view shifts,
and suddenly the "unknown" are discovered. It is remembered that in the year of
Voltaire's death the "unknown philosopher" Louis Claude de Saint-Martin was
already thirty-five; that Retif de la Bretonne wrote more than two hundred
volumes, for which literary historians will never find a place, calling their
author now a "little Rousseau," now the "Balzac of the 18th century"; that in
the epoch of romanticism in Russia there lived Vasily Narezhny, who wrote
about two and a half dozen volumes of novels that were "not noticed" by his
contemporaries, since traits of realism were already appearing in them.

Thus on the metalevel a picture of semiotic unification is created, while at
the level of the semiotic "reality" described by it a diversity of tendencies
seethes. If the map of the upper layer is painted in one even color, then the
lower one is variegated with colors and with many intersecting boundaries. When
Charlemagne, at the end of the 8th century, carried sword and cross to the
Saxons, and when Saint Vladimir, a hundred years later, baptized Kievan Rus',
the great barbarian empires of West and East became Christian states. Yet their
Christianity answered to self-characterization and was located on the political
and religious metalevel, under which pagan traditions and various everyday
compromises seethed. It could not have been otherwise under conditions of mass,
and sometimes forced, baptisms. The terrible massacre committed by Charlemagne
against captive pagan Saxons at Verden could hardly have helped spread the
principles of the Sermon on the Mount among the barbarians.

And yet it would be incorrect to suppose that even a simple change of
self-name had no influence on the "lower-lying" levels, did not help transform
Christianization into evangelization, did not unify the cultural space of these
states already at the level of "real semiotics." Thus semantic currents flow
not only along the horizontal layers of the semiosphere but also act
vertically, forming complex dialogues among its different layers.

The unity of the semiotic space of the semiosphere, however, is achieved not
only by metastructural constructions but, to an even greater degree, by a
unity of relation to the boundary that separates the inner space of the
semiosphere from the outer, its inside from outside.
