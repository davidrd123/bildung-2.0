## Rhetoric As A Mechanism Of Meaning-Generation

Human consciousness is heterogeneous. A minimal thinking device must include at
least two differently organized systems that exchange information worked out
within them. Research into the specific functioning of the large hemispheres of
the human brain reveals a deep analogy with the organization of culture as a
collective intellect. In both cases, we find the presence of at least two
fundamentally different ways of reflecting the world and producing new
information, followed by complex mechanisms for exchanging texts between these
systems. In both cases, in broad outline, we observe an analogous structure:
within one consciousness there are, as it were, two consciousnesses.

One operates with a discrete system of coding and forms texts that are built as
linear chains of joined segments. In this case, the basic bearer of meaning is
the segment, or sign, while the chain of segments, or text, is secondary; its
meaning is derived from the meaning of the signs. In the second case, the text
is primary. It is the bearer of the basic meaning. By its nature it is not
discrete but continual. Its meaning is organized neither by linear nor by
temporal sequence, but is "smeared" across the n-dimensional semantic space of
the given text: the canvas of a painting, a stage, a screen, a ritual action,
social behavior, or a dream. In texts of this type, it is precisely the text
that is the bearer of meaning. Isolating the signs that compose it is difficult,
and at times has an artificial character.

Thus, within both individual and collective consciousness, two types of text
generators are hidden: one is based on the mechanism of discreteness, the other
is continual. Although each of these mechanisms is immanent in its own
organization, there is a constant exchange of texts and messages between them.
This exchange takes place in the form of semantic translation. Yet any exact
translation presupposes that a one-to-one relation has been established between
the units of two systems, as a result of which it becomes possible to map one
system onto another. This allows the text of one language to be expressed
adequately by the means of another.

But when discrete and non-discrete texts are placed side by side, this is in
principle impossible. To the discrete and precisely designated semantic unit of
one text, there corresponds in the other a certain semantic blot, with blurred
borders and gradual transitions into the area of another meaning. If there is
any sui generis segmentation there, it is not comparable with the type of
discrete boundaries belonging to the first text. Under these conditions, a
situation of untranslatability arises. Yet it is precisely here that attempts at
translation are carried out with special persistence and give the most valuable
results. What arises in this case is not exact translation, but an approximate
equivalence conditioned by a certain cultural, psychological, and semiotic
context common to both systems. Such an irregular and inexact, yet in a certain
respect equivalent, translation is one of the essential elements of all creative
thinking. It is precisely these "irregular" rapprochements that give impulses
for the emergence of new semantic links and fundamentally new texts.

A pair of mutually incomparable meaningful elements, between which a relation of
adequacy is established within some context, forms a semantic trope. In this
respect, tropes are not external ornaments, not a kind of applique laid onto
thought from outside. They constitute the essence of creative thinking, and
their sphere is even broader than art. It belongs to creativity in general.
Thus, for example, all attempts to create visual analogues of abstract ideas, to
represent continuous processes by means of discrete formulas, to build spatial
physical models of elementary particles, and so on, are rhetorical figures, or
tropes. And just as in poetry, so in science, an irregular rapprochement often
acts as the impulse for formulating a new regularity.

Over the centuries of its existence, the theory of tropes has accumulated an
extensive literature on the definition of their basic types: metaphor, metonymy,
and synecdoche. This literature continues to grow. Yet it is obvious that, no
matter how much one logicizes the trope, one of its elements has a verbal nature
and the other a visual one, however disguised this second element may be. Even
in logical models of metaphor created for teaching demonstrations, a
non-discrete image, visual or acoustic, forms the implied mediating link between
two discrete verbal components.

But the deeper the situation of untranslatability between two languages is, the
sharper the need for a metalanguage common to them, one that would throw a
bridge between them and help establish equivalences. It was precisely the
linguistic heterogeneity of tropes that caused the hypertrophy of
metastructural constructions in the "rhetoric of figures." A drift toward
dogmatism at the level of meta-description compensated for the inevitable
indeterminacy at the level of the text of figures. Compensation here receives a
special meaning, since rhetorical texts differ from general-language texts in an
essential respect: linguistic texts are produced spontaneously by the bearer of
the language, and explicit rules are relevant here only for the researcher who
constructs logical models of unconscious processes. In rhetoric, the process of
generating texts has a "learned," conscious character. Rules are actively
included in the text itself, not only at the meta-level but also at the level of
the immediate textual structure.

This creates the specificity of the trope, which simultaneously includes an
element of irrationality - the equivalence of textual elements that are known
not to be equivalent and cannot even be placed in one row - and has the
character of hyper-rationalism, connected with the inclusion of conscious
construction directly into the text of the rhetorical figure. This circumstance
is especially noticeable in cases where metaphor is built not on the collision
of words, but as an element of, for example, the language of cinema.

A sharp montage juxtaposition of two visual images would seem to avoid the
collision between discreteness and non-discreteness, or other situations of
principled untranslatability. But closer examination shows that the
metastructure here is built on the basis of assimilating the film frame to the
word of natural language, and that the mechanism of discreteness is introduced
into the very structure of the cinematic metaphor. One can also be convinced of
something else: if one member of a cinematic metaphor can, as a rule, be retold
in words without effort, and is consciously oriented toward such retelling, the
other most often resists such retelling.

Let us give an example. In the film *Ant-Hill* by the Hungarian director Zoltan
Fabri, at the center there is an exceptionally complex, many-layered drama
unfolding in a Hungarian convent at the beginning of the twentieth century. The
events stand in complex metaphorical relations with details of the baroque
surroundings of the church, filmed in close-up. Among these, in particular, a
relief medallion with an image of the sower is emphasized. This member of the
metaphor is deciphered by direct translation into the verbal text of the Gospel
parable of the sower. The other member of the metaphor, the events taking place,
is not verbally retold, but is disclosed in relation to the first member, and to
others like it.

The belonging of the "rhetoric of figures" to the level of secondary modeling
is connected with the role of metamodels, and distinguishes this layer from the
level of primary signs and symbols. Thus, for example, an aggressive gesture in
the behavior of an animal, if it is not connected with a real aggressive action
but replaces it, is an element of symbolic behavior. Yet the symbol is used here
in a primary meaning. Another case is when a gesture that has the character of a
sexual symbol is used to mean submission to the dominant role of a partner in
the general organization of an animal collective, and loses its connection with
sexual content. In the second case, we can speak of the metaphorical character
of the gesture and of the presence of certain elements of gestural rhetoric.

This example shows that the opposition "discrete - continual" is only one
possible, extreme form of the semantic untranslatability that gives birth to
tropes. Collisions of less distant spheres of semantic organization are also
possible, creating contrasts sufficient for the appearance of a rhetoric-
generating situation.

## Rhetorical Figures

In traditional rhetoric, "devices for changing the basic meaning of a word are
called tropes," as Tomashevsky writes. In the neorhetoric of recent decades,
numerous attempts have been made to clarify the meaning both of tropes in
general and of their concrete types - metaphor, metonymy, synecdoche, irony -
in accordance with contemporary linguistic and semiotic ideas. The major work in
this direction belongs to Roman Jakobson. Jakobson, distinguishing two basic
types of trope, metaphor and metonymy, connects them with the two basic axes of
the structure of language: the paradigmatic and the syntactic. Metaphor, for
Jakobson, is a substitution of a concept along the paradigmatic axis, connected
with selection from a paradigmatic row, substitution in absentia, and the
establishment of a semantic link by similarity. Metonymy is located on the
syntactic axis and represents not selection but combination in praesentia, and
the establishment of a link by contiguity.

In considering the cultural function of rhetorical figures, Jakobson on the one
hand broadens it, seeing in it the basis of meaning-formation in any semiotic
system. For this reason he applies the terms "metaphor" and "metonymy" to
cinema, painting, psychoanalysis, and so on. On the other hand, he narrows them,
assigning metaphor to the sphere of semiotic structure, that is, poetry, and
metonymy to the sphere of text, that is, prose. "Metaphor for poetry and
metonymy for prose constitutes the line of least resistance." Thus the
distinction poetry/prose received an objective grounding and moved from the
rank of private categories of verbal art into the number of semiotic
universals.

Jakobson's conception was developed and refined in a number of works. Umberto
Eco, studying the linguistic foundations of rhetoric, takes metonymy as the
initial figure. At its basis he sees the presence of chains of associative
contiguities: in the structure of the code, in the structure of the context, and
in the structure of the referent. The connection of linguistic codes with
cultural codes makes it possible to build metaphorical figures on the basis of
metonymy. Tzvetan Todorov's thought works in the same direction when he connects
metaphor with the doubling of synecdoche.

This last position comes close, in certain respects, to the conception of Group
Mu, the Liege group. In 1970, Group Mu developed a detailed taxonomic
classification of tropes, based on the analysis of semes and semantic-lexical
components. They regard synecdoche as the primary figure. Metaphor and metonymy
are treated by them as derivative figures, the result of various complications
of the initial types of synecdoche. This construction was criticized by Nicolas
Ruwet from linguistic positions and by P. Schofer and D. Rice from a literary
point of view.

Ruwet's opinion seems entirely justified: in the question of rhetoric in general
and tropes in particular, the main task of a predicative theory is to try to
answer the question: under what conditions does a given linguistic expression
receive a figurative meaning? The final definition of the trope reached by
neorhetoric sounds as follows: a trope is a semantic transposition from a sign
in praesentia to a sign in absentia, based on the perception of a connection
between one or more semantic features of the signified; marked by the semantic
incompatibility of micro- and macro-contexts; and conditioned by a referential
link of similarity, causality, inclusion, or opposition.

Classical rhetoric developed a ramified classification of figures. The term
"figure" was first used by Anaximenes of Lampsacus. The question was carefully
developed by Aristotle, whose pupils, especially Demetrius of Phalerum,
introduced the division into "figures of speech" and "figures of thought."
Later, the system of figures was repeatedly considered by ancient, medieval, and
classicist authors, and achieved great complexity. Neorhetoric operates mainly
with three concepts: metaphor, semantic substitution by similarity or likeness
of some seme; metonymy, substitution by contiguity, association, or causality,
with different authors emphasizing different types of connection; and
synecdoche, which some authors regard as the basic, primary figure and others
as a special case of metonymy - substitution on the basis of participation,
inclusion, partiality, or the replacement of plurality by singularity. Schofer
and Rice attempted to restore irony to the number of figures.

## The Typological And Functional Nature Of Figures

The study of the logical foundations of the classification of tropes should not
obscure the question of their typological and functional teleology. The question
"What are tropes?" does not cancel the others: "How do they work in a text?"
and "What is their purpose in the semantic mechanism of speech?" Among those who
have written about neorhetoric, Jakobson and Eco came closest to this question:
the first by pointing to the connection of the problem with the opposition
poetry/prose, the second by introducing associative chains into the discussion.

Attention should be paid to the fact that there are cultural epochs wholly, or
to a significant degree, oriented toward tropes. In such epochs tropes become an
obligatory sign of all artistic speech, and in some limiting cases of all speech
in general. At the same time, one could point to entire epochs in which precisely
the refusal of rhetorical figures becomes artistically significant, and speech,
in order to be perceived as artistic, must reproduce the norms of non-artistic
speech. Among epochs oriented toward the trope one can name the mythopoetic
period, the Middle Ages, the baroque, romanticism, symbolism, and the
avant-garde. By generalizing the semantic principles of all these heterogeneous
text-forming structures, we may perhaps be able to establish the typological
nature of the trope.

In all the styles listed, the replacement of semantic units by others is widely
practiced. Yet it is essential to emphasize that in every case the replacing and
the replaced are not only not adequate by any essential semantic or cultural
parameters, but possess the directly opposite property: incompatibility. The
replacement is carried out according to the principle of collage, where details
painted in oil coexist with glued-on natural objects. The glued detail, in
relation to the painted detail placed beside it, will function as metonymy; in
relation to the potentially painted detail that it replaces, it will function as
metaphor. Painted and glued objects belong to different and incompatible worlds
according to such features as reality/illusion, two-dimensionality/three-
dimensionality, signness/non-signness, and so on. Within a whole range of
traditional cultural contexts, their meeting within one text is absolutely
forbidden. Precisely for that reason, their conjunction produces the
exceptionally strong semantic effect proper to the trope.

The effect of the trope is produced not by the presence of a common seme - as
the number of common semes increases, the effectiveness of the trope decreases,
and tautological identity makes the trope impossible - but by their embeddedness
in incompatible semantic spaces and by the degree of semantic distance between
the non-coinciding semes. Semantic distance may be formed by different aspects
of the untranslatability of the replacing element and the replaced element.
These may be relations of one-dimensionality and multidimensionality,
discreteness and continuity, materiality and immateriality, earthly and other-
worldly, and so on. At the level of the referent, and in the comparison of the
corresponding semantic spaces, the boundaries of the replaced and the replacing
are so incomparable that the task of establishing correspondence acquires an
irrational character. It becomes conditional, approximate, presumed. It creates
not a simple semantic shift but a fundamentally new and paradoxical semantic
situation. It is no accident that cultures whose picture of the world is based
on antinomy and irrational contradiction are typologically drawn toward tropes.

With regard to metaphor this seems obvious. With regard to metonymy, it might
seem that since the replacement is carried out through a connection inside one
sign row, the replacing and replaced members are homogeneous in this case. In
fact, however, metaphor and metonymy are isofunctional in this respect. Their
aim is not to say, by means of a certain semantic substitution, what could also
be said without it, but to express a content, to transmit information, that
cannot be transmitted in any other way. In both cases, for metaphor and for
metonymy, there is no one-to-one relation between the direct and the figurative
meaning. Only approximate equivalence is established. In cases where, through
constant use or for some other reason, a relation of one-to-one correspondence
rather than semantic oscillation is established between the direct and the
figurative meaning, we are dealing with a worn-out trope. It is a rhetorical
figure only genetically, but functions as a phraseologism in its stable
dictionary meaning. This, evidently, is the answer to the question posed by
Ruwet.

Let us give several examples. If the icon, in the semiotic meaning that it
acquired in Byzantium and in the whole Eastern church, can be considered a
metaphor, then the holy relic functions as metonymy. A relic is a part of the
saint's body or a thing that was in direct contact with him. In this sense, the
material, embodied, bodily appearance of the saint is replaced by a bodily part
of him or by a material object connected with him. The icon, on the other hand,
as was first sketched by Origen and grounded in the writings of Gregory of Nyssa
and Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, is a material and expressed sign of the
immaterial and inexpressible essence of divinity.

What is painted on the icon is an image in the primary and direct sense.
Clement of Alexandria directly likened the visible to the verbal: speaking of
Christ, who in becoming human took on an appearance "without form" and lacking
bodily beauty, he notes that one must always understand not the words, but what
they signify. Thus complex semantic relations of inequality and multivalence
are established between the metaphorical expression and the
metaphorical content, excluding the rationalistic operation of mutual
substitution in both directions.

The rhetorical character of the icon is manifested, in particular, in the fact
that not every image can perform the role of the first member of the metaphor,
but only one executed in accordance with an established pictorial canon, which
has fixed the rhetoric of composition, color scale, and other artistic
decisions. Moreover, since the icon is understood as a metaphor arising from the
collision of two differently directed energies - the energy of the divine Logos,
which seeks to express itself to human beings, and the energy of the human
being, who rises in search of higher knowledge - it forms part of a ritual-
rhetorical context. This context embraces not only the process by which the icon
painter creates the icon, but the whole spiritual order of his life. It implies
a strict and righteous life, prayers, fasting, and spiritual ascent.
Interestingly, when Gogol made precisely these demands on the life of the
artist and the writer, all of his work acquired in his own eyes the character of
a gigantic metaphor.

Against the background of such a treatment of the icon, the relic may seem to be
a semantically one-planed phenomenon. But such an idea is superficial. The
relation of the material relic to the body of the saint is, of course, on one
plane. But one should not forget that the very concept "body of the saint"
contains within itself the metaphor of incarnation and a complex, irrational
relation of expression and content.

The metaphorism of the baroque grows on an entirely different ideological and
cultural basis. Yet here too we encounter the fact that tropes - the boundaries
separating one kind of trope from another acquire in baroque texts an
exceptionally unstable character - do not constitute an external replacement of
some elements of the plane of expression by others, but a way of forming a
special structure of consciousness. Here again we discover the characteristic
bringing together of mutually untranslatable spheres: verbal and iconic,
discrete and non-discrete signs. Thus Lope de Vega calls Marino a great painter
for hearing, and Rubens a great poet for sight. Tesauro calls architecture a
"metaphor in stone." In *The Aristotelian Telescope*, Tesauro developed a
doctrine of Metaphor as a universal principle of both human and divine
consciousness. At its basis lies wit: thinking grounded in bringing together the
unlike, joining the unjoinable. Metaphorical consciousness is equated with
creative consciousness, and even the act of divine creation is represented by
Tesauro as a kind of highest Wit, which by means of metaphors, analogies, and
concetti creates the world. Tesauro objects to those who see rhetorical figures
as external ornaments. For him, they constitute the very basis of the mechanism
of thought, of that highest Genius which animates both the human being and the
universe.

Turning to the epoch of romanticism, we find a similar picture. Although
metaphor and metonymy tend toward diffuse merging, the general orientation
toward the trope as the basis of style-formation appears with full clarity. The
idea of organic synthesis, the merging of different separated and unmergeable
sides of life, on the one hand, and the thought that the essence of life is
inexpressible by the means of any one language - natural language or any
isolated language of an individual art - on the other, produced metaphorical and
metonymic recoding of the signs of different semiotic systems. Wackenroder, in
*Outpourings from the Heart of an Art-Loving Friar*, identified the language of
symbols, emblems, and metaphors with art as such. Art, he writes, is a language
of a completely different kind from nature; yet it too possesses a wondrous
power over the human heart, attained by similarly dark and secret paths. It
speaks by means of images of people and thus uses a hieroglyphic writing whose
signs are outwardly familiar and comprehensible to us. Yet it fuses the
spiritual and Platonic with these visible images in such a touching and
delightful way that our whole being and everything within us is moved and
shaken to its foundations.

Finally, at the basis of the poetics of various avant-garde currents lies the
principle of juxtaposition. Figures formed in this way can, as a rule, be read
both as metaphors and as metonymies. Something else is essential: the
meaning-forming principle of the text as a whole becomes the juxtaposition of
segments that are in principle impossible to juxtapose. Their mutual recoding
forms the language of multiple readings, which reveals unexpected reserves of
meaning.

Thus the trope is not an ornament belonging only to the sphere of expression, not
the ornamentalization of some invariant content, but a mechanism for constructing
a content that cannot be constructed within the limits of one language. The
trope is a figure born at the junction of two languages, and in this respect it
is isostructural with the mechanism of creative consciousness as such. From this
follows the position that any logical definitions of rhetorical figures that
ignore their bilingual nature, and the models connected with them, belong to the
metalanguage of our theoretical description, but in no way are the generative
mechanisms by which tropes are produced. Moreover, if we ignore the fact that
the trope is a mechanism for producing semantic multivalence - that is, meaning
that cannot be reduced to a single univocal reading - a mechanism that
introduces into the semiotic structure of culture the degree of
indeterminacy it needs, we will not obtain an adequate description of this
phenomenon.

The function of the trope as a mechanism of semantic indeterminacy has
conditioned the fact that, in explicit form, on the surface of culture, it
appears in systems oriented toward complexity, multivalence, or the
inexpressibility of truth. Yet "rhetoricism" does not belong exclusively to any
epochs of culture. Like the opposition poetry/prose, the opposition
rhetoricism/anti-rhetoricism belongs to the universals of human culture. Both
members of this opposition are interconnected, and the semiotic activity of one
of them implies the actualization of the other. In a culture for which
rhetorical saturation has become a tradition and has entered the inertia of
readerly expectation, the trope enters the neutral fund of language and ceases
to be perceived as a rhetorically active unit. Against this background an
"anti-rhetorical" text, composed of elements of direct rather than figurative
semantics, begins to be perceived as a metatrope: a rhetorical figure that has
undergone secondary simplification, with the second language reduced to zero.

This "minus-rhetoric," subjectively perceived as an approach to reality and
simplicity, is a mirror reflection of rhetoric and includes its aesthetic
opponent in its own cultural-semiotic code. Thus the artlessness of the
neorealist film in fact contains a latent rhetoric, effective against the
background of the worn-out rhetoric of pompous pseudo-historical film epics and
high-society comedies, which has ceased to "work." In turn, the cinematic
baroque of Fellini's films rehabilitates rhetoric as the basis for constructing
meanings of great complexity.

## Metarhetoric And The Typology Of Culture

Metaphor and metonymy belong to the sphere of analogical thinking. In this
capacity they are organically connected with creative consciousness as such. It
is therefore a mistake, as already stated, to oppose rhetorical thinking to
scientific thinking as specifically artistic. Rhetoric is proper to scientific
consciousness to the same degree as to artistic consciousness. In the sphere of
scientific consciousness, two areas can be distinguished. The first is the
rhetorical area: the area of rapprochements, analogies, and modeling. This is
the sphere in which new ideas are advanced, unexpected postulates and hypotheses
are established, and what previously seemed absurd is proposed. The second area
is logical. Here the ideas advanced are tested, the conclusions following from
them are developed, and internal contradictions in proofs and arguments are
removed.

The first, "Faustian," sphere of scientific thinking is an inseparable part of
research and, belonging to science, is subject to scientific description. Yet
the apparatus of such description must itself be built specifically, forming a
language of metarhetoric. Thus all cases of isomorphism, homomorphism, and
homeomorphism, including epimorphisms, endomorphisms, monomorphisms, and
automorphisms, can be regarded as metametaphors. Together they create an
apparatus for describing a broad sphere of analogies and equivalences, allowing
apparently distant phenomena and objects to be brought together and, in a
certain respect, identified. An example of metametonymy can be found in Georg
Cantor's theorem establishing that if a segment contains aleph number of points,
that is, is an infinite set, then any part of this segment contains the same
aleph number of points and in this sense any of its parts is equal to the whole.
Operations of the type of transfinite induction can be regarded as
metametonymies.

Creative thinking, both in science and in art, has an analogical nature and is
built on a fundamentally identical basis: bringing together objects and concepts
that, outside a rhetorical situation, cannot be brought together. From this it
follows that the creation of metarhetoric becomes a general scientific task, and
metarhetoric itself can be defined as the theory of creative thinking.

Thus rhetorical texts are possible only as the realization of a definite
rhetorical situation, which is set by types of analogy and by the way the
parameters according to which these analogies are established are defined.
These indicators, by which relations of analogy or equivalence are established
within some group of texts or communicative situations, are determined by the
type of culture. Similarity and dissimilarity, equivalence and non-equivalence,
comparability and incomparability, the perception of any two objects as
incapable of being brought together or as identical, depend on the type of
cultural context.

One and the same text may be perceived as "correct" or "incorrect" -
impossible, a non-text - as "correct and trivial" or as "correct but
unexpected, violating certain norms while still remaining within the bounds of
meaningfulness," and so on, depending on whether we assign it to artistic or
non-artistic texts and what rules we attribute to the one and the other, that
is, depending on the context of culture in which we place it. Thus texts of
esoteric cultures, when extracted from the general context and separated from
special cultural codes accessible as a rule only to initiates, either cease to
be comprehensible at all or disclose themselves only from the point of view of
an outer semantic layer, preserving secret meanings for a narrow circle of the
admitted. Such are the texts of skalds, Sufi texts, Masonic texts, and many
others.

The question of whether a text is understood in a direct or figurative,
rhetorical meaning also depends on the application to it of more general
cultural codes. Since an essential role is played by the culture's own
orientation, expressed in how it sees itself - in the system of self-
descriptions forming the metacultural layer - a text may appear "normal" in
semantic terms from one perspective and "anomalous," semantically shifted, from
another. The relation of a text to various metacultural structures forms a
semantic play that is a condition of the rhetorical organization of the text.

For example, secondary encryption of semantics, if it is produced in a
univocal way, can form a secret esoteric language, but it is not a trope and
does not belong to the sphere of rhetoric. On the other hand, in a period when
intense verbal play and the metaphorism of the baroque had entered tradition
and become the predictable norm not only of literary language but also of the
fashionable speech of salons and precieuses, the word cleansed of secondary
meanings and reduced to direct and exact semantics became literarily
significant.

Under these conditions, the most active rhetorical figures became refusals of
rhetorical figures. A text freed from metaphors and metonymies entered into a
play relation with readerly expectation, that is, the cultural norm of the
baroque epoch, on the one hand, and with the new, not yet established norm of
classicism on the other. In such a context, the baroque metaphor was perceived
as a sign of triviality and did not perform a rhetorical function, while the
absence of metaphor, playing an active role, became aesthetically significant.

Just as in science an orientation toward building all-embracing hypotheses that
establish correspondences between apparently very distant areas of experience -
an orientation connected with "scientific rhetoric" and "scientific wit" -
alternates with a positivist orientation toward the empirical expansion of the
field of knowledge, so in art "rhetorical modeling" is periodically replaced by
empirical modeling. Thus the aesthetics of realism in its early stage is
characterized mainly by the negative features of anti-romanticism and is
perceived in projection onto romantic norms, creating a "rhetoric of the refusal
of rhetoric," a rhetoric of the second level. Later, however, connecting itself
with positivist tendencies in science, it acquires an independent structure,
which in turn becomes the semiotic background of twentieth-century neo-
romanticism and avant-garde currents.

## The Rhetoric Of The Text

From the moment we begin to deal with a text, that is, with a separate semiotic
formation closed in on itself, possessing an integral, indivisible meaning and
an integral, indivisible function, and separated from the context, the relation
of its elements to the problem of rhetoric changes sharply. If the whole text as
a whole is coded in the system of culture as rhetorical, then any element of it
also becomes rhetorical, regardless of whether, in isolation, it appears to us
to have direct or figurative meaning. Thus, for example, since every artistic
text as such appears in our consciousness as rhetorically organized, any title
of an artistic work functions in our consciousness as a trope or a minus-trope,
that is, as rhetorically marked.

Since it is precisely the textual nature of the utterance that makes us
understand it in this way, elements that signal that what stands before us is
precisely a text receive a special rhetorical load. Thus the categories of
"beginning" and "end" turn out to be rhetorically marked to a high degree; in
relation to them, the significance of this level of organization noticeably
increases. The multiplicity of structural links inside the text sharply lowers
the independence of the individual units that enter into it and raises the
coefficient of textual connectedness. The text strives to turn into a separate
"large word" with one common meaning. This secondary "word," in cases where we
are dealing with an artistic text, is always a trope: in relation to ordinary
non-artistic speech, the artistic text as it were switches into a semiotic space
with a greater number of dimensions.

To imagine what is at stake, let us picture a transformation of the type
"scenario, or artistic verbal narrative, into film," or "libretto into opera."
In transformations of this type, a text with a certain number of coordinates in
semantic space turns into one for which the dimensionality of semiotic space
sharply increases. An analogous phenomenon takes place in the transformation of
a verbal, non-artistic text into an artistic one. Therefore between the elements
and the wholeness of artistic and non-artistic texts there can be no univocal
relation and, consequently, no one-to-one translation. Only conventional
equivalence and different types of analogy are possible. And this is precisely
what constitutes the essence of rhetorical relations.

In cultures oriented toward rhetorical organization, every step in the ascending
hierarchy of semiotic organization gives an increase in the dimensions of the
space of semantic structure. Thus in Byzantine and Old Russian culture the
hierarchy - the world of everyday life and non-bookish speech; the world of
secular art; the world of church art; the divine liturgy; the transcendental
divine Light - forms a chain of continuous irrational complication. First comes
the transition from the non-sign world of things to a system of signs and
social languages. Then comes the joining of signs from different languages,
which is not translatable into any one language separately: the joining of word
and chant, book text and miniature, the joining in the temple action of words,
singing, wall painting, natural and artificial lighting, the smells of incense
and fumigation; the joining in architecture of building and landscape, and so
on. Finally comes the joining of art with transcendental divine Truth. Each step
of the hierarchy is inexpressible by the means of the preceding step, which
represents only an image, an incomplete presence, of it. The principle of
rhetorical organization lies at the basis of this culture as such, turning every
new level of it, for those below it, into a semiotic mystery. The principle of
rhetorical organization of culture is also possible on a purely secular basis:
for Paul I, a parade was a metaphor of Order and Power to the same degree that,
for Napoleon, a battle was a metonymy of Glory.

Thus rhetoric, like logic from the other side, reflects a universal principle of
both individual and collective consciousness, that is, culture.

An essential aspect of contemporary rhetoric is the circle of problems connected
with the grammar of the text. Here the traditional problems of the rhetorical
construction of extensive stretches of text merge with contemporary linguistic
problems. It is important to emphasize that traditional rhetorical figures are
built on the introduction into the text of additional features of symmetry and
order, in a certain respect analogous to the construction of the poetic text.
Yet if the poetic text presupposes obligatory ordering of the lower levels -
with what is unordered or facultatively ordered in the given language being
raised to the rank of obligatory and relevant orderings, while the lexical-
semantic level receives supralinguistic ordering as the result of this primary
organization - then in the rhetorical text the picture is reversed. The lexical-
semantic and syntactic levels are subjected to obligatory organization, while
rhythmic-phonetic ordering appears as a facultative and derivative phenomenon.

For us it is important to emphasize a certain common effect. In both cases, what
in natural language is a chain of independent signs turns into a semantic whole
with semantic content "smeared" across the whole space; that is, it tends to
become a single sign, a bearer of meaning. If a text in natural language is
organized linearly and is discrete by nature, the rhetorical text is integrated
in semantic terms. Entering into a rhetorical whole, individual words not only
"shift" semantically - every word in an artistic text is ideally a trope - but
also merge, their meanings integrating. What arises is what Tynianov, in
relation to the poetic text, called the "tightness of the poetic row."

In the science of recent decades, however, the question of the poetic
connectedness of the text has directly merged not only with problems of literary
study but also with linguistic problems. The rapid development of that branch of
linguistics that received the name "text grammar," devoted to the structural
unity of speech messages at the supraphrasal level, actualized the traditional
problems of rhetoric in their linguistic aspect. Since the mechanism of
supraphrasal unity was seen in lexical repetitions or their substitutes, on the
one hand, and in logical and intonational links on the other, the traditional
forms of rhetorical structures of the paragraph or the whole text seemed to
acquire a directly linguistic meaning.

This approach was criticized by B. M. Gasparov, who pointed, on the one hand,
to the insufficiency of such a mechanism for describing the supraphrasal
connectedness of the text and, on the other, to its loss of properly linguistic
content. In its place, Gasparov proposed a model of obligatory grammatical links
joining segments of speech at the supraphrasal level: according to Gasparov, the
immanent grammatical structure of a sentence imposes predetermined grammatical
restrictions on any phrase that can be attached to it in the given language. The
structure of these links forms the linguistic unity of the text.

Thus two approaches can be formulated. According to one, rhetorical structure
automatically follows from the laws of language and is nothing other than their
realization at the level of the construction of integral texts. From the other
point of view, there is a principled difference between the linguistic and
rhetorical unity of the text. Rhetorical structure does not arise automatically
from linguistic structure, but represents a decisive reinterpretation of it:
shifts occur in the system of linguistic links, facultative structures are
raised in rank, acquiring the character of basic ones, and so on. Rhetorical
structure is introduced into the verbal text from outside, as an additional
ordering of it.

Such, for example, are the various ways of introducing into a text, at its
different levels, additional laws of symmetry that lie at the basis of spatial
semiotics and are not proper to the structure of natural languages. It is this
second approach that seems correct to us. One may even assert that rhetorical
structure not only objectively represents the introduction into the text from
outside of principles of organization immanently foreign to it, but is also
subjectively experienced precisely as foreign in relation to the structural
principles of the text.

Thus, for example, a sharply marked inclusion of a fragment of non-artistic text
in an artistic one - in particular, newsreel frames in a feature film - can
carry a rhetorical load precisely insofar as it is recognized by the audience as
a foreign and irregular inclusion in the text. Against the background of a
newsreel film, a marked fictional inclusion would play the same role.
Traditional oratorical prose, perceived as the domain of rhetoric par
excellence, can be described as the result of the invasion of poetry into the
sphere of prose and the translation of poetic structure into the language of
prosaic means. At the same time, the invasion of the language of prose into
poetry also creates a rhetorical effect. Oratorical speech is also felt by the
audience as "shifted" oral speech into which emphasized elements of "writing"
have been introduced. In this respect, not only the syntactic figures of
classical rhetoric but also constructions that would seem neutral in a written
text, if one refused to pronounce it aloud, are perceived as rhetorical
elements. In equal measure, the introduction of oral speech into written text,
characteristic of twentieth-century prose, or the exchange of places between
"inner" and "outer" speech - for example, in prose that uses the means of a
linguistic text to depict "stream of consciousness" - activates the rhetorical
level of the text's structure.

One could compare with this the rhetorical function of foreign-language texts
included in a linguistic context foreign to them. The rhetorical function
becomes especially noticeable in cases where the foreign-language text can be
read punningly as a text in the native language. Pushkin, for example, supplied
the second chapter of *Eugene Onegin* with the epigraph "O rus," "O Rus'," a
pun-homonymic combination of a quotation from Horace and a Russian text. Such
cases, being limiting cases, reveal the essence of the mechanism of every
foreign inclusion in a text: it does not fall out of the general structure of
the context, but enters into play relations with it, at once belonging and not
belonging to the contextual structure. This position can also be extended to the
claim that a foreign structure is obligatory for the rhetorical level.
Rhetorical organization arises in the field of semantic tension between an
"organic" and a "foreign" structure, and its elements are subject to double
interpretation in this connection. A "foreign" organization, even if
mechanically transferred into a new structural context, ceases to be equal to
itself and becomes a sign or imitation of itself. Thus an authentic document
included in an artistic text becomes an artistic sign of documentariness and an
imitation of an authentic document.

## Stylistics And Rhetoric

In semiotic terms, stylistics is constituted in two oppositions: to semantics
and to rhetoric.

The opposition of stylistics and semantics is realized in the following way.
Every semiotic system, or language, has a hierarchical structure. Semantically,
this hierarchy appears in the splitting of the semantic field of language into
separate closed spaces, between which there is a relation of similarity. Such a
system can be likened to the registers of a musical instrument, for example an
organ. On such an instrument, one and the same melody can be played in different
registers. It will preserve melodic similarity while at the same time changing
its register coloring. If we turn to some individual note, we receive a meaning
identical for all registers. Comparing notes of the same name in different
registers will isolate, on the one hand, what they have in common, and on the
other, what gives away their belonging to one or another register. The first
meaning can be likened to the semantic, the second to the stylistic.

Thus stylistics arises, first, in a case where one and the same semantic content
can be expressed in at least two different ways, and second, where each of these
ways activates the memory of a definite closed and hierarchically connected
group of signs, a definite "register." If two different ways of expressing a
given semantic content belong to one and the same register, no stylistic effect
arises.

Connected with this is the second fundamental opposition: stylistics/rhetoric.
A rhetorical effect arises when signs belonging to different registers collide,
and thereby leads to a structural renewal of the sense of the boundary between
closed worlds of signs. A stylistic effect is created inside a definite
hierarchical subsystem. Thus stylistic consciousness proceeds from the
absoluteness of the hierarchical boundaries that it constitutes, while
rhetorical consciousness proceeds from their relativity. For rhetorical
consciousness they become the object of play.

What has been said applies to the non-artistic text. In the artistic text, with
its tendency to regard any structural element as having an alternative and as
"playful," a rhetorical relation to stylistics is possible. What is called
"poetic stylistics" can be defined as the creation of a special semiotic space
within which freedom of choice of stylistic register becomes possible, so that
the register no longer is automatically set by the character of the
communicative situation. As a result, style acquires additional significance. In
non-artistic communication, the choice of stylistic register is determined by
the sum of pragmatic relations proper to the real type of communication given.
In artistic communication, the text is primary; by its stylistic indicators it
sets an imaginary pragmatic situation. This makes it possible within one text to
set different, most often contrasting, styles against one another, and on that
basis a play with pragmatic situations arises: Hoffmann's romantic irony, the
stylistic contrasts of Byron's *Don Juan* and Pushkin's *Eugene Onegin*.

In the historical dynamics of art, one can distinguish periods oriented toward
rhetorical, inter-register metaconstructions and periods oriented toward
stylistic, intra-register metaconstructions. In the general cultural context,
the first are perceived as "complex" and the second as "simple." The aesthetic
ideal of "simplicity" is connected with a prohibition on rhetorical
constructions and heightened attention to stylistic ones. Yet even in this case
the artistic text differs fundamentally from the non-artistic text, although
subjectively the latter may function as the ideal model for the former.

Attention should be paid to the specific paradox of literary epochs oriented
toward stylistic consciousness. In these periods, the significance of the entire
system of stylistic registers of language is felt with special acuity, yet each
individual text tends toward stylistic neutrality. The reader is included in a
definite system of genre-stylistic norms at the beginning of reading, or even
before it begins. Thereafter, throughout the text, the possibility of changing
structural norms is excluded, as a result of which these norms themselves become
neutral. Artistic consciousness of the rhetorical type pays almost no attention
to the discussion of questions concerning the general hierarchy of registers.
Thus the whole system of genre-stylistic means, their "appropriateness" or
"inappropriateness," their relative value - matters that so occupied the
theorists of classicism - lost meaning in the eyes of the romantics.

But within an individual text, the value and mastery of the author appear, from
the classicist's point of view, in "purity of style," that is, in strict
fulfillment of the norms operating in the given register and at the given part
of it. For the romantic, they appear in the "expressiveness" of the text, that
is, in switching from one system of norms to another. In the first case, an
individual text is valued for the neutrality of its style, associated with
"correctness" and "purity." In the second, such "correctness" will be perceived
as "colorlessness" and "inexpressiveness." It will be opposed by stylistic
contrasts inside the text. Thus the stylistic dominant of artistic consciousness
will paradoxically lead to a weakening of the structural significance of the
category of style inside the text, while the rhetorical dominant will sharpen
the feeling of stylistic significance.

The evolutionary process in art is complex and depends on many factors. Among
other evolutionary constants, however, one could indicate that within a large
historical period "rhetorical" orientations usually precede the "stylistic"
orientations that replace them. This regularity was noticed by D. S. Likhachev.
One could compare with it a characteristic feature in the individual development
of many poets: from stylistic complication at the beginning of the creative path
to "classical" simplicity at the end. Pasternak's formulation - that the outcome
of poetic development is, at the end of the road, to fall "as into heresy / into
unheard-of simplicity" - is characteristic of too many individual poetic fates
to be considered accidental. "The transition from romanticism to realism," "the
transition from rococo to classicism," "the transition from avant-gardism to
neoclassicism": such formulas apply to an enormous number of individual
trajectories of poetic development. All of them fit the formula: transition
from rhetorical orientation to stylistic orientation.

The meaning of such evolution can be disclosed as the search for an individual
language of poetry. At the first stage, such a language takes shape as the
cancellation of already existing poetic dialects. A new linguistic space is
outlined, within whose boundaries linguistic units turn out to be combined that
previously had never entered into any common whole and had been understood as
incompatible. Naturally, under these conditions, the feeling of the specificity
of each of them and of their inability to be placed in one row is activated. A
rhetorical effect arises. If, however, we are dealing with a significant artist,
he reveals the strength to establish such a language as unified in the eyes of
the reader. Later, continuing to create inside this new but already culturally
established language, the poet turns it into a definite stylistic register. The
compatibility of the elements entering such a register becomes natural, even
neutral, while the boundary separating the style of the given poet from the
general literary surroundings is sharply emphasized. Thus in Pushkin's early
poem *Ruslan and Lyudmila*, contemporaries saw a variegation of style: a
combination of reminiscences in different styles from different literary
traditions. But in *Eugene Onegin*, whose style is distinguished by exceptional
citational complexity, an abundance of hints, references, and reminiscences, the
reader sees only the ease of simple authorial speech. What is sharply felt is
its unrepeatably "Pushkinian" character.

Thus the artistic text cannot be exclusively "rhetorical" or "stylistic." It is
a complex interweaving of both tendencies, supplemented by their collision in
metacultural structures, which perform the role of codes in the processes of
social communications.

The general relation of stylistic and rhetorical structural elements can be
represented as a scheme: semantics and stylistics, semantic rhetoric and
stylistic rhetoric.

Possible shifts toward the dominance of any of these elements produce diverse
combinations of more fundamental historical-semiotic categories such as
"romanticism," "classicism," and the like. At the same time, one should keep in
mind that in real texts there also operates a tension between the textual and
metatextual, coding levels, which leads to a doubling of this scheme.
