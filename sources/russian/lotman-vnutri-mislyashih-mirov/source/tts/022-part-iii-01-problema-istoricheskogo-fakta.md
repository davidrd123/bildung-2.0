# Part Three: Cultural Memory. History And Semiotics

## The Problem Of The Historical Fact

What are the tasks of historical science? Is history a science? These
questions have been posed many times, and many answers have been given. A
historian not inclined toward theorizing, one occupied with the investigation
of concrete material, usually tends to be satisfied with Ranke's formula: to
restore the past *wie es eigentlich gewesen*, as it actually happened.

The concept "restore the past" implies clarifying facts and establishing
relations among them. Establishing facts means gathering and comparing
documents and subjecting them to scientific criticism. From Scaliger to Francis
Bradley and his followers, criticism of the document has meant identifying
inauthentic documents, unreliable interpolations, and tendentious versions. An
important side of the historian's preliminary work is the ability to read the
document, the capacity to understand its historical meaning by relying on
textological skills and the researcher's intuition.

Yet even if we suppose the reader of the document to possess wide-ranging
erudition, experience, and acuity, the reader's position will be fundamentally
different from that of colleagues in any other field of science. The issue is
that by the very word "fact," the historian designates something highly
distinctive. Unlike the deductive sciences, which logically construct their
starting positions, or the experimental sciences, which can observe them, the
historian is condemned to deal with texts. The conditions of the experimental
sciences allow, at least in first approximation, the fact to be considered
something primary, initially given, preceding interpretation. The fact is
observed under laboratory conditions, possesses repeatability, and can be
processed statistically.

The historian is condemned to deal with texts. Between the event "as it
happened" and the historian stands a text, and this radically changes the
scientific situation. A text is always created by someone and for some purpose;
the event appears in it in encoded form. The historian must first of all take
the role of decipherer. For the historian, the fact is not the starting point
but the result of difficult efforts. The historian creates facts, striving to
extract extra-textual reality from the text, and from the story about an event,
the event itself.

Positivist "text criticism," widespread in the 19th century, drew attention to
what seemed to the historian to be conscious distortion of truth or the result
of superstition and ignorance. In the first case, the source of "untruth" was
most often considered to be the political prejudices of the text's creator, and
the historian often attributed the psychology and political passions of the
historian's own time to some remote epoch. In the second case, judgment was
made from the standpoint of the level of 19th-century science. Whatever did
not satisfy its conceptions was explained as the product of unenlightened
fantasy. It seemed enough to translate the text into the language of modernity,
for example by subjecting mythological information to psychoanalytic
interpretation, and to remove or "scientifically" interpret the miraculous
elements, in order for the "event" to appear from behind the text.

For a researcher experienced in the semiotic interpretation of sources, it is
obvious that the question must be posed differently. What is needed is the
reconstruction of the code, or rather the set of codes, used by the creator of
the text, and the establishment of their correlation with the codes used by the
researcher. The creator of the text records events that, from that creator's
point of view, appear significant, that is, correlated with elements of the
creator's code, and omits everything "insignificant." If a Russian chronicler,
entering a year into the chronicle, wrote nothing under that date, leaving a
blank, or wrote "it was peaceful," as in the Laurentian Chronicle under 1029,
this does not at all mean that during those years nothing happened from the
point of view of the modern researcher.

Decipherment is always reconstruction. In essence the researcher applies one
and the same method when reconstructing a lost part of a document and when
reading a preserved one. In both cases the researcher proceeds from the fact
that the document is written in another language, whose grammar still has to be
compiled.

Thus before establishing facts "for himself," the researcher establishes facts
for the person who composed the document under analysis. The researcher
encounters the degree to which every document is incomplete in its reflection
of life, and the huge layers of reality that are not considered facts and are
not subject to fixation. This area of the "excluded" is not only enormous but
mobile. One could compile an interesting list of "non-facts" for different
epochs.

Yet knowledge of some general "worldview of the epoch" still does not solve
the problem. Within one and the same epoch there are different genres of texts,
and each of them as a rule has its own code specificity. What is permitted in
one genre is forbidden in another. When researchers oppose the "realism" of
ancient comedy to the conventionality of tragedy, they assume that in comedy we
find "authentic" life, unencoded by genre rules and other code systems. Such a
view is naive. In this use, "realism" most often means coincidence between the
code of the text and the historian's everyday assumptions. An analogous
aberration occurs with a film viewer who watches a film connected with another
national-cultural tradition and naively supposes that the film "simply"
reproduces the life and customs of a distant country with ethnographic
precision.

Consciously or unconsciously, the fact encountered by the historian is always
constructed by the person who created the text. Thus, for example, in an
ancient Egyptian fresco depicting the birth of Queen Hatshepsut, she is shown
as a boy in accordance with the genre ritual. If there were no inscription, or
if it had not been preserved, we would have "realistic" proof of her male
nature.

A complex and heterogeneous picture of the "facts of an epoch" emerges. Every
genre, every culturally significant variety of text selects its own facts. What
is a fact for myth will not be one for the chronicle; a fact for page fifteen
of a newspaper is not always a fact for the front page. Thus from the position
of the sender, a fact is always the result of choosing from the mass of
surrounding events an event that, according to the sender's conceptions, has
meaning.

But a fact is not a concept, not an idea. It is a text. That is, it always has
a real-material embodiment. It is an event to which meaning has been given, not
a meaning to which, as in a parable, the appearance of an event has been given.
As a result, the fact selected by the sender turns out to be broader than the
meaning assigned to it in the code. Therefore, although it is univocal for the
sender, for the receiver, including the historian, it is subject to
interpretation.

The historian not only reconstructs the code of the document's sender in order
to clarify that sender's conception of the reported facts. The historian is
also forced to restore the whole spectrum of possible interpretations of what
contemporaries receiving the text considered to be facts here, and what
meaning they assigned to them. Finally, because a fact, being a text,
inevitably includes elements that are extrasystemic, insignificant from the
point of view of the codes of the epoch that created it, the historian can
single out in those elements what is significant from the historian's own point
of view.

Let us give an example. In 1945, in Upper Egypt near the settlement of Nag
Hammadi, ancient Chenoboskion, a cache was found containing a number of codices
of Gnostic content in the Coptic language. Among them was the so-called
*Gospel of Thomas*. In saying 76, according to the division by Doresse adopted
by M. K. Trofimova, we read the scene in which a person asks Jesus to tell his
brothers to divide the father's possessions with him. Jesus replies by asking
who made him the one who divides, then turns to his disciples and says that he
should not become one who divides.

The reader of this text could choose a direct or a symbolic interpretation of
the saying. In the first case, Jesus refuses to take part in the division of
earthly property as an occupation "of this world." In the second, the meaning
unfolds as an ever-deepening image of evil connected with division, and of good
whose property is union, the merging of opposites. In the final account, the
symbolic reading leads to the Gnostic idea of dividedness as the property of
the material and falsely apparent world, and of unity as the property of the
higher spiritual essence of the All.

But in both cases, for sender and receiver alike, the meaning of the text lay
in Jesus' words, which were subject to penetrating interpretation. The rest of
the text turned out to be "outside the system," since it did not carry the
teaching. Yet for a modern researcher, with a certain approach, the "fact" may
also be that the saying reproduces not only the Savior's words but the whole
scenario of the scene: first the words carrying immediate everyday meaning,
which Christ pronounces while turned toward "a certain person" and standing
with his back to the disciples located in the background of the scene; then,
turning toward the disciples, he translates the whole situation into the realm
of hidden and mysterious meanings with the words, "May I not become one who
divides."

From a certain point of view, precisely the gestural scenario may constitute
the "fact" for the researcher. Whether it is then interpreted in terms of the
poetics of this text, or as testimony to the preservation of visual impressions
from an actually existing scene, is already a question for further
interpretation.

Thus historical science, from its very first step, finds itself in a strange
position. For other sciences, the fact is a starting point, a primary basis
from which science begins to uncover relations and regularities. In the sphere
of culture, the fact is the result of preliminary analysis. It is created by
science in the process of research and does not appear to the researcher as
something absolute. The fact is relative to a certain universe of culture. It
emerges from semiotic space and dissolves back into it as cultural codes
change. At the same time, as a text, it is not completely determined by this
semiotic space, and through its extrasystemic aspects it revolutionizes the
system, pushing it toward restructuring.
