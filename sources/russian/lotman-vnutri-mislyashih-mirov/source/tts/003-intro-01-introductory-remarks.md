# Introductory Remarks

Goethe once wrote that people call us madmen and fantasists, but that once the
mind escapes its sad dependence, the skilled brain of a thinker will in time
create a thinker artificially.

This question has not lost its sharpness. On the contrary, with every new step
of science it returns again and again, changing its formulations. Yet there is
a substantial obstacle on this path: we cannot satisfactorily explain what this
very intellect is that we intend to create artificially. An episode recounted
in the memoirs of Andrei Bely involuntarily comes to mind. His father, the
mathematician and chairman of the Moscow Mathematical Society N. V. Bugaev,
once presided over a scholarly meeting at which a paper on animal intelligence
was being read. The chair interrupted the speaker with the question whether he
knew what intelligence was. It turned out that the speaker did not. Then
Bugaev began asking those sitting in the front row: "You? You?" No one knew.
Bugaev declared: "In view of the fact that no one knows what intelligence is,
there can be no discussion of animal intelligence. I declare the meeting
closed."

Although the incident described took place at the beginning of our century, the
situation in this area has changed little. The reason, it seems, is that
intellectual activity is usually regarded as a unique capacity of the human
being. But an isolated object, standing outside all comparison, cannot be an
object of science. The task, then, comes down to discovering a series of
"thinking objects" whose comparison would allow us to isolate some invariant of
intellectuality. The concept of intellect has many aspects, and the writer of
these lines does not feel capable of giving it an exhaustive definition. Yet if
we restrict ourselves to the semiotic aspect, the task appears solvable.

From this point of view, intellectual capacity can be reduced to the following
functions:

1. The transmission of existing information, that is, texts.

2. The creation of new information, that is, the creation of texts that cannot
   be univocally derived by given algorithms from those already present, and
   that possess a certain degree of unpredictability.

3. Memory: the capacity to store and reproduce information, that is, texts.

The study of semiotic systems created by humanity throughout its cultural
history led to unexpected conclusions: these same functions, to one degree or
another, also belong to semiotic objects. If in texts of a communicative kind
the function of transmitting information predominates, then in artistic texts
created by art, the capacity to generate new messages comes forward.

At the same time, it was established that the minimal working semiotic
structure is not one artificially isolated language, or a text in such a
language, but a parallel pair of mutually untranslatable languages that are
nevertheless connected by a block of translation. Such a mechanism is the
minimal cell for generating new messages. It is also the minimal unit of such a
semiotic object as culture. Thus culture turns out to be two-in-one, at least
minimally, and at the same time an indivisibly unified minimal working semiotic
structure. This approach advanced the concept of the semiosphere and
emphasized the importance of studying the semiotics of culture.

At the same time it became possible to define semiotic objects of this kind as
"thinking structures," since they satisfy the signs of intellect formulated
above. The fact that for their "work" they require an intellectual interlocutor
and an input text should not disturb us. After all, even a completely normal
human intellect, if from birth it is fully isolated from the inflow of texts
from outside and from any dialogue, remains a normal machine, but one not put
into operation. It cannot switch itself on by itself. For intellect to
function, another intellect is required. L. S. Vygotsky emphasized that
initially every higher function was divided between two people and was a mutual
psychological process. Intellect is always an interlocutor.

Observations about the bipolar asymmetry of semiotic mechanisms unexpectedly
received a parallel in studies of the functional asymmetry of the cerebral
hemispheres. The presence in the individual thinking apparatus of mechanisms
functionally isomorphic with the semiotic mechanisms of culture opened new
scientific perspectives. The question of contact between humanistic semiotics
and neurophysiology was unexpected for many, but it met with warm support from
the brilliant linguist Roman Jakobson, who called the enemies of this problem
the defenders of "brainless linguistics." In the Soviet Union these problems
were actively developed in the neurophysiological laboratory of the late
L. Ya. Balonov, in collaboration with V. L. Deglin, T. V. Chernigovskaya,
N. N. Nikolaenko, and others, and in the semiotic aspect by Vyacheslav Vsevolod
Ivanov.

This question, however, led to a still more general scientific problem: the
relation of symmetry and asymmetry, a problem that had already concerned Louis
Pasteur.

The need for "thinking" semiotic structures to receive an initial impulse from
another thinking structure, and for text-generating mechanisms to receive some
external text as a trigger, recalls two things. On the one hand, it recalls the
so-called autocatalytic reactions: reactions in which, in order to obtain the
final product, or to accelerate the chemical process, the final result must
already be present in some quantity at the beginning of the reaction. On the
other hand, this question finds a parallel in the unresolved problem of the
"beginning" of culture and the "beginning" of life. Let us recall that
V. I. Vernadsky refused to answer the question posed in that way, considering
more fruitful the study of the relation between structures that are
binary-asymmetric and at the same time unified. We follow the same path.

In accordance with the three functions of semiotic objects that we have
isolated, we divide the exposition into three parts. The first considers the
mechanism of sense-generation as the result of the mutual tension between
languages that are mutually untranslatable and yet projected onto one another:
the conventional, discrete, verbal language and the iconic, continuous, spatial
language. To this corresponds the minimal act of producing a new message.

The second section is devoted to the semiosphere: the synchronic semiotic space
that fills the boundaries of culture and is the condition for the work of
individual semiotic structures, while at the same time being their product. If
the text stands at the center of the first section, then culture occupies the
corresponding place in the second.

The third section is devoted to questions of memory, the diachrony of depth,
and history as a mechanism of intellectual activity. The main question here is
the semiotics of history.

As a whole, these three sections are meant to show the work of the semiotic
space that envelops human beings and human society as an intellectual world in
constant mutual communication with the individual intellectual world of the
human being.
