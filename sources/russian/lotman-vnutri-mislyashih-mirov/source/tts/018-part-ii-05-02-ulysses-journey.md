# Symbolic Spaces

## 2. The Journey Of Ulysses In Dante's Divine Comedy

Dante compared himself to a geometer. He could equally be compared with a
cosmologist and astronomer, considering that already in the *Vita Nuova* he
began with highly complex and specialized calculations of the laws of cosmic
motion. Yet it would be most accurate to call him an architect, for the entire
*Divine Comedy* is an enormous architectural structure, a construction of the
universe. Such an approach implied the transfer, onto the cosmic universe, of
the psychology of individual creativity: the world as a product of creation had
to possess purpose and meaning, and of each detail one could ask, "What does it
mean?" This question, natural in the perception of an architectural creation,
when applied to Nature and the Universe, transformed them into semiotic texts
whose meaning had to be deciphered. And as in architecture, spatial semiotics
came to the foreground.

The world appeared as an enormous message from its Creator, who encoded a
mysterious communication in the language of spatial structure. Dante deciphers
this message by building this world a second time in his own text, taking the
position not of receiver but of sender of the message. The general orientation
of the poetics of the *Comedy* toward hidden encoding is connected with this. Yet
the specificity of Dante's position as creator of the text lies in the fact
that, rising to the Creator's point of view, he does not abandon the human
point of view.

Let us illustrate this with one example. Later we shall pause over the
significance, for Dante's construction, of the spatial axis "up-down." In the
*Comedy* this axis appears clearly in two senses. One is relative and acts only
within the limits of the Earth. Here "down" is identified with the center of
gravity of the terrestrial sphere, and "up" with any radius away from the
center. Dante and Virgil cross the center of the Earth in the final canto of
the *Inferno*: what seemed to Dante like a return along the same road is
explained by Virgil as a turning at the point where all weight is drawn
together.

However, Dante's cosmic building also has an absolute up and down. People
standing at different poles of the terrestrial sphere may be turned with their
feet toward one another, as Dante notes in the *Convivio*. But the absolutely
oriented vertical is formed by an axis that pierces the Earth. Its lower end is
oriented toward Jerusalem; it passes through Hell, the center of the Earth, and
Purgatory, and comes to rest in the shining center of the Empyrean. This is the
axis along which Lucifer was cast down from heaven.

The contradiction between relative and absolute up and down in Dante's system
has already attracted attention. The philosopher and mathematician Pavel
Florensky tried to resolve it by starting from the concepts of non-Euclidean
geometry and relativistic physics. He argued that the reversal of the normal
depends on whether we remain on the same side, that is, on a one-sided surface,
or pass to the other side, on a two-sided surface. Florensky illustrates this
thought with the example of the *Divine Comedy*. After the boundary at the
center of the Earth, the poet ascends the mountain of Purgatory and rises
through the heavenly spheres. Since the underground passage by which Dante and
Virgil ascend was formed by the fall of Lucifer, the route to heaven follows
the line of Lucifer's fall, but in the opposite direction. Thus, Florensky
concludes, Dante moves all the time along a straight line and, after one
turning-over on the way, returns to his previous place in the same position in
which he left it. Florensky therefore characterizes Dante's space as close to
elliptical space.

Despite the fact that Florensky, in his desire to prove that medieval
consciousness is closer to 20th-century thought than to the mechanical
ideology of the Renaissance, allows himself certain enthusiasms, the problem he
revealed is one of the most important in the text of the *Comedy*: the
contradiction between real-everyday space and cosmic-transcendent space. Yet
the solution to this contradiction should probably be sought on another plane.

If we examine the cosmic scheme of the *Comedy*, we have to note the following.
According to Aristotelian conceptions, the northern hemisphere, as less
perfect, is below, while the southern hemisphere is above the terrestrial
sphere. Therefore Dante and Virgil, descending according to the relative scale
of the earthly opposition "up-down," that is, going deeper from the surface of
the Earth toward its center, are at the same time ascending upward in relation
to the orientation of the world axis. This paradox finds its resolution in the
domain of Dante's semiotics.

In Dante's system of ideas, space has meaning. Every spatial category is
assigned a definite sense. Yet the relation of expression and content here
lacks the arbitrariness characteristic of semiotic systems based on social
convention. In Ferdinand de Saussure's terminology, these are not signs but
symbols. For Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, one of the functions of the
symbol is to make the world of super-being really manifest on the level of
being. The content or meaning of a symbol is not conditionally joined to its
imagistic expression, as in allegory, but shines through it.

The closer a given text stands, hierarchically, to the heavenly light that
constitutes the true content of all medieval symbolism, the more brightly
meaning shines through it and the more unconditional and direct its expression
becomes. The further a text stands from the source of truth on the ladder of
universal hierarchy, the dimmer the reflection of truth and the more
conditional the relation of content and expression. Thus at the highest level,
truth appears to immediate contemplation by the spiritual gaze; at the lowest,
it takes on the character of signs with a purely conventional nature.

Precisely because sinful people and demons of different hierarchical levels use
purely conventional signs, they can lie, commit treacherous acts, betray and
deceive, and in various ways separate content from expression. Righteous people
also use conventional signs in communication with one another, but they do not
turn their conventional nature to evil. Turning to higher sources of truth
opens before them the possibility of penetrating into the unconditional
symbolic world of meanings.

Thus from one step of the hierarchy to another, the nature of the relation
between content and expression changes. As one moves upward, symbolism
increases and the conventional character of signs weakens. Yet in semantic terms each new
hierarchical level will be isomorphic with all the others, and consequently
relations of equivalence will be established among elements of different levels
that have the same meaning.

This has direct bearing on the interpretation of "up" and "down" in the
*Comedy*.

The axis "up-down" organizes the whole semantic architectonics of the text:
all parts and cantos of the *Comedy* are marked in relation to their position
on this basic coordinate. Correspondingly, Dante's movement in the text is
always descent or ascent. These concepts have a strongly symbolic character:
behind real ascent or descent there shines through spiritual elevation or
falling. All the sins, arranged by Dante in a strict hierarchy, receive spatial
fixing, so that the gravity of the sin corresponds to the depth of the sinner's
abode.

Dante and Virgil's descent into Hell has the meaning of movement downward. The
paradoxical situation in which, while descending, they are rising, is
emphasized by the verse about the Moon, which, having passed into the southern
hemisphere, floats at the travelers' feet. Therefore in a certain higher sense,
this descent is ascent: by descending into Hell and coming to know the abyss of
sin, Dante is morally elevated in absolute relation. Descent is equivalent to
ascent. But at the same time, according to earthly criteria, it is precisely a
descent, preserving all the features of real downward movement, including the
physical fatigue of the travelers. Having the meaning of descent, this path
leads the poets to the "city of woe" and makes them contemplators of infernal
torments.

The complex dialectic of the conditional and the unconditional, which we
encounter as soon as we begin to reflect on Dante's basic semiotic axis of
space, introduces us into the center of the moral hierarchy of the *Comedy*.
Attention has often been drawn to the non-trivial distribution of sins in the
*Comedy* among circles of punishment. Dante diverges both from church norms and
from everyday conceptions. If 14th-century readers could not help being struck
by the fact that hypocrites are placed in the sixth ditch of the eighth circle
while heretics are only in the sixth circle, the modern lover of Dante is
astonished to see that murder is punished less severely than theft, or than the
making of counterfeit coins and false jewels. Yet there is a strict logic in
this distribution.

We have already noted that as one descends from the summits of Divine Truth and
Love, the unconditional character of the bond between expression and
content weakens. In earthly life people are guided by divine symbols in matters
of Faith and by conventional signs in their relations with one another. The
conventional nature of these signs contains the possibility of their double
use: they can be used as means of truth, by observing conventions, or of lies,
by violating or distorting them. The devil, father of lies, is the inspirer of
violations of conventions and of every kind of agreement. The violation of true
links between expression and content is worse than murder, for it kills Truth
and is the source of the Lie in all its infernal essence.

Therefore there is a deep logic in the fact that sins consisting of unrighteous
actions are evaluated by Dante as less grave than all cases of lying use of
signs: words, as in slander, flattery, false counsel, and so on; values, as in
counterfeiters and alchemists; documents, as in falsifiers; trust, as in
thieves; ideas and signs of dignity, as in hypocrites and simoniacs. Worst of
all are the violators of agreements and obligations: traitors. Wrong actions
cause individual evil. The violation of pre-established sign relations tears
apart the very basis of human society and turns Earth into Satan's kingdom,
into Hell.

Naturally, lying reigns in Hell. Here the links between sign and content are
torn apart, and lying is not a deviation from the norm but the law. The devils
lie to Virgil when they report that only the sixth bridge over the ditches has
collapsed; in fact, all the bridges have collapsed. But Dante too, in the 33rd
canto of the *Inferno*, while talking with Alberigo, swears that he will remove
the ice from the sinner's eyes, and immediately violates his oath. The gravest
crime, treachery, turns out to be valor there, where rudeness is politeness.

The opposition between Truth and Lie is embodied in the spatial model as the
antithesis of a straight line directed upward and circular movement in the
horizontal plane. The idea that movement in a circle has a sorcerous, magical,
and, from the medieval Christian point of view, devilish nature was universal.
One could compare this with Blessed Augustine's arguments against the idea of
the circular movement of time and the cyclical repetition of events. Augustine
opposes to this the conception of the linear movement of time: Christ died
once for our sins.

In Dante, the ethical model of space is directly correlated with his cosmic
model. Dante's cosmic model was formed under the influence of Aristotle,
Ptolemy, Al-Farghani, and Albert the Great. Yet the ideas of Pythagoras
undoubtedly also affected him. In the light of Pythagorean conceptions of the
highest perfection of the circle and the sphere among geometric figures and
bodies, the circular construction of the circles of Hell receives this
explanation: the circle is an image of perfection, but a circle located above
is the perfection of good, while one located below is the perfection of evil.
The architecture of Hell is the perfection of evil.

Dante was especially affected by the Pythagorean system of binary oppositions,
in particular the opposition of the straight, as equivalent to good, to the
crooked, which is the graphic equivalent of evil. The movement of sinners in
Hell takes place along closed curves, while Dante's own movement is along an
ascending spiral that passes into flight along a straight line. It must be
emphasized, however, that against the background of Pythagorean ideas Dante's
difference appears vividly: it is not the center of the sphere but the summit
of the world Axis that is the point of his spatial and ethical-religious
orientation. The Pythagoreans singled out a number of basic binary oppositions,
such as even/odd, right/left, limited/unlimited, male/female, one/many, and
light/dark, but the opposition basic for Dante, up/down, remains unmarked in
their system.

Thus the spatial model of Dante's world constitutes a definite continuum into
which certain trajectories of individual paths and fates are inscribed. After
death, a human soul follows a definite path in the continuum of the World
Construction, which brings it into the space corresponding to its moral value.
But blessed souls are in the rest of continuous abiding, while sinful souls
perform constant cyclical movements. Sometimes these take the form of direct
spatial movement, endless flights or walking in a circle; sometimes they take
the form of repeated transformations: cut to pieces, they immediately regain
the wholeness of their appearance in order to be cut apart again; burned, they
are reborn from ashes in order to burn again; they grow skin that is endlessly
stripped away from them, and so on.

Against this background the figure of Dante stands out sharply. He possesses
freedom of all movements, since his upward movement includes knowledge of all
false paths. Yet Dante is not the only figure in the *Comedy* who has a sharply
expressed individual trajectory of movement. In this respect one character of
the work must be named: Ulysses. The uniqueness of this figure has repeatedly
drawn the attention of commentators and researchers. One cannot deny that the
journey of Ulysses is a very distinctive episode.

The image of Ulysses in the *Comedy* doubles. Ulysses is placed in the Evil
Ditches as a giver of treacherous counsel. In light of what has been said about
Dante's attitude toward cunning and deceit, this is not surprising. Something
else attracts attention: Ulysses' story about his journey and death. Ulysses,
like Dante, is endowed with an individual path. There is another essential
similarity between their routes in the world continuum: they are heroes of the
straight path. Their similarity also appears in the fact that their paths
embody open movement, an impulse toward infinity. Beginning in precisely marked
places, they move in a chosen direction, rather than striving toward a
previously designated final point.

But there is a fundamental difference in the routes of their movement. The
meaning of Dante's path is embodied in an impulse upward; every step is marked
on this scale, representing descent downward or ascent upward. Ulysses' path is
the only significant movement in the *Comedy* for which the up-down axis is not
relevant: the whole route unfolds on the horizontal plane. If Dante is placed
inside a crystalline cosmic globe whose three-dimensional space is pierced by a
vertical axis, then Ulysses travels as if on a map. It is not accidental that
when Dante, from the constellation Gemini, casts his gaze on Earth, he sees,
as on a map, the movement of Ulysses' ship beyond Cadiz.

Ulysses is Dante's distinctive double. His doubleness appears in two essential
aspects. First, both of them, unlike the other characters whose sins or virtues
have unambiguously fixed them to definite axes of Dante's world, are "heroes of
the path." They are constantly in motion and, more importantly, constantly
cross the boundaries of forbidden spaces. The rest of the crowd of Dante's
characters either remains in place or hurries toward some appointed place whose
boundaries are determined by their place in the Universe; each of these
characters has a space of his own. Only Dante and Ulysses, voluntary or forced
exiles driven by a powerful passion, cross the borders that separate one domain
of the world structure from another.

Second, their shared route links them. Dante and Ulysses hurry in
one direction. By different paths they move toward Purgatory: Dante through
Hell and through the caves made by Lucifer's falling body; Ulysses by sea, past
Spain, Gibraltar, and Morocco. Although Dante's journey takes place in the
infernal world and Ulysses' in real geographical space, the goal toward which
they hurry is one. This is confirmed by the fact that in his journey through
Purgatory and Paradise, Dante as it were takes up the relay of the dead
Ulysses. Twice the poet recalls the drowned hero, and both recollections are
full of meaning.

On the second night in Purgatory, the Siren appears to Dante. The image of the
Siren recalls the sea exploits and courage of Odysseus. But her falseness, her
capacity to separate appearance and essence and to conceal the repulsive under
the cover of the beautiful, involuntarily hints at the world of deceptions in
the Evil Ditches, to which Dante assigned Ulysses. The capacity for
transformations is for Dante a sign of lying; this is precisely how liars are
punished in Hell.

Ulysses is recalled a second time when the poet enters the zone of Gemini.
Finding himself at the antipodal point to the place of Ulysses' death, Dante
flies toward the meridian of the Pillars of Hercules and then, in infinite
height, repeats Ulysses' path until he finds himself above the place of
Ulysses' destruction, on the meridian Zion-Purgatory. There, along the axis of
Lucifer's fall, passing through the place where Ulysses' ship broke apart, he
makes his ascent into the Empyrean. Thus Dante's journey seems to continue
Ulysses' journey from the moment of the latter's death. Up to that point, they
as it were duplicate one another.

But the meaning of every doubleness is to reveal difference on the basis of
similarity. So it is in this case.

Like Dante, Ulysses combines a striving for knowledge of the human being with a
desire to know the mysteries of the structure of the world. Dante clearly
responds to this noble thirst for knowledge. In the *Comedy* one repeatedly
encounters the opposition of genuine human beings to beast-like creatures in
human form. Many infernal punishments are built on the realization of the
metaphor of beast-likeness. Therefore Ulysses' words reminding his companions
that they are human beings, not beasts, and that they were born for noble
knowledge rather than animal existence, are filled with deep meaning for the
poet.

Yet the paths to knowledge are different for Dante and Ulysses. Dante's
knowledge is joined to the constant ascent of the knower along the axis of
moral values. It is knowledge granted at the cost of the knower's moral
perfection. Knowledge elevates, and moral elevation enlightens the mind.
Ulysses' thirst for knowledge is extra-moral. It is connected neither with
morality nor with immorality; it lies on another plane and has no relation to
ethical problems. Even Purgatory is for him only a blank space on the map, and
his striving toward it is a journey dictated by the thirst for geographical
discovery. Dante is a pilgrim; Ulysses is a traveler. It is no accident that
Dante, in his infernal and cosmic pilgrimage, always has a Guide; Ulysses is
guided only by his daring and courage.

With the mind and character of an adventurer, Ulysses combines the
indomitability of Farinata. The epic rogue, the fairy-tale trickster-hero who
in Homer's poetry became the cunning king of Ithaca, acquires in Dante's poem
the traits of a person of the Renaissance, a discoverer and traveler. This
image both attracts Dante by its wholeness and strength, and repels him by its
moral indifferentism. Yet as Dante peers into the image of the heroic
adventurer, seeker, and investigator in all domains except the moral one, an
image being created by the epoch, he discerns in it something more general than
the psychology of the immediate future: features inherent in the scientific,
and more broadly cultural, consciousness of the modern age: the separation of
knowledge and morality, discovery and its result, science and the personality
of the scientist.

It would be a mistake to see in the opposition between Dante and Ulysses
outlined here only a historically long-vanished conflict between the psychology
of a medieval thinker and that of a Renaissance person.

The history of world culture has repeatedly confirmed that thinkers standing at
the threshold of one or another decisive epoch often see its meaning and result
more clearly than the following generations, already drawn into its whirlpool.
Standing at the threshold of the modern age, Dante saw one of the main dangers
of the coming culture. His own ideal was marked by integration. The
encyclopedism of his knowledge, which included practically the entire arsenal
of the science of his time, did not form in his consciousness a sum of
scattered information, but a single integrated building. This, in turn, flowed
into the ideal of world empire and the harmonious construction of the cosmos.
At the center of this gigantic construction stood the human being, powerful
like the giants of the Renaissance, but integrated into the surrounding world,
connected with all the concentric circles of the world structure, and therefore
permeated with moral pathos. The tendency toward singling out the separate
personality and toward specialization, leading to the division of intellect and
conscience, science and morality, which Dante foresaw in the coming epoch, was
deeply alien to him.

Of course it would be naive to identify fully Dante as the hero of the
*Comedy* and Dante as author. Dante the character is Ulysses' antipode,
remembering that no one enclosed in Hell should call forth sympathy. Dante the
author of the *Comedy* cannot refuse Ulysses sympathy, and clearly gives him a
part of his own emotional personality. Dante's thought is born from the complex
dialogic correlation of these images.
