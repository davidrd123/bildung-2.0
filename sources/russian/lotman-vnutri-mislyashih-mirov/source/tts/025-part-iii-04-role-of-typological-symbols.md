# On The Role Of Typological Symbols In The History Of Culture

Analyzing the most archaic sociocultural models, we can distinguish two that
are of special interest in light of their later transformations in cultural
history. With a certain degree of convention, we will call one of them magical
and the other religious. It must be emphasized at once that we are not speaking
about any real cultures, but about typological principles. Religions that have
appeared in cultural history are usually complex combinations of both elements.
In some world religions, in our terminology, magic predominates.

The magical system is characterized by reciprocity. This means that both
agents participating in the relation are actors. For example, a sorcerer
performs certain actions, and in response the conjured force performs its own.
One-sided actions do not exist in the system of magic. If the sorcerer, through
ignorance, performs incorrect actions that are powerless to summon the conjured
force and make it act, then such words and gestures are not recognized as
actions within the magical system.

The magical system is also characterized by compulsion. Certain actions by one
side entail obligatory and precisely foreseen actions by the other. In magical
relations, many texts testify that the sorcerer forces an otherworldly power to
appear and act against its will, even though the sorcerer possesses less power.
The actions performed by one side require definite answering actions by the
other. In this case power is, as it were, distributed equally: otherworldly
forces have power over the sorcerer, and the sorcerer has power over them.

The magical system is characterized, third, by equivalence. Relations between
counterparties in the system of magic have the character of equivalent
exchange, and may be likened to the exchange of conventional signs.

Fourth, it is characterized by contractuality. The interacting sides enter into
a certain kind of contract. This contract may have external expression, such as
the conclusion of agreements or oaths to observe conditions, or it may be
implied. But the presence of a contract implies the possibility of its
violation, just as the conventional-sign nature of exchange implies the
potential possibility of deception and disinformation. From this inevitably
follows the possibility of various interpretations of the contract and the
effort by each side to invest the wording of contractual formulas with content
advantageous to itself.

At the basis of the religious act lies not exchange, but unconditional
entrusting of oneself into another's power. One side gives itself to the other
without accompanying this act with any conditions, except that the receiving
side is recognized as the bearer of superior might. Relations of this type are
characterized by one-sidedness. They have a unidirectional character: the
subject giving itself into another's power counts on protection, but there is
no obligatory link between this act and a responding action. The absence of a
reward cannot serve as a basis for breaking off the relation.

From this follows the absence of compulsion in such relations. One side gives
everything, and the other may give or not give. It may also refuse the worthy
giver and give to the unworthy, to one who does not participate in the given
system of relations or violates it.

These relations do not have the character of equivalence. They exclude the
psychology of exchange and do not admit the idea that the basic values have a
conditionally conventional character. Therefore the means of communication in
this case are not signs, but symbols, whose nature excludes the possibility of
alienating expression from content, and therefore excludes deception or
interpretation.

Consequently, relations of this type have the character not of a contract but
of an unconditional gift.

It should be emphasized that we are speaking of a model of cultural psychology
for these types of relation. Real world religions have never been able to do
without some degree of magical psychology. For example, while rejecting the
idea of an equivalent-exchange relation between human being and god within
earthly life, they have in a number of cases included the idea of reward in the
afterlife, establishing a system of compulsory, that is, univocally conditioned
and therefore just, relation between earthly and otherworldly life.

The official church of the pagan Roman Empire in its last centuries, behind
whose facade deeply concealed cults of a religious character were hidden, was
magical. The system of sacrifices to the gods formed the basis of contractual
relations with them, and the official worship of the emperor had the character
of a convention with the state. Precisely because of the features of magic
noted above, the "religion" of a Roman did not contradict either the Roman's
developed juridical thinking, rooted in the very depths of cultural psychology,
or the entire structure of the elaborated legal state. From the Roman point of
view, Christianity was a deeply anti-state principle, since it was religious in
the exact sense of the word and therefore excluded formal-juridical,
contractual-legal consciousness. For a person of Roman culture, renouncing this
consciousness meant renouncing the very idea of statehood.

The pagan cults in Rus apparently had a shamanistic, that is, magical
character. The coincidence of Rus's adoption of Christianity with the rise of
Kievan statehood entailed a number of significant consequences for the question
that interests us. The resulting dual faith gave two opposed models of social
relations. The relations between prince and retinue, which needed formal
organization, tended toward contractuality. This model reflected most
adequately the emerging system of feudal ties based on patronage and
vassalage, the whole structure of mutual rights and obligations and
etiquette-sign exchange on which the ideological organization of knightly
society rested. The tradition of Russian magical paganism organically entered
here into the order that formed from the European synthesis of tribal
institutions among barbarian peoples and the Roman juridical tradition, which
was firmly preserved in the old cities of the empire with their rights-defending
communes, complex legal relations, and abundance of jurists.

In the West, contractual consciousness, magical in its distant foundation, was
surrounded by the authority of Roman state tradition and occupied an equal
place alongside the religious-authoritarian principle. In Rus, however, it was
understood as pagan by nature. This left its stamp on its social evaluation. It
is revealing that in the Western tradition the contract as such does not have
an evaluative nature. A contract can be concluded with the devil, as in the
life of Saint Theophilus, who sold his soul to the devil and later bought it
back through repentance. But a contract with the forces of holiness and good is
also possible. Thus the *Little Flowers* of Francis of Assisi contains the
famous story of the contract between Francis and the fierce wolf of Gubbio.
Francis accuses the wolf of behaving like the worst of robbers and murderers,
devouring not only animals but also attacking people who bear the image of God.
Then he proposes peace between the wolf and the people of the town. The
exchange is equivalent: the wolf will cease its crimes, and the inhabitants of
Gubbio will cease persecuting it and will provide it with food. The wolf bends
its head to show that it promises. The contract is concluded and observed by
both sides until the wolf's death.

No such texts are known to us either in Russian folk tradition or in medieval
book tradition in Rus. A contract is possible only with diabolical force or
with its pagan equivalents, as in the contract between the peasant and the
bear. This first of all casts an emotional light on contract as such. It is
deprived of the aura of cultural value. In the knightly life of the West, where
relations with God and the saints can be modeled on the system of "suzerain
and vassal" and subordinated to conditional ritual such as knighthood and
service to the Lady, the contract, supported by ritual, gesture, parchment, and
seals, is overshadowed by an aura of holiness and receives the highest value
authority. In Rus, the contract is perceived as a purely human affair, in the
sense of "human" as opposed to "divine." The introduction of kissing the cross
when a contract has to be sealed testifies precisely to the fact that without
unconditional, extra-contractual divine authority, the contract is not
sufficiently guaranteed.

Second, in all cases where a contract is concluded with unclean force, keeping
it is sinful and breaking it is salvific. It is precisely in communication with
unclean force that the conventionality of verbal-sign communication appears,
allowing words to be used for deception. The possibility of different
interpretations of the word, casuistry, is likewise identified not with
clarifying its true meaning but with the desire to deceive. Compare
Dostoevsky's phrase: "A lawyer is a conscience for sale."

One can compare the fairy tale "The Serpent and the Gypsy." The serpent and
the gypsy agree to compete in whistling. The serpent whistles and the leaves
fall from all the trees. The gypsy says that the serpent whistles well, but
still not better than he does. He tells the serpent to blindfold itself first,
because when he whistles the serpent's eyes will fly out of its forehead. The
serpent believes him and covers its eyes. Then the gypsy takes a cudgel and
"whistles" the serpent on the head.

Wordplay that exposes the conditional nature of the sign and turns contract
into deception is possible in relation to the devil, the serpent, the bear, but
unthinkable in communication with God and the world of holiness. The saying of
Daniel the Exile is well known: lie to the world, he says, but not to God; it
is impossible to lie to God or play with the highest powers. It is revealing
that lying and playing are equated.

In connection with this, the system of relations established in medieval
society, the system of mutual obligations between supreme power and feudal
lords, receives a negative evaluation quite early. Daniel the Exile, assuring
the prince that counselors are cunning servants who will bring their lord into
grief, opposes to them the ideal of devotion. He himself is not ashamed of
comparison with a dog: princes and boyars love a good dog. Service by contract
is bad service. Much later, Peter I would write in irritation to Prince
Sheremetev, whom he suspected of secret sympathy for old boyar rights, that
such behavior was like a servant who, seeing his master drowning, refuses to
save him until he checks whether it is written in his contract that he must
pull him out of the water. These words can be compared with Kurbatov's letter
to Peter: "I truly wish good to you, sovereign, without any pretense, as to
God." This comparison is not accidental; it has deep roots.

Centralized power in Rus was built according to the model of religious
relations in a much more direct form than in the West. The isomorphic model
constructed in the *Domostroi* - God in the universe, the tsar in the state,
the father in the family - reflected three degrees of unconditional human
self-entrustment and copied the religious system of relations onto other
levels. The concept of "the sovereign's service" that arose in these
conditions implied the absence of conditions between the sides. On one side,
unconditional and complete self-giving was implied; on the other, mercy.

The concept of service went back genetically to the psychology of unfree
members of the prince's patrimonial apparatus. As the role grew of this
bureaucracy personally dependent on the prince, transforming into a state
bureaucracy, and as the role grew of the prince's hired army, the psychology of
the princely court became the state psychology of service people. Religious
feelings were transferred to the sovereign. Service became serving. Dignity was
defined by mercy. Vasily Gryaznoi writes to Ivan the Terrible: if not for your
sovereign mercy, what kind of person would I be?

The clash of these two types of psychology can be traced throughout the whole
of the Russian Middle Ages. If the psychology of exchange and contract
cultivates signness, ritual, and etiquette, the religious-state position is
oriented toward symbolism and practicalism. The paradoxical combination of
these last two qualities should not surprise us.

Knightly culture is oriented toward signness. In order to acquire cultural
value in this system, a thing must become a sign, that is, must be maximally
cleansed of its practical extra-sign function. Thus "honor" for the feudal lord
of ancient Rus was connected with receiving from the suzerain a rich part of
military booty or a large gift. But after receiving a reward, the laws of honor
required it to be used in such a way as to humiliate its material value as much
as possible and thereby emphasize its sign value. Precious fabrics and furs are
laid down as bridges over swamps and muddy places.

A model of knightly behavior is given in the Russian version of the poem about
Digenes Akritas, the *Deed of Devgenii*. The hero Devgenii decides to obtain
as wife the beautiful daughter of Strategos, whose father and brothers have
killed all seekers of her hand. When he arrives at Strategos's court, the girl
is alone; her father and brothers are away. Devgenii could carry off his
beloved without hindrance. Instead he orders her to stay and inform her father
of the coming abduction. Strategos refuses to believe it. Devgenii breaks down
the gates, rides into the courtyard, and loudly calls out for Strategos and his
strong sons to come out and see the abduction of their sister. Strategos still
refuses to believe that someone brave enough has appeared to challenge him to
combat. After waiting three hours in vain, Devgenii carries off the bride. Yet
the success of the enterprise causes him not joy but grief: he has acquired
great shame. He nevertheless secures the battle, defeats the father and
brothers of the bride, takes them captive, then releases them, sends the bride
home, goes to woo her again, and this time receives her with great honor.

Here everything - bride, battle, wedding - is turned into signs of knightly
honor. It is valuable not in itself, but because of the difficulty of obtaining
it. Without these difficulties it loses value. Battle is valued not as victory
as such, but first as victory gained according to certain conditional rules,
and second as victory in the most difficult conditions. Defeat and death in an
attempt to accomplish an impossible task are valued more highly than victory
and the practical advantages connected with it when these are obtained through
calculation, practical cleverness, or ordinary military conditions. Effect is
valued more highly than effectiveness. The hopeless attempt by Igor
Sviatoslavich to seek the city of Tmutarakan with a small retinue inspires the
author of *The Tale of Igor's Campaign* more than the modest but quite
effective actions of the united retinue of Russian princes in 1183-1184. The
psychology of the singer of *The Song of Roland* is the same.

The sign character of behavior forces the moment of play into emphasis.
Practical result as the goal of action is replaced by correctness in using the
language of behavior. Thus in Western European knightly life the tournament
becomes equivalent to battle. In Rus, hunting assumes the function of the
tournament in the life of the feudal lord. It becomes a specific game,
concentrating the sign values of knightly military behavior. It is not by
chance that Vladimir Monomakh lists his hunts alongside military exploits as
equal objects of pride.

Behavior of the opposite type excludes conditionality. Its basic feature is
orientation toward the rejection of play and of the relativity of semiotic
means, and toward the identification of unconditionality with truth. The
unconditionality of the social meaning of behavior appears here in two ways.
For the social top, there is a tendency toward symbolism of behavior and of the
whole semiotic system. For the bottom, there is an orientation toward the zero
level of semioticity, transferring behavior into a purely practical sphere.

The difference between sign and symbol as expressions of the conditional and
the unconditional in semiotics was noted by Ferdinand de Saussure. A symbol is
characterized by the fact that it is never wholly arbitrary. It is not
completely empty; in it there is a rudiment of natural connection between
signifier and signified. The symbol of justice, the scales, cannot be replaced
by just anything, for example by a chariot.

Power, in the perspective of the symbolic consciousness of the Russian Middle
Ages, is endowed with features of holiness and truth. Its value is
unconditional. It is the image of heavenly power and embodies eternal truth.
The rituals with which it surrounds itself are likenesses of the heavenly
order. Before it, an individual person appears not as a contracting party, but
as a drop flowing into the sea. Giving himself, the person demands nothing in
return except the right to give himself.

Thus Shafirov, while in Istanbul, advised Peter I after the battle of Poltava
to carry out an armed raid to abduct Charles XII from Turkish territory. He
wrote that even if they discovered this had been done by the Russian side,
nothing else would happen except that he, Shafirov, would suffer there. Many
similar examples could be given. What matters here is that the bearer of a
conventional psychology, when faced with the need to sacrifice life, regarded
death as a kind of exchange of life for glory. "If a man is killed in war, what
wonder is that?" Danila of Galicia says to his warriors. Others die at home
without glory; these die with glory. From the opposite position, no exchange of
values can be in question. A poetry of nameless death arises. The reward is
dissolution into an absolute from which no reciprocity is expected. Dracula
does not promise his warriors glory and does not connect death with the idea of
just reward. He simply offers them death at his order without any conditions:
whoever wants to think of death, let him go with me to war.

By extending religious feeling to statehood, this type of social psychology
demanded from society something like the transfer of all semiosis to the tsar,
who became a symbolic figure, a kind of living icon. The lot of the remaining
members of society was behavior with zero semiotics. They were required to
perform purely practical activity. It is revealing that practical activity
continued to be valued very low. This allowed Ivan the Terrible to call his
collaborators "toilers," as if lowering them to the level occupied in early
feudal society only by slaves, who stood outside social semiotics altogether.

Subjects are required to perform practical service that brings real results.
Concern for the social-sign side of their life and activity is perceived as
"laziness," "cunning," or even "treason." The change in attitude toward hunting
is revealing. From an affair of honor it turns into a shameful amusement that
distracts from affairs of state. The sovereign retains the right to hunt, but
precisely as amusement. Already in the tale "On the Battle at the Piana," the
passion of careless commanders for hunting is opposed to the sovereign's
military service. Later, Ivan the Terrible writes in this same spirit to
Vasily Gryaznoi, mocking him as if he thought he had ridden out with dogs after
hares, only for the Crimeans to tie him to their saddle strap. Gryaznoi, who
had not been offended by the nickname "toiler," is offended here and writes
that he received his wounds and injuries not on a hunt but in battle, in the
sovereign's service.

The eighteenth century brought deep changes in the whole system of Russian
culture. Yet the new stage of social psychology and cultural semiotics was a
transformation of the preceding one, not a complete break from it. Most visible
on the cultural-everyday surface of life was the change in official ideology.
The state-religious model did not disappear; it underwent interesting
transformations. In axiological terms, its top and bottom changed places.
Practical activity was raised from the realm of the "low" to the very top of
the value hierarchy. The desymbolization of life, accompanied by demonstrative
trampling of the symbolism of the preceding period into the mud and exposing it
to public ridicule, raised the authority of practical action. The poetry of
craft, useful skills, and actions that are neither signs nor symbols but are
valuable in themselves formed a significant part of the pathos of Peter's
reforms and of Lomonosov's scientific activity.

Mandelstam saw this pathos as the essence of the eighteenth century. He recalls
Lomonosov's lines to Shuvalov about those who think wrongly of things and value
glass below minerals, and asks where this pathos, this high pathos of
utilitarianism, came from; where this inner warmth came from that heats poetic
reflection on the fate of manufacturing industry, in such striking opposition
to the brilliant and cold indifference of nineteenth-century scientific
thought.

In Peter's behavior it was emphasized that he, born to the scepter, stretched
out his hands for work. The ideal of the tsar-worker was repeated many times,
from Simeon Polotsky to Pushkin's "Stanzas." Yet the inverted system not only
differed from its original form, but also resembled it. Petrine statehood was
not an embodied symbol, since it itself represented final truth and, having no
instance above itself, was no one's representative or image. But like
pre-Petrine centralized statehood, it demanded faith in itself and complete
dissolution in itself. The human being entrusted himself to it. A secular
religion of statehood was created, and "practicality" ceased to be
extra-semiotic empiricism.

The specific weight of the semiotics of contract in the general structure of
the culture of the epoch also changed fundamentally. Almost completely
destroyed along with the whole cultural heritage of the early Russian Middle
Ages, it received powerful support from Western cultural influence. In the
speeches of Feofan Prokopovich and other publicists of Peter's camp, the
political conception of Pufendorf and Hugo Grotius developed, refracted in a
distinctive way through Russian tradition. The power of the tsar is thought of
as given by God and justified by reference to the apostle Paul. But at the same
time it is asserted that the tsar, having accepted power, enters into a silent
contract, obligating himself to reign for the good of his subjects. Having
ceased to be a symbol, the tsar is now as practically obligated to serve his
subjects as they are obligated to serve him. Whatever one's rank is, the
argument runs, the task demanded by that rank is the deed most necessary and
pleasing to God: the tsar must reign so that the people are secure, justice
prevails among the authorities, and the fatherland is preserved from enemies;
the senator must dwell wholly in what his calling requires; and every person
must consider what his calling demands and fulfill it in action.

The introduction of a system of state distinctions and ranks, which in the
eighteenth century competed with the principle of unconditional and innate
nobility by blood, was also based on the exchange of dignity for signs. The
equivalence of this exchange, violated in practice, was supposed in theory to
be strictly observed. The statutes of orders and the system of promotion in
rank, based on strict order by length of service, were oriented toward this.
The fact that a person passed over for an award could, according to the morals
and laws of the epoch, remind the authorities of himself and demand the award
by listing his rights to it, shows that in the consciousness of the epoch this
was not an extra-legal mercy, but a regulated exchange of obligations between
the service person and power, confirmed by rules.

The spirit of contractuality permeating the culture of the eighteenth century
forced the evaluation of traditional institutions to be rethought, or at least
rephrased. It is characteristic that although everyone knows autocracy exists
in Russia, and although recognition of this is part of official ideology, in
particular the official titulature, and of course part of state practice,
admitting this fact is considered an undesirable breach of good manners.
Catherine II argues in the *Nakaz* that Russia is a monarchy, not an autocracy:
it is governed by laws, not arbitrariness. Alexander I would repeatedly
emphasize that autocracy is a sad necessity of which he personally does not
approve. For him, as for Karamzin, it would be a fact, not an ideal.

This tendency appears especially clearly in the understanding of the rights of
the nobility. Already Kantemir, in his second satire "On the Envy and Pride of
Ill-Natured Nobles: Philaret and Eugene," treated the privileges of the noble
as an advance received for the services of the fathers, an advance that must be
paid off by personal service to the state. Under the pen of writers such as
Sumarokov, this thought became a theory of exchanging personal services for
honors received for the services of ancestors. The noble who has no personal
services is like a deceiver who takes and gives nothing in return.

Against this background the opposite process also unfolds. Simultaneously with
the tendency to rationalize sign exchange and shift the center of gravity to
its content, a countercurrent exists: the effort toward the irrational
isolation of signness as such. Conventionality, the unmotivated character of
the sign, and ritual are emphasized. Thus the rapidly developing closed noble
culture cultivates etiquette and the theatricalization of everyday life. A
semiotics of corporate honor is affirmed, and duels develop as a ritual
procedure for restoring insulted honor.

The developing culture of the dandy is built on play arising from the
conditional link between content and expression in signs. A need emerges for
dictionaries that explain the meanings of conditional forms of expression, in
particular the gallant language of love. Thus the *Love Lexicon* of Dreux du
Radier, adapted for Russian conditions by A. V. Khrapovitsky, is built on the
principle of an ordinary dictionary: word, example of phraseological use,
dictionary article. "Anxiety" in the phrase "I suffer mortal anxiety" means:
following accepted rules, I give the proper appearance to my ardor. If a beauty
says pleasantly, "You are talking nonsense," this means: although I want to
have a lover, I fear your usual indiscretion. "Torment" in "I suffer unbearable
torment" usually means: I pretend to be in love, but since you have often seen
the theater and think love cannot exist without torment, I must gather
passionate words to please you.

Similar metatexts are needed for understanding the language of beauty patches.
A velvet patch on the temple says illness, a taffeta patch on the left side of
the forehead says pride, a patch under one of the lower eyelashes says tears,
on the upper lip a kiss, on the lower lip inclination, and so on. This alphabet
key, like a diplomatic cipher, is not fixed; it is selected and changed at will
for the security of one's communications. The languages of fans develop as
well. The spread of masquerades introduces an element of relativity even into
oppositions that would seem to be given by nature: men dress as women, women as
men. Catherine II writes, for example, of a plan for a ball at the Hermitage in
which ladies would come in undress and without hoops or huge head ornaments;
four stalls with costumes and masks would be placed in the hall, with clothes
for men on one side and for ladies on the other, and the stalls would be
labeled in reverse: "clothes for ladies" on the men's side and "for gentlemen"
on the ladies' side.

It should be kept in mind that popular consciousness remains in the position of
identifying the unmotivated sign with the diabolical. The widespread
interpretation in moralistic literature that links the sign relativism of
dandy culture with godlessness and moral relativism is connected with this.

It would be mistaken to consider the dandy culture of the eighteenth century
from the same position as its critics and see in it only an ugly social
anomaly. It was precisely within it that consciousness of the autonomy of the
sign was worked out, and this became an important stimulus for the formation of
the personal culture of the Romantic era. The fact that at the beginning of
this culture in Russia stands Trediakovsky with *A Journey to the Island of
Love*, and that the curtain falls on it with Karamzin as author of *Letters of
a Russian Traveler*, compels us to see in it more than a chain of caricatures.

The intensity of social conflicts at the end of the eighteenth century caused
further shifts in the structure of the languages of culture. The connection of
the world of signs with the social structure of society discredited the sign as
such in the eyes of the Enlightenment thinker of the eighteenth century.
Following Voltaire, the Enlighteners subjected the "age-old prejudices" to
all-sided critique, which in practice meant revising the whole stock of
semiotic representations accumulated over centuries. Rousseau, exposing the lie
of the world of civilization, located its initial principle in the
conditionality of the connection between expression and content in the word.
His opposition of the word to intonation, gesture, and mimicry in effect meant
the antithesis between the unmotivated and the motivated sign. Yet while
striving to free himself from signs, Rousseau built his social ideal on the
basis of the social contract, that is, on the idea of equivalent exchange of
values among people. Such exchange is impossible if the conventionality of
signs is destroyed. Rejecting social semiotics, he wanted to preserve its
results.

At the opposite pole, Masonic ideology took shape. The Masons were opponents
of the contractual theory of society. They opposed to it the idea of entrusting
oneself to a certain absolute - the order, ideal humanity, God - and dissolving
oneself in it without recompense. Yet although they were subjectively oriented
toward the Middle Ages, they remained people of the eighteenth century. Their
emblems were not medieval symbols. They were a conditional secret language for
initiates, located on the semiotic scale closer to the language of beauty
patches than to medieval symbolism.

Both attempts to break beyond linguistic conventionality proved vain. The
eighteenth century ended with two grand masquerades: the "Roman" masquerade of
revolutionary Paris and the knightly masquerade at the court of Paul I.

The legacy of Rousseau in Russia has an interesting fate for the relation
between contractual and non-contractual principles in Russian culture. It is
well known that the influence of his ideas there was exceptionally deep and
long-lasting, much longer-lasting than in France. Yet the brilliant paradoxical
quality of the ideas of the "citizen of Geneva" allowed them to be interpreted
extremely broadly, in accordance with the inner dynamics of Russian culture.

In the eighteenth century, for the Russian reader, Rousseau was the author of
the discourse on whether the revival of the sciences and arts contributed to
the purification of morals, of *Julie, or the New Heloise*, of *Emile*, and
most of all of *The Social Contract*. The influence of the latter was enormous.
Ideas of the contractual origin of society lay at the basis of all political
thought in the last third of the eighteenth century. Radishchev, a Helvetian
when he speaks of other matters, immediately becomes a Rousseauist when he
turns to questions of sociology and law. *The Social Contract* is also cited
by a person in every way opposite to Radishchev, the aristocrat and rationalist
Prince M. Shcherbatov. Polemicizing with Catherine II's *Nakaz*, he translates
Rousseau's argument that rulers were originally chosen by peoples for the
establishment of their welfare, and that in concluding a contract with these
rulers the people could not surrender their natural freedom, since without it
their welfare cannot be achieved. If such a careless people were found, they
would have to be considered insane, and no contract made by the insane has
force.

It is revealing that Shcherbatov was so convinced that contract is the only
form of justification for civil society that he completely missed what was
important for Rousseau: the opposition between the idea of exchanging rights
between person and society and the idea, absurd in Rousseau's eyes, of
"gratuitously giving oneself."

For the generation of the Decembrists, Rousseau was still connected with the
idea of the social contract. By mid-century, however, an interesting
transformation had occurred. Leo Tolstoy, whose views were enormously shaped by
Rousseau and who in his youth wore Rousseau's portrait on his chest beside the
cross, recalled in old age that he knew almost all of the Genevan thinker's
writings, including the musical works, by heart. Yet the ideas of *The Social
Contract* left no noticeable trace in Tolstoy's consciousness. For him,
Rousseau was the protester against civilization and the author of pedagogical
ideas of inequality, the enemy of lies in all their manifestations, the
merciless author of the *Confessions*. On the other side, Dostoevsky, the
passionate denouncer of Rousseau who attacks him with a fervor revealing
jealous interest, also invariably has the *Confessions* in mind. The idea of
contract is deeply alien to the very structure of Dostoevsky's thinking, for
whom "gratuitous self-giving" would seem not madness but the norm of religious
behavior.

It is interesting that the generation of "nihilists" of the 1860s and 1870s,
who proclaimed materialism and atheism, having renounced gratuitous
self-giving to Truth coming from God, immediately found another addressee to
whom they could selflessly entrust themselves. This addressee turned out to be
the deified People. It is not by chance that Maxim Gorky called the literature
of the Populists "holy scripture about the peasant."

On the other hand, in popular legal consciousness in Russia, contract and
exchange were closely connected with deception, since one of the contracting
sides was imagined as the devil or one of his substitutes: the lord, the
"German," the oath toward whom obligates no one. It is not by chance that the
folkloric role of the merchant is that of swindler. One could also point to

Foreigners visiting Russia tended to accuse Russian merchants of unfaithfulness
and cunning. Paradoxically, however, the cause lay in the attitude toward
contract as such. A case of deception in relation to the "alien" - and contract
was thought of as a form of relation precisely with the alien - was treated as
a kind of implied condition. Deception here was akin to the folkloric cunning
of the trickster hero.

The popular attitude toward relations within one's own milieu was completely
different. Here deception was considered a grave sin, but no contract was
needed; it was replaced by trust. In this respect the memoirs of the serf
peasant N. Shipov from the first half of the nineteenth century are remarkable.
They tell the curious life story of a serf-millionaire who paid his landlord,
Saltykov, more than five thousand rubles in assignats a year in quitrent.
Shipov was a person of tireless energy and varied gifts. His story introduces
us to the world of serfs richer than their landlord, conducting extensive
trade, setting up factories. But their property was the property of people
deprived of the right of property, and it lacked any legal guarantee.
Therefore all of their very significant monetary operations were based on
trust, not on legal securities. Since the landlord could take everything away
at any moment - "Who knows? Anything can happen to a serf slave," Shipov
remarks melancholically - all turnover, sometimes reaching thousands of
rubles, was conducted on the basis of personal trust and in concealment. Of
course, episodes of deception and violation of trust occurred here too, but
they were strictly condemned as immoral.

Thus we can observe how a certain typological opposition, while varying
according to the conditions of environment and epoch, nevertheless preserves an
invariant basis. As a result, in order to understand the real content of a
historical-semiotic category, in this case the concept of contract, one must
illuminate it crosswise from both typological and historical points of view.

Very recently, the opposition proposed here between contract and
self-entrustment as applied to the culture of ancient Rus was called into
question by the well-known scholar of the Russian Middle Ages Ya. S. Lurie. He
writes that if the observation has any foundation, it applies only to
Vladimir-Suzdal Rus from the second half of the thirteenth century. For the
state structure of one of ancient Rus's largest states, the Novgorod land,
already from the twelfth century, what is characteristic is precisely the
contractuality of political order: the ritually sealed contract between the
veche and city administration on one side and the princes invited to Novgorod
on the other.

I consider this objection the best confirmation of my thought. Of course a
trading republic, a member of the Hanseatic League, where even the feudal elite
was an urban trading aristocracy, related to contract differently from the rest
of Rus, and precisely from the Vladimir-Suzdal Rus out of which the Muscovite
tsardom grew. Of course, we are speaking only of typological tendencies, which,
using Hegel's expression, always "realize themselves through
non-realization," and indicate a tendency, not one hundred percent of the
facts. No society can stand without elements of contractuality. But the issue
is something else: how does this society evaluate one or another category, and
what place does it assign that category in the hierarchy of its values?
