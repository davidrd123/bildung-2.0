# Preface To The English Edition

Umberto Eco

The range of Yuri Mikhailovich Lotman's scholarly work is extraordinarily
broad. His interests include aesthetics, poetics, the theory of semiotics, the
history of culture, mythology, and film theory - all this in addition to his
main discipline, the history of Russian literature. Lotman held the position
of professor of Russian literature at Tartu University in Estonia. He is drawn
to any theme. From the analysis of cultural phenomena such as blue jeans, or
from remarks on demonology, he easily passes to the philological reading of
literary texts, and from discussion of problems of interpretation to unexpected
parallels from mathematics or biology.

Even readers not familiar with the content of all Lotman's works will be able
to appreciate the breadth of the theoretical approach underlying this book. It
will nevertheless be useful to pause over several aspects of Lotman's ideas.
This will help the reader become more closely acquainted with the themes and
methods of research represented here.

In the 1960s, the words "semiotics," or "semiology," and "structuralism"
exploded the calm of the European academic world. Paris became the center of
the new scholarly paradigm, but gradually this phenomenon spread across Europe
and entered the universities of both Americas. The stunning effect produced in
Great Britain by new approaches to the study of natural language, and as a
result to the study of the languages of art, is described in David Lodge's
novel *Small World*. This is one more proof that a work of art often contains
far more information about the world and society than mountains of scholarly
dissertations.

*Small World* appeared in 1984, when L. M. O'Toole and Ann Shukman had already
for several years been publishing the series *Russian Poetics in Translation*,
Russian and Soviet works on semiotics in English. Nevertheless, in the 1960s
and 1970s Lotman's works were better known on the Continent than in Great
Britain. Interest in the structural study of language, supported by the
influence of Roman Jakobson, drew attention to the works of the Prague School.
At the same time, Russian Formalism of the 1920s was rediscovered. Until then
the Formalists had been known mainly through Victor Erlich's fundamental book
*Russian Formalism*. In 1965 Tzvetan Todorov translated a number of Russian
Formalist texts into French, and then the most important works of Shklovsky,
Tomashevsky, and Tynianov began to appear in translations into European
languages, mainly Italian.

Having become interested in Russian Formalism, Italian and French scholars in
the first half of the 1960s also began to discover the research in semiotics
being published at that time in Russia, mainly in Moscow and Tartu: works by
Ivanov, Revzin, Uspensky, Zholkovsky, Shcheglov, Segal, Toporov, Egorov, and
also two representatives of the older generation, the linguist Shaumyan and the
mathematician Kolmogorov. But the leading role in the development of the new
field, a role both foundational and, thanks to the Tartu *Semiotics* volumes,
coordinating, belonged to Yuri Mikhailovich Lotman.

The Formalists taught that a work of art is a semiotic device that can be
described as a set of rules and devices, specified effects and intentional
deformations of social codes. Representatives of Russian-Estonian semiotics
borrowed from the Formalists such concepts as "device" and "estrangement" and
took a step forward by presenting the generation of the artistic text as a
process of individual recreation of a set of procedures that organize the
social texture of communication.

Trying to explain the "mystery of creativity" in terms of analyzable device,
the Formalists encountered a whole series of contradictions. For example, they
could not renounce the understanding of the image as an inexpressible event.
Moreover, after the works of Levi-Strauss and Propp it became entirely clear
that the Formalists never made the transition from formal analysis of the text
to its structural description. They did not draw all the conclusions from the
fact that the shaping of a work of art inevitably entails the organization of
its content. As a result, the Formalists studied sign systems while ignoring
the semantic systems discovered by the young Russian semiologists, such as
religion and the picture of the world. Finally, the theories of novelistic
structure proposed by Tomashevsky and Shklovsky clearly show that the formal
method could claim only to describe individual devices or systems of rules
operating within one concrete genre.

From this point of view, the neo-semioticians went much further. Information
theory, game theory, structural analysis in linguistics, and cultural
anthropology allowed them to postulate a universal domain of communicative
phenomena. It is impossible to define a system of rules corresponding to a
given communicative phenomenon without also postulating relations of structural
homology with the system of rules applicable to any other communicative
phenomenon. The young Russian semiologists developed a universal semiotic
theory, and a corresponding method, according to which the laws governing each
communicative sector were considered variants of codes of higher levels.

To understand the interest aroused by the works of scholars who until then had
been unknown in the West, except to some specialists in Russian studies, one
must remember that the words "semiotics" and "structuralism" began to designate
a whole complex of interconnected phenomena. The task of semiotics is to study
the entire diversity of sign systems, the most important of which is verbal
language, and all possible communicative processes generated by these systems.
Research of this kind must demonstrate that sign systems exist even when the
very fact of their existence is not obvious and not predictable.

Structuralism is a method extremely useful for explaining linguistic systems,
as the works of Saussure, Hjelmslev, and Jakobson show. In the 1960s, first of
all in France, structuralism began to be used to explain other systems as well,
including the system of cultural phenomena. The role of Levi-Strauss's works in
this respect is exceptional. Yet not all semiologists used the method of
structural analysis. Charles Sanders Peirce and Charles Morris proposed a
semiotic theory that cannot be called structuralist. This must be emphasized
especially because, it seems to me, Lotman, who began with a structuralist
approach to phenomena of signification and communication, and who still does
not renounce the structuralist method, is nevertheless not limited by that
method. This book confirms it.

Take one example. The theoretical problem that created the greatest difficulty
for structuralists of the 1960s was the fact that some sign systems change in
the course of communicative processes, that is, in historical processes
unfolding in time. Another problem: if a semiotic system is considered as a
code, or rather as a system of rules, then where can communicative processes
whose code is hard to define come from, or processes in which different codes
conflict with one another? The key to understanding the evolution of Lotman's
ideas lies in the solution of these problems.

In the 1960s, Lotman spoke about the usefulness of the structural approach and
the application of exact methods in literary studies. On the one hand, he
remained faithful to Saussure's opposition between language and speech. On the
other, he remained faithful to the opposition between code and message proposed
by Jakobson and information theory. In 1967 Lotman wrote an article, "On Exact
Methods in Russian Literary Studies," for the Italian journal *Strumenti
Critici*. In this article he repeated theoretical positions he had expressed
earlier and set out the foundations of his research method. They can be
summarized as follows.

First, the opposition between the exact sciences and the humanities must be
eliminated. Second, a purely historical study of literature inevitably reduces
itself to the study of the history of social thought. Third, the Russian
Formalists of the 1920s began the study of literary "technique," and now the
time had come to introduce into the arsenal of literary studies the methods of
linguistic structuralism, semiotics, Peircean semiotics included,
information theory, cybernetics, and mathematical-statistical analysis.

Fourth, semiotic systems are models that explain the world in which we live. It
is clear that in explaining the world they simultaneously construct it. From
the beginning, Lotman represented semiotics as a cognitive science. Among such
systems, language stands out as the primary modeling system: we perceive the
world using the model offered by language itself. Myth, cultural norms,
religion, the language of art, and the language of science are secondary
modeling systems.

Fifth, if texts are models of the world, then the totality of texts that make
up the culture of a particular period is a secondary modeling system. We must
therefore develop a typology of cultures. This is necessary both in order to
reveal universal features inherent in all cultures and in order to identify
concrete systems, such as the "language" of medieval or Renaissance culture.

Sixth, if culture is analyzed as a code or system, it may turn out, as in the
analysis of natural language, that real processes are more varied and less
predictable than the semiotic model that explains them. Not all cultural
phenomena can be explained by reconstructing the cultural code alone. But such
reconstruction makes it possible to explain why a given culture produced these
phenomena.

Lotman, however, understood that to consider a text as a message built on the
basis of a linguistic code is not at all the same thing as considering the text
itself, or culture as a set of texts, as a code. The researcher was always
aware that historical periods possessing one single cultural code simply do not
exist, although constructing a code-model can be a useful abstraction, and that
there are many different codes in any culture. It seems to me that in solving
this problem Lotman goes beyond structuralist dogmatics, proposing an approach
more complex and at the same time clearer. Faced with the rigidity of the
structuralist opposition between code and message, Lotman distinguished the
study of grammar from the study of the text. This distinction can be made even
within one culture.

A culture is governed either by a system of rules or by a repertoire of texts
that impose particular models of behavior. In the first case, combinations of
discrete units generate texts that are considered correct or incorrect
depending on whether they satisfy combinatory rules. In the second case,
society itself produces texts that form macro-units from which rules are
eventually derived. But first of all, texts offer models that can be followed
and imitated.

A grammar-oriented culture depends on "handbooks"; a text-oriented culture
depends on "the Book." A handbook is a code that allows new messages and texts
to be created. A book is a text built according to rules that are still
unknown, but which, once analyzed and reduced to the form of a handbook, can
offer us new ways of creating texts.

Lotman reminds the reader of two coexisting traditions of language learning.
When adults are taught a foreign language, they are usually taught rules. In
order to begin speaking the language, they master a set of linguistic units,
assimilate the laws of their combinability, and learn to connect them. By
contrast, a child learns to speak by being in constant contact with correctly
constructed texts. It is assumed that the child will acquire linguistic
competence even without fully recognizing the laws that govern the language.

The difference between the grammatical and textual ways of learning language
led the author of this book to look at cultural texts in a new way and to
describe culture as a totality of texts and as nonhereditary collective memory.

Proceeding from this, Lotman carried out subtle analyses of a whole series of
cultures. Interesting examples of such research are included in the present
book. A vivid example is the chapter "The Concept of Boundary." Lotman managed
to combine the structural method, which requires a synchronic approach, that
is, the description of a cultural system at a given moment in time, with
professional historicism. A historian is interested first of all in the
formation of a culture and in the possibility of comparing cultural systems
separated from one another in time.

Even when different cultures seem to use the same elements, these elements
enter as components into different systems. Lotman gives us an excellent
example of cultural typology by comparing the cultural language of the Middle
Ages with the cultural language of the Enlightenment. In the first case we have
a culture in which everything, not only words but also objects, designates a
higher reality, and in which objects are significant not from the point of view
of their physical nature or function, but only insofar as they designate
something else. In the second case we have a cultural system for which the
world of objects is real, while words and signs become purely conventional
constructs and means of lying. Here no one except the "noble savage," who is
not familiar with cultural constructs, is capable of seeing reality.

Another example is the analysis of the concepts "honor" and "glory" in
medieval Rus. For us the words honor and glory are more or less synonymous,
whereas in Russian culture of the Middle Ages the word honor means recognition
and approval granted by higher persons to lower ones, while glory can relate
only to people who stand on the highest levels of the social hierarchy.

The method of analysis in the works on cultural typology can be called
structural, since the concepts honor and glory are opposed here in just the
same way two phonemes are opposed in a phonological system, acquiring meaning
through mutual distinction. Yet in the course of his research Lotman understood
that a cultural code is much more complex than a linguistic one. In his later
works he shows increasing attention to the richness and diversity of historical
experience.

Already in the 1960s Lotman clearly understood that the multiplicity of codes
in a given culture gives rise to contrasts and hybrids, or, in other words,
"creolization." In the studies of his last decade he developed the concept of
the semiosphere, by analogy with the concept of the biosphere.

The book the reader holds in hand sets out in detail what Lotman has in mind
when speaking of the semiosphere. Nevertheless, I would like to pause over the
main definition. Imagine a museum hall in which exhibits from different epochs
are displayed in separate cases: inscriptions in known and unknown languages,
instructions for decipherment, explanatory texts for the exhibition prepared
by methodologists, route maps for excursions, and rules of visitor behavior.
Add guides and visitors to this hall, and imagine all of it as a single
mechanism, which in a certain respect it is. This gives an image of the
semiosphere. At the same time, one should not forget that all elements of the
semiosphere exist not in a static but in a mobile, dynamic relation,
constantly changing their formulas of relation to one another. This is
especially noticeable in traditional elements inherited from earlier states of
culture.

Thirty years earlier, Lotman had already considered the text as a unity. The
concept of structural unity lies at the foundation of his *Lectures on
Structural Poetics* of 1964, republished in 1970 under the title *The Structure
of the Artistic Text*. In his final works, the concept of unity is extended to
the whole semiosphere. In the article "On the Semiosphere," published in
*Sign Systems Studies* in 1984, Lotman argues that the entire semiosphere, that
is, culture as semiosphere, must be considered as a single mechanism. Only in
this way can we understand culture in all its diversity.
