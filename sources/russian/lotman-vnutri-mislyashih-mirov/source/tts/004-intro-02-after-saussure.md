# After Saussure

In recent decades, semiotics and structuralism, both in the Soviet Union and
in the West, have gone through a period of testing. These tests, to be sure,
had different characters. In the Soviet Union they had to live through a period
of persecution and ideological accusation, which was followed in official
science by a conspiracy of silence or an embarrassed half-recognition. In the
West these scholarly directions were tested by fashion. Enthusiasm for them
was wide and went beyond the limits of science.

Yet neither persecution nor fashion, so important in the eyes of outside
public opinion, has determining influence on the fate of scientific ideas. Here
the decisive word belongs to the depth of the concepts themselves. The depth
and significance of scientific ideas are determined, first, by their capacity
to explain and unite facts that had previously remained scattered and
unexplained, that is, by their capacity to combine with other scientific
concepts. Second, they are determined by their capacity to reveal problems that
need solution, especially where the preceding view had considered everything
already clear. This second feature means compatibility with future scientific
concepts. Therefore those ideas have a long scientific life that are capable,
while preserving their initial positions, of undergoing dynamic transformation
and evolving together with the world around them.

Speaking of semiotics now, at the end of the twentieth century, one should
keep in mind three different aspects of it. In the first, it appears as a
scientific discipline predicted already by Saussure. This is a field of
knowledge whose object is the sphere of sign communication. Saussure wrote that
one can imagine a science studying the life of signs within the life of
society; such a science would be part of social psychology and therefore of
general psychology, and he would call it semiology.

The consideration of language as one of the semiotic systems, Saussure argued,
would become the foundation of all the social sciences. It would not only shed
light on the problems of linguistics. When rites, customs, and so on are
considered as signs, these phenomena too will appear in a new light, and the
need will arise to unite them all within semiology and explain them by the laws
of this science.

In the second aspect, semiotics appears before us as a method of the humanities
that penetrates various disciplines and is defined not by the nature of the
object but by the way it is analyzed. From this point of view, one and the same
scientific object permits both semiotic and non-semiotic approaches. The same
linguistics can provide enough examples.

Finally, the third aspect is best defined as the distinctive scientific
psychology of the researcher, the cast of the researcher's knowing
consciousness. Just as a film director becomes used to looking at the
surrounding world through fingers folded into the shape of a frame, "cutting"
separate pieces out of an integral landscape, the semiotic researcher
habitually transforms the surrounding world by illuminating semiotic structures
within it. Everything King Midas touched with his golden hand turned to gold.
In a similar way, everything that attracts the attention of the semiotic
researcher is semioticized in that researcher's hands. This is connected with
the effect of the describer on the described object, which will be discussed
later.

These three aspects in their totality make up the field of semiotics.

Looking retrospectively at the path traveled by semiotics from the moment when,
largely through the efforts of Roman Jakobson on the one hand and through the
general direction of thought on the other, it attracted wide attention in the
second half of the 1950s, one can define its leading tendencies with the words
continuation and overcoming. This applies to the legacy of Russian Formalism,
and to the work of Bakhtin or Propp. But it applies most of all to the legacy
of Saussure, whose works, even after Jakobson's criticism, which opposed to the
Swiss scholar the ideas of Charles Peirce, remain powerful blocks in the
foundation of semiotics.

Among Saussure's ideas, for the aspect that interests us, it makes sense to
single out the following:

1. The opposition between language and speech, or respectively between code and
   text.

2. The opposition between synchrony and diachrony.

Both oppositions had the most fundamental character for Saussure. Language, he
argued, is a grammatical system virtually existing in each brain, or more
exactly in a whole collectivity of individuals, because language does not exist
fully in any one of them; it exists fully only in the collective. By separating
language and speech, we separate the social from the individual, the essential
from the secondary and more or less accidental.

Proceeding from these premises, Saussure formulated the dominant position of
language both in the act of speech and in linguistic science. Language is
something quite definite within the heterogeneous mass of facts of speech
activity. It represents the social aspect of speech activity, external in
relation to the individual, who by himself can neither create it nor change it.
Language exists only by virtue of a kind of contract concluded among the
members of the collective.

Language, unlike speech, is an object accessible to independent study. We do
not speak dead languages, but we can perfectly well master their mechanism. As
for the remaining elements of speech activity, the science of language can get
along without them. More than that, it is possible only on the condition that
these other elements are not mixed into its object.

The second opposition that we singled out had an equally fundamental character.
It was precisely synchrony that was assigned structural character, and synchrony
turned out to be the bearer of the relational connections that constitute the
essence of language. Synchrony is homeostatic, while diachrony is a list of its
external and accidental disruptions. In reacting to them, synchrony restores
its integrity. "Language is a system whose parts can and must all be considered
in their synchronic interdependence." Changes never occur in the whole system
at once, but only in one or another of its elements, and they can be studied
only outside the system. In the diachronic perspective, Saussure says, we deal
with phenomena that have no relation to systems, although they condition them.
Language stands opposed to everything accidental, fluid, and extra-systemic:
"Language is a mechanism that continues to function despite the damage done to
it."

These ideas cannot be separated from the whole building of contemporary
semiotics. To reject them would be to pull the cornerstones from its
foundation. But it is precisely through their example that we can see what deep
transformations both the basic positions and the whole shape of the building of
semiotics underwent in the second half of the twentieth century.
