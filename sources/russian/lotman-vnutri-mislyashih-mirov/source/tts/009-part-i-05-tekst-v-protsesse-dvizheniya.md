# Text In The Process Of Movement: Author and Audience, Design and Text

The relation between text and audience is marked by mutual activity: the text
tries to assimilate the audience to itself, to impose its system of codes on
the audience, and the audience answers it in kind. The text includes, as it
were, an image of "its own" ideal audience, while the audience includes an
image of "its own" text. There is an anecdote from the biography of the
well-known mathematician P. L. Chebyshev. A lecture devoted to the mathematical
problem of cutting fabric attracted an unintended public: tailors, designers,
fashionable ladies, and so on. But the lecturer's very first phrase, "Let us
suppose, for simplicity, that the human body has the form of a sphere," sent
them fleeing. Only the mathematicians remained in the hall, finding nothing
surprising in such an opening. The text had "selected" its audience, creating
it in its own image and likeness.

Communication with an interlocutor is possible only when there is some memory
held in common with that interlocutor. But in this respect there are fundamental
differences between a text addressed "to everyone," that is, to any addressee
whatever, and one that has in view a concrete person personally known to the
speaker. In the first case, the memory of the addressee is constructed as what
is obligatory for anyone who speaks the given language and belongs to the given
culture. It is deprived of the individual and is abstract; it includes only a
certain irreducible minimum. Naturally, the poorer the memory, the more
detailed and expanded the message must be, and the less admissible ellipses,
silences, a rhetoric of hints, and complicated pragmatic-referential relations
become. Such a text constructs an abstract interlocutor, the bearer only of
general memory, deprived of personal and individual experience. It is addressed
to everyone and each person.

A text addressed to an addressee personally known to us, to a person designated
for us not by a pronoun but by a proper name, is built differently. We know the
volume of this person's memory, and the character of its filling, in an intimate
and close way. In this case there is no need to clutter the text with
unnecessary details; references to the addressee's memory are enough. A hint is
a means of actualizing memory. Elliptical constructions, local semantics, and a
tendency toward the formation of a "domestic," "intimate" vocabulary will
develop strongly. The text will be valued not only by the measure of its
intelligibility for this addressee, but also by the degree to which it is
unintelligible to others. Thus an orientation toward one or another type of the
addressee's memory forces one to resort now to a "language for others," now to
a "language for oneself" - one of the two opposed structural potentials hidden
in natural language.

Having command of a certain set of linguistic and cultural codes, we can,
through analysis of a given text, determine what type of audience it is oriented
toward. This will be determined by the character of the memory required for
understanding it. Reconstructing the type of "common memory" for the text and
its recipients, we discover the "image of the audience" hidden in the text. It
follows that the text contains within itself a folded system of all the links
of the communicative chain. Just as we extract the author's position from it,
we can also reconstruct, on its basis, the ideal reader of the text. This image
acts actively on the real audience, restructuring it in its own likeness. The
personality of the recipient of the text, while representing a semiotic unity,
is inevitably variable and capable of "tuning itself to the text." For its part,
the image of the audience, since it is not explicit but only contained in the
text as a certain flickering position, is open to variation. As a result, a
complex play of positions takes place between text and audience.

In the most general terms, one can say that the antithesis between a memory
shared by all members of a society and a maximally individualized memory can be
correlated with the opposition between official and intimate speech, which in
modern culture also finds a parallel in the opposition between written and oral
speech. This last subdivision, however, has many traditions: it is obvious that
printed writing and manuscript writing acquire quite different pragmatics, just
as oral oratorical speech and whispering do. The end point here will be inner
speech. Mandelstam's line, "I will say it in rough draft - in a whisper,"
remarkably brings together the un-faired, unfinished draft - a notation "for
oneself" - and the whisper.

This picture acquires particular complexity in the artistic text, where the
image of the audience and the pragmatic aspects connected with it are not
automatically conditioned by the type of text, but become elements of free
artistic play and therefore acquire additional significance. When a love poem,
an intimate poem, or a friendly poem, by title and sense seemingly addressed to
one single person, is published in a book or journal and therefore changes its
audience, now addressing any reader, it turns from a personal message - a fact
of everyday life - into a fact of art. A change in audience brings with it a
change in the volume of common memory possessed by the text and its addressees.
In an artistic text containing a concrete biographical address, a double address
is created: on the one hand, it imitates orientation toward some single
addressee, which requires intimacy; on the other, the text is addressed to any
reader, which requires an expanded volume of memory.

In the artistic text, orientation toward some type of common memory ceases to
follow automatically from the communicative function and becomes a significant,
that is, a free, artistic element capable of entering into playful relations
with the text.

This can be illustrated by several examples from Russian poetry of the 18th and
early 20th centuries.

In the hierarchy of poetic genres of the 18th century, the determining idea was
that the more valuable the poetry, the more abstract the addressee to whom it
is related. The person to whom the poem is addressed is constructed in the text
as the bearer of maximally abstract values and memories: cultural, state, or
national. Further, if the matter concerns a fully real addressee personally
known to the poet, prestige requires that the poet address this person as a
"stranger," selecting the qualities that are "known to everyone" and contained
in some abstract memory. Thus, for example, the poet Vasily Maykov, addressing
verses to the grandee Count Chernyshev, lists facts of Chernyshev's biography
which, unquestionably, were known to Chernyshev himself. But the poem constructs
a "high" image of Chernyshev as an abstract "statesman" who shares common
memory with all the personages of this row. Thus the real facts of the life of
Chernyshev as a concrete personality are not contained in the memory of
Chernyshev the hero of the poetic text, nor equally in the memory of readers
who appear as the realization of national-state memory. In a poem addressed to
Chernyshev, the poet explains who Chernyshev is:

O you, hero tested by events,
Whom the Russian host has seen as leader
And knows how great your soul is
When you acted against Frederick!
Then, when that monarch became our ally,
He himself tested your courage and reason.

A. Voeikov builds the opening of his epistle to his wife in exactly the same
way. To remove the official address here and not list the qualities and
circumstances of the addressee's life would mean to translate the epistle from
a solemn genre into an intimate one; that is, it would imply the presence
between sender and addressee of a certain common memory belonging only to them.

One could compare this with the ceremonial portrait of the 18th century: even
if the patrons were the person depicted or that person's family, and even if the
portrait was intended for a family interior, it remained obligatory to depict
the sitter in ceremonial uniform, with all orders and regalia. The viewer was
constructed as a "stranger." By contrast, when at the end of the century Count
Sheremetev's serf artist N. I. Argunov painted a brilliant portrait of the
count's beloved, later his wife, the serf Parasha Zhemchugova-Kovaleva, in a
home dress and, with unheard-of boldness, pregnant, the viewer here had to
identify with one single person: the lover-husband himself, Count Sheremetev.

Pushkin often used the capacity of a text to offer the audience a conditional
position of intimate closeness to the sender of the text. In doing so, the poet
consciously omitted, as already known, or was content to hint at circumstances
that could not have been familiar to the reader of the printed text. Hinting at
facts known in advance only to a small circle of friends, Pushkin invited
readers, as it were, to feel themselves intimate friends of the author and to
enter into the play of hints and omissions. Thus, for example, the fragment
"Women" - under this title a fragment from the first version of the fourth
chapter of *Eugene Onegin* was published in *Moskovsky vestnik* in 1821 -
contains the lines:

With the words of the prophetic poet
It is permitted me too to say:
Temira, Daphne, and Lileta -
Like a dream, I have forgotten them long ago.

A reader of our time, wishing to learn whom one should understand by the
"prophetic poet," turns to the commentary and establishes that Delvig is at
issue, and that the implied lines are from his poem "Fanny":

Temira, Daphne, and Lileta
Long ago, like a dream, were forgotten by me,
And only my successful verse
Preserves them for the poet's memory.

But it should not be forgotten that this poem was published only in 1922. In
1821 it had not been printed and remained unknown to contemporaries, if we mean
the basic mass of readers to whom Pushkin addressed his 1821 publication, since
Delvig treated his early poems with exceptional strictness, printed very
selectively, and did not circulate rejected ones in manuscript copies.

Thus Pushkin referred readers to a text that was known in advance not to be
known to them. What sense did this have? Among the potential readers of *Eugene
Onegin* there was a small group for whom the hint was transparent: Pushkin's
Lyceum friends. Delvig's poem had been written back in the 1810s, probably in
the Lyceum, and perhaps for a close circle of friends from the post-Lyceum
period.

Thus Pushkin's text, first, split the audience into two groups: an extremely
small one to which the text was understandable thanks to acquaintance with
extra-textual details of circumstances lived through together with the author,
and the main mass of readers, who felt a hint here but could not decipher it.
But the understanding that the text requires a position of close acquaintance
with the poet forced readers to imagine themselves in precisely that relation
to these verses. As a result, the secondary action of the undeciphered hint was
that it transferred each reader into the position of an intimate friend of the
author, someone who shared with him a special, unique community of memory and
therefore could converse in hints. In the same way, a person who has entered a
close circle of intimate people where everyone speaks through hints at
circumstances unknown to him at first feels alienation and sharply experiences
his exclusion from the collective; but later, seeing that he has been accepted
into it as an equal, he replaces the absence of direct experience with indirect
experience and lives through, with particular intensity, the trust shown to him,
his inclusion in the intimate circle. Pushkin includes the reader in such a
game.

The opposite game is also possible: close acquaintances are conditionally moved
into the position of "strangers." Such, for example, is the naming of children
by full official-ritual names. In a story by L. N. Tolstoy, the father addresses
his newborn son: "Ivan Sergeich!" he says, touching the little child under the
chin with his finger. The mother who tells the story joins the game: "But I
quickly covered Ivan Sergeich again." In Russian linguistic etiquette, naming a
person with an indication of the person's father - the patronymic, here
"Sergeevich" from "Sergei" - is possible only as a sign of respect and is
applied only to adults. Such an address to a child is a sign of semi-ironic
play.

In the real speech act, the use by one or another participant of the means of
official or intimate languages is determined by the participant's extralinguistic
relation to the partner. The artistic text allows the audience to move along
the scale of this hierarchy in accordance with the author's design. For the
time of reading, it turns the reader into a person of whatever degree of
closeness the author chooses to indicate. At the same time, the reader does not
cease to be a person who occupies a definite real relation to the text, and the
play between the reader's real pragmatics and the pragmatics assigned by the
author of the artistic text creates the specific experience of the work of art.
On the one hand, the author arbitrarily changes the volume of the reader's
memory and can force the audience to remember what was not known to it. On the
other hand, the reader cannot forget the real content of his memory. Thus the
text simultaneously forms its audience, and the audience forms its text.

We have already paused over the dichotomy between orientations toward the
maximally exact transmission of a message and toward the creation of a new
message in the process of transmission. Each of these orientations forms its
own idea of the degree of the addressee's activity.

The ideal of adequacy could be represented by such a model: a chain of
biochemical impulses regulating physiological processes inside one organism. In
this case the recipient is the final link in the chain of transforming
impulses. In a well-arranged chain, this will be a passive reading device,
valuable for its "transparency," for the fact that it contributes nothing "from
itself" to the information.

The potential possibility of distortion, errors, and with them meaningful
transformation of information and the emergence of new messages arises at the
moment when impulses are replaced by signs with their fundamental asymmetry of
content and expression. But the need for signs arises when the circulation of
information inside an organism is replaced by communication between organisms.
At some stage, directly impulsive connections still play a significant role in
communication between individuals as well - parapsychological phenomena may be
connected with this - but as the units in contact become more complex, such
connections are more and more displaced by signs.

From what has been said one can conclude that, to the extent that a collective
can be regarded as a single organism, one can speak of a smaller role for the
activity of the recipient of messages. The recipient will be an executor or
keeper of information to a much greater degree than its creator. From this
follows a paradoxical conclusion: mythological rituals and other actions that
merge archaic collectives at certain moments as if into a single organism and
give members of these collectives unity of emotions and a heightened sense of
participation - the experience of oneself as part - are functionally similar to
the metalinguistic and metacultural structures of individualistic society. Both
play the role of hoops on a barrel, turning a conglomerate into a single
organism. In this respect the spread of ecstatic dances and the music that
stimulates them in the 20th century is an entirely regular phenomenon. The
culture of the late 20th century sharpened the contradiction between a person's
experience of himself as a part and as a whole, separate being. On the one
hand, a person experiences the pressure of social relations, which deprives him
of the possibility of manifesting individuality and reduces him to the level of
a recipient of orders. On the other, the high individualization and
specialization of the whole structure of life suppresses all impulsive and
direct connections, makes all communication difficult, including sign
communication, and creates the effect of alienation and destroyed
communicativity. Spontaneous explosions of passions, ecstatic experiences of
art, and destructive revolts appear in these cases as therapeutic means. On the
one hand, knocking a person out of the routine clip of everyday life and
including him in maximally simplified situations, they allow him to experience
the illusion of release and individual arbitrariness. On the other, they
sharpen directly intuitive and impulsive connections with other people. Opposite
psychological states are experienced simultaneously: "I do what I want" and "I
am the same as everyone," "you flow as a drop with the masses" (Mayakovsky).

The complication of the semiotic structure of the recipient of the text and
the recipient's transformation into a personality are conditions for replacing
the simple transmission of a message with a creative process. But the degree to
which creative activity is distributed among the different elements of the
communication chain can vary substantially. The polar cases here are the
concentration of activity in the author-text link with, correspondingly, its
lowering at the level of the recipient, and the maximal activation of the
creative possibilities of the addressee while these functions are weakened in
the other links of the chain.

Let us consider two cases:

1. Some already fixed text is transmitted along the communicative chain, for
example, an actor reads a poem or a reader reads a book.

2. The text does not exist before the act of communication and arises in the
process of transmission.

In the first case, the first stage of the movement of the text is its
actualization. A text that had been in a potential state - the book stood on a
shelf, the play had not been staged, and so on - acquires reality in the
consciousness of the addresser. Here, at the boundary between the collective
memory of culture and individual consciousness, the first semiotic
transformation of the text takes place. An artistic text is a text "with a set
toward expression" (R. O. Jakobson). Consequently, the actualization of the
text is always connected with the highlighting of its structure "to the ear,"
whether this means actual reading aloud or inner speech. One can compare this
with the musician's habit of "reading" a score and hearing it with the inner
ear. Reflecting on Peirce's works, R. O. Jakobson emphasized that a certain
degree of iconicity is inherent in all linguistic signs to one degree or
another. It is especially condensed in poetic language. One could add that the
word in oral speech is much more iconic than in written speech. Besides the
psychological delay of attention on sound, it receives gesture - intonational,
kinetic, mimetic - and becomes visible. Thus, for example, the declamation,
even silent declamation, of Derzhavin's lines,

The death of the righteous husband is beautiful!
Like an organ falling silent...

acts as a developer, revealing the structural organization of the text. The
almost inevitable reading of the word "falling-silent-organ" with the voice
lowered at the end of the word "falling-silent" and somewhat weakly raised on
the final word, accompanied almost inevitably by a downward movement of the
hand, creates an iconic sound image of fading organ sound and the echo's
resonance, as well as subjective visual associations. The text doubles, as it
were: it remains rows of graphically expressed words and, at the same time, is
realized in a certain iconic space. Its meaning also doubles, oscillating
between these semantic spheres. But linguistic and iconic signs are located in
spaces that are mutually not completely translatable. Consequently, there
arises here that incomplete determination of correspondences which creates
conditions for the increment of meaning. Already at this stage there has been
an increase of information.

Thus, at the boundary where the text enters the semiotic space of the
transmitting personality, it acquires, as it were, an additional semantic
dimension. But its further immersion in this space is connected with further
transformations. The structure of codes forming the semiotic personality of the
author of the text and of its first interpreter is not identical in advance. A
certain correspondence is necessary for primary elementary understanding of the
text, at minimum the understanding of what language it is written in. But the
diversity of traditions, contexts, coincidences and noncoincidences at various
levels of the hierarchy of the coding structure creates not an unambiguous
translation from "your" language into "mine," but a spectrum of
interpretations, always open to possible new readings.

When we say that one of the mechanisms of recoding is cultural tradition, it
should be kept in mind that "tradition" as a code differs from
"contemporaneity." The "contemporaneity" that codes, or interprets, a text is
as a rule realized in the form of a language, that is, of norms, rules,
prohibitions, expectations, and prescriptions according to which texts not yet
created must be created, or texts "incorrectly" interpreted from the position
of "contemporaneity" must be interpreted. "Contemporaneity" is turned toward
the future. "Tradition" always appears as a system of texts preserved in the
memory of a given culture, subculture, or personality. It is always realized as
a certain particular case, considered as precedent, norm, rule. Therefore
"tradition" is open to broader interpretations than "contemporaneity." A text
passed through the code of tradition is a text passed through certain other
texts that perform the role of interpreter. But since an artistic text cannot,
in principle, be interpreted unambiguously, here one multiplicity of
interpretations is passed through another multiplicity, which leads to a new
leap in possible interpretations and a new increment of meanings. At the same
time, the texts that enter into "tradition" are not dead in their turn: when
they enter the context of "contemporaneity," they come alive, revealing
previously hidden semantic potentials. Thus before us is a living picture of
organic interaction, dialogue, in the course of which each participant
transforms the other and is transformed under the other's influence. It is not
passive transmission, but a living generator of new messages.

An analogous process takes place at another semiotic boundary, when the
transmitted text comes into contact with the addressee. The generation of new
meanings is the dominant aspect of the work performed by the artistic text in
the system of culture.

The second case is marked by its own distinctive traits: the text is not
transformed but created in the process of transmission. Since the reader, in a
certain respect - only in a certain respect, as will be said further on -
repeats in mirror fashion the path of the creator of the text, the generative
aspect has importance for understanding the text. Meanwhile, little attention
has so far been given to the question of generating the artistic text. As far
as I know, A. K. Zholkovsky and Yu. K. Scheglov have dealt with it most
thoroughly, and in this connection it is necessary to pause over their work.

Beginning in 1967, when the authors published a declaration for the construction
of generative poetics, and into the early 1980s, they published more than six
dozen works illustrating, on extensive and varied material, the model of
generating the artistic text they had developed. Their books *Mathematics and
Art (A Poetics of Expressiveness)*, published in Moscow in 1976, and *Poetics
of Expressiveness: A Collection of Articles*, published in Vienna in 1980, give
the most summarized idea of the conception as a whole.

The conception of Zholkovsky and Scheglov still seems not to have been
appraised according to its merits, although the very attempt to build an
integral system for the generation of the artistic text and to illustrate it
broadly deserves attention. This is not the place to analyze the strong and
weak sides of the Zholkovsky-Scheglov model, but we will inevitably have to
touch on some of its aspects.

The basic traits of the model "Theme, Devices of Expressiveness, and Text" are as
follows. The content invariant of the various levels and components of a
literary text is called its theme. The text is the expressive embodiment of the
theme, and its structure has the form of a derivation of the text from the
theme on the basis of typical transformations, or devices of expressiveness.
Themes are "inexpressive"; they are meant to fix pure content. Devices are
"contentless"; they increase expressiveness without changing content.

Elsewhere, the correspondence between theme and text is represented as a
derivation of the text from the theme, performed on the basis of universal
transformations: devices of expressiveness. A separate text is regarded as a
special turn of an invariant theme, its passage through new material. At the
same time, the theme is derived intuitively by the researcher through the
extraction, from a definite and preferably maximal number of texts, of some
invariant of sense. Then the "theme" revealed in this way is worked over by
"devices of expressiveness," as a result of which an artistic "text" is
obtained. Between theme and text there are relations of symmetry: if the author
generates the text from the theme, then the reader reads the theme out of the
text.

The difference lies only in the fact that, when moving from theme to text, a
non-artistic type of utterance turns into an artistic one. In fact, a
non-artistic text turns into an artistic one, since even a theme taken in the
most abstract form, if expressed in words, invariably represents a text:
expressions such as "excellent calm," "window," or "confession," at the level
of natural language, are unquestionably texts. In fact we encounter here theses
that lay at the foundation of the rhetorics of earlier years, when the artistic
text was treated as "adorned," formed from a given theme with the help of
rhetorical figures.

The authors' basic assumptions raise doubts:

1. That an artistic text is obtained from a non-artistic one by "adornment,"
that is, in the terminology of the model of the "poetics of expressiveness," by
"working it over" with means of artistic expressiveness.

2. That the artistic text, translated into the language of "means of
expressiveness," "increases expressiveness without changing content," and that
therefore art is a way of speaking at length about what could have been said
briefly.

3. The authors warn at once that the derivation they are discussing is in the
sense of competence, not in the sense of performance: that is, it is a way of
fixing the logic of correspondences between the text and the theme available to
it, but in no case a reconstruction of the history of the creation of the text
from its initial design. On this basis, they do not try to verify their
constructions against cases of a really documented creative process of
transition from design to final text, although such documentation is broadly
represented, for example, in the manuscripts of Pushkin, Dostoevsky, early
Pasternak, and others.

Despite this warning, let us begin precisely with the question of real artistic
text-generation. The conclusion that the logically first link of the creative
process is "theme" in Zholkovsky and Scheglov's understanding is not proved by
anything; it is simply made by analogy with the model for generating a
non-artistic text, "sense - text." Meanwhile, there is enough evidence in favor
of the view that the first link of this chain is, as a rule, a symbol. Even
when artists speak of some sound or even smell as the "seed of unfolding" of a
future text, further examination shows that what is at issue is the symbolic
expressor of some individual semiotics: for example, an associative
symbolization of childhood, of a decisive episode in one's emotional biography,
and so on. That is, the artistic function, at least potentially, is present in
the design from the beginning.

Let us refer, for example, to Dostoevsky's quite definite testimony.
Dostoevsky, with striking insistence, defined the creation of the initial theme
of a novel as the most artistically significant part of the work, calling it
"the poet's business." Development, by contrast, he understood as "the artist's
business," putting into this expression the presence of professional mastery.
In the word "artist" Dostoevsky clearly includes an archaic semantics:
craftsman, master. Compare a note in the drafts of *The Adolescent*:

To write a novel, one must first of all stock up on one or several impressions
that have truly been lived through by the author's heart. In this lies the
poet's business. From this impression develop theme, plan, an ordered whole.
Here the business is already that of the artist, although artist and poet help
one another in both things and in both cases.

Dostoevsky returned to this thought many times. He even calls this primary
"theme" of the novel a poem, emphasizing its poetic nature. On May 15/27, 1869,
he wrote to A. N. Maykov that, in his opinion, the poem appears in the poet's
soul like a native precious stone, a diamond, entirely ready in all its essence;
this is the poet's first work as maker and creator, the first part of his
creation. If one likes, the poet is not even the creator, but life itself, the
powerful essence of life. Then comes the poet's second work, no longer so deep
and mysterious, but only the work of an artist: having received the diamond, to
cut it and set it. In another letter he wrote: "Being more a poet than an
artist, I forever took on themes beyond my strength." Pushkin had the same
thing in view when he wrote that even the plan of Dante's *Inferno* was already
the fruit of high genius.

Thus one may consider that there is authoritative evidence in favor of the
view that the chain generating the artistic text begins, not only
psychologically but also logically, not with a logically expressed "theme" still
lying outside art, but with a capacious symbol that gives room for varied
interpretations and already has an artistic nature.

Another substantial objection is connected with the idea of the symmetry of the
model of the "poetics of expressiveness." We have already had sufficient
occasion to state the conviction that the generation of new meanings is always
connected with asymmetrical structures. If the storage of information is most
reliably ensured by symmetrical structures, then generation is connected with
mechanisms of asymmetry. The discovery of asymmetrical binarity in a semiotic
object always forces us to suppose some form of intellectual activity. We
cannot imagine the generation of an artistic text as the automatic unfolding of
an unambiguously given algorithm. The creative process should be assigned to
irreversible processes, and this means that the transition from stage to stage
inevitably includes elements of chance and unpredictability. Consequently, if
we use Zholkovsky and Scheglov's terminology, then by "folding" the text in the
reverse direction we will not obtain the initial theme, just as by unfolding
the theme twice we will obtain one and the same text with no greater
probability than that with which, after scattering typographical letters on the
floor, we would obtain *War and Peace*.

One need not even mention that different artistic structures - for Zholkovsky
and Scheglov, different expressive devices - cannot express one and the same
content, and that the thesis that "devices are contentless" seems more than
debatable.

The consideration of the real creative process in those cases where writers'
manuscripts give sufficient documentation for it seems to be weighty
confirmation of these considerations.

The study of the logical aspect of the creative process cannot repeat the
capricious paths by which one or another concrete work was created, but neither
can it ignore the typical stages through which the generation of real texts
passes in those cases where we can trace this process with sufficient detail.
Moreover, we believe that the real process can serve as a criterion for testing
the truth of our logical models, while logical models can serve as means of
interpreting textual reality.

The general regularity that can be drawn from the study of creative manuscripts
of a number of writers lies in the sequential replacement of stages. Design is
replaced by narrative. Here the set toward symbolism, polysemy, and the
multidimensionality of the text's semantics yields to a striving for exactness
in the expression of thought. At the boundaries of these stages, relations of
asymmetry and untranslatability arise, and this entails the generation of new
meanings.

We have already said that the first link in the generation of the text can be
considered the emergence of the initial symbol, whose capaciousness is
proportional to the breadth of the plots potentially hidden in it. It is no
accident that the determination of such seemingly marginal moments as title and
epigraphs can serve as a signal that the "theme," in Zholkovsky and Scheglov's
terminology, has been defined. Thus, for example, the need to fulfill many
obligations at once, the richness of creative imagination, and the inherited
connection among different designs all led to the fact that in Dostoevsky's
manuscripts it is practically impossible at times to determine to which of the
simultaneously developing plots one or another manuscript text belongs. The
beginning of work on *Demons* is surrounded by a whole cloud of parallel
designs that later would be partly absorbed by *Demons*: *Kartuzov*, *The Life
of a Great Sinner*, the novel about the Prince and the Moneylender. Some of
them, for example *Envy*, should apparently be directly connected with work on
*Demons*. The "theme" of the novel was apparently defined at the moment when
impressions from the Nechaev trial, polemic with Turgenev, reflection on the
problem "people of the 1840s - nihilists," and many other life and literary
impressions concentrated in the symbolism of the Pushkin epigraph from the
ballad "Demons" and were embodied in the Gospel image of possession by demons.
This symbol illuminated, as it were, the already existing plot preparations and
forecast further plot movement. Separate sketches and preparations lined up
into a plot. Thus the symbolic concentration of different things in one was
replaced by the linear unfolding of one thing into different episodes.

If we continue observing the history of the creation of *Demons*, this
transition is expressed in the creation of plans: a summary enumeration of
episodes strung along the syntagmatic axis of narration. But as soon as a
tendency toward exposition, toward narrative construction, is outlined, we
witness the growing internal resistance to this tendency. Every serious plot
move in Dostoevsky immediately becomes overgrown with variants and with other
developments of the same move. The wealth of fantasy that allowed Dostoevsky to
"play through" an enormous number of possible plot moves is truly astonishing.
The text in fact loses linearity. It turns into a paradigmatic set of possible
variants of development. And so it is at almost every turn of the plot. A
syntagmatic construction is replaced by a certain multidimensional space of
plot possibilities.

At the same time, the text fits less and less into verbal expression. It is
enough to look at a page of Dostoevsky's manuscript to be convinced how far the
writer's work at this stage is from the creation of a "normal" narrative text.
Phrases are thrown onto the pages without observance of temporal sequence in
the filling of lines or sheets. Most often there is no certainty that two lines
located next to one another were written sequentially. Words are written in
different scripts and sizes, in different directions. The page resembles a wall
on which a prisoner in a cell, at different times, has made feverish notes,
internally connected for him but lacking connection for an external observer.
Many notes are not texts but mnemonic abbreviations of texts preserved in the
author's consciousness. Thus at this stage the manuscript pages in Dostoevsky
tend to turn into signs of an enormous multidimensional whole living in the
writer's consciousness, and not into a sequential exposition of a linearly
organized text. In addition, these notes are of different planes: variants of
plot episodes, exhortations to himself, general theoretical reflections of a
philosophical character, and separate words-symbols that still have to unfold
into future episodes not yet created by the author's imagination. Resorting to
varied means of emphasis - underlining, writing in larger letters or in print
script - Dostoevsky consciously fixes intonation at this stage of work, as if
emphasizing that the graphic form is not the text, but only its projection.

Then the next stage arrives: the extraction of linear elements from this
continuum and the construction of the narrative text. Multidimensionality is
replaced by linearity. The preceding stage abounds in multivalent symbols that
give room for the most varied concretizations in the narrative fabric of the
future novel. Thus, for example, in the preparatory materials for *Demons* the
"slap in the face" is mentioned repeatedly: for Dostoevsky it is a complex
multidimensional symbol. Already in *Kartuzov*, an early sketch of *Demons*,
this word is placed in the title and emphasized typographically. Later, in the
preparatory materials for *Demons*, and then for *The Adolescent*, who gave the
slap to whom and under what circumstances changes, but the slap itself, as a
symbol of extreme humiliation, remains. The symbol defines a bundle of possible
plot moves, but it does not predetermine which of them will be chosen. In the
same way, the little red spider at which the hero looks while his victim is
hanging herself, a spider that appeared in Stavrogin's "Confession" and did not
enter the final text of the novel, will later flicker in the preparatory
materials for *The Adolescent* as a conventional designation for a whole set of
situations multiplied by the author's fantasy.

This is how the relation of preparatory materials to the later narrative text
takes shape. It resembles the relation of a ball of wool to the thread unwound
from it: the ball exists spatially in a certain single time, while the thread
is wound out of it in temporal movement, linearly. The scheme of movement in a
text created by Dostoevsky can be represented as a passage from symbolic,
iconic, nondiscrete, spatial modes - the initial symbol, preparatory materials,
and paradigm - toward verbal, narrative, discrete, temporal modes: plan, text,
and syntagm.

Thus the "generation" of a text is connected with repeated semiotic
transformation. At the boundary between different semiotic regimes, in crossing
the zero line, an act of translation takes place and a not fully predictable
reformulation of meanings occurs. It should be emphasized that this is a
logical model, not a description of the real creative process, since in the
continuous flow of Dostoevsky's novel designs it is often practically
impossible to isolate moments of "initial symbol." In the same way,
"preparatory materials" continuously include pieces of narrative texts, while
the latter, in drafts corrected and reworked many times, tend to turn into
preparatory materials, so that the division has a rather conditional-logical
than factual character. Even the boundaries separating one novel from another
are often erased in Dostoevsky. B. V. Tomashevsky wrote that "Dostoevsky writes
novel after novel in search of some single novel," and the latest researchers
of the writer's manuscripts have good grounds for observing that it seems more
expedient to speak of a certain single draft arranged in the order of the main
stages of creativity.

Iconic texts - nondiscrete, spatial ones - and verbal texts - discrete, linear
ones - are mutually untranslatable; they cannot in principle express "one and
the same" content. Therefore, at the points where they are juxtaposed,
uncertainty increases, and this is the reserve for the increase of information.
Thus, in the process of creating a text, the writer simultaneously creates,
from an enormous number of potentially given materials - tradition,
associations, his own previous work, the texts of surrounding life, and so on -
a certain channel through which he passes new texts arising in his creative
imagination, conducting them across thresholds of transformation and increasing
their semantic load through unexpected combinations, translations, linkages,
and so forth. When, as a result of this, a structurally organized dynamic whole
takes shape, we speak of the appearance of the text of the work.

The reader repeats this process in the reverse direction, ascending from text
to design. But it should be kept in mind that the very reading in the reverse
direction is already inevitably a creative act. A meaning-generating structure
is always asymmetrical, and this is especially noticeable when considering such
apparently symmetrical texts as the palindrome. Recall the analysis of a
Chinese palindrome by the well-known sinologist Academician V. M. Alekseev.
Having indicated that a Chinese character taken in isolation gives an idea only
of a semantic nest, while its concrete semantic and grammatical characteristics
are disclosed only in correlation with a textual chain, and that without taking
account of the order of word-signs one cannot determine either their
grammatical categories or the real semantic filling that concretizes the very
general abstract semantics of the isolated character, Alekseev shows the
striking grammatical and semantic shifts that occur in the Chinese palindrome
depending on the direction in which it is read. In a Chinese palindrome, that
is, in the reverse order of the words of a normal poem, all the Chinese
syllable-words, while remaining punctually in their places, are called upon to
play different roles, both syntactic and semantic.

From this Alekseev drew an interesting methodological conclusion: the
palindrome is priceless material for studying the grammar of the Chinese
language. The conclusions are clear. First, the palindrome is the best possible
means of illustrating the interdependence of Chinese syllable-words without
resorting to artificial, unartistic, crude classroom experiments in rearranging
and combining them for exercises in Chinese syntax. Second, the palindrome is
the best Chinese material for constructing a theory of the Chinese word and the
simple sentence, and perhaps not only of Chinese. Thus reverse reading leads to
the decomposition of signs that are indivisible in the normal situation into
elements that bear grammatical and semantic meanings.

Russian palindromes demonstrate a different effect.

The poet S. Kirsanov, in a short note, offers exceptionally interesting
self-observations on the psychology of the author of Russian palindromes. He
tells how, while still a schoolboy, he involuntarily said to himself, "The seal
is not fierce" - in Russian, a palindrome - and suddenly noticed that this
phrase could be read in reverse order. From that time on, he often caught
himself reading words backward. With time he began to see words "as wholes,"
and such self-rhyming words and combinations of words arose involuntarily.

Thus the mechanism of the Russian palindrome consists in seeing the word, even
if only with the mind's eye. This then allows it to be read in reverse order.
Consequently, in the Chinese language, reverse reading turns an unarticulated
word-character, a sign with strong traits of iconicity, into an articulated
sequence of morphogrammatical elements and reveals a hidden structure. In
Russian, by contrast, the palindrome requires the ability to "see the word as a
whole," that is, to perceive it as an integral drawing. The eye does not move
linearly from letter to letter, but embraces the word as a whole outside time;
more extended palindrome phrases composed of several words must likewise be
embraced as wholes. Thus reverse reading changes the semiotic nature of the
text into its opposite.

One may suppose that, if on the one hand reading in the reverse direction
activates mechanisms of the functional asymmetry of the brain's hemispheres, on
the other hand, at the higher levels of culture, it is connected with the
opposition between the overt and the hidden, the profane and the sacred or
esoteric. The use of palindromes in incantations, magical formulas,
inscriptions on gates and graves is indicative: these are boundary and
magically active places of cultural space, places where earthly or "normal"
forces collide with infernal or "reverse" forces. At the same time, these same
boundaries are places of intensified semiotic activity.

It is relevant to recall that Bishop and poet Sidonius Apollinaris attributed
the authorship of a known Latin palindrome to the devil himself:

Signa te, signa, temere me tangis et angis.
Roma tibi subito motibus ibit amor.

Cross yourself, cross yourself; not knowing it, you touch and oppress me by
this. Rome, by these signs and gestures, you suddenly call love to yourself.

The relation writer-reader can, in a certain sense, be likened to the two
directions of reading a palindrome. First of all, their relation to the text is
asymmetrical. From the writer's point of view, the text is never finished: the
writer is always inclined to work it over and finish it further. He knows that
any detail of the text is only one possible realization of a potential
paradigm. Everything can be changed. For the reader, the text is a cast
structure in which everything is in its own, uniquely possible place;
everything bears meaning and nothing can be changed. The author perceives the
final text as the last draft, while the reader perceives the draft as a
finished text. The reader hyperstructures the text and is inclined to reduce
the role of chance in its structure to a minimum.

But this is not the only point. The reader brings into the text his own
personality, his own cultural memory, codes, and associations. These are never
identical with the author's.

Between text and reader, or audience, two opposite types of relation inevitably
take shape: the situation of understanding and the situation of
misunderstanding. Understanding is achieved by the unity of the coding systems
of author and audience; in the most elementary case, by the unity of natural
language and cultural tradition. But the concept of cultural tradition can be
treated in the narrowest and in the broadest sense. At the ultimate level, the
presence of certain universals for all earthly human civilizations makes any
text of human culture in principle translatable to some degree into the
language of another culture, that is, to some degree understandable. These
universals include, for example, the semiotics of "up" and "down," "right" and
"left," the isomorphism of body and world, the dualism of living and dead, and
so on. The number of basic elements out of which a picture of the world is
built is relatively small and has a universal character. Differences arise at
the level of combinations.

However, a certain degree of understanding is at the same time a degree of
misunderstanding. One may point to the following example. The textual
composition of different cultures inevitably includes a certain set of genres,
since the fact that a text belongs to a definite genre known to the reader, by
force of the "memory of genre" (M. M. Bakhtin), creates a significant economy
of code. If by genres we understand the most general groupings of texts, such
as sacred/profane, official-state/individual-everyday, scientific texts that
tend toward expression in metalanguages, artistic texts that tend toward
expression in the languages of the arts, and so on, then we obtain a relatively
uniform set. From this point of view one can write, for example, a history of
science or religion, of the novel or the folktale. But in the perspective of
value we obtain rows of an entirely different composition. Every culture
inevitably includes a dichotomy of texts of high and low value. Its extreme
manifestation will be the opposition between what saves and what destroys.
Salvation may be expected from religion, which will be opposed in one case to
science, in another to art, and in a third to profane statehood. The "savior"
may turn out to be science or art, again in a variety of oppositions. And what
seemed destructive to the author may seem to the reader included in the
"saving." Having defined a certain archaeological find as a toy, the recipient
of information still does not know whether to treat it with contempt or
delight. The question will be decided by the relation between the evaluation of
the world of the child in the transmitting and receiving cultures.

Text and reader seem to seek mutual understanding. They "fit themselves" to one
another. The text behaves like an interlocutor in a dialogue: it restructures
itself, within the limits left to it by its reserve of internal structural
indeterminacy, according to the model of the audience. The addressee answers in
the same way: he uses his informational flexibility for a restructuring that
brings him closer to the world of the text. At this pole, relations of
tolerance arise between text and addressee.

One must not lose sight, however, of the fact that not only understanding but
also misunderstanding is a necessary and useful condition of communication. An
absolutely understandable text is at the same time an absolutely useless text.
An absolutely understandable and understanding interlocutor would be convenient
but unnecessary, since this person would be a mechanical copy of my "I," and my
information would not increase from communication with him, just as the amount
of cash does not increase when a wallet is moved from one pocket to another. It
is no accident that the situation of dialogue does not erase the individual
specificity of the participants, but fixes it and makes it significant.
