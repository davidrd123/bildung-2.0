# Symbolic Spaces

## 1. On The Concept Of Geographical Space In Russian Medieval Texts

The concept of geographical space belongs to one of the forms by which the
world is spatially constructed in human consciousness. Having arisen in
definite historical conditions, it receives different contours depending on the
character of the general models of the world of which it is a part. The
present brief excursus does not aim to characterize the medieval feeling of
geographical space fully. We seek only to indicate several traits that
distinguish it from the modern one.

In the medieval system of thinking, the very category of earthly life is
evaluative: it is opposed to heavenly life. Therefore earth as a geographical
concept is simultaneously perceived as the place of earthly life, entering
into the opposition "earth/heaven," and consequently receives a
religious-moral meaning not characteristic of modern geographical concepts. The
same ideas are transferred to geographical concepts in general: certain lands
are perceived as righteous or sinful lands. Movement in geographical space
becomes movement along a vertical scale of religious and moral values, whose
upper step is in heaven and whose lower step is in hell.

At the same time one should recall that in Russian medieval consciousness, the
opposition "earth/heaven" or "earthly life/afterlife" did not imply that the
second member of the opposition lacked a spatial feature. The thought that
earthly life is opposed to heavenly life as space is opposed to non-space was
characteristic of mystical currents of the Middle Ages, but it was decisively
rejected by more "realistically" thinking Orthodox Christianity. Archbishop
Vasilii of Novgorod wrote in condemnation to Bishop Feodor of Tver, who had
asserted the non-spatial, purely ideal existence of the afterworld. Vasilii
insisted that paradise could not be treated as merely mental or visionary.

More than that, because the earthly world is "perishable" and transient, while
the afterworld is imperishable and eternal, its "materiality" is much more
"real": the holy objects that fill its space are not subject to spoilage,
decay, or destruction. They are not non-material, but eternally material.

Thus earthly life is opposed to heavenly life as temporary life is opposed to
eternal life, but not in the sense of spatial extension. More than that, the
concepts of moral value and local position appear fused together: moral
concepts have a local feature, and local concepts have a moral feature.
Geography appears as a variety of ethical knowledge.

Every movement in geographical space becomes marked in religious and moral
terms. It is not accidental that in medieval literature a human being's
penetration into hell or paradise is always thought of as a journey, a movement
in geographical space. This determines both the composition of the *Divine
Comedy* and the structure of *The Journey of the Mother of God Through the
Torments*, where the guide, the archangel Michael, asks the Mother of God
where she wishes to go: to the south or to the north, to the east or west, to
the right or to the left, to paradise or to the place of great torments.

These conceptions appeared most distinctly in the well-known *Epistle of
Archbishop Vasilii of Novgorod to Bishop Feodor of Tver*. There we find the
assertion that paradise is in the east, in Eden. Four rivers flow from
paradise: the Tigris, the Nile, the Euphrates, and the Phison. Hell is placed
in the west, on the "breathing sea," the Arctic Ocean, and the archbishop
appeals to many Novgorod witnesses of this. Paradise too can be visited as a
result of geographical movement; this had happened, he says, to Novgorod
seafarers, who after long wandering were carried by the wind to high mountains
where they heard much rejoicing and the voices of glad singing.

In accordance with these conceptions, medieval people also understood
geographical travel as movement across a "map" of religious and moral systems:
some countries were thought of as heretical, pagan, or holy. Social ideals, and
all the social systems that the consciousness of that time could imagine, were
thought of as realized in some geographically located point. Geography and
geographical literature were utopian in essence, and every journey acquired
the character of pilgrimage.

This special approach to geography, which was not yet perceived as a distinct
natural-scientific discipline but rather resembled a form of religious-utopian
classification, is highly characteristic of the Middle Ages. Connected with it
is a special relation to the traveler and to travel: a long journey increases a
person's holiness. At the same time, striving toward holiness implies the need
to renounce settled life and set out on the road. A break with sin was thought
of as departure, as spatial movement. Thus entering a monastery was movement
from a sinful place to a holy place, and in this sense was likened both to
pilgrimage and to death, which was also thought of as a spatial-geographical
movement.

It is indicative that for mystics who affirmed the "mental" character of
paradise, for example the Trans-Volga elders, the need for wandering, for
movement in geographical space, falls away. Prayer turned inward, ecstatic
expectation of the "Tabor light," is no longer linked with movement in space.
In Masonic literature of the 18th century, the geographical field of meanings
was completely replaced by a moral one, and the plot of movement in
geographical space was perceived as an allegory of moral rebirth. The question
of the relation between the motif of travel and the ethical formation of
personality in 18th-century literature lies beyond the bounds of this
excursus. Another path by which this connection was destroyed was the birth of
a new, natural-scientific approach to geography.

In this sense it is interesting to compare *The Tale of the Indian Kingdom*
and Afanasii Nikitin's *Journey Beyond Three Seas*. India appears before us in
these two texts in completely different form. In the first case, it is a
utopian country antithetically connected with the Russian land in a single
system of social, moral, and religious relations. Moreover, the beautiful
utopian India is not a country in which only social relations are arranged in a
special way, more happily than in Rus'. Medieval Russian utopia implies the
existence of a special geography, a special climate, and a different animal and
plant world. Movement in geographical space brings the traveler to another
degree of blessedness. And an unusual degree of blessedness implies an unusual
geography.

John, the "king and priest" of the Indian Kingdom, says of his land that there
are people half bird and half human, people with dogs' heads, and beasts such
as elephants, dromedaries, crocodiles, and monstrous animals. There is grass
from which every beast flees; there are no thieves, robbers, or envious people,
because the land is full of every richness. There are no vipers, frogs, or
snakes, and if one enters, it dies. In connection with this there arises a
stable conviction in medieval literature that every degree of blessedness
corresponds to its own climate. Paradise is a place with an especially
grace-filled climate adapted to human life in the earthly sense, while hell is
its opposite in this respect. In paradise the soil is blessed; everything grows
of itself and in abundance. In hell the climate is impossible for life: ice and
fire.

The India of Afanasii Nikitin is something altogether different from the India
of John the "king and priest." It is a country of distinctive climate and
customs, but it has no special place on the ladder of blessedness and sin. In
this sense one cannot say that it embodies in geographical space some special
degree of grace, while the Russian land occupies some other degree in the same
system. Here these connections simply do not exist. It is all the more
remarkable that at the same time the medieval concept of space is being
destroyed and replaced by an idea of geographical extension in the spirit of
the modern age. Afanasii Nikitin's experience of geographical space is closer
to the Renaissance than to the Middle Ages.

In speaking of the medieval concept of geographical space, one must also pause
over the idea of chosenness, which flowed organically from the division of
lands into righteous and sinful. Generated by the growth of the tendency to
close oneself off, characteristic of medieval society at some of its stages,
this idea also left its imprint on the conception of space. The opposition
"one's own/alien" is perceived as a variant of the oppositions
"righteous/sinful" and "good/bad." This system no longer allows one's own land
to be opposed to the blessed utopia of an alien country: everything not one's
own is thought of as sinful.

This feeling was vividly embodied by A. N. Ostrovsky in Feklusha's words in
*The Storm*. She says, in effect, that there are lands where there are no
Orthodox tsars, where sultans rule and judge all people unjustly, because such
is the limit appointed to them. "With us," she says, "the law is righteous;
with them it is unrighteous." And there is also a land where all people have
dogs' heads, because of unbelief.

It is interesting that in *The Tale of the Indian Kingdom*, people half dog
and half human live precisely in a righteous land, that is, an alien and
wondrous one.

The combination of medieval spatial-geographical conceptions with the idea of
the chosenness of one's own land is reflected in a distinctive way in the works
of the archpriest Avvakum. For him alien lands are "sinful" lands. But because
Orthodoxy had fallen in Rus' as well, one's own land in the
spatial-geographical sense becomes "abroad." If someone wants to be crowned
with a martyr's crown, Avvakum says, there is no need to go to Persia: Babylon
is at home. The use of a geographical term, "Babylon," as a synonym for a
concept that in our conception is not geographical at all reveals the
distinctiveness of the medieval understanding of locality.

One can give a scheme from which it becomes clear that for the medieval
consciousness of ancient Rus', a change of moral status meant movement in
space, a transition from one local situation to another. The fusion of the
geographical, or local, element with the ethical element led to a series of
interesting consequences. First, the motive cause of travel is often not one's
own desire, but the necessity of reward for virtue or punishment for vice.

The journey of the righteous person moves from the parental or one's own home,
through the monastery, toward holy lands and paradise. The journey of the
sinner moves from the same starting space, through the house of sin, toward
unclean lands and hell.

In the Prologue Life of Saint Agapius, a voice commands Agapius to leave the
monastery and learn what has been prepared for those who love God. The
fratricide Sviatopolk, by contrast, is unable to remain in one place and runs
through the Polish land, driven by God's wrath, until he reaches the desert.
The outcome of the journey, the point of arrival, is determined not by
geographical circumstances in our sense and not by the intentions of the
traveler, but by that person's moral worth. The distinctive character of this
journey is emphasized not only by the stable comparison of its first stage, the
monk, and its last, the dead person, but also by the idea that bodily, still in
life, visits to paradise or hell are entirely possible. More than that, from
the idea that a person's local position in space should correspond to moral
status there inevitably followed a situation popular in medieval literature:
the righteous person taken alive into paradise, or the sinner sent alive into
hell.

The apocryphal life of Saint Agapius is very indicative in this respect. Here
the righteous person completes the entire cycle of the journey. First he leaves
home, possessions, and wife, enters the monastery, and becomes a monk. Then,
obedient to the voice, he leaves the monastery and sets out on the road. The
journey ends in a meeting with a saint who leads him into paradise. Paradise in
this case is characterized precisely not by mental existence but by eternal
materiality. Saint Elijah gives Agapius a piece of the bread that he himself
eats. This bread is entirely similar to earthly bread, since it too is intended
for nourishment and can be eaten by human beings. It differs from earthly bread
only by its special durability: an insignificant piece is enough to feed many
people for a long time.

The scheme also allows several further observations. The left cell, from which
the transition is made, is unified; the right cell is doubled depending on what
kind of journey is set, the journey of the righteous person or of the sinner.
But this "unity" of the left cell is conditional. The parental, or one's own,
home is a place of abundant and "cool" life if the transition from it is to be
made to the monastery. But it can also be given the traits of a place of harsh
obedience if what we have before us is the journey of a sinner striving for
"liberation" from moral obligations.

The medieval conception of space entered into contradiction with certain ideas
proper to Orthodox Christianity. Thus the antithesis between earthly and
afterworldly life prescribed sorrow on earth and rejoicing after death for the
righteous person. But the conception of paradise and hell as included within
geographical space replaced this sharp opposition with a gradual scale on which
righteousness and joy increase together. The intrusion of "local" ethics
deformed certain fundamental ideas of Christianity. In the link "home -
monastery," the geographical factor is still little felt, and the usual scale
of Christian ethical evaluations operates: sorrow enters with a positive sign,
and joy with a negative one. Therefore the norm of monastic behavior will be a
"narrow life," and the place chosen for the monastery is the wilderness.
Descriptions of fertile soil, abundance of fruit, and a good climate do not
enter the stereotype of desert dwelling.

But already at the second link, as the role of the spatial-geographical factor
increases, things change. "Holy lands" possess a favorable climate, and
accordingly rejoicing in these regions constitutes the norm of life, not its
violation. By contrast, sinful lands are sorrowful, but life in them does not
increase a person's merit. The most distant point, paradise, is opposed to
ordinary countries precisely by the sign of rejoicing, joy, and suitability
for life in the earthly sense.

Taking into account the special significance of geographical remoteness, one
can explain why a local sign of distance necessarily entered medieval utopia. A
beautiful land is a land to which the road is long. Gogol, who possessed an
acute historical intuition, understood medieval concepts of geographical space.
In the story *A Terrible Vengeance*, saturated with folk fantasy, he introduced
an episode in which a terrible sinner, a sorcerer, fleeing punishment, decides
to escape from Kiev southward to Crimea. He drives his horse south, but his
sins mysteriously carry him west. He rides for one day, then another, but
Kaniv still does not appear. Church tops glitter in the distance, but this is
not Kaniv: it is Shumsk. Amazed that he has ridden in a completely different
direction, the sorcerer drives the horse back toward Kiev. After a day a city
appears, but it is not Kiev. It is Galich, even farther from Kiev than Shumsk
and already near the Hungarians. Not knowing what to do, he turns the horse
back again, but feels once more that he is riding in the opposite direction and
still forward.

The scientific thinking of the modern age changed the experience of
geographical space. Yet the asymmetry of geographical space and its close
connection with the general picture of the world mean that even in modern
consciousness it remains an area of semiotic modeling. It is enough to point
to the ease of metaphorization that produces concepts such as West and East,
to the semiotic meaning of renamings of geographical points, and so on.
Geography is exceptionally easily transformed into symbolism. This is
especially noticeable when one or another geographical point becomes the site
of stubborn military action, or of national or religious conflicts, or is
evaluated differently in colliding national traditions.

The history of geographical maps is the notebook of historical semiotics.
