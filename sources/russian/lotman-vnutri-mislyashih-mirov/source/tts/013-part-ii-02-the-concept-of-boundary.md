# The Concept Of Boundary

The inner space of the semiosphere is, paradoxically, at the same time uneven
and asymmetrical, and also unified and homogeneous. Although it consists of
conflicting structures, it also possesses individuality. The self-description
of this space implies the first-person pronoun. One of the basic mechanisms of
semiotic individuality is the boundary. This boundary can be defined as the
line at which a periodic form comes to an end. This space is defined as "ours,"
"one's own," "cultural," "safe," "harmoniously organized," and so on. Opposed
to it is "their-space," "alien," "hostile," "dangerous," "chaotic."

Every culture begins with the division of the world into an inner, "one's own,"
space and an outer, "their," space. How this binary division is interpreted
depends on the typology of the culture. Yet the division itself belongs to the
universals. The boundary may separate the living from the dead, sedentary
peoples from nomads, city from steppe; it may have a state, social, national,
confessional, or some other character. It is striking how civilizations
unconnected with one another find coinciding expressions for characterizing the
world lying on the other side of the boundary. Thus the Kievan monk-chronicler
of the 12th century described the life of other East Slavic tribes, still
pagan, as beastlike: they killed one another, ate unclean things, had no
marriage but carried girls off by the water, lived in the forest like wild
beasts, and gathered between villages for games, dancing, and demonic songs.
And here is how, in the 8th century, a Frankish Christian chronicler described
the customs of the pagan Saxons: fierce by nature, devoted to demonic worship,
enemies of our religion, they respect neither human nor divine rules and
consider what is forbidden to be permitted.

The mirror relation of "our" world and "their" world is well expressed in
these last words: what is forbidden among us is permitted among them.

Every existence is possible only in forms of definite spatial and temporal
concreteness. Human history is only a special case of this regularity. The
human being is immersed in the real space given by nature. The constants of the
earth's rotation, the movement of the sun across the sky, the movement of the
heavenly bodies, and temporal natural cycles have a direct influence on the way
the human being models the world in consciousness. No less important are the
physical constants of the human body, which set definite relations to the
surrounding world. The dimensions of the human body determine the fact that the
world of mechanics and its laws appears "natural" to the human being, while the
world of particles or cosmic spaces can be imagined only speculatively and only
after a certain violence has been done to consciousness. The relation among the
average weight of the human body, the force of terrestrial gravity, and the
vertical position of the body led to the emergence, universal for all human
cultures, of the opposition up/down, with various content interpretations:
religious, social, political, moral, and so on. One may doubt that the
expression "he reached the heights," understandable to a person of any culture,
would be just as self-explanatory to a thinking fly or to a human being who had
grown up in weightlessness.

"Up," "the summit," requires no explanation. A line from Boileau's *Satires* -
"Whoever does not reach the summit falls very low" - is as understandable as
Camus' sentence in *The Myth of Sisyphus*: "The struggle itself toward the
heights is enough to fill a man's heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy."

However great the temporal and spatial distance between Camus and Yan
Vyshatich, the leader of a military expedition against pagans in 11th-century
Rus', their understanding of the semantics of up and down was the same. Before
executing pagan sorcerers, shamans, Yan asked them where their god was and
received, in the chronicler's rendering, the answer: "He sits in the abyss." To
this Yan authoritatively explained that this was no god if he sits in the
abyss: the demon sits there, while God is in heaven. The chronicler liked this
formula, and almost in the same words he made it repeated by a pagan priest
from the land of the Chud', an Estonian: the Novgorodian asks what sort of gods
theirs are and where they live; the sorcerer replies that they are in the
abysses, black in appearance, winged, with tails, and that they go up under the
sky to listen to the Russian gods, for "your gods are in heaven."

The asymmetry of the human body became the anthropological basis for its
semiotization. The semiotics of right/left has a character just as universal
for all human cultures as the opposition up/down. The same is true of the
initial asymmetry masculine/feminine and living/dead: that is, moving, warm,
breathing as opposed to motionless, cold, not breathing. The consideration of
cold and death as synonyms is confirmed by a huge number of texts in different
cultures, and the identification of death with petrification, with turning to
stone, is just as common. Compare the numerous legends about the origin of
particular mountains and cliffs.

Vernadsky noted that life on Earth proceeds in a special space-time continuum
created by life itself. He proposed the possibility of a scientific hypothesis
according to which, for living matter on planet Earth, the question is not one
of a new geometry, not one of Riemann's geometries, but of a special natural
phenomenon proper, so far, only to living matter: a phenomenon of space-time
that does not geometrically coincide with the space in which time appears as a
fourth coordinate, but instead with space in which time appears as the change
of generations.

Conscious human life, that is, the life of culture, also requires a special
structure of space-time. Culture organizes itself in the form of a definite
space-time and cannot exist outside such organization. This organization is
realized as the semiosphere and, at the same time, by means of the semiosphere.

The external world in which the human being is immersed, in order to become a
factor of culture, undergoes semiotization: it is divided into a region of
objects that signify something, symbolize, point, that is, have meaning, and
objects that represent only themselves. At the same time, the different
languages filling the semiosphere, this hundred-eyed Argus, single out
different things in the reality lying outside them. The stereoscopic picture
that appears in this way appropriates the right to speak in the name of culture
as a whole. Simultaneously, despite all the differences among the substructures
of the semiosphere, they are organized in a common coordinate system: on the
temporal axis, past, present, and future; on the spatial axis, inner space,
outer space, and the boundary between them. Extra-semiotic reality too, its
space and time, is recoded according to this coordinate system so that it can
become "semiotizable," capable of becoming the content of a semiotic text.

As has already been said, the spread of metastructural self-description from
the center of culture to its entire semiotic space, which for the historian
unifies the whole synchronous slice of the semiosphere, in fact creates only
the appearance of unification. If at the center the metastructure appears as
"one's own" language, then on the periphery it turns out to be an "alien"
language, incapable of adequately reflecting the semiotic practice lying under
it. It is like the grammar of a foreign language. As a result, at the center of
cultural space, areas of the semiosphere, rising to the level of
self-description, acquire a rigidly organized character and at the same time
achieve self-regulation. But simultaneously they lose dynamism and, having used
up their reserve of indeterminacy, become inflexible and incapable of
development.

On the periphery, and the farther from the center the more noticeably, the
relations between semiotic practice and the norm imposed on it become more and
more conflictual. Texts generated in accordance with these norms hang in the
air, deprived of any real semiotic environment, while organic creations
determined by the real semiotic milieu come into conflict with artificial
norms. This is the region of semiotic dynamics. It is here that the field of
tension is created in which future languages are worked out.

Thus, for example, it has long been observed that peripheral genres in art are
more revolutionary than those located at the center of culture, enjoying the
highest prestige and being perceived by contemporaries as art par excellence.
In the second half of the 20th century we witnessed the stormy aggression of
marginal forms of culture. One example of this is the "career" of cinema,
which turned from a fairground spectacle, free of theoretical constraints and
regulated only by its technical possibilities, into one of the central arts
and, moreover, especially in recent decades, one of the most described arts.
The same can be said of the art of the European avant-garde as a whole. The
avant-garde lived through a period of "rebelling periphery," became a central
phenomenon dictating its laws to the epoch and striving to color the whole
semiosphere in its own color, and then, having in fact frozen, became the
object of intensified theorizing on the metacultural level.

The same regularities can appear even within a single text. Thus it is known
that in early Renaissance painting it is precisely at the periphery of the
canvas and in the distant landscape planes that genre and everyday elements
accumulate, while the central figures remain strictly canonical. This process
reaches a peak in Piero della Francesca's enigmatic painting *The Flagellation
of Christ* in Urbino, in the Ducal Palace, where the peripheral figures have
boldly moved into the foreground, while the scene of the flagellation has been
set back into depth and muted coloristically, providing as it were a semantic
background for the colorful triple portrait in the foreground.

Analogous processes may unfold not in space but in time, in the movement from
sketch to final text. There are many cases in which preliminary variants, in
painting as in poetry, are more boldly connected with the aesthetics of the
future than the "normalized" final text that has passed through authorial
self-censorship. Many examples of shots excluded by directors in the process of
editing say the same thing.

An analogous example in another sphere is the activity of semiotic processes
during the European Middle Ages in those regions where the Christianization of
"barbarians" did not abolish popular pagan cults, but as it were covered them
with its official mantle, from the hard-to-reach mountain regions of the
Pyrenees and the Alps to the forests and marshes where Saxons and Thuringians
lived. It was precisely on this soil that "folk Christianity," heresies, and
finally Reformation movements would later arise.

The stormy semiotic activity stimulated by such a situation leads to the
accelerated "maturation" of peripheral centers and to the working out by them
of their own meta-descriptions, which in turn can come forward as claimants to
the universal structure of meta-description for the whole semiosphere. The
history of culture gives many examples of such competition. In practice, the
attentive historian of culture discovers in every synchronous slice of culture
not one system of canonizing norms but a paradigm of competing systems.

A characteristic example is the simultaneous existence in 17th-century Germany
of the language societies and the Fruitbearing Society, which set themselves a
purist task - the purification of the German language from barbarisms,
especially Gallicisms and Latinisms, and the grammatical normalization of the
language - and the "Noble Academy of Faithful Ladies," also called the "Order
of the Golden Palm," which pursued the directly opposite aim: the propagation
of French and of a precious style of behavior. One may also point to the
competition between the French Academy and the Blue Salon of Madame de
Rambouillet. The last example is especially revealing: both centers actively
and consciously worked at creating their own "language of culture." When the
French Academy was founded, one of its first-order tasks was to purify and fix
the language; for "gallant culture" too, the question of language stood in the
first place. A contemporary defense of the language of the Precieuses makes
this especially valuable admission: if "jargon" meant only corrupted speech,
like the speech of the lower people, then the speech of the Precieuses could
not be called jargon, since they seek what is most beautiful. But the word also
means affected speech; consequently, precious jargon is a good manner of
speaking. It is not the language actually spoken by those called Precieuses,
but selected phrases made to order.

This last admission is especially valuable: it points directly to the
artificial and normative character of the language of the Precieuses. If in
satires against the Precieuses the matter was represented as a criticism of
corrupted usage from the position of a high norm, then from the point of view
of the supporters of gallant culture, what was at stake was the elevation of
usage into norm, that is, the creation of an abstract image of real usage.

Equally interesting is the controversy in relation to space. Richelieu, the
inspirer of the idea of the Academy, saw the limits for the spread of purified
and ordered French within the borders of an ideal absolutist France, the limit
of his state dreams. The salon of Rambouillet created its own ideal space. The
quantity of documents of "precious geography" is striking: beginning with
Mademoiselle de Scudery's "Map of the Land of Tenderness," the "Map of the
Kingdom of the Precieuses," the "Map of Courtship," and the "Journey to the
Island of Love." An image of multistage space is created: real Paris is
transformed, through a series of conventional renamings, into Athens. But at an
even higher level the ideal space of the "Land of Tenderness" is created and
identified with the "true" semiosphere.

One can compare this with the utopian geography of the Renaissance. In this
case, it is characteristic, on the one hand, to create "above" reality the
image of an ideal city, island, or state, including its geographical and
cartographic description - compare Thomas More's *Utopia* and Francis Bacon's
*New Atlantis* - and, on the other, to realize the metastructure on the
practical level by creating projects for ideal cities and attempts to realize
such projects. The brilliant drawings of ideal cities by Luciano Laurana, the
plans drawn by Durer and Leonardo da Vinci, Filarete's plan of Sforzinda, and
Francesco di Giorgio Martini's plan of the ideal city all represented the
direct intrusion of metastructure into reality, since they were calculated for
realization.

The hottest points of semio-forming processes, however, are the boundaries of
the semiosphere. The concept of boundary is ambiguous. On the one hand, it
separates; on the other, it connects. It is always a boundary with something
and therefore simultaneously belongs to both bordering cultures, to both
mutually adjacent semiospheres. The boundary is bi- and polylingual. The
boundary is a mechanism for translating texts of an alien semiotics into the
language of "ours," a place where the "external" is transformed into the
"internal." It is a filtering membrane that transforms alien texts enough for
them to fit into the internal semiotics of the semiosphere while still
remaining foreign.

In Kievan Rus' there was a term for nomads who settled on the frontiers of the
Russian land, became agriculturalists, and, entering alliances with Russian
princes, went on campaigns together with them against their own nomadic
tribesmen. They were called "our pagans." The word for "pagan" meant at the
same time pagan, alien, improper, non-Christian. The oxymoron "our pagans"
expresses the boundary situation very well.

In order for Byron to enter Russian culture, his cultural double had to arise:
"the Russian Byron." This figure would simultaneously be a person of two
cultures. As "Russian," he fits organically into the internal processes of
Russian literature and speaks its language, in the broad semiotic sense. More
than that, he cannot be removed from Russian literature without leaving in it a
gaping emptiness filled by nothing. But at the same time he is Byron, an
organic part of English literature, and in the Russian context he will perform
his function only if he is experienced precisely as Byron, that is, as an
English poet. Only in this context is Lermontov's exclamation understandable:
"No, I am not Byron, I am another."

Not only individual texts or authors, but whole cultures, in order for
intercultural contacts to be possible, must have such equivalent images in
"our" culture, like bilingual dictionaries. The double role of this image
appears in the fact that it is simultaneously a means of communication and an
obstacle to communication. A revealing example is this: Pushkin's early
romantic poems, the stormy biography of his youth, and his exile created in
the consciousness of his readers a stereotypical image of the romantic poet,
through the prism of which all his texts were perceived. Pushkin himself in
these years actively participated in forming the "mythology of his
personality," which entered into the general system of "romantic behavior."
Later, however, this image stood between the work of the evolving poet and his
readers. His strict work, oriented toward life's truth and rejecting
romanticism, was perceived by readers as a "fall" and "betrayal" precisely
because the image of the early Pushkin still lived in their consciousness.

Just as, when the metalinguistic structure of the semiosphere changes, works
appear about "unknown" and "forgotten" cultural figures, so, when
image-stereotypes change, works arise with titles like "The Unknown
Dostoevsky" or "Goethe As He Really Was," suggesting to the reader that until
now he had known "the wrong" Dostoevsky or Goethe and that the hour of true
understanding has only just arrived.

Something analogous is observed when texts of one genre invade the space of
another. Innovation consists precisely in this: the principles of one genre are
restructured according to the laws of another, while this "other" genre
organically enters the new structure and at the same time preserves the memory
of another coding system. Thus when Pushkin inserts into the fabric of the tale
*Dubrovsky* the authentic text of an 18th-century legal complaint, and
Dostoevsky includes in *The Brothers Karamazov* his carefully composed
imitation of authentic speeches by prosecutor and defense attorney, these texts
appear simultaneously as organic fabric of the novelistic narrative and as
foreign documents-quotations, falling out of the aesthetic key of artistic
narration.

The idea of a boundary separating the inner space of the semiosphere from the
outer gives only a primary, rough division. In fact, the whole space of the
semiosphere is cut across by boundaries of different levels, boundaries of
individual languages and even of texts. The inner space of each of these
sub-semiospheres has a certain semiotic "I" of its own, realized as the
relation of some language, group of texts, or individual text - taking into
account that languages and texts are arranged hierarchically at different
levels - to some metastructural space that describes them. The penetration of
the semiosphere by private boundaries creates a multilevel system. Definite
areas of the semiosphere can, at different levels of self-description, form a
semiotic unity, a certain continuous semiotic space bounded by a single
boundary, or a group of closed spaces whose discreteness is marked by
boundaries between them, or, finally, part of some more general space,
delimited on one side by a fragment of boundary and open on the other.
Naturally, this will correspond to a hierarchy of codes, and different levels
of significance will be activated in the single reality of the semiosphere.

An important criterion here is the question of what, in a given system, is
perceived as a subject: for example, the subject of law in the legal texts of a
given culture, or "personality" in one or another system of sociocultural
coding. The concept of "personality" is identified with the boundaries of a
human being's physical individuality only under definite cultural and semiotic
conditions. It may be collective, may include or exclude property, and may be
connected with a definite social, religious, or moral position. The boundary of
personality is a semiotic boundary. Thus, for example, wife, children, unfree
servants, and vassals can in some systems be included in the personality of the
master, patriarch, husband, patron, or suzerain, without independent
"personhood"; in other systems they may be regarded as separate persons. A
situation of indignation and rebellion arises when two modes of coding collide:
when the social-semiotic structure describes a given individual as a part,
while he understands himself as an autonomous unit, a semiotic subject and not
an object.

When Ivan the Terrible executed, together with disgraced boyars, not only their
families but also all their servants, not only household servants but peasants
of their villages, or when peasant resettlement, renaming of villages, and
leveling of buildings were applied, this was dictated, despite the tsar's
pathological cruelty, not by considerations of danger, as if a serf from a
provincial estate could be dangerous to the tsar, but by the idea that all of
them were one person, parts of the personality of the punished boyar, and
therefore shared responsibility with him. Such a view was apparently not alien
to Stalin with his psychology of an Eastern tyrant.

From the European legal point of view, formed by post-Renaissance individual
legal consciousness, it seemed inexplicable why another person should suffer
for one person's guilt. As late as 1732, the wife of the English ambassador in
Petersburg, Lady Rondeau, by no means hostile to the Russian court and even
inclined to idealize it, reported to her European correspondent about the exile
of the Dolgorukov family and wrote that the correspondent might be surprised by
the exile of women and children, but in Russia, when the head of the family
falls into disgrace, the whole family is persecuted.

The same concept of collective, in this case clan-based, rather than
individual personality lies at the foundation of blood vengeance, when the
whole clan of the murderer is perceived as the responsible person. The
historian S. M. Solovyov convincingly connected mestnichestvo, which in the
eyes of an 18th-century enlightener who devoutly believed in progress appeared
only as a manifestation of "ignorance," with a special collective experience
of the clan as a single personality. According to this logic, with such strong
clan union and such responsibility of all members of the clan for one another,
the significance of the separate person necessarily disappeared before the
significance of the clan. A known Ivan Petrov was not thought as one Ivan
Petrov alone, but only as Ivan Petrov with brothers and nephews. When one
member of the clan rose in service, the whole clan rose; when one member was
lowered, the whole clan was lowered.

Thus under Tsar Alexei Mikhailovich in the 17th century, the stolnik boyar
Matvei Pushkin, belonging to one of the thirty-one noblest clans, refused to go
on a diplomatic assignment as second person after the prominent statesman and
favorite of the tsar, the less noble Nardyn-Nashchokin. He preferred to go to
prison, endured threats of confiscation of all property and the tsar's anger,
and answered with dignity that the tsar might order him executed, but
Nashchokin was, before him, a young man and not a genealogical equal.

A space that in one system of coding appears as a single personality may, in
another, turn out to be the place of collision among several semiotic subjects.

The crossing of semiotic space by numerous boundaries creates, for every
message moving through it, a situation of repeated translations and
transformations, accompanied by the generation of new information, which takes
on an avalanche-like character.

The function of any boundary and any film - from the membrane of a living cell
to the biosphere as, according to Vernadsky, the film covering our planet, and
to the boundary of the semiosphere - comes down to limiting penetration,
filtering, and adaptively reworking the external into the internal. At
different levels this invariant function is realized in different ways. At the
level of the semiosphere it means separating one's own from the alien, filtering
the external, to which the status of a text in a foreign language is assigned,
and translating this text into one's own language. In this way the external
space is structured.

In cases where the semiosphere also includes real-territorial features, the
boundary acquires spatial meaning in the direct sense. The isomorphism of
different kinds of settlement - from archaic villages to the ideal-city
projects of the Renaissance and the Enlightenment - with ideas about the
structure of the cosmos has been noted many times. Connected with this is the
tendency of the center of construction toward the most important cultic and
administrative buildings. At the periphery are located the least valued social
groups. Those who are below the line of social value are located at the
boundary of the suburb; the etymology of the Russian word for suburb means
"before the place," that is, before the city, on its boundary line. In terms of
vertical orientation, these will be attics and cellars, and in the modern city,
the metro. If the apartment becomes the center of "normal" dwelling, then the
stairway and entrance become the boundary space between home and non-home. It
is no accident that precisely these spaces become "one's own" for boundary, or
marginal, groups of society: the homeless, drug users, youth. Public-use places
in cities, stadiums, and cemeteries also belong to boundary places. No less
revealing is the change in accepted norms of behavior when moving from the
boundary of such a space toward its center.

Certain elements, however, are located outside altogether. If the inner world
reproduces the cosmos, then beyond its boundary lies chaos, the antiworld,
extrastructural iconic space inhabited by monsters, infernal forces, or people
connected with them. Beyond the boundary of the settlement should live, in the
village, the sorcerer, the miller, and sometimes the blacksmith; in the
medieval city, the executioner. "Normal" space has not only geographic but also
temporal boundaries. Beyond its boundary lies night time. If one needs to visit
the sorcerer, one goes at night. In antispace lives the robber: his house is
the forest, an anti-home; his sun is the moon, the "thieves' little sun" in
the Russian proverb; he speaks an anti-language and performs anti-behavior,
whistling loudly, swearing obscenely, sleeping when people work and robbing
when people sleep, and so on.

The "night world" of the city is likewise located at the boundary of cultural
space or beyond it. This travestied world is oriented toward anti-behavior.

We have already drawn attention to the process by which the periphery of
culture moves into the center and the center is pushed out to the periphery.
The movement of these counter-directed flows between center and the "periphery
of the periphery," the boundary region of culture, appears with still greater
force. Thus after the October Revolution of 1917 in Russia, this process
received varied nonmetaphorical realization: the poor from the suburbs moved en
masse into "bourgeois apartments," from which former residents were evicted or
into which they were "compressed." The transfer of the highly artistic wrought
iron fence that before the revolution had surrounded the tsar's garden around
the Winter Palace in Petrograd to a workers' outskirts district, where it was
placed around a square in the suburb, had an obvious symbolic meaning. The
tsar's garden itself remained without any fence at all, "open." In utopian
projects for the socialist city of the future, created in abundance at the
beginning of the 1920s, the idea was often repeated that at the center of such
a city, "on the site of palace and church," would stand a gigantic factory.

In the same sense, Peter I's transfer of the capital to Petersburg, to the
boundary, is characteristic. The transfer of the political-administrative
center to the geographic boundary was at the same time the movement of the
boundary into the ideological-political center of the state. Later pan-Slavist
projects for transferring the capital to Constantinople moved the center even
beyond all real borders.

Equally, we may observe the movement of norms of behavior, language, style of
dress, and so on from the boundary sphere of culture into its center. Jeans
serve as an example: work clothing intended for people engaged in physical
labor became youth clothing, because youth, rejecting the nuclear culture of
the 20th century, saw its ideal in peripheral culture. Then, spreading through
the whole sphere of culture, jeans became neutral, that is, "common," clothing,
the most important mark of nuclear semiotic systems. The periphery is brightly
colored, marked; the nucleus is "normal," that is, it has neither color nor
smell, it "simply exists." Therefore the victory of one or another semiotic
system is its movement into the center and its inevitable "decoloring." One
can compare this with the "ordinary" age cycle: rebellious young people become
"normal" respectable gentlemen with the years, at the same time undergoing an
evolution from provocative "coloredness" to "decoloring."

The intensification of semiotic processes in the boundary strip of the
semiosphere is connected with the fact that precisely here constant incursions
from outside take place. The boundary, as we have said, is two-sided, and one
side of it always faces outer space. More than that, the boundary is an area of
constituted bilinguality. This usually receives direct expression in the
linguistic practice of the population on the boundary of cultural areas. Since
the boundary is a necessary part of the semiosphere and no "we" can exist if
"they" are absent, culture creates not only its own type of internal
organization, but also its own type of external "disorganization." In this
sense one can say that the "barbarian" is created by civilization and needs it
just as much as civilization needs the barbarian. The external space beyond the
limits of the semiosphere is the place of an unbroken dialogue. It does not
matter whether a given culture sees in the "barbarian" a savior or an enemy, a
bearer of healthy moral qualities or a depraved cannibal: it is dealing with a
construct built as its own inverted reflection. Thus in the thoroughly rational
positivist society of 20th-century Europe there inevitably had to arise images
of the "prelogical savage" or of the irrational subconscious, an anti-sphere
lying beyond the limits of the rational space of culture.

Since in reality any semiosphere is not immersed in an amorphous "wild" space
but comes into contact with other semiospheres possessing their own
organization, although from the point of view of the first they may seem like
non-organizations, constant exchange arises here, the working out of a common
language, a koine, and the formation of creolized semiotic systems. Even in
order to wage war, one must have a common language. It is known that if, on
the one hand, in the final period of Roman history barbarian soldiers raised
emperors of Rome to the throne, then, on the other hand, many military leaders
of the "barbarians" had at one time undergone "training" in the Roman legions.
On the boundaries of China, the Roman Empire, and Byzantium we observe one and
the same picture: the technical achievements of settled civilizations pass to
nomads, who turn them against the sources from which they received them. Yet
these collisions inevitably lead to cultural leveling and to the creation of a
certain new semiosphere of a higher order, into which both sides are included
already as equal participants.
