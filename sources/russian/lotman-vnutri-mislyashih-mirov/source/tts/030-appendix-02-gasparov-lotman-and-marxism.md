# Lotman And Marxism

M. L. Gasparov

In Roman Jakobson's obituary for B. Eikhenbaum, it is said in passing that
Eikhenbaum irritated official literary scholars also because when they forced
him to write on the topic "Lenin on Leo Tolstoy," even that turned out better
for him than for them.

Probably the same can be said of Lotman. In the history of Russian literature
he worked on authors who were entirely reliable: Radishchev, the Decembrists,
Pushkin. And Radishchev really was a revolutionary for him, the Decembrists
heroes, Pushkin a universal genius, and even Karamzin turned out to be very
sympathetic to the French Revolution. But at the same time they became much
more complex and deeper than in the usual portraits, even portraits signed by
good scholars. For official Soviet literary studies, if Radishchev was good,
then Karamzin had to be bad. Lotman did not work that way, and this was
irritating.

Soviet literary studies was built on Marxism. In Marxism, method and ideology
coexisted. The method of Marxism was dialectical and historical materialism.
Materialism meant the axiom that being determines consciousness, including the
consciousness of the bearer of culture, the poet and the reader. Historicism
meant that culture is a consequence of the social-economic phenomena of its
time. Dialectics meant that the development of culture, like everything else in
the world, occurs as the result of the struggle of its internal contradictions.

Ideology, however, taught something else. History had already ended, and the
eternity of an ideal classless society was beginning; the whole past had been
only an approach to it. All internal contradictions had already played their
role, and only external ones remained, contradictions between good and bad
phenomena. Cultural phenomena had to be divided into good and bad, with every
effort made to ensure that the good were good in every respect, and the bad the
reverse. Absolute truth had been reached, and the consciousness possessing it
now itself created new being. The ideology of victorious Marxism decisively did
not coincide with the method of struggling Marxism, but this was carefully
concealed. Lotman treated the Marxist method seriously, and ideology as it
deserved. It is known that the most dangerous person for dogma is the one who
takes it seriously. The official guardians felt this.

As long as Lotman wrote about literary history, this was noticed less. One
could accept that Radishchev was, after all, good, and close one's eyes to the
ways this was proven. When Lotman began writing theory, official hostility
became open. The turning point came with three books about the same thing,
addressed to an increasingly broad circle of readers: *Lectures on Structural
Poetics* in 1964, *The Structure of the Artistic Text* in 1970, and *Analysis
of the Poetic Text* in 1972. How the same material was developed differently
for specialists and for teacher-training readers is a separate side of Yuri
Mikhailovich's talent. The book consisted of a detailed introduction on the
principles of analysis and twelve analyses of individual poems from Batyushkov
to Zabolotsky. Similar analyses were also scattered through separate articles
and chapters of his works. They are enough to speak of Lotman's individual
manner as an analyst, and this manner appears especially clearly against the
background of other approaches, both then and now, to the analysis of poetic
text.

The practice of such analyses became customary among us in the 1960s. At their
base lay exercises by university lecturers, offered to students for clarity.
Earlier they had remained within seminar walls; now they spilled into print.
This was progress, a consequence of the Khrushchev thaw. Before this, in the
epoch of dogmatic literary studies, the only outlet from the world of
ideological content had been books titled *The Mastery of Pushkin*, or
Ostrovsky, or Mayakovsky, where it was shown by which artistic features the
writer conveys ideological content to the reader. Compared with this, analyses
of individual poems were of course an achievement. On the small field of one
poem, ideological content receded into the background, while the means that
carried it moved forward and, in good analysts, even formed a structure.
Lotman took the final step: the concept of structure, into which all elements
of the poem combine, from ideological declarations down to the differential
features of phonemes, became fundamental for him.

Talk about the selection of phonemes, the echoes of rhythms, antitheses of
verb tenses, the smallest semantic shades of words, intersecting semantic
fields - all this took up so much space that it looked almost provocative,
especially when applied to the classical poems of Pushkin, Tyutchev, and
Nekrasov, over which one was long supposed to reflect only on lofty thoughts
and feelings. At the same time there were, as it were, no legitimate grounds
for attacking Lotman's methodology. That irritated the critics most of all.

In fact, when Lotman began the analysis of a poem by charting its vocabulary,
rhythm, and phonics, he thereby strictly followed the rule of materialism:
being determines consciousness. At the beginning there are the writer's words
written on paper, and from the perception of them, conscious when it concerns
dictionary meaning, subconscious when it concerns stylistic shades or sound
associations, our understanding of the poem is formed. No highest content of
Pushkin's freedom-loving or love poetry can be grasped apart from its verbal
expression. It is therefore methodologically incorrect to begin the analysis
with ideological content and then descend to "mastery." The poet's thought is
subject to reconstruction, and the path from thought to text to formalization.

Lotman studied the formalization of the poet's path from thought to text in
the article "The Poems of Early Pasternak and Certain Questions of the
Structural Study of Text." From the analysis it followed that the principles by
which what is suitable and unsuitable for poetry is selected, at all levels
from ideas to language and meter, may differ, and never fully coincide with the
criteria of ordinary consciousness and natural language. This meant that the
poetic systems of Pushkin and Pasternak are equally based on the opposition
between the logic of the "poet" and the logic of the "crowd," and have equal
right to exist, depending only on historically changing taste. For an ideology
that considered its own taste absolute, such equalization was unpleasant.

Lotman studied the formalization of the reader's path from text to the poet's
thought in all his works on textual analysis. As a winning demonstration
example he chose a Pushkin line from "Liberty": "Arise, fallen slaves!"
Semantically, the word "arise" means "rebel"; stylistically, it simply means
"stand up," marked by a high style. The first meaning is closer to our ear, but
only the second fits without contradiction into the logic of the poem. This
meant that to understand poetry it is not enough to rely on one's own sense of
language. One must study the poet's language as a foreign language, in which
the connection of words by style, or even by sound, may mean more than
connection by dictionary meaning. For an ideology that believed world culture
had only one language and that it was the mistress of this language, this too
was unpleasant.

Meanwhile here too Lotman strictly held to Marxist premises: the premises of
dialectics. He wrote that the methodological basis of structuralism is
dialectics. One of the basic principles of structuralism is the rejection of
analysis according to the principle of a mechanical list of features; the
researcher does not list "features," but builds a model of relations. The
dialectical proposition of the universal interconnection of phenomena meant
that in the poem alliterations, rhythms, metaphorics, images, and ideas
coexist, tightly intertwined with one another and perceptible only by contrast
against one another. Phonic and stylistic contrasts link with semantic ones,
and as a result an opposition such as explosive and non-explosive consonants
turns out to be intertwined with the opposition "I" and "you" or "freedom" and
"slavery."

What is important is that this interconnection is never full and univocal. It
is impossible to derive the iambic meter or metaphorical style of a poem
directly from its ideological content. Each preserves the semantic associations
of all its previous uses, and some of them coincide with the semantics of the
new context while others contradict it. This is the structure of the text, and
it is a dialectical structure: one in which everything combines into tense
oppositions.

The main dialectical opposition that makes the text of a poem alive is that
the text represents a field of tension between norm and violations of it. When
reading a poem, and still more many poems of one poetic culture, the reader
forms a system of expectations. If a poem has begun with a Pushkinian iamb,
then stresses will be expected on every second syllable, the vocabulary will be
elevated and, for us, slightly archaic, the images will mainly belong to the
Romantic set, and so on. At every step these expectations are either confirmed
or not confirmed. In iambic verse stresses are often skipped; after the
Romantic line about Lensky's death, "the fire on the altar went out," the poem
continues with images of an empty house and windows whitened with chalk. It is
precisely the confirmation or non-confirmation of these readerly expectations
by the real text that is felt as aesthetic experience. If confirmation is one
hundred percent, with no new information, the poem is felt as bad, boring
poetry. If non-confirmation is one hundred percent, and new information does
not rest on existing information, the poem is felt as not poetry at all. The
measure of information becomes the criterion for evaluating poetry. Marx
thought that science reaches perfection only when it manages to use
mathematics; Lotman recalls this unpopular testimony of Lafargue in the same
article, "Literary Studies Must Be A Science."

Lotman inherited from Marxism not only materialism and dialectics, but also its
historicism, and in exactly the same way this historicism of method collided
with the anti-historicism of ideology. The ideological scheme is egocentric. It
imposes on all epochs one and the same system of values: ours. Whatever does
not fit the system is declared an annoying contradiction, the result of
historical immaturity. For the Marxist method, contradictions were the motor of
history; for Marxist ideology, on the contrary, they were obstacles to history.

It is precisely this static egocentrism that Lotman rejects in the name of
historicism. For each culture he reconstructs its own system of values, and
what from outside seemed mosaic eclecticism turns out from inside to be orderly
and non-contradictory, even in such glaring cases as Radishchev denying the
immortality of the soul at the beginning of a work and affirming it at the
end. Of course this non-contradictoriness is temporary. With the passage of
time, unnoticed contradictions begin to be felt, and felt contradictions lose
significance. A system breaks, and, for example, raznochinets culture replaces
noble culture. The presence of potential contradictions within a cultural
system turns out to be the motor of its development, exactly as Marxist
dialectics required. But the demonstratively non-contradictory ideology that
each culture always tried to compose for itself and others turns out to be a
fiction, a mystification of real life behavior. For the thoroughly
ideologized official Soviet culture, this view of the ideologies of past
cultures was very unpleasant.

Driven by the softening and sharpening of internal contradictions, history
moves by jolts: now smooth development, now explosion; now evolution, now
revolution. This too is a commonplace of Marxism, and Lotman also adopted it.
But he remembered one more proposition from the alphabet of Marxism, one so
elementary that people rarely thought about it: truth is always concrete. This
means that, as a historian, he thinks not so much about how these epochs appear
to us as about how they see themselves. Or, to use his own expressions, he
represents them not in common nouns but in proper names.

To look at the cultural system of an epoch from within means to stand at its
point of view and forget what will come afterward. There is an anecdotal phrase
attributed to L. Sabaneev: "Berlioz was a convinced predecessor of Wagner."
Historicism demands that we understand that each generation thinks not about
whom it should precede and does not try to please us or guess our answers to
all questions. It solves its own tasks. It does not know the future path of
history in advance. Many equally possible paths stand before it. Scientific
historicism commands us to see historical reality not as goal-directed but as
conditioned. Not goal-directed, that is, not straining to become our pedestal
and nothing more.

Lotman insisted that history is lawful but not fatal, that there are always
unused possibilities in it, that what did not come to pass behind us is far
greater than what did. It was precisely the lost possibilities of culture that
attracted Lotman in unnoticed writers and unnoticed works, from the time when
he wrote about Andrei Turgenev, Mamonov, and Kaisarov to the final years when
he lectured on Pushkin's unrealized plans. But of course not only this:
respect for small names has always been a noble tradition of philology, as
opposed to criticism, of science as opposed to ideology, of research as opposed
to evaluative ranking. This was especially so when the subjects were untimely
deaths and unfulfilled hopes. In official literary studies, which had
expropriated the classics, there was an increasing tendency to replace the
history of the process with a medallion gallery of literary generals.

To imagine this situation of choosing a path without the answer prompted by
retrospection, a choice of one path by renouncing others, is already not so
much a scientific task as an artistic one, as in Tynianov, who begins *The
Death of Vazir-Mukhtar* with the words: "Nothing had yet been decided." The
choice is not made by a faceless epoch. It is made by each individual person.
It is in that person's consciousness that fragments of contradictory ideas form
a structure, and that structure is above all ethical, with the key concepts of
shame and honor. About his favorite time, the 1800s and 1810s, Lotman says
that the main cultural creativity of that epoch manifested itself in the
creation of a human type. It did not produce peak creations of the mind, but it
did produce a sharp rise in the average level of spiritual life. This attention
to the average level and to the non-famous people who raised it is also a
democratic tradition of philology. I would dare say that Lotman understood his
own place in our contemporaneity in this way. He did not throw down challenges
or write manifestos. He raised the average level of spiritual life. And now we
make use of that rise.

Thus Lotman moves the front edge of science into the place where art usually
rules: into the world of human characters and fates, the world of proper names.
He admires the unpredictability of historical concreteness. He had a student,
Rafik Papayan, a verse scholar. Later Papayan was arrested for Armenian
nationalism and imprisoned. Then, during perestroika, he was nominated for
deputy, and his rival in the electoral district turned out to be the former
prison warden under whom he had served. When I told Lotman this, he clapped his
hands and exclaimed: "That is why I love history!" This interest is almost
more aesthetic than intellectual. But Lotman does not replace science with art:
science remains science.

When one recalls the human portraits that appear in Lotman's
historical-cultural works, from the Decembrist Zavalishin to Natalia Dolgorukaia,
two comparisons first suggest themselves of their own accord: Kliuchevsky and
Gershenzon were equally artistic. Then the comparison becomes more precise:
rather Kliuchevsky than Gershenzon. Because there is one word impossible in
Lotman's portraits: the word "soul." For Lotman, human personality is not a
substance but a relation, a point of intersection of social codes. A Marxist
would say: a point of intersection of social relations. Once again, the
difference is only in language.

This is exactly why Pushkin could be simultaneously an Enlightenment
rationalist, an aristocrat, a Romantic, and a sober spectator of his century;
could know the value of conventions and still let himself be killed in a duel.
Each of these intersecting lines can be traced separately, and then one obtains
that "history of culture without names" or "typology of culture without names"
that would have delighted Wolfflin and Varron. Lotman knew very well how to
perform such an analysis of the impersonal mechanisms of culture, but it was
not very interesting to him, not because it was a scheme, but because it was
too crude a scheme.

For Lotman, analysis of binary oppositions in a poem and the picture of an
epoch around a poet supplemented one another as science and art. But such
complementarity can be realized in different ways. Veselovsky worked all his
life on the impersonal history of literature, and in old age wrote a remarkable
psychological portrait of Zhukovsky, without any connection to the former work,
simply for the soul. Tynianov began to write a novel about Pushkin when he saw
that the image of Pushkin that had formed in his consciousness could not be
grounded in scholarly proof, but only made artistically convincing: the image
was primary, the argument auxiliary. In Lotman, as in Kliuchevsky, it is the
reverse. Each of his portraits is an illustration in the proper sense of the
word, material for an exercise in historical-cultural analysis. For him a
person, like a phoneme, is composed of differential features. All the
intersecting cultural codes can be isolated in that person, and the author
refrains from doing this only so that the thoughtful reader can estimate them
mentally. Here the conception is primary, the image auxiliary.

Each epoch revises the classics from its own point of view, in relation to its
own demands and needs. Each reader creates "my Pushkin" in his own image and
likeness. This is the reader's individual creativity against the background of
the general creativity of humanity, both writerly and readerly. Lotman was more
interested in the opposite: recreating the true, unique Pushkin and looking at
the world through his eyes. When one reads Lotman's historical-cultural
portraits, one does not feel that one is evaluating history. Rather, one feels
that history is evaluating oneself, and evaluating sternly.

The ability to stand at another, historically distant point of view is the
humanistic enrichment of culture. In this lies the moral meaning of the
humanities. To look not at history from modernity, but at modernity from
history, means to consider oneself and one's surroundings not as the final goal
of history, but only as one of its many potential variants.

For Lotman, the thought of unrealized possibilities in history was not only a
game of dialectical intelligence. It was also the experience of two generations
of our century: those who in the 1930s parted from the unused possibilities of
the 1920s, and in the postwar years from the unfulfilled hopes of the war
years. Lotman did not encourage younger colleagues inclined to notice hints of
present unpleasantness in the Russian culture of the past. But he ended his
last book, *Culture and Explosion*, precisely with considerations about the
perestroika present: about the possibility of transition from the binary
structure of Russian culture to the ternary structure of European culture. "To
miss this possibility would be a historical catastrophe."

Lotman never declared himself either a Marxist or an anti-Marxist. He was a
scholar. To speak of the "true" Pushkin as opposed to "my" Pushkin means to
believe in truth, not absolute truth, but objective truth, for whose sake
science exists. "The only thing with which science, by its nature, can serve
the human being is to satisfy the need for truth," Lotman wrote in the same
article on literary studies as a science. This is not a banality. In the
twentieth century, which began with the creative self-exaltation of decadence
and ends with the creative playfulness of destructivism, faith in truth and
science is not an axiom but a life position. Soviet culture received this
faith, worked out by humanity, from the hands of Marxism. Marxism arose at the
turn from the utopianism of the Romantics to the positivism of the
post-Romantics. Its method was scientific, its ideology utopian. To renounce
the scientific method for the sake of ideological dogma, or for the sake of
destructivist arbitrariness, is not much different.

Lotman worked in literary studies for more than forty years. He began work in
the epoch of dogmatic literary studies. Now, by contrast, anti-dogmatic
literary studies triumphs. Soviet ideology demanded that the scholar describe a
picture of the world uniformly given for everyone. Destructivist ideology
demands that the scholar describe a picture of the world individually created
by the scholar himself, the more whimsical the better. Extremes meet. For
Lotman, both are not research but the imposition of truth, whether state truth
or one's own. Therefore the new narcissistic philology remained alien to him.
"The perception of the artistic text is always a struggle between listener and
author," he wrote. In this struggle he unambiguously took the side of the
author. Historical truth was dearer to him than creative self-assertion. This
is the position of science as opposed to the position of art. In the history of
our culture from the 1960s through the 1990s, Y. M. Lotman's structuralism
stands between the epoch of dogmatism and the epoch of anti-dogmatism,
opposing both as scientificity opposes two forms of anti-scientificity.

Many historical-cultural antitheses that seemed strikingly irreconcilable to
contemporaries turn out, for descendants, to have been imaginary. Romanticism
opposed itself to Classicism with all its might, and Realism opposed itself to
Romanticism. But intelligent people saw and understood that Classicism had
enough shelves and compartments to sort all Romantic innovations into them, and
that Realism was nothing other than a branch grown enormously from the trunk of
Romanticism: also exoticism, only not historical or geographical but social and
psychological. What happened was a simple narrowing, and of course deepening,
of the field of experiments: that same cutting off of unused possibilities that
so interested Lotman. In exactly the same way, the Marxist method in the social
sciences was broad enough to contain the approaches that later took shape as
structuralism. Lotman remembered this and did not consider it necessary, to use
Pushkin's phrase, to bite the nurse's breast just because his teeth had come
in.
