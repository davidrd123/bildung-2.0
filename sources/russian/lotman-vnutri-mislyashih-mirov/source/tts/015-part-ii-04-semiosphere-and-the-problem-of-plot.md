# Opening Bridge: Semiotics Of Space

The semiotics of space has exceptionally great, if not dominant, significance
in creating the picture of the world of one or another culture. The nature of
this phenomenon is connected with the specificity of space itself. The
inevitable foundation of culture's mastering of life is the creation of an
image of the world, a spatial model of the universe. In this case, spatial
modeling reconstructs the spatial appearance of the real world.

But another use of spatial images is also possible. The mathematician A. D.
Aleksandrov writes that in studying topological properties we again confront
the possibility of thinking of an abstract set of objects that possess only
properties of this kind. Such a set is called an abstract topological space.
And he continues: the isolation of these properties in their pure form leads us
to the idea of the corresponding abstract space. If, when some definite feature
is isolated, there forms a set of elements continuously adjacent to one
another, then we may speak of an abstract space according to this feature. In
this sense one may speak of ethical, color, mythological, and other spaces.
Spatial modeling becomes a language in which non-spatial ideas can be
expressed.

Especially indicative is the reflection of the spatial picture of the
semiosphere in the mirror of artistic texts.

## Semiosphere And The Problem Of Plot

Semiotic systems are in a state of constant motion. Changeability is the law of
the semiosphere's existence. It changes as a whole, and constantly changes its
inner structure. But there is also another kind of change. Within each of the
substructures that make up the semiosphere, one can distinguish elements that
are stably fixed in its space and elements that possess a relative freedom of
movement. The first are fixed in social, cultural, religious, and other
structures. The second possess a higher degree of freedom than their
surroundings in choosing their behavior.

The hero of the second type is capable of committing acts, that is, of crossing
boundaries of prohibition inaccessible to others. Like Orpheus or Soslan from
the Nart epic, such a hero can cross the boundary that separates the living
from the dead; or, like the Friulian *benandanti*, can engage in night battles
with witches; or, like a berserker, can rush into battle in violation of all
its rules, naked or in a bear skin, howling like a beast and killing both his
own and the enemy. He may be a noble robber or a picaro, a sorcerer, a spy, a
detective, a terrorist, or a superman. What matters is that he is capable of
doing what is forbidden to others and of crossing structural boundaries of
cultural space. Every such crossing is an act; a chain of acts forms a plot.

Plot is a syntagmatic concept, and therefore is connected with the experience
of time. For this reason we encounter two typological forms of event, which
correspond to two types of time: cyclical and linear. In archaic cultures,
cyclical time dominates. Texts created according to the laws of cyclical time
are not plotted in our sense, and in general can be described only with great
difficulty by means of categories familiar to us. Their first feature is the
absence of the categories of beginning and end. The text is thought of as a
certain continuously repeating device, synchronized with the cyclical processes
of nature: with the change of the seasons, the time of day, and the phenomena
of the stellar calendar. Human life was considered not as a linear segment
enclosed between birth and death, but as an endlessly repeating cycle.

In this case, a narration may begin from any point that performs the role of a
beginning for the given telling, which is a private manifestation of a
beginningless and infinite Text. Such telling has no aim of informing some
listeners of something unknown to them. It is a mechanism that secures the
continuity of the cyclical processes in nature itself. Therefore the choice of
one or another
plot episode of the Text as the beginning and content of today's telling does
not belong to the teller. It is part of a ritual chronologically fixed and
conditioned by the course of natural cycles.

Another feature connected with cyclicity is the tendency toward the unconditional
identification of different characters. The cyclical world of mythological
texts forms a multilayered device with clear signs of topological
organization. This means that cycles such as the day, the year, or the cyclic
chain of deaths and births of a human being or a god are regarded as mutually
homeomorphic. Therefore although night, winter, and death are not alike in
some respects, bringing them together is not a metaphor, as modern
consciousness perceives it. They are one and the same, or more precisely,
transformations of one and the same thing.

Characters and objects mentioned on different levels of the cyclic
mythological device are different proper names of one thing. By virtue of its
exceptional capacity to undergo topological transformations, the mythological
text with astonishing boldness declares to be identical entities whose
convergence would present great difficulties for us.

The topological world of myth is not discrete. As we shall try to show,
discreteness arises here because of inadequate translation into discrete
metalanguages of a non-mythological type.

This central text-forming device performs an extremely important function: it
builds a picture of the world and establishes unity among its distant spheres,
in effect carrying out a number of the functions of science in pre-scientific
cultural formations. Its orientation toward establishing iso- and
homeomorphisms, and toward reducing the world's diverse variety to invariant
images, allowed texts of this kind not only functionally to take the place of
science, but also to stimulate a number of cultural achievements of a purely
scientific kind, for example in the calendar-astronomical domain. The
functional kinship of these systems can be seen clearly in the work of
historians of ancient Greek science.

The texts generated by the central text-forming device played a classificatory,
stratifying, and ordering role. They reduced the world of excesses and
anomalies surrounding the human being to norm and order. Even if, in being
retold in our language, these texts take on the appearance of plots, in
themselves they are not such. They dealt not with singular and non-lawlike
phenomena, but with events that are timeless, endlessly reproducible, and in
this sense immobile. Even when the story concerned the death and dismemberment
of a god's body and the god's subsequent resurrection, we do not have a plotted
narrative in our sense, because these events are thought of as belonging to a
certain position of the cycle and as primordially repeated. The regularity of
the repetition makes them not an excess or a chance event, but a law
immanently inherent in the world.

The central cyclical text-generating device could not, typologically, be the
only one. As a counter-mechanism, it needed a text-generating device organized
in accordance with linear temporal movement and fixing not laws but anomalies.
Such were oral stories about "incidents," "news," and various fortunate and
unfortunate excesses. If in the first case what was fixed was principle, then
here it was chance. If historically the first mechanism produced law-giving and
normative texts of both sacred and scientific character, then the second
produced historical texts, chronicles, and annals.

The fixation of singular and chance events, crimes, disasters, everything
thought of as a violation of some primordial order, formed the historical
kernel of plotted narrative. It is not accidental that the elementary basis of
artistic narrative genres is called the "novella," that is, "news," and, as
has been noted many times, has an anecdotal basis.

It should also be noted in passing that the pragmatic nature of these
originally opposite types of text is fundamentally different. In the world of
mythological texts, because of the spatial-topological laws of its
construction, the structural laws of homeomorphism are what stand out first:
relations of equivalence are established between the positions of heavenly
bodies and parts of the human body, between the structure of the year and the
structure of age, and so on. This leads to the creation of an elementary
semiotic situation: every message must be interpreted, must receive translation
when it is transformed into signs of another level. Since the microcosm of the
human inner world and the macrocosm of the surrounding universe are identified,
any narrative about external events can be perceived as having an intimate and
personal relation to anyone in the audience. Myth always speaks about me.
"News," or anecdote, tells about another. The first organizes the listener's
world; the second adds interesting details to the listener's knowledge of that
world.

The modern plotted text is the fruit of the interaction and interference of
these two typologically primordial kinds of text. Yet the process of their
interaction, if only because in real historical space it stretched over a huge
period of time, could not be simple or univocal.

The destruction of the cyclic-temporal mechanism of texts, or at least the
sharp narrowing of its sphere of functioning, led to a mass translation of
mythological texts into the language of discrete-linear systems. This includes
verbal retellings of myth-rituals and myth-mysteries, and the creation of
those novella-like pseudo-myths that come first to mind when mythology is
mentioned.

The first and most palpable result of such translation was the loss of
isomorphism among levels of the text. As a result, characters from different
layers ceased to be perceived as various names of one person and broke apart
into a multitude of figures. The result was a multiplicity of heroes, which is
impossible in principle in texts of the genuinely mythological type. Since the
transition from cyclical construction to linear construction was connected with
so deep a restructuring of the text that all sorts of variations that occurred
in the course of the historical evolution of plotted literature cease to seem
fundamental by comparison, it becomes not very important what we use to
reconstruct the mythological proto-basis of a text: ancient retellings of myth
or 20th-century novels. Sometimes later texts even provide a more convenient
basis for reconstructions of this kind.

The most obvious result of the linear unfolding of cyclical texts is the
appearance of double characters. From Menander, Alexandrian drama, and Plautus
to Cervantes and Shakespeare, and through Dostoevsky to the novels of the 20th
century, for instance the system of double characters in *Klim Samgin*, there
runs a tendency to supply the hero with a companion-double, and sometimes with
a whole bundle, a paradigm of companions. In one of Shakespeare's comedies we
deal with a square: two twin heroes whose servants are also twins, in *The
Comedy of Errors*.

The pattern is: Antipholus of Ephesus and Antipholus of Syracuse; Dromio of
Ephesus and Dromio of Syracuse. It is obvious that this is a case in which
four characters in a linear text, when translated back into a cyclical system,
would have to "fold up" into one person. The identification of twins, on the
one hand, and of pairs of comic and "noble" doubles, on the other, naturally
leads to this. The appearance of double characters, the result of the breaking
up of a bundle of mutually equivalent names, later became a plot language that
could be interpreted in very different ways in various ideological-artistic
models, from material for the creation of intrigue to contrastive combinations
of characters, or the modeling of the inner complexity of human personality in
Dostoevsky's works.

As an example of the intrigue-forming effect of this process, let us refer to
Shakespeare's comedy *As You Like It*.

The characters of the comedy break down into clearly equivalent pairs, which,
under a conditional reverse translation into cyclical time, mutually fold up
and ultimately form one person. At the head of the list stand two characters:
the brother dukes, one of whom lives "in the forest," while the other rules
after seizing his domain. The characters who are "at court" and "in the
forest" relate to each other according to the principle of complementary
distribution: the movement of one of them from the forest to the court
immediately calls forth the reverse movement of the other. Apparently they
cannot meet in one and the same setting at the same time. And since movement
"into the forest" and return from it is a usual mythological, and later
fairy-tale, formula of dying and resurrection, it is obvious that in
mythological space these doubles will make up a single image.

But the opposition between the two brother dukes is duplicated at another level
by the antithesis of Oliver and Orlando, the older and younger sons of Roland
de Bois. Like the ruling duke, Oliver turns out to be the usurper of his
brother's inheritance and drives him into the forest. The parallel between Duke
Frederick and Oliver is drawn very clearly in the text of the comedy. That the
line separating "court" from "forest" is the boundary beyond which
mythological transformation begins follows from the fact that both villains,
having crossed this line, are instantly transformed into heroes of virtue:
Frederick renounces his plan, the world, and the throne; Oliver likewise
confesses that he was himself, but is now no longer that former self, having
learned the sweetness of repentance.

Thus a square is formed, in which characters placed on one horizontal line are
one and the same hero at different moments of his plot movement, when unfolded
onto the linear scale of the plot, while characters placed on one vertical line
are different projections of one character. The court/forest square is Duke
Frederick and the old duke living in exile; Oliver de Bois and Orlando de Bois.

The parallelism of images is not limited to this. The female characters clearly
represent hypostases of the main heroes: these are the daughters of the two
dukes, Rosalind and Celia, who in a reverse cyclical transformation of the plot
will obviously enter into the single central image as its name-hypostases. The
basic plot division at this level undergoes a significant transformation: both
girls withdraw "into the forest," one by exile and the other voluntarily, but
at the same time they undergo transformations. They disguise themselves,
Rosalind also changes sex by dressing as a boy, and they change names, a
typical detail of mythological reincarnation: Rosalind becomes Ganymede, Celia
becomes Aliena.

A new system of equivalences begins with the tying of the love intrigue. Before
us is a clear system of parallelisms. The double nature of Ganymede as
boy-girl, emphasized also by the ambiguity of the name, creates the basis for
new mythological identifications, which on the level of Shakespeare's text are
perceived as comic confusion. Orlando-Rosalind, Oliver-Celia, Ganymede-Phoebe,
Silvius-Phoebe, Touchstone-Audrey, and William-Audrey all repeat one and the
same situation and the same type of relation at different levels, duplicating
one another. Even the clown is given a double in the form of an even lower
character, the rustic fool. Oliver-Celia is a lowered duplicate of
Orlando-Rosalind; in the invariant scheme the first will be reduced to
Frederick and the second to his exiled brother. The square Ganymede-Phoebe and
Phoebe-Silvius is a lowered variant of all of them, and the square
Touchstone-Audrey and Audrey-William is the same in relation to that second
level. In the end, all the more or less significant characters of the comedy
will be reduced in cyclical space to a single image.

It remains to mention only one character, opposed to all the active persons of
the comedy: the melancholic Jaques. He alone is excluded from the intrigue and
does not return from the forest with the old duke, but remains in that same
space, now beside the voluntarily exiled Frederick. He also possesses the most
sharply expressed character: he is the permanent critic of the human world that
lies outside the forest. Since "court" and "forest" form an asymmetrical space
of the type "earthly world - afterworld" in myth, or "real world -
ideal-fairy-tale world" in Shakespeare, a character of Jaques' type is
necessary in order to give the artistic space orientation. He does not merge
with a character mobile with respect to plot space, but is a personified
spatial category, an embodied relation of one world to another. It is not
accidental that he is the only character who does not move across the boundary
between the worlds of "court" and "forest."

One may suppose that double characters are only the most elementary and
striking product of the linear rephrasing of the hero of a cyclical text. In
fact, the very appearance of different characters is the result of the same
process. As we have already observed, characters divide into mobile ones, free
with respect to plot space, able to change their place in the structure of the
artistic world and cross the boundary, the basic topological feature of this
space, and immobile ones, which are in effect a function of this space.

The initial, typologically primary situation is this: a certain plot space is
divided by one boundary into an inner and an outer sphere, and one character
receives the plot possibility of crossing it. This is replaced by a derivative
and more complex situation. The mobile character is divided into a
bundle-paradigm of different characters of the same kind, while the obstacle,
or boundary, also multiplying quantitatively, singles out a subgroup of
personified obstacles, immobile enemy-characters attached to definite points of
plot space, "villains" in V. Ya. Propp's terminology. As a result, plot space is
"populated" by numerous heroes, variously connected and opposed to one another.
From this follows a partial conclusion: the more noticeably the world of
characters is reduced to singleness, one hero and one obstacle, the closer it
is to the primordial mythological type of structural organization of the text.
From this point of view, lyric poetry, with its reduction of plot to the scheme
"I to he or she" or "I to you," turns out to be the most "mythological" of the
genres of modern verbal art. This supposition is confirmed by other features
as well, for example by the pragmatic properties of mythological texts noted
above. Naturally, lyric poetry is perceived by the reader more deeply and more
naturally than empirical genres as a model of the reader's own personality.

Another fundamental result of the same process was the markedness and the
marked modeling function of the categories of the beginning and the end of the
text.

A text that had separated from ritual and acquired independent verbal existence
automatically acquired a marked beginning and end in its linear arrangement. In
this sense, eschatological texts should be considered the first evidence of the
decomposition of myth and the development of narrative plot.

The elementary sequence of events in myth can be reduced to a chain: entrance
into a closed space, exit from it. This chain is open in both directions and
can be endlessly multiplied. Since closed space can be interpreted as "cave,"
"grave," "house," "woman," and correspondingly endowed with the features of
dark, warm, and moist, entrance into it is interpreted at different levels as
"death," "conception," "return home," and so on, and all these acts are
thought of as mutually identical. The resurrection/birth that follows
death/conception is connected with the fact that birth is thought of not as the
act of the emergence of a new personality that had not existed before, but as
the renewal of one that already existed. To the same extent that conception is
identified with the death of the father, birth is identified with his return.
This is connected, in particular, with the obvious fact that not only
synchronous double characters but also diachronic doubles of the father-son
type represent the division of a single cyclical text-image. The doublehood of
all the Karamazov brothers among themselves, and their common relation to
Fyodor Karamazov according to the scheme degradation-rebirth, complete
identification or contrastive opposition, is convincing evidence of the
stability of this mythological model.

The mythological origin of plot doublehood is obviously connected with the
redistribution of the boundaries of textual segmentation and of the marks of
identity and difference in the central actor.

In cyclical myths growing from this basis, one can determine the order of
events, but one cannot establish the temporal boundaries of the narrative:
every death is followed by rebirth and rejuvenation, followed again by aging
and death. The transition to eschatological narratives gave plot development a
linear form. This immediately translated the text into the categories of
narrative genre familiar to us. Action included in linear temporal movement was
built as a narrative about the gradual decrepitude of the world, the aging of
the god, then his death, dismemberment, torment, eating, burial, the last two
being synonymous as inclusions in closed space, and resurrection, which marked
the destruction of evil and its final eradication. Thus the growth of evil was
connected with the movement of time, and the disappearance of evil with the
annihilation of that movement, with universal and eternal stoppage.

The signs of the destruction of the primordially mythological structure in
this case will also include the collapse of relations of isomorphism. Thus, for
example, the Eucharist, from an action identical with burial, and also with
torment and dismemberment, which was connected on one side with the chewing and
tearing apart of food and on the other was identical, for example, with
tortures in the course of an initiation rite, itself a death into a new
quality, became a sign.

One rudiment of myth in the eschatological legend can be seen in the fact that
the sharply marked end of the text still does not coincide with the biological
end of the hero's life, death. Death, or its equivalents, such as withdrawal
and residence in obscurity, after which a new "appearance" of the hero must
follow, or miraculous sleep in a mysterious place, rock or cave, ending with
awakening and return, is located in the middle of the narrative, not at its
crown. This leads to a passing observation. If one agrees that the
eschatological legend is the product of myth's linear rephrasing typologically
closest to myth, and probably historically the earliest, then one must conclude
that the obligatory happy ending we encounter in the wonder tale is not only
the initial form of narrative with an expressed category of ending, but for a
certain stage the only form, with no structural alternative in the shape of a
tragic ending. The eschatological ending, by its nature, can only be the final
triumph of the good principle and the condemnation and punishment of the evil
one. The "good" and "bad" endings familiar to us are secondary in relation to
it, as realization or non-realization of this primordial scheme.

The category of beginning was not marked to the same degree in the texts of
eschatological legends, although it was expressed by stable openings and
steady situations, connected with the idea of the existence of some ideal
initial state, its later corruption, and its final restoration.

The "beginnings" in culturally peripheral texts of the chronicle type were
much more marked. In the description of an excess, the indication of who
"began first" or "what it all began with" may be perceived by the modern reader
as the establishment of a causal connection. The high modeling role of the
category of "beginning" is manifest in *The Tale of Bygone Years*, which in
essence was a collection of narratives about beginnings: the beginning of the
Russian land, the beginning of princely power, the beginning of the Christian
faith in Rus', and so on. Crime too interests the chronicler first of all from
this point of view. The essence of an event is clarified by pointing to who
first carried out a similar action, as when fratricide is condemned by
reference to Cain. In *The Tale of Igor's Campaign*, the attitude toward Igor's
self-willed campaign is formulated as a reference to Oleg Gorislavich, the
initiator of internecine strife; this is intensified by the fact that Oleg is
also by blood the "beginner" of Igor's line.

The translation of mythological text into linear narrative conditioned the
possibility of mutual influence between two polar kinds of texts: those
describing the lawful course of events, and those describing chance deviation
from that course. This interaction to a significant degree determined the later
fates of narrative genres.

Temporary death as a form of transition from one state into another, higher
one, occurs in an extremely broad circle of texts and rites. Among the latter
one should include the whole complex of initiation rites, and such religious
procedures as tonsure into monasticism, taking the schema, and initiation as a
shaman. As a rule, death here is connected with the tearing apart or cutting up
of the body, the burial or eating of pieces, and subsequent resurrection. V. Ya.
Propp, citing a broad circle of sources, including N. P. Dyrenkova's work on
receiving the shamanic gift according to the beliefs of Turkic peoples, notes
that the sensation of being cut up, sliced, and having one's entrails sorted
through is a necessary condition of shamanism and precedes the moment when a
person becomes a shaman. There too he gives numerous reports that the
appearance of a prophetic gift is preceded by the piercing of the tongue or
ears, the introduction of a snake into the body, and so on.

Since the rites named above have already been examined in a broad mythological
context by Propp, Eliade, and others, it is not especially difficult to
establish their meaningful correlation with the single mythological invariant
"life - death - resurrection or renewal," or, at a more abstract level,
"entrance into a closed space - exit from it." The difficulty lies elsewhere:
in explaining the stability of this scheme even in cases when direct connection
with the world of myth has clearly been broken off. When Pushkin in "The
Prophet" gave an exceptionally precise, detailed, and now richly confirmed
picture of the acquisition of a shamanic, that is, prophetic, gift, down to
such details as the insertion into the mouth of a "small snake" embodying
magical abilities, he did not know the sources available to the modern
ethnographer. Similarly, in order to understand his poem, we do not have to
remember the parallels from the prophet Isaiah and the Quran that probably
served as the nearest sources for the initiation images in "The Prophet."

In order to perceive Pushkin's text, it is no more necessary to know about the
connection of its images with an initiation rite, or a rite of consecration as
a shaman, than it is necessary to know the origin of grammatical categories in
order to use a language. Such knowledge is useful, but it is not the minimal
condition for understanding the text. The hidden mythic-ritual frame has turned
into the grammatically formal basis for constructing a text about the dying of
the "old" human being and the rebirth of the seer.

We find this double process even more visibly in Alberto Moravia's novel
*Disobedience*: on one side, the forgetting of the content side of the
initiation complex to the degree of its complete formalization, and therefore
its transformation into something not consciously felt by the reader, and
perhaps by the author; on the other, the nonetheless characteristic presence of
this complex after it has become unconscious. The action consists in the
transformation of a modern youth into a man. The novel touches on modern
questions of youth rebellion, rejection of the world, and the painful passage
from rebellious egocentrism and the cult of self-destruction to an open
acceptance of life. Yet the movement of the plot is built according to an
ancient scheme. The end of childhood, the end of the first life, is marked by
an ever-growing attraction toward death and by the conscious breaking of the
ties that connect the hero to the world. Rebellion against parents and the
bourgeois world becomes rebellion against life as such. Then comes a long
illness, bringing the hero to the edge of death and serving as an unambiguous
substitute for it. The pages describing the delirium of the dying youth are
equivalent to a "descent into the afterworld" in mythological texts. The first
connection with a woman, his nurse, marks the beginning of return to life, the
transition from nihilism and rebellion to the acceptance of the world, a new
birth. This clearly mythological scheme, reproducing the classical contours of
initiation, is expressively completed by the final image of the novel: the
train on which the recovered youth travels to a mountain sanatorium dives into
the dark hole of a tunnel and bursts out of it into open space. The two ends of
the tunnel correspond very clearly to the ancient mythological idea according
to which one end of the tunnel symbolizes death, the other birth.

Lotman notes that Moravia's conscious orientation toward myth can be seen in
the kind of death imagined by the youth who longs for self-destruction. He does
not imagine suicide, but the tearing apart and eating of his body by wild
beasts. In the novel this is psychologically motivated by stories heard in
childhood, by books about Christian martyrs, and so on; nevertheless, we can
easily sense here one of the universal motifs of death in myth: tearing apart
and eating. Ethnography gives abundant material on the sequence in which
tearing apart is followed by burial in the earth, simultaneously burial and the
sowing of a field, after which comes new and more perfect birth. Moravia's
hero leaves life and the world of childhood, parents, and property in the form
of tearing money apart and burying the scraps in the ground. Thus a very
archaic plot, the overthrow of an old deity, the tearing of the body into
pieces, and the sowing of the earth with those pieces, followed by renewal of
both god and human being and the beginning of a "new life," becomes the
language in which the writer narrates sharply modern collisions.

We have already noted that archaic structures of thinking have lost content in
modern consciousness and, in this respect, can be compared quite well with the
grammatical categories of language, forming the foundations of the syntax of
large narrative blocks of text. Yet as is known, in the artistic text there is
a constant exchange: what in Language has already lost independent semantic
meaning undergoes secondary semanticization, and conversely. In connection with
this, there also occurs a secondary animation of mythological turns of
narrative, which cease to be purely formal organizers of textual sequences and
become overgrown with new meanings, often returning us, consciously or
involuntarily, to myth. The Moravia novel just described gives a revealing
example. The fact that an image suggested by modern technology, the train and
the tunnel, is built as a suggestive expression of one of the most archaic
mythological complexes, transition into a new state as death and new birth, the
chain "death - sexual union - rebirth," entrance into darkness and exit from it
as the invariant model of transformations in general, is deeply indicative of
the mechanism by which the mythological layer is activated in the structure of
modern art.

If we consider the central and peripheral spheres of culture as certain
organized texts, then we can note different types of their inner construction.

The central myth-forming mechanism of culture is organized as a topological
space. When it is projected onto the axis of linear time and from the domain of
ritual-play action into the sphere of verbal text, it undergoes significant
changes: acquiring linearity and discreteness, it receives the traits of a
verbal text built according to the principle of a certain phrase. In this sense
it naturally becomes comparable with purely verbal texts arising on the
periphery of culture. Yet precisely this comparison allows us to discover very
deep differences. The central sphere of culture is built according to the
principle of an integrated structural whole, the phrase. The peripheral sphere
is organized as a cumulative chain formed by the simple attachment of
structurally independent units. Such an organization corresponds most closely
to the function of the first as a structural model of the world, and of the
second as a distinctive archive of excesses.

Each of these groups of texts corresponds to its own conception of the universe
as a whole.

The law-forming center of cultures, genetically going back to the original
mythological core, reconstructs the world as fully ordered, endowed with a
single plot and a highest meaning. Although it is represented by a text or
group of texts, in the general system of culture these act as a normalizing
device located at a metalevel in relation to all the other texts of the given
culture. All the texts of this group are organically connected with one
another, which appears in their ability to fold naturally into a certain single
phrase. Since in content this phrase is connected with eschatological ideas,
the picture of the world generated by this phrase alternates the tragic tension
of plot with final appeasement.

The system of peripheral texts reconstructs a picture of the world in which
chance and disorder dominate. This group of texts is also capable of moving to
a certain metalevel, but it cannot be reduced to any single and organized text.
Since the plot elements that make up this group of texts are excesses and
anomalies, the overall picture of the world appears maximally disorganized. The
negative pole in it will be realized by narratives about various tragic cases,
each of which is a certain violation of order, so that paradoxically the least
probable thing in this world turns out to be the most probable. The positive
pole is manifested by the miracle, the resolution of tragic conflicts in the
least expected and probable way. Yet since the overall ordering of texts is
absent, the beneficent miracle in this group of texts is never final. Thus the
picture of the world created here is, as a rule, chaotic and tragic.

Although with respect to each concrete culture we can single out its relative
orientation toward one or another text-generating mechanism and one or another
group of texts, here we can speak only of self-orientation. The real mechanism
of culture implies the presence of both centers, their mutual tension, and
their influence on each other. Struggling for the dominant position in the
hierarchy of the given culture, each of these groups acts upon its counterpart,
trying to define itself as a text of the highest rank and assigning its
opponent the place of a private manifestation of itself on a lower textual
level. Examples in which ordered texts are placed at the highest structural
level of culture are trivial. In philosophy they can be illustrated by a series
of systems from Plato to Hegel, and in the theory of science, for example, by
Ferdinand de Saussure's conception. The opposite construction is connected, for
example, with Norbert Wiener's picture of the world, with its universal and
advancing entropy, from the point of view of which information is only a chance
and local episode.

When the dying Tyutchev asked that "a little light" be made around him, he
expressed a conviction he had carried through his whole life: that the world is
chaotically disordered, and that light, reason, and law are only local,
accidental, and unstable forms of the "play of disorders," with a
characteristic reference to Pascal. According to Tyutchev, the human being is
located on the boundary between these two hostile worlds, belonging by natural
essence to the world of chaos, and by thought to the logos alien to nature:

And why, then, in the common chorus
Does the soul not sing what the sea sings,
And why does the thinking reed murmur?

The dispute between causally determined and probabilistic approaches in
20th-century theoretical physics is an example of the conflict described above
in the sphere of science.

The dialogic conflict of the two original textual groupings acquires an
entirely new meaning from the moment art arises. This word "moment" is not
given any chronological meaning here, since we can separate the pre-artistic
period of the existence of texts from the artistic period only logically, and
not historically.

In the artistic text it becomes possible to realize the optimal relation
between them, in which the conflicting structures are arranged not
hierarchically, that is, on different levels, but dialogically, on the same
level. Therefore artistic narrative turns out to be the most flexible and
effective modeling device, capable of describing very complex structures and
situations as wholes.

Conflicting systems do not cancel one another; they enter into structural
relations and generate a new type of ordering. We shall try to illustrate how
this type of narrative structure is realized with the particular example of
Dostoevsky's novels, convenient precisely because of their dialogic structure,
which M. M. Bakhtin analyzed deeply. As Bakhtin himself demonstrated, however,
dialogic structure is not the exclusive property of Dostoevsky's novels, but
belongs to the novel form as such. One could say more broadly that it belongs
to artistic texts of certain types. But here we are not interested in the
principle of dialogicity in all its many aspects. We have before us a much
narrower task: to trace the integration, in the narrative form of the novel, of
two opposed plot-forming principles.

In Dostoevsky's novels one can easily isolate two oppositely directed spheres,
as researchers have already noted many times: the domain of everyday action
and the world of ideological conflicts.

The first, the domain of plot development, is itself divided into the world of
everyday events and the sphere of the detective-criminal plot.

It has long been observed that everyday events in Dostoevsky develop according
to the "logic of scandals," whose natural formal expression is the connection
between episodes by means of the little word "suddenly." Developing this
observation, one could say that events of the everyday series follow one
another in Dostoevsky's narrative according to the law of least probability.
Relying on everyday experience, the reader forms in consciousness certain
expected possibilities, some of which are evaluated as very probable, others as
merely possible, and still others as barely probable or entirely improbable.
Encountering one or another event in the text of the novel, the reader
naturally applies this scale of expectations to it. Onto this scale is of
course layered another, conditioned by the reader's literary experience as a
consumer of artistic texts: a subconscious expectation may distinguish what is
most probable in life from what is most probable in an artistic text of a given
type. This allows the reader to construct the most probable next link of plot
development. In Dostoevsky's text, what the reader least expects, that is, what
is least probable by the laws both of life experience and of literary
construction, turns out to be the only thing possible for the author.

Consider, for example, the chapters "The Lame Woman" and "The Wise Serpent" in
*Demons*. The initial situation is already built as a violation of the most
probable: Stepan Trofimovich, invited to Varvara Petrovna's for an important
and confidential conversation, arrives and finds no one there. At the same
time, another strange event occurs: in church, an unknown and strangely
behaving woman approaches Varvara Petrovna. She will later turn out to be
Maryia Timofeevna Lebyadkina. Varvara Petrovna, contrary to common sense and
to the essence of her character, invites her home. Liza, behaving in an
entirely inexplicable way, intervenes and against everything forces Varvara
Petrovna to take her along as well.

Stepan Trofimovich and the narrator expect Varvara Petrovna alone, but hear
the noise of many steps, which is already somewhat strange. Someone seems to
enter with a strange quickness, and we receive a special warning that Varvara
Petrovna could not enter in such a way. Precisely for this reason, the person
entering turns out to be Varvara Petrovna. The young women follow behind her,
somewhat delayed and much more quietly.

Then comes Maryia Timofeevna's strange and scandalous behavior. As soon as
this incident is exhausted, Praskovya Ivanovna, Liza's mother, unexpectedly
appears, and between her and Varvara Petrovna there occurs a scene at once
scandalous and unexpected: the meek and downtrodden Praskovya Ivanovna behaves
aggressively. The scene ends with Varvara Petrovna's fainting and
reconciliation. Then a new person appears, Darya Pavlovna. The conversation,
however, is not about the marriage proposal to her by Stepan Trofimovich, for
the sake of which he had been invited, since contrary to probability everyone
has forgotten about this, but about something quite different. A new
complication is introduced: Darya Pavlovna reports that, at Nikolai
Vsevolodovich's request, she gave money to Captain Lebyadkin, thereby revealing
some mysterious relations among people whose very acquaintance with each other
had seemed improbable.

Next the appearance of Captain Lebyadkin himself is reported, and, contrary to
the assertions of those present that he is not the sort of person who can enter
society and that inviting him is impossible, he is invited and enters. At the
same time Varvara Petrovna sends Liza out of the room, saying in effect that
Liza especially has nothing to do there; naturally, Liza stays. Captain
Lebyadkin's appearance is, on one side, a new link in the chain of absurdities,
and on the other unexpected by its insufficient ugliness: he is decently and
even smartly dressed, and he is not drunk. His behavior turns out to be
improper for him precisely because it is not ugly enough.

When the bell is rung to have Lebyadkin led out, the servant who enters instead
announces that Nikolai Vsevolodovich has just arrived and is coming in. After
this, not Nikolai Vsevolodovich Stavrogin but an unknown young man appears,
who turns out to be the son of Stepan Trofimovich. Then Nikolai Vsevolodovich
appears, still on the threshold when Varvara Petrovna asks him the most
unexpected question: whether this unfortunate, lame woman is his lawful wife.
Nikolai Vsevolodovich says nothing, respectfully kisses his mother's hand, and
gently leads Maryia Timofeevna out of the room. In his absence, Pyotr
Stepanovich "explains" Nikolai Vsevolodovich's behavior in the best possible
sense. The intrusive desire of this gentleman who has suddenly fallen from the
sky to tell other people's anecdotes is itself strange and outside ordinary
manners.

Captain Lebyadkin, terrorized by Pyotr Stepanovich, is disgracefully driven
out, and a true apotheosis of Nikolai Vsevolodovich begins. But then there is
an unexpected hysterical scene with Liza. No sooner is she calmed than Pyotr
Stepanovich makes an unexpected revelation, as a result of which his father is
disgracefully driven out. Then, unexpectedly, Shatov, who has been sitting
silently in the corner the whole time, strikes Nikolai Vsevolodovich in the
face, and Liza faints.

It is enough to glance over this list of episodes to be convinced that their
sequence is not conditioned by any inner connection. A completely chance,
atomic sequence of separate isolated condensations of action is emphasized by
the fact that in a whole series of cases predictability does in fact exist, but
in inverted form: episodes follow one another not in the order of greatest
probability, but in the order of least probability.

There is a meaningful difference between the unexpectedness of episodes at the
everyday level and at the detective level. In the detective story, the
disconnectedness and randomness of sequences is only apparent. It exists for
the reader, who does not know the secret of the plot and who until a certain
time takes the unimportant for the essential and conversely. Since the reader
must be kept in this ignorance as long as possible, the erroneousness of the
reader's suppositions is concealed. The false development is given the most
logical and externally convincing appearance. The lack of connection among
individual episodes in this case shows through only now and then, in order to
hint at the false character of the connections accepted by the reader.

There is such a subterranean logic of criminal action in *Demons*, including
in the episode cited above. A certain part of the events only seems to be an
accumulation of absurd coincidences, and the disclosure of secret crimes will
bring logic and organization into their sequence. But this cannot be said of
the entire chain of episodes in these chapters. For most of them, the
absurdity and chance character of their linkage remain just that. Moreover, if
in the detective story the absurdity or incorrectness of the false connections
established by one who does not know the hidden springs of the action is
concealed until a certain moment, then in the passage that interests us, as in
others like it, Dostoevsky carefully warns us, as if afraid the reader will not
notice the principle of the text's construction. It is "a day of
unexpectedness"; everything is resolved in a way no one would have supposed.

Each of the levels singled out has its own syntagmatic organization, proper
only to it, and this secures the complexity of their interrelations.

With regard to the ideological core of Dostoevsky's novels, it has already
been said that they directly organize the plot movement of the text. Still more
important is Bakhtin's indication that monological construction, the natural
result of the linear unfolding of myth into a normalizing text, is replaced in
Dostoevsky's nuclear structure by dialogue. Dostoevsky's ideas, once they
enter his polyphonic novel, change the very form of their being: they are freed
from monological closure and completion, are dialogized throughout, and enter
the great dialogue of the novel.

Thus the ideological core absorbs the structural features of peripheral texts.
At the same time the opposite process also occurs, whose character is clearly
visible in Dostoevsky's typical depiction of the everyday layer as a chain of
scandals and ugly incidents. One might think that the everyday layer in
Dostoevsky, saturated with chance and with violations of every possible lawful
expectation, is the embodiment of irrationality, the "sinfulness" of the
material world. This is so and not so, because unpredictability and even
absurdity in Dostoevsky are features not only of scandal, but also of miracle.
Both poles, marking final destruction and final salvation, have the common
feature of being unmotivated and non-lawlike. Thus the eschatological moment
of the instantaneous and final resolution of all the tragic contradictions of
life is not brought into life from outside, from the realm of ideas, but is
found in its own thickness.

The model of such a fusion of "scandal" and "miracle," demonstrating their
kindred nature, is the card game or roulette.

On one side, it embodies the ugly essence of ugly life: "Today was a ridiculous,
ugly, absurd day." On the other, it embodies the eschatological miracle of the
resolution of all conflicts. At the center of *The Gambler* is the thirst for
miracle. The win is a miraculous event. Though it is completely justified by
arithmetic, for the hero it remains miraculous. It is repeatedly emphasized
that the matter is not money, but the thirst for instantaneous and final
salvation. It is not accidental that the win is connected with a purely
mythological idea of resurrection, the end of the old, sinful life and the
beginning of an entirely new existence: what am I now? Zero. What can I be
tomorrow? Tomorrow I can rise from the dead and begin to live again. Again: to
be reborn, to be resurrected.

In the same sense it is significant that Astley calls roulette a supremely
Russian game, and that the opening antithesis is between German gradualism and
the Russian striving for instantaneous ruin, wasteful and ugly, or
instantaneous salvation, miracle: to grow rich suddenly, in two hours, without
labor. In this sense, *The Gambler* already contains Raskolnikov, with his
striving instantly to perish or instantly to save everyone. But Sonya too
brings Raskolnikov the miracle of instantaneous salvation of the soul.

Thus if dialogism is the penetration of the manifoldness of life into the
ordering sphere of theory, mythologism at the same time penetrates into the
domain of excess.

Dostoevsky's novels are a vivid illustration of what can be considered a
general property of narrative artistic texts.

We have seen how, as a result of the linear unfolding of mythological text, an
originally single character divides into pairs and groups. Yet the opposite
process also takes place. The reason is that after the categories of beginning
and end have been singled out as a result of translation into a linear system,
the identification of these concepts with the biological boundaries of human
existence is a relatively late phenomenon. In the eschatological legend and in
texts isomorphic with it, the segmentation of human existence into continuous
segments can be carried out in ways quite unexpected for present-day
consciousness. Thus, for example, the isomorphism of burial or eating with
conception, and of birth with rebirth, can lead to the fact that a narrative
about the fate of the hero may begin with his death, while birth-rebirth falls
in the middle of the story.

A complete eschatological cycle consists of the hero's existence, as a rule
not beginning with birth; his aging, corruption, falling into the sin of
incorrect behavior, or an original defect, for instance that the hero is ugly,
a fool, or sick; then death; rebirth; and a new, already ideal existence, which
as a rule ends not with death but with apotheosis. This is perceived as a
narrative about a single character. The fact that in the middle of the story
there is death, change of name, complete change of character, and a diametrical
reevaluation of behavior, so that an extreme sinner becomes an extreme
righteous person, does not make one see here a story about two heroes, as
would be characteristic of a modern narrator.

An example is the famous episode from the *Acts of the Apostles*. The story of
Saul-Paul begins not with the hero's birth, but with mention of him as a
participant in the execution of the first martyr, Stephen. Later it is reported
that he, breathing threats and murder against the disciples of the Lord, was a
zealous persecutor of Christians. On the road to Damascus, a light from heaven
suddenly shone around him. He heard a voice from above, lost his sight, and
then, when he miraculously regained it, became the Lord's "chosen vessel" and
began to be called Paul.

This narrative is exceptionally remarkable as an ideal realization of the
scheme: birth and death do not frame the hero's story, but are placed in its
middle, for the event on the Damascus road is of course death, and the rebirth
that follows it is birth. The change of name is not accidental. At the ends of
the narrative we do not find such boundaries: it begins not with birth and ends
not with death. No less interesting is something else: from the point of view
of such criteria as the "unity of action" of the classical epoch or the "logic
of character" in a realist text, there are no grounds for identifying Saul and
Paul as one character. Yet in the text mentioned, these are not two characters
who existed successively, but one person.

Under the influence of mythic-legendary tradition, this scheme of character
construction penetrates later literary works as well, becoming the language in
which texts about "illumination" or a sudden change in the hero's essence are
realized. Such, for example, are fairy tales with their transformation of the
fool into a tsar: the journey into the forest, to Baba Yaga, the expression
"he climbed in through one ear, came out through the other, and became a fine
young man," and other such elements all basically mean death and resurrection.
We find a direct transfer of this scheme into later works in narratives about
great sinners who become righteous, for instance Andrew of Crete or Pope
Gregory. A vivid example is Nekrasov's "Vlas."

At the beginning of the poem the hero is a great sinner: people say that once
there was no God in the peasant; he drove his wife into the grave with
beatings and sheltered robbers and horse thieves. Then comes illness. The
expressive picture of hell testifies that in this case the illness is
functionally equal to death: people say that in delirium he kept seeing the
end of the world and sinners in hell, tormented by nimble demons. The return
to life brings about the hero's complete rebirth: Vlas gives away his estate,
is left barefoot and naked, and, filled with inconsolable sorrow, walks slowly
through villages and towns.

The critical character of the moment of rebirth, equal to death, is often
emphasized by giving the hero a double. As we have already noted, the essence
of the double is a single character that has split in two. This double does not
resurrect or become young again, but dies. We find such episodes in a number of
texts, from the myth of Medea, with the magical rejuvenation of a ram subjected
to dismemberment and the death of King Pelias under the same procedure, to the
ending of Yershov's *The Little Humpbacked Horse*. Ivan dives into the cauldron
and comes out transformed, while the tsar, trying to repeat the procedure,
plunges in and is boiled there.

The scheme "fall - rebirth" is widely represented in modern literature as
well. For example, it organizes a number of Pushkin's lyric poems, such as
"Rebirth." Let us recall the well-known verses of Mikhalevich in Turgenev's
*A Nest of the Gentry*: he gives himself with all his heart to new feelings,
becomes like a child in soul, burns everything he had worshiped, and worships
everything he had burned.

Before us is a character made up of two directly opposed parts, the transition
from one to the other being thought of as renewal. Childhood falls not at the
beginning, but in the middle of the temporal development of the image: "like a
child in soul I became." Tolstoy's *Resurrection* is built according to the
same scheme. For all the difference among the concrete historical ideas
transmitted with the help of this plot mechanism, the recurrence of titles such
as "Rebirth" and "Resurrection" cannot be accidental.

The fact that the everyday identification of literary character and human
being was superimposed on the scheme of the eschatological legend led to the
possibility of modeling the inner world of a human being on the pattern of the
macrocosm, and of interpreting one person as a conflictually organized
collective.

Plot is a powerful means of making sense of life. Only as a result of the
emergence of narrative forms of art did the human being learn to distinguish
the plot aspect of reality: to divide the non-discrete stream of events into
certain discrete units, to connect them with meanings, that is, to interpret
them semantically, and to organize them into ordered chains, that is, to
interpret them syntagmatically. The isolation of events, the discrete units of
plot, and their endowment with definite meaning, on one side, and with a
definite temporal, causal, or some other ordering, on the other, constitute the
essence of plot.

The more human behavior acquires features of freedom in relation to the
automatism of genetic programs, the more important it becomes for the human
being to construct plots of events and behaviors. But in order to construct
such schemas and models, one must possess some language. This role is
performed by the original language of artistic plot, which later becomes
constantly more complex, moving very far from the elementary schemes to which
we have drawn attention in this chapter. Like every language, the language of
plot, in order to transmit and model some content, must be separated from that
content. Models that arose in the archaic epoch are separated from concrete
messages, but they can serve as material for the textual construction of those
messages. At the same time, it should be remembered that in art, language and
text constantly change places and functions.

By creating plotted texts, the human being learned to distinguish plots in
life and, in this way, to interpret this life.
