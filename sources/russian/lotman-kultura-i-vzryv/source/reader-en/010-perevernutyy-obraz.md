## The Inverted Image

In the space that lies beyond the limits of the norm, founded on the norm and violating the norm, we encounter a whole range of possibilities: from deformity, the destruction of the norm, to a fullness of positive qualities located above the norm. In both cases, however, what is at issue is not the impoverishment of the norm, its simplification and freezing, but "life overflowing the brim," as Ludwig Tieck called one of his tales.

One of the most elementary devices for going beyond the limits of predictability is a trope, especially frequent in the visual arts, in which two opposed objects exchange dominant features. This device was widely used in the extensive Baroque literature of the "inverted world." In numerous texts, the sheep ate the wolf, the horse rode on the human being, the blind led the sighted. These inverted plots were as a rule used in satirical texts. Swift's entire poetics is built on them, for example the transposition of people and horses.

The rearrangement of elements while preserving the same set of elements usually outrages a stereotyped audience with particular force. This is precisely the device most often disparaged as indecent. A satirical journal of the end of the last century, for example, in the comedy *Madame Europe's Sister*, gave a rather stereotyped caricature of decadence:

We are priests of the inaccessible, the strange.
Of all smells, the smell of a dead rat is dearest to me;
Of sounds, I value the croaking of toads;
And among women, those are loveliest to me who are bald.

Yet in this case what interests us is not so much works of art, where the very possibility of recombination is predetermined by the freedom of fantasy, as facts of travesty in everyday behavior.

The inverted world is built on the dynamics of the nondynamic. The everyday realization of such a process is fashion, which also introduces a dynamic principle into the seemingly immobile spheres of everyday life. In societies where types of clothing are strictly subordinated to tradition or dictated by calendar changes, and in any case do not depend on linear dynamics and the arbitrariness of human will, there can be expensive and cheap clothing, but there is no fashion. More than that, in such a society, the more highly a garment is valued, the more it is preserved, and conversely, the longer it is preserved, the more highly it is valued. Such, for example, is the relation to the ritual garments of heads of state and church hierarchs.

The regular change of fashion is a sign of a dynamic social structure. More than that, fashion itself, with its constant epithets "capricious," "changeable," and "strange," emphasizing the unmotivated and seemingly arbitrary character of its movement, becomes a certain metronome of cultural development.

The accelerated character of fashion's movement is connected with the strengthened role of the initiative-bearing personality in the process of movement. In the cultural space of clothing there is a constant struggle between the striving for stability and immobility, a striving psychologically experienced as justified by tradition, habit, morality, and historical or religious considerations, and the opposite orientation toward novelty and extravagance, everything that enters into the idea of fashion.

Thus fashion becomes, as it were, the visible embodiment of unmotivated novelty. This allows it to be interpreted both as an area of ugly caprice and as a sphere of innovative creativity. An obligatory element of fashion is extravagance. This is not refuted by the periodically arising fashion for traditionality, since in this case traditionality itself is an extravagant form of denying extravagance.

To include a certain element in the space of fashion means to make it noticeable and to endow it with significance. Fashion is always semiotic. Inclusion in fashion is a continuous process of turning the insignificant into the significant. The semioticity of fashion appears, in particular, in the fact that it always implies an observer. One who speaks the language of fashion is the creator of new information, unexpected for the audience and incomprehensible to it. The audience must not understand fashion and must be outraged by it. In this lies fashion's triumph. Another form of triumph is incomprehension joined with indignation.

In this sense, fashion is simultaneously elite and mass. Its elitism consists in the fact that it is not understood, but fashion's triumph lies in its opposition to the crowd. Outside a shocked public, fashion loses its meaning. Therefore the psychological aspect of fashion is connected with the fear of being unnoticed and consequently feeds not on self-confidence but on doubt in one's own value.

Behind Byron's fashionable innovation lies insecurity. The opposite tendency, the fashionable refusal of fashion, was realized by Chaadaev: "the coldness of calm pride," in Pushkin's phrase. P. Ya. Chaadaev can serve as an example of refined fashion. His dandyism consisted not in the striving to chase fashion but in the firm conviction that he possessed the right to establish it. The sphere of extravagance in his clothing lay in the daring absence of extravagance.

If Denis Davydov, adapting his clothing to the demands of the "national" mood of 1812, put on a peasant caftan, began to grow a beard, hung an icon of Saint Nicholas instead of the Order of Saint Anna, and spoke to the peasants in the people's language, Chaadaev emphasized the extraordinary nature of the situation by wholly refusing any extraordinariness in dress. He did not recognize that field conditions and the hardships of campaign life permitted some freedom in clothing, that the demand for the snow-white cleanliness of a collar on the battlefield was not as strict as in a ballroom, or that face, gesture, and gait under grapeshot had the right to differ from relaxed movements in high society. A demonstrative refusal of everything that constituted the romantic *couleur locale* of campaign life gave Chaadaev's behavior, under campaign conditions and enemy fire, the character of external extravagance.

Fashion, like all other forms of behavior lying beyond the everyday norm, implies constant experimental testing of the boundaries of the permitted.

The sphere of unpredictability is a complex dynamic reservoir in any process of development. In this connection, a specific phenomenon of Russian culture, *samodurstvo*, is of interest. A modern academic dictionary explains *samodur* with Dobrolyubov's phrase: "The samodur constantly tries to prove that no one is an authority over him and that whatever he wants, he will do." Yet this explanation explains little and does not clarify the historical-cultural meaning of the concept.

The word *samodur* is internally contradictory etymologically. Its first part, the pronoun *sam*, "self," carries the meaning of hypertrophied personality, as in autocracy, self-rule, self-love, self-development, self-sufficiency. The second part is connected with the semantics of stupidity. The very joining of these two semantic elements is an oxymoron, a semantic contradiction. It can be interpreted as the joining of initiative and stupidity, on the one hand, and initiative and madness, on the other, since the Russian word *durnoi* in everyday usage can mean both.

The joining of these two semantic groups can generate two different semantic shades. On the one hand, it can mean the self-assertion of stupidity. Then it is the union of stability and stupidity. The fool performs extravagant acts in order to test the boundaries of his power and the strength of these boundaries. A classic example of such a samodur is Ostrovsky's Bruskov, Kit Kitych, with the formula characteristic of heroes of this type: "If I want, I eat it with kasha; if I want, with buttered buttermilk."

On the other hand, the behavior of the samodur can be realized as meaningless and boundless innovation, the violation of stability for the sake of violation itself. The act is unpredictable, like the behavior of the romantic madman, among whose actions there is no causal connection. From this point of view, explosion can be defined as the dynamics of a motionlessly frozen system.

A similar effect can be produced by excessively slowed mobility that gives no outlet to dynamic impulses. Samodurstvo is akin to *yurodstvo*, the behavior of the holy fool, and the latter reveals something in it. The word *yurodivyi*, like *samodur*, is untranslatable into European languages. Yet Russian holy fools included a significant place for arrivals from the West, including Germans. Thus in the very fact of holy foolishness a contradiction is laid down: the intervention of a stranger, often a foreigner and always one who comes "not from this world," into the most intimate depths of Russian religious everyday life.

The holy fool is characterized by the joining of extreme humiliation and extreme authority. He rejects all social signs, but precisely by this rejection acquires a special sign power. He can insult the tsar, violate rules, behave indecently, and at the same time be understood as the bearer of a higher truth. His madness is not stupidity but a semiotically marked excess, an inversion of all ordinary social relations.

The same logic of inversion appears in another large complex of material: the exchange of gender signs. A special place here belongs to the chevalier d'Eon, whose biography turned the change of male and female image into a social and political sign. Constantly changing the image of man and woman, d'Eon at the end of life behaved quite strangely. Whether confused about his own sex or wishing to embody both extremes in himself, he, while excellently skilled with the sword, became in London, after losing all possible adventurous sources of income, a fencing teacher, conducting these martial lessons in women's clothing.

As a mirror reflection of such behavior, one could name cases in which women appropriate male clothing. It is characteristic that a woman's dressing in male clothing, her transformation into a man, was connected with her appropriation of martial behavior. The tragic role such clothing played in the fate of Joan of Arc is well known. Dressing in male clothing, to which Joan was forced by violence and deception, became one of the chief pieces of evidence in the trial against her, because it was evaluated as a blasphemous violation of the distribution of roles given by God.

Connected with this, in particular, is the medieval idea, which lasted long afterward, of the sinfulness of the actor's profession. The very fact of arbitrary change of costume seemed suspicious to a consciousness that did not separate expression from content. From this point of view, a change of appearance was perceived as equivalent to a perversion of essence. Still more forbidden was the scenic change of sex. The widespread prohibition against women appearing on stage at all, as a result of which the performer of a female role was doomed to cross-dressing, created an inescapably sinful situation for the stage.

The appearance of the woman actor did not remove the problem but gave it an even more refined character. The bearer of sin became not the change of sex but the change of its sign: the actress's dressing as a queen and the actor's dressing as a king turned not only the stage but also extra-stage reality into a world of signs. Hence the countless number of plots about the blurring of boundaries between stage and life.

It was not only the stage that provoked exchange in the sign functions of clothing. Palace coups in eighteenth-century Russia, usually headed by women, were accompanied by the ritual dressing of the pretender to the throne in a costume that was, first, male, second, Guards, and third, specifically that of the regiment playing the leading role in the coup. Male clothing and riding on horseback became obligatory accessories of the ritual of transforming the pretender into empress.

On the eve of the December uprising, Ryleev and Bestuzhev wrote in an agitational song about how in Russia tsars are ruled and strangled, how Peter's corporals quietly escorted him from the courtyard, while his wife rode bravely on horseback before the palace. The picture drawn by the poets is thoroughly mythological: the coup occurred on the road to Peterhof, not in Petersburg, and the episode in which Catherine accompanied the troops on horseback should be considered apocryphal. But for us this only increases the value of the poetic text, which turns a historical episode into myth.

In reality, this mythologized picture corresponded to the pretender's dressing in a male Guards costume. The ritual character of the gesture is obvious. In Dashkova's description, the beginning of the coup is marked by dressing in men's clothing: "Without losing time I put on a man's woolen cloak." Catherine's very declaration of herself as empress appears as a chain of dressings. Dashkova notices that the empress is still wearing the ribbon of Saint Catherine and not the blue ribbon of the Order of Saint Andrew. She asks Count Panin to remove his order and then places it on the empress's shoulders. The phrase "placed it on her shoulders" is full of meaning. It signifies not simple dressing but ritual elevation to dignity. By this Dashkova hints that Catherine owes the throne to her.

Dashkova continues: they set off for Peterhof at the head of the troops. The empress borrowed a uniform from Captain Talyzin, while Dashkova took one from Lieutenant Pushkin, both Guards officers being approximately their height. This sequence turns clothing into a ritual of power.

Fonvizin, in his satirical *Universal Court Grammar*, demonstratively separated "female character" and "female sex": "What is court gender? There is a difference between a male and female soul. This difference does not depend on sex, for at court sometimes a woman is worth a man, and some man is worse than a woman." Shcherbatov, speaking of Elizabeth, likewise creates an effect by combining masculine grammatical forms with female details of clothing, then later returning her to grammatical feminine gender when he shifts style.

Examples showing that female emancipation in the nineteenth century practically meant appropriating the role and clothing of a man include George Sand, who joined appropriation of a male pseudonym with dressing in men's clothing, and the famous cavalry maiden N. A. Durova. In the notes admired by Pushkin, Durova describes her "transformation into a man," the choice not only of male clothing but of a male destiny, as the acquisition of freedom: "So I am free! Freedom! Independence! I have taken what is mine, what belongs to me, my freedom: freedom! precious gift of heaven, inalienably belonging to every human being! I knew how to take it, to protect it from all claims for the future, and from now until the grave it will be both my lot and my reward!"

A woman's dressing in male clothing could have another meaning if it was accompanied by a man's dressing in female clothing. This can be considered one variant of emancipation. If male clothing merely emphasized the piquant features of the appearance of a young and slender beauty, giving them a certain shade of ambiguity, then a man's dressing in women's clothing invariably lowered him in rank, turning him into a comic hero. Hence numerous cases of forced dressing of men in women's clothing in eighteenth-century novels and comedies, and Pushkin's comic "Mavrushka" in *The Little House in Kolomna*.

Among the papers of Catherine II there is a plan for an entertainment intended for a narrow circle of visitors to her Hermitage. In the spirit of the French "inverted ballets" of the late sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries, women were to dress in male costumes and men in female ones. The appearance of the giant Orlov brothers or one-eyed Potemkin in women's clothing was supposed to provoke laughter, but also comically lowered their social role.

The preservation by a woman of the "female role" could also acquire the meaning of extravagance. In this case, however, some special sociocultural position was required, sharply falling out of average female stereotypes. For Russia in the first half of the nineteenth century, two polar examples are Anna Alekseevna Orlova-Chesmenskaya and Sofya Dmitrievna Ponomareva.

Anna Orlova-Chesmenskaya was the only daughter of Count Alexei Orlov, a Catherine-era grandee who had risen with his brothers during the coup that overthrew Peter III and who participated in the murder of the former emperor. Her early childhood passed in her father's house. Alexei Orlov's fabulous wealth, open and truly lordly style of life, and adherence to popular everyday culture combined in a tense and contrasting atmosphere. Anna received a good education, freely knew three European languages, and was taught high-society behavior by foreign governesses.

The death of Catherine and the accession of Paul I made the Orlovs' position disgraced and even dangerous. Alexei Orlov and his daughter went abroad because it was safer. Pushkin later recorded a conversation revealing the mood of the Orlov brothers: Orlov was a regicide by vocation; it was like a bad habit. Speaking of Paul I, he asks why the monster is tolerated. When asked what should be done, surely one should not strangle him, he answers, "Why not?" Asked whether he would agree to his daughter Anna being involved in such a matter, he answers not only that he would agree, but that he would be very glad.

Life in her father's house proceeded in a highly tense and contrasting atmosphere. According to her biographer, she often fled from the noisy festivities in her father's house and was found praying in church. Yet other evidence corrects this somewhat icon-like memoir style. At her father's wish and for the pleasure of guests, she danced with a shawl and tambourine, danced Cossack, gypsy, Russian dances, and more. Two servant girls performed in her place the figures considered insufficiently proper for a young countess, while guests formed a reverent circle around her.

After her father's death, the countess did not wish to tolerate guardians over herself, which quarreled her with her uncle, the youngest of the Orlov brothers, and she began the extravagant life of a young independent woman without a male protector. The extravagance to which her unheard-of wealth entitled her did not frighten her. She rejected suitors, and after one love affair tragic for her, apparently decided to remain unmarried. Probably an Orlov trait appeared here: the striving for boundless freedom.

She buried her father, then led a life moderate in expenditures and giving no ground for gossip. The best testimony to this is the silence about her in contemporary reports. Her unhappy love, whose details are almost unknown to us, and perhaps the Orlov family inability to obey anyone, caused her decision never to marry. Yet she shows no mystical impulses at this time. Even her pilgrimages to holy places were carried out with great comfort, as a whole convoy with carriages, kitchen, provisions, and hay carts.

At the same time, honors and ranks followed one another for her. In 1817 she was made a chamber-maid of honor; Alexander I granted her the empress's portrait with diamonds; under Nicholas she received the Order of Saint Catherine; in 1828 she accompanied the empress on a long journey through Russia and Europe. But all this was the upper, display side of life. Having everything, the countess apparently secretly dreamed of abandoning everything. Possessing boundless freedom, she thirsted for boundless obedience. Orlov blood demanded unlimited maximalism. Half-measures and worldly prudence did not attract her. Orlova sought a harsh guide. Behind this maximalism appears a thirst for a feat. In another epoch she might have found a completely different, perhaps rebellious, expression for her uncompromisingness.

As her spiritual protector she chose the hieromonk Amphilochius. He soon died, and after this Orlova's paths crossed with those of Photius.

Photius, born Petr Nikolaevich Spassky in 1792, came from a poor family. His father, a church clerk, beat him terribly. In seminary, where he studied badly, he was beaten too. After seminary he entered the St. Petersburg Academy but studied poorly and after a year was forced to leave it. He became a teacher in the Arkhangelsk religious school. It seemed that fate had assigned him the role of a spiritual Akaky Akakievich, an eternal laborer, a poor and tormented failure. But circumstances turned otherwise.

In seminary he met and won the trust of Archimandrite Innokenty, a harsh ascetic. Photius later admitted that he carefully noted all of Innokenty's words, actions, appearances, and deeds. In 1817 he took monastic vows and was appointed priest in the Second Cadet Corps. The corps educated children from a social world previously closed to Photius. The inaccessible became accessible.

Photius's further behavior was provocatively extravagant. Perhaps he really did experience mystical visions, driving himself by ascetic behavior, chains, and fasting to the highest nervous tension. But it is also indisputable that some of his mystical experiences smell of crude pretense, and most importantly, that he used them with great skill and cunning. His ambition was boundless. Once in Petersburg, with the help of Golitsyn, against whom he was at the same time secretly digging, he gained access to the sovereign and managed to make a strong impression on the emperor.

Thereafter Photius joined the behavior of fanatic and intriguer. Skillfully changing face, he relied on various forces. Among court ladies he appeared in the mask of a harsh denouncer, an ascetic, flaunting coarseness and playing the role of mystical intermediary with the Lord himself. His coarseness sharply distinguished him from the other hierarchs of Alexander's epoch. He even allowed himself denunciations of such sharpness that they created for him an aura of apostolic simplicity. His actions influenced the emperor. He was soon admitted to Alexander as an intimate interlocutor and skillfully used this.

Yet Photius's "boldness," including the episode that brought him fame, his proclamation of anathema against his superior and then all-powerful patron Golitsyn, covered subtle calculation. It was no accident that he chose the Novgorod Yuriev Monastery. Once again he became Arakcheev's closest neighbor. The monastery became, as it were, the spiritual fortress of the military settlements. It is difficult to say whether Arakcheev used Photius to bring down the hated Golitsyn or Photius used Arakcheev to strengthen his own position, but it is obvious that they formed a close alliance to mutual advantage.

Such was the man whose influence turned Countess Orlova-Chesmenskaya in the 1820s almost into a slave. She became an obedient executor of his power and sacrificed enormous sums to his monastery. Photius displayed personal disinterestedness and apostolic severity of everyday life, but diligently enriched the monastery. Orlov self-will, pride, and thirst for boundless scope psychologically grounded the idea of saving the soul as voluntary slavery. It was violence against herself.

Society circulated rumors of a love affair between Orlova and Photius. More important for us is that the mystical refusal of the sin of self-will ultimately led the countess into the hands of Photius, and through him into the network of Arakcheev and Alexander's political theater. Photius could acquire such meaning only because he played Arakcheev's cards. At the same time, one should not submit to the consciously imposed illusion. Many historians' conviction that in these years Alexander handed everything over to Arakcheev and himself sank into mystical fog is only partly correct and, more importantly, coincides with the emperor's stable tactic: hiding behind the back of the latest "favorite," on whose head all public dissatisfaction falls.

Behind Golitsyn's fall, behind Arakcheev's back, the profile of Alexander himself is clearly visible. Metternich thought he led the tsar by the nose, Photius thought he deceived Alexander with mystical spectacles, everyone in Russia thought Arakcheev really governed the country, and all believed the tsar played the most passive role in politics. One may suppose that Alexander, as always hiding behind substitute figures, held power in his own hands until the last minute. Anna Orlova was only a small step in this grand phantasmagoric design.

Another path by which a talented woman of that time could manifest her individuality led to extravagance of behavior. One possible route was the literary salon. Salon culture flourished in France in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. It was in principle unofficial and nonofficial; in this sense it stood opposed to the Academy. In Madame de Rambouillet's salon, political and literary unofficiality merged. This tradition passed into the philosophical salon of the Enlightenment.

The model of the philosophical salon was built as a gathering of celebrities, skillfully and tastefully selected so that excessive unanimity would not destroy the possibility of discussion, while the discussions remained dialogues of friends or at least comrades-in-arms. The art of intellectual conversation is cultivated in such a salon as an elegant play of minds, joining enlightenment and elitism. The sun of all these planets is a lady, the owner of the salon. As a rule, the mistress of the salon belongs to an age that excludes romantic infatuation with her. In social origin she is usually higher than her admirers. In her is embodied the world in which the philosophers are immersed and which they energetically undermine.

From this plot grew the famous legend of Cazotte, who supposedly prophesied to all the participants of a salon what the philosophical future they dreamed of would be. In prophetic inspiration he told them of the still uninvented guillotine and of the Terror awaiting them all. The whole episode is of course legendary and retrospectively composed. It can be compared with Repin's painting *What Freedom!*, where a student and a female student stand on an ice floe being flooded by waves and joyfully greet the beginning of the ice drift and the boundless space opening before them.

The salon in Russia of the 1820s was a peculiar phenomenon, oriented toward the Parisian salon of the prerevolutionary epoch and at the same time significantly different from it. As in Paris, the salon is a kind of solar system revolving around a chosen lady. But if in the French salon it was only an exception for the hostess to be at the same time a charming woman bringing a gallant coloration into the life of the salon, in the Russian salon this became obligatory. The hostess of the salon joins sharpness of mind and artistic giftedness with beauty and attractiveness. The salon's visitors are bound to her less by the ties that joined the encyclopedists in one or another salon than by a collective service of knights to a chosen lady.

Such were the salons of Sofya Ponomareva in 1820-1823 and Zinaida Volkonskaya in the second half of the 1820s.

V. E. Vatsuro studied Sofya Dmitrievna Ponomareva's salon, and his analysis reveals the "novel of life" of this remarkable woman, who raised everyday life to the level of art. We can call Ponomareva's salon a work of art because it represented something unrepeatable: it copied nothing and could have no continuation.

In extensive poetic reflections, Sofya Ponomareva is characterized first of all as unexpected in her behavior, unpredictable and capricious. "O willful Sofya!" Baratynsky wrote. The many poems given to her by admirers, and later epitaphs dedicated to her, steadily repeat the image of the woman-child. Her pose is that of a playful child playing with life. Gnedich wrote that she bloomed and shone and was the joy of eyes; she played childishly with life. Delvig wrote that she played with earthly life like a child with a toy and soon broke it.

The "childlike behavior" of an adult woman asserts infantilism as an individual norm recognized only in this exceptional case. This is what is permitted "only to her," what cannot be imitated and is inaccessible to repetition. The uniqueness of this infantilism is determined by the fact that it is fused with unique talent and female attractiveness. This woman-child, unpredictable, appears alternately in a feminine and a childlike hypostasis. Her admirers painfully try to distinguish whether they are dealing with a capricious child who naively puts herself into ambiguous situations without suspecting their ambiguity, or a passionate woman throwing herself into infatuations like a moth into fire.

The situation of unpredictability is supplemented by the fact that the heroine of this novel possesses a sharp mind and a sharp tongue. Her life woven of surprises is crowned, with the compositional completeness of a work of art, by unexpected death at the very height of youth and success. If Sofya Ponomareva's life is poetry realized in behavior, then the creative participation in it of poets turned this poetic life into poetic texts. Passing through the ordeal in the flame of Ponomareva's salon, and then in Izmailov's literary society, these texts settled onto the pages of journals and almanacs, gradually turning into facts of literary history.

A different atmosphere reigned in Zinaida Volkonskaya's salon, which flourished in Moscow in the mid-1820s. Born into the family of Prince Beloselsky-Belozersky, known for frivolity and literary amateurism, given from youth the most refined education, speaking and writing in five languages, the rich and carefree Princess Volkonskaya joined the style of European salons with a light shade of bohemianism and unconcealed political independence. Even the fact that later, in her Roman palace, she placed near marble antique fragments dedicated to Pushkin and Venevitinov a marble bust of Alexander I was not without a frondeur shade. The respectful gesture toward the dead emperor emphasized opposition to the reigning one. Nicholas I forced the princess to leave Russia, punishing her for her "seductive" conversion to Catholicism and probably also for her demonstratively emphasized oppositional stance.

Volkonskaya's salon did not have a political character, but an island on which an artificial atmosphere of the cult of beauty was created acquired, against the background of Nicholas's order, an unexpectedly nonneutral character. Pushkin's later words about true freedom are relevant here: not depending on authorities or the people, giving account to no one, serving and pleasing only oneself, not bending conscience or thoughts or neck before power or livery, wandering here and there by one's own whim, marveling at divine beauties of nature and trembling joyfully before creations of art and inspiration. "Here is happiness, here are rights."

The direction of Volkonskaya's "aestheticizing" independence appeared in her farewell to Maria Volkonskaya, who was going to Siberia after her husband sentenced to hard labor. One must recall that Volkonsky's mother, a lady-in-waiting of the dowager empress, disowned her sentenced son and at the same time went to Moscow to participate in the celebrations for Nicholas I's coronation. Against this background, Zinaida Nikolaevna organized in Moscow a noisy, demonstrative farewell for her daughter-in-law, inviting famous Italian musicians and singers and the flower of intellectual society, including Pushkin.

Maria Volkonskaya wrote that in Moscow she stayed with Zinaida Volkonskaya, who received her with tenderness and kindness she would never forget. Knowing her passion for music, Zinaida invited all the Italian singers then in Moscow and several talented young women. The beautiful Italian singing delighted her, and the thought that she was hearing it for the last time made it still more beautiful. She kept saying, "More, more, only think, I shall never hear music again!" Pushkin was there too.

The celebration arranged by Zinaida Volkonskaya for her daughter-in-law was not a sign of political sympathy with the Decembrists. It demonstrated something else: the independence of art from power. But in the conditions then existing, apoliticism became a political position.

The idea of Volkonskaya's salon as a "magical island" where admirers of the arts of different views and talents gathered is reflected in Myasoedov's famous painting of the assembly of "priests of the arts." In memoirs of Mickiewicz, Vyazemsky wrote that in Moscow Princess Zinaida Volkonskaya's house was an elegant gathering place for all the remarkable and select personalities of contemporary society. Representatives of high society, dignitaries and beauties, youth and mature age, people of intellectual labor, professors, writers, journalists, poets, and artists all came together there. Everything in this house bore the stamp of service to art and thought.

Volkonskaya's salon had, as societies of this type should, its own legend. This was the legend of Venevitinov's unrequited love for the salon's hostess, a love tragically interrupted by the young genius's death. Such a legend was an obligatory adornment of the salon atmosphere. Yet overall, the unifying force of the gathering around Zinaida Volkonskaya was not erotic feeling, but worship of art. Emphatic aestheticism gave Volkonskaya's salon a somewhat cold character. Between Ponomareva and Volkonskaya there is a telling difference in social position and whole atmosphere, yet the kinship of their opposition to everyday reality is all the more striking. In both salons we see an attempt to break out "above the vulgar."

Another feminine path that also led to rising "above shameful life," in Pasternak's phrase, was the path society considered reprehensible. In poetry, for example under Lermontov's pen, this created the romantic image of the public woman. In everyday reality it lost its romantic coloration and returned to literature only as the image of the "humiliated and insulted," socially interpreted by Nekrasov and religiously by Dostoevsky.

The romanticized revolt of a woman in Russia of the first half of the nineteenth century was realized in the image of the romantic heroine. In the language of romanticism, "proprieties" were translated as "conventions," and revolt against them had two faces: poetic freedom in literature and erotic freedom in real life. The first "naturally" took on male behavior and was embodied on paper, the second in "female" behavior and was realized in everyday life. Such is the dual nature of the image of Zakrevskaya.

In Pushkin's verse she appears with her blazing soul and stormy passions, among northern women moving past all the conditions of society like a lawless comet in the calculated circle of stars. In the poetic world, Zakrevskaya's conversations were reflected in Pushkin's "Confidant," where the intoxicating language of mad and rebellious passions frightened the poet with its unbridledness. Yet in male behavior, however sincere the passion, this was still literature. The relations between literature and life in the male behavior of that time were complex and made possible many different interpretations.

The opposite of "artistic" and "romantic-erotic" female behavior in Russia at the beginning of the nineteenth century was high-society *comme il faut*, whose main feature was social facelessness. Two strikingly close descriptions can be cited, one drawing an everyday picture and the other belonging to literature. Both recreate high society. Their similarity is dictated by the fact that at this level originality is equated with impropriety.

The first example belongs to A. F. Tyutcheva's memoirs and describes the salon of Sofya Karamzina. During Karamzin's life, the salon of Ekaterina Andreevna and Nikolai Mikhailovich Karamzin had been one of the cultural centers of Petersburg in the 1810s, with Decembrist youth gathering just above it in Nikita Muravyov's study. Now, in the 1830s, the picture has changed sharply, and this is all the more noticeable because the salon's hostess is convinced that she is preserving her father's tradition. Sofya Karamzina's salon is deprived of its intellectual cultural center. It is a well-adjusted machine of faceless social communication.

Before the beginning of an evening, Sofya, like an experienced general on the battlefield and a learned strategist, arranged large red armchairs with light straw chairs between them, creating comfortable groups for conversation. She knew how to arrange things so that each guest naturally and as if by chance found himself in the group or beside the neighbor best suited to him. She had, in this respect, a real organizational genius. Tyutcheva sees her fluttering like a zealous bee from one group of guests to another, joining some, separating others, catching a witty word, an anecdote, noting a pretty toilette, organizing cards for the old and games of wit for the young, entering into conversation with some lonely mother, encouraging a shy debutante, in a word, raising the art of social management to the degree of art and almost virtue.

The picture in Tyutcheva's memoirs so resembles a scene from Tolstoy's *War and Peace* that it is difficult to avoid the thought that Tolstoy had access to the then unpublished memoirs. The emotional evaluation in Tolstoy's novel is directly opposite, but this only emphasizes the similarity of the picture itself. The root of this similarity lies in a principled orientation toward nonoriginality as the highest salon ideal. Tolstoy describes Anna Pavlovna's evening as a machine set in motion, with conversational spindles humming evenly and without ceasing, and the hostess serving her guests first the vicomte and then the abbe like delicacies plated by a good headwaiter.

The romantic and the high-society salons embody two opposite points of female high-society behavior of that epoch. If in the romantic epoch freedom for a woman was thought as feminine freedom, the democratic rise of the second half of the nineteenth century emphasized in woman the human being. In vulgarized everyday behavior, this was expressed as a striving to conquer men's hairstyles, men's professions, men's gestures, and men's manner of speech. The experiment of appropriating male clothing, carried out by George Sand, still seemed too extravagant.

This opposition was realized as a struggle between fashionable high-society ball dress, for example the crinoline, and the modest clothing of the "working woman." In Aleksei K. Tolstoy's satires, the stereotyped language of nihilists is imitated, that is, the instantaneous transformation of explosion into stamp.

As we see, the long period of female emancipation, which passed under the slogan of equalizing women's and men's rights, was in fact interpreted by society as a woman's right to occupy "male" social roles and professions. Only by the second half of the nineteenth century did the question take on another meaning. The struggle was now not for becoming a "man," but for man and woman to be perceived as equivalent within the single concept "human being." For this it was necessary that at least one woman prove her superiority in spheres traditionally considered a male monopoly.

In this sense, not only for Russian but for European cultural history as a whole, the appearance of figures such as Sofya Vasilievna Kovalevskaya was a truly historical turning point, comparable only with the equalization of the roles of men and women in the political struggle of the People's Will. Kovalevskaya's receiving the academic degree of professor in 1884 and, a year later, appointment to the chair of mechanics at Stockholm University can be compared only with Alexander III's government's equalizing Sofya Perovskaya with the male members of the People's Will in the right to be executed. Woman ceased to receive either scientific or political discounts. In the latter case Alexander III unexpectedly appeared as a Robespierre, proving that the guillotine knows no gallant difference between man and woman.

The Korvin-Krukovsky sisters, Anna Jaclard and Sofya Kovalevskaya by marriage, realized both possibilities as if in pair. Anna rejected Dostoevsky's love; he asked for her hand and was refused, while her chosen one became a Paris Communard who escaped a death sentence only by fleeing from behind bars. Her younger sister, Sofya Kovalevskaya, was precisely the kind of personality that matures at a moment of sharp revolution in culture. It is not accidental that the Renaissance produced people of many-sided giftedness whose talents could not fit into any one sphere of culture, or that the next explosion, at the turn of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, called forth the encyclopedists in France and Lomonosov in Russia. Explosion generates many-sided talents. In the cultural function of woman, such an explosion occurred at the end of the nineteenth century. It produced Sofya Kovalevskaya: mathematician, professor and teacher, writer creating literary works in several languages, attractive precisely through the many-sidedness of her cultural activity.

The professor's chair and the Petersburg gallows marked the absolute equalization of man and woman. Only for the first one had to go to Stockholm, while the second was carried out at home. Equality was affirmed. Now woman no longer had to pretend to be capable of playing the role of man. This produced an unheard-of effect: the beginning of the twentieth century poured into Russian poetry a whole constellation of brilliant women poets. Both Akhmatova and Tsvetaeva not only did not conceal the feminine nature of their muse, they emphasized it. And nevertheless their poems were not "women's" poems. They appeared in literature not as poetesses but as poets.

The summit of women's conquest of a universal human position is not the Valkyries of "women's rights" and not preachers of lesbianism, but women displacing men in politics and state activity, "Lady Thatcher" in politics and twentieth-century science. Yet the brightest, almost symbolic picture appears before us in the opposition of Marina Tsvetaeva and Boris Pasternak. The point is not even that in their personal relations Tsvetaeva displays courage while Pasternak displays "femininity." More important is how this romance, which remained epistolary and literary, was reflected in poetry.

In Tsvetaeva's lyric, in its hypertrophied passion, the female "I" receives traditionally male features: an offensive impulse, the perception of poetry as labor and craft, courage. The man is assigned an auxiliary and nonpoetic role. Tsvetaeva's attributes are the "masculine sleeve" and the writing desk. It is hard to find other poems in which femininity is drenched with such contempt. Against this poetry, Pasternak's principled femininity acquires contrastive meaning. The femininity of his poetry appears not even in plot but in principled responsiveness and passivity. This poetry does not take, rule, or impose; it gives itself over to the elemental and suprapersonal, whose victory lies only in being "defeated by all." Tsvetaeva breaks into the world with her language and is blind to everything that is not her emanation. Pasternak absorbs the world into himself. Tsvetaeva is immersed in "her own"; Pasternak, like Doctor Zhivago, is always constant because he always dissolves in what belongs to another.

This does not exclude the fact that later in Russia the very idea of equality between man and woman turned into a form of oppression of woman, because natural difference was sacrificed to an unrealizable utopia that in practice became exploitation.

The problem that has interested us, as we have seen, is realized at the boundary of physiology and semiotics, constantly shifting its center of gravity now to one sphere, now to the other. As a semiotic problem it cannot be artificially separated from other sociocultural languages, in particular from the conflict between physiological and cultural aspects.

In Japan this led to the opposition between the woman for the family and the geisha, the woman for pleasure. In European medieval life among crowned persons, it led to the legalized antithesis between the wife who continues the lineage and the mistress who grants pleasure. The separation of these two cultural functions generated, in a whole series of cases, the antithesis of "normal" and homosexual love.

The spread of homosexuality in Petersburg officers' schools most likely had a physiological root, since it was connected with the isolation of a large number of adolescents and young men. But it acquired a different character in cases where homosexual love turned into a particular regimental tradition. What in an everyday perspective can be considered a vice becomes, in a semiotic perspective, a sign of social ritual. In the Nicholas era, homosexuality was the ritual vice of the Chevalier Guards, just as unrestrained drunkenness was among the hussars.

Thus, for example, the scandalous intimacy of Dantes, a royalist emigre, a handsome man without a penny, gathering in himself all the vices of the old aristocracy scattered over foreign lands, elegant, always cheerful, and frivolous, would have been impossible in the orderly and morally strict atmosphere of official Petersburg society if it had not belonged to the ritual vice of the Chevalier Guards. In official Petersburg it was customary to pretend that this "defect" simply did not exist. Unofficially, ritualized vices were perceived as signs of initiation. In this case homosexuality gave a refinement of *fin de siecle*. French emigres brought the direct breath of "end of the century" into Petersburg salons long before the word "decadence" sounded in Russian criticism.
