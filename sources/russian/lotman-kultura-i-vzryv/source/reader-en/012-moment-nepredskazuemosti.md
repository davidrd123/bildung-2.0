## The Moment of Unpredictability

The moment of explosion is the moment of unpredictability. Unpredictability should not be understood as a limitless and wholly undetermined possibility of transition from one state to another. Every moment of explosion has its own set of equally probable possibilities for transition into the next state; beyond that set lie changes that are manifestly impossible, and these we exclude from consideration. Whenever we speak of unpredictability, we mean a definite set of equally probable possibilities, of which only one is realized.

At the same time, every structural position represents a set of variants. Up to a certain point they appear as indistinguishable synonyms. But movement away from the site of explosion separates them more and more in semantic space. Finally there comes a moment when they become bearers of semantic difference. As a result, the total set of semantic distinctions is continually enriched by new shades of meaning. This process, however, is regulated by an opposite drive: the tendency to limit differentiation by turning cultural antonyms into synonyms.

In Pushkin's description of the possible futures of Lensky, as we noted earlier, the reader is placed before a whole bundle of potential trajectories for the further development of events at the moment when Onegin and Lensky draw near, raising their pistols. At that moment the next state cannot be predicted unambiguously. One must also take into account that Lensky, as a student of a German university, should have been a very good shot. An analogous situation occurs in the duel between Pushkin and Dantes.

Where the participants in a duel do not intend merely to exchange a pair of shots in order afterward to reconcile, having fulfilled the ritual of honor, but are firmly resolved to carry the duel through to the death of the opponent, the natural tactic of an experienced duelist is not to hurry with the first shot, especially while walking. Shooting while walking more than halves the chance of hitting the opponent, especially if the duelist has a definite intention: to hit the legs or shoulder in order to wound heavily but not kill, or to hit the head or chest in order to bring the opponent down on the spot.

Therefore an experienced duelist, meaning a cool and calculating one, allows the opponent to shoot first while walking. After that he approaches the barrier, summons the opponent to the barrier, and can decide his fate almost unerringly at his own discretion. Such was Pushkin's tactic. He had firmly resolved to kill or heavily wound Dantes, for only in that way could he tear apart the net that had entangled him. After such an outcome he faced exile to Mikhailovskoe; he was not a military man and therefore could not be reduced to the ranks, and rural exile could only be preceded by church penance. Natalia Nikolaevna would naturally have had to go with him. But this was exactly what Pushkin so much desired. Dantes had left him no other choice.

Dantes, frivolous and socially debauched, despising his opponent as the ugly husband of a beautiful woman, a traditionally comic figure, saw in the duel one more entertaining event. He probably did not imagine that he would have to abandon his pleasant, cheerful Petersburg life, which was connected with rapid promotion. Pushkin, like Lensky, did not hurry with the first shot. But if Onegin fired first and from a greater distance because he evidently did not desire a bloody outcome, then Dantes's tactic was different. As an experienced duelist, he guessed Pushkin's tactic and fired first in order to forestall his opponent's shot, hoping to kill him with a bullet while walking. Even an excellent marksman, as Dantes apparently was, firing while walking under Pushkin's aimed barrel, hit his opponent not in the chest but in the stomach, which did not exclude the possibility of a severe but nonfatal wound. Such an outcome suited neither participant in the duel.

These reflections were needed so that, following Pushkin, we might think about which potential possibilities were not realized at the moment when Onegin's bullet was still in the barrel of the pistol. Pushkin strives to make us perceive the tragic outcome of the duel against the background of unrealized possibilities that nevertheless could have become realized. But Onegin did fire: "the appointed hour had struck." In the novel, Lensky's death was predetermined by the poet's design. In lived reality, however, a predetermined future does not exist at the moment of the shot. What exists is a bundle of equally probable "futures." Which of them will be realized cannot be predicted in advance.

Chance is the intervention of an event from some other system. One cannot exclude, for example, that Dantes or Onegin, at the moment when his finger pressed the trigger, might have slipped on trampled snow, causing a slight and perhaps completely invisible tremor of the hand. The killer's bullet would have flown past. Then the other bullet, from the pistol of the excellent marksman Pushkin, or the former German student Lensky, would have been murderously accurate. Lensky would have had to mourn his beloved friend, and Pushkin's life would have followed other, unpredictable paths.

Thus the moment of explosion creates an unpredictable situation.

Arguments of the type "Pushkin was doomed, and if not Dantes's bullet then some other situation would have led him to the same tragic death, and therefore this example cannot be considered chance" rest on an incorrect substitution of phenomena. If Pushkin's fate is considered on the scale of the last two years of his life, then it appears generally predictable and cannot be called accidental. But we must not forget that in doing this we have changed scale and thereby changed the object under consideration. On that scale we are evaluating a much larger series, in which the event will indeed be perceived as predictable. If, however, we are considering the separate event, the duel, the character of the predictable and unpredictable changes sharply. One can say that Pushkin in Petersburg in the 1830s was doomed. One cannot say that at the moment when he took the pistol in his hand he was already doomed. One and the same event, depending on the series into which it is inserted, can change its degree of predictability.

Next a very curious process occurs: the completed event casts a retrospective gaze backward. In doing so, the character of what has happened is decisively transformed. It must be emphasized that a view from the past into the future and a view from the future into the past decisively change the observed object. Looking from the past into the future, we see the present as a set of whole rows of equally probable possibilities. Looking into the past, what is real for us acquires the status of fact, and we are inclined to see in it something uniquely possible. Unrealized possibilities become, for us, things that fatally could not have been realized. They acquire an ephemeral quality.

The foundation of Hegelian philosophy, for example, is built on this. Pasternak made an error in quotation, attributing to Hegel a statement by A. Schlegel, but the inaccuracy itself is highly revealing:

Once Hegel, by accident
And probably at random,
Called the historian a prophet
Who predicts backward.

The witty statement that attracted Pasternak's attention does in fact reflect very deeply the foundations of Hegel's conception and Hegel's relation to history. The retrospective gaze allows the historian to consider the past as if from two points of view. Being in the future in relation to the event described, he sees before him the whole chain of actions that actually took place. But transferring himself mentally into the past and looking from the past into the future, he already knows the results of the process. Those results, however, are presented as if they had not yet occurred and are offered to the reader as predictions. In the course of this process, chance disappears entirely from history.

The historian's position may be compared with that of a theater spectator who is seeing a play for the second time. On the one hand, he knows how it will end, and there is nothing unpredictable in the plot for him. For him the play is, as it were, in the past tense, from which he extracts knowledge of the plot. At the same time, however, as a spectator looking at the stage, he is in the present tense and lives through again the feeling of uncertainty, his supposed "not knowing" of how the play will end. These mutually exclusive experiences paradoxically merge into a certain simultaneous feeling.

Thus the completed event appears in multilayered illumination. On the one hand, it carries the memory of the explosion just lived through; on the other, it acquires the features of inevitable predestination. Psychologically connected with the latter is the drive to return once more to what has happened and subject it to "correction" in one's own memory or retelling. In this sense we should pause over the psychological basis of memoir-writing, and more broadly over the psychological grounding of historical texts.

The causes that impel a culture to re-create its own past are complex and diverse. Here we will stop only at one, perhaps less often noticed. It is the psychological need to remake the past, to introduce corrections into it, and at the same time to experience this corrected process as true reality. Thus we are speaking about the transformation of memory.

There are many well-known anecdotal stories about liars and fantasists who fooled their listeners. If this is viewed from the perspective of the cultural-psychological motivation of such behavior, it can be interpreted as a doubling of the event and its translation into the language of memory, not in order to fix reality there, but in order to correct that reality into a more acceptable form. Such a tendency is inseparable from the very concept of memory and, as a rule, from what is incorrectly called the subjective selection of facts. In some cases, however, this department of memory becomes hypertrophied.

An example can be found in the well-known memoirs of the Decembrist D. I. Zavalishin. Dmitry Irinarkhovich Zavalishin lived a tragic life. He was undoubtedly a talented man, possessing varied knowledge that set him apart even against the background of the Decembrists. In 1819 he brilliantly completed the cadet corps, made a round-the-world sea voyage, and early attracted the attention of his superiors by his gifts, particularly in mathematics. It would have seemed that a successful career in the ranks lay open before him. He was the son of a general, but he had neither especially firm connections nor wealth. Education and talent nevertheless opened the most optimistic prospects before him.

But Zavalishin possessed a quality that completely changed his fate. He was a liar. Pushkin once remarked that "a tendency to lie does not prevent sincerity and frankness," at least in childhood. In this sense Zavalishin remained a child his whole life. Although his life began brightly enough, even that was not enough for him. Compared with the flight of fantasy, it was dull and uninteresting. He adorned it with lies. He sent Alexander I a letter in which he drew before the sovereign a picture of the organization of a worldwide monarchic conspiracy, while at the same time projecting the creation of a vast colony with its capital on the western coast of North America.

Shortly before the Decembrist uprising, Zavalishin obtained some information about the existence of a secret society and tried to enter it, but Ryleev did not trust him and blocked his penetration into the Decembrist circle. Whether Zavalishin was admitted to the secret society remains disputed. At any rate, he took no part in the real life of the Northern Society. This did not prevent him, at his own risk, from drawing several young men into "his" fantastic society, which he represented as a powerful and extremely resolute organization. One can imagine with what rapture he painted before his listeners completely fantastic pictures of a decisive and bloody conspiracy.

His boasting did not go unpunished. Despite his practically insignificant participation in the Decembrist movement, Zavalishin was sentenced as one of the most dangerous conspirators, in the first category, to life imprisonment. Fantastic projects did not leave him even in penal servitude. In his memoirs he narrates now a split among the exiled Decembrists into democrats, with himself of course at their head, and aristocrats; now an attempted escape from Siberia through China to the Pacific Ocean. It is possible that conversations of this sort may have arisen among the exiled Decembrists, though their realization naturally remained in the realm of fantasy. In Zavalishin's consciousness, however, this became a thought-out, carefully prepared plan that only accidentally did not take place.

The summit of his fantasy is the memoirs written at the end of his life. Zavalishin "remembers" not his tragic, failure-filled life as it actually was, but the brilliant life of his imagination, made up solely of successes. All his life he is surrounded by enthusiasm and recognition. His stories of childhood, such as the episode with Bernadotte, vividly recall General Ivolgin's story in Dostoevsky's *The Idiot* about his meeting with Napoleon. Having entered the secret society, according to his own stories, Zavalishin immediately became head of the organization. He "remembers" fantastic scenes of numerous stormy secret meetings where members gathered only to "listen to Zavalishin." Ryleev envies him. Ryleev's administration in Petersburg is in a miserable condition, while he, Zavalishin, has managed to organize major underground centers in provincial cities. His trip to Simbirsk on the eve of the uprising is described as an inspection mission from the secret society to verify the province's preparation for revolt. Even on the approaches to Simbirsk he is met by jubilant conspirators who report to him on their activity.

At the same time, Zavalishin's memoirs are an extremely valuable source not only for studying his psychology but also for researching the political history of Decembrism. One only has to introduce corrections that make it possible to calculate reality. The study of such monuments of lying is interesting not only from a psychological point of view. Karamzin once wrote of poetry:

Who is a poet? A skillful liar.
Glory and crown belong to him.

And elsewhere:

Lie, untruth, phantom of truth,
Be now my goddess.

This "phantom of truth" is especially important. Such a formula builds a bridge from Zavalishin's lie to poetry.

Karamzin's expression "phantom of truth" joins concepts that would seem to be opposite. First, lie is joined with truth; second, it turns out not to be truth itself but its phantom, that is, an apparent doubling. Already this unexpected link between truth and lie forces us to think about two questions: is lie only evil, and if it performs some essential function beyond people's inclination to deceive one another, then what is that function?

Animals, when hunting or defending themselves, can employ tactics of deception. But lie is alien to them, that is, unmotivated and disinterested untruth. Gogol, in creating the figure of Khlestakov, produced a whole poem of lying as pure art, lying that takes pleasure in itself and revels in its own poetry. Khlestakov can use the results of his lie for gain, and Osip does this much better, but he does not lie for the sake of greed. He lies because he has "extraordinary lightness in his thoughts." His thoughts possess the capacity to tear themselves away from reality and form their own world, limited and controlled by nothing. Lying gives Khlestakov a certain degree of freedom, raising him above the paltry reality of a minor Petersburg official. The unexpected connection between lying and freedom forces us to take it seriously.

In Pushkin's epoch, contemporaries preserved the memory of great liars as masters of a special art. At the beginning of the nineteenth century, Prince M. Tsitsianov was a famous liar who entered the legends of his time. P. A. Vyazemsky recalled that during a downpour Tsitsianov appeared at a friend's house. Asked whether he had come by carriage, he answered that he had come on foot. Asked how he had not got wet at all, he replied that he knew how to move very deftly between the drops of rain. Tsitsianov also told how in his village a peasant woman, after a long pregnancy, gave birth to a seven-year-old boy whose first words at the hour of birth were, "Give me vodka!" Vyazemsky saw in this a special "poetry" of narration.

Pushkin entered into his table talk the image of a liar whom he compared with Shakespeare's Falstaff. The liar's four-year-old son, a little Falstaff himself, supposedly repeated in his father's absence: "How brave papa is! How the sovereign loves papa!" When the child was asked who told him this, he answered: "Papa."

In *The Idiot*, Dostoevsky depicts an unusual scene. The liar and fantasist General Ivolgin shamelessly invents the story of his imaginary closeness with Napoleon, a story not only improbable but absolutely impossible. Prince Myshkin, Ivolgin's interlocutor, understands that this is a lie and is inwardly tormented by shame for him and sympathy toward him. At the same time, he sees in the story some special unreal reality.

Myshkin quietly says that all this is extremely interesting, if it really happened. The general, intoxicated by his own story, cries out that there was much more than these miserable political facts; he alone witnessed the nocturnal tears and groans of the great man. He tells how Napoleon, abandoned by everyone, recognized that this child pitied him, and how both wept together. Myshkin answers: "You did beautifully; among evil thoughts you led him toward a good feeling."

Thus lying appears not only as a distorted handling of true reality, but also as a fully independent and free sphere of speech. This connects it with the quite extensive space of "speaking for the sake of speaking," a kind of "pure art." It is not difficult to notice that speech with the purpose of information by no means occupies the whole space of our speaking. A significant part of speech has a self-sufficient character. This deserves reflection.

I have happened to observe something that rarely attracts the attention of natural scientists: the sleeping state of animals. Once I noticed that my dog, who several days earlier on a walk had had to chase a hare for quite a long time, though the hare escaped safely, reproduced in sleep, in a thin sleeping voice, the kind of bark emitted by a hound chasing a hare. At the same time, with her paws she repeated not the gestures of rapid running but their unmistakable playful imitation. One could suppose that she was living through in sleep a repetition of the chase she had experienced while awake. It was precisely a repetition: an imitation including not only similarities but also differences.

In oral culture, enormous layers of information were preserved and passed on from generation to generation. This depended both on a refined collective culture and on the singling out of individual "geniuses" of memory. Writing made a significant part of this culture unnecessary. This was especially evident in epistolary and diary culture. In relatively recent times, the progress of oral forms of communication, the telephone and radio, brought about the degradation of epistolary culture. In the same way, the dynamics of the spread of newspapers and radio simplify and lead to the degradation of whole areas of traditional culture.

The displacement of dream culture from the sphere of information storage was more than compensated by the separation of word from reality. Speaking constituted a closed, self-directed, and completely independent domain. The possibility of various forms of unreal speaking separated vulgar lying from the flexible mechanism of human consciousness.
