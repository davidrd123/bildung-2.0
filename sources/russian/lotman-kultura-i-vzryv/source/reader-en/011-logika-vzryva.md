## The Logic of Explosion

We are immersed in the space of language. Even in the most basic conventional abstractions, we cannot break out of this space, which not only wraps around us but is also part of us, just as we are part of it. At the same time, our relations with language are far from idyllic. We make enormous efforts to break beyond its limits; we attribute lies, deviations from naturalness, and most of our vices and perversions precisely to language. Attempts to struggle against language are as ancient as language itself. History convinces us, on the one hand, of their hopelessness and, on the other, of their inexhaustibility.

One of the foundations of the semiosphere is its heterogeneity. On the temporal axis, as already noted, there coexist subsystems whose cyclic movements have different speeds. In our period, European women's fashion may have an annual turnover, while the phonological structure of language changes so slowly that everyday consciousness tends to perceive it as unchanging. Many systems collide with others and, while in motion, change their appearance and their orbits. Semiological space is filled with freely moving fragments of various structures; yet these fragments stably preserve within themselves the memory of a whole, and when they enter foreign spaces they may suddenly and violently restore themselves. Semiotic systems, colliding within the semiosphere, reveal an ability to survive and transform themselves and, like Proteus, to become other while remaining themselves. For that reason, one should speak with great caution about the complete disappearance of anything in this space.

It appears that there are no completely stable, unchanging semiotic structures at all. If such a hypothesis were admitted, then one would also have to recognize, at least in purely theoretical terms, the finitude of their possible combinations. Even the phonological system, one of the most stable, can be enriched by the crossing of closed systems; eighteenth-century Russian dandy speech, for example, admitted French nasal vowels. If semiotic closure were absolute, then Lermontov's ironic epigraph, "Poets resemble bears, who feed themselves by sucking their own paws," would have to be taken literally.

It must be emphasized, however, that the boundary separating the closed world of semiosis from extra-semiotic reality is permeable. It is constantly crossed by incursions from the extra-semiotic sphere. Bursting in, these incursions bring dynamics with them and transform the space itself, while at the same time they are themselves transformed according to its laws. Semiotic space also constantly expels whole strata of culture. They form layers of sediment beyond culture and wait for their hour to break into it again, forgotten enough to be perceived as new. Exchange with the extra-semiotic sphere forms an inexhaustible reservoir of dynamics.

This "perpetual motion" cannot be exhausted. It does not submit to the laws of entropy, because it constantly re-creates its diversity, nourished by the system's lack of closure.

Yet this source of diversity would turn into a generator of chaos if counter-directed structures did not become involved.

An essential difference between contemporary structural analysis and formalism, as well as the early stage of structural research, lies in the very delimitation of the object of analysis. The cornerstone of those earlier schools was the idea of a separate, isolated, stable, self-sufficient text. The text was the constant, the beginning, and the end of the investigation. The concept of text was in essence a priori.

Contemporary semiotic research also treats the text as one of its basic starting concepts. But the text itself is not conceived as a stable object possessing permanent features; it is conceived as a function. An individual work can function as a text, but so can a part of it, a compositional group, a genre, and in the final account literature as a whole. The point is not merely that the concept of text is widened. The difference is much more fundamental. The presumption of a creator and an audience is introduced into the concept of text, and these two may not coincide in scope.

Thus, not long ago, a heated but quite fruitless argument broke out in Soviet literary studies about so-called "canonical" editions of texts. It is characteristic that the supporters of this emptied-out idea were those Moscow literary scholars who were more successful in administrative than scholarly spheres. A negative attitude toward it was taken by the experienced textual scholar and consistently sober-minded researcher B. V. Tomashevsky.

The contemporary point of view rests on the idea of the text as the intersection of the viewpoints of the creator of the text and its audience. A third component is the presence of definite structural features perceived as signals of text. The intersection of these three elements creates optimal conditions for perceiving an object as a text. But a sharply expressed presence of some of these elements may be accompanied by the reduction of others.

From the author's position, for example, a text may appear unfinished, still in a dynamic state, while an external point of view, that of the reader, publisher, or editor, will tend to attribute finishedness to it. On this basis arise numerous conflicts between author and publisher. Leo Tolstoy refused to regard proof sheets as finished texts, seeing in them only a stage of a continuous process. For him the text in fact meant process. Pushkin began to revise *The Bronze Horseman*, but after completing half of a labor forced by censorship and yet transformed into an artistic process, he abandoned the work. Thus one of Pushkin's most important works lacks the so-called "final authorial will." The publisher, oriented toward readers, is forced into compromise, joining two different stages of revision. The research position gives us a text caught in the moment of becoming. Final authorial will turns out to be a fiction.

M. O. Chudakova's studies revealed the multi-level process of Bulgakov's work on *The Master and Margarita*. That process also did not receive final completion. Here too we face a conflict between the research, or authorial, point of view and the publishing, or readerly, point of view. This contradiction, lying at the very foundation of the question, creates the necessity for different types of edition. An academic edition differs not simply by the authority of its researchers or the splendor of its design, but by a principled orientation toward the writer's perception.

The contemporary perception of the text as an element within an artistic process implies an internal contradictoriness in the basic research position.

One of the basic questions posed by the text can be described as follows. A number of European languages have the category of articles, which divide names into those immersed in a delimited world of things personally familiar and intimate to the speaker, and those belonging to an abstract, general world reflected in the national language. The absence of articles in Russian does not mean the absence of this category. It is simply expressed by other means. One can say that the world of a young child, extremely restricted by the spatial sphere of personal experience, is filled with unique things. In language this is reflected by the dominance of proper names and by a tendency to perceive the whole lexicon through that prism. We have already cited the example of Vladimir Solovyov's childhood language. His drive to turn the world into a space of proper names appeared with special clarity there. One may also compare the epic motif of naming the world, present in many national epics and always performing one function: naming the world transforms chaos into cosmos.

The world of proper names, with its intimacy, is a peculiar linguistic parallel to the cosmic idea of the maternal womb. The world of common nouns, the bearer of the idea of objectivity, stands over against it. These two registers are unified in their conflict. Real speech freely flows from one sphere into the other, but the spheres do not merge. On the contrary, their contrast is only emphasized by this movement.

The relations between these linguistic mechanisms become fundamentally new as soon as we enter the limits of the artistic text. The novel is especially interesting in this regard. It creates a space of the "third person." By its linguistic structure, this space is given as objective, located outside the world of both reader and author. Yet at the same time the author experiences it as something he creates, colored by all the intimacy of related hues, and the reader experiences it as personally lived. The third person is enriched by the emotional halo of the first person.

This is not simply a matter of the author's capacity to experience lyrically the fate of a hero, or the reader's capacity to respond to tone and digressions in authorial narration. The most objective construction of a text does not contradict the subjectivity of the reader's experience. Such a possibility is potentially embedded in language. A newspaper report about a natural disaster on the other side of the globe is experienced by us differently than similar reports about regions geographically close to us, and differently again if they directly concern us and our loved ones. The message has moved from the space of common nouns into the world of proper names, and the emotional experience of news from that world is intrinsically intimate.

The artistic text turns this tendency into one of its most important structural elements. In principle, it forces us to experience any space as a space of proper names. We oscillate between the subjective world personally familiar to us and its antithesis. In the artistic world, the "alien" is always "one's own," but at the same time "one's own" is always "alien." Therefore a poet, having created a work penetrated by personal emotions, can experience it as a catharsis of feeling, a liberation from tragedy. Lermontov said of his *Demon* that he had "got rid of it in verse."

Yet artistic liberation from oneself can become not only the end of one explosive contradiction, but also the beginning of another. All of Charlie Chaplin's artistic space, for example, can be considered a single work. The extraordinary individuality of his talent, and the direct connection of each film with a certain unified space beyond the screen, fully justifies such a perception.

No less justified, however, is the view of Chaplin's legacy as a path, a succession of independent texts closed in on themselves. Chaplin's early screen debuts, such as *Making a Living* in 1914, were built on a contradiction between the cinematographic cliches traditional at that time and circus technique. His virtuosic command of gesture and pantomime produced an effect of unexpectedness on screen and created a language entirely new for cinema.

The next stage continued the gains of the first, but at the same time was based on a principled and unpredictable transition to a new system of artistic language. Films such as *The Tramp*, *The Recruit*, *A Dog's Life*, and *Shoulder Arms* transferred circus eccentricity into a setting that led the viewer into a world of an entirely different genre. The contradiction between gestural technique and plot collisions, very vivid in *Shoulder Arms*, where the hero is transferred into the trench life of the First World War, creates a sharp transition into another system of cinematic language. This transition is not the simple logical continuation of what preceded it. The path later characteristic of Chaplin takes shape: a constant and unexpected change of cinematic language for the viewer. It is no accident that each turning point would be accompanied by public bewilderment and the creation of a "new" Chaplin.

In *The Gold Rush*, the splitting of the unified becomes the leading artistic principle. This is fixed in the contradiction between the dandyish upper part of the hero's clothing and the rags in which he is dressed below the waist. At the same time, contradictions between clothing and gesture play an active role. While the hero is a ragged tramp-prospector, his gestures reveal an impeccable gentleman. But as soon as he becomes a millionaire and pulls luxurious clothes onto himself, he turns into a tramp: vulgar scratching, awkward gestures, everything betrays the mismatch between clothing and role.

Here also appears a device that will be very important in later films: the loss and recovery of memory depending on what role the hero performs. The inserted episode with the dancing doll, which Charlie makes perform refined dances and various movements, seems at first to have no direct relation to the plot. It is inserted as an amusement in which the poor hero indulges while waiting in vain for the coquettish heroine to visit. In fact, however, this episode is the key to the whole film. The viewer sees the inner Charlie, embodied grace and artistry, enlivening the comic, coarse, and primitive structure. The whole episode is a victory of essence over appearance. Without it, the happy ending of the film would be a primitive tribute to film convention. It gives the ending the character of a utopian hope for the possibility of happiness.

From this springboard there became possible a stage of serious, topical films, one that did not follow from Chaplin's already formed cinematic language with unambiguous predictability. *The Great Dictator* took the inner contradiction of the image to its limit: the hero split into two antithetical characters, each casting light on the other. The meaning of the satire was the removal of the mask from theatrical greatness. This transformed clowning from a technical device for organizing language into its content. Two social-philosophical films, *City Lights* and *Modern Times*, created in the age of the Great Depression, completed the emergence of a new language built on the contradiction between appearance and essence. The language of film comedy received philosophical universality because it became a means of re-creating tragedy.

The cycle of films from 1947 to the beginning of the 1950s also met with incomprehension. Critics spoke of Chaplin's decline. His appearance without the little mustache and the characteristic mask of Charlot, without the conventional masquerade clothing, seemed like a renunciation of the great conquests of the preceding period. In fact, films such as *Limelight* and *A King in New York* were a new rise. The mask of Charlot had already become so joined to Chaplin's image, had entered so deeply into the viewer's expectation, that it could be cast aside.

More than this, in *Limelight* Chaplin boldly introduced self-citation. In the film, an old actor forced to earn money on cheap variety stages suffers complete failure. This is Chaplin himself, appearing without the mask. A way out is found when Chaplin and another long-forgotten genius of cinema, Buster Keaton, appear on stage and, without makeup, play themselves. They turn their age, their failures, and their defeats into plot and role, playing before the public the tragicomedy of real existence. What had been language becomes plot, and the contradictions between Chaplin and Charlot make it possible to renew tragically the old drama of "actor and human being." The mask comes alive and lives an independent life, displacing the person, like Andersen's Shadow.

Explosion can be realized as a chain of successive explosions replacing one another and superimposing a many-staged unpredictability onto the dynamic curve.

The problem of predictability and unpredictability is fundamental in deciding such an important question as genuine and imaginary art. Artistic creativity is always immersed in a broad space of surrogates. This should not be understood as an unambiguous condemnation. Surrogates of art are harmful in their aggressiveness. They tend to wrap around genuine art and displace it. Wherever the question is reduced to commercial competition, they always win.

Yet within their proper limits, they are not only necessary but useful. They perform a broad educational role and constitute, as it were, the first step on the path toward mastery of the language of art. Their destruction is impossible and would be as destructive as their seizure of the place of genuine art. They can perform tasks not proper to art, but nevertheless imperatively imposed by society on the artist: enlightenment, propaganda, moral education, and so on.

A special place is occupied by those quasi-artistic works that, in essence, present tasks to be solved. In folklore these are riddles; in the art of the newest period they occupy the broad space of the detective story. The detective story is a task pretending to be art. Externally, the detective plot resembles the plots of the novel or tale. A chain of events unfolds before the reader, and the reader is drawn into a situation typical of artistic prose: the need to make choices so that the plot receives meaning.

In Chesterton's novel *The Man Who Was Thursday*, two poets collide. One is an anarchist; the other is a detective. The matter is complicated by the fact that both appear in masks. The detective depicts a dull gentleman. The anarchist's mask is more complex: in order to avoid suspicion, he plays the role of an anarchist, that is, of a bloodthirsty phrase-maker already familiar to society. The essence of both men is revealed in their artistic tastes. The anarchist curses the prose of railway travel, in which the sequence of stations is predetermined. He sees poetry in unexpectedness, and unexpectedness is brought by explosion. To this the detective replies that each time the train reaches a station, he feels it has broken through an ambush and won a battle against chaos. The fact that after Sloane Square there will necessarily be Victoria is not empty routine for him. Much else could have happened, and when he reaches it he feels that he has barely escaped death. When the conductor cries "Victoria!" it is a herald's cry announcing victory, truly Victoria, a victory of the sons of Adam.

The piquancy here lies in the fact that we find ourselves in a world where wellbeing is less probable than murder. Therefore the absence of an event carries more information than its presence.

Thus Chesterton seems to introduce us into a world of unpredictability and therefore to create a text subordinated to artistic laws. In fact, however, this is no more than a brilliantly constructed mystification. Just as any problem has only one correct solution and the audience's art consists in finding it, Chesterton's plot leads us toward one unambiguous truth. The tangled knots of the plot are meant to mask this path, making it accessible only to the person who possesses the secrets of the single solution.

Such a situation is fundamentally opposed to art, whether we are speaking of its brilliant or mediocre manifestations.

In this sense the example of Edgar Allan Poe is interesting. The reader is prompted, as it were, to imagine that the terrible riddle offered by the author presupposes one and only one "correct" solution. A stable schema is imposed on Poe's composition: a riddle that can and must be guessed, immersed in a frame of fantastic horrors. Modern stories by Clive Barker are built according to just such a schema. In fact, the artistic force of Poe's work lies precisely in his setting before readers riddles that cannot be solved. These are not problems of modernity wrapped in the fantastic candy wrappers of plot, but the unsolvable fantastic itself. Poe opens before readers a path that has no end, a window into a world unpredictable and lying beyond logic and experience. His tales do not imply a clever and unambiguous "answer."

The artistic text has no single solution. This feature is well revealed by certain external signs. A work of art can be used an infinite number of times. It is absurd to say, "I will not go to the Rembrandt room; I have already seen it," or "I have already heard this poem or symphony." But it is quite natural to say, "I have already solved that problem," or "I have already guessed that riddle." Texts of the second type do not allow repeated use. Yet we read Chesterton a second time because he is not only a detective problem but artistic prose, in which not everything is reducible to plot solution. Above that solution another plane arises: the poetry of paradox, and paradox is nourished by unpredictability. The movement of the best representatives of fantasy in the second half of the twentieth century tries to carry us into a world so foreign to everyday experience that it drowns the thin predictions of technical progress in a sea of unpredictability.

Thus art expands the space of the unpredictable, the space of information, and at the same time creates a conditional world that experiments with this space and proclaims triumph over it.
