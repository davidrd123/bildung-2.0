## A System With One Language

The now traditional communication model of the type transmitter, text, language, receiver, later refined by Roman Jakobson, became the basis for all communication models. From the standpoint of this scheme, the goal of communication, as the word itself suggests, is the adequacy of communication: *communitas*, commonality, communication. Interference is treated as an obstacle caused by inevitable technical imperfection. In the ideal model, so it seems in the sphere of theory, it can be disregarded.

At the base of these reflections lies an abstraction. It assumes the complete identity of transmitter and receiver, and then transfers this identity to linguistic reality. Yet the abstract model of communication implies not only the use of one and the same code, but also an identical volume of memory in transmitter and receiver.

In fact, replacing the term "language" with the term "code" is not nearly as harmless as it seems. The term "code" carries the idea of a structure that has just been created, artificial, and introduced by instantaneous agreement. A code does not imply history. Psychologically, then, it orients us toward an artificial language, which is taken as the ideal model of language in general. "Language," by contrast, unconsciously calls up the idea of a historically extended existence. Language is a code plus its history.

This understanding of communication conceals fundamental conclusions. The transmission of information inside a "structure without memory" really does guarantee a high degree of identity. If we imagine a transmitter and receiver with identical codes and entirely deprived of memory, then understanding between them will be ideal. But the value of the information transmitted will be minimal, and the information itself strictly limited. Such a system will not be able to fulfill all the various functions historically assigned to language.

One may say that perfectly identical transmitter and receiver will understand one another well, but they will have nothing to talk about. The ideal of such information will indeed turn out to be the transmission of commands. The model of ideal understanding cannot be applied even to a person's internal communication with himself, because in this latter case what is implied is the transfer of an intense dialogue into one personality. In the words of Goethe's Faust: two souls, alas, live in my breast, and one wants to tear itself away from the other.

Normal human communication, and even more, the normal functioning of language, is founded on the assumption of an initial nonidentity between speaker and listener.

Under these conditions, the normal situation is an intersection between the linguistic space of speaker and listener. Imagine two intersecting circles: A and B.

If there is no intersection, communication is presumed impossible. A complete intersection, the identity of A and B, makes communication empty of content. Thus a certain intersection of these spaces is admitted, and at the same time the intersection of two opposing tendencies: the striving to ease understanding, which will constantly try to expand the area of intersection, and the striving to increase the value of the message, which is connected with the tendency to increase the difference between A and B as much as possible.

Thus normal linguistic communication must include the concept of tension, of a certain force-like resistance that spaces A and B exert on one another.

The area where A and B intersect becomes the natural basis for communication. Meanwhile, the nonintersecting parts of these spaces would seem to be excluded from dialogue. But here we encounter another contradiction: the exchange of information within the intersecting part of the semantic space suffers from the same defect of triviality. The value of dialogue turns out to be connected not with that intersecting part, but with the transmission of information between the nonintersecting parts.

This brings us face to face with an irresolvable contradiction: we are interested in communication precisely with the situation that makes communication difficult, and at the limit makes it impossible. Moreover, the more difficult and inadequate the translation of one nonintersecting part of the space into the language of the other, the more valuable, informationally and socially, the fact of this paradoxical communication becomes. One may say that the translation of the untranslatable turns out to be the bearer of information of high value.

Consider two examples: on the one hand, translation where the languages are relatively close; on the other, translation where they are different in principle. In the first case, translation will be relatively easy. In the second, it is inevitably connected with difficulties and will generate semantic indeterminacy. If the first case is the translation of a nonliterary text from one natural language into another, then reverse translation will return us, to a certain degree, to the initial meaning. But if we consider the case of translation from the language of poetry into the language of music, then unambiguous exactness of meaning becomes impossible in principle. This is also reflected in the enormous variability that appears in reverse translation.

The case of translation from the language of literary prose into the language of cinema is one of the most complicated realizations of the second variant, because the commonality between the language of literary prose and the language of cinema is imaginary. The difficulties here do not decrease; they increase. Ignoring this is the source of numerous failures in film adaptation.

Linguistic communication appears to us as a tense intersection of adequate and inadequate linguistic acts. Moreover, misunderstanding, conversation in languages that are not fully identical, appears to be just as valuable a semantic mechanism as understanding. The exclusive victory of either pole destroys the information created in the field of their mutual tension.

Different forms of contact, with ordinary linguistic communication at one pole and artistic communication at the other, are shifts from the neutral central point now toward ease of understanding, now in the opposite direction. But the absolute victory of either pole is theoretically impossible and practically destructive.

The situation in which the minimal meaning-generating unit is not one language but two creates a whole chain of consequences. Above all, the very nature of the intellectual act can be described in terms of translation. The definition of meaning is translation from one language into another, with outside-language reality also thought of as a kind of language. It is attributed a structural organization and a potential ability to function as the content of a diverse set of expressions.
