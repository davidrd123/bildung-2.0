## The Phenomenon of Art

The transformation undergone by a real moment of explosion, once it has passed through the selective grid of modeling consciousness and turned the accidental into the lawful, does not yet complete the process of consciousness. Memory enters the mechanism. It makes it possible to return again to the moment preceding the explosion and once more, now retrospectively, play through the entire process. There are now, as it were, three strata in consciousness: the moment of primary explosion, the moment of its editing in the mechanisms of consciousness, and the moment of their new doubling in the structure of memory. This last stratum forms the basis of the mechanism of art.

Nineteenth-century positivist philosophy, on the one hand, and Hegelian aesthetics, on the other, fixed in our consciousness the idea of art as a reflection of reality. At the same time, various neo-romantic, symbolist, and decadent views broadly disseminated the idea of art as something opposed to life. This opposition was embodied in the antithesis between the freedom of creativity and the slavery of reality. Neither view can be called true or false. They isolate and carry to an impossible maximalism tendencies that in real art are inseparably interconnected.

Art re-creates a fundamentally new level of reality, one that differs from reality by a sharp increase of freedom. Freedom is introduced into those spheres that do not possess it in reality. That which has no alternative receives an alternative. Hence the growth of ethical evaluation in art. Art, precisely because of its greater freedom, appears as if outside morality. It makes possible not only the forbidden, but also the impossible. In relation to reality, therefore, art appears as the domain of freedom.

Yet the very sensation of this freedom implies an observer who casts a gaze at art from reality. For this reason the domain of art always includes a feeling of estrangement. And this inevitably introduces the mechanism of ethical evaluation. The very decisiveness with which aesthetics denies the inevitability of ethical reading in art, the very energy spent on such proofs, is the best confirmation of ethical reading's firmness. The ethical and the aesthetic are opposite and inseparable, like the two poles of art.

The relation between art and morality repeats the general fate of oppositions in the structure of culture. Polar principles realize themselves in mutual conflict. Each tendency understands victory as the complete destruction of its antithesis. Yet victory understood in this way is a program of suicide, because each tendency is defined by the reality and being of its antithesis.

The sharp increase of degrees of freedom in relation to reality makes art a pole of experimentation. Art creates its own world, which is built as a transformation of extra-artistic reality according to the law "if, then..." The artist concentrates the force of art in those spheres of life where he investigates the results of increased freedom. In essence, there is no difference between taking as the object of attention the possibility of violating the laws of family, society, common sense, custom and tradition, or even time and space. In every case, the laws organizing the world divide into two groups: impossible changes and possible but categorically forbidden changes. Changes that are possible and not forbidden are not counted as changes here; they are introduced as the antithesis of pseudo-changes to genuine ones.

Hoffmann's hero can stretch up to the ceiling and then curl into a ball again, just as the giant in *Puss in Boots* can turn into a lion and a mouse. For these heroes, therefore, transformation is an act. But these criteria do not apply to Tolstoy's heroes. Olenin does not experience a limitation of freedom from being deprived of transformations possible for Hoffmann's heroes, because such changes do not enter his alphabet. But the fact that the freedom possible for Uncle Eroshka or Lukashka is impossible for him becomes the hero's tragedy. Freedom each time appears as a presumption defined by the rules of the world into which the work introduces us.

The genius of art in general lies in mental experiment, which makes it possible to test the inviolability of particular structures of the world. This also determines art's relation to reality. It tests the consequences of experiments in expanding freedom or limiting it. Derivative from this are plots that examine the experimental consequences of refusing experiment. When the object of art is patriarchal society or any other idealization of immutability, then, contrary to widespread notions, the stimulus for creating such art is not an immobile and stable society, but a society experiencing catastrophic processes. Plato preached immutable art at a time when the ancient world was sliding irresistibly toward catastrophe.

The object of art, the plot of an artistic work, is always given to the reader as something already accomplished, preceding the narration of it. This past is illuminated at the moment when it passes from a state of incompletion into a state of completion. This is expressed, in particular, in the fact that the entire course of plot development is given to the reader as a past that at the same time is as if present, and as a conditional thing that at the same time is as if real. The action of a novel or drama belongs to the past tense in relation to the moment of reading. But the reader weeps or laughs, that is, experiences emotions that outside art belong to the present tense. In the same way, the conditional is emotionally switched into the real.

The text fixes art's paradoxical capacity to transform the conditional into the real and the past into the present. In this, among other things, lies the difference between the time of the plot's flow and the time of its ending. The first exists in time; the second is switched into the past, which is simultaneously a departure from time altogether. This fundamental difference between the spaces of the plot and its ending makes meaningless any reasoning about what happened to the heroes after the end of the work. When such reasoning appears, it testifies to a non-artistic perception of the artistic text and is the result of readerly inexperience.

Art is a means of knowledge and, first of all, of knowledge of the human being. This proposition is repeated so often that it has become trivial. But what should be understood by the expression "knowledge of the human being"? The plots we define by this expression share one common feature: they transfer the human being into a situation of freedom and investigate the behavior chosen there. No real situation, from the most everyday to the most unexpected, can exhaust the whole sum of possibilities and therefore all the actions that reveal what is potentially embedded in a person. The genuine essence of the human being cannot reveal itself in reality. Art transfers the human being into the world of freedom and thereby reveals the possibilities of his actions.

Thus every work of art sets a certain norm, its violation, and the establishment, at least in the domain of fantasy's freedom, of some other norm. Plato's cyclic world, by destroying unexpectedness in human behavior and introducing unappealable rules, thereby destroys art. Plato reasons logically. Art is a mechanism of dynamic processes.

In the 1960s, the English director Lindsay Anderson created the film *If*. The action takes place in a college where young men experience complex conflicts between the destructive sexual, mercenary, and ambitious passions that overwhelm them and the conventions of the idiotic norm into which society, represented by teachers, drives them. Images arising in the heroes' souls under the influence of passions appear on screen with the same reality as genuine events. In the viewer's eyes, the real and the beyond-real become hopelessly confused. When two schoolboys skip class, go into a cafe, and throw a beautiful, powerful waitress to the floor, the viewer experiences all the emotions of witnessing an erotic scene, but is then made to understand that what actually takes place is only the purchase of cigarettes with poor boys' money. Everything else is "if."

The film ends with the arrival of parents for the weekend. On the screen, parents approach the school in a friendly crowd with flowers and presents; at the same time, with the same imitation of cinematic reality, their sons, stationed with automatic weapons on the school roof, open fire on their mothers and fathers in long bursts. The external plot of the film concerns problems of child psychology. But at the same time it raises the question of the very language of art. That question is "if." Through this question, unlimited possibilities of variants are introduced into life.

All kinds of artistic creativity can be represented as varieties of mental experiment. The essence of the phenomenon subject to analysis is included in a system of relations not proper to it. In this case the event proceeds as an explosion and therefore has an unpredictable character. The unpredictability, or unexpectedness, of the unfolding of events forms, as it were, the compositional center of the work.

The post-folkloric work of art differs from the reality it reflects in that it always has an end. The point of ending is at the same time the point of a repeated retrospective gaze at the plot. This backward observation transforms what seemed accidental into the inevitable and subjects all events to a secondary revaluation. In this sense, structures that create texts with a marked title, a meaningful ending, or both, are of interest. Among the diverse artistic meanings of these compositional elements, only one interests us here: their capacity to change content depending on the reader's point of view.

The title of Pushkin's story *The Queen of Spades* or Chekhov's drama *The Seagull*, for example, changes for the reader or spectator as he moves from one plot episode to another, and the ending demands a return to the beginning and a second reading of it. What was organized by movement along the temporal axis occupied by reading is transferred into the synchronic space of memory. Sequence is replaced by simultaneity, and this gives the events new meaning. Artistic memory in this situation behaves analogously to what Florensky attributed to dream: it moves in the direction opposite to the axis of time.

A particular but interesting case is newspaper or journal publication of works in installments. The limiting examples are Pushkin's *Eugene Onegin* and Tvardovsky's *Vasily Terkin*, in which one chapter or another is published before the next has been written or even thought through. Each new chapter places the poet under the necessity of overcoming his own ignorance. From this comes Pushkin's characteristic double position in *Onegin*: he is the author, arbitrarily creating the history of his heroes, and he is also their contemporary, learning the circumstances of their life from stories and letters, from direct observations of real life.

From this same structure comes what is sometimes treated by researchers, including the present author, as Pushkin's oversight, an accidental error. In *Eugene Onegin* we read:

Tatyana's letter lies before me;
I keep it sacredly.

And elsewhere, now about Onegin:

She, from whom he keeps
A letter where the heart speaks.

If we add to this Kuchelbecker's paradoxical but in fact very deep statement in his fortress diary that, for a person who knows Pushkin as well as Kuchelbecker does, it is obvious that Tatyana is the poet himself, then before us is an exceptionally complex interweaving of external and internal points of view. The background for them is the text's constant leaping from the idea of its extra-authorial reality to the thought of the author's complete and unlimited power over his work.

All this can be considered a particular case of the general law by which the artistic text leaps onto an initially unpredictable semantic orbit. Later the whole preceding history is reinterpreted in such a way that the unpredictable is retrospectively rethought as the only possible thing.

From a historical-cultural point of view, it is interesting to consider different stages of art not in the evolutionary or historical aspect, but as something unified. In the phenomenon of art one can distinguish two opposite tendencies: the tendency to repeat what is already known and the tendency to create what is fundamentally new. Does the first of these cases contradict the thesis that art, as the result of explosion, always creates an initially unpredictable text?

As a limiting case of the first tendency, let us consider not folklore and not the performing skill of several artists re-creating one and the same work, but repeated listening to the same tape recording of a musical or literary text. In this case one cannot refer to differences of interpretation or to the result of the individual peculiarity of performers. A situation has been artificially created that we can define as the reproduction of one and the same thing. In passing, let us note that precisely here the difference between art and non-art appears with unexpected clarity. It is unlikely that anyone would listen several times in a row to a radio broadcast of the same "latest news." The late N. Smirnov-Sokolsky, speaking in the 1950s with young philologists, asked what the greatest bibliographic rarity was, and answered himself: "Yesterday's newspaper: everyone has read it and everyone has thrown it away."

The repetition of one and the same text does not, however, mean the receipt of zero information. Repetition of the newspaper loses meaning because new information is expected from a text arriving from outside. But in cases where we listen to the same recording repeatedly, what changes is not what is transmitted, but the one who receives. The structure of human intelligence is exceptionally dynamic.

The idea that in archaic folkloric society there were no individual differences and that calendar-changing experiences were uniform for the whole collective should be assigned to romantic legends. The simultaneous presence of the parallel cults of Apollo and Dionysus, and the systematic intrusion into the most diverse cults of ecstatic states that greatly expand the boundaries of predictability, is enough to reject the romantic myth of the absence of individuality in archaic society. The human being became human when he became aware of himself as human. This happened when he noticed that different members of the human herd have different faces, different voices, and different experiences. The individual face, as well as individual sexual choice, was probably the first invention of the human being as human.

The notion that archaic humanity lacked individual differences is as much a myth as the idea of an original disorderliness of sexual relations at the earliest stage of human development. The latter is the result of European travelers uncritically transferring ecstatic rituals they happened to observe onto the ordinary everyday norms of behavior of "savages." Yet the gestures and laws of everyday behavior, including sexually colored dances, are rituals of magical influence on ordinary life and therefore by definition must differ from it. In them permission must be given for what is prohibited. In order to calculate everyday behavior from ritual behavior, therefore, one must subject the latter to a deciphering transformation.

This is similar to the case in which researchers of folk life, encountering references to unclean forces, dead people, and other magical characters doing everything with the left hand, concluded that the creators of this mythology were ancient left-handed people. In fact, knowing the laws of folklore, even an extraterrestrial could conclude from these texts that the human norm is work with the right hand. Thus even unchanging preservation is transformation. The more unchanged the incoming text, the more active the perception of the one who understands.

It is no accident that in contemporary theater the spectator calmly and without intervening in the action perceives the most terrible episodes and scenes. In archaic theater, stage action very easily turned into turbulent actions by the spectators. Compare ancient legends about Athenian spectators, excited by stage actors' declamations about the power of love, rushing toward their women. An example of the exciting action of a seemingly neutral text is the episode from *The Captain's Daughter* where the father rereads the annually received Court Calendar. This book always has a strong effect on him. He reads it with special involvement, muttering that someone who had once served as a sergeant in his company is now a lieutenant general and holder of Russian orders, until at last he throws the calendar onto the sofa.

In all these cases, creative initiative belongs to the addressee of information. The listener, or reader, is the genuine creator.

The archaic stage of art's development is no more alien to creativity than any art. Yet this artistic aspect takes a peculiar form. First of all, the poles of traditional and improvisational creativity separate. They may be opposed by genre or collide within one work. In any case, however, they are interconnected: the stricter the laws of tradition, the freer the explosions of improvisation. The improviser and the bearer of memory, sometimes realized as "fool and sage," "drunkard and philosopher," the one who invents and the one who knows, are interconnected figures, bearers of undivided functions. This two-in-one structure, perceived one-sidedly, generated conceptions according to which folk creativity is, in one case, the fruit of free improvisation and, in the other, the embodiment of impersonal tradition.

Archaic creativity, whose features we trace in folklore, is peculiar not because of its supposed immobility, but because of a different distribution of functions between performer and listener. Archaic forms of folklore are ritual. This means that they have no passive spectator. To be within certain limits, temporal or spatial, is to be a participant. In the spirit of symbolist ideas, Vyacheslav Ivanov tried to revive the joining of ritual performers and spectators into a single collective. Yet these attempts too clearly smelled of imitation. The point is that archaic ritual requires an archaic worldview, and this cannot be artificially re-created.

The fusion of actor and spectator implied the fusion of art and action. For a child not yet accustomed to perceiving drawing according to the adult system, there exists not a drawing but drawing; not a result, but an action. Anyone who has observed children drawing knows that in many cases the process interests them more than the result. The child drawing becomes excited, dances about and shouts, and often tears up or crosses out the drawing. One often sees an adult trying to hold back the child's excitement, saying: "Now it is good; if you go on, you will spoil it." But the child cannot stop.

This state of uncontrolled excitement into which the performer enters in the process of creativity, and which in ecstatic forms, according to many observations, ends in complete exhaustion, can be compared with the state of inspiration in later stages of art's existence.

In the art of "historical epochs," however, there occurs a division between performers and audience. If action in the sphere of art is always action "as if," then in the art of historical epochs it produces conditional co-presence. The spectator in art is simultaneously a non-spectator in real actuality: he sees but does not intervene, is co-present but does not act, and does not participate in the stage action. This last point is essential. Mastery of any art, whether a gladiator's fight with a lion in the circus arena or cinema, requires nonintervention by the spectator in artistic space. Action is replaced by co-presence, which at once coincides with presence in ordinary, non-artistic space and is completely opposite to it.

Thus dynamic processes in culture are built as a peculiar pendulum oscillation between the state of explosion and the state of organization realizing itself in gradual processes. The state of explosion is characterized by the moment of identification of all opposites. The different appears as one and the same. This makes possible unexpected leaps into completely different, unpredictable structures of organization. The impossible becomes possible. This moment is experienced as switched off from time, even if in reality it stretches across very large temporal intervals. Recall Blok's words:

The past gazes passionately into the future;
There is no present...

This moment ends by switching into the state of gradual movement. What had been united in one integrated whole scatters into different and opposite elements. Although in fact there was no choice, since it was replaced by chance, the past is retrospectively experienced as choice and purposive action. The laws of gradual development enter the process. They aggressively seize the consciousness of culture and strive to include in memory their transformed picture of it. According to that picture, explosion loses its unpredictability and appears as the rapid, energetic, or even catastrophic development of the same predictable processes. The antithesis between explosive and predictable is replaced by the concepts of fast, or energetic, and slow, or gradual.

Thus all historical descriptions of catastrophic explosive moments, wars or revolutions, are built in order to prove the inevitability of their results. History, faithful to its apostle Hegel, stubbornly proves that for it nothing accidental exists and that all events of the future are secretly embedded in phenomena of the past. The lawful consequence of such an approach is the eschatological myth: the movement of history toward an irresistible final result.

Instead of this model, we propose another, in which the unpredictability of the timeless explosion is constantly transformed in human consciousness into the predictability of the dynamics generated by it, and back again. The first model can metaphorically imagine the Lord as a great pedagogue who, with extraordinary art, demonstrates to someone a process known to him in advance. The second can be illustrated by the image of a creator-experimenter who has set up a great experiment whose results are unexpected and unpredictable even for him. Such a view turns the universe into an inexhaustible source of information, into that Psyche possessing self-increasing Logos of which Heraclitus spoke.

In its search for a new language, art cannot be exhausted, just as the reality it knows cannot be exhausted.
