## Semantic Intersection as Meaning-Explosion. Inspiration

The problem of the intersection of semantic spaces is complicated by the fact that the circles we draw on paper are a particular visual metaphor, not an exact model of this object. Metaphoricity, when it appears under the mask of models and scientific definitions, is especially treacherous. Any semantic space can be represented as two-dimensional, with clear and unambiguous boundaries, only metaphorically.

It is more realistic to imagine a certain semantic mass whose boundaries are formed from a multitude of individual usages. Metaphorically, this can be compared with the boundaries of space on a map. In actual movement through a terrain, the geographical line becomes blurred. Instead of a clear line, there is a spot.

The intersections of semantic spaces that generate new meaning are connected with individual consciousness. When they spread through the entire space of a given language, these intersections form what are called linguistic metaphors. These latter are facts of the general language of the collective.

At the other pole are artistic metaphors. Here too semantic space is not unambiguous. Metaphors that are cliches common to certain literary schools or periods, and metaphors that gradually pass from the trivial area into the area of individual creativity, illustrate different degrees of semantic intersection.

The limit case here is the metaphor that is innovative in principle, evaluated by bearers of traditional meaning as illegitimate and as offending their feeling for reason. This shocking metaphor is always the result of a creative act, though this does not prevent it from later becoming widespread and even trivial.

This constantly operating process of the "aging" of various modes of meaning-generation is compensated, on the one hand, by the circulation of new, previously forbidden structures, and on the other hand, by the rejuvenation of old, already forgotten meaning-generating structures.

Individual meaning-formation is the process that, in Tynyanov's not entirely exact retelling of Lomonosov's expression, is defined as "bringing somewhat distant ideas together." Or, following Zhukovsky's expression:

"Visible to it, to the soul, in a moment became
what had been invisible from ancient times."

This joining of the unjoinable under the influence of a certain creative tension is defined as inspiration.

With his characteristic clarity of definition, Pushkin perceptively rejected Kuchelbecker's romantic formula, which identified inspiration with rapture:

"Inspiration? It is the disposition of the soul toward the liveliest reception of impressions, and consequently toward the rapid comparison of concepts, which also assists in explaining them.

Inspiration is needed in poetry as in geometry."

Thus for Pushkin, poetic tension is not opposed to logical, scientific discovery and is not a refusal of Knowledge. It is Knowledge's highest tension, in whose light what had been incomprehensible outside it becomes evident.

Yet one could also cite the opposite point of view: creative inspiration is thought of as the highest tension, tearing a person out of the sphere of logic and into the area of unpredictable creativity. Blok expressed this process with documentary precision:

### The Artist

In hot summer and in blizzard winter,
On the days of your weddings, feasts, funerals,
I wait for a light, never-before-heard ringing
To startle my mortal boredom.

There it is. It has arisen. And with cold attention
I wait in order to understand, fix, and kill it.
And before my sharp-eyed expectation
It draws an almost imperceptible thread.

Is it a whirlwind from the sea? Or paradise Sirins
Singing in the leaves? Or does time stand still?
Or have May apple trees scattered
Their snowy blossom? Or is an angel flying?

Hours last, carrying worlds.
Sounds, movement, and light widen.
The past gazes passionately into the future.
There is no present. There is no pitiful present.

And finally, at the limit of conceiving
A new soul, unknown powers,
A curse strikes the soul as with thunder:
Creative reason overcame it, killed it.

And I lock into a cold cage
The light, kind, free bird,
The bird that wanted to carry death away,
The bird that flew to save the soul.

Here is my cage, steel and heavy,
Like gold in the evening fire.
Here is my bird, once merry,
Rocking its hoop, singing at the window.

Its wings are clipped, its songs are learned.
Do you like standing beneath the window?
You like the songs. But I, exhausted,
Wait for the new and grow bored again.

The poem, devoted to the role of the unconscious, is nevertheless sustained with strict, almost terminological precision. In this sense, even beyond the immediate text, it reproduces the fundamental contradiction of the moment.

The unfolding of the text's meaning passes through several stages. The first is the external, "your" world, the world on the other side of poetry, the world of "weddings, feasts, funerals." The next layer is the world of the poetic "I." Yet it is not single. Its outer layer is the space of logic. This world is consciously grasped.

The third, poetic world is immersed in depth and, in its usual state, remains asleep. At certain unpredictable moments it awakens as a "light, never-before-heard ringing." What is essential here is the passage from the world expressible in words into the world that lies beyond them. For Blok this is most likely a sonic world: a "ringing."

The next stage is self-observation. The logical verbal world, with "cold attention," strives to "understand, fix, and kill" it, to express the extraverbal in words and what lies beyond logic by means of logic.

The following stanza is devoted precisely to this hopeless attempt. Blok, as a poet whose material is the word, is forced to try, within the limits of this material, to express what in principle cannot be expressed by it. In the third stanza there follows a series of fragmentary sentences. Their interrogative intonations express doubt about the adequacy of the word to its meaning. This is combined with a principled incompatibility of meanings: they do not form a single logical picture. The syntactic construction of the stanza seems like a continuation of Zhukovsky's famous question:

Is the inexpressible accessible to expression?

The stanza that demonstrates the absence of an adequate language and the need to replace it with many mutually exclusive languages is followed by the compositional center of the poem: the description of the moment of the highest tension of poetic inspiration. The world of features and objectifications is replaced by a world of higher clarity that removes contradictions in a certain deep unity.

"Hours last." The possibility of extending an hour, and the fact that the brief minute of inspiration is called "hours," testifies that what is at stake is not a unit of time measurement, but a breakthrough into non-time. Opposites are identified. "Sounds, movement, and light" appear as synonyms. Especially interesting is the disappearance of the present. It proves to be only a space deprived of reality, like a geometrical line, between past and future. And the formula "the past gazes passionately into the future" sounds like a prophetic anticipation of contemporary ideas about the transfer, in our consciousness, of the past into the future.

The moment of highest tension removes all boundaries of untranslatability and makes the incompatible one. Before us is a poetic description of new meaning. Blok wanted to break beyond the limits of meaning, but the same thing happened to him as to the prophet Balaam, who intended to curse and blessed instead. Blok described with exceptional accuracy what he considered indescribable.

Then follows the descent along the same stages. "Inexpressible" inspiration is cast into words:

And I lock into a cold cage
The light, kind, free bird,
The bird that wanted to carry death away,
The bird that flew to save the soul.

Then frozen and vulgarized poetry moves into the external sphere, to the reader.

The inexpressibility reproduced by Blok in fact turns out to be translation. The entire process of creativity is reproduced as a certain tension that makes the untranslatable translatable.

The polar opposition between Pushkin's and Blok's statements merely exposes their deep unity. In both cases, what is at stake is the moment of unpredictable explosion that turns the incompatible into the adequate, the untranslatable into the translatable.

Blok's text is especially interesting because it shows the metaphoricity of the word "moment," which we have just used. In reality, the issue is not a contraction of time, but a falling out of it, a certain leap of transition from the "past" into the "future." The entire content of the poem is a description of semantic explosion, a crossing over the boundary of unpredictability.

It is interesting that Pushkin, from entirely different positions and persistently avoiding romantic terminology, in fact describes inspiration as the same passage from untranslatability to translation. Thus, while working on *Boris Godunov*, he reports in a letter that the tragedy is being written easily. When he comes to the "scenes that require inspiration," he skips them and writes further. As with Blok, two possibilities of creativity are distinguished: within the limits of some already given language, and beyond the line of unpredictable explosion.

In the unfinished novel *Egyptian Nights*, we find an interesting description of the moment of creativity. It is characteristic that Pushkin avoids already vulgarized romantic terminology and, while describing inspiration, makes the word itself taboo:

"Nevertheless he, Charsky, was a poet, and his passion was irresistible: when such trash came over him, as he called inspiration, Charsky locked himself in his study and wrote from morning until late at night. He admitted sincerely to his close friends that only then did he know true happiness. The rest of the time he strolled about, primping and pretending, and heard at every moment the famous question: 'Have you written anything new?'"

Semiotic space appears before us as a multilayered intersection of different texts that together make up a definite layer, with complex internal relations, different degrees of translatability, and spaces of untranslatability. Beneath this layer is located the layer of "reality": that reality which is organized by various languages and stands with them in a hierarchy of correlations. Together, these two layers form the semiotics of culture.

Beyond the limits of the semiotics of culture lies reality located outside the limits of language. The word "reality" covers two different phenomena. On the one hand, there is phenomenal reality, in Kant's definition, that is, the reality that correlates with culture, now opposing it, now merging with it. In another, noumenal sense, in Kant's terminology, one can speak of reality as a space fatally beyond culture. Yet the whole building of these definitions and terms changes if at the center of our world we place not one isolated "I," but a complexly organized space of numerous mutually correlated "I"s.

Thus external reality would be transcendental, according to Kantian ideas, if the layer of culture possessed one single language. But the relations of the translatable and the untranslatable are so complex that possibilities are created for a breakthrough into the space beyond. This function too is performed by moments of explosion, which can create something like windows in the semiotic layer.

Thus the world of semiosis is not fatally closed in on itself. It forms a complex structure that all the time "plays" with the space lying outside it, now drawing that space into itself, now throwing out into it its own elements that have already been used and have lost their semiotic activity.

Two conclusions can be drawn from what has been said. First, the abstraction of one language of communication as the basis of semiosis is a bad abstraction, because in an unnoticed way it distorts the entire essence of the mechanism. Of course, we can consider cases that approach the relative identity of transmitting and receiving mechanisms and, consequently, communication by means of one single channel. But this is not the general model. It is a particular space abstracted from the "normal" polylingual model.

The second aspect is connected with the dynamic nature of linguistic space. The idea that we can give a static description and then impart motion to it is also a bad abstraction. A static state is a particular model, ideally existing only in abstraction, and a speculative abstraction from the dynamic structure that represents the only reality.
