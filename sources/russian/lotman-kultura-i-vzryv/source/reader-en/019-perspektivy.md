## Perspectives

Thus explosions are an inevitable element of the linear dynamic process. It has already been said that in binary structures this dynamics is marked by sharp peculiarity. In what does this peculiarity consist?

In ternary structures, even the most powerful and deep explosions do not embrace the whole complex wealth of social strata. The central structure may undergo an explosion so powerful and catastrophic that its thunder will certainly reverberate through the entire thickness of culture. Even so, in the conditions of a ternary structure, the assertion by contemporaries, and after them by historians, that the entire order of the old system has been completely destroyed is a mixture of self-deception and tactical slogan.

The point is not only that absolute destruction of the old is impossible in either ternary or binary structures, but also something deeper: ternary structures preserve certain values of the preceding period, moving them from the periphery to the center of the system. By contrast, the ideal of binary systems is the complete destruction of everything already existing as stained by irreparable vices. The ternary system strives to adapt the ideal to reality; the binary system strives to realize in practice an unrealizable ideal.

In binary systems, explosion embraces the whole thickness of everyday life. The mercilessness of this experiment does not manifest itself immediately. At first it attracts the most maximalist layers of society by the poetry of instant construction of "a new earth and a new heaven," by its radicalism.

The price that has to be paid for utopias appears only at the next stage. A characteristic feature of explosive moments in binary systems is that they experience themselves as unique moments, incomparable with anything else in the whole history of humanity. What is declared marked is not any concrete layer of historical development but the very existence of history. In the ideal, this is the apocalyptic "there will be no more time"; in practical realization, it is the words with which Saltykov ends his *History of a Town*: "History ceased its flow." Connected with this is the striving to replace jurisprudence with moral or religious principles.

In *The Captain's Daughter* there is a passage that attracts attention from this point of view. Masha Mironova comes to the capital to save Grinev, who has fallen into trouble. Between her and the state-minded Catherine II there occurs a remarkable conversation. Learning that her interlocutor is the daughter of Captain Mironov, executed by the Pugachevites, Catherine says: "You are an orphan; probably you are complaining of injustice and offense?" The reply is unexpected: "No, ma'am. I came to ask mercy, not justice."

This theme, which Pasternak in *Doctor Zhivago* called "the unprincipledness of the heart," preoccupied Pushkin in the 1830s, and he returned to it repeatedly. In *Angelo*, the hero is a severe moralist who has fallen into sin. Having recognized his fall, he shows toward himself the same inhuman principledness that had guided him in condemning criminals. The duke asks what Angelo deserves, and without tears or fear, with gloomy firmness, Angelo answers: execution. Yet what triumphs is not justice, but mercy. Isabella, like an angel, feels pity in her soul for the sinner, and the whole meaning of the poem is compressed into the final line, graphically set apart: "And the duke forgave him." In this same series of appeals to mercy sounds Pushkin's final line: "And I called for mercy toward the fallen."

Mercy stands opposed to Law, which had essentially been the hero of the ode *Liberty*. In the antithesis between mercy and justice, the Russian idea, founded on binarism, stands opposed to Latin rules permeated by the spirit of law: `Fiat justitia, pereat mundus`, "Let justice be done though the world perish," and `Dura lex, sed lex`, "The law is harsh, but it is the law."

All the more significant is Russian literature's stable striving to see in law a dry and inhuman principle, opposed to such informal concepts as mercy, sacrifice, and love. Behind this was outlined the antithesis between state law and personal morality, politics and holiness.

With Gogol, especially with *Selected Passages from Correspondence with Friends*, begins the tradition of opposing state law and human morality. Although Gogol's advice to correct a drunken peasant by admonitions such as "Ah, you unwashed snout" drew general disapproval, including from the Slavophiles, the idea of constructing a non-juridical society, in one form or another, was proclaimed throughout the second half of the nineteenth century. The lawyer is invariably a negative figure in both Tolstoy and Dostoevsky.

Judicial reform was undoubtedly the most carefully thought-out fruit of the age of transformations, and yet it was subjected to the most ferocious attacks from both right and left. Utopia was opposed to political liberalism. Through Sonya's mouth, Dostoevsky called Raskolnikov to kneel in the middle of the square and repent before the people. In *The Power of Darkness*, together with the tongue-tied Akim, who precisely for that reason proclaims the truth hidden from the "scribes and Pharisees," Tolstoy proclaims the supremacy of conscience over law and repentance over trial. When the police official demands an act to be drawn up, Akim stops him: do not talk about an act; God's business is going on, a man is repenting, and you are talking about an act.

The binary situation cuts across evolution with breaks that sever its continuity, "nature is all in ruptures," following words Mandelstam attributed to Lamarck. In civilizations of the Western type, as already said, explosion tears apart only part of the layers of culture, even if a very significant part. Continuity intertwines with breaks, forming a single historical connection. In binary structures, moments of explosion can break the chain of continuous sequences, which inevitably leads to deep crises but also to radical renewals.

Thus the age of reforms in Russia in the last third of the nineteenth century was disrupted by the simultaneous transition of both the government and the democrats to methods of terror. The desire to stop history collided with the striving to force it to leap. The hostile sides came together only in one thing: their dislike of "gradualists." At the same time, the high moral impeccability of one or another figure in the hostile parties proved far from impeccable from the point of view of the interests of the integral structure.

The integral structure is oriented toward the average and toward survival; its mechanism is justice. Partisanship is directed toward truth, always its own truth, at any cost. The extreme embodiment of the first is compromise; of the second, war to the victorious end and the complete destruction of the opponent. Therefore victory must be preceded by bloody struggle and perhaps defeat. In exchange, victory is imagined as the "last and decisive battle," the establishment of the kingdom of God on earth.

The Holy Virgin who appeared to Savonarola, promising him support in carrying out his ideas, warned him that the renewal and consolation of the church could not occur without great turmoil and bloody collisions, especially in Italy, whose luxury, pride, and countless sins of its leaders were the cause of these evils. The Madonna showed Savonarola the spectacle of Florence and all Italy engulfed in rebellions and wars. But then she showed him another globe: Florence all in lilies, winding along the irregularities of the walls and stretching everywhere on long stems, while angels hovering above the city walls looked at them.

The miraculous utopian transfiguration of humanity always begins in theory with a redemptive sacrifice, with the spilling of blood. In practice, it is doomed to drown in blood.

Among the brilliant constellation of nineteenth-century Russian writers that won worldwide recognition, our gaze always somehow slips past the solitary and enigmatic figure of Krylov. Recognized by readers of all social strata, he is entirely solitary in the literary struggle of his epoch. He does not need to idealize the people, because he himself is the people. He is free of illusions. His sober mind is not alien to skepticism, but his main pathos is sobriety. He does not believe in miracles, whoever performs them, tsar or people. He does not embellish the peasant, although in the deep foundations of his self-consciousness he is, of course, a democrat.

At times Krylov rises almost to prophecy. In a version of the fable "Parnassus" not intended for publication, he wrote of the gods going out of fashion in Greece, their throne wavering, their temples being cleared, and everything they had managed to accumulate being stripped from them. "God forbid being demoted from gods!" Yet the expulsion of the gods led to the new owner, to whom Parnassus was allotted, pasturing donkeys on it. Neither the old nor the new calls forth illusions in Krylov. His pathos is common sense, liberation from illusions and from the power of words and slogans. But Krylov is strong not only as a destroyer of illusions. Perhaps no one in the whole history of Russian culture managed so organically to unite genuine popular rootedness with the highest European culture.

Krylov is of course not the only, though he is the brightest, representative of this direction in Russian culture: a direction alien to both Slavophilism and Westernism, alien to the very principle "whoever is not with us is against us," the guiding principle of binary systems. In this connection one should also name Ostrovsky and especially Chekhov.

What has been said has direct relation to the events now unfolding on the former territory of the Soviet Union. From the point of view of the questions we are studying, the process we are witnessing can be described as a switch from a binary system to a ternary one. Yet one cannot fail to note the peculiarity of the moment: the transition itself is thought in the traditional concepts of binarism.

In fact, two possible paths are being worked out. One, which led Gorbachev to the real loss of power, consisted in replacing reforms with declarations and plans and drove the country into a dead end fraught with the darkest forecasts. The other, expressed in various plans such as "500 days" and other projects for the rapid transformation of the economy, drives out one wedge with another, explosion with explosion.

The transition from thinking oriented toward explosions to evolutionary consciousness now acquires special significance, since the whole preceding culture familiar to us gravitated toward polarity and maximalism. Yet self-understanding is not adequate to reality. In the sphere of reality, explosions cannot disappear. The question is only the overcoming of the fatal choice between stagnation and catastrophe.

In addition, ethical maximalism is so deeply rooted in the very foundations of Russian culture that there is hardly any reason to speak of the "danger" of the absolute affirmation of the golden mean, and still less to fear that leveling contradictions will slow creative explosive processes. If forward movement, whose only alternative is catastrophe with difficult-to-predict limits, nevertheless overcomes the boundary on which we stand, the order that arises will hardly be a simple copy of the Western one. History knows no repetitions. It loves new, unpredictable roads.
