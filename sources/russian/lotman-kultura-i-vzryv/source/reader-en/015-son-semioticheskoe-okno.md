## Dream: A Semiotic Window

In the history of consciousness, a turning point was the emergence of the possibility of a temporal interval, a pause, between impulse and reaction. The initial biological schema is built as "stimulus-response." In the ideal sense, the space between these elements is instantaneous, determined by the physiological time needed to carry out the immediate reaction. Such a schema characterizes all living things and preserves its power over the human being as well. On it rests the whole sphere of impulses and instant reactions: on the one hand, immediate actions; on the other, those instant reactions for which sign communication proves too slow.

A fundamentally new stage begins with the emergence of a temporal gap between receiving information and reacting to it. Such a condition first of all requires the development and improvement of memory. Another most important result is the transformation of reaction from immediate action into a sign. The reaction to information becomes an independent structure capable of accumulation, with an increasingly complex and self-developing mechanism.

At this stage, reaction has lost its immediate impulsiveness, but has not yet become relatively free and therefore potentially governable. Its mechanism is still conditioned by physiological impulses lying outside the speaker's conscious will, but it is already sufficiently independent. The primary expression of this stage is dream.

One can suppose that in a psychological condition in which thoughts and actions were inseparable, dream visions constituted a sphere in which their division and independent, isolated experience were impossible. Speech and gesture, and more broadly the entire sphere of language with its possibilities, connected much more powerful mechanisms and muffled the potential capacity of dream to become a developed sphere of self-sufficient consciousness. Yet this domain did not surrender its positions without resistance.

It is entirely obvious that archaic human beings possessed a much greater culture of dream, that is, they probably saw dreams and remembered them as much more coherent. Nor should we forget that shamanic culture undoubtedly possessed a technique for managing dreams, not to mention systems for their coherent retelling and interpretation. Retelling in dream culture, as Pavel Florensky showed, plays an enormous role, because it further organizes the system of dreams, in particular by giving them a temporal, linear composition.

The development of speaking pushed this domain into the background of culture and caused its primitivization. Progress and dynamics in some areas of thought inevitably cause regression in spheres that had to yield their leading position. Thus the development of writing undoubtedly caused the degradation of oral culture. The need disappeared for a developed mnemonics that had previously occupied an exceptionally high place, especially mnemonic devices such as singing and poetry.

We will not touch on the quite complex and tangled problem of the Freudian conception of dream. We will stop only at the parallel between that method and the most ancient interpretations of dreams, reaching back to the epoch before culture.

Entering the world of dreams, archaic humanity, still without writing, found itself before a space similar to reality and, at the same time, not reality. This world, it was natural to suppose, had meaning, but its meaning was unknown. These were signs of unknown things, that is, signs in pure form. Their meaning was indeterminate and had to be established. Therefore at the beginning there lay a semiotic experiment.

Apparently the biblical assertion that in the beginning was the word should be understood in this same sense. The word preceded its meaning: the human being knew that it was a word, that it had meaning, but did not know what meaning. He spoke, as it were, in a language he did not understand. The perception of dream as a message implied an idea of the one from whom this message came. Later, in more developed mythological spheres, dream is identified with an Alien prophetic voice; it represents His address to me.

At an early stage, one can suppose, something occurred that resembles our experience of cinema: first person and third person merged rather than being distinguished. "I" and "he" were mutually identical. At the next stage there arose the problem of dialogue. We observe a parallel sequence in a child's acquisition of language. This quality of dream as pure form allowed it to be a space ready for filling. The shaman interpreting dreams is as "scientific" as the experienced Freudian. Dream is a semiotic mirror, and each person sees in it the reflection of his own language.

The basic feature of this language is its enormous indeterminacy. This makes it inconvenient for transmitting constant messages and extraordinarily suited to inventing new information. Dream was perceived as a message from a mysterious other, though in fact it is an informationally free "text for the sake of text." Just as art becomes conscious of itself after winning the right to be free of meaning and of any external tasks, the possibility of being meaningful precedes the concept of correct meaningfulness.

Yet the need for the collectively understood transmission of information sharply dominated the drive to expand the boundaries of linguistic invention. The language of dream could not withstand this competition. In the movement of languages, culture systematically creates periods in which simplified but adequately understood languages triumph over rich but individually understood ones. Thus at the end of the twentieth century we are witnessing the retreat of the languages of art, especially poetry and cinema, before the assault of languages serving technical progress. In Europe in the first half of our century the balance of forces was exactly the opposite. Of course, every "victory" in culture is only a shift of emphasis within dialogue.

A shift of functions occurred. Dream, precisely because of the qualities that made it inconvenient for practical-communicative functioning, naturally passed into the sphere of communication with divinities, divination, and prediction. Poetry's entry into the sacred sphere marked the beginning of the displacement of dream's monopoly even there, although the connection between poetic inspiration and mystical dream is a universal phenomenon in many cultures. Dream was displaced to the periphery of sacred space.

Dream-as-prediction, a window into the mysterious future, is replaced by the idea of dream as a path inward into oneself. For the function of dream to change, the place of mysterious space had to change. From external it became internal. Our comparison of the Freudian and the shaman contains not a grain of pejorative meaning. It indicates the capacity, at different cultural moments, of magic as a science of prediction and medicine as a means of salvation to perform identical functions: to become objects of belief.

Belief has enormous power over the human being and can in fact work miracles. If the patient believes, then the remedy helps him. Emile Zola, in the novel *Doctor Pascal*, told how a doctor invented a universal medicine that helped all patients miraculously. Both patients and doctor were seized by enthusiasm and believed sacredly in the medicine's miraculous power. One day Doctor Pascal accidentally injected a patient with distilled water. The effect was as curative as that of the medicine. The patients believed so strongly in the injections that they recovered from distilled water. Belief is not "opium for the people," but a powerful means of self-organization. Belief in the mysterious meaning of dreams is based on belief in the meaning of the message as such. One can say that dream is the father of semiotic processes.

Dream is distinguished by polylinguality: it immerses us not in visual, verbal, musical, and other spaces separately, but in their fused unity, analogous to reality. It is unreal reality. The translation of dream into the languages of human communication is accompanied by a decrease in indeterminacy and an increase in communicativity. Later, this path will pass to art. In the step from dream-vision to linguistic communication with the gods, the gods' transition into the language of riddles and mysterious sayings, with a high degree of indeterminacy, proved entirely natural.

In light of what has been said, the "primitive syncretism" that attracted the attention of Academician A. N. Veselovsky receives new illumination. This is not the acting out "in persons" of certain texts, but the reproduction of an event in another language, one nevertheless extremely close to it. The root division of the space of life and death, which we now experience as the antithesis between practical reality, on the one hand, and the many-voiced space that includes all diverse modeling systems, on the other, was reduced at the early archaic stage to a linguistic binary: practical language and dream.

Of course, such a picture is a rough schema. In the real sphere it was realized as a complex and contradictory semiotic tangle floating in semiotic space.

In contemporary culture, dream has another feature that makes it a convenient field for various Freudian-type theories. Dream is a message with the image of its source hidden. This zero space can be filled by various bearers of messages depending on the type of interpreting culture. The criminal brother in Schiller's *The Robbers* repeated the Helvetian formula: "Dreams come from the stomach." Schiller himself considered dream the voice of a person's repressed conscience, a hidden inner judge. The ancient Romans saw dreams as predictions of the gods; modern Freudians see in them the voice of repressed sexuality.

If we generalize all these interpretations, bringing them under a common formula, we receive the idea of a powerful authority hidden in mysterious depths and governing the human being. This authority speaks with the person in a language whose understanding, in principle, requires the presence of a translator. Dream needs an interpreter, whether this is a modern psychologist or a pagan priest.

Dream has one more feature: it is individual. One cannot penetrate into someone else's dream. Therefore it is a principled "language for one person." Connected with this is the extreme difficulty of communication in this language. To retell a dream is as difficult as, say, to retell a musical work in words. This unretellability of dream makes every act of remembering it a transformation, only approximately expressing its essence.

Thus dream is surrounded by numerous limitations that make it an extremely fragile and multi-meaning means of storing information. But precisely these "defects" allow one to attribute to dream a special and very significant cultural function: to be a reserve of semiotic indeterminacy, a space that still has to be filled with meanings. This makes dream an ideal `Ich-Erzahlung`, capable of being filled by diverse interpretations, both mystical and aesthetic.
