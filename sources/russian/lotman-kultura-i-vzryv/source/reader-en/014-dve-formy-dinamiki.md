## Two Forms of Dynamics

The relation among different forms of dynamics determines the specificity of two basic types of process. We have designated the difference between them as the opposition between explosion and gradual development. Yet we should once again warn against taking these concepts literally, in a sense dictated at its foundation by everyday experience. In particular, the concept of explosion is connected with the everyday meaning of the word only in isolated cases.

Repeated circular movements, such as calendrical ones, cannot be considered explosive despite all the changes connected with them. What matters here is something else: as a result of development, which may proceed in very turbulent forms, the system returns to its starting point. Thus pulsating development cannot be considered explosive, even if it includes individual stages that unfold violently.

The history of literary scholarship in the 1920s and 1930s knew two conceptions of romanticism. The author of one of them, V. M. Zhirmunsky, proposed a model of literary history as a chain of constant replacements between two fundamental types of art. He conditionally called them "classical and romantic art." He explained that he was speaking not of a historical phenomenon in its individual richness and peculiarity, but of a certain constant, timeless type of poetic creativity.

Zhirmunsky further explained this position as follows. The classical poet has before him an objective task: to create a beautiful work of art, finished and perfect, a self-sufficient world subordinated to its own special laws. By contrast, the romantic poet strives above all to tell us about himself, to "reveal his soul." He confesses and draws us into the emotional depths and human peculiarity of his soul.

The poems of Homer, the tragedies of Shakespeare and Racine, Plautus's and Moliere's comedies, Pushkin's *Poltava* and *The Stone Guest*, Goethe's *Wilhelm Meister* and *Hermann and Dorothea* contain, for the reader, no compulsion to go beyond the borders of a perfect and internally finished work of art and seek behind it the living human personality of the poet, his "spiritual biography." Byron's and Alfred de Musset's poems, Rousseau's *New Heloise*, the young Goethe's *Faust*, Fet's lyric poetry, and Alexander Blok's lyric and dramatic works, such as *The Stranger* and *The Rose and the Cross*, seem instead to draw us into a real spiritual world lying on the other side of the strict boundaries of art.

Thus the antithesis classicism-romanticism includes not only the opposition of two styles, but also a fundamentally different solution to the question of art and life. For us it is important to emphasize that, in Zhirmunsky's opinion, these two types of cultural model constitute a universal constant, regardless of whether they replace one another or coexist chronologically. Something else is also important: they are conceived as permanently present types of culture, not included in historical dynamics.

Unlike Zhirmunsky, G. A. Gukovsky began from a stadial conception of literary development. From his point of view, literary movement is a temporal sequence of typological stages replacing one another. Thus the peculiarity of a text is created at the intersection of typology and chronology and cannot be separated from the historical approach.

We will not touch on the historical criticism of either conception. In their time they played a positive role in the development of literary theory. What is essential for us now is to attend to the difference in their methodological foundations. Zhirmunsky studies literature as a shift of states entirely free of explosions; Gukovsky studies it as a chain of explosions. It is interesting that, however much Gukovsky tries to mask this, the transition from one stage to another occurs "suddenly," by a certain decisive break.

Another example is also revealing. It is well known that N. Ya. Marr's conception of decisive qualitative breaks in the development of language, drawn from Hegelian laws of dialectic, sharply diverged from the realities of linguistic development, which as a rule has a gradual character. This circumstance was emphasized in attacks on Marrism by Stalin and Vinogradov. It is all the more notable that when Marr's ideas returned to their native ground, the sphere of culture, folklore, and literature, they began to look far less absurd.

The stadial theory of cultural development that we touched on in connection with Gukovsky was also shared by Zhirmunsky. A special meeting of the literary departments of Leningrad State University in 1949 was devoted to questions of stadiality in the development of culture. Zhirmunsky and Gukovsky gave the main reports there. The meeting was later subjected to unscientific criticism, and a barrage of political accusations was directed at the speakers, but a scholarly discussion of the problem did not take place.

O. M. Freidenberg drew attention to the explosive character of processes in culture, folklore, and literature. Works by the "Marrists" devoted to problems of cultural processes not only lacked the arbitrary character that marks Marr's late linguistic works, but have preserved scholarly interest up to the present. Such, for example, is Freidenberg's work *Thersites*, which contains a scholarly analysis of the function of the fool in folklore and literature across the centuries.

In polemic with the formalists, Freidenberg concentrates attention on the impossibility of separating text from myth, and myth from everyday behavior. The fool, for her, is not simply a literary device or literary topic, but an imprint of social ideology. In life, especially court life, the fool had a Thersites-like appearance. A person born with a hump and definite features of ugliness went into the role of fool. The literary view was therefore adjacent to the everyday view. Both were called forth by the general semantics of the "fool," who represented one of the metaphors of death, just as the "king" represented one of the metaphors of life.

Freidenberg then considers the movement of the fool's image through genre stages. At a later stage, invective becomes a ritual action, particularly a military ritual, and the functions of the fool are performed by the king. In order to raise the spirit of the leader or army, he publicly pronounces abuse by name, and the militant spirit is renewed. Freidenberg formulates her conclusion as follows: paleontological semantics takes shape as a schematic structure of plot; the plot structure creates content foreign to that structure; and one state passes into its opposite.

History is rich in paradoxes, and opposites often gravitate toward one another. In this sense it would be an interesting task, not without historical irony, to trace how the deep movements of thought in O. M. Freidenberg and M. M. Bakhtin tend toward rapprochement, breaking through the polar opposition of terminologies, views, and authorial personalities.

What has been said belongs to distant stages of scholarly development. It is therefore all the more interesting to note their continuing fruitfulness and to point to the deep roots of contemporary scholarly ideas.

Strangely enough, the mechanisms of gradual processes have been studied much less. It seemed that here the problem itself was absent: it was enough simply to point to slowed development, gradualness, or even the absence of dynamics in one or another phenomenon. Yet gradual processes constitute an exceptionally important aspect of historical movement. First of all, we must abandon the idea of their stability.

Especially interesting are those movements that cannot even be defined as processes, because in them the starting and ending points of the cycle coincide. Between these supporting points, however, complex movements may occur. Calendrical cycles are one example. They are undoubtedly sources for the idea of cyclic changes in linear development within human consciousness. But calendrical processes themselves are only one of the manifestations most noticeable to human beings of a pulsating cyclicity that permeates both cosmic and elementary levels.

The significance of slow and pulsating processes in the general structure of human existence is not inferior to the role of explosive ones. It should be added that in historical reality all these types of process intertwine and act on one another, now accelerating and now slowing the general movement.
