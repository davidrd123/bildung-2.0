## "The End! How Sonorous This Word..."

In 1832, in a letter to M. A. Lopukhina, Lermontov wrote:

The end! How sonorous this word!
How many-few thoughts are in it!

This "many-few" introduces us into the very essence of the "problem of the end."

Human behavior is meaningful. This means that human activity implies some goal. But the concept of a goal inevitably includes the idea of a certain end of the event. The human drive to attribute meaning and purpose to actions and events implies the division of continuous reality into certain conditional segments. This is inevitably joined to the human drive to understand what is the object of observation. Pushkin emphasized the connection between meaning and understanding in "Verses Composed at Night During Insomnia":

I want to understand you;
I seek meaning in you...

That which has no end has no meaning. Meaning-making is connected with the segmentation of nondiscrete space. In this respect, the attribution of meanings to reality, in particular in the process of artistic meaning-making, inevitably includes segmentation.

Life has no beginning and no end.
But you, artist, firmly believe
In beginnings and endings.

Two essential consequences follow from what has been said. The first, in the sphere of reality, is connected with the special semantic role of death in human life. The second, in the domain of art, determines the dominant role of beginnings and endings, especially endings. A demonstrative case of refusal of ending, as in *Eugene Onegin*, only confirms this regularity. The ordinary process of making sense of reality is connected with transferring onto it discrete divisions, in particular literary plots. Pushkin performs a bold experiment by introducing the nondiscreteness of life into poetry. Thus there arises a novel demonstratively deprived of an ending. Compare Tvardovsky's refusal, in poetry, of the literary categories of beginning and end and his opposition to them of living reality lying outside them:

In a word: "a book about a fighter."
Well then, what is the matter?
No beginning, no end:
It does not fit the file.

The purely literary problem of ending has, in reality, the problem of death as its equivalent.

Beginning-ending and death are inseparably connected with the possibility of understanding life's reality as something meaningful. The tragic contradiction between the infinity of life as such and the finitude of human life is only a particular manifestation of a deeper contradiction between the genetic code, which lies outside the category of life and death, and the individual being of the organism. From the moment individual being becomes conscious being, the being of consciousness, this whole contradiction is transformed from a characteristic of an anonymous process into a tragic property of life.

Just as the concept of art is connected with reality, the idea of text and textual boundary is inseparably joined with life and death.

The first stage of the aspect under consideration is realized in biology as the problem of reproduction and death. The continuity of the process of reproduction is antithetically connected with the discontinuity of individual being. Yet until this contradiction becomes an object of self-consciousness, it "as if does not exist." An unconscious contradiction does not become a factor of behavior. In the sphere of culture, the first stage of struggle against "ends" is the cyclic model that dominates mythological and folkloric consciousness.

After, or more precisely as, mythological thinking was replaced by historical thinking, the concept of the end acquired a dominant character. The need to reconcile the nondiscreteness of being with the discreteness of consciousness, and the immortality of nature with the mortality of the human being, generated the idea of cyclicity; the transition to linear consciousness stimulated the image of death-rebirth. From this followed the mythological idea of the aged father reborn in the young son and the idea of death-birth.

Here, however, there also ran an essential division. In the cyclic system, death-rebirth was experienced by one and the same eternal divinity. Linear repetition created the image of another, as a rule a son, in whose image the dead man was reborn in his likeness. Since, at the same time, the feminine principle was conceived as nondiscrete, that is, immortal and eternally young, the new young hero affirmed himself by sexual union with the feminine principle, sometimes understood as his own mother. From this come the ritual union of the Venetian doge with the sea, or the rite observed by D. K. Zelenin at the end of the nineteenth century, in which peasants rolled the priest across a field and forced him to imitate sexual intercourse with the earth.

This ritual communion of the mortal yet renewed young father-son with the immortal mother, transformed in the pseudo-scientific mythology of the fin de siecle, generated a significant part of Freudian ideas. And since the general direction of the epoch proceeded under the sign of turning ideas and fantastic theories into a secondary quasi-reality that seemed to confirm the very ideas of which it was itself the product, the illusion of a certain scientific verification arose.

The linear construction of culture makes the problem of death one of the dominant problems in the system of culture. Religious consciousness is the path of overcoming death by "trampling down death by death." Yet culture is too deeply immersed in human space to limit itself to this and simply remove the problem of death as imaginary. The concept of death, or ending, cannot be solved by simple negation, if only because cosmic and human structures intersect here. Baratynsky had this in mind when he wrote of death:

When the blooming world arose
From the equilibrium of wild forces,
The Almighty entrusted
Its order to your keeping.

For such consciousness, death becomes "the resolution of all riddles" and "the release from all chains." But to understand the place of death in culture means to make sense of it in the direct meaning of those words, that is, to attribute meaning to it. And this means inclusion in some semantic series that defines sets of synonyms and antonyms.

The basis of reconciliation with death, if religious motivations are excluded, is its naturalness and its independence from human will. The inevitability of death, its connection with ideas of old age and illness, allow it to be included in a neutral evaluative sphere. All the more significant, therefore, are cases in which death is joined with ideas of youth, health, and beauty: images of violent death. The addition of voluntariness gives this theme an optimal semantic load.

The complexity of the meanings created here lies in the fact that in order to make a situation the bearer of meaning, it must be perceived as contradicting the natural, that is, neutral, order of things. The drive to preserve life is the natural feeling of every living thing. The possibility of referring to the natural laws of nature and to immediate feeling free of theories is a weighty enough foundation to give reasoning based on it indisputable persuasiveness.

It is precisely the persuasiveness of this position that forces Shakespeare, though through Falstaff's mouth, to say that the time has not come and he has no desire to give up life before it is due. Why hurry if God does not demand it? Honor may spur him on, but what if honor unspurs him when he goes into battle? Can honor set a leg? No. Or an arm? No. Can it stop the pain of a wound? No. Then honor is a poor surgeon. What is honor? A word. What is in that word? Air. Who has honor? The man who died on Wednesday. Does he feel it? No. Hear it? No. Perhaps it will live among the living? No, because slander will not allow it. Honor, then, is no more than a heraldic shield carried behind a coffin.

Falstaff is a coward. But the objectivity of Shakespeare's genius made us hear not the muttering of a coward but the impeccable exposition of a conception that anticipates sensualist ethics. Leaping ahead of centuries, Falstaff expounds sensualist ideas more fully and objectively than Franz Moor does. In Falstaff's monologue, before us is not cowardice but courage: the courage of a theoretician unafraid to draw extreme conclusions from his initial premises. The comic character here grows into an ideologue, and the conflict acquires the character of a clash of ideas. Each of them, internally, possesses not only psychological but also logical justification.

The position opposite to Falstaff's is realized in culture as an apology for heroism, finding its extreme manifestation in heroic madness. The Arabic inscription on Shamil's order in the Hermitage collection reads: "He who foresees the consequences will not do the great." In these words lies a finished philosophy of history. It recalls numerous apologies of heroic madness, all the way to Beranger's "Madmen," which in V. Kurochkin's translation produced such a loud response in the Russian populist milieu.

A special form of victory over death and overcoming it is suicide, for example the deliberate suicide of the Lafargue spouses.

Michel Vovelle's extensive work *Death and the West: From 1300 to the Present* persuasively shows that the stability and boundedness of an initial language stimulate its avalanche-like transformations, which turn the original content into a formality. Precisely this makes it possible, by turning the idea of death into a formalized set of expressive means, to separate it from the primary series of meanings and transform it into one of the universal languages of culture.

An analogous process occurs in the cultural symbolism of sex. The fundamental error of Freudianism is its ignoring of the fact that something can become a language only at the cost of losing immediate reality and being transferred into a purely formal, "empty" sphere, and therefore a sphere ready for any content. So long as sex preserves its immediate emotional, always individual, reality and its physiological foundation, it cannot become a universal language. To do that it must formalize itself and completely separate itself, as the example of the defeated baboon shows, from sexuality as content.

The spread of various versions of Freudianism, covering whole layers of twentieth-century mass culture, convinces us that they rest least of all on immediate impulses of natural sexuality. They testify that phenomena, once they have become language, hopelessly lose connection with immediate extra-semiotic reality. Epochs in which sex becomes the object of intensified cultural attention are times of its physiological decline, not its flourishing. From the domain of the semiotics of culture, sex returns secondarily into physiological practice, but already as a tertiary metaphor of culture. Attempts to return into physiological practice everything that culture produces first of all with the word do not make culture a metaphor of sex, as Freud claimed; they make sex a metaphor of culture. For this, only one thing is required of sex: that it cease to be sex.
