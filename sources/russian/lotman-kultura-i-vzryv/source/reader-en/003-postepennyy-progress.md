## Gradual Progress

Forward movement is realized in two ways. Our sense organs react to small portions of stimuli, which at the level of consciousness are perceived as a certain continuous movement. In this sense, continuity is meaningful predictability. Its opposite is unpredictability: change realized in the order of explosion. Against this background, predictable development appears to be a much less essential form of movement.

The unpredictability of explosive processes is by no means the only path to the new. More than that, whole spheres of culture can carry out their movement only in the form of gradual changes. Gradual and explosive processes, while they form an antithesis, exist only in relation to one another.

The destruction of one pole would lead to the disappearance of the other. All the explosive dynamic processes mentioned in the preceding chapter are realized in a complex dynamic dialogue with mechanisms of stabilization. We should not be misled by the fact that in historical reality they appear as enemies, each striving for the complete destruction of the other pole. Such a thing would be fatal for culture. Fortunately, it is not realizable. Even when people are firmly convinced that they are putting some ideal theory into practice, the practical sphere includes opposite tendencies as well. They may take on distorted forms, but they cannot be destroyed.

Gradual processes possess a powerful force of progress. In this respect, the relation between scientific discoveries and technical realizations is interesting. The greatest scientific ideas are, in a certain sense, akin to art: their origin is like an explosion. The technical realization of new ideas develops according to the laws of gradual dynamics.

For this reason, scientific ideas can be untimely. Technology, of course, also knows cases in which its possibilities remained unrecognized, for example the use of gunpowder in ancient China in the practice of fireworks and pyrotechnics. But on the whole it is characteristic of technology that practical needs act as powerful stimulators of progress. Thus the new in technology is the realization of the expected; the new in science and art is the realization of the unexpected.

From this follows a more particular consequence: in the familiar phrase "science and technology," the conjunction "and" does not cover an ideal harmony at all. It lies on the border of a deep conflict.

In this sense the position of historians of the school of *la longue duree* is very characteristic. Through their efforts, gradual slow processes were introduced into history as equal constituent parts of it. The development of technology, everyday life, and trade pushed into the background both the turns of political struggle and the phenomena of art. Jean Delumeau's interesting and innovative book *La civilisation de la Renaissance* is devoted to a careful examination precisely of those spheres of historical reality that are organized by gradual dynamic processes.

It is revealing that Delumeau demonstratively dedicates his book to civilization, not to culture. We could interpret this opposition as an antithesis between gradual and explosive processes.

Delumeau examines geographical discoveries, the role of technical progress, trade, changes in forms of production, and the development of finance. Technical inventions, and even historical discoveries, are inscribed into this picture of universal movement, a picture drawn monumentally by Delumeau's hand. This movement appears before us as the smooth flow of a broad and powerful river. The individual person, with his discoveries and inventions, realizes himself only to the extent that he gives himself over to the force of this flow.

A mined field with unpredictable places of explosion and a spring river carrying its powerful but directed current: these are the two visual images that arise in the consciousness of the historian studying dynamic, explosive processes and gradual processes.

The mutual necessity of these two structural tendencies does not cancel their reciprocal conditionedness. On the contrary, it sharply brings it out. One of them does not exist without the other. Yet from the subjective point of view of each of them, the other appears as an obstacle that must be overcome and as an enemy whose destruction should be sought.

Thus, from the point of view of the "explosive" position, the opposite position appears as the embodiment of a whole complex of negative qualities. An extreme example may be the perception of gradualists, a term from Ivan Turgenev, by nihilists, or of liberals by revolutionaries. But this same opposition can also be translated into the language of the romantic antithesis "genius and crowd."

Bulwer-Lytton gives a dialogue between a genuine dandy and a vulgar imitator of dandyism, a dialogue between a genius of fashion and his pitiful copyist:

"True," agreed Ralston, entering into a conversation about the relation between the genuine dandy and the tailor. "True. Stultz tries to make a gentleman, not coats. Every stitch of his aspires to aristocratism; there is a dreadful vulgarity in that. You can recognize a coat made by Stultz unerringly anywhere. That is enough to reject it. If a man can be recognized by the unchanging, and moreover by no means original, cut of his clothes, then in essence there is nothing more to say about him. A man must make the tailor, not the tailor the man."

"True, by God!" cried Sir Willoughby, as badly dressed as dinners are badly served at Lord Ixxx's. "Completely true! I have always urged my Schneiders to sew for me neither according to fashion nor against it; not to copy my coats and pantaloons from those made for others, but to cut them according to my build, and in no case in the manner of an isosceles triangle. Look at this coat, for instance." And Sir Willoughby Townshend straightened up and froze so that we might freely admire his attire.

"Coat!" exclaimed Ralston, arranging an expression of naive astonishment on his face, and with distaste took the edge of the collar between two fingers. "A coat, Sir Willoughby? In your opinion, this object is a coat?"

Two points of view on Sir Willoughby are possible. From the point of view of genuine dandyism, he is a copyist and imitator. From the point of view of the audience around him, he is a dandy who destroys habitual norms and creates new ones. Thus there arises the problem of genuine explosion and the imitation of explosion as a form of anti-explosive structure. This is the relation between Pechorin and Grushnitsky, between Lermontov and Martynov. Belinsky's criticism of Marlinsky is built in this connection.

The mutual accusation that people of explosion and gradualists level against one another is affected originality on one side and vulgarity on the other. The stagnation that settled into Russian society after the defeat of the Decembrists, under conditions of censorship repression, Pushkin's death, and Baratynsky's voluntary self-isolation, generated a wave of imaginary innovation. Precisely those writers who were most closely connected with the vulgarity of the average reader's tastes imitated stormy innovation. Vulgarity stylized itself as originality. The stamp of this self-masking lies on Eisenstein's late films.

Yet in complex secondary models the accusers exchange swords, like Hamlet and Laertes, and then the refined line appears:

It is unbeautiful to be famous.

Pasternak, in essence, develops Pushkin's idea of the poetic quality of the ordinary and, in the high sense, the unchanging.

If we refuse evaluations, then before us are two sides of one process, mutually necessary and constantly replacing each other within the unity of dynamic development. The contradictory complexity of the historical process successively activates now one form, now the other.

At the present moment, European civilization, including America and Russia, is living through a period of general discrediting of the very idea of explosion. In the eighteenth through twentieth centuries, humanity lived through a process that can be described as the realization of a metaphor: sociocultural processes came under the influence of the image of explosion not as a philosophical concept, but in its vulgar correlation with the explosion of gunpowder, dynamite, or the atomic nucleus.

Explosion as a phenomenon of physics, only metaphorically transferable to other processes, became identified for modern human beings with ideas of destruction and became a symbol of destructiveness. But if associations such as the age of great discoveries, the Renaissance, or art in general lay at the basis of our present-day notions, then the concept of explosion would remind us rather of phenomena such as the birth of a new living being or any other creative transformation of the structure of life.

In Belinsky's literary-critical legacy there is a somewhat unexpected idea, first noticed and subjected to historical analysis by Nikolai Mordovchenko. It concerns the opposition of geniuses and talents and, correspondingly, of literature and journalism.

Geniuses, the creators of art, are unpredictable in their creativity and do not submit to the directive influence of criticism. At the same time, between genius and reader there is always, in Pushkin's phrase, a certain "inaccessible line." The reader's failure to understand a work of genius is not an exception but the norm.

From this Belinsky drew a bold conclusion: a genius who works for eternity and posterity may not only be misunderstood by his contemporaries, but may even be useless to them. His usefulness is hidden in historical perspective. But the contemporary needs art, even if it is not so deep and not so durable, that is capable of being received by the reader today.

This idea of Belinsky's is well interpreted through the antithesis between "explosive" and "gradual" processes. Another feature follows from it. In order to be mastered by contemporaries, a process must have a gradual character. At the same time, however, the contemporary is drawn toward moments of explosion inaccessible to him, at least in art. The reader would like his author to be a genius, but at the same time he would like this author's works to be understandable.

This is how Kukolnik or Benediktov are created: writers who occupy the vacant place of genius and are its imitation. Such an "accessible genius" delights the reader with the comprehensibility of his work, and criticism with its predictability. The critic who unerringly indicates the future paths of such a writer is inclined to attribute this to his own insight.

In this sense, Marlinsky's prose, in its antithesis to the prose of Merimee or Lermontov, can be interpreted as a peculiar orientation toward the level of the reader. This is all the more curious because Marlinsky's romantic position placed him "above vulgarity" and required him to combine romanticism with Sternean mockery of the reader.

The question here cannot be reduced to the opposition of one artistic movement to another, because this two-stepped character of genuine explosion appears at different stages of art and is not peculiar only to art. The same Belinsky, in creating the natural school, treated the writers of this direction in principle as belletrists who were creating art needed by the reader and located at a level understandable to him.

Thus between literature and belles lettres there is the same interval as between the moment of explosion and the new stage of gradual development that arises on its basis. In essence, analogous processes also occur in the sphere of cognition. With a certain degree of conditionality, they can be defined as the opposition between theoretical science and technology.
