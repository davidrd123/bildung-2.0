## "I" and "I"

At the beginning of the *Confessions*, Rousseau wrote: "I alone. I know my heart and I know men. I am made differently from anyone I have seen; I dare to think that I resemble no one in the world." From *Emile* to the *Confessions*, Rousseau traveled a long road. The "I" in *Emile* is a pronoun and refers to the one who expresses the essence of speaking in the first person. In *Emile*, the question is the essence of the human being as such. Therefore the narrator is the embodiment of natural reason, while the pupil is Nature finding itself.

In the *Confessions*, "I" is a proper name: that which has no plural and cannot be alienated from one single and irreplaceable human personality. The epigraph is not accidental: "Intus et in cute," taken from the Roman poet Aulus Persius Flaccus, meaning inwardly and under the skin. Rousseau traveled the path from "I" as pronoun to "I" as proper name. This is one of the basic poles of human thought.

Tradition has instilled in us the idea that the course of human consciousness is directed from the individual, or singular, toward the universal. If by individual we mean the ability to increase the number of differences, to find the different in one and the same thing, then this is of course one of the basic achievements of cultural progress. It is only necessary to note that the ability to see the different in one thing and the one in difference are two inseparable sides of a single progress of consciousness. Failure to distinguish the different does not emphasize similarity; it destroys it, because it eliminates comparison altogether.

The structure of "I" is one of the basic indicators of culture. "I" as a pronoun is much simpler in structure than "I" as a proper name. The latter is not a firmly delimited linguistic sign.

Different languages use different grammatical means to express the distinction between a word designating any thing and precisely this thing. In Russian, one could express this by using capital letters in the second case. A little table is any little table; in German the indefinite article `ein` functions in an analogous way. But Little Table with a capital letter is my little table, personally familiar and unique. It is the Little Table that has features absent from "little tables in general." For example, it may have an ink stain. An ink stain cannot be a feature of the little table in general, but it may be an inseparable mark of this particular little table.

Fonvizin, in *The Minor*, reproduces the following scene:

"Pravdin: Door, for example: what kind of word is it, a noun or an adjective?

Mitrofan: Door, which door?

Pravdin: Which door! This one.

Mitrofan: This one? An adjective.

Pravdin: Why?

Mitrofan: Because it is attached to its place. Over there by the closet, a door has been standing for six weeks, not yet hung; so that one is a noun for the time being.

Starodum: Then by your reasoning the word fool is an adjective, because it is attached to a foolish person?

Mitrofan: Of course."

Fonvizin's rationalist consciousness presents abstract thought to us as true, and concrete thought as a manifestation of stupidity.

Another point of view, however, is possible. We have already noted that for Rousseau in the *Confessions*, the bearer of truth is precisely an "I" from which no plural can be formed. Above we gave the example of Vladimir Solovyov's use of proper names as a child. For the rationalist, the concrete world is only an example for illustrating general rules. The relation between text and moral in the fable is usually built according to this schema. The moral represents an unambiguous logical interpretation of meaning.

It is characteristic that Krylov, overcoming the genre rationalism of the fable, created a much more complex system of relations between "text" and "moral." In his work, the moral becomes not the expression of an abstract truth, but the voice of popular talk, of common sense.

Rousseau's daring gesture, declaring the highest meaning of "nontypical truth" and the value of Jean-Jacques as a separate and unique personality, opened the way to the re-creation of the human image in the novel and portraiture of the following epoch. These genres are not allegorical. Diderot's *Salons* are indicative here, since they persistently drive out allegorism and affirm the portrait-like character of the human image in painting. They affirm a new type of artistic consciousness: the hero is at once unrepeatably singular and, at the same time, somehow connected with the reader and viewer by features of likeness. The handmade behaves like the living. Connected with this is a broad chain of mythological plots, reaching back at least to antiquity, about the animation of a handmade work of art.

It is characteristic that rationalist consciousness, especially widespread in school practice, persistently strives to interpret the artistic text in a one-plane way. It does not matter that essay topics of earlier times such as "Onegin as a typical representative of noble society" have been replaced by topics such as "Tatyana, Russian in soul." The general tendency to "straighten contradictions" and reduce variety to unambiguousness has not changed. More than once one has heard, for example, that complicating the image of Tatyana with the contradiction introduced by the lines:

She knew Russian poorly...
And expressed herself with difficulty
In her own native tongue,

lowers the image's "educational" significance.

It would be too simple, however, to assign blame to school practice and lay everything on teachers. The issue is a contradiction inherent in art, one that is at once its strength and its weakness and cannot be overcome in principle, because it allows the artistic text to operate simultaneously with proper names and common nouns.
