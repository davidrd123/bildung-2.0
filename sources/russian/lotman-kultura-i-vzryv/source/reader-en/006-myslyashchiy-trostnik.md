## The Thinking Reed

The problem of culture cannot be solved without defining its place in outside-cultural space. The question can be formulated in this way: the distinctiveness of the human being as a cultural creature requires that he be opposed to the world of nature, understood as outside-cultural space.

The boundary between these two worlds will not only separate the human being from other outside-cultural beings. It will also pass through human psychology and activity. In certain aspects, the human being belongs to culture; in others, he is connected with the outside-cultural world. It would be equally imprudent to exclude the animal world categorically from the sphere of culture. Thus the boundary is blurred, and the definition of each concrete fact as belonging to the cultural or the outside-cultural sphere has a high degree of relativity.

But what is relative when applied to an individual case is sufficiently tangible when we speak in categories of abstract classification. With this qualification, we can single out the world of culture as an object for further reflection.

In a poem with the epigraph "There is musical modulation in the reedy banks," Tyutchev posed with philosophical precision this question that was always tormenting for him: the question of the double nature of the human being as a creature inscribed in nature and yet not contained by it.

There is songfulness in the sea waves,
Harmony in elemental disputes,
And a slender musical rustle
Flows in the wavering reeds.

An untroubled order is in everything,
Full consonance in nature;
Only in our phantom freedom
Do we recognize discord with it.

From where, and how, did discord arise?
And why in the common chorus
Does the soul not sing what the sea sings,
And why does the thinking reed murmur?

And from the earth to the outermost stars,
Still unanswered even now,
Is the voice crying in the wilderness,
The desperate protest of the soul?

Tyutchev's theme of the "double abyss," which has a large philosophical-poetic tradition, receives a specific interpretation here. Nature, according to Tyutchev, is endowed with harmony. Opposed to it is the disharmony of the human soul.

The philosophical concept of harmony implies "unity in diversity." In music it means the consonance of many tones into one whole, and from there it was borrowed by the Pythagoreans, who taught the harmony of the spheres: the regular movement of the heavenly bodies around the central fire. Citing this definition, E. L. Radlov adds that Swedenborg speaks of an "established," or "constabilized," harmony that conditions the order of the world's organization.

Tyutchev's rupture between human being and world is a contradiction between harmony and disharmony. At the same time, harmony is thought by Tyutchev as eternally unchanging or eternally repeated being. The human being, meanwhile, is included in disharmonic, that is, nonsymmetrical, unfolded movement. We approach the fundamental question: the conflict between closed movement, that is, movement that repeats itself correctly, and movement directed linearly.

In the world of animals we encounter movement along a closed circle. Calendar changes, age changes, and other changes in this world are subordinate to the law of repeatability. In this sense, the difference between learning among animals and among humans is characteristic, despite the parallelism of these processes that scholars have often noted. Here and below, when speaking of animals, we will have in mind chiefly the higher mammals.

Among animals, significant forms of behavior, the goal and object of learning, stand opposed to insignificant forms. Significant behavior has a ritualized character. Hunting, competition among males, determining the leader, and other significant moments of behavior are shaped as a complex system of "correct" poses and gestures, that is, poses and gestures that have meaning and are understandable both to the actor and to his partner. All significant forms of behavior have the character of dialogues.

The meeting of two rival predators is ordinarily possible when one of them, owing to extraordinary circumstances, most often human intervention but sometimes hunger or natural disaster, has been knocked out of "its own" space and turned into a "wanderer." Contrary to human legends about the "disorganization" and "disorderliness" of animal behavior, such a meeting is shaped by a complex system of gestural rituals serving the exchange of information.

Through a system of gestures, animals inform one another of their "rights" to this space, their strength, their hunger, their intention to enter into decisive battle, and so on. Quite often this dialogue ends when one of its participants, without entering into battle, acknowledges itself defeated and communicates this to the opponent by a definite gesture, for example by tucking its tail.

Konrad Lorenz noted that when at the end of a duel one raven offers its eye to another raven, or one predator offers its throat to another predator, signs of admitting defeat, the battle ends, and the victor, as a rule, does not use the opportunity to destroy the defeated one physically. The difference expressed here between the combat behavior of animals and human beings generated the original meaning of the Russian proverb: "A raven will not peck out another raven's eye."

Movement in a closed circle also constitutes a definite side of human behavior, and in the archaic world a dominant one. Plato considered the ideal to be the construction of the social world according to such a model:

"The Athenian: Where laws are beautiful, or will be so later, can one suppose that all people endowed with creative gifts will be allowed, in the sphere of musical education and games, to teach what pleases the poet himself in rhythm, melody, and words? Is it permissible for boys and youths, the children of law-abiding citizens, to be subjected to the random influence of choruses in the matter of virtue and vice?

Clinias: How could it be? This is without rational foundation.

The Athenian: Yet at present this very thing is permitted in all states except Egypt.

Clinias: What laws about this exist in Egypt?

The Athenian: It is astonishing even to hear of them. Apparently from ancient times the Egyptians recognized the proposition we have just stated: in states, young people must become accustomed to beautiful bodily movements and beautiful songs. Having established what is beautiful, the Egyptians announced it at sacred festivals, and no one, neither painters nor anyone else who creates images of every sort, nor in general those occupied with the musical arts, was allowed to introduce innovations or invent anything different. Nor is this allowed now. So if you pay attention, you will find that works of painting or sculpture made there ten thousand years ago, and this is not ten thousand years as a figure of speech but truly so, are neither more beautiful nor uglier than present creations, because both were made by means of one and the same art."

It is no accident that for Plato the ideal embodiment of art is the "ancient sacred round dances." The closed round dance, a symbol of cyclical repetition in nature, becomes for Plato the ideal embodiment of art.

Cyclical repeatability is the law of biological existence. The animal world and the human being as part of that world are subordinated to it. But the human being is not wholly immersed in this world. As a "thinking reed," he stands in an original contradiction with the fundamental laws of his surroundings.

This is manifested in the differences characteristic of the essence of learning. The animal, as we have noted, learns a system of ritual behavior. Superiority is achieved through strength or speed in carrying out certain gestures, but never through the invention of a new gesture unexpected by the opponent. The animal can be compared with a dancer who is capable of perfecting a dance step but cannot sharply and unexpectedly replace the dance itself with something else.

Animal behavior is ritual. Human behavior tends toward the invention of the new, something unpredictable for its opponents. From the human point of view, stupidity is attributed to the animal. From the animal point of view, dishonesty, that is, nonobedience to the rules, is attributed to the human being. The human being constructs his image of the animal as a stupid human being. The animal constructs the image of the human being as a dishonorable animal.

An incomprehensible riddle of human history is the very fact that the pre-human survived despite being surrounded by predators infinitely superior to him in strength, teeth, and claws. It is impossible to attribute human survival to the ability to use tools. The earliest tools did not possess a decisive superiority over the fangs and claws of predators, and in addition human beings had young who were not armed with axes and spears.

Of course, we know of cases in which wolves starving in winter attack people, and analogous cases of attacks by other predators. But these are not the rule; they are excesses. One cannot say that the wolf feeds on people. The wolf feeds on mice, small rodents, hares, and in individual cases young horned cattle. Hunger also produces cases of cannibalism among humans, but from this one cannot conclude that humans feed on humans. Here we exclude from consideration cases of ritual cannibalism, which among humans have the character of magical acts, and cases of man-eating among animals, for example among tigers, which clearly have the character of an individual perversion.

In a "normal" situation, animals do not at all strive to make contact with the human being, even for the purpose of eating him. They remove themselves from him, while the human being from the very beginning, as hunter and trapper, strove toward contact with them.

The relation of animals to the human being can be called removal, a striving to avoid contact. If one attributes human psychology to animals, this could be called disgust. More likely, it is a striving to avoid instinctively unpredictable situations, something similar to what a human being experiences when encountering a madman.
