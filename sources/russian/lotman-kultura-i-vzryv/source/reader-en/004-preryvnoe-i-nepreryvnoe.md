## The Discontinuous and the Continuous

Until now we have been attending to the relation between moments of explosion and gradual development as two stages that alternately replace one another. But their relations also unfold in synchronous space. In the dynamics of cultural development, they are related not only by sequence, but also by their existence in a single, simultaneously operating mechanism.

Culture, as a complex whole, is composed of layers with different speeds of development. Any synchronous slice of it therefore reveals the simultaneous presence of different stages. Explosions in some layers can be combined with gradual development in others. This, however, does not exclude interaction among these layers.

For example, the dynamics of processes in the spheres of language and politics, morality and fashion, demonstrate different rates of movement. Faster processes can have an accelerating influence on slower ones, and the slower ones can appropriate the self-name of the faster and thereby accelerate their own development. Even so, their dynamics are not synchronous.

Still more essential is the simultaneous combination, in different spheres of culture, of explosive and gradual processes. The question is complicated by the fact that they appropriate inadequate self-names for themselves. This usually mystifies researchers. Researchers are inclined to reduce synchrony to structural unity and to interpret the aggression of some self-name as the establishment of structural unity. First a wave of self-namings, and then a second wave, that of scholarly terminology, artificially unify the picture of the process, smoothing over the contradictions among structures. Yet precisely in these contradictions lie the foundations of the mechanisms of dynamics.

Both gradual and explosive processes perform important functions in a synchronously operating structure. Some provide innovation; others provide continuity. In the self-assessment of contemporaries these tendencies are experienced as hostile, and the struggle between them is interpreted in the categories of a military battle of annihilation. In reality, they are two sides of one connected mechanism, its synchronous structure. The aggressiveness of one does not suppress the development of the opposite one, but stimulates it.

Thus, for example, the aggressiveness of the Karamzin-Zhukovsky tendency at the beginning of the nineteenth century stimulated the aggressiveness and development of the Shishkov-Katenin-Griboedov tendency. The victorious march of the "anti-romanticism" of Balzac-Flaubert was synchronously combined with the flourishing of Hugo's romanticism. The intersection of different structural organizations becomes a source of dynamics.

So long as it preserves its activity for an audience, an artistic text is a dynamic system. Traditional structuralism proceeded from a principle formulated already by the Russian formalists: the text is considered as a closed, self-sufficient, synchronically organized system. It is isolated not only in time, from past and future, but also spatially, from the audience and from everything located outside it.

The contemporary stage of structural-semiotic analysis has complicated these principles. In time, the text is perceived as a kind of freeze-frame, an artificially stopped moment between past and future. The relation between past and future is not symmetrical. The past is given in two manifestations. Internally, it appears as the immediate memory of the text, embodied in its internal structure, in that structure's inevitable contradictoriness, in its immanent struggle with its own internal synchronism. Externally, it appears as the relation to extra-textual memory.

If the viewer mentally places himself in that "present time" realized in the text, for example in a given painting at the moment when he looks at it, he turns his gaze, as it were, toward the past. The past converges like a cone whose apex rests on the present time. Turning toward the future, the audience is plunged into a bundle of possibilities that have not yet made their potential choice.

The unknownness of the future allows significance to be attributed to everything. Chekhov's famous gun, which according to the writer's own instruction must necessarily fire at the end of the play if it appears at the beginning, by no means always fires. Chekhov's rule had meaning only within the bounds of a definite genre, and one that had already settled into frozen forms. In reality, it is precisely not knowing whether the gun will fire or not, whether the shot will be a mortal wound or merely the fall of a can that imitates one, that gives the moment plot significance.

The indeterminacy of the future has its own boundaries, however blurred they may be. Excluded from it is what, within the given system, plainly cannot enter into it. The future appears as a space of possible states. The relation of present and future can be pictured in the following way. The present is a flash of semantic space that has not yet unfolded. It contains within itself, potentially, all the possibilities of future paths of development.

It is important to emphasize that the choice of one of these paths is determined neither by laws of causality nor by probability. At the moment of explosion these mechanisms are completely switched off. The choice of the future is realized as chance. For this reason it possesses a very high degree of informativeness. At the same time, the moment of choice is also the cutting off of those paths that are destined to remain only potentially possible, and the moment when the laws of causal connections again come into force.

The moment of explosion is at the same time the place where the informativeness of the whole system sharply increases. Here the curve of development leaps onto an entirely new, unpredictable, and more complex path. The dominant element that arises as the result of the explosion and determines future movement may be any element from the system, or even an element from another system, accidentally drawn by the explosion into the interweaving of possibilities of future movement. At the next stage, however, it already creates a predictable chain of events.

The death of a soldier from a shell fragment that accidentally crosses his path cuts off a whole chain of potentially possible future events. The elder of the Turgenev brothers died of an accidental illness at the very beginning of his creative life. In Kuchelbecker's words, this brilliant young man, whose talent could probably have been compared with Pushkin's, would have left a great mark on Russian literature. Here one can recall Pushkin's words about Lensky:

His silenced lyre
Could have raised, through the ages,
A deep, unbroken ringing...

If we remove the moment of unpredictability from the historical process, we make it wholly redundant. From the position of a bearer of Reason who occupies an external point of view toward the process, whether this is God, Hegel, or any philosopher who has mastered the "sole scientific method," this movement is deprived of informativeness.

Meanwhile, every attempt to forecast the future at its cardinally explosive moments demonstrates the impossibility of unambiguously foreseeing sharp turns of history. The historical process can be compared to an experiment. But this is not the illustrative demonstration that a physics teacher presents to his audience while knowing the result exactly in advance. It is the experiment a scientist sets for himself in order to discover laws still unknown even to him. From our point of view, the Chief Experimenter is not a teacher demonstrating his knowledge, but a researcher revealing the spontaneous information of his experiment.

The moment when the explosion is exhausted is the turning point of the process. In the sphere of history, this is not only the initial moment of future development, but also the site of self-knowledge. Those mechanisms of history are switched on that must explain to history itself what has happened.

Further development returns us, as it were, already in consciousness, to the starting point of the explosion. What has happened receives a new existence by being reflected in the observer's representations. Here a fundamental transformation of the event takes place: what happened by chance, as we have seen, appears as the only possible thing. Unpredictability is replaced, in the observer's consciousness, by regularity. From his point of view, the choice was fictive. "Objectively," it had been predetermined by the entire causal movement of preceding events.

This is precisely the process that occurs when the complex interweaving of causally conditioned and chance events called "history" becomes the object of description, first by contemporaries and then by historians. This double layer of descriptions is directed toward removing chance from events. Substitution of this kind is easily carried out in those spheres of history where gradualness dominates and explosive events play a minimal role. These are the layers of history in which, first, action develops most slowly, and second, the individual person plays a smaller role.

Thus descriptions of history by researchers of the French school of *L'histoire nouvelle* produce their most convincing results when slow and gradual processes are studied. It is equally regular that the history of technology is, as a rule, perceived by an epoch as anonymous. A painting is remembered by the painter's surname, but makes of automobile are remembered by firms and model names.

In Chekhov's story "A First-Class Passenger," the hero, a famous engineer who has built many bridges in his life and has made a number of technical discoveries, is outraged that his name is unknown to the public:

"In my lifetime I have built a couple dozen magnificent bridges in Russia, constructed water systems in three cities, worked in Russia, England, and Belgium... Secondly, I have written many specialized articles in my field. I found ways of extracting certain organic acids, so you will find my name in all foreign chemistry textbooks... I will not burden your attention with a list of my merits and works. I will only say that I have done much more than many a famous person. And what of it? Here I am, already old, getting ready to croak, one might say, and I am as well known as that black dog running along the embankment."

The hero of the story then becomes indignant that his mistress, a talentless provincial singer, enjoys wide fame and that her name has been repeated in the newspapers many times: "An empty girl, capricious, greedy, and besides that a fool." The hero indignantly relates the following episode:

"I remember it as if it were now. We were having the solemn opening of traffic across a newly built bridge... Well, I thought, now the public will stare their eyes out at me. Where can I hide? But I worried for nothing, my dear sir."

The hero did not attract the public's attention.

"Suddenly the public began to stir: whisper-whisper... Faces smiled, shoulders moved. 'They must have noticed me,' I thought. As if! Don't count on it."

The public's animation had been caused by the appearance of the very same singer of whom he had spoken so ironically.

The hero of the story blames the ignorance and lack of culture of the public. True, he immediately falls into a comic position himself, since he has never heard anything about his interlocutor, who turns out to be a major scholar. The narrator complains of injustice. Yet the roots of the phenomenon captured by Chekhov lie deeper. It is not only the superficiality and lack of culture of society that cause the injustice Chekhov noticed. The point is that the creative work of even a bad singer is personal by its nature, while the creative work of even a good engineer is, as it were, dissolved in the general anonymous progress of technology.

If the bridge had collapsed, the engineer's name would probably have been remembered, because that would have been an extraordinary event. A good bridge dissolves into the general flow of the level of technology. Its merits, if they are not extraordinary, are noticed by no one. The development of technology is predictable in general outline, and this is demonstrated, for example, by the most successful works in the field of science fiction. So long as a discovery is not included in the regular process of sequential development, it is not mastered by technology.

Thus the moment of explosion is marked by the beginning of another stage. In processes that occur with the active participation of mechanisms of self-consciousness, this is a turning point. Consciousness is transferred back, as it were, to the moment preceding the explosion, and retrospectively makes sense of everything that happened. The process that actually took place is replaced by its model, generated by the consciousness of a participant in the act.

A retrospective transformation occurs. What happened is declared to have been the only possible thing, the "basic, historically predetermined" thing. What did not happen is understood as something impossible. Chance is assigned the weight of the lawful and inevitable.

In this form, events are transferred into the memory of the historian. He receives them already transformed under the influence of the primary selection of memory. It is especially important that in his material all accidents have been isolated out; explosion has been transformed into regular linear development. If talk of explosion is admitted, the concept itself decisively changes its content. It is filled with the idea of the energy and speed of the event, of its overcoming the resistance of opposing forces, while the idea of the unpredictability of the results of choosing one among many possibilities is decisively excluded. Thus the moment of informativeness is excluded from the concept of explosion. It is replaced by fatalism.

The historian's gaze is a secondary process of retrospective transformation. The historian looks at the event with a gaze directed from the present into the past. By its very nature, this gaze transforms the object of description. A picture of events that is chaotic for the simple observer emerges from the historian's hands secondarily organized. The historian is inclined to proceed from the inevitability of what happened. But his creative activity manifests itself elsewhere: from the abundance of facts preserved by memory, he constructs a continuous line that leads with the greatest reliability to this final point.

This point, whose foundation is chance, and which is covered from above by a whole layer of arbitrary suppositions and quasi-convincing causal links, acquires under the historian's pen an almost mystical character. It is seen as the triumph of divine or historical predestinations, as the bearer of the meaning of the entire preceding process. A concept objectively alien to history is introduced into history: the concept of a goal.

Ignatius Loyola once had the courage to say that the end justifies the means, but this principle was widely known before the appearance of the Jesuits and guided people who had never heard of Jesuits and never thought about them. It is the basis for the justification of history and the importation into it of High Meaning. Yet it is a fact of history, not an instrument for knowing history. And it is no accident that each such event, the "salt of the salt of history," is cancelled by the next explosion and consigned to oblivion. Reality lies elsewhere.

At the moment of explosion, eschatological ideas, such as the assertion that the Last Judgment or world revolution is near, whether it begins in Paris or Petersburg, and other analogous historical facts, are significant not because they generate the "last and decisive battle" after which the kingdom of God on earth must follow. They are significant because they call forth an unheard-of tension of popular forces and bring dynamics into layers of history that had seemed immobile.

It is characteristic of human beings to evaluate these moments in the categories proper to them, positive and negative. For the historian it is enough to point to them and make them the object of study in the degree of objectivity available to him.
