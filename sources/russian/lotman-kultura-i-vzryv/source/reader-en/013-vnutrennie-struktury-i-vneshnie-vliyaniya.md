## Internal Structures and External Influences

The dynamics of culture cannot be represented either as an isolated immanent process or as a passive sphere of external influences. Both tendencies are realized in mutual tension, and they cannot be abstracted from that tension without distorting their very essence.

Intersection with other cultural structures can take different forms. In order for an external culture to invade our world, it must cease to be "external" to that world. It must find a name and a place for itself in the language of the culture into which it bursts from outside. But in order to be transformed from "alien" into "one's own," this external culture must, as we see, undergo renaming in the language of the internal culture.

The process of renaming does not pass without consequence for the content that receives the new name. The feudal structure that arose on the ruins of the ancient world, for example, widely used old names. One may point to a name such as the Holy Roman Empire, or to the striving of barbarian kings to call themselves emperors and appropriate the symbols of Roman imperial power. To say that this old symbolism did not correspond to the new political reality is still to say too little. The divergence from reality troubled no one, because no one seeks reality in ideological symbolism. What was the inheritance of the past was perceived as a prophecy of the future.

Napoleon's "eagles" can be assessed as parade-stage props, but one cannot erase from them a symbolically expressed program, signs of the language by means of which the epoch deciphered its own reality. When Pushkin, addressing Napoleon, wrote:

Was it long ago that your eagles flew
Over the dishonored earth?

he was not simply reproducing conventional symbolic stencils. He was setting forth a conception that "joins both hems of time," the Roman and Napoleonic empires. The adoption of one symbolic language or another actively influences people's behavior and the paths of history. In this way, the broad cultural context absorbs elements invading it from outside.

But the opposite can also occur. The invasion can be so energetic that what is introduced is not an individual element of a text, but a whole language. That language may completely displace the language into which it invades, or it may form a complex hierarchy with it, as in the relation between Latin and the national languages in medieval Europe. Finally, it may play the role of catalyst: without participating directly in the process, it may accelerate its dynamics. Such was the intrusion of Chinese art into the structure of the Baroque. In this last case the intrusion will often take the form of fashion, which appears, intervenes in the dynamics of the main culture, and then disappears without trace. This, in essence, is the function of fashion: it is meant to be the metronome and catalyst of cultural development.

Intrusion into the sphere of culture from outside occurs through naming. External events, however active they may be in the extra-cultural sphere, for example in the areas of physics, physiology, material superiority, and so on, do not influence human consciousness until they themselves become "human," that is, until they receive semiotic meaningfulness. For human thought, only that which enters into one of its languages exists.

Purely physiological processes such as sexual intercourse or the effect of alcohol on the organism are physical and physiological realities. But precisely in their case an essential law appears: the more remote a given sphere is by nature from the sphere of culture, the more effort is applied to introduce it into that sphere. One could point here to the vastness of the space allotted in culture to the semiotics of wine and love even in culture's highest domain, poetry. Poetry transforms the use of wine, and in a number of cultures narcotic substances, from a physico-chemical and physiological fact into a fact of culture. This phenomenon is so universal, so surrounded by prohibitions and prescriptions, by poetic and religious interpretation, and so densely incorporated into the semiotic space of culture that a person cannot perceive the effect of alcohol apart from its psycho-cultural area.

Traditional study imagines culture as a certain ordered space. The real picture is much more complex and disorderly. The accidents of individual human fates and the interweaving of historical events at different levels populate the world of culture with unpredictable collisions. The harmonious picture that appears to the researcher of an individual genre or an individual closed historical system is an illusion. It is a theoretical model which, if it is realized at all, is realized only as the average among different non-realizations, and more often is not realized at all.

We do not encounter "pure" historical processes that represent the realization of research schemes. More than this, precisely the disorderliness, unpredictability, and "smearing" of history that so distress the researcher constitute the value of history as such. They fill history with unpredictability, with sets of probable accidents, that is, with information. They transform historical science from a kingdom of schoolroom boredom into a world of artistic diversity.

In a certain sense, culture can be imagined as a structure immersed in a world external to it, drawing this world into itself and throwing it back out reworked, or organized, according to the structure of its own language. Yet this external world, which culture regards as chaos, is in fact also organized. Its organization occurs according to the rules of some language unknown to the given culture. At the moment when texts of this external language are drawn into the space of culture, an explosion occurs.

From this point of view, explosion can be interpreted as the moment of collision between languages alien to one another: the assimilating language and the language being assimilated. An explosive space arises, a bundle of unpredictabilities. The particles thrown out by it initially move along trajectories so close that they can be described as synonymous paths of one and the same thing. In the sphere of artistic creativity they are still perceived as one and the same phenomenon, colored only by insignificant variants. Later, however, movement along diverse radii carries them farther and farther apart. Variants of one thing turn into sets of different things. Thus the various heroes of Lermontov's work genetically ascend to one common point of explosion, but afterward they become not only different images but images contrasting with one another.

In this sense one can say that explosion does not form synonyms, although the external observer is precisely inclined to unite different trajectories into synonymous bundles. A foreign reader of nineteenth-century Russian literature, for example, tends to create conditional generalizations such as "the Russian writer of this epoch." Everything falling inside that outlined circle will, from such a point of view, be considered as synonymy.

From the internal point of view of the given culture, however, the work of no writer can be considered the synonym of another, at least when original creativity is in question. Each is a separate, individual, and unrepeatable path. This does not cancel their inclusion in certain generalizing categories. Pushkin's Lensky, from a certain point of view, can be considered the "representative" of some generalized classification, but Pushkin had the right to write in connection with his death:

For us
The life-giving voice has fallen silent.

Here there occurs a switch from the category of common nouns into that of proper names. For an observer external to a given culture, as for a commander, soldiers are marked by common nouns; for those close to them, for their families, they belong to the category of proper names and have no plural. This, among other things, is the root difference between two ways of perceiving culture.

Those who stand at the author's point of view regard both people of culture and their artistic works in the category of proper names. Yet the tradition of studying culture that manifested itself vividly, for example, in the Hegelian school and won strongholds in criticism and literary teaching, principally transforms proper names into common nouns. The writer becomes a "product" and the hero a "representative." Such a transformation has repeatedly been subjected to criticism and mockery. Even so, we should not forget that we have no mechanism of knowledge other than the transformation of "one's own" into "alien," and of the subject of knowledge into its object.

Neither the world of proper names nor the world of common nouns, in isolation, can encompass real actuality. Actuality is given to us in their dialogic relation, and in this lies another aspect of the necessity of art.

The world of common nouns tends toward processes of gradual development, and this is inseparably connected with the interchangeability of its elements. The space of proper names is the space of explosion. It is no accident that historically explosive epochs throw "great people" to the surface, that is, actualize the world of proper names. The history of the disappearance of proper names from those areas where they would seem impossible to replace could itself form a special research topic.

The connection between the expression of the world of proper names and explosive processes is confirmed by the following consideration. The history of language and the history of literary language can be considered disciplines of one order. They are usually included as parts of a general science, historical linguistics. Yet one cannot fail to notice an essential difference between them. The history of language studies anonymous processes, and these processes are characterized by gradualness. N. Ya. Marr's idea of explosions in the development of linguistic processes was quite justifiably criticized in the 1950s. The history of language tends toward anonymity and gradualness.

The history of literary language, by contrast, is characterized by an inseparable connection with individual writerly personalities and by the explosive character of the process. From this follows the difference between these two related disciplines: one tends toward predictability, the other toward unpredictability. Something else is still more important. The development of language does not presuppose an element of self-knowledge as a necessary factor. Self-knowledge, in the forms of creating a grammatical level and elements of meta-description, invades language from outside, from the sphere of general culture. The sphere of literary language, however, rests on the foundation of self-knowledge and represents the intervention of an individual creative principle.

In this sense, the explosive character of the sphere of literary language appears entirely natural. One may recall the innovative experiments of Yuri Sergeevich Sorokin, who already at the end of the 1940s posed in principle the question of studying and teaching literary language in connection with the individual creativity of the writer. Some traditional linguists perceived this as an attack on the foundations of the history of language. Anonymity was attributed to the latter as an inseparable quality.

Thus the dynamic development of culture is accompanied by the constant exchange of places between external and internal processes. The same can be said of gradual and explosive processes.
