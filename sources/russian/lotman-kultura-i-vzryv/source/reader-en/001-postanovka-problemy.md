## Posing the Problem

The fundamental questions of any semiotic system are, first, its relation to the outside-system, to the world that lies beyond its limits, and second, the relation of statics to dynamics. The latter question could be formulated this way: how can a system develop while remaining itself? Both questions belong among the most fundamental, and at the same time among the most difficult.

The relation of a system to the reality lying outside it, and their mutual impenetrability, have repeatedly been made objects of inquiry since Kant. In our case they take the form of the antinomy between language and the world beyond language. The space that lies outside language and beyond its limits enters the domain of language and is transformed into "content" only as a component part of the content-expression dichotomy. To speak of unexpressed content is nonsense.

This does not exclude the case in which expression can be marked by a significant zero, present as absence: "And only silence speaks understandably," as Tyutchev says.

Thus the issue is not the relation between content and expression, but the opposition between the domain of language, with its content and expression, and the world lying outside language. In fact, this question merges with the second problem: the nature of linguistic dynamics.

The relation of language to outside-language reality is one of the fundamental problems. The plane of content, in the form in which Ferdinand de Saussure introduced this concept, is a conventional reality. Language creates its own world. At the same time, the question arises of the degree of adequacy between the world created by language and the world that exists outside any connection with language, lying beyond its limits. This is the old Kantian problem of the noumenal world.

In Kantian terminology, the plane of content "is self-consciousness, producing the representation I think, which must be able to accompany all other representations and be one and the same in every consciousness. Consequently, this self-consciousness cannot be accompanied by any other representation, and therefore I also call it original apperception. I also call its unity the transcendental unity of self-consciousness, in order to designate the possibility of a priori knowledge on the basis of this unity. Indeed, the manifold representations given in a certain intuition would not all together be my representations if they did not all belong together to one self-consciousness; in other words, as my representations, even if I were not conscious of them as such, they must nevertheless conform to the condition under which alone they can be together in one common self-consciousness, since otherwise they would not all belong to me."

Thus from the outset the existence of two degrees of objectivity is assumed: the world that belongs to language, that is, objective from its point of view, and the world that lies beyond language. Lotman deliberately introduces a certain transformation into Kant's idea here, identifying Kant's "I" with the subject of language.

One of the central questions will be the question of translating the world of the system's content, its internal reality, onto the outside reality that lies beyond language. Two more specific questions follow from this. First, the need for more than one language, at minimum two, in order to reflect reality beyond language. Second, the inevitability that the space of reality will not be covered by any one language separately, but only by their totality.

The idea that one ideal language could serve as the optimal mechanism for expressing reality is an illusion. The minimal working structure is the presence of two languages, together with the inability of each, taken separately, to encompass the external world. This inability is not a defect but a condition of existence, because it is precisely this inability that dictates the necessity of the other: another person, another language, another culture.

The idea that a model with one maximally perfect language would be optimal is replaced by the image of a structure with at least two languages, and in fact with an open list of different languages, mutually necessary to one another because each separately is incapable of expressing the world. These languages both overlap one another, reflecting the same thing in different ways, and are arranged on a single plane, forming internal boundaries within it. Their mutual untranslatability, or limited translatability, is the source of the adequacy between the outside-language object and its reflection in the world of languages.

The situation of a multiplicity of languages is original and primary. Only later, on its basis, does there arise the striving toward a single, universal language, toward a single and final truth. This latter striving becomes the secondary reality created by culture.

The relations between multiplicity and singularity belong among the basic, fundamental features of culture. Here logical and historical reality diverge. Logic constructs a conditional model of a certain abstraction: a single case is introduced that is supposed to reproduce an ideal commonality. Thus, in order to understand the essence of humanity, the philosophy of the Enlightenment modeled the image of Man.

Real movement developed along a different path. One may take as a conventional starting point herd behavior and/or genetically inherited behavior, which was neither individual nor collective, since it did not know this opposition. What did not enter into this usual type of behavior was signally nonexistent. Opposed to this "normal" behavior, which had no marked features, was only the behavior of the sick, the wounded, those perceived as "nonexistent."

For example, in *War and Peace*, Tolstoy showed the essence of this ancient herd psychology with great depth when he described the death of Platon Karataev during the retreat of the Russian prisoners together with the withdrawing French army. Pierre Bezukhov, who is making this difficult march with him, stops noticing his friend. Even the moment when a French soldier kills Platon Karataev, Pierre sees and does not see. Psychological and physiological vision split apart.

The next stage consists in atypical behavior entering consciousness as a possible violation: deformity, crime, heroism. At this stage, individual or abnormal behavior and collective or "normal" behavior are distinguished from one another. Only at the following stage does individual behavior become possible as an example and norm for the general, while the general becomes an evaluative point for the individual. That is, a single system arises in which these two possibilities are realized as inseparable aspects of one whole.

Thus individual and collective behavior arise simultaneously as mutually necessary contrasts. They are preceded by unconsciousness and, consequently, by the social "nonexistence," as it were, of both one and the other. The first stage of falling out of the unconscious is illness, wounding, deformity, or else periodic physiological excitations, for example those of the spring cycle. In the course of these processes individuality is singled out, and then dissolves again into nonindividuality.

Fixed, permanent differences in behavior, such as sex and age differences, are transformed from physiological into psychological differences only with the singling out of personality, that is, with the appearance of freedom of choice.

In this way, psychology and culture gradually win space back from unconscious physiology.
