## The Fool and the Madman

The binary opposition of fool and madman can be regarded as a generalization of two oppositions: fool versus clever person, and clever person versus madman. Together they form one ternary structure: fool, clever person, madman. In such a construction, fool and madman are not synonyms but antonyms, extreme poles.

The fool lacks flexible reaction to the situation around him. His behavior is wholly predictable. The only form of activity available to him is the violation of the correct relations between situation and action. His actions are stereotyped, but he applies them in the wrong place: he weeps at a wedding, dances at a funeral. He cannot invent anything new. Therefore his acts are absurd, but fully predictable.

Opposed to the "fool" is the "clever person," whose behavior is defined as normal. He thinks what one ought to think according to custom, the rules of reason, or practical experience. Thus his behavior is also predictable. It is described as a norm and corresponds to the formulations of laws and the rules of customs.

The third element of the system is insane behavior, the behavior of the madman. It is distinguished by the fact that its bearer receives an additional freedom in violating prohibitions. He can perform acts forbidden to the "normal" person. This gives his actions unpredictability. This last quality, destructive as a constantly operating system of behavior, unexpectedly proves highly effective at moments of acutely conflictual situations.

We have said that acute moments in animal behavior are subordinated to their inclusion in ritualized, programmed actions. The human being is characterized by the capacity for individual initiative, that is, by switching from the sphere of predictable actions, gestures, and deeds worked out as most effective into the sphere of an action that is new in principle and unpredictable.

A participant in battle, competition, or any conflict situation can behave "according to the rules," realizing traditional norms. In this case, victory will be achieved by skill in carrying out the rules. In order always to be victorious, a hero of this type must possess a type of activity common to all, but in quantitatively hypertrophied forms. He will be a hero of enormous height and gigantic strength. The direction of fantasy will develop toward a world of giants. Thus the victory of a hero of this type, and in particular his triumph over people, will be determined by his material superiority.

Yet world folklore also knows another situation: the victory of the weak, ideally the child, over the strong. This situation generates the entire cycle of stories about the triumph of the weak clever one over the gigantic strong fool. The clever one wins by inventiveness, wit, cunning, and deception, ultimately by amorality.

Odysseus saves himself from the Cyclops he has defeated through witty wordplay and cunning. Since the Cyclops is a giant, Odysseus in the struggle with him appears not in the function of a mighty warrior but as a weak, small trickster. The "dwarfish" size of Odysseus and his warriors in this situation is confirmed by the gigantic size not only of the Cyclops but also of his sheep, which allow the Greeks to slip out of the cave by clinging underneath the animals' bellies. The blinded and fooled giant checks the sheep by feeling their backs, but does not think to touch their bellies. The clever one is the one who performs actions unexpected and unpredictable for his enemies. Intelligence is realized as cunning.

The clever Puss in Boots from Perrault's tale deceives the stupid ogre-magician by first inviting him to turn into a lion, and then into a mouse, which he immediately eats in triumph.

The unpredictability of actions is effective because it knocks the opponent out of the situations familiar to him. In conflict with a stupid giant capable only of stereotyped action, the small trickster uses the weapon of unexpectedness, creating a situation in which stereotyped behavior becomes meaningless and ineffective.

The image of the child is bivalent. There are folkloric situations in which childhood is only a way of emphasizing nonchildlike strength or nonchildlike gigantic size, as with the infant Hercules or Rabelais's giant child-characters. To this also belongs Daniel the Prisoner's Old Russian saying, which transfers the situation to a strong army led by a fool: "The beast is great, but it has no head."

This situation is repeated when the "normal" person clashes with the madman. The "fool" has less freedom than the normal person; the "madman" has more. His actions acquire an unpredictable character, placing his "normal" opponent in a defenseless situation.

This led in various cultures to the device of using the madman under conditions of battle. Scandinavian epics, for example, describe berserkers. A berserker is a warrior who is constantly, or at the moment of battle, in a state of "battle madness." He takes part in battle naked or covered with only one skin, but in any case he does not feel pain from wounds. In battle he becomes like a beast, throwing off all limitations of human behavior. He gnaws his own shield and rushes at enemies, violating all rules of battle.

The inclusion of one such warrior in a warship sharply increases the combat effectiveness of the whole detachment, because it knocks the opponent out of a familiar, orientable situation. It is interesting that in this case the assimilation of the human being to the animal is thought of as liberation from all prohibitions, although, as we have seen, the real behavior of the animal is fenced in by far more prohibitions than human behavior.

In the *Saga of Grettir* we encounter a mixture characteristic of an epoch when these ideas are being destroyed. Belief in the fantastic madness of berserkers has already been shaken, and this name is applied simply to sea bandits. The following plot is characteristic: clever and skillful Grettir defeats by cunning the stupid and strong berserkers, whose leaders are characterized this way: "they were from Halogaland, stronger and taller than other people. They were berserkers and, falling into fury, spared no one." Yet these characters, traditionally called berserkers, in fact appear in the function of "stupid strongmen."

Comparable to this are the cases known in medieval German practice, one recorded in Estonia as late as the eighteenth century, of a human being's "transformation" into a wolf. Such a werewolf, combining the behavior of a predatory beast and a robber-rapist, can become invincible from the point of view of his opponents. It is characteristic that sources describe such behavior as nocturnal, sharply opposed to that same person's daytime behavior.

One may compare with berserkers the hero of the Irish sagas, Cuchulain. His appearance reveals a complex contamination of the most ancient archaic elements with features generated by the concept of everyday madness. Cuchulain is endowed with mythological features, but it is characteristic that combat successes are attributed to a hero of small stature who is capable of experiencing "battle madness": "There were seven pupils in his royal eyes, four in one eye and three in the other. There were seven fingers on each hand, seven on each foot. He possessed many gifts: first of all, the gift of wisdom, until battle heat took possession of him; then the gift of feats, the gift of playing various board games, the gift of prophecy, the gift of insight."

In fact, the widespread use of alcohol in a combat situation creates, in essence, the same effect of battle madness. It is revealing that the use of alcoholic intoxication in battle cannot be the norm for field troops and is, as a rule, characteristic of special detachments. The writer of these lines can refer to the enormous psychological impact at the beginning of the Second World War of a spectacle he witnessed: a detachment of German motorcyclists rushing straight at hastily dug enemy trenches, perhaps unconsciously reproducing the behavior of berserkers. The motorcycles were hung with submachine-gunners, four men seated on each, absolutely naked but wearing leather boots whose wide tops were stuffed with magazines. The submachine-gunners were drunk and rushed straight at the enemy, shouting loudly and firing long bursts continuously into the air. To recreate the impression, one should add that in this very situation both the memoirist and his fellow soldiers saw a submachine gun for the first time.

The use of madness as effective combat behavior is known among a broad range of peoples and is based on a common psychological foundation: the creation of a situation in which the opponent loses orientation.

Episodes of madness, of mad behavior, precisely mad and not foolish behavior, that is, behavior possessing a certain superhuman meaningfulness and at the same time demanding superhuman deeds, occur widely enough in literature. As for everyday behavior, in it they are realized as a certain difficult-to-attain ideal. In many cultural contexts this peculiar frenzy is connected with the ideal behavior of the lover or the artist.

In chapter 25 of the first part of *Don Quixote*, Cervantes gives us an exceptionally interesting episode: Don Quixote's imitation of the love-madness of heroes of chivalric romances. Don Quixote says that the glorious Amadis of Gaul was not merely one of the best knights in the world but the only one, the first, the unsurpassed one. Amadis was the guiding star, the bright luminary, the sun of brave and enamored knights, and all who fight under the banner of love and chivalry must imitate him. Therefore, Don Quixote says, the knight errant most closely approaches the model of chivalric behavior when he imitates Amadis of Gaul more than anyone else.

Amadis especially showed prudence, valor, courage, endurance, steadfastness, and strength of feeling when, rejected by Lady Oriana, he imposed penance on himself and withdrew to the Poor Rock, taking the name the Dark Handsome One, a name that of course contained deep meaning and corresponded to the way of life he had chosen with great eagerness.

Sancho replies to Don Quixote's monologue by pointing out the lack of justification, from the point of view of common sense, for such behavior in his master, since those knights had been driven to madness by the deceits or severity of their beloveds. What reason does Don Quixote have to go mad? Has his lady rejected him?

Don Quixote's answer transfers us from the sphere of everyday logic into the sphere of the superlogic of amorous chivalric madness: "That is the whole point," Don Quixote answered. "That is precisely where the unusual character of the enterprise I have conceived lies. A knight errant who goes mad for some reason deserves neither reward nor gratitude. The trick is to lose one's mind without any reason at all and to let my lady understand that if I have gone crazy just like that, what will happen when she drives me to it?"

Don Quixote's theoretical reflections are accompanied by mad behavior. With extraordinary speed he removes his trousers and, left in only his shirt, turns two somersaults through the air, head down and heels up, displaying things that make Sancho, so as not to see them a second time, satisfied and convinced that he can now bear witness to his master's madness, pull on the reins.

The concept of madness, not in its medical sense but as a definition for a type of permissible, though strange, behavior, can occur in two opposite meanings. It is connected with another concept: theatricality. Dividing the whole world around us into the natural, or normal, and the mad, Leo Tolstoy saw the brightest example of the latter in theater. The possibility of theatrical space, in which people on stage seem not to see the people in the hall and artificially imitate resemblance to ordinary life, was for Tolstoy a visible embodiment of madness. Indeed, in distinguishing "normal" and "mad" life, the problem of the stage occupies one of the central places.

The antithesis of theatrical and nontheatrical behavior appears as one interpretation of the opposition of the mad to the normal. In this sense it is essential to keep in mind the possibility of using the terms "theatricality" and "the theater of behavior" in two opposite senses. Michelle Perrot and Anne Martin-Fugier, in the chapter "Actors" placed in volume four of *A History of Private Life*, do not give the reader a description of theater at all. The issue is the everyday behavior of the normal family in French society. The cult of family coziness, the life of the family, is described as a kind of theater. Michelle Perrot's chapter "Figures and Roles" is especially characteristic, reproducing the ritual of family role-playing.

Thus the norm of life is experienced as theater. This implies that the describer is compelled to experience the habitual as strange, that is, to establish her point of view somewhere beyond the limits of normal forms of life. The situation is analogous to cases of describing one's native language, which naturally requires the position of estrangement indicated already by the formalists. The natural must be represented as strange, the known as unknown. Only in this case can everyday life be considered as theater.

The cases of which we will speak further have a different character. Here the sphere of art, and more broadly the semiotically marked sphere, will be experienced as something opposed to naturalness. "Life and theater," from this point of view, appear as antipodes. It is precisely their opposition that gives meaning and semiotic value to their mixing.

A special case of the relation between the theatrical and the nontheatrical is provided by the norm of chivalric behavior, in particular courtly love. Here the relation of rule and excess acquires a specific character. The rules of courtly love are formulated both in special treatises and in the lyrics of the troubadours. Yet realization of these rules is not calculated for the possibilities of practical behavior and itself represents a heroic excess. One can strive toward these rules, but one cannot attain them, and their realization always represents a kind of unique miracle. Thus a chivalric feat can be described as the realization of rules, as it appears to the researcher, as a heroic miracle from the point of view of the external observer, and as a peculiar combination of personal merit and higher reward for the knight himself.

An interesting example is Nero's theatricalization of life. The emperor who regarded himself as a brilliant professional actor and performed on stage simultaneously theatricalized life beyond the stage. Precisely because life is not theater, its transformation into theater becomes saturated with meanings.

Suetonius writes of Nero's operatic performances that he sang tragedies too, appearing in masks of heroes and gods, heroines and goddesses, and the features of the masks resembled his own face or the faces of women he loved. Here two opposite tendencies are interestingly mixed, and in their unity they reveal the essence of theater. The ancient actor, in order to separate himself from his natural extra-theatrical appearance, uses a mask. Nero, however, gives the mask the features of portrait resemblance to himself. Thus if the actor's mask as it were erases his extra-theatrical personality, here it is, on the contrary, thrust forward. But Nero's own face, in order to become part of the spectacle, must be destroyed and restored, that is, replaced by its own mask.

Still more refined acting appears in the exchange of sex. Of course, in Nero's practice this acquired a perverse character, as if reproducing his lust, which destroyed every boundary, and his abolition of all natural limitations imposed by sex. But one cannot deny here the emphasis on theater's capacity to reduce sexual difference to a problem of makeup. Let us recall that in Shakespeare's theater, for example, all female roles were played by men, and that the transfiguration of sexes is one of the most ancient and universal theatrical devices.

The episode Suetonius tells immediately afterward is no more than an anecdote, interesting precisely because of its repeatability. It stretches from the ancient anecdote about the painters' competition in which Apelles took part, where one painting depicted fruits so naturally that birds flew down and pecked at it, but another surpassed it: the painter painted a cloth covering the picture and deceived even his competitors, who began demanding that he remove the cloth and show his work. At the other pole is the episode of the American sentry standing in the theater by the governor's box. The soldier shot at Othello with the words: "No one will say that while I was here a black man killed a white woman!"

Suetonius's anecdote is built on the same thing: the mixing of theater and life. The constant movement from the role of emperor into the role of actor and back becomes Nero's norm of behavior. When he sang, no one was permitted to leave the theater, even out of necessity. Therefore, it is said, some women gave birth in the theater, and many, unable to listen to him and praise him any longer, climbed over the walls because the gates were locked, or pretended to be dead.

The mixing of semantic layers is characteristic: the emperor on stage pretends to be an actor; the actor performs dramatic roles of emperors; and spectators who wish to leave the hall perform the roles of the dead. This double role of emperor and actor appears in the fact that in theatrical competitions "he declared himself the victor," and, doubling himself, placed near his bed statues of himself in the dress of a cithara-player. The transformation is characteristic. If we regard the theatrical passion as a human weakness, then the statue is meant to remind us that in essence he is emperor. Nero resorts to transposition: the statue reminds us that he is an actor and deified not as the head of Rome but as a person of art.

Nero also manifests a passion for masquerade beyond the stage. Disguised, he wanders at night through disreputable places, and when preparing for war he orders his concubines to be "shorn like men and armed with axes and shields." The constant mixing of theater and everyday reality, the consideration of life beyond the stage as spectacle, lay at the basis of the famous episode of the fire of Rome. He watched the fire from the Tower of Maecenas, enjoying, in his words, the splendid flame, and wearing theatrical costume he sang *The Fall of Troy*. This whole theater of life ends in the deathbed phrase: "What a great actor is dying!"

For Suetonius, the constant mixing of theater and life characterizes a perverse, unnatural situation that turns all of life into spectacle. And this, in turn, is only part of the shift of all oppositions: actor and spectator, emperor and performer, Roman and Greek, and even man and woman. It is the possibility of performing any roles and at the same time appearing in the function of power, for whose amusement these roles are performed. Nero sets himself the task, on the one hand, of exhausting all variants, as it were, and on the other, of going beyond the limits of possibilities and realizing the impossible. We later find a similar complex in Ivan the Terrible.

Beginning the discussion of the "theater of everyday behavior," we opposed the French concept of theatricalizing the everyday, formalized gesture and act, repeated to the point of habitualness, to the unpredictability of behavioral excesses that interests us. Despite the apparent opposition of these positions, they are not without interconnection. An extravagant act without models, when repeated, can move from the area of "explosion" into the sphere of habit. As soon as an act has been performed, it can become an object of imitation. Thus Brummell's innovative inventions in men's fashion were immediately picked up by pretenders to dandyism and turned into a fact of mass culture. Cervantes, in the passage cited above, showed how madness becomes a sphere of imitation, that is, moves into mass culture.

Norm has no features. It is a spaceless point between madman and fool. Explosion also has no features, or rather, it has the whole complex of possible features. As individual behavior it falls out of the norm and in this case is defined as madness. Becoming mass behavior, it turns into foolishness.

From what has been said it is clear that any form of madness must inevitably represent an excess of individual behavior and lie beyond predictability. For example, the hero of the Byzantine epic *Digenis*, in the Russian version *Devgeny*, performs a number of acts that from the everyday point of view can be evaluated as strange or mad, but from their own internal position are the realization of a chivalric striving for perfection.

The folkloric plot of the "dangerous" bride, courtship of whom had already cost many suitors their lives, is reinterpreted here in a chivalric spirit. The hero Devgeny, learning of the existence of an inaccessible beauty whose numerous suitors have already been killed by her bloodthirsty father and brothers, immediately falls in love with the girl. From the point of view of everyday reason, he has had good fortune: he arrived at the palace, around which lay the bodies of his murdered predecessors, while the girl's dangerous relatives were absent, won her sympathy, and married her without any obstacles.

What follows is a plot "logical" from Don Quixote's point of view and absurd if we imagine Sancho Panza's opinion. Fortune obtained so easily does not please Devgeny but, in a way inexplicable to the modern reader, plunges him into despair. He strives to enter into battle, now unnecessary, with Stratigovna's father and brothers. Before us is a revealing transformation of the plot: battle, victory, acquisition of the bride is replaced by acquisition of the bride, battle, victory. All logical reasons for battle have been eliminated, but from the chivalric point of view battle does not need logical reasons. It is a self-valuing deed. It does not influence the hero's fate, but proves that the hero is worthy of his fate.

The relatives' refusal of battle plunges Devgeny into despair incomprehensible to us: "I have gained great shame if there is no pursuit after me. I want to return and heap reproach on them." There follows a complex system of rituals, meaningless from the point of view of common sense but filled with deep meaning in the system of chivalric norms. The excess here lies not in the strangeness of Stratig's actions, but in the superhuman consistency in chivalric ritual that Devgeny realizes.

The meaning of such texts differs in principle from the habitual notions of the modern reader. They depict not events as such, but an ideal of behavior toward which a real person can only strive. Therefore Devgeny's careful fulfillment of the ritual of the feat and the ritual of the wedding becomes Cervantes's parody when Don Quixote tries to carry them out in living practice.

Having defeated his father-in-law and brothers-in-law, who had tried to take his wife from him, Devgeny does not solve the difficult problem, but finds himself in an even more hopeless position. Without victory he risked losing his wife, but after victory his wife has become the daughter and sister of prisoners dishonored by defeat, and has ceased to be socially equal to her husband. Marriage again becomes impossible for another reason. Devgeny releases the captive relatives, then performs ideally chivalric acts, goes to them, declares himself their vassal, again asks for his wife's hand, solemnly receives the relatives' consent, and celebrates a full feudal wedding in splendor.

Here we encounter a characteristic situation: the inimitable heroic act is realized not through romantic lawlessness but through the humanly impossible fulfillment of the most refined and unfulfillable ideal norms. This determines, in particular, the wholly specific relation between literature, such as heroic epic, ballad, and chivalric romance, and reality. Literature sets unheard-of, fantastic norms of heroic behavior, and the lives of heroes try to realize them. Literature does not reproduce life; life strives to recreate literature. Hence the "absurdity" of the knight's behavior as an everyday norm.

In this sense the hero of *The Tale of Igor's Campaign* realizes typically chivalric behavior. After the united army of Russian princes had vainly tried to accomplish a comparatively difficult but still real task, defeating the Polovtsians on the Dnieper and restoring the Dnieper links with the sea, Igor rushed into a battle that was supposed to accomplish not a real but heroically grandiose goal: to break through the Polovtsian steppes and restore the primordial but already lost links with Tmutarakan.

The all-princely plan, conceived in Kiev under the leadership of the grand prince of Kiev, presupposed the collective action of the Kievan princes. As is known, Igor did not participate in this campaign. Since in Rus' people knew about his long, sometimes military and sometimes friendly relations with the Polovtsians, his absence from the united army could be interpreted in a way insulting to his honor. Personal chivalric motives, the desire to restore the flawlessness of his honor, impelled Igor to attempt the realization of a plan fantastic in its heroism.

The author's double relation to Prince Igor's behavior is characteristic. As a person still immersed in the atmosphere of chivalric honor, he admires precisely the hopelessness of the heroic plan conceived, the fact that real and practical considerations are cast aside in a hopeless chivalric impulse. But at the same time, he is also a person standing outside narrow chivalric ethics. The chronicler describing the same events opposes to the knight's heroic individualism the religious collectivism of Christianity. For him Christianity is a national principle located above chivalry.

*The Tale of Igor's Campaign* is alien to Christian motivations and wholly immersed in chivalric ethics. Igor is condemned here as a rebellious vassal who, by his heroic egoism, has violated the will of his suzerain, the prince of Kiev. The suzerain, by definition, is consecrated by higher authority as the head of the Russian land. Thus Igor is included in a double ethic: as a feudal lord he is adorned by courage that brings him to madness, and as a vassal by heroic submission. The incompatibility of these two ethical principles only emphasizes their value.

Medieval ethics is at once dogmatic and individual, the latter to the extent that a person must strive toward an unattainable ideal in every sphere of his activity. Connected with this is a specific trait of medieval behavior: maximalism. The ordinary is not valued. Value is attributed to the same action only when it is performed either on unheard-of scales or under incredibly difficult conditions that make it practically impossible.

Medieval cultural texts have a high degree of semiotic saturation. Yet the relation between the text that realizes the norm and the text that violates it, the "mad" text, is built on fundamentally different bases from the romantic structure more familiar to the modern reader. In the latter, "rule" and "madness" are opposite poles, and passage into the space of madness automatically means the violation of all rules.

In medieval consciousness we witness a completely different structure: the ideal, highest degree of value, whether holiness, heroism, crime, or love, is attained only in a state of madness. Thus only the madman can fulfill the highest rules. Rules become not a phenomenon of mass behavior but something proper only to an exceptional hero in an exceptional situation.

Thus if in romantic consciousness rules are the lot of vulgarity and are easily fulfilled, in medieval consciousness they are the unattainable goal of an exceptional person. Correspondingly, the creator of rules also changes. For the romantic, it is the crowd. The transformation of the Russian word *poshlyi* is characteristic, from its original meaning "ordinary." For medieval consciousness, the norm is what is unattainable, only an ideal point toward which impulses strive.

This, in particular, determines the ambiguity of medieval texts that describe norms of behavior and, consequently, the possibility of interpreting them in two ways. A researcher who builds his work on the basis of various normative texts, especially if he seeks forms of real behavior in works of art, though corresponding corrections should be made when using legal documents as well, is inclined to think that real everyday behavior represented the exact fulfillment of such rules. Yet one must also take into account the second view of these same texts: to see in them an ideal that one can approach but cannot attain.

By the way, the riddle of all Don Quixote's painful failures is built on this. He takes an ideal norm for an everyday norm. What should be admired, what at best should be striven toward, or pretended to be striven toward, he uses as a norm of everyday behavior. Hence the source of his tragicomic failures.

Thus from one and the same text one can extract features of everyday reality and ideal representations of the people of an epoch. The picture is further complicated by the fact that medieval life is a multi-stepped ladder. Between the ideal realization of rules, the lot of heroes, and the equally ideal complete violation of them, the behavior of the devil, there exists an extended ladder that comes closest to real life.

Real texts rarely manifest theoretical models in "pure" form. As a rule, we deal with dynamic, transitional, fluid forms that do not fully realize these ideal constructions but are only organized by them to some degree. Such models are located at another level in relation to texts. They are realized either as a tendency, a cultural code that shines through but is not formulated, or as metatexts: rules, instructions, laws, or theoretical treatises, of which the Middle Ages were so rich.

Consideration of the material convinces us that early Russian medieval texts, in the aspect that interests us, have a strict system, and that this system in its basic features is the same as in analogous social structures of other cultural cycles.

First of all, it should be noted that the early Middle Ages knew not one but two models of glory: Christian-ecclesiastical and feudal-chivalric. The first was built on a strict distinction between earthly glory and heavenly glory. The relevant feature here turned out to be not "glory/ingloriousness," or "fame/obscurity," "praise/reproach," but "eternity/perishability." Earthly glory is instantaneous. Thomas Aquinas held that "to act virtuously for the sake of earthly glory does not mean to be truly virtuous," and Saint Isidore asserted that "no one can embrace at the same time divine glory and the glory of this age." Raymond Lull likewise held that true honor belongs to God alone.

We find analogous assertions in Russian texts. Vladimir Monomakh says, in effect: we sinful humans are alive today and dead tomorrow, today in glory and honor, tomorrow in the grave and without memory, while others divide up what we had gathered. This point of view interests us not in itself, but because it exerted strong pressure, especially in Rus', on properly chivalric texts, creating such creolized, internally contradictory works as the *Life of Alexander Nevsky*, and strongly influencing the monk-chronicler's concept of honor and glory.

In properly secular, retinue, and chivalric texts, the semiotics of honor and glory is developed in much greater detail. Western material on this question has been collected in Maria Rosa Lida de Malkiel's monograph *The Idea of Glory in the Western Tradition: Antiquity, the Western Middle Ages, Castile*. From the data she cites it follows clearly that in the classical model of Western chivalry there is a strict distinction between the sign of chivalric dignity connected with a materially expressed signified, the reward, and the verbal sign, praise.

The first is invariably built as a correlation between the size of the reward and the magnitude of dignity. The concept of remuneration as a material signifier whose signified is the knight's dignity passes through numerous texts. Although *honneur* and *gloire* are constantly used in tandem as a dual formula, their meaning is deeply different. Ultimately, the differences reduce to the opposition between a thing in a sign function and a word also performing the role of social sign. We probably find the system closest to early Russian medieval usage in *The Song of My Cid*.

"Honor" here invariably has the meaning of booty, reward, a sign whose value is determined by the value of its plane of expression taken in a nonsign function. That is why the Cid can exclaim:

Let them know in Galicia, Castile, and Leon
With what wealth I rewarded, or supplied,
Each of my two sons-in-law.

However, "honor" is not only a sign of dignity; it is also a sign of a definite social relation. Honor is connected with the social basis and therefore subordinated to the whole system of legal institutions. The king, as the source of honor, takes this gift away from the hero. The hero then wins back his lost honor through loyalty, feats, and breadth of action. Lida de Malkiel emphasizes the specific combination of a poetry of loyalty and service with extreme individualism, willfulness, and respect for one's own independence, which makes the chivalric concept of honor difficult to recode into the language of later political terms.

The connection of honor with vassal relations is clearly traced in Russian sources as well. It is revealing that honor is always given, taken, rendered, or shown. This microcontext is never applied to glory. Honor is invariably connected with an act of exchange requiring a material sign, with a certain reciprocity of social relations that gives the right to respect and public value.

When analyzing the concept of honor and its verbal derivatives in texts of the Kievan period, one must keep one feature in mind: "honor" is always included in contexts of exchange. It can be given, rendered, and received. The apparent synonymy of seemingly opposite verbs, "to give" and "to take," is explained by the ambivalence of the action itself in the system of early feudal vassal relations. The act of giving a gift was at once a sign of entering into vassalage and being accepted into it. In entering this new dependence, the future vassal had to bring himself and his lands as a gift to the feudal protector, but with the expectation of immediately receiving them back, often with additions, now as a fief reward.

Thus the gift turns into a sign that is exchanged, thereby confirming definite social agreements. Connected with this ambivalence of giving-receiving, in which every recipient is at the same time a giver, is the fact that honor is rendered from below upward and shown from above downward. At the same time it is invariably emphasized that the source of honor, as of wealth, for subordinates is the feudal head.

Later we may also encounter cases in which honor is rendered by an equal to an equal. But in this case before us is already a ceremony of politeness, a formal ritual signifying conditional entry into service, having no more real meaning than the formula usual at the end of nineteenth-century letters: "I remain Your Excellency's most obedient servant."

"Glory" too is a sign, but a verbal sign, and therefore one that functions in a fundamentally different way. It is not transmitted and received, but "reaches distant tongues" and later posterity. Sound features are always attributed to it: it is "proclaimed," it is "heard." False glory perishes "with noise." In addition, it has the features of collective memory. Hierarchically, it occupies a higher place than honor.

Honor has sizes established according to one's place in the hierarchy. In the medieval Castilian epic *The Book of Apollonius*, the king addresses an unknown knight who has arrived during a feast:

Friend, choose yourself a place.
You know what place befits you
And how, according to courtesy,
You ought to act,
Since we, not knowing who you are,
May make a mistake.

The volume of honor shown here is more important than the personal name. The latter can be hidden, but the former must be known; otherwise social communication becomes simply impossible. Church texts are different in this respect, as in the *Life of Alexius the Man of God*, built on the hero's conscious lowering of his social status.

Glory is measured not by magnitude but by longevity. Glory in its basic usage is praise. Unlike honor, it does not know a direct correlation between expression and content. If honor is connected with the idea that a greater expression designates a greater content, then glory, as a verbal sign, treats this connection as conditional: an instantaneous human word signifies immortal glory. Since the medieval person is inclined to consider all signs as iconic, he tries to remove this contradiction as well by connecting glory not with ordinary words but with especially authoritative words. These may be divine words, words written "in charters," composed by "singers," or words of "distant tongues." Here too we find a complete parallelism between the terminology of the Russian and Western early Middle Ages.

In principle, glory is attached to the higher levels of the feudal hierarchy. But an exception is possible: a warrior who wins glory at the price of death as if equalizes himself with the highest level. He becomes equal to all. And, as we remember from the *Izbornik* of 1076, glory is given by equals.

This system distinguishing honor and glory is consistently maintained in the oldest texts of the Kievan period: the *Izbornik* of 1076, the *Deeds of Devgeny*, the *Jewish War*, partly the *Primary Chronicle*, in combinations with the church interpretation of the term, and a number of other texts. The *Jewish War* is especially revealing in this respect. It was observed long ago, already by Barsov, that the terminology and phraseology characterizing the ideological and everyday order of retinue Rus' are reflected in the Russian translation of Flavius's books, corresponding to usage in original Russian monuments contemporary with the translation, especially the chronicles. Such are the concepts of honor and glory, which replace other concepts in the Greek text: joy, abundant entertainment, reward.

When the translator of the *Jewish War* renders the Greek *euphemia*, joy, as "honor," he is not simply reinterpreting Greek terminology, but doing so with a striking knowledge of the basic concepts of pan-Romance chivalry. If we recall that "praise" here is an indisputable synonym of "glory," we again see the formula "honor and glory," essential for Russian feudalism and common to the early Middle Ages of a number of countries. In passing, it would be appropriate to note the specificity of the term "merriment" in *The Tale of Igor's Campaign*. In this case it was most likely used as a synonym for honor-reward. Conversely, the formula "voices grew sad, merriment drooped" can be considered a negative transformation of the combination "glory and honor": "voice" is synonymous with "praise" and "song," the sounding sign of dignity, while "merriment" is material, honor.

Thus the dual unity of the formula "honor and glory" does not remove the sharp terminological specificity of each of its components, especially for the retinue-chivalric environment. In church texts, where the author attributes to a character, for example a deity or saint, the full totality of possible merits and value, "glory and honor" are used as inseparable elements of a formula. It is precisely in these texts that their retinue specificity begins to be erased. With the spread of church ideology, the combination begins to be perceived as tautological.

In this chapter we have used both documentary data reflecting everyday reality and literary texts representing ideal models of that reality. The laws of these two ways of reproducing life are different, but at the same time their connectedness must be emphasized, especially in the medieval epoch.

The person of archaic epochs is inclined to expel chance from the world. Chance seems to him the result of some more mysterious and more powerful law unknown to him. Hence the whole extensive practice of divination, in the course of which the accidental is raised to the degree of prediction.

In this sense, one episode from book 23 of the *Iliad*, the funeral games for Patroclus, is interesting. In the athletic competitions held at the funeral, Odysseus and Ajax competed in running. Ajax ran fastest ahead of all, but famous Odysseus ran close behind him. Ajax was winning, though Odysseus ran so closely behind Ajax that he constantly struck footstep into footstep before the dust had fallen from them.

Homer develops the further content in two planes. Ajax accidentally slipped in fresh bull dung and fell into it face-first. This allowed Odysseus to overtake his rival and receive first prize. The episode could be a classic illustration of the intervention of unpredictable chance in the course of events.

Yet the poet also gives another parallel explanation: Odysseus prayed in his heart to bright-eyed Pallas Athena, asking the daughter of the aegis-bearer to hear him and mercifully quicken his feet. He spoke in prayer, and the daughter of the aegis-bearer heard him.

This double interpretation gives, as it were, two equally probable explanations. Without Athena's help Odysseus would not have won, but he would not have won without the puddle of bull dung either. This simultaneously opens the road into the world to accidents, and at the same time sees in each accident the direct result of the gods' intervention. Homer achieves a synthesis of the idea of divine power and the unpredictability of events, their informational value, by including in each event a whole chain of factors determining it. His gods are constantly in conflict, and if the outcome of human conflict is explained by the will of the gods, then the outcome of the conflict among the gods remains unpredictable. The collision of fate and human will has the same character, a conflict Greek authors resolved in different ways but that always troubled them.

Events unpredictable in their consequences break into the world. These events give impetus to a broad series of further processes. The moment of explosion, as we have already said, is as if switched off from time, and from it a path leads to a new stage of gradual movement, marked by return to the axis of time. Explosion, however, generates a whole chain of other events. First of all, its result is the appearance of a whole set of equally probable consequences. Of these, only one is destined to be realized and become a historical fact.

The selection of this unit, which has become historical reality, can be defined as accidental, or as the result of the intervention of other laws external to the given system and lying beyond its limits. That is, in some other perspective it may be completely predictable, but within the given structure it represents chance. Thus the realization of this potential can be characterized as the nonrealization of a whole set of others.

Pushkin felt this very sharply when he placed in Tatyana's mouth the words:

And happiness was so possible,
So near...

It is characteristic that fate is understood here as the result of an accidental, imprudent action, and therefore, for Pushkin, as unpredictable:

But my fate
Is already decided; perhaps
I acted imprudently.

Even more significant are the author's reflections, unique in the history of the novel, on the lost future of the murdered Lensky. Perhaps he was born for the good of the world, or at least for glory; his silenced lyre could have raised a thunderous, unbroken ringing through the ages. Perhaps a high step awaited the poet on the steps of light. Perhaps his suffering shade carried with it a sacred secret, and for us a life-giving voice perished.

The omitted stanza present in the fair manuscript offers still other possibilities: he might have filled newspaper issues with immortal glory, not because Lensky could have become a journalist, but because newspapers might have written about him, that is, because he might have become a historical person. He might have completed a terrible path and breathed his last before solemn trophies, like Kutuzov or Nelson, or in exile like Napoleon, or hanged like Ryleev. Or perhaps the poet awaited an ordinary lot: the years of youth would have passed, the fire of his soul would have cooled, and in the village, happy and horned, he would have worn a quilted dressing gown, known life in reality, had gout at forty, drunk, eaten, grown bored, grown fat, and declined.

The path of both an individual human being and humanity is strewn with unrealized possibilities, with lost roads. Hegelian consciousness, which has entered even unnoticed into the very flesh of our thought, educates in us reverence before realized facts and contempt toward what could have happened but did not become reality. Reflections on these lost roads are treated by the Hegelian tradition as romanticism and assigned to the department of empty dreams, like those indulged in by Gogol's well-known philosopher Kifa Mokievich, whose existence "was turned more toward the speculative side."

One can, however, imagine another view, according to which precisely these "lost" paths represent one of the most moving problems for the historian-philosopher. Here, however, one should recall the two poles of historical movement. One of them is connected with processes developing by nonexplosive, gradual movement. These processes are relatively predictable. Processes arising as a result of explosions have a different character. Here each accomplished event is surrounded by a whole cloud of unaccomplished ones. The paths that could have begun from them are lost forever. Movement is realized not only as a new event, but in a new direction.

This approach is most revealing in cases where the issue is historical events unique by their nature, in which an accidental occurrence breaks open the beginning of a new and unpredictable regularity. The writing of a work of genius that became a turning point in the cultural history of humanity would not have occurred and would not have been compensated for by anything if its author had died in childhood from an accidental catastrophe. The outcome of the battle of Waterloo could not be unambiguously predicted, just as one could not predict at Napoleon's birth whether this child would survive or not. But the accomplishment of these facts pressed some button in the historical machine that might have remained unused.

In this sense, each event can be considered from two points of view. Pushkin's work influenced the transformation of the artistic work into a commodity: "inspiration is not for sale, but one can sell the manuscript." *Eugene Onegin* played the decisive role here. Yet if Pushkin had not written his novel in verse, or even if Pushkin had not been born at all, this process would still inevitably have occurred. But if we look at another historical connection and suppose that Pushkin had been torn out of literature by biographical circumstances in his earliest period, then the whole chain of events leading to Gogol, Dostoevsky, Tolstoy, Blok, and through them in the nearer perspective to Solzhenitsyn, and to the strengthening of the role Russian literature played in the civic destinies of the Russian intelligentsia and therefore in the destinies of Russia generally, would have been entirely different from what we know.

Thus one and the same event can be included both in a predictable series and in the circumstances of explosion. Every "great" event not only opens new roads but also cuts off whole bundles of probabilities of the future. If this is remembered, then the description of these lost paths becomes for the historian no longer pious reflections on optional themes.

One more circumstance must also be taken into account: different but typologically similar historical movements, for example the movements of romanticism in different European countries or different forms of anti-feudal revolutions, can at the moment of explosion choose different roads. Comparing them demonstrates, as it were, what would have happened in one or another country if the results of the explosion there had been different. This introduces an entirely new aspect into the comparative study of cultures: what is lost in one historical-national space may be realized in another, and their comparison gives reflections on what would have happened if the historical choice had occurred differently a more grounded character.

Reasoning about different solutions of the same event can be used for comparative study: the failed putsch in Moscow and the events in Yugoslavia show what would have happened if the putsch had not failed. Comparison of different realizations of a typologically single event can be considered a form of studying "lost" roads. From this point of view, one may interpret the history of the Renaissance, the Reformation, and the Counter-Reformation as different variations of a single historical model. Such a view allows us to study not only what happened, but also what did not happen but could have happened.

Comparative study, for example, of economic processes on one side and works of art on the other gives us not cause and effect, but two poles of a dynamic process, untranslatable into one another and at the same time penetrated by mutual influence. In the same relation stands the antinomy of mass and maximally individual historical phenomena, of predictability and unpredictability, the two wheels of the bicycle of history.

We have already said that a person who lives according to the laws of customs and traditions is, from the point of view of the enterprising hero of an explosive epoch, foolish, while the latter, from the position of his opponent, is treacherous and dishonorable. Now it makes sense to see in these conflicting characters links of a single chain.

Periods of scientific discoveries and technical inventions can be considered two stages of intellectual activity. Discoveries have the character of intellectual explosions. They are not read out of the past, and their consequences cannot be unambiguously predicted. But at the very moment when the explosion has exhausted its internal energy, it is replaced by a chain of causes and effects: the time of technology begins. Logical unfolding selects from the explosion those ideas for which the time has already come, those that can be used. The rest, for a time, sometimes a very long time, is consigned to oblivion.

Thus in the periodic alternation of advances in scientific theory and technical successes one can discern an alternation of periods of predictability and unpredictability. Of course, such a model has a high degree of conditionality. In reality, a sequential chain of explosions and gradual developments never exists in isolation. It is immersed in bundles of processes synchronous with it, and these lateral influences, constantly intervening, can disturb the clear picture of alternating explosions and gradualities. But this does not prevent us from considering this sequential chain in isolation theoretically.

It appears especially clearly in social and historical processes. We spoke of how the "foolish" knight and the "rogue" look in each other's eyes. Let us now look at them as heroes of successive stages of development. The hero of the byliny, or the person of the chivalric epoch, or the "good peasant" in the idyllic literature of the eighteenth century, are heroes of stable or slowly developing processes. The movement in which they are immersed oscillates between calendar change, rite, and feats repeated to the point of rituality. Under these conditions, in order to be victorious, the hero does not need to invent anything new. His exceptionality is marked by the gigantic scale of his height and strength. He does not need invention, because he invents nothing. At this stage, however, he does not yet receive a negative characterization. He is not foolish, because the feature of intelligence or foolishness is not marked at all.

But then the moment of explosion arrives. Unchangingness and repeatability are replaced by unpredictability. The age of strength is replaced by a flash of cunning. We have seen that heroes of these two types, placed opposite one another, turn into enemies expressing struggling epochs. But in chronological sequence they appear as two mutually conditioned and inevitable stages.

Their sequence can be traced curiously in the fates of a number of historical phenomena. Russian culture of the second half of the nineteenth century, for example, develops under the sign of the high value of the individual principle in the human being. This is especially noticeable because at the level of self-knowledge the idea of the people dominates, and literature under Tolstoy's or Dostoevsky's pen, and political struggle embodied in populist movements, declare the people to be their ideal. The people's happiness is the goal of revolutionary practice, but the people are not those who act; they are those for whose sake action is taken.

Nekrasov writes that the spectacle of the people's suffering is unbearable, and that the happiness of noble minds is to see contentment around them. For Grisha Dobrosklonov, fate prepared a glorious path, a loud name as defender of the people, consumption, and Siberia. According to Dostoevsky's plan, Alyosha Karamazov was to lay down "his life for his friends," ending his life on the scaffold for another person's sin.

Thus the idea of the people was expressed in the individual hero, just as it was expressed in the diversity of heroic personalities whose portraits were drawn by Stepniak-Kravchinsky. It is precisely the idea of diversity that organizes the unity of these portraits. Such a structure was determined by the fact that the epoch before the explosion was already illuminated by its kindling light. The fact that the epoch of explosion gives a diversity of characters and a wide scatter of individualities is confirmed by the biographies of people of the Renaissance or the Petrine epoch.

But then the explosion is completed. Revolution is replaced by stagnation, and the idea of individuality disappears, replaced by the ideal of "sameness." From the mid-1920s onward, what is singled out in the heroic personality is the absence of individuality. A "million-fingered hand" compressed into one smashing fist is poeticized.

Mayakovsky writes:

The unit is nonsense,
the unit is zero,
one,
even if
very important,
will not lift
a simple
five-inch beam,
much less
a five-story house.

The idea of nameless heroism is insistently repeated in literature that sings of heroism in a nonheroic epoch, from the song of the Red Cavalry, "We are all nameless heroes, and all our life is struggle," to Pasternak's wartime poem:

Nameless heroes of besieged cities,
I will hide you in the heart of hearts.
Your valor is higher than words.

It is interesting that neither Pasternak nor his readers of those years felt a dissonance in this text. "I will hide you in the heart of hearts" is a quotation from Hamlet's speech, where Shakespeare's hero creates the ideal of a noble personality. It is precisely this, the personality, that Hamlet intends to hide in the depths of the heart. In Pasternak's text this place is assigned to nameless impersonality.

One may note, however, that the ritual of reverence for the tomb of the unknown soldier that arose after the First World War was also born of a nonheroic epoch. Compare inscriptions on graves that oppose the idea of anonymity of the feat even when the names of the dead remain unknown: the Paris inscription on the grave of the heroes of the 1830 revolution, "You died so quickly that the Fatherland did not have time to remember your names"; Olga Bergholz's words, "No one is forgotten and nothing is forgotten"; or the memorial ribbons on the graves of Jewish children in Prague, the full list of names of all the victims.

The heroic personality whose name is written onto the pages of history and the nameless typical hero embody two types of historical development that replace one another.
