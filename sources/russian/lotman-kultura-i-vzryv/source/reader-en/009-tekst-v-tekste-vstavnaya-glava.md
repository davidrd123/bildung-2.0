## Text Within Text

In the cases we have examined, choice was realized as the realization of one among several potential possibilities. This approach represented a conditional abstraction. Attention was given to a separate developing system, located as if in isolated space.

The real picture is more complex. Any dynamic system is immersed in a space in which other equally dynamic systems are located, together with fragments of structures that have collapsed, peculiar comets of this space. As a result, any system lives not only according to the laws of self-development, but is also included in diverse collisions with other cultural structures. These collisions have a significantly more accidental character. Forecasting them is practically impossible. Yet it would be more than imprudent to deny their reality and significance.

In fact, what is realized here is an identification of the accidental and the lawful. What is lawful in "one's own" system appears as "accidental" in the system with which it has unexpectedly collided. As accidental, it will sharply increase the freedom of further paths.

The history of the culture of any people can be considered from two points of view: first, as immanent development, and second, as the result of diverse external influences. These two processes are closely interwoven, and separating them is possible only as a research abstraction. From what has been said it follows, among other things, that any isolated consideration of either immanent movement or influences inevitably leads to a distortion of the picture.

The complexity, however, lies not in this, but in the fact that any intersection of systems sharply increases the unpredictability of further movement. The case in which an external intrusion leads to the victory of one of the colliding systems and the suppression of the other characterizes far from all events. Quite often collision generates a third thing, something new in principle, which is not an obvious, logically predictable consequence of either of the systems that collided.

The matter is complicated by the fact that the newly formed phenomenon very often appropriates the name of one of the colliding structures, while in fact hiding something fundamentally new under the old facade. Thus, beginning with the reign of Elizabeth Petrovna, Russian noble culture underwent an exceptionally powerful "Frenchification." At the end of the eighteenth and beginning of the nineteenth century, the French language became, in the noble environment and especially the metropolitan one, an inseparable part of Russian culture. Above all, it conquered the space of women's language.

Pushkin touched this question with sufficient detail and cultural precision in the third chapter of *Eugene Onegin*, when he encountered the problem of the language in which he should convey Tatyana's letter. The question was resolved with emphatically frank conventionality: Tatyana wrote, of course, in prose; the poet translates prose into the language of the poetry depicting it. At the same time, he warns that the letter was written in French and that it is conveyed to the reader just as conditionally by means of the Russian language:

I foresee another difficulty:
Saving the honor of my native land,
I shall no doubt have to translate
Tatyana's letter.
She knew Russian poorly,
Did not read our journals,
And expressed herself with difficulty
In her own native tongue;
And so, she wrote in French...

Pushkin initially thought of giving Tatyana's letter as a prose insertion into the novel, perhaps even in French. The solution he chose, a poetic retelling in Russian, is still more complex semiotically, since it emphasizes the conventionality of the link between expression and content.

Here two problems from the history of language intersect. The intrusion of the French language into Russian, and their fusion into a certain single language, creates a whole functional set. For example, the mixture of French with Russian forms a "ladies'" language, especially its "fashionable" variety. Pushkin's letter to his brother Lev from Chisinau gives an example: "First I want to scold you. How are you not ashamed, my dear, to write half-Russian, half-French letters? You are not a Moscow cousin." "Moscow cousin" is a term designating a provincial woman of fashion, one who wears out clothes and customs already obsolete in Petersburg, a constant comic character of scenes from Moscow life.

The French language performed for educated Russian society of Pushkin's epoch the role of the language of scientific and philosophical thought. Here the appearance in the literature of the French Enlightenment of a number of scientific books addressed to a female audience had a special influence. The drawing of women into scientific reading in Russia, begun by E. R. Dashkova, also contributed to the fact that the general scientific function of language was fixed to French. Not only the fashionable woman, but also the Russian learned woman spoke and wrote in French.

If the characterization of Tatyana as one who expressed herself with difficulty in her own native tongue carries a shade of "ladies'" linguistics, then in "Roslavlev" Pushkin's heroine appears already as the bearer of a high culture belonging equally to men and women. Her library consisted chiefly of works by eighteenth-century writers. French literature, from Montesquieu to Crebillon's novels, was familiar to her. She knew Rousseau by heart. There was not a single Russian book in the library except the works of Sumarokov, which Polina had never opened. She said she had difficulty making out Russian print and probably had read nothing in Russian, not excluding the little verses presented to her by Moscow poets.

It should be noted that the composition of the library characterizes the tastes of the Russian educated reader as a whole, not only of women. Polina's library belonged to her father. The fusion in this case of "male" and "female" languages is emphasized by how easily Pushkin places his own favorite thoughts in the heroine's mouth. She says, in effect, that for thirty years they have been reproached because they do not read Russian and supposedly do not know how to express themselves in the national language. The point, she says, is that they would be glad to read Russian, but Russian literature is not much older than Lomonosov and is still extremely limited. It has several excellent poets, but one cannot demand from all readers an exclusive taste for verse. In prose there is only Karamzin's *History*; the first two or three novels appeared only two or three years ago. Meanwhile in France, England, and Germany, one remarkable book follows another. Even translations are scarcely seen, and when they are seen, she still prefers the originals. Russian journals are interesting to Russian men of letters. All news and concepts have to be drawn from foreign books. Thus "we think in a foreign language," at least all those who think and follow the thoughts of the human race. The best-known Russian writers confessed this to her.

Griboedov's attacks on the mixture of languages, just like Pushkin's defense of them, prove that before us is not a whim of fashion and not a grimace of ignorance, but a characteristic feature of a linguistic process. In this sense the French language constitutes an organic element of Russian cultural-linguistic communication.

It is revealing that Tolstoy in *War and Peace* abundantly introduces French precisely in order to reproduce the speech of Russian nobles. Where he conveys the speech of French people, it is as a rule given in Russian. In this case French is used in the first words of speech as an indicator of linguistic space, or where it is necessary to reproduce a characteristic trait of French thinking. In neutral situations Tolstoy does not turn to it.

In the intersection of Russian and French languages in this epoch there arises a contradictory situation. On the one hand, the mixing of languages forms a certain single language of culture. On the other hand, using this language implies an acute sense of its inorganic nature and internal contradictoriness. This manifested itself, in particular, in the persistent struggle against this mixture, in which people saw now the absence of a literate style, now even a lack of patriotism or provinciality, as in Griboedov's phrase "a mixture of languages, French with Nizhny Novgorod."

The intrusion of a "fragment" of text in a foreign language can play the role of generator of new meanings. This is emphasized, for example, by the possibility of introducing speech in "no" language that nevertheless turns out to be extraordinarily saturated with meaning. Compare Mayakovsky's creation of macaronic speech in a conventional "foreign" language:

Thief impudent. Tore jasmine from limes.
Ogu-reyshun!

Or recall the well-known episode from *War and Peace* describing the conversation of a French and a Russian soldier, where the latter, wishing to speak in a "foreign" language, resorts to glossolalia.

A typical case of the intrusion of a foreign text is "text within text": a fragment of text, torn from its natural semantic connections, is mechanically introduced into another semantic space. Here it can perform a whole series of functions: it can play the role of semantic catalyst, change the character of the main meaning, remain unnoticed, and so on. We are especially interested in cases where unexpected textual intrusion receives essential semantic functions. This appears with particular clarity in artistic texts.

"Text within text" is a specific rhetorical construction in which the difference in codedness among different parts of the text becomes a revealed factor of the author's construction and the reader's perception of the text. The switching from one system of semiotic awareness of the text to another at some internal structural boundary constitutes, in this case, the basis for the generation of meaning.

Such a construction first of all sharpens the moment of play in the text. From the position of another mode of coding, the text acquires features of heightened conventionality; its play character is emphasized: ironic, parodic, theatricalized meaning, and so on. At the same time, the role of textual boundaries is emphasized, both external boundaries separating the text from nontext and internal boundaries dividing areas of different codedness.

The actuality of boundaries is emphasized precisely by their mobility, by the fact that when orientations toward one or another code change, the structure of boundaries also changes. Thus, against the background of an already formed tradition that includes a pedestal or a picture frame in the area of nontext, Baroque art introduces them into the text, for example by turning a pedestal into a rock and connecting it narratively into a single composition with the figure. A characteristic example of introducing the pedestal into the text of a monument is the rock on which Falconet placed his Peter the Great in Petersburg.

Paolo Troubetzkoy, creating a design for a monument to Alexander III, introduced into it a sculptural quotation from Falconet's creation: the horse was placed on a rock. The quotation had the meaning of a polemical rhyme. The rock that under Peter's hooves gave the statue a forward impulse was transformed by Troubetzkoy into a cliff and an abyss. His rider had galloped to the limit and stopped heavily over the precipice. The meaning of the equestrian statue was so obvious that the sculptor was asked to replace the rock with a traditional pedestal.

The play moment is sharpened not only by the fact that these elements turn out, in one perspective, to be included in the text, and in another, excluded from it, but also by the fact that in both cases their degree of conventionality differs from that inherent in the main text. When figures in Baroque sculpture climb onto or leap down from the pedestal, or in painting crawl out of their frames, this emphasizes, rather than erases, the fact that one of them belongs to material reality while the others belong to artistic reality. The same play with the spectator's sensations of different kinds of reality occurs when theatrical action descends from the stage and moves into the real-everyday space of the auditorium.

Play on the opposition "real/conditional" is characteristic of any "text within text" situation. The simplest case is the inclusion within a text of a section coded by the same, but doubled, code as the rest of the work's space. These will be painting within painting, theater within theater, film within film, or novel within novel.

The double codedness of definite sections of text, identified with artistic conventionality, leads to the main space of the text being perceived as "real." Thus in *Hamlet* before us is not only "text within text" but also *Hamlet* within *Hamlet*. In order to entertain "mad" Hamlet, the king orders a troupe of traveling actors accidentally met on the road to be brought to Elsinore. To test their art, Hamlet asks them to perform an excerpt from some play that first comes to his mind. At this moment he has no plan in his head. The choice of the play is accidental; Hamlet simply names his favorite work. This is shown by Shakespeare's making him replace the excerpt with another one.

Hamlet's joking reply to Polonius's foolish remark interrupting the recitation proves that the connection between the scene being acted and Hamlet's own circumstances has not yet been recognized by him. But when the actor speaks the words "But who had seen the mobled queen..." a sudden light flashes in Hamlet's consciousness, altering the meaning of the whole monologue. From this point on, he and the spectator perceive the actor's monologue in relation to the events in Elsinore, and the part of the monologue already read acquires new meaning. Hamlet's insight is emphasized by Polonius's dullness:

Hamlet: "The mobled queen?"

Polonius: "That's good. 'Mobled queen' is good."

First Actor:

"... Running barefoot, in blind tears,
threatening the flame; a rag thrown
over her crowned brow..."

The excerpt, which was nontext, becomes part of the text of *Hamlet* when included in it, that is, it turns into text. At the same time, it transforms the whole text into which it is included, translating it onto another level of organization.

The play staged on Hamlet's initiative repeats, in an emphatically conventional manner, the play composed by Shakespeare. First comes pantomime, then the emphatic conventionality of rhymed monologues interrupted by prose replies from spectators: Hamlet, the king, the queen, and Ophelia. The conventionality of the first play emphasizes the reality of the second. To accentuate this feeling for readers, Shakespeare introduces metatextual elements into the text: on stage, before us, the directing of a play is carried out. As if anticipating Fellini's *8 1/2*, Hamlet gives the actors instructions before the public about how they should act. Shakespeare shows on the stage not only a stage, but, more importantly, a rehearsal of a stage.

For us, what is essential is that the break from the "main" narrative introduces, at first glance, an entirely foreign excerpt of "someone else's" text, connected with nothing.

Doubling is the simplest type of bringing code organization into the sphere of consciously structural construction. It is no accident that myths about the origin of art are connected precisely with doubling: rhyme as the offspring of echo, painting as a shadow outlined in charcoal on stone, and so on. Among the means of creating local subtexts with doubled structure in the visual arts, the mirror motif occupies an important place in painting and cinema.

The mirror motif is widely encountered in the most diverse works, such as Velazquez's *Venus and Cupid* and Van Eyck's *Portrait of Arnolfini and His Wife*. Yet we immediately encounter the fact that doubling by means of a mirror is never simple repetition. The right-left axis changes, or, more often, to the plane of the screen or canvas there is added an axis perpendicular to it, creating depth or adding a point of view lying outside the plane.

In Velazquez's painting, to the point of view of the viewers, who see Venus from behind, there is added a point of view from the depth of the mirror: Venus's face. In Van Eyck's portrait the effect is still more complicated. A mirror hanging on the wall in the depth of the painting reflects from behind the figures of Arnolfini and his wife, who are turned frontally on the canvas, and the guests entering from the viewers' side, whom they are greeting but whom the viewers see only in the mirror. Thus from the depth of the mirror a gaze is cast perpendicular to the canvas, toward the gaze of the viewers, and it goes beyond the painting's own space.

In fact, the mirror played the same role in the Baroque interior, expanding the properly architectural space in order to create illusory infinity, doubling artistic space by reflecting paintings in mirrors, or breaking open the boundary "inside/outside" by reflecting windows in mirrors.

Yet the mirror can also play another role. In doubling, it distorts, and by this distortion exposes the fact that the image that seems "natural" is a projection bearing within itself a definite language of modeling. In Van Eyck's portrait, the mirror is convex. The figures are given not only from the front and the back, but also in projection onto a flat and a spherical surface. In Visconti's *Senso*, the figure of the heroine, deliberately impassive and frozen, is opposed to her dynamic reflection in the mirror. One may also compare the stunning effect of reflection in the broken mirror in Henri-Georges Clouzot's *Le Corbeau*, or the broken mirror in Carne's *Le Jour se leve*. To this one could compare the extensive literary mythology of reflections in the mirror and of the world beyond the looking-glass, rooted in archaic ideas about the mirror as a window into the otherworld.

The literary equivalent of the mirror motif is the theme of the double. Just as the world beyond the looking-glass is a strange model of the everyday world, the double is an estranged reflection of the character. By changing the character's image according to the laws of mirror reflection, or enantiomorphism, the double represents a combination of features that allow one to see their invariant basis and shifts. The replacement of right-left symmetry can receive the most diverse interpretations: the dead person as double of the living, nonbeing as double of being, the ugly of the beautiful, the criminal of the saintly, the insignificant of the great, and so on. This creates a wide field of possibilities for artistic modeling.

The sign nature of the artistic text is dual at its foundation. On the one hand, the text pretends to be reality itself, pretends to have independent being, independent of the author, a thing among things of the real world. On the other hand, it constantly reminds us that it is someone's creation and that it means something. In this double illumination there arises a play in the semantic field "reality/fiction," which Pushkin expressed in the words: "Over fiction I will pour out tears."

The rhetorical joining of "things" and "signs of things," collage, in a single textual whole produces a double effect, emphasizing at once the conventionality of the conditional and its unconditional authenticity. Documents can function as "things," as realia taken from the external world rather than created by the hand of the author of the text, texts whose authenticity in the given cultural context is not placed in doubt. Such are, for example, chronicle film insertions into an artistic motion picture, as in Tarkovsky's *Mirror*, or Pushkin's device of pasting into *Dubrovsky* an extensive authentic eighteenth-century court case, changing only the proper names.

More complex are cases in which the feature of "authenticity" does not follow from the subtext's own nature, or even contradicts it, and despite this, in the rhetorical whole of the text, precisely this subtext is assigned the function of authentic reality.

Let us consider from this point of view Mikhail Bulgakov's novel *The Master and Margarita*. The novel is built as the interweaving of two independent texts: one narrates events unfolding in Moscow, contemporary with the author; the other in ancient Yershalaim. The Moscow text has the features of "reality." It has an everyday character, is overloaded with plausible details familiar to the reader, and appears as a direct continuation of the modernity familiar to the reader. In the novel it is presented as a certain primary text of neutral level.

In contrast, the narrative about Yershalaim constantly has the character of "text within text." If the first text is Bulgakov's creation, the second is created by the heroes of the novel. The irreality of the second text is emphasized by the fact that it is preceded by metatextual discussion of how it should be written: Jesus "in fact never existed alive. This is what must be emphasized." Thus, with respect to the first subtext we are invited to believe that it has real denotata, while with respect to the second we are demonstratively convinced that it has no such denotata.

This is achieved both by the constant emphasis on the textual nature of the chapters about Yershalaim, first Woland's tale and then the Master's novel, and by the fact that the Moscow chapters are presented as reality that can be seen, while the Yershalaim chapters are presented as a tale that is heard and read. The Yershalaim chapters are invariably introduced by the endings of the Moscow chapters, which become their beginnings, emphasizing their secondary nature. Woland begins speaking softly, and for some reason his accent disappears: "Everything is simple: in a white cloak..." Then the next chapter begins: "In a white cloak with blood-red lining, with a shuffling cavalryman's gait, the procurator of Judea, Pontius Pilate, came out." The chapter "The Execution" is introduced as Ivan's dream. Later the Yershalaim text is introduced as the Master's writing, as Margarita rustles the notebook pages, looks at them, kisses them, and rereads the words: "Darkness, coming from the Mediterranean Sea, covered the city hated by the procurator..."

Dream, along with inserted novellas, is a traditional device for introducing text into text. More complex are works such as Lermontov's "Dream," where the dying hero sees in a dream the heroine, who in a dream sees the dying hero. The repetition of the first and last stanzas creates a space that can be imagined as a Moebius strip, one surface of which signifies dream and the other waking reality.

However, as soon as this inertia in the distribution of real and unreal is established, a play with the reader begins through the redistribution of the boundaries between these spheres. First, the Moscow world, the "real" one, is filled with the most fantastic events, while the "invented" world of the Master's novel is subordinated to strict laws of everyday plausibility. At the level of linkage, elements of metatextual narration are introduced into the "Moscow" line as well, though very rarely, creating the scheme: the author tells of his heroes, and his heroes tell the story of Yeshua and Pilate.

Finally, in the ideological-philosophical sense, this deepening into a "story about a story" appears to Bulgakov not as a removal from reality into a world of verbal play, as occurs for example in Jan Potocki's *The Manuscript Found in Saragossa*, but as an ascent from the clowning appearance of the pseudo-real world to the authentic essence of world mystery. A mirroring is established between the two texts, but what seems to be the real object appears only as the distorted reflection of what itself had seemed to be a reflection.

An essential and very traditional means of rhetorical combination of differently coded texts is the compositional frame. "Normal," that is neutral, construction is based in particular on the fact that the framing of the text, such as the picture frame, the binding of a book or the publisher's advertisements at its end, the actor clearing his throat before an aria, the orchestra tuning its instruments, or the words "Now listen" before an oral tale, is not introduced into the text. It plays the role of warning signals about the beginning of the text, but itself remains outside its limits.

Once the frame is introduced into the text, the audience's center of attention shifts from the message to the code. A more complex case is one in which text and framing interweave, so that each is in a certain respect both framing and framed text.

It is also possible to have a construction in which one text is given as continuous narration, while others are introduced into it in deliberately fragmentary form: quotations, references, epigraphs, and so on. It is assumed that the reader will unfold these seeds of other structural constructions into texts. Such inclusions can be read both as homogeneous with the text surrounding them and as heterogeneous to it. The more sharply expressed the untranslatability between the codes of the inserted text and the main code, the more perceptible the semiotic specificity of each becomes.

No less multifunctional are cases of double or multiple coding of an entire text. We have had occasion to note cases in which theater coded people's life behavior, turning it into "historical" behavior, while "historical behavior was considered as a natural subject for painting." In this case too, the rhetorical-semiotic moment is most emphasized when distant and mutually untranslatable codes are brought together. Thus Visconti in *Senso*, a film made in the 1950s at the height of neorealism's triumph and after the director himself had made *La Terra trema*, demonstratively passed the film through the operatic code. Against the background of this general two-plane coding, he gives shots in which a living actor, Franz, is mounted with a Renaissance fresco.

Culture as a whole can be considered as a text. It is exceptionally important, however, to emphasize that this is a complexly arranged text, breaking down into a hierarchy of "texts within texts" and forming complex interweavings of texts. Since the word "text" itself contains the etymology of weaving, we can say that by interpreting it this way we return the concept "text" to its original meaning.

Thus the very concept of text undergoes a certain clarification. The idea of a text as a uniformly organized semantic space is supplemented by reference to the intrusion of diverse "accidental" elements from other texts. These enter into unpredictable play with the basic structures and sharply increase the reserve of possibilities for the unpredictability of further development.

If a system developed without unpredictable external intrusions, that is, if it represented a unique structure closed in on itself, then it would develop according to cyclical laws. In that case, ideally, it would represent repeatability. Taken in isolation, even with explosive moments included in it, the system would in time exhaust them. The constant, principled introduction into the system of elements from outside gives its movement the character of linearity and unpredictability at the same time.

The combination in one and the same process of these fundamentally incompatible elements lies at the basis of the contradiction between reality and knowledge of it. This appears most vividly in artistic knowledge: reality, transformed into plot, is assigned such concepts as beginning and end, meaning, and others. The familiar phrase used by critics of artistic works, "that does not happen in life," presupposes that reality is strictly limited by the laws of logical causality, while art is the sphere of freedom. The relation of these elements is much more complex: unpredictability in art is both a consequence and a cause of unpredictability in life.
