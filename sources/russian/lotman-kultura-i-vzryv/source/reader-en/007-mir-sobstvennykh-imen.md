## The World of Proper Names

The identification of heuristic conveniences with empirical reality has generated many scientific myths. Among them should be included the idea that the natural path of cognition proceeds from the concrete and individual to the abstract and general.

Let us first turn to the data of zoosemiotics. A cat, like many other animals, distinguishes the total number of her kittens, but does not individualize them by appearance. Substitution of "her own" kitten by a "strange" one, if there is no noticeable change in the overall number, does not disturb her.

Let us note that a child looking at recently born kittens immediately distinguishes them by individual features of coloring, the arrangement of stripes and spots, and even individual inclinations of behavior. The mother cat's vision is oriented toward perceiving the collective as a single whole.

A duel between male deer is conducted for dominance over the herd, and the herd follows the victor, collectively erasing the defeated one from its memory. One cannot imagine the following picture: one female of the deer herd, not sharing the common feelings, remains with the defeated male. Of course, the real picture is considerably more complex. One could cite cases of sharp individual markedness, especially among paired animals during periods when they form families. And yet the fact remains unchanged: the language of animals does not know proper names.

Proper names appear only among domestic animals and always as the fruit of human intervention. In the language of even the most complex animals, it has so far not been possible to detect a language of proper names. When the hero of Chekhov's story trains his new wife, a girl in age and psychology, to kiss his hand because all women previously kissed his hand, he realizes the behavior of a male, in the exact and not the pejorative sense, creating for himself an individually undifferentiated image of "his woman."

What has been said stands in striking contradiction to the behavior of the child, the offspring of the human being. Contrary to a banal and widespread idea, one can say that the principled difference between human and animal appears nowhere with such convincing sharpness as in the child. The sharply protruding "animality" of the child in the sphere of physiology conceals, for the human observer, the sharp distinctiveness of cultural-psychological behavior. Cases in which the child behaves not like an animal, that is, like a human being, seem banal. They are not noted.

Perhaps the sharpest manifestation of human nature is the use of proper names and the related singling out of individuality, the originality of a separate person, as the basis of that person's value for the "other" and for "others." "I" and "other" are two sides of a single act of self-consciousness and are impossible without one another.

Just as only the possibility of lying turns truth into conscious and freely chosen behavior, the possibility of abnormal, criminal, immoral behavior turns the conscious choice of a moral norm into an act of behavior. The human offspring is a far less pleasant creature than his counterpart in the animal world. He is capricious, lies, consciously does harm, violates prohibitions. This happens because he has the possibility of performing or not performing certain acts and experimentally feels out the boundaries of his possibilities. The possibility of doing evil is the first step toward the capacity consciously not to do it.

Children's whims have no equivalents in the behavior of animal young, although the acquisition of negative experience broadly enters into animal pedagogy.

The sharp difference between animal and human young appears in the direction of their talents. If giftedness is defined as a special capacity to master some type of activity, then a "gifted" animal carries out already existing actions with special success. Human training of animals is not being considered here. The behavior of the child, whether "good" or "bad," is experimental by its nature. This is also the principled difference between education and training, although in real practice every education includes a moment of training.

Conscious behavior is impossible without choice, that is, without a moment of individuality. Consequently, it implies the existence of a space filled with proper names.

When we speak of individuality, we do not identify this concept with the idea of a separate human biological individual. Any social group can function as an individuality. What is required is only that it possess not merely unity of behavior, which a herd also possesses, but also freedom to choose behavior. Without this, the quality of personality is absent.

The language of animals, so far as one can judge, does not possess proper names outside human intervention. Meanwhile, it is precisely proper names that create the tension between the individual and the general that lies at the basis of human consciousness. Mastering the tension between the individual word, created *ad hoc*, and the general word "for everyone," the child enters a fundamentally new mechanism of consciousness.

Most often this manifests itself in the aggressiveness of the sphere of proper names. A tendency arises toward its unlimited expansion, although the opposite case is also possible. What matters is the very fact of semantic tension, not the always short-lived victory of one tendency or another.

This is a period of turbulent word creation, because only the new word, the unique word just created, is absolutely inseparable from what it designates. This tendency aggressively penetrates from the language of the child into the speech of the parents. Thus one hears parents say: "Her name is Tanya, but we call her Tyulya, because at one time this was one of her first words and she named herself that."

The distinction between "one's own" and "alien" words divides the child's world into one's own and alien, laying down that boundary of consciousness which is preserved as one of the most important dominants of culture. Thus there arises a semantic boundary that will later play a foundational role in the social, cultural, cosmogonic, and ethical structuring of the world.

This feature of child consciousness is a moment in the laying down of what is specifically human. In a number of individuals it appears especially vividly. Vladimir Solovyov, as a child, had proper names for each of his pencils. Connected with this is the striving to make taboo, for "outsiders," the use of one's own "childhood" name. Initially, behind this stands the psychology of defensive protection of the secrets of "one's own" world. Later, the adolescent may be ashamed of his recent belonging to childhood and use this to motivate the taboo on his early language. The motives may change, but the taboo remains.

The child's striving to expand the sphere of proper names by introducing into it the whole circle of nouns belonging to his "home" world has been noted many times, and it has been seen as a manifestation of a special concreteness of child consciousness. But the opposite tendency is also active. Running through a park from tree to tree, a three-year-old child strikes birches, firs, and poplars with excitement and delight, exclaiming, "Tree!" Then, with the same cry, he strikes an electric pole and bursts into laughter. This was a joke; he does not consider the pole a tree.

Before us is not only the capacity to generalize, but also the possibility of playing with this capacity, which is connected with processes inherent only in human consciousness. The separation of word from thing is the boundary that creates an abyss between the human being and the rest of the animal world. And the child, a border guard at the edge of this abyss, perhaps reveals more vividly than "adults" the traits of the consciousness of which he is a neophyte.

In the *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality*, Rousseau expressed a brilliant thought about the psychology of distinguishing things and phenomena:

"It must be supposed that the first words people used had in their minds a much broader meaning than the words used in languages that had already taken shape; and that, not knowing the division of speech into its component parts, they first gave each word the sense of a whole sentence. Every object first received its own special name independently of genera and species, which these first teachers were not able to distinguish, and all individuals appeared to their minds as isolated, as they are in the picture of nature. If one oak was called A, then another oak was called B, for the first idea that arises in us at the sight of two objects is that they are not one and the same, and it often takes considerable time to notice what they have in common. Thus the more limited knowledge was, the more extensive the vocabulary became. The difficulties connected with this whole nomenclature could not be easily removed, for in order to arrange living beings according to general and generic designations, it was necessary to know properties and differences; observations and definitions were needed. That is, natural history and metaphysics were required..."

Rousseau then, with his characteristic boldness, asserts that an ape "passing from one nut to another" does not perceive these objects as something unified. Such an ape, contrary to the philosopher's opinion, would of course die of hunger. It is precisely at the line separating ape from child that the sources of the human specific begin. Here begins the play between proper names and common nouns, between "this one" and "any one."

And precisely because the concept "this and only this" is a new concept, it first of all attracts the attention of the neophyte. There is no "I" without "others." But only in human consciousness do "I" and "all others except me" form something single and conflictual at the same time.

One of the initial semiotic mechanisms of the human being begins with the possibility of being "only oneself," a thing, a proper name, and simultaneously appearing as a "representative" of a group, one of many, a common noun. This possibility of appearing in the role of another, of replacing someone or something, means being not what you are.

For example, when in a fight between male baboons the defeated one assumes a posture of sexual submission, we encounter a very interesting example of the starting point of semiosis. Contrary to possible Freudian interpretations, before us is not a case of the triumph of sexual drives, but a vivid manifestation of the possibility of liberation from their power in their own kingdom, their transformation into language, that is, into something formal, separated from its own meaning.

The male baboon uses the female's sexual gesture as a sign of nonsexual relations: evidence of readiness to be subordinated. Natural biological postures, given by nature and genetically embedded, are repeated as signs to which it is possible to attribute an arbitrary meaning.

Mikhail Bulgakov describes the following case in *The Theatrical Novel*. The director's imperative demands impose a taboo on the depiction of bloody scenes. As a result, an action in which the author had depicted a quarrel between characters and the death of one of them was transformed:

"At the dress rehearsal he fired a real shot in the wings. Well, Nastasya Ivanovna felt faint, since she had never once in her life heard a shot, and Lyudmila Silvestrovna went into hysterics. And from then on the shots stopped. They made a change in the play: the hero did not shoot, but waved a watering can and shouted, 'I'll kill you, scoundrel!' and stamped his feet, from which, in Ivan Vasilyevich's opinion, the play only benefited."

The comic sense of the episode lies in the comparison of the author's text and the editorial reworking of this text by Ivan Vasilyevich's censorship. This permits us to consider the given texts as antonymic from the author's point of view and as improved synonyms in the director's opinion. The possibility for two texts to appear in relation to one another simultaneously as synonyms and as antonyms brings us to a new question.

Such semantic play is not necessarily connected with the artistic function. Its sphere is wider. It is one of the mechanisms of meaning-generation. Its particular feature is that the very nature of the meaning is determined only from context, that is, as a result of turning to a broader space lying outside it.

"Where are you wandering from, wise head?"

Only the knowledge that these words are addressed to a donkey, and this knowledge requires a significantly broader context, allows us to determine that we are dealing with irony. In essence, then, here one must speak not about the structure of the text but about its function. In fact, irony implies personal knowledge of the addressee. Mockery always, directly or in masked form, has in mind a concrete singularity, that is, it is connected with a proper name.

The problem becomes still more complicated when we enter the domain of artistic texts.

The artistic text proceeds in principle from the possibility of complicating the relation between first and third person, that is, between the pull toward the space of proper names and objective narration in the third person. In this respect, the very possibility of the artistic text is suggested to us by the psychological experience of dreams. It is precisely here that the human being receives the experience of "flickering" between first and third person, between real and conditional spheres of activity.

Thus in dream the grammatical capacities of language acquire "as if reality." The domain of the visible, which had previously been naively identified with reality, turns out to be a space in which all transformations permitted by language are possible: conditional and unreal narration, a set of actions in the space of time, change of point of view.

One of the features of dream is that the categories of speaking are transferred into the space of vision. Without this experience, such spheres as art and religion, that is, the highest manifestations of consciousness, would have been impossible.

The transfer of the sphere of dreams into a part of consciousness entails fundamental changes in its very nature. Only these changes make it possible to throw a bridge from dream to artistic activity. The experience extracted from dreams is subjected to the same transformation that we perform when we recount dreams. Pavel Florensky's brilliant hypothesis, according to which in the moment of retelling a dream the beginning, end, and direction of the dream exchange places, has not yet been tested experimentally, and therefore we will not dwell on it further.

More essential for us is the transformation to which we subject a dream in verbally retelling it. Here there is an obvious increase in the degree of organization: the structure of narration is imposed on our speech.

The transformation of the visible into the tellable inevitably increases the degree of organizedness. This is how a text is created. The process of telling displaces from our memory the real imprints of the dream, and a person becomes convinced that he really saw exactly what he told. Thereafter, this verbally retold text is deposited in our memory.

Yet this is only part of the process of remembering. The verbally organized text is thrown back into the visual images preserved in memory and is remembered in visual form. Thus a structure of visible narration is created, combining the feeling of reality inherent in everything seen with all the grammatical possibilities of unreality. This is the potential material for artistic creativity. The capacity of higher animals to see dreams allows one to suppose that the boundary of art is not so very far from their consciousness. But our information in this area is so problematic that it deprives us of the possibility of drawing any conclusions.

Art is the most developed space of conditional reality. This is precisely what makes it a "testing ground" for the sphere of mental experiment and, more broadly, for processes of intellectual dynamics. In the present connection we are interested in art's capacity to combine the space of proper names and common nouns.

Whole extensive spheres of art, rooted moreover in the most archaic layers, are connected with the first person and constitute *ich-Erzahlung*, narration in the first person. Yet this "I" simultaneously turns out to be the bearer of the meaning "all others in the position of I." In essence, this too is the contradiction that Pushkin defined in the words:

Over fiction I will pour out tears.

In *Diary of One Week*, Radishchev brings his hero to the theater for Saurin's play *Beverley* and asks himself: "He drinks poison. What is that to you?"

This question has a natural answer so long as, according to the laws of folklore, the performer was simultaneously the spectator of himself, and "I" and "he" merged in one action. The division into performer and spectator, singer and listener, created an entirely new and internally contradictory situation. The performer is "he" for me, but I transfer all his speech and feelings to my "I." I see how my own feelings look.

In this sense, the special role of ballet is characteristic at the moment when relations between first and third person become more complicated. I transfer to the stage the functions of the third person: everything that can be seen I transfer to another person, to him, while everything that remains in the sphere of internal experiences I appropriate to myself, appearing as the embodiment of the first person.

However, the relation "stage-spectator" gives only one aspect of the question. Another is realized along the axis "I - other spectator." Gogol wrote in *Theater Departure*: "Fables!... But there the balconies and railings of the theaters groan; everything shook from bottom to top, turning into one feeling, one instant, one person; all people met as brothers in one movement of the soul, and a grateful hymn thunders in friendly applause to the one who has not been in the world for five hundred years."

It should be emphasized, however, that the value of this unity lies precisely in the fact that it is based on the individual difference of spectators among themselves and does not cancel this difference. It is a psychological violence possible only in the atmosphere of emotional tension created in the theater.
