## Working Translation

DIALOGUE THREE


GERVASIO

The hour has indeed come, and these men have not arrived. Since I have no other
thought that pulls me, I want to take pleasure in hearing these men reason,
from whom, besides being able to learn some chess move of philosophy, I still
have a fine pastime concerning those crickets that dance in that heteroclite
brain of Polihimnio the pedant. While he says that he wishes to judge who
speaks well, who discourses better, who makes incongruities and errors in
philosophy, when it is time to say his part and he does not know what to offer,
he comes to string out from inside the handle of his windy pedantry a little
salad of tiny proverbs, phrases in Latin or Greek, which never make to the
purpose of what others are saying. Hence, without too much difficulty, there is
no blind person who cannot see how much he is mad according to the letter, while
others are wise in the vulgar tongue. Now here he is, by my faith, coming along
as if even in the movement of his steps he knows how to walk according to the
letter. Welcome, *dominus magister*.

Polihimnio. That *magister* does not matter to me, since in this deviant and
enormous age it is attributed no more to my peers than to any beard-trimmer,
cobbler, and pig-castrator. Therefore let this be consulted: do not be called
Rabbi.

Gervasio. How, then, do you wish me to call you? Does "most reverend" please
you?

Polihimnio. That is priestly and clerical.

Gervasio. Do you feel like "most illustrious"?

Polihimnio. Let arms yield to the toga: this belongs to knights as well as to
those in purple.

Gervasio. The Caesarean majesty, eh?

Polihimnio. What is Caesar's to Caesar.

Gervasio. Take, then, the *domine*, please; take the gravity-thundering one,
the *divum pater*! But let us come to ourselves: why are you all so late?

Polihimnio. So I believe the others are entangled in some other affair, while
I, so as not to let this day pass without a line, have been occupied with the
contemplation of the type of globe commonly called the world-map.

Gervasio. What have you to do with the world-map?

Polihimnio. I contemplate the parts of the earth, climates, provinces, and
regions, all of which I have traversed with ideal reason, and many also with my
steps.

Gervasio. I would like you to discourse somewhat within yourself, because this
seems to me to matter more to you, and I believe you care less about it.

Polihimnio. Let envy be absent from the word; because with this I come much
more effectively to know myself.

Gervasio. And how will you persuade me of this?

Polihimnio. Because from the contemplation of the megacosm, by necessary
deduction made from similars, one can easily arrive at cognition of the
microcosm, whose particles correspond to the parts of that.

Gervasio. So we will find inside you the Moon, Mercury, and other stars?
France, Spain, Italy, England, Calicut, and other countries?

Polihimnio. Why not? By a certain analogy.

Gervasio. By a certain analogy I believe you are a great monarch; but, if you
were a woman, I would ask you whether there is room in you to lodge a little
boy, or to preserve in you one of those plants Diogenes mentioned.

Polihimnio. Ah, ah, in a certain mode witty. But this petition does not square
with a wise and erudite man.

Gervasio. If I were erudite and esteemed myself wise, I would not come here to
learn together with you.

Polihimnio. You, yes; but I do not come to learn, because now it is mine to
teach; it also concerns me to judge those who wish to teach. Therefore I come
for another end than that for which you should come, to whom it belongs to be a
recruit, isagogical, and disciple.

Gervasio. For what end?

Polihimnio. To judge, I say.

Gervasio. Truly, it suits your peers more than others to make judgment of the
sciences and doctrines, because you are the only ones to whom the liberality of
the stars and the munificence of fate have granted the power to draw the juice
from words.

Polihimnio. And consequently from the senses too, which are joined to words.

Gervasio. As the soul to the body.

Polihimnio. Those words, when well understood, also make one consider the sense
well; therefore from cognition of languages, in which I, more than any other in
this city, am exercised, and I esteem myself no less learned than anyone who
keeps a school of Minerva open, proceeds cognition of any science whatever.

Gervasio. Therefore all those who understand the Italian language will
comprehend the philosophy of the Nolan?

Polihimnio. Yes, but some other practice and judgment are also needed.

Gervasio. For some time I thought that this practice was the principal thing,
because one who does not know Greek can understand the whole sense of Aristotle
and know many errors in him, as one openly sees that this idolatry, which turned
around the authority of that philosopher, especially as to natural things, is
altogether abolished among all who understand the senses that this other sect
brings. And one who knows neither Greek nor Arabic, and perhaps not even Latin,
like Paracelsus, can have known the nature of medicines and medicine better
than Galen, Avicenna, and all who make themselves heard in the Roman tongue.
Philosophies and laws do not go to perdition through shortage of interpreters
of words, but through shortage of those who go deep into the meanings.

Polihimnio. So, then, you come to count one like me in the number of the foolish
multitude?

Gervasio. May the gods not wish it; because I know that with knowledge and
study of languages, which is a rare and singular thing, not only you but all
your peers are most valiant in making judgment of doctrines, after having
sifted the meanings of those who put themselves into the field for us.

Polihimnio. Because you speak the very truth, I can easily persuade myself that
you do not say it without reason. Therefore, as it is not difficult for you, it
will not be burdensome for you to bring it forward.

Gervasio. I will speak, always referring myself to the censure of your prudence
and letters. It is a common proverb that those who are outside the game
understand it more than those who are inside it; just as those who are in the
audience can better judge the acts than the characters who are on stage, and one
who is not of the chapel or concert can make better judgment of the music. The
same appears in card play, chess, fencing, and other similar things. So you
other lord pedants, by being excluded and outside every act of science and
philosophy, and by not having and never having had participation with Aristotle,
Plato, and others like them, can better judge and condemn them with your
grammatical sufficiency and presumption of your natural gift than the Nolan can,
who finds himself in the same theater, in the same familiarity and domesticity,
so that he easily fights them after having known their inner and most profound
meanings. You, I say, by being outside every profession of gentlemen and
pilgrim minds, can judge them better.

Polihimnio. I would not know how to answer so suddenly to this most impudent
man. My voice has stuck in my throat.

Gervasio. Therefore your peers are as presumptuous as others are not who have
their foot inside; and therefore I assure you that worthily you usurp the office
of approving this, reproving that, glossing that other, making here a concord
and collation, there an appendix.

Polihimnio. This most ignorant man, from the fact that I am skilled in good
humane letters, wishes to infer that I am ignorant in philosophy.

Gervasio. Most learned, messer Polihimnio, I mean that if you had all the
languages that there are, as our preachers say, seventy-two...

Polihimnio. And a half.

Gervasio. ...from this it would not only not follow that you are fit to make
judgment of philosophers, but further it could not prevent you from being the
greatest awkward animal that lives in a human face; and also there is nothing
to prevent someone who has barely one of the languages, even a bastard one, from
being the wisest and most learned in the whole world. Now consider the profit
that two such men have made, of whom one is a French arch-pedant, who made the
*Schools* on the liberal arts and the *Animadversions* against Aristotle; and
the other an Italian dung of pedants, who dirtied so many quires with his
*Peripatetic Discussions*. Everyone easily sees that the first most eloquently
shows himself to be little wise; the second, simply speaking, shows that he has
much of the beast and ass. Of the first we can still say that he understood
Aristotle, but that he understood him badly; and if he had understood him well,
he would perhaps have had the wit to wage honorable war against him, as the
most judicious Telesio of Cosenza has done. Of the second we cannot say that he
understood him either badly or well, but that he read and reread him, sewed and
unsewed him, and compared him with a thousand other Greek authors, friends and
enemies of him; and in the end made a very great labor not only without any
profit, but even with a very great disprofit, so that whoever wishes to see into
how much madness and presumptuous vanity a pedantic habit can precipitate and
sink should see that one book, before the seed of it is lost. But here are
Teofilo and Dicsono Arelio present.

Polihimnio. Be present happily, lords. Your presence is the cause that my
excandescence does not come to exaggerate thunderous sentences against the vain
propositions this chattering fruit-waster has held.

Gervasio. And from me it has taken the material to play around the majesty of
this most reverend owl.

Dicsono Arelio. Every thing goes well if you do not grow angry.

Gervasio. What I say, I say in play, because I love the signor master.

Polihimnio. I too, when I grow angry, do not grow angry seriously, because I do
not hate Gervasio.

Dicsono Arelio. Good. Then let me discourse with Teofilo.

## Translation Pressure

The pedant prelude keeps pressing the same rule from `Dialogo primo`: knowledge
of words is not knowledge of things. Bruno uses Gervasio to puncture
Polihimnio's claim that language mastery authorizes philosophical judgment.

The Paracelsus/Galen/Avicenna contrast shows Bruno's pragmatic side. Knowing
the thing can outrun linguistic and textual authority.

This batch also prepares the methodological pluralism that Teofilo will state
later in the dialogue: different ways of philosophizing can be useful if they
actually reach the thing.
