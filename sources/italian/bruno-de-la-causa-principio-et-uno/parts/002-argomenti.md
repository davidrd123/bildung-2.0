## Working Translation

Argument of the first dialogue.


There in the first dialogue you have an apology, or whatever else it may be,
concerning the five dialogues around *The Ash Wednesday Supper*, etc.




Argument of the second dialogue.


In the second dialogue you have, first, the reason for the difficulty of such
knowledge, in order to know how far the knowable object is removed from the
cognitive power. Second, in what mode and to what extent the principle and cause
is made clear from the caused and principiated thing. Third, how much knowledge
of the substance of the universe contributes to notice of that on which it
depends. Fourth, by what means and way we in particular attempt to know the
first principle. Fifth, the difference and concordance, identity and diversity,
between the signified of this term "cause" and this term "principle." Sixth,
what the cause is that is distinguished into efficient, formal, and final, and
in how many modes the efficient cause is named, and with how many reasons it is
conceived; how this efficient cause is in a certain mode intimate to natural
things, by being nature itself, and how in a certain mode it is exterior to
them; how the formal cause is joined to the efficient, and is that through which
the efficient operates, and how the same is stirred up by the efficient from the
womb of matter; how the efficient and the form coincide in one subject-principle,
and how the one cause is distinct from the other. Seventh, the difference
between the universal formal cause, which is one soul through which the infinite
universe, as infinite, is not one animal positively but negatively, and the
particular formal cause, multipliable and multiplied into infinity; which, the
more it is in a more general and superior subject, the more perfect it is. Hence
the great animals, which are the stars, must in great comparison be esteemed
more divine, that is, more intelligent without error and operators without
defect. Eighth, that the first and principal natural form, formal principle, and
efficient nature is the soul of the universe, which is the principle of life,
vegetation, and sense in all things that live, vegetate, and sense. And by way
of conclusion it is held to be a thing unworthy of a rational subject to be able
to believe that the universe and its other principal bodies are inanimate,
because from their parts and excrements derive the animals that we call most
perfect. Ninth, that there is no thing so lacking, broken, diminished, and
imperfect that, insofar as it has a formal principle, it does not likewise have
soul, although it does not have the act of a supposit that we call animal. And
it is concluded, with Pythagoras and others, that they have not opened their
eyes in vain, as an immense spirit, according to diverse reasons and orders,
fills and contains the whole. Tenth, it is made understood that, since this
spirit persists together with matter, which the Babylonians and Persians called
shadow, and since both the one and the other are indissoluble, it is impossible
that in any point any thing should see corruption, or come to death according to
substance, although according to certain accidents every thing changes face and
is transmuted now under one composition and now under another, through one
disposition or another, now leaving and retaking this or that being. Eleventh,
that the Aristotelians, Platonists, and other sophists have not known the
substance of things; and it is shown clearly that in natural things, beyond
matter, everything they call substance is most pure accident; and that from the
knowledge of the true form is inferred the true notice of what life is and what
death is; and, with the vain and childish terror of death completely extinguished,
one knows a part of the happiness that our contemplation brings, according to
the foundations of our philosophy, since it removes the dark veil of the mad
sentiment concerning Orcus and greedy Charon, through which the sweetest part of
our life is stolen and poisoned. Twelfth, the form is distinguished, not
according to the substantial reason through which it is one, but according to
the acts and exercises of the facultative powers and specific grades of the
being that it comes to produce. Thirteenth, the true definitive reason of the
formal principle is concluded: how form is a perfect species, distinct in matter
according to the accidental dispositions dependent on the material form, as on
that which consists in diverse grades and dispositions of active and passive
qualities. It is seen how it is variable and how invariable; how it defines and
terminates matter, and how it is defined and terminated by matter. Last, with a
certain similitude accommodated to common sense, it is shown how this form, this
soul, can be whole in the whole and in any part of the whole.




Argument of the third dialogue.


In the third dialogue, after in the first there has been discourse concerning
form, which has more reason of cause than of principle, one proceeds to the
consideration of matter, which is held to have more reason of principle and
element than of cause. There, leaving aside the preludes that are at the
beginning of the dialogue, first it is shown that David of Dinant was not mad in
his own degree in taking matter as a most excellent and divine thing. Second,
how by diverse ways of philosophizing diverse reasons of matter can be taken,
although truly it is one first and absolute matter; because it is verified in
diverse grades and is hidden under diverse such species, different thinkers can
take it differently according to those reasons that are appropriate to
themselves, not otherwise than number, which is taken purely and simply by the
arithmetician, harmonically by the musician, typically by the cabalist, and as a
subject otherwise by other madmen and other wise men. Third, the signified by
the name matter is declared through the difference and similitude between the
natural and artificial subject. Fourth, it is proposed how the pertinacious
should be dealt with, and how far we are obligated to answer and dispute. Fifth,
from the true reason of matter it is inferred that no substantial form loses
being; and it is strongly demonstrated that the Peripatetics and other vulgar
philosophers, although they name substantial form, have known no substance other
than matter. Sixth, a constant formal principle is concluded, just as a constant
material principle is known; and with the diversity of dispositions that are in
matter, the formal principle is transported to the multiform figuration of
diverse species and individuals; and it is shown whence it has happened that
some, raised in the Peripatetic school, have refused to know any substance other
than matter. Seventh, how it is necessary for reason to distinguish matter from
form, potency from act; and what was said secondarily is repeated: how the
subject and principle of natural things can, by diverse modes of philosophizing,
be taken differently without incurring calumny; but more usefully according to
natural and magical modes, more variously according to mathematical and rational
modes, especially if these so serve the rule and exercise of reason that by
them, in the end, some worthy thing is put into act and some fruit of practice
is brought back, without which every contemplation would be judged vain.

Eighth, two reasons are proposed by which matter is customarily considered,
namely as it is a potency and as it is a subject. And beginning from the first
reason, it is distinguished into active and passive, and in a certain mode it is
brought back into one. Ninth, from the eighth proposition it is inferred how the
supreme and divine is all that it can be, and how the universe is all that it
can be, and other things are not all that they can be. Tenth, as a consequence
of what is said in the ninth, it is shown briefly, deeply, and openly whence in
nature there are vices, monsters, corruption, and death.

Eleventh, in what mode the universe is in no part and in all parts; and place
is given to an excellent contemplation of divinity.

Twelfth, whence it comes that the intellect cannot grasp this most absolute act
and this most absolute potency. Thirteenth, the excellence of matter is
concluded, which coincides with form just as potency coincides with act. Last,
both from this, that potency coincides with act and the universe is all that it
can be, and from other reasons, it is concluded that the whole is one.




Argument of the fourth dialogue.


In the fourth dialogue, after matter has been considered in the second dialogue
insofar as it is a potency, matter is considered insofar as it is a subject.
There first, with the Polyhymnian amusements, the reason of matter is brought
forward according to common principles, both those of some Platonists and those
of all Peripatetics. Second, reasoning according to its own principles, it is
shown by several reasons that there is one matter of corporeal and incorporeal
things. Of these reasons, the first is taken from the potency of the same genus;
the second, from the reason of a certain proportional analogy between corporeal
and incorporeal, absolute and contracted; the third, from the order and ladder
of nature, which ascends to a first completer or comprehender; the fourth, from
what must be one indistinct thing before matter becomes distinguished into
corporeal and non-corporeal, which indistinct thing is signified by the supreme
genus of the category; the fifth, from the fact that, just as there is one
common reason for the sensible and the intelligible, so there must be one for
the subject of sensibility; the sixth, from the fact that the being of matter is
absolute from being body, whence with no less reason it can fit incorporeal
things than corporeal things; the seventh, from the order of superior and
inferior that is found in substances, because where this is, there is
presupposed and understood a certain communion, which is according to matter
that is always signified by genus, as form is signified by the specific
difference; the eighth is from an extraneous principle, but one conceded by
many; the ninth, from the plurality of species that is said to be in the
intelligible world; the tenth, from the similitude and imitation of three
worlds, metaphysical, physical, and logical; the eleventh, from the fact that
every number, diversity, order, beauty, and ornament is around matter.

Third, four contrary reasons are brought forward briefly, and these are
answered. Fourth, it is shown how the reason is diverse between this and that,
of this and that matter, and how in incorporeal things matter coincides with
act, and how all the species of dimensions are in matter, and all qualities are
comprehended in form. Fifth, that no wise person ever said that forms are
received by matter as from outside, but that matter, bringing them forth as from
its womb, sends them from within. Therefore it is not a *prope nihil*, an almost
nothing, a naked and pure potency, if all forms are as if contained by it, and
from it, by virtue of the efficient cause, which can also be indistinct from it
according to being, produced and brought forth; and that they have no lesser
reason of actuality in sensible and explicated being except according to
accidental subsistence, since everything that is seen and made open through
accidents founded on dimensions is pure accident, while the individual substance
always remains and coincides with individual matter. Hence it is seen clearly
that from explication we can take nothing other than accidents, so that
substantial differences are hidden, as Aristotle, forced by truth, said. In
such a way, if we consider well, from this we can infer that the omniform
substance is one, that the true and being is one, which according to innumerable
circumstances and individuals appears, showing itself in so many and such
diverse supposits.

Sixth, how far outside every reason is what Aristotle and others like him
understand concerning matter's being in potency, which certainly is nothing,
since, according to them themselves, matter is so thoroughly permanent that it
never changes or varies its own being, but around it there is every variety and
mutation, and what is after having been able to be is always, even according to
them, the composite. Seventh, the appetite of matter is determined, showing how
vainly it is defined by that appetite, without departing from the reasons taken
from the principles and suppositions of those very thinkers who proclaim matter
so much as daughter of privation and as similar to the irreparable greed of the
desiring woman.




Argument of the fifth dialogue.


In the fifth dialogue, since it treats especially of the one, the foundation of
the edifice of all natural and divine knowledge is completed. There, first, is
brought forward the proposition of the coincidence of matter and form, of
potency and act, so that being, logically divided into that which is and can be,
is physically undivided, indistinct, and one; and this together is infinite,
immobile, impartible, without difference of whole and part, principle and
principiated. Second, that in it the age is not different from the year, the
year from the moment, the palm from the stadium, the stadium from the parasang,
and in its essence this and that specific being is not one thing and another;
and therefore in the universe there is no number, and therefore the universe is
one. Third, that in the infinite the point is not different from body, because
potency is not one thing and act another; and there, if the point can run in
length, the line in breadth, the surface in depth, the one is long, the other
broad, the other deep; and every thing is long, broad, and deep; and
consequently the same and one; and the universe is all center and all
circumference. Fourth, how from the fact that Jove, as they name him, is more
intimately in the whole than the form of the whole can be imagined to be in it,
because he is the essence through which everything that is has being, and since
he is in everything, every thing has the whole more intimately than its own
form, it is inferred that all things are in each thing, and consequently all is
one. Fifth, answer is made to the doubt that asks why all particular things
change, and particular matters, in order to receive other and other being, force
themselves toward other and other forms; and it is shown how in multitude there
is unity, and in unity there is multitude; and how being is many-moded and
many-one, and in the end one in substance and truth. Sixth, it is inferred
whence that difference and that number proceed, and that these are not being,
but of being and around being. Seventh, it is warned that whoever has found this
one, I mean the reason of this unity, has found that key without which it is
impossible to have entrance to the true contemplation of nature. Eighth, with a
new contemplation it is repeated that the one, the infinite, being, and that
which is in everything, is through everything, indeed is the same *ubique*; and
that thus infinite dimension, by not being magnitude, coincides with the
individual, just as infinite multitude, by not being number, coincides with
unity. Ninth, how in the infinite there is no part and part, whatever may be
explicitly in the universe; where, however, all that we see of diversity and
difference is nothing other than a diverse and different face of the same
substance. Tenth, how in the two extremes that are said to be at the extremity
of the ladder of nature, there are not more two principles than one, two beings
than one, two contraries and different things than one concordant and the same.
There height is depth, the abyss is inaccessible light, darkness is clarity,
the great is small, the confused is distinct, strife is friendship, the divisible
is indivisible, the atom is immense, and the reverse. Eleventh, how certain
geometric nominations such as point and one are taken to advance toward the
contemplation of being and one, and are not sufficient of themselves to signify
that. Hence Pythagoras, Parmenides, and Plato should not be interpreted so
stupidly, according to Aristotle's pedantic censure. Twelfth, from the fact that
substance and being are distinct from quantity, measure, and number, it is
inferred that substance is one and individual in the whole and in any thing.

Thirteenth, the signs and verifications are brought forward by which the
contraries truly concur, are from one principle, and are in truth and substance
one; and this, after being seen mathematically, is concluded physically.

Here, most illustrious Signor, is whence one must exit before wishing to enter
into the more special and appropriate knowledge of things. Here, as in its own
seed, is contained and implicated the multitude of conclusions of natural
science. From here derives the interweaving, disposition, and order of the
speculative sciences. Without this isagogy one attempts, enters, and begins in
vain. Take, then, with grateful mind this principle, this one, this fountain,
this head, so that its offspring and generation may be animated to come forth
and put themselves forward, its streams and greater rivers may diffuse
themselves, its number may successively multiply, and its members may further
arrange themselves, until, with night ceasing together with its sleepy veil and
dark mantle, bright Tithonus, kinsman of the divine Muses, adorned with his
family, encircled by his eternal court, after the nocturnal torches have been
banished, adorning the world with new day, drives again the triumphant chariot
from the vermilion womb of this lovely Dawn. Farewell.

## Translation Pressure

The `argomenti` are not disposable summaries. They are a doctrinal map of the
whole work and identify the pressure points before the dialogues begin:
cause/principle, form/matter, act/potency, substance/accident, soul of the
universe, and the one.

`Principiato` is preserved as `principiated` where Bruno pairs it with
`causato`. This is ugly English, but it keeps the cause/principle distinction
audible until the dialogues clarify it.

`Soggetto` is translated as `subject` for now, but the glossary should keep
open whether some passages require `substrate`.

The source has a few joined forms that look like e-text artifacts:
`efficiente,etè`, `partietescrementi`, and `Orcoetavaro`. The translation
silently separates the obvious words, but the source issue should be revisited
if the work is promoted.
