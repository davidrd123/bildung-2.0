## Working Translation

Teofilo. Very well. And I add to this that just as this efficient is universal
in the universe and is special and particular in the parts and members of it, so
too are its form and its end.

Dicsono Arelio. Now enough has been said about causes; let us proceed to reason
about principles.

Teofilo. Now, to come to the constitutive principles of things, first I will
reason about form because it is in a certain mode the same as the already named
efficient cause; for the intellect, which is a potency of the soul of the world,
has been called the proximate efficient of all natural things.

Dicsono Arelio. But how can the same subject be principle and cause of natural
things? How can it have the reason of an intrinsic part and not of an extrinsic
part?

Teofilo. I say that this is not inconvenient, considering that the soul is in
the body as the pilot in the ship. This pilot, insofar as he is moved together
with the ship, is part of it; considered insofar as he governs and moves it, he
is not understood as part, but as distinct efficient. So the soul of the
universe, insofar as it ensouls and informs, comes to be an intrinsic and formal
part of it; but insofar as it directs and governs, it is not part, and does not
have the reason of principle but of cause. Aristotle himself grants us this:
although he denies that the soul has toward the body the reason that the pilot
has toward the ship, nevertheless, considering it according to that potency by
which it understands and knows, he does not dare name it act and form of body,
but says, as of an efficient separated from matter according to being, that it
is something that comes from outside, according to its subsistence divided from
the composite.

Dicsono Arelio. I approve what you say, because if being separated from the
body and having the reason of efficient cause belong to the intellective potency
of our soul, this must much more be affirmed of the soul of the world. For
Plotinus says, writing against the Gnostics, that "the soul of the world rules
the universe with greater ease than our soul rules our body"; then there is a
great difference between the mode by which that soul and this one governs. That
one rules the world not as bound, in such a way that it itself does not bind
what it takes; that one does not suffer from other things nor with other things;
that one without impediment rises to supernal things; that one, giving life and
perfection to the body, receives from it no imperfection; and therefore it is
eternally joined to the same subject. This one, then, is manifestly of contrary
condition. Now if, according to your principle, the perfections that are in
inferior natures must be attributed and known more highly in superior natures,
we must without any doubt affirm the distinction you have brought forward. This
is affirmed not only in the soul of the world, but also of each star, since, as
the said philosopher wishes, all have power to contemplate God, the principles
of all things, and the distribution of the orders of the universe. And he wishes
that this does not occur by way of memory, discourse, and consideration,
because every work of theirs is eternal work, and there is no act that can be
new to them; and therefore they do nothing that is not altogether fitting,
perfect, with a certain and prefixed order, without act of cogitation. So too,
by the example of a perfect writer and cithara-player, Aristotle also shows
that because nature does not discourse and think again, one should not conclude
that she operates without intellect and final intention; for exquisite musicians
and writers are less attentive to what they do, and do not err like the more
rough and inert ones, who, with more thinking and attention, make the work less
perfect and also not without error.

Teofilo. You understand it. Now we come to the more particular matter. It seems
to me that those detract from the divine goodness and from the excellence of
this great animal and simulacrum of the first principle who do not wish to
understand or affirm that the world with its members is animated, as if God
envied his image, as if the architect did not love his singular work, of which
Plato says that he took pleasure in his workmanship because of the similitude
that he beheld in it. And certainly what can be presented to the eyes of
divinity more beautiful than this universe? And since it consists of its parts,
to which of these should more be attributed than to the formal principle? I
leave to a better and more particular discourse a thousand natural reasons
beyond this topical or logical one.

Dicsono Arelio. I do not care that you strive in this, since there is no
philosopher of any reputation, even among the Peripatetics, who does not wish
the world and its spheres to be animated in some mode. I would now like to
understand in what mode you wish this form to insinuate itself into the matter
of the universe.

Teofilo. It joins itself to matter in such a way that the nature of body, which
according to itself is not beautiful, insofar as it is capable comes to make
itself participant in beauty, since there is no beauty unless it consists in
some species or form, and there is no form that is not produced by soul.

Dicsono Arelio. It seems to me I hear something very new: do you perhaps wish
not only the form of the universe, but all the forms of natural things, to be
soul?

Teofilo. Yes.

Dicsono Arelio. Are all things, then, animated?

Teofilo. Yes.

Dicsono Arelio. Now who will grant you this?

Teofilo. Now who will be able to disprove it with reason?

Dicsono Arelio. It is common sense that not all things live.

Teofilo. The most common sense is not the truest.

Dicsono Arelio. I easily believe that this can be defended. But it will not be
enough to make a thing true because it can be defended, since it must also be
possible to prove it.

Teofilo. This is not difficult. Are there not philosophers who say that the
world is animated?

Dicsono Arelio. Certainly there are many, and very principal ones.

Teofilo. Now why will those same philosophers not say that all the parts of the
world are animated?

Dicsono Arelio. Certainly they say it, but of the principal parts, and those
that are true parts of the world, since with no lesser reason they wish the soul
to be whole in the whole world and whole in any part of it than the soul of the
animals sensible to us is whole throughout.

Teofilo. Now which do you think are not true parts of the world?

Dicsono Arelio. Those that are not first bodies, as the Peripatetics say: the
earth with the waters and other parts, which, according to what you say,
constitute the whole animal; the moon, the sun, and other bodies. Besides these
principal animals, there are those that are not primary parts of the universe,
of which they say some have vegetative soul, others sensitive, others
intellective.

Teofilo. Now, if the soul, because it is in the whole, is also in the parts, why
do you not wish it to be in the parts of the parts?

Dicsono Arelio. I do wish it, but in the parts of the parts of animate things.

Teofilo. Now what are those things that are not animate, or are not part of
animate things?

Dicsono Arelio. Does it seem to you that we have few of them before our eyes?
All things that do not have life.

Teofilo. And what are the things that do not have life, at least a vital
principle?

Dicsono Arelio. To conclude it, do you wish that there is no thing that does
not have soul and that does not have vital principle?

Teofilo. This is what I finally wish.

Polihimnio. Therefore, does a dead body have soul? Therefore, will my wooden
shoes, my slippers, my boots, my spurs, my ring, and my gloves be animated? Are
my toga and my cloak animated?

Gervasio. Yes, sir, yes, master Polihimnio, why not? I indeed believe that your
toga and your mantle are well animated when they contain an animal, as you are,
inside; the boots and spurs are animated when they contain the feet; the hat is
animated when it contains the head, which is not without soul; and the stable is
also animated when it contains the horse, the mule, or Your Lordship. Do you
not understand it this way, Teofilo? Does it not seem to you that I have
understood it better than the *dominus magister*?

Polihimnio. Whose herd? As if there are not asses, even again and again subtle?
Do you dare, you tasteless abecedarian, to wish to equiparate yourself with an
arch-teacher and moderator of a Minerval school like me?

Gervasio. Peace be with you, lord master, servant of servants and footstool of
your feet.

Polihimnio. May God curse you forever and ever.

Dicsono Arelio. Without anger; leave these things to us to determine.

Polihimnio. Therefore let Theophilus pursue his dogmas.

Teofilo. So I will do. I say, then, that the table as table is not animated,
nor the garment, nor leather as leather, nor glass as glass; but as natural and
composite things, they have in themselves matter and form. Let a thing be as
small and minimal as one wishes, it has in itself part of spiritual substance,
which, if it finds the subject disposed, extends itself to be plant, to be
animal, and receives members of whatever body is commonly called animated. For
spirit is found in all things, and there is no smallest corpuscle that does not
contain in itself such a portion that it does not animate.

Polihimnio. Therefore, whatever is, is animal.

Teofilo. Not all things that have soul are called animated.

Dicsono Arelio. Therefore, at least, all things have life?

Teofilo. I concede that all things have in themselves soul, have life,
according to substance and not according to act and operation knowable by all
Peripatetics and those who define life and soul according to certain reasons
that are too gross.

Dicsono Arelio. You uncover for me some plausible mode by which one could
maintain the opinion of Anaxagoras, who wished every thing to be in every thing,
because, the spirit or soul or universal form being in all things, from every
thing every thing can be produced.

Teofilo. I do not say plausible, but true. For that spirit is found in all
things, which, if they are not animals, are animated; if they are not sensible
according to the act of animality and life, they nevertheless are so according
to the principle and a certain first act of animality and life. And I say no
more, because I wish to suspend judgment concerning the property of many little
stones and gems, which, broken and cut and set in disordered pieces, have
certain powers to alter the spirit and engender new affections and passions in
the soul, not only in the body. And we know that such effects do not proceed,
nor can come, from a purely material quality, but necessarily refer back to a
symbolic vital and animal principle. Besides, we see the same sensibly in dead
branches and roots, which, purging and gathering humors, altering spirits, show
necessarily effects of life. I leave aside that not without reason the
necromancers hope to effect many things through the bones of the dead, and
believe that these retain, if not that same act, still such and such an act of
life as comes to the point for extraordinary effects. Other occasions will make
me discourse at greater length concerning the mind, the spirit, the soul, the
life that penetrates all, is in all, and moves all matter; fills the womb of
matter, and rather exceeds it than is exceeded by it, since spiritual substance
cannot be overcome by material substance, but rather comes to contain it.

## Translation Pressure

This is the first explicit panpsychic or hylozoic pressure point: all things
have soul and life according to substance, even where they do not show the act
or operation that Peripatetics recognize as life.

`Animated` is unstable in English here. Bruno distinguishes having soul/life
from being commonly called animate or animal. I keep `animated`, `animal`, and
`life` close to the surface even where the result is awkward.

Gervasio's jokes are not interruptions to skip. They force Teofilo to clarify
the difference between artifacts as artifacts and natural composites as bearing
matter and form.

The passage on gems, dead roots, and necromantic bones is a real risk point for
modern readers. It should be preserved as Bruno's extension of vital principle,
not silently rationalized into a weaker metaphor.
