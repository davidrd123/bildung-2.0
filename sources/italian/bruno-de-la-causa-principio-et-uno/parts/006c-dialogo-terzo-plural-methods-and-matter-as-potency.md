## Working Translation

Dicsono Arelio. Therefore you approve the study of diverse philosophies?

Teofilo. Very much, for one who has abundance of time and intelligence; for
others I approve the study of the better one, if the gods will that he guesses
it rightly.

Dicsono Arelio. I am certain, however, that you do not approve all philosophies,
but the good and the better.

Teofilo. So it is. As also in diverse orders of healing, I do not reject what
is done magically by application of roots, suspension of stones, and murmuring
of incantations, if the rigor of theologians lets me speak as a pure naturalist.
I approve what is done physically and proceeds through apothecary recipes, with
which choler, blood, phlegm, and melancholy are pursued or fled. I accept that
other which is done chemically, which abstracts fifth essences and, by the work
of fire, from all those composites makes mercury fly, salt subside, and sulfur
flash or be isolated. But nevertheless, in the matter of medicine, I do not wish
to determine among so many good modes which is the best, because the epileptic
on whom the physicist and the chemist have wasted time, if he is cured by the
magus, will with reason approve this one more than that one and the other
physician. Discourse similarly through the other species: none of them will
come to be less good than another if this one, like the others, comes to effect
the end it proposes. In the particular case, then, this physician who will heal
me is better than the others who kill me or torment me.

Gervasio. Whence does it happen that these sects of physicians are so hostile
among themselves?

Teofilo. From avarice, envy, ambition, and ignorance. Commonly they barely
understand their own method of healing; so far do they fall short of being able
to have reason of another's. Besides, the greater part, not being able to rise
to honor and gain by their own virtues, studies to prefer itself by lowering the
others, showing that it despises what it cannot acquire. But of these, the best
and true physician is the one who is not so physical that he is not also
chemical and mathematical. Now, to come to the proposition, among the species of
philosophy, that one is best which most conveniently and highly effects the
perfection of the human intellect, is more correspondent to the truth of nature,
and, insofar as possible, makes us cooperators with nature, whether by divining,
I mean according to natural order and reason of vicissitude, not by animal
instinct as beasts do and those who are like them, not by inspiration of good or
evil demons as prophets do, not by melancholic enthusiasm as poets and other
contemplatives do; or by ordering laws and reforming customs; or by healing; or
else by knowing and living a more blessed and more divine life. Here, then, you
see how there is no sort of philosophy that has been ordered by regulated
sentiment which does not contain in itself some good property not contained by
the others. I understand the same of medicine, which derives from such
principles as presuppose a not imperfect habit of philosophy, as the operation
of foot or hand does that of the eye. Therefore it is said that one cannot have
a good beginning of medicine who does not have a good end of philosophy.

Dicsono Arelio. You please me very much, and I praise you very much, because
just as you are not as plebeian as Aristotle, neither are you as injurious and
ambitious as he, who wished the opinions of all other philosophers with their
modes of philosophizing to be altogether despised.

Teofilo. Although, of however many philosophers there are, I know none more
founded on imaginations and removed from nature than he; and if sometimes he
says excellent things, they are known not to depend on his principles, and
therefore they are always propositions taken from other philosophers, as we see
many divine ones in the book *On Generation*, in the *Meteorology*, *On
Animals*, and *Plants*.

Dicsono Arelio. Returning, then, to our proposition: do you wish that matter can
be defined differently without error and without incurring contradiction?

Teofilo. True, as of the same object there can be judgments of diverse senses,
and the same thing can insinuate itself differently. Besides, as has been
touched, consideration of one thing can be taken from diverse heads. The
Epicureans have said many good things, although they did not rise above
material quality. Heraclitus has given many excellent things to know, although
he did not climb above soul. Anaxagoras does not fail to make profit in nature,
because he wishes to know an intellect not only within nature, but perhaps
outside and above it, the same that by Socrates, Plato, Trismegistus, and our
theologians is called God. So no less can one who begins from the experimental
reason of simples, as they call them, promote well to uncovering the arcana of
nature than those who begin from rational theory. And among these, no less the
one who begins from complexions than the one who begins from humors, and this
one no more than the one who descends from the sensible elements, or, from
higher up, those absolute elements, or from one matter, the highest and most
distinct principle of all. For sometimes the one who makes a longer road will
not for that reason make so good a pilgrimage, especially if his end is not so
much contemplation as operation. As to the mode of philosophizing, then, it will
be no less convenient to explicate the forms as from an implicated thing than
to distinguish them as from a chaos, to distribute them as from an ideal
fountain, to drive them into act as from a possibility, to bring them back as
from a womb, or to dig them out into the light as from a blind and dark abyss;
because every foundation is good if it is approved by the building, every seed
is fitting if the trees and fruits are desirable.

Dicsono Arelio. Now, to come to our scope, please bring forward the distinct
doctrine of this principle.

Teofilo. Certainly, this principle that is called matter can be considered in
two modes: first, as a potency; second, as a subject. Insofar as it is taken in
the same signification as potency, there is no thing in which, in a certain mode
and according to its proper reason, it cannot be found; and the Pythagoreans,
Platonists, Stoics, and others have put it no less in the intelligible world
than in the sensible. And we, understanding it not exactly as they understood it
but with a higher and more explicated reason, reason in this mode about potency
or possibility. Potency is commonly distinguished into active, by which its
subject can operate, and passive, by which it either can be, or can receive, or
can have, or can be subject of an efficient in some way. Not reasoning at
present about active potency, I say that the potency that signifies in a
passive mode, although it is not always passive, can be considered either
relatively or absolutely. And so there is no thing of which being can be said
of which being-able-to-be is not said. And this answers so thoroughly to active
potency that the one is in no mode without the other; whence, if there has
always been potency of making, producing, creating, there has always been
potency of being made, produced, and created, because the one potency implies
the other; I mean, by being posited, it necessarily posits the other. This
potency, because it does not say weakness in that of which it is said, but
rather confirms virtue and efficacy, indeed in the end is found to be all one
and altogether the same thing with active potency, no philosopher or theologian
doubts to attribute to the first supernatural principle. For the absolute
possibility by which the things that are in act can be is not before actuality,
nor yet after it. Further, being-able-to-be is with being in act, and does not
precede it; because if what can be made itself, it would be before it was made.
Now contemplate the first and best principle, which is all that it can be, and
would not itself be all if it could not be all; in it, therefore, act and
potency are the same thing. It is not so in other things, which, although they
are what they can be, nevertheless could perhaps not be, and certainly could be
other or otherwise than what they are, because no other thing is all that it can
be. The human being is what he can be, but is not all that he can be. The stone
is not all that it can be, because it is not lime, is not vase, is not dust, is
not grass. That which is all that it can be is one, which in its being
comprehends every being. It is all that is and can be whatever other thing is
and can be. Every other thing is not so. Therefore potency is not equal to act,
because it is not absolute act but limited act; besides, potency is always
limited to one act, because it never has more than one specified and particular
being; and if it nevertheless looks to every form and act, this is through the
means of certain dispositions and with a certain succession of one being after
another. Every potency and act, then, which in the principle is as if
complicated, united, and one, in other things is explicated, dispersed, and
multiplied. The universe, which is the great simulacrum, the great image, and
the only-begotten nature, is also all that it can be, through the same species
and principal members and containment of all matter, to which nothing is added
and from which nothing is lacking, of the whole and unique form; but it is not
all that it can be through the same differences, modes, properties, and
individuals. Therefore it is nothing other than a shadow of the first act and
first potency, and for this reason in it potency and act are not absolutely the
same thing, because no part of it is all that it can be. Besides, in that
specific mode we have named, the universe is all that it can be according to an
explicated, dispersed, distinct mode. Its principle is unitively and
indifferently; because everything is everything and the same most simply,
without difference and distinction.

## Translation Pressure

This batch gives Bruno's most useful methodological rule so far: every
foundation is good if the building approves it, every seed is fitting if the
trees and fruits are desirable. Method is judged by philosophical fruit.

Matter as potency here rises beyond a merely passive substrate. At the absolute
limit, potency and act coincide in the first principle; in finite things they
are dispersed, successive, and limited.

`Complicato` and `esplicato` matter. I translate them as `complicated` and
`explicated`, preserving the folded/unfolded structure rather than smoothing
into merely implicit/explicit.
