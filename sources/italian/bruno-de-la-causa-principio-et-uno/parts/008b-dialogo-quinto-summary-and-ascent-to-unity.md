## Working Translation

Dicsono Arelio. So this world, this being, true, universe, infinite, immense,
in every one of its parts is whole, so that it is the same everywhere. Hence
whatever is in the universe, with regard to the universe, whatever it may be
with respect to the other particular bodies, is everywhere according to the mode
of its capacity; because it is above, below, within, right, left, and according
to all local differences, because in the whole infinite there are all these
differences and none of them. Every thing that we take in the universe, because
it has in itself that which is whole everywhere, comprehends in its own mode the
whole soul of the world, although not totally, as we have already said; this
soul is whole in any part of the universe. Therefore, as act is one and makes
one being wherever it is, so in the world one should not believe that there is
plurality of substance and of what truly is being.

Next I know that you have as manifest that each one of all these innumerable
worlds that we see in the universe is not in it so much as in a containing place
and as in an interval and space, as in a comprehender, conserver, mover, and
efficient, which is as wholly comprehended by each of these worlds as the whole
soul is by each part of the same. Therefore, although a particular world moves
toward and around another, as the earth toward the sun and around the sun,
nevertheless with respect to the universe nothing moves toward it or around it,
but in it.

Further, you wish that just as the soul, even according to the common saying, is
in the whole great mass to which it gives being, and together is indivisible,
and therefore is likewise wholly in the whole and wholly in any part, so the
essence of the universe is one in the infinite and in any thing taken as a
member of it, so that altogether the whole and every part of it comes to be one
according to substance. Hence Parmenides did not speak inconveniently in saying
one, infinite, immobile, whatever his intention may be, which is uncertain,
reported by a not very faithful reporter.

You say that all that is seen of difference in bodies as to formations,
complexions, figures, colors, and other properties and commonalities is nothing
other than a diverse face of the same substance; a labile, mobile, corruptible
face of one immobile, persevering, and eternal being, in which are all forms,
figures, and members, but indistinct and as if agglomerated, not otherwise than
in the seed, in which the arm is not distinguished from the hand, the torso from
the head, the nerve from the bone. This distinction and unagglomerating does not
come to produce another and new substance, but comes to put into act and
completion certain qualities, differences, accidents, and orders around that
substance. And what is said of the seed with regard to the members of animals
is likewise said of food with regard to being chyle, blood, phlegm, flesh, seed;
likewise of some other thing that precedes being food or another thing; likewise
of all things, climbing from the lowest grade of nature to its highest, climbing
from the physical universality known by philosophers to the height of the
archetypal one believed by theologians, if it pleases you, until one comes to an
original and universal same substance of the whole, which is called being,
foundation of all diverse species and forms; as in the smith's art there is one
substance of wood subject to all measures and figures that are not wood but of
wood, in wood, around wood. Therefore everything that makes diversity of genera,
species, differences, properties, everything that consists in generation,
corruption, alteration, and change, is not being, is not existence, but condition
and circumstance of being and existence, which is one, infinite, immobile,
subject, matter, life, soul, true and good.

You wish that, because being is indivisible and most simple, because it is
infinite and act whole in the whole and whole in every part, in the mode in
which we say part in the infinite, not part of the infinite, we cannot in any
way think that earth is part of being, the sun part of substance, since
substance is impartible. Rather, it is permitted to say substance of the part,
or better, substance in the part; just as it is not permitted to say part of the
soul is in the arm, part of the soul is in the head, but rather soul in the part
that is the head, substance of the part or in the part that is the arm. Because
being portion, part, member, whole, so much as, greater, lesser, as this, as
that, of this, of that, concordant, different, and of other reasons that do not
signify one absolute, therefore cannot be referred to substance, to the one, to
being, but by substance, in the one, and around being, as modes, reasons, and
forms. Thus, as it is commonly said that quantity, quality, relation, action,
passion, and other surrounding genera are around one substance, so in the one
supreme being, in which act is indifferent from potency, which can be all
absolutely and is all that it can be, it is complicatedly one, immense,
infinite, comprehending all being; and it is explicatedly in these sensible
bodies and in the distinct potency and act that we see in them. Therefore you
wish that what is generated and generates, whether equivocal or univocal agent,
as those who philosophize commonly say, and that from which generation is made,
are always of the same substance. For this reason the sentence of Heraclitus
will not sound badly in your ear, when he said that all things are one, which
through mutability has all things in itself; and because all forms are in it,
consequently all definitions belong to it; and therefore contradictory
enunciations are true. And what makes multitude in things is not being, is not
the thing, but what appears, what is represented to sense and is on the surface
of the thing.

Teofilo. So it is. Besides this, I want you to apprehend more heads of this
most important science and of this most solid foundation of the truths and
secrets of nature. First, then, I want you to note that there is one and the
same ladder by which nature descends to the production of things, and intellect
ascends to cognition of them; and that both the one and the other proceeds from
unity to unity, passing through the multitude of means. I leave aside that, with
their mode of philosophizing, the Peripatetics and many Platonists make purest
act proceed to the multitude of things as to the middle from one extreme, and
purest potency from the other, just as others wish, by a certain metaphor, that
darkness and light concur in the constitution of innumerable grades of forms,
effigies, figures, and colors. To those who consider two principles and two
princes, there come to aid others who are enemies and impatient of polyarchy,
and make those two concur in one, which is likewise abyss and darkness, clarity
and light, deep and impenetrable obscurity, supernal and inaccessible light.

Second, consider that the intellect, wishing to liberate and unbind itself from
the imagination to which it is joined, besides having recourse to mathematical
and imaginable figures so that either through them or through their similitude
it may comprehend the being and substance of things, also comes to refer the
multitude and diversity of species to one and the same root. Thus Pythagoras,
who put numbers as specific principles of things, understood unity as the
foundation and substance of all; Plato and others, who put species as consisting
in figures, understood the point as the same stock and root of all, as substance
and universal genus. And perhaps surfaces and figures are what Plato finally
understood by his Great, and the point and atom is what he understood by his
Small, twin specific principles of things; these then are reduced to one, as
every divisible to the indivisible. Those, then, who say that the substantial
principle is the one wish substances to be like numbers; the others, who
understand the substantial principle as point, wish the substances of things to
be like figures; and all agree in positing an individual principle. But the mode
of Pythagoras is better and purer than that of Plato, because unity is cause
and reason of individuality and punctuality, and is a principle more absolute
and more accommodable to universal being.

Gervasio. Why did Plato, who came afterward, not do similarly or better than
Pythagoras?

Teofilo. Because he wished rather, by speaking worse and in a less convenient
and appropriate mode, to be esteemed master than, by speaking better and more
rightly, to be reputed disciple. I mean that the end of his philosophy was more
his own glory than truth; seeing that I cannot doubt that he knew very well that
his mode was more appropriate to corporeal things and things considered
corporeally, and that the other was no less accommodated and appropriable to
these than to all the others that reason, imagination, intellect, and one and
the other nature could fabricate. Everyone will confess that it was not hidden
from Plato that unity and numbers necessarily examine and give reason of point
and figures, and are not examined and do not necessarily take reason from
figures and points, just as dimensioned and corporeal substance depends on the
incorporeal and individual; besides, this is absolute from that, because the
reason of numbers is found without that of measure, but that cannot be absolute
from this, because the reason of measures is not found without that of numbers.
Therefore the arithmetical similitude and proportion is more accommodated than
the geometrical for guiding us, by means of multitude, to the contemplation and
apprehension of that indivisible principle, which, by being the unique and
radical substance of all things, cannot possibly have a certain and determined
name, and such a diction that signifies positively rather than privatively; and
therefore it has been called by some point, by others unity, by others infinite,
and according to various reasons similar to these.

Add to what has been said that, when the intellect wishes to comprehend the
essence of a thing, it goes on simplifying as much as it can. I mean that from
composition and multitude it withdraws, rejecting corruptible accidents,
dimensions, signs, and figures toward what underlies these things. Thus we do
not understand long writing and prolix speech except by contraction to one
simple intention. In this the intellect openly shows how the substance of things
consists in unity, which it goes seeking either in truth or in similitude.
Believe that he would be the most consummate and perfect geometer who could
contract into one single intention all the intentions dispersed in the
principles of Euclid; the most perfect logician who contracted all intentions
to one. Hence comes the grade of intelligences: because the lower cannot
understand many things except with many species, similitudes, and forms; the
higher understand better with few; the highest with very few, perfectly. The
first intelligence in one idea most perfectly comprehends the whole; the divine
mind and absolute unity, without any species, is itself what understands and
what is understood. So then, ascending to perfect cognition, we go complicating
the multitude, just as, descending to the production of things, unity goes
explicating itself. The descent is from one being to infinite individuals and
innumerable species; the ascent is from these to that.

To conclude, then, this second consideration, I say that when we aspire and
strive toward the principle and substance of things, we make progress toward
indivisibility; and we never believe that we have arrived at the first being and
universal substance until we have arrived at that one individual in which all is
comprehended. Meanwhile we do not believe that we comprehend more of substance
and essence than we know how to comprehend of indivisibility. Hence the
Peripatetics and Platonists reduce infinite individuals to one individual reason
of many species; they comprehend innumerable species under determinate genera,
which Archytas first wished to be ten; determinate genera to one being, one
thing. But this thing and being is understood by these as a name and diction and
a logical intention, and finally a vanity. For, then treating physically, they
do not know one principle of reality and being of all that is, but only an
intention and name common to all that is said and comprehended. This certainly
happened through weakness of intellect.

Third, you must know that, since substance and being are distinct and absolute
from quantity, and consequently measure and number are not substance but around
substance, not being but a thing of being, it happens that necessarily we must
say that substance essentially is without number and without measure, and
therefore one and individual in all particular things, which have their
particularity from number, that is, from things that are around substance.
Hence whoever apprehends Polihimnio as Polihimnio does not apprehend particular
substance, but substance in the particular and in the differences that are
around it; by these differences this human being is put in number and multitude
under one species. Here, as certain human accidents make multiplication of
these called individuals of humanity, so certain animal accidents make
multiplication of these species of animality. Likewise certain vital accidents
make multiplication of this animated and living thing. Not otherwise certain
corporeal accidents make multiplication of corporeity. Similarly certain
accidents of subsistence make multiplication of substance. In such a manner
certain accidents of being make multiplication of entity, truth, unity, being,
true, one.

## Translation Pressure

This batch makes `complication` and `explication` the spine of Bruno's method:
knowledge ascends by complicating multitude into unity, while nature descends by
explicating unity into many forms.

Bruno prefers arithmetic to geometry because unity grounds point and figure more
deeply than point and figure can ground unity. This will matter for later Cusa
comparison.

`Part in the infinite, not part of the infinite` is a key phrase for preserving
the all-in-all doctrine without treating the infinite as a composed aggregate.
