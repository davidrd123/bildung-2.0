## Working Translation

Armesso. Now does it seem to you that it belongs to a philosopher to stand upon
revenge?

Filoteo. If those who trouble me were a Xanthippe, I would be a Socrates.

Armesso. Do you not know that longanimity and patience suit everyone, through
which they come to be like heroes and eminent gods, who, according to some,
avenge themselves late, and according to others, neither avenge themselves nor
grow angry?

Filoteo. You deceive yourself in thinking that I have been standing on revenge.

Armesso. On what, then?

Filoteo. I have been standing on correction, in the exercise of which we too are
like the gods. You know that poor Vulcan has been dispensed by Jove to work even
on feast days; and that cursed anvil never leaves off or tires of bearing the
blows of so many and such fierce hammers, where no sooner is one lifted than the
other is lowered, so that the just thunderbolts with which delinquents and
guilty ones are punished do not fail.

Armesso. There is a difference between you and Jove's smith and the husband of
the Cyprian goddess.

Filoteo. It is enough that perhaps I too am not unlike them in patience and
longanimity, which I exercised in that affair by not slackening the whole bridle
to indignation, nor touching anger with a stronger spur.

Armesso. It does not belong to everyone to be a corrector, especially of the
multitude.

Filoteo. Say also, especially when the multitude does not touch him.

Armesso. It is said that you should not be solicitous in another's homeland.

Filoteo. And I say two things: first, that one should not kill a foreign
physician because he attempts to make those cures that the natives do not make;
second, that for the true philosopher every land is homeland.

Armesso. But if they accept you neither as philosopher nor as physician nor as
countryman?

Filoteo. Not for that reason will I fail to be.

Armesso. Who bears witness to it for you?

Filoteo. The gods who placed me there, I who find myself there, and those who
have eyes and see me there.

Armesso. You have very few and little-known witnesses.

Filoteo. Very few and little-known are true physicians; almost all are true
sick people. I return to saying that some do not have liberty to make, and
others to permit, such treatments to be made to those who offer honorable
wares, whether they are foreigners or not.

Armesso. Few know these wares.

Filoteo. The gems are not less precious for that, and we ought with all our
force to defend them and make them defended, liberated, and vindicated from the
trampling of swinish feet with every possible rigor. And may the heavenly powers
be propitious to me, my Armesso, that I never made similar revenges for sordid
self-love or for vulgar care of a particular man, but for love of my so much
beloved mother philosophy and for zeal for her injured majesty. By her false
relatives and children, because there is no vile pedant, lazy dictionary, stupid
Faun, or ignorant horse who, either by showing himself loaded with books, by
letting his beard grow long, or by putting himself into grand posture in other
ways, does not wish to title himself of the family, she has been reduced to such
a state that among the common people saying a philosopher is worth as much as
saying a trickster, a useless fellow, a pedant, a mountebank, a stage-jumper, a
charlatan, good for serving as pastime in the house and as a scarecrow for birds
in the country.

Elitropio. To tell the truth, the family of philosophers is esteemed more vile
by the greater part of the world than the family of chaplains; because those,
taken from every sort of rabble, have not brought the priesthood into contempt
as much as these, named from every genus of beasts, have put philosophy into
vilification.

Filoteo. Let us praise antiquity, then, in its own genus, when philosophers
were such that from them men were promoted to be legislators, counselors, and
kings; such were counselors and kings that from this being they were raised to
be priests. In these times the greatest part of priests are such that they are
despised, and through them the divine laws are despised; almost all those we see
as philosophers are such that they are vilified, and through them the sciences
come to be vilified. Besides, among these the multitude of rogues, like nettles,
with contrary dreams, is accustomed also on its side to oppress rare virtue and
truth, which shows itself to the rare.

Armesso. I do not find a philosopher who grows so angry over despised
philosophy, nor, O Elitropio, do I see anyone so affected for his science as
this Teofilo. What would it be if all the other philosophers were of the same
condition, I mean so little patient?

Elitropio. These other philosophers have not found so much, do not have so much
to guard, do not have so much to defend. Easily they too can hold vile that
philosophy which is worth nothing, or another that is worth little, or one that
they do not know; but the one who has found truth, which is a hidden treasure,
kindled by the beauty of that divine face, becomes jealous that it not be
defrauded, neglected, and contaminated, no less than another sordid affection
can be jealous over gold, carbuncle, and diamond, or over a carcass of feminine
beauty.

Armesso. But let us return to ourselves, and come to the point. They say of
you, Teofilo, that in that *Supper* of yours you tax and injure a whole city, a
whole province, a whole kingdom.

Filoteo. I never thought this, never intended it, never did it; and if I had
thought, intended, or done it, I would condemn myself as the worst of men, and
would be prepared for a thousand retractions, a thousand revocations, a thousand
palinodes; not only if I had injured a noble and ancient kingdom such as this
one, but any other whatever, even if esteemed barbarous; I do not say only any
city whatever, even if defamed as uncivil, but also any lineage whatever, even
if reported as savage, and any family whatever, even if named inhospitable.
Because there can be no whole kingdom, city, stock, or house that can or should
be presumed to be of one same humor, and where there cannot be opposite or
contrary customs, so that what pleases one cannot displease another.

Armesso. Certainly, as for me, who have read and reread and well considered the
whole, although concerning particulars I do not know why I find you somewhat
too effusive, concerning the general matter I see you proceeding chastely,
reasonably, and discreetly; but the rumor has been spread in the way I tell you.

Elitropio. The rumor of this and other things has been spread by the vileness
of some of those who feel themselves touched; they, desirous of revenge, seeing
themselves insufficient in their own reason, doctrine, ingenuity, and force,
besides feigning as many other falsities as they can, to which none but those
like them can give faith, seek company by making the particular punishment be
esteemed a common injury.

Armesso. Rather I believe they are persons not without judgment and counsel, who
think the injury universal because you manifest such customs in persons of such
a generation.

Filoteo. Now what are these named customs, such that similar, worse, and many
more strange in genus, species, and number are not found in places of the most
excellent parts and provinces of the world? Will you perhaps call me injurious
and ungrateful to my homeland if I said that similar and more criminal customs
are found in Italy, in Naples, in Nola? Would I perhaps thereby come to degrade
that region, pleasing to heaven and once placed both as head and right hand of
this globe, governess and tamer of the other generations, and always esteemed
by us and others as teacher, nurse, and mother of all virtues, disciplines,
humanities, modesties, and courtesies, if one were to exaggerate further what
our own poets have sung of it, who make it no less mistress of all vices,
deceptions, avarices, and cruelties?

Elitropio. This is certain according to the principles of your philosophy,
through which you wish that contraries have coincidence in the principles and
proximate subjects, because those same minds that are most apt for high,
virtuous, and generous undertakings, if they are perverse, go precipitating into
extreme vices. Besides, rarer and more select minds are usually found where in
common there are more ignorant and foolish people, and where in the most general
way people are less civil and courteous, in the more particular way extreme
courtesies and urbanities are found; so that, in diverse manners, it seems to
many generations that the same measure of perfections and imperfections has
been given.

Filoteo. You speak the truth.

Armesso. With all this I, like many others with me, am grieved, Teofilo, that in
our loving homeland you have fallen among such supposits as have given you
occasion to lament with an ashen supper, when others, and many others, would
have made manifest to you how much this country of ours, although it is said by
yours to be *penitus toto divisus ab orbe*, is inclined to all studies of good
letters, arms, chivalry, humanities, and courtesies, in which, as much as the
sinew of our forces permits, we strive not to be inferior to our ancestors and
not to be conquered by other generations, especially by those that esteem
themselves to have nobility, sciences, arms, and civilities as if from nature.

Filoteo. By my faith, Armesso, in what you report I neither should nor could
contradict you with words, reasons, or conscience, because with every dexterity
of modesty and arguments you make your cause. Therefore through you, as through
one who has not approached with barbarous pride, I begin to repent and take
displeasure in having received matter from those aforementioned men for
saddening you and others of most honorable and humane complexion; therefore I
would wish that those dialogues had not been produced, and if it pleases you I
will strive that they come no further into the light.

Armesso. My sadness, together with that of other most noble men, is so far from
proceeding from the publication of those dialogues that I would easily procure
that they be translated into our language, so that they might serve as a lesson
to those poorly and badly accustomed people who are among us. Perhaps, when they
saw with what stomach they are taken and with what outlines their discourteous
encounters are described and how badly those signify, it could be that, if
through good discipline and good example that they see in the better and greater
people they do not wish to withdraw from that path, at least they may come to
change and conform themselves to them for shame of being counted among such and
such people, learning that the honor of persons and bravery do not consist in
being able and knowing how to be troublesome in those modes, but in the exact
contrary.

Elitropio. You show yourself very discreet and prudent in the cause of your
homeland, and you are not ungrateful and unrecognizing toward others' good
offices, as many poor in argument and counsel can be. But Filoteo does not seem
to me so advised in conserving his reputation and defending his person; because,
as nobility is different from rusticity, so contrary effects should be hoped and
feared in a rustic Scythian, who will turn out wise and through good success
will be celebrated if, departing from the banks of the Danube, he goes with bold
reproof and just complaint to test the authority and majesty of the Roman
Senate, which from his blame and invective knows how to take occasion to build
upon it an act of extreme prudence and magnanimity, honoring its rigid reprover
with statue and colossus; and in a gentleman and Roman senator, who through bad
success can turn out little wise if, leaving the pleasant banks of his Tiber, he
goes, even with just complaint and most reasonable reproof, to test the Scythian
rustics, who from that take occasion to build towers and Babylons of arguments
of greater vileness, infamy, and rusticity, by stoning him and slackening the
bridle to popular fury, in order to make the other generations know better how
great the difference is between dealing and finding oneself among human beings
and among those who are made in the image and likeness of them.

Armesso. It will never be true, O Teofilo, that I ought or could esteem it
worthy that I, or another who has more salt than I, should wish to take up the
cause and protection of those who are the matter of your satire as if they were
people and persons of the country, to whose defense we are incited by natural
law itself. For I will never confess, and will never be anything other than the
enemy of anyone who affirmed that these are part and members of our homeland,
which consists of nothing other than persons as noble, civil, well-mannered,
disciplined, discreet, humane, and reasonable as any other. Where, although
these are contained in it, certainly they are not found there otherwise than as
filth, dregs, dung, and carrion; in such a way that they could not be called
part of kingdom and city in any other mode than the bilge is part of the ship.
And therefore we are so far from needing to take offense on behalf of such
people that, by taking offense, we would become shameful. From these I do not
exclude a great part of doctors and priests, of whom, although some by means of
the doctorate become gentlemen, nevertheless for the most part that rustic
authority, which before they did not dare show, afterward through the boldness
and presumption added to them by the reputation of being lettered and priests,
they come audaciously and magnanimously to put into the field. Hence it is no
marvel if you see many and many who with that doctorate and priesthood know more
of herd, flock, and stable than those who are actually horse-groom, goatherd,
and plowman. For this reason I would not have wished that you had borne
yourself so sharply toward our University also, as if not pardoning the general
body, nor having respect for what it has been, will be, or could be in the
future, and in part is at present.

Filoteo. Do not distress yourself, because although it is presented to us by a
thread on this occasion, nevertheless it makes no error that all the others
esteemed greater do not make similarly, and for the most part under the title
of doctors they drive out ringed horses and diademed asses. I do not take from
it, however, how well it was instituted at the beginning: the beautiful orders
of studies, the gravity of ceremonies, the disposition of exercises, the decorum
of habits, and many other circumstances that make for the necessity and ornament
of an academy; whence, without any doubt, there is no one who should not confess
it first in all Europe and consequently in all the world. And I do not deny
that, as to the gentleness of spirits and acuteness of wits that both parts of
Britannia naturally produce, it is similar and can be equal to all those that
are truly most excellent. Nor has the memory been lost that before speculative
letters were found in the other parts of Europe, they flourished in this place;
and from those principles of metaphysics, although barbarous in language and
cowled in profession, the splendor of a most noble and rare part of philosophy,
which in our times is almost extinguished, was diffused to all the other
academies of the non-barbarous provinces. But what has troubled me and gives me
at once annoyance and laughter is that, while I find no one more Roman and more
Attic in language than in this place, for the rest, speaking of the more
general run, they boast of being altogether unlike and contrary to those who
were before. Those earlier men, little solicitous of eloquence and grammatical
rigor, were all intent upon speculations, which these people call sophisms. But
I esteem more the metaphysics of those men, in which they advanced beyond their
prince Aristotle, although impure and dirtied with certain vain conclusions and
theorems that are neither philosophical nor theological but belong to idle and
badly employed minds, than whatever these of the present age can bring with all
their Ciceronian eloquence and declamatory art.

Armesso. These are not things to despise.

Filoteo. It is true; but, if one of the two must be chosen, I esteem more the
cultivation of the mind, even if it were sordid, than any however most eloquent
words and tongues.

Elitropio. This proposition makes me remember Friar Ventura, who, treating a
passage of the holy Gospel that says *Reddite quae sunt Caesaris Caesari*,
brought in to the point all the names of the coins that existed in the times of
the Romans, with their marks and weights, which I do not know from what devil
of an annal or notebook he had gathered, and there were more than one hundred
and twenty of them, in order to make known how studious and retentive he was.
When the sermon was finished, a good man approached him and said, "My reverend
father, please lend me a carlino." To this he answered that he was of the
mendicant order.

Armesso. To what end do you say this?

Elitropio. I mean to say that those who are very versed concerning words and
names, and are not solicitous about things, ride the same mule as this reverend
father of mules.

Armesso. I believe that, besides the study of eloquence, in which they surpass
all their ancients and are not inferior to other moderns, they are not beggars
in philosophical and otherwise speculative professions either; without skill in
these they cannot be promoted to any degree, because the statutes of the
university, to which they are bound by oath, provide that *nullus ad
philosophiae et theologiae magisterium et doctoratum promoveatur, nisi
epotaverit e fonte Aristotelis*.

Elitropio. Oh, I will tell you what they have done in order not to be perjured.
Of three fountains that are in the University, to one they have given the name
*Fons Aristotelis*, the other they call *Fons Pythagorae*, the other they call
*Fons Platonis*. Since water is drawn from these three fountains to make beer
and ale, and even oxen and horses do not fail to drink of that water,
consequently there is no person who, having stayed fewer than three or four
days in those studies and colleges, does not come to be imbibed not only with
the fountain of Aristotle, but also with that of Pythagoras and Plato.

Armesso. Alas, you speak far too truly. Hence it happens, O Teofilo, that
doctors go cheaply like sardines, because as they are created, found, and fished
with little labor, so they are bought with little price. Now then, such being
among us the common run of doctors in this age, while preserving the reputation
of some celebrated men for eloquence, doctrine, and civil courtesy, such as
Tobia Mattheo, Culpeper, and others I do not know how to name, it happens that
so far is someone, by calling himself doctor, from being esteemed to have a new
degree of nobility, that rather he is suspected of the contrary nature and
condition unless he is particularly known. Hence it happens that those who by
lineage or by some other accident are noble, even when the principal part of
nobility, which is through doctrine, is added to them, are ashamed to take a
degree and be called doctors, it being enough for them to be learned. And of
these you will have a greater number in the courts than pedants can be found in
the University.

## Translation Pressure

This batch clarifies the first dialogue's actual work: Bruno reframes public
attack as correction, then turns the defense of philosophy into an attack on
pedantry and degree culture.

The important distinction is not simply philosophy versus anti-philosophy. Bruno
is separating speculative depth from verbal refinement: older barbarous
metaphysicians may be philosophically superior to modern Ciceronian stylists.

`Soggetto` and `supposito` keep appearing in social and metaphysical registers.
The glossary should keep both open rather than forcing them into one English
solution.
