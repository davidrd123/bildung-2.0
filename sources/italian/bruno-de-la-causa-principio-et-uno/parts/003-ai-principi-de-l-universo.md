## Working Translation

GIORDANO THE NOLAN

TO THE PRINCIPLES OF THE UNIVERSE

Holding back from the beginning the field that swells with Lethe,

let him depart, O Titan, and seek the stars, I pray.

Wandering stars, look on as I, doubled,

proceed into the circle, if you have opened this path.

Let the twin gates of sleep be allowed to open wide,

as your courses hasten me through the void;

what greedy time has long held covered,

may I be allowed to bring forth from thick shadows.

What obstructs you, sick mind, from hastening to your birth,

though these things must be given to an unworthy age?

While a wave of shadows submerges the lands,

lift your summit, our Olympus, into clear Jove.



TO HIS OWN SPIRIT

Mountain, although the earth holds you braced on deep roots,

you are able to stretch with your summit to the stars.

The kindred mind calls from the highest height of things,

where you are divided between the shades and Jove.

Do not lose your rights here, and, lying back on the bottom,

do not, driven downward, stain the waters of black Acheron.

Rather, let nature attempt the more sublime recesses,

for, when God touches, you will be a fervid fire.



TO TIME

Slow old man, and the same swift one, closing and releasing:

who would call you good, who evil?

You are lavish and tenacious: the gifts you extend, you take away;

you who were present as parent are yourself present as destroyer.

Things drawn from your entrails you hide back in your entrails,

you for whom it is permitted, ready in breast and throat, to seize.

Since you make all things and destroy all things, from this

could I not call you good, could I not call you evil?

But where you, raging, will be frustrated in your dreadful blow,

threatening with your scythe, spare to stretch out that hand,

where no pressed footsteps of black Chaos appear,

lest you seem good, lest you seem evil.



OF LOVE

Love, through whom I discern truth so high,

who opens the gates of diamond and blackness;

through the eyes my god enters, and through seeing

is born, lives, is nourished, and has eternal reign.

He makes one see all that heaven, earth, and hell contain,

makes present the true images of absent things,

regains strength and, drawing straight, strikes,

and always wounds the heart, uncovers every inward part.

O then, vile crowd, attend to truth,

give ear to my speech, which does not deceive;

open, open, if you can, your eyes, mad and crooked.

You believe him a child because you understand little;

because you change quickly, he seems fugitive;

because you are blind, you call him blind.

Cause, principle, and sempiternal one,

from which being, life, and motion depend,

and which extends in length, breadth, and depth

as much as is spoken in heaven, earth, and hell;

with sense, with reason, with mind I discern

that act, measure, and count do not comprehend

that vigor, mass, and number that stretches

beyond every lower, middle, and supernal thing.

Blind error, greedy time, wicked fortune,

deaf envy, vile rage, unjust zeal,

cruel heart, impious mind, strange daring

will not suffice to make the air dark for me,

will not set the veil before my eyes,

will never make me not behold my beautiful sun.

## Translation Pressure

The threshold poems are already working the whole vocabulary of the book:
principles, time, love, truth, darkness, vision, the one, act, measure, number,
and the extension of being through length, breadth, and depth.

The Latin verses need a second pass from someone or something better calibrated
to Neo-Latin verse. I have translated them literally enough to keep the images
and sequence active, but I would not treat this as final Latin work.

The Italian sonnets make vision central. `Per gli occhi entra il mio nume` is
not just love poetry; it prepares the epistemic and contemplative role of sight
that the dialogues will keep pressing.

The second sonnet confirms that `causa`, `principio`, and `uno` should stay
visible as a triad. It also introduces `atto`, `misura`, and `conto` as terms
that should be tracked with `potenza`, `materia`, and `forma`.
