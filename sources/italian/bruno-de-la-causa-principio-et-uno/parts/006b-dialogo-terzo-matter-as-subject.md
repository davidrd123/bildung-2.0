## Working Translation

Teofilo. Democritus, then, and the Epicureans, who say that what is not body is
nothing, consequently wish matter alone to be the substance of things; and also
that matter is the divine nature, as a certain Arab called Avicebron said, as he
shows in a book entitled *Fountain of Life*. These same men, together with the
Cyrenaics, Cynics, and Stoics, wish forms to be nothing other than certain
accidental dispositions of matter. And for a long time I was rather adherent to
this opinion, only because it has foundations more corresponding to nature than
those of Aristotle. But after having considered more maturely and having regard
to more things, we find that it is necessary to know in nature two genera of
substance: one which is form and the other which is matter. For it is necessary
that there be a most substantial act, in which there is the active potency of
all, and also a potency and a subject in which there is no lesser passive
potency of all: in that one there is power of making; in this one there is
power of being made.

Dicsono Arelio. It is manifest to everyone who measures well that it is not
possible that that which always can make all should be without one who always
can be made all. How can the soul of the world, I mean every form, which is
indivisible, be the figurator without the subject of dimensions or quantity,
which is matter? And how can matter be figured? Perhaps by itself? It appears
that we could say matter comes to be figured by itself if we wish to consider
the universe, the formed body, as matter, and call it matter; as we will call an
animal, with all its faculties, matter, distinguishing it not from form, but
only from the efficient.

Teofilo. No one can prevent you from using the name matter according to your
mode, just as in many sects it likewise has the reason of many significations.
But this mode of considering that you mention, I know, cannot suit anyone except
a mechanic or a physician who stays with practice, as one who divides the
universal body into mercury, salt, and sulfur. To say this does not so much show
the divine genius of a physician as it could show a most foolish man who wished
to call himself philosopher. The philosopher's end is not only to come to that
distinction of principles that is made physically by the separation that
proceeds from the virtue of fire, but also to that distinction of principles to
which no material efficient reaches. For the soul, inseparable from sulfur,
mercury, and salt, is the formal principle; this is not subject to material
qualities, but is altogether lord of matter, is not touched by the work of
chemists, whose division terminates at the three named things, and who know
another species of soul than this one of the world, which we must define.

Dicsono Arelio. You speak excellently, and this consideration contents me very
much, because I see some so little prudent that they do not distinguish the
causes of nature absolutely, according to the whole ambit of their being, which
are considered by philosophers, from those taken in a limited and appropriated
mode. The first mode is excessive and vain for physicians insofar as they are
physicians; the second is cut short and diminished for philosophers insofar as
they are philosophers.

Teofilo. You have touched that point in which Paracelsus is praised, because he
treated medicinal philosophy, and Galen is blamed insofar as he brought in
philosophical medicine, making a tedious mixture and a web so tangled that in
the end it renders one a somewhat exquisite physician and a very confused
philosopher. But let this be said with some respect, because I have not had
leisure to examine all the parts of that man.

Gervasio. Please, Teofilo, first do me this favor, since I am not so practiced
in philosophy: declare to me what you understand by this name matter, and what
that is which is matter in natural things.

Teofilo. All those who wish to distinguish matter and consider it by itself,
without form, have recourse to the similitude of art. So do the Pythagoreans,
so the Platonists, so the Peripatetics. You see one species of art, such as that
of the carpenter, which for all its forms and all its works has wood for
subject; as the smith has iron, the tailor cloth. All these arts in their proper
matter make diverse images, orders, and figures, none of which is proper and
natural to that matter. So nature, to which art is similar, must have a matter
for its operations, because it is not possible that there be any agent that, if
it wishes to make something, does not have that from which to make it; or if it
wishes to operate, does not have that on which to operate. There is, then, a
species of subject, of which, with which, and in which nature effects its
operation, its work; and this subject is formed by nature with so many forms
that they present to the eyes of consideration so much variety of species. And
just as wood by itself has no artificial form, but can have all by the operation
of the carpenter, so the matter of which we speak by itself and in its nature
has no natural form, but can have all by operation of the active agent principle
of nature. This natural matter is not as sensible as artificial matter, because
the matter of nature has absolutely no form; but the matter of art is already a
thing formed by nature, since art can operate only on the surface of things
formed by nature, such as wood, iron, stone, wool, and similar things. But
nature operates from the center, so to speak, of its subject or matter, which is
altogether without form. Therefore the subjects of the arts are many, and the
subject of nature is one; because those, being differently formed by nature, are
different and various; this one, because it is in no way formed, is altogether
indifferent, seeing that every difference and diversity proceeds from form.

Gervasio. So that things formed by nature are matter for art, and one formless
thing alone is matter for nature?

Teofilo. So it is.

Gervasio. Is it possible that, just as we clearly see and know the subjects of
the arts, we can likewise know the subject of nature?

Teofilo. Quite well, but by diverse principles of cognition; because just as we
do not know colors and sounds with the same sense, so we do not see the subject
of the arts and the subject of nature with the same eye.

Gervasio. You mean that with the sensitive eyes we see that, and with the eye
of reason this.

Teofilo. Good.

Gervasio. Now please form this reason.

Teofilo. Willingly. The relation and regard that the form of art has to its
matter is the same, according to due proportion, as the form of nature has to
its matter. Just as in art, while forms are varied infinitely, if that were
possible, there is always one same matter that perseveres under them, as for
example the form of the tree is a form of trunk, then of beam, then of plank,
then of bench, then of footstool, then of box, then of comb, and so it goes
discoursing, while nevertheless the being wood always perseveres; not otherwise
in nature, while forms are varied infinitely and one succeeds another, there is
always one same matter.

Gervasio. How can this similitude be soldered?

Teofilo. Do you not see that what was seed becomes grass, and from what was
grass becomes ear, from what was ear becomes bread, from bread chyle, from chyle
blood, from this seed, from this embryo, from this man, from this cadaver, from
this earth, from this stone or another thing, and so further, to come to all
natural forms?

Gervasio. I easily see it.

Teofilo. It must therefore be one same thing that of itself is not stone, not
earth, not cadaver, not human being, not embryo, not blood or anything else; but
after it was blood, it becomes embryo, receiving the being embryo; after it was
embryo, it receives the being human being, making itself human; just as that
thing formed by nature, which is subject of art, from what was tree is plank and
receives being plank; from what was plank, receives the being door, and is door.

Gervasio. Now I have understood it very well; but this subject of nature seems
to me unable to be body or of a certain quality, because this which goes
slipping now under one natural form and being, now under another form and being,
does not show itself corporeally, as wood or stone do, which always make
themselves seen for what they are materially or subjectively, let them be placed
under whatever form one wishes.

Teofilo. You speak well.

Gervasio. Now what shall I do when it happens to me to confer this thought with
some pertinacious person who does not wish to believe that there is thus one
single matter under all the formations of nature, just as there is one under all
the formations of each art? For that which is seen with the eyes cannot be
denied; that which is seen by reason alone can be denied.

Teofilo. Send him away, or do not answer him.

Gervasio. But if he will be importunate in demanding evidence from me, and will
be some person of respect whom one cannot send away sooner than he can send me
away, and who holds it as an injury that I do not answer him?

Teofilo. What will you do if a blind demigod, worthy of whatever honor and
respect, is insolent, importunate, and pertinacious in wishing to have cognition
and demanding evidence of colors, indeed, of the exterior figures of natural
things, as if to say: what is the form of the tree? What is the form of
mountains? Of a star? Further, what is the form of the statue, of the garment?
And so of other artificial things, which are so manifest to those who see?

Gervasio. I would answer him that if he had eyes, he would not ask evidence of
them, but could see them by himself; but, being blind, it is also impossible for
another to demonstrate them to him.

Teofilo. Similarly you will be able to say to these people that, if they had
intellect, they would not ask any other evidence of it, but could see it by
themselves.

Gervasio. They would be ashamed of this answer, and others would judge it too
Cynic.

Teofilo. Then you will say to them more covertly thus: "My most illustrious
lord," or, "Sacred Majesty, as some things cannot be evident except by the hands
and by touching, others except by hearing, others except by taste, others except
by the eyes, so this matter of natural things cannot be evident except by the
intellect."

Gervasio. Perhaps that one, understanding the stroke because it is not so dark
nor covered, will say to me: "You are the one who has no intellect; I have more
of it than however many of your peers are found."

Teofilo. You will not believe him any more than if a blind man told you that
you are blind and that he sees more than however many think they see as you
think you do.

Dicsono Arelio. Enough has been said to demonstrate more evidently than I have
ever heard what the name matter signifies and what should be understood as
matter in natural things. So the Pythagorean Timaeus, from the transmutation of
one element into another, teaches us to find the matter that is hidden and
cannot be known except by a certain analogy. "Where the form of earth was," he
says, "afterward appears the form of water," and here one cannot say that one
form receives the other, because one contrary does not accept or receive the
other, that is, dry does not receive humid or dryness does not receive humidity,
but from a third thing dryness is driven out and humidity introduced, and that
third thing is subject of both contraries and is contrary to neither. Therefore,
if one is not to think that earth has gone into nothing, one must judge that
something which was in the earth has remained and is in the water; which thing,
by the same reason, when water is transmuted into air, because the virtue of
heat comes to attenuate it into smoke or vapor, will remain and will be in the
air.

Teofilo. From this one can conclude, even despite them, that no thing is
annihilated and loses being except the exterior and material accidental form.
Therefore both matter and the substantial form of whatever natural thing, which
is soul, are indissoluble and unannihilable, losing being altogether and
throughout. Certainly all the substantial forms of the Peripatetics and others
like them cannot be such, since they consist in nothing other than a certain
complexion and order of accidents; and everything they know how to name besides
their prime matter is nothing other than accident, complexion, habit of quality,
principle of definition, quiddity. Hence certain subtle cowled metaphysicians
among them, wishing rather to excuse than accuse the insufficiency of their god
Aristotle, have found humanity, bovinity, olivity, as specific substantial
forms; this humanity, as Socrateity, this bovinity, this horseness to be the
numeral substance. They have done all this in order to give us a substantial
form that merits the name of substance, as matter has the name and being of
substance. But for all that they have never profited at all; because, if you
ask them in order, "In what does the substantial being of Socrates consist?"
they will answer, "In Socrateity." If further you ask, "What do you understand
by Socrateity?" they will answer, "The proper substantial form and proper matter
of Socrates." Now leave aside this substance that is matter, and tell me: "What
is substance as form?" Some answer: "His soul." Ask: "What is this soul?" If
they say an entelechy and perfection of a body that can live, consider that this
is an accident. If they say that it is a principle of life, sense, vegetation,
and intellect, consider that although that principle is some substance
fundamentally considered, as we consider it, nevertheless this person puts it
forward only as accident; because being principle of this or that does not say a
substantial and absolute reason, but an accidental reason relative to what is
principiated, just as what pronounces what I do or can do does not say my being
and substance, but rather what says what I am, as I and absolutely considered.
See then how they treat this substantial form that is soul, which, if by chance
it has been known by them as substance, nevertheless they have never named or
considered it as substance. You can see this confusion much more evidently if
you ask these people in what the substantial form of an inanimate thing
consists, as the substantial form of wood. Those who are more subtle will feign:
in woodness. Now take away that matter, which is common to iron, wood, and
stone, and say: "What substantial form of iron remains?" Never will they tell
you anything other than accidents. And these are among the principles of
individuation and give particularity, because matter is not contractible to
particularity except by some form; and this form, because it is constitutive
principle of a substance, they wish to be substantial, but then they cannot show
it physically except as accidental. And in the end, when they have done
everything they can, they have a substantial form, yes, but not natural, rather
logical; and so, in the end, some logical intention comes to be posited as
principle of natural things.

Dicsono Arelio. Did Aristotle not notice this?

Teofilo. I believe he noticed it most certainly, but could not remedy it.
Therefore he said that the ultimate differences are unnameable and unknown.

Dicsono Arelio. So it seems to me that he openly confesses his ignorance; and
therefore I too would judge it better to embrace those principles of philosophy
that, in this important question, do not allege ignorance, as do Pythagoras,
Empedocles, and your Nolan, whose opinions you touched yesterday.

Teofilo. This is what the Nolan wishes: that there is an intellect that gives
being to every thing, called by the Pythagoreans and the Timaeus giver of
forms; a soul and formal principle that makes itself and informs every thing,
called by the same the fountain of forms; and a matter from which every thing
comes to be made and formed, called by all the receptacle of forms.

Dicsono Arelio. This doctrine pleases me very much, because it seems to lack
nothing. And truly it is necessary that, as we can posit a constant and eternal
material principle, we likewise posit a formal principle. We see that all
natural forms cease from matter and newly come into matter, whence really no
thing seems to be constant, firm, eternal, and worthy of having the estimation
of principle except matter. Besides, forms do not have being without matter; in
matter they are generated and corrupted, from its womb they come out and into it
they are gathered. Therefore matter, which always remains the same and fecund,
must have the principal prerogative of being known as the only substantial
principle, that which is and always remains; and all forms together should not
be understood except as diverse dispositions of matter, which go and come, some
cease and are renewed, whence all do not have the reputation of principle.
Therefore some have been found who, having well considered the reason of
natural forms as it could be had from Aristotle and others like him, have
concluded in the end that these are nothing but accidents and circumstances of
matter; and therefore the prerogative of act and perfection must be referred to
matter, and not to things of which we can truly say that they are not substance
or nature, but things of substance and nature, which they say is matter. Among
them matter is a necessary, eternal, and divine principle, as to that Moor
Avicebron, who calls it God who is in all things.

Teofilo. They have been brought to this error by not knowing any form other
than accidental form. And this Moor, although from the Peripatetic doctrine in
which he was nourished he had accepted substantial form, nevertheless, by
considering it as a corruptible thing, not only mutable around matter, and as
that which is brought forth and does not bring forth, founded and does not
found, is rejected and does not reject, he despised it and held it vile in
comparison with stable, eternal, progenitrix, mother matter. And certainly this
happens to those who do not know what we know.

Dicsono Arelio. This has been very well considered; but it is time that from
digression we return to our proposition. We now know how to distinguish matter
from form, both from accidental form, however one wishes it to be, and from
substantial form. What remains to see is its nature and reality. But first I
would like to know whether, because of the great union that this soul of the
world and universal form has with matter, that other mode and manner of
philosophizing could be tolerated, that of those who do not separate act from
the reason of matter, and understand it as a divine thing, and not pure and
formless in such a way that it does not itself form and clothe itself.

Teofilo. Not easily, because nothing absolutely operates in itself, and there is
always some distinction between that which is agent and that which is made, or
around which there is action and operation. Hence it is good in the body of
nature to distinguish matter from soul, and in this soul to distinguish that
reason of the species. Hence we say there are three things in this body: first,
the universal intellect, instilled in things; second, the vivifying soul of the
whole; third, the subject. But not for this will we deny that he is a philosopher
who, in the genus of his philosophizing, takes this formed body or, as we wish
to say, this rational animal, and begins to take as first principles in some
mode the members of this body, as air, earth, fire; or etherial region and
star; or spirit and body; or else void and full, understanding the void,
however, not as Aristotle took it; or else in another fitting mode. That
philosophy will not seem to me worthy of being rejected, especially when, upon
whatever foundation it presupposes, or form of building it proposes, it comes to
effect the perfection of speculative science and cognition of natural things, as
in truth has been done by many more ancient philosophers. For it is the mark of
an ambitious, presumptuous, vain, and envious brain to wish to persuade others
that there is not more than one single way of investigating and coming to
cognition of nature; and it is the mark of a madman and a man without discourse
to give himself to understand this. Although, then, the more constant and firm,
more contemplative and distinct way, and the higher mode of considering, must
always be preferred, honored, and pursued more, nevertheless that other mode
which is not without good fruit is not to be blamed, although it is not the same
tree.

## Translation Pressure

The matter argument works through analogy but insists on a different organ of
knowledge. Art's matter is visible because it is already nature-formed; nature's
matter is seen by the eye of reason.

Bruno's critique of Peripatetic substantial form is sharp: if the supposed form
can only be named as humanity, woodness, quiddity, or principle of definition,
it is a logical intention, not a natural principle.

The passage gives a mature version of Bruno's pluralism. Different philosophical
foundations may be tolerated when they reach speculative fruit, but the higher
and more distinct way remains preferable.
