## Working Translation

DIALOGUE FOUR


POLIHIMNIO.

And the mouth of the vulva never says, enough. That is, namely, to wit, as much
as to say: matter, which is signified by these things, is never filled by
receiving forms. Now, since there is no one else in this Lyceum, or rather
Anti-Lyceum, alone, thus I say, alone, as least of all alone, I will walk about
and speak with myself. The matter of the Peripatetics, then, by the prince and
by the high-grade genius of the great Macedonian's moderator, no less than by
divine Plato and others, now chaos, now hyle, now sylva, now mass, now potency,
now aptitude, now mixed with privation, now cause of sin, now ordered to evil,
now non-being in itself, now unknowable in itself, now knowable by analogy to
form, now blank tablet, now unpainted thing, now subject, now substrate, now
underlay, now field, now infinite, now indeterminate, now almost nothing, now
neither what, nor what kind, nor how much; at last, after they have aimed much
with various and diverse nomenclatures, to define this nature, as they hit the
target itself, it is called female. At last, I say, to embrace all words in one,
by those who better weigh the thing itself, it is called female. And by Hercules,
not without no mediocre cause has it pleased these senators of the Palladian
kingdom to place these two things, matter and female, in the same equilibrium,
since by experience made of their rigor they have been led to that rage and that
frenzy. Now here comes to me by thread a rhetorical color. These are a chaos of
irrationality, hyle of wickednesses, forest of villainies, mass of filth,
aptitude for every perdition. Another rhetorical color, called by some a
complexion! Where was in potency, not only remote but also proximate, the
destruction of Troy? In a woman. Who was the instrument of the destruction of
Samsonic strength, of that hero I mean who with that jawbone of an ass he found
became unconquered triumphator over the Philistines? A woman. Who at Capua
tamed the impetus and force of the great captain and perpetual enemy of the
Roman republic, Hannibal? A woman! Exclamation! Tell me, O cithara-playing
prophet, the cause of your fragility: "Because in sins my mother conceived me."
How, O our ancient protoplast, when you were a paradisal gardener and cultivator
of the tree of life, were you so bewitched that you thrust yourself and the
whole human germ back into the deep abyss of perdition? "The woman whom you gave
me: she, she deceived me." Without doubt, form does not sin, and from no form
does error proceed except by being joined to matter. So form, signified by the
male, being placed in familiarity with matter and having come into composition
or copulation with it, answers natura naturans with these words, or else with
this sentence: "The woman whom you gave me," that is, the matter you gave me as
consort, "she deceived me"; this is, she is cause of every sin of mine.
Contemplate, contemplate, divine genius, how the excellent philosophers and
discreet anatomists of the entrails of nature, in order to put fully before our
eyes the nature of matter, have found no more accommodated mode than to warn us
with this proportion, which signifies that the state of natural things through
matter is like the domestic, political, and civil state through the female sex.
Open, open your eyes, etc. Oh, I see that colossus of laziness, Gervasio, who
interrupts the thread of my nervous oration. I fear I have been heard by him;
but what does it matter?

Gervasio. Hail, best master of doctors!

Polihimnio. If, in your manner, you do not wish to delude me too, hail!

Gervasio. I would like to know what you were ruminating alone.

Polihimnio. Studying in my little museum, I fell upon that place in Aristotle,
at the foot of the first book of the *Physics*, where, wishing to elucidate
what first matter was, he takes the female sex for a mirror. That sex, I say,
backward, fragile, inconstant, soft, pusillanimous, infamous, ignoble, vile,
abject, neglected, unworthy, reprobate, sinister, shameful, cold, deformed,
empty, vain, indiscreet, insane, perfidious, slothful, stinking, filthy,
ungrateful, truncated, mutilated, imperfect, inchoate, insufficient, cut off,
amputated, attenuated, rust, caterpillar, tares, plague, sickness, death,

placed among us by nature for God

as a burden and as a heavy penalty.


Gervasio. I know that you say this more to exercise yourself in the oratorical
art and show how copious and eloquent you are than because you have such a
sentiment as you show by the words. For it is ordinary with you, lord humanists,
who call yourselves professors of good letters, when you find yourselves full
of those concepts you cannot retain, that you go to unload them nowhere else
than upon poor women, just as, when some other choler presses you, you come to
vent it upon the first delinquent among your schoolboys. But beware, lord
Orpheuses, of the furious indignation of the Thracian women.

Polihimnio. I am Polihimnio, I am not Orpheus.

Gervasio. Therefore you do not blame women in earnest?

Polihimnio. By no means, by no means indeed. I speak in earnest, and I
understand no otherwise than as I say, because I do not, in the manner of
sophists, profess to show that white is black.

Gervasio. Why then do you dye your beard?

Polihimnio. But I speak frankly; and I say that a man without woman is similar
to one of the intelligences; he is, I say, a hero, a demigod, who has not taken
a wife.

Gervasio. And he is similar to an oyster and a mushroom too, and is a truffle.

Polihimnio. Hence the lyric poet divinely said:

Believe, Pisos, nothing is better than the celibate life.


And if you wish to know the cause, hear Secundus the philosopher: "The female,"
he says, "is an impediment to quiet, a continual damage, a daily war, a prison
of life, a tempest of the house, the shipwreck of man." This was well confirmed
by that Biscayan who, made impatient and put in choler by a horrible fortune and
fury of the sea, with a grim and choleric face turned to the waves and said:
"O sea, sea, if only I could marry you!" wishing to infer that the female is the
tempest of tempests. Therefore Protagoras, asked why he had given his daughter
to an enemy, answered that he could do no worse to him than give him a wife.
Further, a good Frenchman will not make me lie: when Cicala, master of the ship,
commanded him, as all the others who were suffering a most dangerous storm at
sea, to throw the heaviest things into the sea, he first threw his wife into it.

Gervasio. You do not report, by contrast, so many other examples of those who
have esteemed themselves most fortunate because of their women. Among these, so
as not to send you too far, behold under this same roof the signor of
Mauvissiere, who has fallen upon one not only endowed with no mediocre bodily
beauty that veils and mantles her soul, but further, with the triumvirate of
very discreet judgment, prudent modesty, and most honorable courtesy, keeps the
mind of her consort bound with an indissoluble knot, and is powerful to captivate
whoever knows her. What will you say of the generous daughter, who has barely
seen the sun for a lustrum and a year, and by languages you will not be able to
judge whether she is from Italy or France or England; by her hand around musical
instruments you will not be able to grasp whether she is corporeal or
incorporeal substance; by the mature goodness of her customs you will doubt
whether she has descended from heaven or else has sprung from earth? Everyone
sees that in her, no less for the formation of so beautiful a body has the blood
of both parents concurred than, for the fabric of her singular spirit, the
virtues of the heroic mind of those same parents.

Polihimnio. A rare bird, like Maria of Boshtel; a rare bird, like Maria of
Castelnovo.

Gervasio. That rarity you speak of in women can likewise be said of men.

Polihimnio. In the end, to return to the proposition, woman is nothing other
than matter. If you do not know what woman is, because you do not know what
matter is, study the Peripatetics somewhat; by teaching you what matter is, they
will teach you what woman is.

Gervasio. I see well that, by having a Peripatetic brain, you apprehended little
or nothing of what Teofilo said yesterday concerning the essence and potency of
matter.

Polihimnio. Of the other thing, let it be as one wishes; I stand on the point
of blaming the appetite of the one and the other, which is cause of every evil,
passion, defect, ruin, corruption. Do you not believe that, if matter were
content with the present form, no alteration or passion would have dominion over
us, we would not die, we would be incorruptible and eternal?

Gervasio. And if it had been content with that form it had fifty years ago,
what would you say? Would you be Polihimnio? If it had stood firm under that of
forty years past, would you be so adulterous, I mean so adult, so perfect, and
so learned? As it pleases you, then, that the other forms have yielded to this
one, so it is in the will of nature, which orders the universe, that all forms
yield to all. I leave aside that it is a greater dignity of this substance of
ours to make itself every thing by receiving all forms than to be partial by
retaining one alone. Thus, according to its possible, it has the similitude of
the one who is all in all.

Polihimnio. You begin to turn out learned, going outside your ordinary natural.
Apply it now, if you can, to similars, bringing forward the dignity that is
found in the female.

Gervasio. I will do it most easily. Oh, here is Teofilo.

Polihimnio. And Dicsono. Another time, then. So much for these matters.

## Translation Pressure

This opening is deliberately ugly. Polihimnio's matter/female analogy is a
pedantic misuse of Aristotle; Gervasio answers both rhetorically and
philosophically.

Gervasio's key reversal is that matter's not remaining under one form is not a
defect but its dignity: it can become all things rather than being partial.

This batch links the social/rhetorical female defense from `Dialogo primo` to
the metaphysics of matter in `Dialogo quarto`.
