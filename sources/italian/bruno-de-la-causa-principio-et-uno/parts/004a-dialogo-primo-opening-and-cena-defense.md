## Working Translation

GIORDANO BRUNO THE NOLAN

On Cause, Principle, and One.




DIALOGUE ONE


INTERLOCUTORS

Elitropio, Filoteo, Armesso.

Elitropio. Like guilty men accustomed to darkness, who, freed from the bottom
of some dark tower, come out into the light, many of those exercised in vulgar
philosophy and others will fear, marvel, and, being unable to endure the new
sun of your clear concepts, will be troubled.

Filoteo. The defect is not in the light, but in the eyes: the more beautiful and
excellent the sun will be in itself, so much the more hateful and displeasing it
will be to the eyes of nocturnal owls.

Elitropio. The undertaking you have taken up, O Filoteo, is difficult, rare, and
singular, while you wish to draw us out from the blind abyss and bring us to the
uncovered, tranquil, and serene aspect of the stars, which we see disseminated
with such beautiful variety through the cerulean mantle of heaven. Although the
helping hand of your pious zeal aids human beings alone, nevertheless the
effects of the ungrateful toward you will not be less various than the animals
that the benign earth generates and nourishes in her maternal and capacious
womb, if it is true that the human species, especially in its individuals, shows
the variety of all the others, because in each one the whole is more expressly
present than in those of other species. Thus these will be seen who, like a dim
mole, no sooner feel the open air than, digging the earth again, they will
attempt to return to their native dark recesses; those, like nocturnal birds, no
sooner have seen the crimson ambassador of the sun rise from the luminous east
than, by the weakness of their eyes, they will be invited back to their gloomy
retreat. All the animate beings banished from the aspect of the celestial lamps
and destined for the eternal cages, pits, and caverns of Pluto, recalled by
Alecto's frightful and Erinyan horn, will open their wings and direct their
swift course to their dwellings. But the animate beings born to see the sun,
having arrived at the end of the hateful night, thanking the benignity of
heaven, and disposing themselves to receive in the center of the globular
crystal of their eyes the rays so long desired and awaited, will with unusual
applause of heart, voice, and hand adore the east; from whose golden balcony,
when fair Titan has driven forth his fiery horses and broken the sleepy silence
of damp night, human beings will reason, the easy, unarmed, and simple woolly
flocks will bleat, the horned herds under the care of rough plowmen will low.
The horses of Silenus, so that again, in favor of the wandering gods, they may
give fright to the most stupid of their giants, will bray; the tusked swine,
wallowing in their muddy bed, will deafen us with ill-timed grunting. Tigers,
bears, lions, wolves, and deceitful foxes, thrusting their heads from their
caves and contemplating from deserted heights the level field of the hunt, will
send from their wild breasts their gruntings, snarls, noises, growls, roars, and
howls. In the air and on the branches of ramified trees, roosters, eagles,
peacocks, cranes, doves, blackbirds, sparrows, nightingales, crows, magpies,
ravens, cuckoos, and cicadas will not be negligent in repeating and redoubling
their noisy cries. From the liquid and unstable field also, white swans,
many-colored ducks, busy mergansers, marsh birds, hoarse geese, and querulous
frogs will touch our ears with their noise, so that the warm light of this sun,
diffused through the air of this more fortunate hemisphere, will be accompanied,
greeted, and perhaps troubled by as many and such diversities of voices as there
are spirits that drive them out from the depths of their own breasts.

Filoteo. It is not only ordinary, but also natural and necessary, that every
animal make its own voice; and it is not possible for beasts to form regulated
accents and articulate sounds like human beings, since complexions are contrary,
tastes diverse, and nourishments various.

Armesso. Please grant me liberty to say my part too; not about the light, but
about certain circumstances by which the sense is not so much accustomed to be
consoled as the feeling of one who sees and considers is troubled. For the sake
of your peace and quiet, which I desire for you with fraternal charity, I would
not want comedies, tragedies, laments, dialogues, or whatever we wish to call
them, to be formed from these discourses of yours, like those that a short time
ago, because they had gone out into the field for a stroll, forced you to stay
shut up and withdrawn at home.

Filoteo. Speak freely.

Armesso. I will not speak as a holy prophet, as an abstracted divine, as an
apocalyptic assumption, nor like Balaam's angelicized ass. I will not reason as
one inspired by Bacchus, nor swollen with wind by the whore Muses of Parnassus,
or as a Sibyl impregnated by Phoebus, or as a fatidic Cassandra, nor as one
filled from the toenails to the top of the hair with Apollonian enthusiasm, nor
as a seer illuminated in the oracle or Delphic tripod, nor as an Oedipus refined
against the knots of the Sphinx, nor as a Solomon before the enigmas of the
queen of Sheba, nor as Calchas, interpreter of the Olympian senate; nor as an
inspirited Merlin, or as one who has come out of the cave of Trophonius. But I
will speak in the ordinary and vulgar way, as a man who has had other thoughts
than to go distilling the juice of the greater and lesser nape, so that at last
the hard and pia mater remain dry; as a man, I say, who has no brain other than
my own. Not even the gods of the last serving and of the pantry in the heavenly
court, those, I mean, who do not drink ambrosia or taste nectar, but quench
their thirst there with the dregs of the casks and spilled wines, unless they
wish to make account of lymphs and nymphs, those, I say, who are usually more
domestic, familiar, and conversable with us, that is, neither the god Bacchus,
nor that drunken rider of the ass, nor Pan, nor Vertumnus, nor Faunus, nor
Priapus, deign to drive one straw more and in excess into me, although they are
accustomed to give plenty of their favors even to horses.

Elitropio. Too long a proem.

Armesso. Patience, for the conclusion will be brief. I mean, briefly, that I
will make you hear words that do not need to be deciphered as if placed in
distillation, passed through an alembic, digested in a bain-marie, and sublimed
in a recipe of fifth essence, but just such as my nurse stuffed into my head.
She was almost as thick-skinned, broad-chested, big-bellied, broad-hipped, and
large-buttocked as that London woman I saw at Westminster, who, as a stomach
warmer, has a pair of great teats that look like the boots of the giant Saint
Sparagorio, and which, dressed in leather, would surely be worth making into two
Ferrarese bagpipes.

Elitropio. And this could be enough for a proem.

Armesso. Now then, to come to the rest, I would like to understand from you,
leaving aside for a little while the voices and tongues concerning the light and
splendor that your philosophy can bring, with what voices you wish that luster
of doctrine that comes out of the book of *The Ash Wednesday Supper* to be
greeted particularly by us. What animals are those that have recited *The Ash
Wednesday Supper*? I ask whether they are aquatic, aerial, terrestrial, or
lunatic. And leaving aside the sayings of Smith, Prudenzio, and Frulla, I wish
to know whether those err who say that you make the voice of a rabid and
infuriated dog; besides that sometimes you play the ape, sometimes the wolf,
sometimes the magpie, sometimes the parrot, sometimes one animal and sometimes
another, mixing grave and serious propositions, moral and natural, ignoble and
noble, philosophical and comic.

Filoteo. Do not marvel, brother, because this was nothing other than a supper,
where brains are governed by the affections that are offered to them by the
efficacy of the flavors and fumes of drinks and foods. As the material and
corporeal supper can be, so consequently the verbal and spiritual one follows;
therefore this dialogical supper has its varied and diverse parts, just as that
other one is accustomed to have its own varied and diverse parts; this one has
its own conditions, circumstances, and means no differently than that one could
have its own.

Armesso. Please, make me understand you.

Filoteo. There, as is ordinary and proper, there are usually things for salad,
for the meal, fruits, ordinary fare, kitchen and spice shop, things for the
healthy and for the sick, cold and hot, raw and cooked, aquatic and terrestrial,
domestic and wild, roasted and boiled, ripe and unripe, and things for
nourishment alone and for taste, substantial and light, salty and insipid, sour
and sweet, bitter and pleasant. So here, by a certain consequence, there have
appeared its contrarieties and diversities, accommodated to contrary and diverse
stomachs and tastes, to which it may please them to make themselves present at
our typical symposium, so that there be no one who complains of having arrived
there in vain, and whoever does not like this may take that.

Armesso. It is true; but what will you say if, further, in your banquet, in your
supper, there appear things that are good neither for salad nor for meal,
neither for fruits nor for ordinary fare, neither cold nor hot, neither raw nor
cooked, that are good neither for appetite nor hunger, not good for the healthy
nor for the sick, and that ought not to come from the hands of either cook or
apothecary?

Filoteo. You will see that in this too our supper is not unlike any other that
can be. Just as there, in the best part of eating, either some overly hot morsel
burns you, so that you must throw it back out anew, or, weeping and tearing,
send it wandering around the palate until that cursed push can be given down
through the gullet; or some tooth is stupefied, or your tongue is intercepted
and gets bitten with the bread, or some little stone breaks and calcines between
your teeth and makes you reject the whole mouthful, or some hair from the cook
gets tangled in your palate and almost makes you vomit, or some fishbone stops
in your throat and makes you cough sweetly, or some little bone crosses your
throat and puts you in danger of suffocating; so in our supper, to our and the
common misfortune, there have been found things corresponding and proportional
to these. All this happens through the sin of the ancient protoplast Adam, by
whom perverse human nature is condemned always to have disgusts joined to
tastes.

Armesso. Piously and holily. Now what do you answer to what they say, that you
are a rabid Cynic?

Filoteo. I will easily concede part of this, if not all.

Armesso. But you know that for a man it is not so much disgraceful to receive
outrages as to make them?

Filoteo. It is enough that mine are called revenges, and those of others are
called offenses.

Armesso. Even the gods are subject to receiving injuries, suffering infamies,
and bearing blame; but to blame, defame, and injure belongs to the vile,
ignoble, worthless, and wicked.

Filoteo. This is true; therefore we do not injure, but throw back the injuries
that are made not so much to us as to despised philosophy, doing it in such a
way that other displeasures are not added to those already received.

Armesso. You wish, then, to seem a dog that bites, so that no one may dare to
trouble you?

Filoteo. So it is, because I desire quiet, and displeasure displeases me.

Armesso. Yes, but they judge that you proceed too rigorously.

Filoteo. So that they do not return another time, and others learn not to come
to dispute with me and another, treating these conclusions with such mediating
terms.

Armesso. The offense was private; the revenge is public.

Filoteo. It is not unjust for that reason, because many errors are committed in
private that are justly punished in public.

Armesso. But with this you come to spoil your reputation, and make yourself
more blameworthy than they are, because publicly it will be said that you are
impatient, fantastic, bizarre, a flighty head.

Filoteo. I do not care, provided they or others are not troublesome to me
further; and for this I show the Cynic staff, so that they leave me to be in
peace with my own affairs; and if they do not wish to caress me, let them not
come to exercise their incivility upon me.

## Translation Pressure

This first movement explicitly frames philosophical reception as a problem of
vision and creaturely voice. The same new sun that frees some eyes drives other
creatures back into night.

The defense of *The Ash Wednesday Supper* is not only biographical. It gives a
theory of mixed philosophical form: a banquet contains multiple dishes for
multiple stomachs, so a dialogue can mix comic, natural, moral, and serious
materials without ceasing to be philosophical.

The satire is deliberately bodily and vulgar. Cleaning it into polite prose
would misrepresent Bruno's defense of mixed form.
