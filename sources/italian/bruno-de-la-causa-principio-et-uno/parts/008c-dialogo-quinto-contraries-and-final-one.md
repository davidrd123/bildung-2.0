## Working Translation

Fourth, take the signs and verifications by which we wish to conclude that
contraries concur in one, whence it will not be difficult in the end to infer
that all things are one, as every number, both even and odd, both finite and
infinite, is reduced to unity; which unity, iterated with the finite, posits
number, and with the infinite denies number. You will take the signs from
mathematics, the verifications from the other moral and speculative faculties.
Now, as to the signs, tell me: what is more dissimilar to the straight line than
the circle? What is more contrary to the straight than the curved? Yet in the
principle and minimum they agree, seeing that, as Cusanus divinely noted,
inventor of more beautiful secrets of geometry, what difference will you find
between the minimum arc and the minimum chord? Further, in the maximum, what
difference will you find between the infinite circle and the straight line? Do
you not see how the circle, the larger it is, the more with its arc it goes
approaching straightness? Who is so blind that he does not see how arc BB, by
being larger than arc AA, and arc CC larger than arc BB, and arc DD larger than
the other three, look toward being part of a greater circle, and with this
nearer and nearer to the straightness of the infinite line of the infinite
circle, signified by IK? Here certainly one must say and believe that, just as
the line that is greater, according to the reason of greater greatness, is also
straighter, similarly the greatest of all must be superlatively straighter than
all, so that in the end the infinite straight line comes to be infinite circle.

Here then is how not only the maximum and the minimum agree in one being, as we
have demonstrated at other times, but also in the maximum and in the minimum the
contraries come to be one and indifferent. Further, if it pleases you to compare
finite species to the triangle, because from the first finite and first
terminated thing all finite things are understood, by a certain analogy, to
participate in finitude and termination, as in all genera all analogous
predicates take grade and order from the first and maximum of that genus, since
the triangle is the first figure, which cannot be resolved into another species
of figure more simple, as, on the contrary, the quadrangle is resolved into
triangles, and therefore it is first foundation of every terminated and figured
thing, you will find that the triangle, as it is not resolved into another
figure, similarly cannot proceed into triangles whose three angles are greater
or lesser, although they are various and diverse, of various and diverse
figures, as to greater and lesser, minimum and maximum magnitude. Therefore if
you posit an infinite triangle, I do not say really and absolutely, because the
infinite has no figure, but I say infinite by supposition and insofar as angle
gives place to what we wish to demonstrate, that triangle will not have an angle
greater than the minimum finite triangle, not only than the middle ones and
another maximum.

Leaving aside the comparison of figures and figures, I mean of triangles and
triangles, and taking angles and angles, all, however large and small, are
equal, as appears in this square,

which by the diameter is divided into so many triangles, where one sees that
not only are the right angles of the three squares A, B, C equal, but also all
the acute angles that result by division of said diameter, which constitutes so
many double triangles, all of equal angles. Hence by a very express similitude
one sees how the one infinite substance can be whole in all things, although in
some finitely, in others infinitely, in these with lesser, in those with greater
measure.

Add to this, to see further that in this one and infinite the contraries agree,
that the acute and obtuse angle are two contraries. Do you not see how they are
born from one individual and same principle, that is, from one inclination made
by perpendicular line M, which joins the lying line BD at point C? This line, on
that point, by a simple inclination toward point D, after it made indifferently
right angle and right angle, comes to make so much greater difference of acute
and obtuse angle the more it approaches point C; and when it has arrived and
been united to that point, it makes the indifference of acute and obtuse, with
the one and the other likewise being annulled, because they are one in the
potency of the same line. As that line has been able to unite itself and become
indifferent with line BD, so it can disunite itself and become different from
it, stirring up from the same one and individual principle the most contrary
angles, which are the maximum acute and maximum obtuse down to the minimum acute
and minimum obtuse, and beyond to the indifference of right angle and that
concord that consists in the contact of perpendicular and lying line.

As to the verifications, then, who does not know, first, concerning the first
active qualities of corporeal nature, that the principle of heat is indivisible
and therefore separated from every heat, because the principle should not be any
thing of the principiated things? If this is so, who should doubt to affirm that
the principle is neither hot

Figure 3.

nor cold, but one same thing of hot and cold? Whence does it happen that one
contrary is principle of the other, and therefore that transmutations are
circular, if not because there is one subject, one principle, one term and one
continuation and concurrence of the one and the other? Are not minimum hot and
minimum cold all one? From the term of maximum heat is not the principle of
motion toward cold taken? Hence it is open that not only do the two maxima
sometimes occur in resistance and the two minima in concordance, but also the
maximum and the minimum through the vicissitude of transmutation. Hence not
without reason physicians are accustomed to fear in the best disposition; in the
supreme grade of felicity the provident are more timid. Who does not see that
the principle of corruption and generation is one? Is not the last of the
corrupted the principle of the generated? Do we not say together: that taken
away, this posited; that was, this is? Certainly, if we measure well, we see
that corruption is nothing other than a generation, and generation nothing other
than a corruption; love is a hatred, hatred is a love, in the end. Hatred of
the contrary is love of the fitting; love of this is hatred of that. In
substance, then, and root, love and hatred, friendship and strife are one same
thing. From where does the physician more conveniently seek the antidote than
from the poison? Who offers better theriac than the viper? In the greatest
poisons, the best medicines. Is not one potency of two contrary objects? Now
whence do you believe this is, if not from the fact that, as one is the
principle of being, so one is the principle of conceiving the one and the other
object; and that, as contraries are around one subject, so they are apprehended
by one and the same sense? I leave aside that the circular rests on the plane,
the concave settles and resides in the convex, the irascible lives joined to
the patient, the proudest is especially pleased by the humble, the miser by the
liberal.

In conclusion, whoever wishes to know the greatest secrets of nature should
look and contemplate around the minima and maxima of contraries and opposites.
Profound magic is to know how to draw out the contrary after having found the
point of union. Poor Aristotle tended toward this with thought, positing
privation, to which a certain disposition is joined, as progenitrix, parent, and
mother of form; but he could not add to it. He could not arrive there because,
planting his foot in the genus of opposition, he remained trapped in such a way
that, not descending to the species of contrariety, he did not reach nor fix his
eyes on the target, from which he wandered entirely, saying that contraries
cannot actually agree in the same subject.

Polihimnio. You have determined highly, rarely, and singularly about the whole,
the maximum, being, principle, the one. But I would like to see you distinguish
unity, because I find a "Woe to the solitary!" Besides, I feel great anguish
because in my purse and pouch there lodges no more than one widowed solidus.

Teofilo. That unity is all which is not explicated, is not under distribution
and distinction of number, and is not such singularity as you would perhaps
understand; rather it is complicating and comprehending.

Polihimnio. Example? Because, to tell the truth, I understand, but do not grasp.

Teofilo. As the denary is likewise one unity, but complicating; the centenary
is no less unity, but more complicating; the millenary is not less unity than
the others, but much more complicating. What I propose to you in arithmetic you
must understand more highly and simply in all things. The highest good, the
highest appetible, the highest perfection, the highest beatitude consists in
the unity that complicates the whole. We delight in color, but not in any one
explicated color whatever, but most of all in one that complicates all colors.
We delight in voice, not in a singular one, but in a complicating one that
results from the harmony of many. We delight in one sensible thing, but most of
all in the one that comprehends in itself all sensibles; in one knowable that
comprehends every knowable; in one apprehensible that embraces all that can be
comprehended; in one being that completes all, most of all in that one that is
the whole itself. As you, Polihimnio, would delight more in the unity of a gem
so precious that it countervailed all the gold in the world, than in the
multitude of thousands of thousands of such coins as the one you have in your
purse.

Polihimnio. Very well.

Gervasio. Here I am learned; because as one who does not understand one
understands nothing, so one who truly understands one understands all; and the
more one approaches the intelligence of the one, the more one approaches the
apprehension of all.

Dicsono Arelio. So I, if I have understood well, depart very enriched by the
contemplation of Teofilo, faithful reporter of the Nolan philosophy.

Teofilo. Praised be the gods, and magnified by all living beings the infinite,
most simple, most one, highest, and most absolute cause, principle, and one.

End of the Five Dialogues

On Cause, Principle, and One.

## Translation Pressure

The mathematical figures are preserved as figure markers, but the local source
does not include actual diagram images. A promoted edition should recover or
reconstruct the diagrams.

Cusanus is explicitly named here as `Cusano`; the Cusan maximum/minimum and
straight/curved coincidence are no longer only a resonance but a cited source.

The final definition of unity is `complicating and comprehending`, not isolated
singularity. Polihimnio's comic coin example helps prevent a thin numerical
reading of one.
