# Encounter 003: Matter Has None So That It May Have All

Status: bounded source-local encounter. Useful, not promoted.

## Why This Passage

This is the decisive reversal in the fourth dialogue. What the Peripatetic
habit reads as lack, Bruno reads as capacity and plenitude.

## Core Passage

Matter does not have fixed dimensions according to itself. The objection says:
therefore it has none. Dicsono answers: for that reason it has none, so that it
may have all.

The follow-through matters. Matter does not receive dimensions from outside;
it sends and drives them from its womb. It is not deprived of forms like ice is
without heat, but like a pregnant one is without the offspring she brings forth
from herself.

## Mechanism

Absence of determinate form is not simple privation. In matter, non-possession
of one fixed form is the condition for containing all forms in potency.

This is not empty receptivity. It is indeterminate plenitude.

## Pressure

The working translation should preserve the rhetorical simplicity of the actual
Italian line: `E noi diciamo che per questo non ne ha alcuna, a fine che le
abbia tutte` - it has none so that it may have all.

The Latin shorthand `ideo habet nullas, ut omnes habeat` is useful as a compact
working tag for the structure of the reversal, but it should not be cited as
the source wording. The line is too important to paraphrase into smoother
metaphysics.

## Use

Use this encounter as the anchor for Bruno's positive doctrine of matter. It
blocks readings of `potenza` as mere lack and supports the later claim that
matter is divine parent, source, and fountain of forms.

## Open Questions

- Should `potenza` stay as `potency` everywhere in this zone, or do some public

- How should the pregnancy image be handled in a modern reader without
  softening Bruno's dependence on gendered analogy?
