## Why This Passage

The third and fourth dialogues converge here. Matter is not the passive place
where form arrives. It is the persistent subject in whose womb forms are
conserved, from which they appear, and to which they return.

## Core Passage

The third dialogue argues that forms do not have being without matter: they are
generated in it, perish in it, come out from its womb, and are gathered back
into it.

The fourth dialogue sharpens the point against Aristotle. If forms are said to
be drawn from the potency of matter rather than introduced into matter from
outside, then matter should be said to contain and include forms. The thing
that explicates what it holds implicatedly should be called divine, the best
parent, progenitrix, mother of natural things, indeed nature itself in
substance.

## Mechanism

Generation is separation, birth, and efflux from within matter, not apposition
or reception from outside.

Matter is therefore not merely the subject of change. It is the conservative
source that makes change possible because forms have their durable ground in
it.

## Pressure

This is the strongest pressure point for:

- `seno della materia`
- `fonte de le forme`
- `implicazione`
- `esplicazione`
- `parente`, `genetrice`, `madre`

The translation should keep "womb" and "mother" where Bruno uses them, while
the notes can mark how gendered and polemical the reversal is.

## Use

Use this encounter when tracking matter as a positive principle rather than a
mere substrate. It is also the best local evidence for linking the fourth
dialogue back to the universal intellect passage: form is explicated from
within, not added from without.

## Open Questions

- Should a later glossary split `seno`, `fonte`, and `madre`, or keep them as
