# Encounter 002: Life In All Things According To Substance

## Why This Passage

This is the sharpest place where Bruno commits to life and soul in all things
while protecting the claim from the crude reading that every artifact is an
animal.

## Core Passage

The exchange moves through a provocation: if all natural forms are soul, are
all things animated? Teofilo answers yes, but then distinguishes common
animated operation from substantial vital principle.

A table as table, a garment as garment, leather as leather, and glass as glass
are not animated in the ordinary operational sense. But as natural composites,
even the smallest thing has matter and form and a portion of spiritual
substance. Bruno's final formulation is that all things have soul and life
according to substance, not according to the act and operation recognized by
Peripatetic definitions.

## Mechanism

The distinction is between:

- life according to substance or first principle,
- life according to observable act and operation.

The first is universal. The second is differential and depends on disposed
subjects and recognizable operations.

## Pressure

This passage makes `anima`, `vita`, `spirto`, `animato`, and `animal` a single
pressure cluster. Translating everything as "alive" would be too blunt.

The working draft should continue to preserve the stepped vocabulary: soul,
life, spirit, animated, animal. Bruno's claim is not only pan-vital intensity;
it is also terminological sorting.

## Use

Use this encounter when distinguishing Bruno from both inert matter and naive
animism. The passage gives the internal constraint: not every thing with soul
is called animate, and not every vital principle appears as animal operation.
