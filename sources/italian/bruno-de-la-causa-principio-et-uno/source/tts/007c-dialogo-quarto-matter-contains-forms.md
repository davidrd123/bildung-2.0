# Dialogo Quarto, Matter Contains Forms

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
As being-able-to-be coincides with being.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Therefore it does not differ from form?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
In no way in absolute potency and absolute act. This, however, is in
the extreme of purity, simplicity, indivisibility, and unity, because it is
absolutely all. If it had certain dimensions, certain being, certain figure,
certain property, certain difference, it would not be absolute, would not be
all.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Every thing, then, that comprehends whatever genus is
individual?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
So it is, because the form that comprehends all qualities is none of
them; what has all figures has none of them; what has all sensible being is not
therefore sensed. More highly individual is what has all natural being; more
highly, what has all intellectual being; most highly, what has all being that
can be.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
In similitude to this ladder of being, do you wish there to be
the ladder of being-able-to-be? And do you wish that, as the formal reason
ascends, so the material reason ascends?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
It is true.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
You take this definition of matter and potency deeply and
highly.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
True.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
But this truth will not be able to be grasped by everyone,
because it is indeed arduous to grasp the mode in which one has all species of
dimensions and none of them, has all formal being and does not have any being
form.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Do you understand how it can be?

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
I believe so; because I grasp well that act, in order to be
all, must be not some particular thing.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
The same thing cannot be whole and something; I too grasp that.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Therefore you will be able to grasp to the point that, if we wished to
posit dimensionability as the reason of matter, such a reason would not be
repugnant to any sort of matter; but one matter comes to differ from the other
only by being absolute from dimensions and by being contracted to dimensions.
By being absolute, it is above all and comprehends all; by being contracted, it
comes to be comprehended by some and is under some.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
You say well that matter according to itself does not have
certain dimensions, and therefore is understood as indivisible, and receives
dimensions according to the reason of the form it receives. It has one set of
dimensions under the human form, another under the horse form, another under the
olive, another under the myrtle. Therefore, before it is under any of these
forms, it has in faculty all those dimensions, just as it has potency to receive
all those forms.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
They say, however, for that reason that it has no dimensions.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
And we say that for this reason it has none, so that it may
have all.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
Why do you wish rather that it includes all than excludes all?

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Because it does not come to receive dimensions as from outside,
but to send and drive them forth as from its womb.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
He speaks very well. Besides, it is also the customary mode of speech
of the Peripatetics, who say that the whole dimensional act and all forms go out
and come forth from the potency of matter. Averroes understands this in part:
although an Arab and ignorant of the Greek language, nevertheless in Peripatetic
doctrine he understood more than any Greek we have read; and he would have
understood more if he had not been so addicted to his god Aristotle. He says
that matter in its essence comprehends interminate dimensions, wishing to signal
that these come to be terminated now with this figure and dimensions, now with
that and that other, these and those others, according to the change of natural
forms. By this sense one sees that matter sends them as from itself and does not
receive them as from outside. Plotinus, prince in the sect of Plato, also
understood this in part. Making a difference between the matter of superior and
inferior things, he says that that matter is all together, and since it
possesses all, it has nothing into which to change; but this one, with a certain
vicissitude through the parts, makes itself all, and at times and times becomes
thing and thing; therefore it is always under diversity, alteration, and motion.
Thus, then, that matter is never formless, just as neither is this one, although
that and this are so differently: that one in the instant of eternity, this one
in the instants of time; that one together, this one successively; that one
explicatedly, this one complicatedly; that one as many, this one as one; that
one through each and thing by thing, this one as whole and every thing.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
So that not only according to your principles, but further
according to the principles of others' modes of philosophizing, you wish to
infer that matter is not that prope nihil, that pure potency, naked, without
act, without virtue and perfection.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
So it is. I say that it is deprived of forms and without them, not as
ice is without heat, as the deep is deprived of light, but as the pregnant one
is without her offspring, which she sends and draws forth from herself; and as
in this hemisphere the earth, by night, is without light, which by its own
shaking it is powerful to reacquire.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Here also in these inferior things, if not altogether, act comes
very much to coincide with potency.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
I leave it to you to judge.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
And if this potency below came finally to be one with that
above, what would it be?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
You judge. From here you can ascend to the concept, I do not say of
the highest and best principle, excluded from our consideration, but of the soul
of the world, as it is act of all and potency of all, and is whole in all.
Whence in the end, granted that there are innumerable individuals, every thing
is one; and to know this unity is the scope and term of all natural philosophies
and contemplations, leaving in its own terms the higher contemplation that
ascends above nature, which for one who does not believe is impossible and
nothing.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
It is true, because one ascends there by supernatural light,
not natural.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Those who judge every thing to be body do not have this: either
simple, as ether, or composite, as the stars and astral things; and they do not
seek divinity outside the infinite world and infinite things, but within this
world and in those things.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
In this alone the faithful theologian seems to me different
from the true philosopher.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
I believe so too. I believe you have grasped what I mean.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Quite well, I think. So that from your speech I infer that,
although we do not let matter climb above natural things and we plant our foot
on its common definition that the most vulgar philosophy brings forward, we will
still find that it retains a better prerogative than that philosophy recognizes.
In the end that philosophy gives it nothing other than the reason of being
subject of forms and receptive potency of natural forms without name, without
definition, without any term, because without every actuality. This seemed
difficult to certain cowled men, who, not wishing to accuse but to excuse this
doctrine, say that it has only entitative act, that is, one different from what
simply is not and has no being at all in nature, like some chimera or thing that
is feigned; because in the end this matter has being, and this suffices for it,
thus, without mode and dignity, which depend on actuality that is nothing. But
you would ask Aristotle for the reason: "Why do you, O prince of Peripatetics,
wish rather that matter be nothing because it has no act than that it be all
because it has all acts, whether confused or most confused, as it pleases you?
Are you not the one who, always speaking of the new being of forms in matter or
of the generation of things, says that forms proceed and clear out from the
inside of matter, and you were never heard to say that by the work of an
efficient they come from the outside, but that the efficient draws them out from
within? I leave aside that the efficient of these things, called by you with a
common name Nature, you make an internal principle and not an external one, as
happens in artificial things. Then it seems to me fitting to say that matter
does not have in itself any form and act when it comes to receive it from
outside; then it seems to me fitting to say that it has them all when one says
that it drives them all from its womb. Are you not the one who, if not forced by
reason, nevertheless pushed by the custom of speaking, in defining matter, says
rather that it is 'that thing from which every natural species is produced' than
you ever said it is 'that in which things are made,' as it would be fitting to
say when the acts did not come out from it and consequently it did not have
them?"

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
Certainly Aristotle with his own was accustomed to say rather that
forms are led out from the potency of matter than led into it, emerge rather
from matter itself than are thrust into it. But I would say that it pleased
Aristotle to call the explication of form act rather than the implication.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
And I say that expressed, sensible, and explicated being is not
the principal reason of actuality, but is a thing consequent on and effect of
that actuality; just as the principal being of wood and reason of its actuality
does not consist in being bed, but in being of such substance and consistency
that it can be bed, bench, beam, idol, and every formed thing of wood. I leave
aside that according to a higher reason of natural matter, all natural things
are made from it than artificial things from artificial matter, because art
stirs up forms from matter either by subtraction, as when it makes a statue from
stone, or by apposition, as when, joining stone to stone and wood and earth, it
forms a house; but nature from its matter makes everything by way of separation,
birth, effluxion, as the Pythagoreans understood, including Anaxagoras and
Democritus, and the sages of Babylon confirmed. Moses too subscribed to these,
for, describing the generation of things commanded by the universal efficient,
he uses this mode of speaking: "Let the earth produce its animals; let the
waters produce living souls," as if he said: let matter produce them. For
according to him the material principle of things is water; whence he says that
the efficient intellect, called by him spirit, "brooded over the waters," that
is, gave them procreative virtue, and from them produced the natural species,
which all are then said by him, in substance, to be waters. Hence, speaking of
the separation of lower and higher bodies, he says that "the mind separated the
waters from the waters," from the middle of which he indicates the dry land
appeared. All, then, wish things to be from matter by way of separation and not
by way of apposition and reception. Therefore one must rather say that matter
contains forms and includes them than think it is empty of them and excludes
them. That, then, which explicates what it holds implicated must be called a
divine thing and excellent parent, progenitrix and mother of natural things,
indeed the whole of nature in substance. Do you not say and wish so, Teofilo?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Certainly.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Indeed I marvel greatly how our Peripatetics have not continued
the similitude of art. Among many matters that art knows and treats, it judges
that one better and more worthy which is less subject to corruption and more
constant in duration, and from which more things can be produced. Therefore it
judges gold to be nobler than wood, stone, and iron, because it is less subject
to corruption; and what can be made of wood and stone can be made of gold, and
many other things besides, greater and better because of its beauty, constancy,
treatability, and nobility. Now what should we say of that matter from which
human being, gold, and all natural things are made? Should it not be esteemed
more worthy than artificial matter, and have the reason of better actuality?
"Why, O Aristotle, do you not wish that what is foundation and base of
actuality, I mean of what is in act, and what you say always is and endures
eternally, be more in act than your forms, than your entelechies, which go and
come, so that, if you wished to seek the permanence of this formal principle
also..."

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
Because principles must always remain.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
"...and not being able to recur to the fantastic ideas of Plato,
as so hostile to you, you will be forced and necessitated to say either that
these specific forms have their permanent actuality in the hand of the
efficient, and this you cannot say, because that is named by you stirrer and
drawer-out of forms from the potency of matter; or that they have their
permanent actuality in the womb of matter, and this it will be necessary for
you to say, because all forms that appear as if on its surface, which you call
individual and in act, both those that were and those that are and will be, are
principiated things, not principle. And certainly I believe the particular form
to be on the surface of matter as the accident is on the surface of composite
substance. Hence expressed form must have lesser reason of actuality in respect
of matter, just as accidental form has lesser reason of actuality in respect of
the composite."

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
In truth Aristotle resolves himself poorly, for he says, together with
all the ancient philosophers, that principles must always remain; and then, when
we seek in his doctrine where natural form, which goes fluctuating on the back
of matter, has its perpetual permanence, we will not find it in the fixed stars,
because these particulars that we see do not descend from on high; not in the
ideal seals separated from matter, because those certainly, if they are not
monsters, are worse than monsters, I mean chimeras and vain fantasies. What
then? They are in the womb of matter. What then? Matter is fountain of
actuality. Do you want me to say more and make you see into what absurdity
Aristotle has fallen? He says matter is in potency. Now ask him when it will be
in act. A great multitude with him will answer: when it has form. Now add and
ask: what is it that has being anew? They will answer despite themselves: the
composite and not matter, because matter is always the same, is not renewed, is
not changed. As in artificial things, when from wood a statue is made, we do not
say that new being comes to the wood, because it is nothing more or less wood
now than it was before; but what receives being and actuality is what is newly
produced, the composite, I mean the statue. How then do you say that potency
belongs to that which will never be in act or have act? Matter is not, then, in
potency to be, or that which can be, because it is always the same and
immutable, and is that around which and in which there is mutation, rather than
that which is changed. What is altered, increased, diminished, moved in place,
corrupted, is always, according to you Peripatetics yourselves, the composite,
never matter. Why then do you say matter is now in potency, now in act?
Certainly no one should doubt that, whether by receiving forms or by sending
them from itself, as to its essence and substance, matter receives no greater or
lesser actuality; and therefore there is no reason by which it should be called
in potency. Potency fits what is in continual motion around matter, and not
matter itself, which is in eternal state and is rather cause of state; because
if form, according to its fundamental and specific being, is of simple and
invariable essence, not only logically in concept and reason but also physically
in nature, it will have to be in the perpetual faculty of matter, which is a
potency indistinct from act, as in many modes I explicated when I discoursed so
many times about potency.
