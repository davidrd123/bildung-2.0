# Dialogo Quinto, One, Infinite, Immobile

DIALOGUE FIVE

TEOFILO.

The universe, then, is one, infinite, immobile. One, I say, is absolute
possibility; one the act; one the form or soul; one the matter or body; one the
thing; one being; one the maximum and best. This cannot be able to be
comprehended, and therefore is unfinishable and interminable, and for that
reason infinite and unterminated, and consequently immobile. It does not move
locally, because it has no thing outside itself to which it may transport
itself, seeing that it is the whole. It is not generated, because there is no
other being that it could desire or await, seeing that it has all being. It is
not corrupted, because there is no other thing into which it changes, seeing
that it is every thing. It cannot diminish or grow, seeing that it is infinite;
as nothing can be added to it, so there is nothing from which anything can be
subtracted, because the infinite does not have proportionable parts. It is not
alterable into another disposition, because it has no outside from which it
suffers and through which it comes into some affection. Further, because it
comprehends all contrarieties in its being in unity and agreement, and can have
no inclination to another and new being, or to another and other mode of being,
it cannot be subject of mutation according to any quality, nor can it have a
contrary or diverse thing that alters it, because in it every thing is
concordant. It is not matter, because it is not figured or figureable, not
terminated or terminable. It is not form, because it does not inform or figure
another, seeing that it is all, is maximum, is one, is universe. It is neither
measurable nor measure. It is not comprehended, because it is not greater than
itself. It is not contained, because it is not smaller than itself. It is not
equaled, because it is not other and other, but one and the same. Being the same
and one, it does not have being and being; and because it does not have being
and being, it does not have part and part; and because it does not have part and
part, it is not composite. This is limit in such a way that it is not limit, is
form in such a way that it is not form, is matter in such a way that it is not
matter, is soul in such a way that it is not soul, because it is the whole
indifferently, and therefore it is one; the universe is one.

In this certainly height is not greater than length and depth; whence by a
certain similitude it is called, but is not, a sphere. In the sphere, length,
breadth, and depth are the same thing because they have the same limit; but in
the universe, breadth, length, and depth are the same thing because they
likewise have no limit and are infinite. If it has no middle, quadrant, and
other measures, if there is no measure in it, there is no proportional part,
nor absolutely any part that differs from the whole. For if you wish to say
part of the infinite, you must call it infinite; if it is infinite, it concurs
in one being with the whole. Therefore the universe is one, infinite,
impartible. And if in the infinite no difference is found, as of whole and part,
and as of other and other, certainly the infinite is one. Under the comprehension
of the infinite there is no greater part and lesser part, because no part,
however greater, approaches the proportion of the infinite more than another
part, however smaller; and therefore in infinite duration the hour does not
differ from the day, the day from the year, the year from the age, the age from
the moment, because moments and hours are not more than ages, and those have no
less proportion to eternity than these. Similarly in the immense, the palm does
not differ from the stadium, the stadium from the parasang, because one
approaches the proportion of immensity no more by parasangs than by palms.
Therefore infinite hours are not more than infinite ages, and infinite palms
are not of greater number than infinite parasangs. You do not approach the
proportion, similitude, union, and identity of the infinite more by being human
than ant, by being a star than a human, because you do not come closer to that
being by being sun or moon than by being human or ant; and therefore in the
infinite these things are indifferent. And what I say of these, I understand of
all other things of particular subsistence.

Now, if all these particular things in the infinite are not other and other, are
not different, are not species, by necessary consequence they are not number;
therefore the universe is also one and immobile. This, because it comprehends
all and does not suffer other and other being, and does not allow with itself or
in itself any mutation, consequently is all that it can be; and in it, as I said
the other day, act is not different from potency. If act is not different from
potency, it is necessary that in it point, line, surface, and body do not
differ. For that line is surface just as line, by moving, can be surface; that
surface is moved and is made body just as surface can move and, with its flux,
can become body. It is necessary, then, that the point in the infinite not
differ from body, because the point, by running out from being point, becomes
line; by running out from being line, becomes surface; by running out from being
surface, becomes body. The point, then, because it is in potency to be body,
does not differ from being body where potency and act are one same thing.

Therefore the individual is not different from the divisible, the simplest from
the infinite, the center from the circumference. Because, then, the infinite is
all that it can be, it is immobile; because in it all is indifferent, it is one;
and because it has all the greatness and perfection that can be had further and
further, it is maximum and best immense. If the point does not differ from body,
center from circumference, finite from infinite, maximum from minimum, we can
surely affirm that the universe is all center, or that the center of the
universe is everywhere, and that the circumference is not in any part insofar as
it is different from the center; or else that the circumference is everywhere,
but the center is not found insofar as it is different from it. Here is how it
is not impossible, but necessary, that the best, maximum, incomprehensible is
all, is everywhere, is in all, because, as simple and indivisible, it can be
all, be everywhere, be in all. And so it has not been said vainly that Jove
fills all things, inhabits all parts of the universe, is center of what has
being, one in all and through whom all is one. Since he is all things and
comprehends all being in himself, he comes to make every thing be in every
thing.

But you would say to me: why, then, do things change, why does particular matter
force itself toward other forms? I answer you that it is not mutation that seeks
other being, but another mode of being. And this is the difference between the
universe and the things of the universe, because that comprehends all being and
all modes of being; of these, each has all being, but not all modes of being,
and cannot actually have all circumstances and accidents, because many forms are
incompossible in the same subject, either by being contrary or by belonging to
diverse species; as the same individual supposit cannot be under the accidents
of horse and human being, under the dimensions of a plant and an animal.
Further, the universe comprehends all being totally, because outside and beyond
infinite being there is no thing that is, since it has no outside or beyond; of
these, then, each comprehends all being, but not totally, because beyond each
there are infinite others. Therefore understand all being in all, but not
totally and in every mode in each. Therefore understand how every thing is one,
but not in one mode.

Therefore one does not err who says that being, substance, and essence are one.
As infinite and unterminated, according to substance, duration, greatness, and
vigor alike, it has no reason of principle or of principiated, because, every
thing concurring in unity and identity, I mean in same being, it comes to have
absolute reason and not relative. In the one infinite, immobile, which is
substance, which is being, there is found the multitude, the number, which,
because it is mode and multiformity of being and comes to denominate thing by
thing, does not for this reason make being more than one, but many-moded and
multiform and many-figured. Therefore, considering deeply with the natural
philosophers, leaving the logicians in their fantasies, we find that everything
that makes difference and number is pure accident, pure figure, pure complexion.
Every production, of whatever sort it may be, is an alteration, while substance
always remains the same, because there is only one, one divine, immortal being.
Pythagoras could understand this, he who does not fear death but awaits
mutation. All philosophers commonly called physicists could understand it, who
say that nothing is generated according to substance nor corrupted, unless we
wish to name alteration in this way. Solomon understood this, who says that
there is no new thing under the sun, but what is was already before. You have,
then, how all things are in the universe, and the universe is in all things; we
in it, it in us; and thus all concurs in one perfect unity. Here is how we
should not trouble our spirit; here is how there is no thing by which we should
be dismayed. Because this unity is alone and stable, and always remains; this
one is eternal. Every countenance, every face, every other thing is vanity, is
as nothing; indeed, everything that is outside this one is nothing. Those
philosophers have found their friend Sophia who have found this unity. Wisdom,
truth, and unity are altogether the same thing. All have known how to say that
true, one, and being are the same thing, but not all have understood; because
some have followed the mode of speaking but have not grasped the mode of
understanding of true sages. Aristotle, among others, who did not find the one,
did not find being, and did not find the true, because he did not know being as
one. And although he was free to take the signification of being common to
substance and accident, and further to distinguish his categories according to
so many genera and species by so many differences, nevertheless he did not fail
to be no less poorly advised in the truth by not going deep into the cognition
of this unity and indifference of constant nature and being; and, as a very dry
sophist, with malicious explications and light persuasions, he perverted the
sentences of the ancients and opposed himself to truth, perhaps not so much
through weakness of intellect as through force of envy and ambition.
