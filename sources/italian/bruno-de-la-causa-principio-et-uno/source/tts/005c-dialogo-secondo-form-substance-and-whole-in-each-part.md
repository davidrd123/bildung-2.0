# Dialogo Secondo, Form, Substance, and Whole in Each Part

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
This seems to me conformable not only to the sense of
Pythagoras, whose sentence the Poet recites when he says:

First, heaven and lands and liquid plains,

and the shining globe of the moon and Titanian stars,

spirit within nourishes, and mind, infused through the limbs,

stirs the whole mass and mingles itself with the whole body;

but also to the sense of the theologian who says: "The spirit fills and
replenishes the earth, and that which contains the whole." And another,
speaking perhaps of the commerce of form with matter and potency, says that it
is exceeded by act and form.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
If, then, spirit, soul, and life are found in all things and, according
to certain grades, fill all matter, it certainly comes to be the true act and
the true form of all things. The soul of the world, therefore, is the formal
constitutive principle of the universe and of what is contained in it. I say
that, if life is found in all things, soul comes to be form of all things: it is
president over matter everywhere and rules in composites; it effects the
composition and consistency of the parts. And therefore persistence seems no
less to belong to such a form than to matter. I understand this form to be one
of all things; nevertheless, according to the diversity of the dispositions of
matter and according to the faculty of the active and passive material
principles, it comes to produce diverse figurations and effect diverse
faculties, sometimes showing an effect of life without sense, sometimes an
effect of life and sense without intellect, sometimes seeming to have all its
faculties suppressed and repressed either by the weakness or by another reason
of matter. Thus, as this form changes seats and vicissitude, it is impossible
for it to be annulled, because spiritual substance is no less subsistent than
material substance. Therefore only the exterior forms change and are also
annulled, because they are not things but of things; they are not substances,
but accidents and circumstances of substances.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
Not beings, but of beings.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Certainly, if any thing of substances were annulled, the world
would come to be emptied out.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Therefore we have an intrinsic formal principle, eternal and
subsistent, incomparably better than the one feigned by the sophists who turn
around accidents, ignorant of the substance of things, and who come to posit
substances as corruptible, because they call chiefly, firstly, and principally
substance that which results from composition. This is nothing other than an
accident, which contains in itself no stability and truth, and resolves into
nothing. They say that what results from composition is truly human; that soul
is truly either perfection and act of living body, or else a thing that results
from a certain symmetry of complexion and members. Hence it is no marvel if
they make so much and take so much fright at death and dissolution, as those
for whom the loss of being is imminent. Against this madness nature cries with
loud voices, assuring us that neither bodies nor soul should fear death,
because both matter and form are most constant principles:

O race stunned by fear of icy death,

why do you fear Styx, why shadows and vain names,

the matter of seers and dangers of a false world?

Whether the pyre by flame or old age by wasting

has taken bodies away, do not think they can suffer any evils:

souls lack death and dwell in received homes.

All things are changed; nothing perishes.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
In conformity with this, it seems to me that Solomon, esteemed
wisest among the Hebrews, says: "What is that which is? That very thing which
was. What is that which was? That very thing which is. Nothing new under the
sun." So this form that you posit is not non-existent and adhering to matter
according to being; it does not depend on body and matter in order to subsist?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
So it is. And further I do not yet determine whether every form is
accompanied by matter, just as I already say securely of matter that there is no
part at all destitute of form, except when matter is understood logically, as by
Aristotle, who never tires of dividing by reason what is undivided according to
nature and truth.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
You do not wish that there be any other form than this eternal
companion of matter?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
There is another still more natural, which is the material form, of
which we will reason afterward. For now note this distinction of form. There is
one sort of first form, which informs, extends itself, and depends; because this
informs the whole, it is in the whole; because it extends itself, it
communicates the perfection of the whole to the parts; and because it depends
and does not have operation of itself, it comes to communicate the operation of
the whole to the parts, and similarly the name and the being. Such is material
form, as that of fire, because every part of fire heats, is called fire, and is
fire. Second, there is another sort of form, which informs and depends, but does
not extend itself; and such a form, because it perfects and actuates the whole,
is in the whole and in every part of it; because it does not extend itself, it
does not attribute the act of the whole to the parts; because it depends, it
communicates the operation of the whole to the parts. Such is the vegetative and
sensitive soul, because no part of the animal is animal, and nevertheless each
part lives and senses. Third, there is another sort of form, which actuates and
makes perfect the whole, but does not extend itself, nor does it depend as to
operation. Because this actuates and makes perfect, it is in the whole, and in
all and every part; because it does not extend itself, it does not attribute the
perfection of the whole to the parts; because it does not depend, it does not
communicate the operation. Such is soul insofar as it can exercise the
intellective potency and is called intellective; it does not make any part of a
human being such that it can be named human, or is human, or can be said to
understand. Of these three species the first is material, which cannot be
understood nor can be without matter; the other two species, which in the end
concur into one according to substance and being, and are distinguished
according to the mode we have said above, we denominate that formal principle
which is distinct from the material principle.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
I understand.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Besides this I want it to be noticed that, although speaking according
to the common mode we say there are five grades of forms, namely of element,
mixed thing, vegetative, sensitive, and intellective, nevertheless we do not
understand this according to the vulgar intention; because this distinction is
valid according to the operations that appear and proceed from the subjects, not
according to that reason of the primary and fundamental being of that spiritual
form and life, which same form fills everything, though not according to the
same mode.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
I understand. So that this form, which you posit as principle,
is subsistent form, constitutes perfect species, is in its own genus, and is not
part of species like the Peripatetic form.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
So it is.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
The distinction of forms in matter is not according to the
accidental dispositions that depend on the material form.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
True.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Hence also this separate form does not come to be multiplied
according to number, because every numerical multiplication depends on matter.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Yes.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Further, it is invariable in itself, but variable through the
subjects and diversity of matters. And such a form, although in the subject it
makes the part differ from the whole, nevertheless does not differ in the part
and in the whole; although one reason belongs to it as subsistent of itself,
another insofar as it is act and perfection of some subject, and another then
with regard to a subject with dispositions of one mode, another with those of
another.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Exactly so.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
You do not understand this form as accidental, nor as similar
to the accidental, nor as mixed with matter, nor as inhering in it, but as
in-existing, associated, assisting.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
So I say.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Further, this form is defined and determined by matter, because
having in itself facility to constitute particulars of innumerable species, it
comes to contract itself to constitute one individual; and on the other side,
the potency of indeterminate matter, which can receive whatever form, comes to
be terminated to one species, so that the one is cause of the definition and
determination of the other.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Very well.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Therefore, in a certain mode you approve the sense of
Anaxagoras, who calls the particular forms of nature latent; somewhat that of
Plato, who deduces them from the ideas; somewhat that of Empedocles, who makes
them come from intelligence; in a certain mode that of Aristotle, who makes them
as if come out from the potency of matter?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Yes, because, as we have said, where the form is, in a certain mode all
is there; where soul, spirit, life is, all is there. The former is the intellect
through the ideal species; the forms, if it does not stir them up from matter,
nevertheless it does not go begging them from outside matter, because this
spirit fills the whole.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
I would like to know how form is the soul of the world, whole
everywhere, if it is indivisible. It must therefore be very large, indeed of
infinite dimension, if you say the world is infinite.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
It is quite reasonable that it should be large. So also of Our Lord
said a preacher at Grandazzo in Sicily, where, as a sign that he is present in
the whole world, he ordered a crucifix as large as the church, in similitude of
God the Father, who has the empyrean heaven for canopy, the starry heaven for
seat, and has legs so long that they reach down to earth, which serves him as
footstool. A certain peasant came to ask him, saying, "My reverend father, now
how many ells of cloth will be needed to make his stockings?" And another said
that all the chickpeas, beans, and broad beans of Melazzo and Nicosia would not
be enough to fill his belly. See, then, that this soul of the world should not
be made in this fashion too.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
I would not know how to answer your doubt, Gervasio, but I can answer
that of master Polihimnio. Still, I will speak with a similitude, to satisfy
the question of both of you, because I want you too to carry away some fruit
from our reasonings and discourses. You must know briefly, then, that the soul
of the world and the divinity are not all present throughout the whole and in
every part in a mode by which some material thing could be there, because this
is impossible for any body and any spirit whatever, but in a mode that is not
easy to explicate to you otherwise than by this. You must notice that, if the
soul of the world and universal form are said to be everywhere, this is not
understood corporeally and dimensionally, because they are not such, and so they
cannot be in any part; but they are whole everywhere spiritually. As, for
example, even a rough one, you could imagine a voice, which is whole in a whole
room and in every part of it, because from every part it is heard whole. Just as
these words that I say are heard whole by all, even if there were a thousand
present; and if my voice could reach the whole world, it would be whole
everywhere. I say then to you, master Polihimnio, that the soul is not
indivisible like the point, but, in a certain mode, like the voice. And I answer
you, Gervasio, that the divinity is not everywhere as the God of Grandazzo is in
his whole chapel; because that one, although he is in the whole church, is not
therefore whole in the whole, but has the head in one part, the feet in another,
the arms and torso in other and other parts. But the divinity is whole in
whatever part, as my voice is heard whole from all parts of this hall.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
I have perceived very well.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
I too have understood your voice.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
I believe it well concerning the voice; but concerning the
proposition I think it entered you through one ear and went out through the
other.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
I think it has not even entered, because it is late, and the clock I
keep inside my stomach has struck the hour of supper.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
This is, that is, he has his brain in dishes.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Enough, then. Tomorrow we will convene to reason perhaps
concerning the material principle.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Either I will wait for you, or you will wait for me here.

End of the Second Dialogue.
