# Dialogo Quarto, One Matter of Corporeal and Incorporeal

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Do we not see that the Peripatetics, as also the Platonists, divide
substance by the difference of corporeal and incorporeal? As, then, these
differences are reduced to the potency of the same genus, so the forms must be
of two kinds. For some are transcendent, that is, superior to genus, which are
called principles, such as entity, unity, one, thing, something, and others
similar; others are of a certain genus distinct from another genus, as
substantiality, accidentality. Those of the first manner do not distinguish
matter and do not make another and another potency of it; but, as most universal
terms that comprehend both corporeal and incorporeal substances, they signify
that most universal, most common, and one potency of both. Next, "what impedes
us," said Avicebron, "that just as, before we recognize the matter of accidental
forms, which is the composite, we recognize the matter of substantial form,
which is part of it; so, before we know the matter that is contracted to be
under corporeal forms, we come to know a potency that is distinguishable by the
form of corporeal and incorporeal nature, dissoluble and indissoluble?" Again,
if everything that is, beginning from the highest and supreme being, has a
certain order and makes a dependence, a ladder on which one climbs from
composite things to simple, from these to the simplest and most absolute by
proportional, copulative, and participative means of the nature of one and the
other extreme, and, according to the proper reason, neutral means, there is no
order where there is not some participation; there is no participation where
some connection is not found; there is no connection without some participation.
It is therefore necessary that of all things that are subsistent there be one
principle of subsistence. Add to this that reason itself cannot do otherwise
than, before anything distinguishable, presuppose an indistinct thing; I speak
of those things that are, because I do not understand being and non-being to
have real distinction, but only vocal and nominal. This indistinct thing is one
common reason, to which the difference and distinctive form are added. And
certainly it cannot be denied that, just as every sensible thing presupposes the
subject of sensibility, so every intelligible thing presupposes the subject of
intelligibility. There must therefore be one thing that answers to the common
reason of both subjects, because every essence is necessarily founded on some
being, except the first essence, which is the same as its being, because its
potency is its act, because it is all that it can be, as was said yesterday.
Further, if matter, according to the adversaries themselves, is not body and
precedes, according to its nature, corporeal being, what then can make it so
alien to the substances called incorporeal? And Peripatetics are not lacking who
say: "Just as in corporeal substances there is found something formal and
divine, so in divine substances there must be something material, so that lower
things may be accommodated to higher things and the order of the one may depend
on the order of the others." And the theologians, although some of them are
nourished in Aristotelian doctrine, still should not be troublesome to me in
this, if they accept that they are more debtors to their Scripture than to
philosophy and natural reason. "Do not adore me," said one of their angels to
the patriarch Jacob, "because I am your brother." Now if this one, who speaks
as they understand, is an intellectual substance and affirms by his saying that
that man and he agree in the reality of one subject, whatever formal difference
stands, it remains that philosophers have an oracle of these theologians as
witness.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
I know that this is said by you with reverence, because you
know that it is not fitting for you to beg reasons from such places, which are
outside our harvest.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
You speak well and truly; but I do not allege this as reason and
confirmation, but to avoid scruple as much as I can, because I fear no less to
appear than to be contrary to theology.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Natural reasons will always be admitted by discreet
theologians, however far they discourse, provided they do not determine against
divine authority, but submit themselves to it.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Such are and always will be mine.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Good, then continue.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Plotinus too says in the book On Matter that "if in the intelligible
world there is multitude and plurality of species, it is necessary that there
be something common beyond the property and difference of each of those; what is
common holds the place of matter, what is proper and makes distinction holds the
place of form." He adds that "if this world is in imitation of that one, the
composition of this is in imitation of the composition of that. Further, that
world, if it does not have diversity, does not have order; if it does not have
order, it does not have beauty and ornament; all this is around matter."
Therefore the superior world must be esteemed not only indivisible throughout,
but also, by some of its conditions, divisible and distinct; and its division
and distinction cannot be grasped without some subject matter. And although he
declares that all that multitude agrees in one impartible being outside every
dimension, I will say that this is matter, in which so many forms are united.
That which, before it is conceived as varied and multiform, was in a uniform
concept, and before it was in a formed concept, was in that formless one.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Although in what you have said briefly you have brought many
strong reasons for coming to conclude that matter is one, that the potency by
which everything that is is in act is one, and that with no lesser reason it
belongs to incorporeal substances than to corporeal ones, since those have being
through being-able-to-be no otherwise than these have being through
being-able-to-be, and since further by other powerful reasons, for those who
powerfully consider and comprehend them, you have demonstrated this; nevertheless,
if not for the perfection of the doctrine, then for its clarity, I would like
you to specify in some other way: how in the most excellent things, such as
incorporeal things, something formless and indefinite is found; how there can
there be reason of the same matter and yet, by the advent of form and act, they
are not likewise called bodies; how, where there is no mutation, generation, or
corruption, you wish there to be matter, which has never been posited for any
other end; how we will be able to say that intelligible nature is simple and
also say that in it there is matter and act. I do not ask this for myself, to
whom the truth is manifest, but perhaps for others who can be more morose and
difficult, as, for example, master Polihimnio and Gervasio.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
I yield.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
I accept, and I thank you, Dicsono, because you consider the necessity
of those who do not have the courage to ask, as is fitting to the civility of
the transalpine tables, where those who sit second are not permitted to stretch
their fingers outside their own little square or round plate, but must wait for
it to be placed in hand, so that they do not take a mouthful that is not paid
for with its "great thanks."

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
I will say, for resolution of the whole, that just as the human being,
according to the proper nature of the human being, is different from the lion
according to the proper nature of the lion, but according to the common nature
of animal, corporeal substance, and other similar things they are indifferent
and the same thing; similarly, according to its proper reason, the matter of
corporeal things is different from that of incorporeal things. Therefore
everything you bring forward about being a constitutive cause of corporeal
nature, being subject of transmutations of all sorts, and being part of
composites, belongs to this matter by its proper reason. Because the same matter,
I mean more clearly, the same that can be made or can be, or is made, is through
the means of dimensions and extensions of the subject, and those qualities that
have being in quantity; and this is called corporeal substance and supposes
corporeal matter. Or it is made, if indeed it has being anew, and is without
those dimensions, extension, and qualities; and this is called incorporeal
substance, and likewise supposes the said matter. Thus to one active potency
both of corporeal and incorporeal things, or to one being both corporeal and
incorporeal, corresponds one passive potency both corporeal and incorporeal,
and one being-able-to-be both corporeal and incorporeal. If therefore we wish to
speak of composition in both the one and the other nature, we must understand it
in one and another manner, and consider that in eternal things matter is said to
be always under one act, while in variable things it always contains now one,
now another. In those, matter has once, always, and together all that it can
have, and is all that it can be; but this matter has it many times, in diverse
times and certain successions.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Some, although they concede that there is matter in incorporeal
things, nevertheless understand it according to a very different reason.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Let there be however much diversity according to the proper reason by
which the one descends to corporeal being and the other does not, the one
receives sensible qualities and the other does not, and it does not seem that
there can be a common reason between that matter to which quantity and being
subject of qualities that have being in dimensions are repugnant, and the nature
to which neither the one nor the other is repugnant; rather, the one and the
other are one same thing, and, as has been said many times, the whole difference
depends on contraction to being corporeal and not being corporeal. As in being
animal every sensitive thing is one, but, by contracting that genus to certain
species, it is repugnant to the human being to be lion, and to this animal to be
that other one. And I add to this, if it pleases you, because you would say to
me that what never is should be judged impossible and against nature rather than
natural, and therefore, since that matter is never found dimensioned, it should
be judged that corporeity is against its nature; and if this is so it is not
likely that there is one nature common to the one and the other before the one
is understood to be contracted to corporeal being. I add, I say, that we can no
less attribute to that matter the necessity of all dimensional acts than, as you
would wish, the impossibility. That matter, by being actually all that it can
be, has all measures, has all species of figures and dimensions; and because it
has them all, it has none, because what is so many diverse things must be none
of those particulars. It belongs to what is all to exclude every particular
being.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
You wish, then, that matter is act? Do you also wish that matter
in incorporeal things coincides with act?
