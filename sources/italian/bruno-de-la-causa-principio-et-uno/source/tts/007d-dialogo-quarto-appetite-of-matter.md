# Dialogo Quarto, Appetite of Matter

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
Please, say something about the appetite of matter, so that we may
take some resolution concerning a certain altercation between me and Gervasio.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
Please do it, Teofilo, because this man has broken my head with the
similitude of female and matter, and with saying that woman is no less content
with males than matter with forms, and he goes discoursing.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Since matter receives no thing from form, why do you wish it to
appetite form? If, as we have said, it sends forms from its womb, and
consequently has them in itself, how do you wish it to appetite them? It does
not appetite those forms that are daily changed on its back, because every
ordered thing appetites that from which it receives perfection. What can a
corruptible thing give to an eternal thing? An imperfect thing, such as the form
of sensible things, which is always in motion, to an eternal thing? An imperfect
thing, such as the form of sensible things, which is always in motion, to
another so perfect that, if it is well contemplated, it is a divine being in
things, as perhaps David of Dinant wished to say, badly understood by some who
report his opinion? Matter does not desire form in order to be conserved by it,
because the corruptible thing does not conserve the eternal thing. Besides, it
is manifest that matter conserves form; hence such form should rather desire
matter in order to perpetuate itself, because by separating from matter, form
loses its being, and not matter, which has all that it had before form was
found, and can have the others. I leave aside that when the cause of corruption
is given, one does not say that form flees matter or leaves matter, but rather
that matter rejects that form in order to take another. I leave to the point
that we have no more reason to say that matter appetites forms than, on the
contrary, that it hates them, speaking of those that are generated and
corrupted; because the fountain of forms, which is in itself, cannot appetite,
since one does not appetite what one possesses. For by the same reason by which
one says it appetites what it sometimes receives or produces, likewise, when it
rejects and takes that away, one can say that it abominates it; indeed, it
abominates more powerfully than it appetites, since eternally it rejects that
numeral form which it retained for a short time. If, then, you remember this,
that it rejects as many as it takes, you must equally make it lawful for me to
say that it has them in disgust, as I will make you say that it has them in
desire.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
Now here on the ground are not only the castles of Polihimnio, but
also of others than Polihimnio.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
More sparingly these things to men...

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
We have understood enough for today; until we see one another
tomorrow!

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Then, farewell.

End of the Fourth Dialogue.
