# Dialogo Primo, Pedants and Interlocutors

[[speaker: Filoteo]]
Do not complain, Armesso, because in every place where there are
doctors and priests, one and the other seed of those men is found. Where those
who are truly learned and truly priests, although promoted from low condition,
cannot fail to be civilized and ennobled, because science is a most exquisite
path to making the human spirit heroic. But those others show themselves so
much the more expressly rustic, the more they seem to wish to thunder on high
either with the divum pater or with the giant Salmoneus, when they stroll
about as purpled satyr or Faun with that frightful and imperial grand posture,
after having determined in the regental chair to which declension belongs the
phrase hic, et haec, et hoc nihil.

[[speaker: Armesso]]
Now let us leave these propositions. What book is this that you hold
in your hand?

[[speaker: Filoteo]]
They are certain dialogues.

[[speaker: Armesso]]
The Supper?

[[speaker: Filoteo]]
No.

[[speaker: Armesso]]
What then?

[[speaker: Filoteo]]
Others, in which On Cause, Principle, and One is treated according
to our way.

[[speaker: Armesso]]
What interlocutors? Perhaps we have some other devil of a Frulla or
Prudenzio who will newly put us in some trouble.

[[speaker: Filoteo]]
Do not doubt; except for one, among all the others the subjects are
quiet and most honest.

[[speaker: Armesso]]
So that, according to what you say, we will still have something to
heat up in these dialogues too?

[[speaker: Filoteo]]
Do not doubt, because rather you will be scratched where it itches
than pricked where it hurts.

[[speaker: Armesso]]
Still?

[[speaker: Filoteo]]
Here for one you will find that learned, honest, loving, well-bred,
and very faithful friend Alessandro Dicsono Arelio, whom the Nolan loves as his
own eyes; he is the cause that this matter has been put into the field. He is
introduced as the one who offers matter for consideration to Teofilo. For the
second you have Teofilo, who is I; according to the occasions, I come to
distinguish, define, and demonstrate concerning the subject matter. For the
third you have Gervasio, a man who is not of the profession, but who for pastime
wishes to be present at our conferences; and he is a person who neither smells
good nor stinks, and who takes the affairs of Polihimnio as comedy and, step by
step, gives him field to exercise his madness. This sacrilegious pedant you
have for the fourth: one of the rigid censors of philosophers, whence he affirms
himself a Momus; one deeply affected concerning his flock of scholastics, whence
he names himself in Socratic love; one, perpetual enemy of the female sex,
whence, not being a physicist, he esteems himself Orpheus, Musaeus, Tityrus,
and Amphion. This is one of those who, when they have made a fine construction,
produced an elegant little epistle, snatched a fine phrase from the Ciceronian
kitchen, here Demosthenes has been resurrected, here Tully vegetates, here
Sallust lives; here is an Argus who sees every letter, every syllable, every
word; here Rhadamanthus calls the shades of the silent; here Minos, king of
Crete, moves the urn. They call orations to examination; they make discussion
of phrases, saying: these smell of the poet, these of the comic writer, this of
the orator; this is grave, this is light, that is sublime, that other is a low
genus dicendi; this speech is rough, it would be smooth if it were formed
thus; this is an infant writer, little studious of antiquity, he does not smell
of Arpinum, he is tasteless of Latium. This word is not Tuscan, is not usurped
from Boccaccio, Petrarch, and other approved authors. One does not write homo
but omo, not honore but onore, not Polihimnio but Poliinnio. With this
he triumphs, is content with himself, and his own doings please him more than
anything else. He is a Jove who, from the high watchtower, beholds and considers
the life of other human beings subject to so many errors, calamities, miseries,
and useless labors. He alone is happy, he alone lives the celestial life, when
he contemplates his divinity in the mirror of a Spicilegium, a Dictionary, a
Calepino, a Lexicon, a Cornucopia, a Nizzolio. Endowed with this sufficiency,
while each person is one, he alone is all. If it happens that he laughs, he is
called Democritus; if it happens that he grieves, he is called Heraclitus; if he
disputes, he is called Chrysippus; if he discourses, he is named Aristotle; if
he makes chimeras, he is called Plato; if he bellows a little sermon, he titles
himself Demosthenes; if he construes Virgil, he is Maro. Here he corrects
Achilles, approves Aeneas, reproves Hector, exclaims against Pyrrhus, laments
Priam, accuses Turnus, excuses Dido, commends Achates; and in the end, while he
renders word for word and strings together wild synonymies, he thinks nothing
divine alien to himself. And so, boastful, descending from his chair like one
who has disposed the heavens, regulated senates, tamed armies, reformed worlds,
it is certain that, if it were not for the injury of time, he would do in
effects what he does in opinion. O times, O customs! How rare are those who
understand the nature of participles, adverbs, and conjunctions! How much time
has passed without finding the reason and true cause why the adjective must
agree with the substantive, why the relative must join with the antecedent, by
what rule it is now placed before, now after the sentence, and with what
measures and what orders are intermingled those interjections of one grieving
or rejoicing, heu, oh, ahi, ah, hem, ohe, hui, and other condiments without
which every discourse is most insipid?

[[speaker: Elitropio]]
Say what you want, understand it as you please. I say that for the
happiness of life it is better to esteem oneself Croesus and be poor than to
hold oneself poor and be Croesus. Is it not more fitting to blessedness to have
a gourd that seems beautiful to you and contents you than a Leda, a Helen, who
annoys you and becomes wearisome to you? What then does it matter to these
people to be ignorant and ignobly occupied, if they are so much the happier the
more they please only themselves? Fresh grass is as good to the ass, barley to
the horse, as to you fine bread and partridge; the pig is as content with acorns
and broth as a Jove with ambrosia and nectar. Do you perhaps wish to take these
people away from that sweet madness, by care of which afterward they would break
your head? I leave aside that who knows whether this or that is madness? A
Pyrrhonian said: who knows whether our state is death, and that of those we call
the dead is life? So who knows whether all happiness and true blessedness
consists in the proper copulations and appositions of the members of sentences?

[[speaker: Armesso]]
Thus the world is disposed: we play Democritus over the pedants and
grammarians, the solicitous courtiers play Democritus over us, the little
thinking monks and priests Democritize over everyone; and reciprocally the
pedants mock us, we mock the courtiers, all mock the monks; and in conclusion,
while one is mad to another, we will come to be all different in species and
concordant in genus and number and case.

[[speaker: Filoteo]]
Diverse, therefore, are the species and manners of censures, various
are their grades, but the most harsh, hard, horrible, and frightful are those of
our arch-teachers. Therefore to these we must bend the knees, bow the head,
turn the eyes and raise the hands, sigh, weep, exclaim, and ask mercy. To you,
then, I turn, O you who bear in hand the caduceus of Mercury to decide in
controversies and determine the questions that occur among mortals and among
the gods; to you, Menippuses, who, seated on the globe of the moon, look at us
with turned and lowered eyes, holding our gestures in disgust and disdain; to
you, shield-bearers of Pallas, standard-bearers of Minerva, stewards of
Mercury, magnates of Jove, milk-brothers of Apollo, hand-servants of Epimetheus,
cupbearers of Bacchus, grooms of the Evantes, scourgers of the Edonides,
drivers of the Thyads, stirrers of the Maenads, suborners of the Bassarids,
riders of the Mimallonides, concubinaries of the nymph Egeria, correctors of
enthusiasm, demagogues of the wandering people, decipherers of Demogorgon,
Dioscuri of the fluctuating disciplines, treasurers of the Pantamorph, and
scapegoats of the high priest Aaron; to you we commend our prose, we submit our
Muses, premises, subsumptions, digressions, parentheses, applications, clauses,
periods, constructions, adjectivations, epithetings. O you sweetest little
water-carriers, who with pretty little elegances steal our spirit, bind our
heart, fascinate our mind, and put our little harlot souls into the brothel:
bring our barbarisms back to good counsel, prick our solecisms, plug the
ill-smelling chasms, castrate our Sileni, bridle our Noems, make eunuchs of our
long-winded speeches, patch our eclipses, curb our tautologies, moderate our
acrilogies, forgive our foul expressions, excuse our redundancies, pardon our
ill-headed sayings. I return to conjuring you all in general, and in particular
you, severe, supercilious, and most savage master Polihimnio, that you dismiss
that contumacious rage and that so criminal hatred against the most noble female
sex, and do not trouble for us whatever beauty the world has and heaven with
its many eyes beholds. Return, return to yourselves, and recall your intellect,
so that you may see that this livor of yours is nothing other than express mania
and frenzied fury. Who is more senseless and stupid than the one who does not
see the light? What madness can be more abject than, by reason of sex, to be an
enemy to nature itself, like that barbarous king of Sarza who, because he had
learned from you, said:

Nature cannot make a perfect thing

Since Nature is called female.

Consider the truth somewhat; raise your eye to the dawn of the science of good
and evil; see the contrariety and opposition that are between the one and the
other. Look at who are the males, who are the females. Here you see as subject
the body, which is your friend, male; there the soul, which is your enemy,
female. Here male chaos, there female disposition; here sleep, there waking;
here lethargy, there memory; here hatred, there friendship; here fear, there
safety; here rigor, there gentleness; here scandal, there peace; here fury,
there quiet; here error, there truth; here defect, there perfection; here hell,
there happiness; here Polihimnio the pedant, there Polyhymnia the Muse. And
finally all vices, lacks, and crimes are male, and all virtues, excellences, and
goodnesses are female. Hence prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance, beauty,
majesty, dignity, divinity, are so named, so imagined, so described, so painted,
so they are. And to go out from these theoretical, notional, and grammatical
reasons, fitting to your argument, and come to the natural, real, and practical:
should this single example not suffice to tie your tongue and plug your mouth,
which will confound you with however many others are your companions, if it had
to send you to find a male better than or similar to this Diva Elizabeth who
reigns in England, who, because she is so endowed, exalted, favored, defended,
and maintained by the heavens, others' words or forces will strive in vain to
bring down? To this lady, I say, than whom there is none more worthy in the
whole kingdom, none more heroic among the nobles, none more learned among the
robed, none wiser among the counselors. In comparison with her, as much for
bodily beauty, as for knowledge of languages among common and learned people,
as for notice of the sciences and arts, as for prudence in governing, as for
the felicity of great and long authority, as for all other civil and natural
virtues, most vile are the Sophonisbas, the Faustinas, the Semiramises, the
Didos, the Cleopatras, and all the others of whom Italy, Greece, Egypt, and
other parts of Europe and Asia could glory for past times. Witnesses for me are
the effects and the fortunate success that the present age beholds not without
noble marvel: when on the back of Europe the Tiber runs angry, the Po
threatening, the Rhone violent, the Seine bloody, the Garonne turbid, the Ebro
rabid, the Tagus furious, the Meuse troubled, the Danube restless, she with the
splendor of her eyes for five lustra and more has made the great Ocean
tranquil, which with continuous reflux and flow, happy and quiet, receives in
its wide bosom its beloved Thames, which, beyond every fear and trouble, safe
and glad, strolls while it winds and winds again along the grassy banks. Now
then, to begin from the beginning, what...

[[speaker: Armesso]]
Be silent, be silent, Filoteo; do not force yourself to add water to
our Ocean and light to our sun. Leave off showing yourself abstracted, not to
say worse, by disputing with absent Polihimnios. Give us some copy of these
present dialogues, so that we do not spend this day and these hours idly.

[[speaker: Filoteo]]
Take them, read.
