# Dialogo Secondo, Cause, Principle, and Universal Intellect

DIALOGUE TWO

INTERLOCUTORS

Dicsono Arelio, Teofilo, Gervasio, Polihimnio.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Please, master Polihimnio, and you, Gervasio, do not interrupt
our discourses further.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
Let it be done.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
If this fellow, who is the magister, speaks, without doubt I cannot
be silent.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
So you say, Teofilo, that every thing that is not first
principle and first cause has principle and has cause?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Without doubt and without any controversy.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Do you believe, for this reason, that whoever knows caused and
principiated things knows the cause and principle?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Not easily the proximate cause and proximate principle; most
difficultly, even in vestige, the first cause and principle.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Now how do you understand that things which have first and
proximate cause and principle are truly known, if, according to the reason of
the efficient cause, which is one of those that concur in the real knowledge of
things, they are hidden?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
I leave aside that it is an easy thing to arrange demonstrative
doctrine, but demonstrating is difficult. It is a very easy thing to arrange the
causes, circumstances, and methods of doctrines; but then our methodicians and
analysts poorly put into execution their organs, principles of methods, and arts
of the arts.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
Like those who know how to make such fine swords but do not know how
to use them.

[[speaker: Polihimnio]]
Stop.

[[speaker: Gervasio]]
May your eyes be stopped, so that you never can open them.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
I say, however, that it is not required of the natural philosopher to
bring forward all causes and principles, but only the physical ones, and of
these the principal and proper ones. Although, therefore, because they depend on
the first principle and cause, they are said to have that cause and that
principle, nevertheless the relation is not so necessary that from knowledge of
the one knowledge of the other is inferred. And therefore it is not required
that they be ordered in one same discipline.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
How is this?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Because from knowledge of all dependent things we cannot infer any
notice of the first principle and cause except in a mode less effective than
vestige, since the whole derives from his will or goodness, which is the
principle of his operation, from which the universal effect proceeds. The same
can be considered in artificial things: inasmuch as whoever sees the statue does
not see the sculptor; whoever sees the portrait of Helen does not see Apelles,
but sees the effect of the operation that proceeds from the goodness of Apelles'
genius. All this is an effect of the accidents and circumstances of the
substance of that man, who, as to his absolute being, is not known at all.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
So that to know the universe is like knowing nothing of the
being and substance of the first principle, because it is like knowing the
accidents of accidents.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
So it is; but I would not want you to imagine that I understand there
to be accidents in God, or that he can be known as if through his accidents.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
I do not attribute so hard a wit to you; and I know that one
thing is to say they are accidents, another that they are his accidents, another
that every thing estranged from the divine nature is as if his accidents. I
believe that in the last mode of speaking you understand the effects of divine
operation to be such; although they are the substance of things, indeed the
natural substances themselves, nevertheless they are like most remote accidents
for making us touch the apprehensive knowledge of the divine supernatural
essence.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
You speak well.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Here, then, since the divine substance is infinite and since it
is very far from those effects that are the last limit of the course of our
discursive faculty, we can know nothing of it except in the mode of vestige, as
the Platonists say; of remote effect, as the Peripatetics say; of garments, as
the cabalists say; of shoulders or posterior parts, as the Talmudists say; of
mirror, shadow, and enigma, as the apocalyptics say.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
More than that: because we do not perfectly see this universe, whose
substance and principal part is so difficult to comprehend, it happens that with
much less reason do we know the first principle and cause through its effect
than Apelles could be known through his formed statues. For we can see these
all and examine them part by part, but not the great and infinite effect of
divine power. Therefore that similitude must be understood without proportional
comparison.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
So it is, and so I understand it.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
It will therefore be well to abstain from speaking of so high a
matter.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
I consent to this, because it is enough morally and
theologically to know the first principle insofar as the supernal powers have
revealed and divine men have declared it. Besides, not only any law and
theology whatever, but also all reformed philosophies conclude that it is the
thing of a profane and turbulent spirit to wish to throw itself into asking the
reason and wishing to define concerning those things that are above the sphere
of our intelligence.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Good. But those are not so much worthy of reproof as those are most
worthy of praise who strive toward the knowledge of this principle and cause,
in order to apprehend its greatness as much as will be possible by discoursing
with the eyes of regulated senses around these magnificent stars and shining
bodies. These are so many inhabited worlds, great animals, and most excellent
divine powers, which seem and are innumerable worlds not very dissimilar from
this one that contains us; and since it is impossible that they have being from
themselves, seeing that they are composed and dissoluble, although for that
reason they are not worthy of being dissolved, as was well said in the Timaeus,
it is necessary that they know principle and cause, and consequently with the
greatness of their being, living, and operating they show and proclaim in one
infinite space, with innumerable voices, the infinite excellence and majesty of
their first principle and cause. Leaving aside, then, as you say, that
consideration insofar as it is superior to every sense and intellect, let us
consider the principle and cause insofar as, in vestige, it either is nature
itself or shines within the ambit and womb of nature. You, then, ask me in
order, if you wish me to answer you in order.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
So I will do. But first, because you customarily say cause and
principle, I would like to know whether these are taken by you as synonymous
names.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
No.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Now, then, what difference is there between the one and the
other term?

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
I answer that when we call God first principle and first cause, we
understand one same thing under diverse reasons. When we speak in nature of
principles and causes, we speak of diverse things under their diverse reasons.
We call God first principle insofar as all things are after him, according to a
certain order of prior and posterior, either according to nature, or according
to duration, or according to dignity. We call God first cause insofar as all
things are distinct from him as the effect from the efficient, the thing
produced from the producer. And these two reasons are different, because not
everything that is prior and more worthy is cause of what is posterior and less
worthy; and not everything that is cause is prior and more worthy than what is
caused, as is very clear to one who discourses well.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Now say, in a natural proposition, what difference there is
between cause and principle.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Although at times the one is usurped for the other, nevertheless,
speaking properly, not every thing that is principle is cause, because the point
is principle of the line, but is not its cause; the instant is principle of the
operation; the terminus from which is principle of motion and not cause of
motion; the premises are principle of argumentation, but are not its cause.
Therefore principle is a more general term than cause.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Therefore, narrowing these two terms to certain proper
significations, according to the custom of those who speak more reformately, I
believe you wish principle to be what intrinsically concurs in the constitution
of the thing and remains in the effect, as they say matter and form do, which
remain in the composite, or else the elements from which the thing comes to be
composed and into which it goes to be resolved. You call cause that which
concurs in the production of things exteriorly and has being outside the
composition, as is the efficient and the end to which the produced thing is
ordered.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
Very well.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
Now, since we are resolved concerning the difference of these
things, I first desire that you report your intention concerning causes, and
then concerning principles. And as to causes, I would first like to know of the
first efficient; of the formal cause that you say is joined to the efficient;
and further, of the final cause, which is understood as mover of this.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
I very much like your order of proposing. Now, as to the effecting
cause, I say that the universal physical efficient is the universal intellect,
which is the first and principal faculty of the soul of the world, which is its
universal form.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
You seem to me not so much conformable to the opinion of
Empedocles as more secure, more distinct, and more explicated; besides, as far
as the above has made me see, more profound. Therefore you will do us a welcome
thing by coming to a declaration of the whole in detail, beginning by saying
what this universal intellect is.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
The universal intellect is the intimate, most real, and proper faculty
and potential part of the soul of the world. This is one and the same, which
fills the whole, illuminates the universe, and directs nature to produce its
species as is fitting; and thus it has regard to the production of natural
things as our intellect has to the congruent production of rational species.
This is called by the Pythagoreans mover and agitator of the universe, as the
Poet explained when he said, "And mind, infused through the limbs, stirs the
whole mass and mingles itself with the whole body." This is named by the
Platonists the craftsman of the world. This craftsman, they say, proceeds from
the superior world, which is altogether one, to this sensible world, which is
divided into many, where not only friendship but also discord reigns because of
the distance of the parts. This intellect, infusing and offering something of
its own into matter, while it keeps itself quiet and immobile, produces the
whole. It is called by the magi most fecund in seeds, or else sower, because it
is the one that impregnates matter with all forms and, according to the reason
and condition of those forms, comes to figure it, form it, and interweave it
with so many marvelous orders, which cannot be attributed to chance, nor to any
other principle that does not know how to distinguish and order. Orpheus calls
it the eye of the world, because it sees within and without all natural things,
so that the whole may come to be produced and maintained not only intrinsically
but also extrinsically in its proper symmetry. By Empedocles it is called the
distinguisher, as that which never tires in explicating the forms confused in
the womb of matter and in stirring up the generation of one thing from the
corruption of another. Plotinus calls it father and progenitor, because it
distributes the seeds in the field of nature and is the proximate dispenser of
forms. By us it is called the internal artisan, because it forms matter and
figures it from within. As from within the seed or root it sends and explicates
the trunk; from within the trunk it drives out the branches; from within the
branches the formed boughs; from within these it unfolds buds; from within it
forms, figures, and interweaves, as with nerves, leaves, flowers, and fruits;
and from within, at certain times, it recalls its humors from the leaves and
fruits to the boughs, from the boughs to the branches, from the branches to the
trunk, from the trunk to the root. Similarly in animals, unfolding its work
first from the seed and from the center of the heart to the external members,
and from those at last folding back toward the heart the unfolded faculties, it
does as if it were already rolling up again the threads already extended. Now,
if we believe that the work, like a dead thing, that we know how to feign with
certain order and imitation on the surface of matter is not produced without
discourse and intellect, when, stripping and chiseling a piece of wood, we make
the image of a horse appear, how much greater must we believe that artisan
intellect to be, which from the intrinsic part of seminal matter joins the
bones, stretches the cartilages, hollows the arteries, breathes into the pores,
interweaves the fibers, branches the nerves, and with such marvelous mastery
disposes the whole? How much greater an artisan, I say, is this, which is not
attached to one single part of matter, but operates continually whole in the
whole? There are three kinds of intellect: the divine, which is all; this
worldly one, which makes all; the other particulars, which become all. For
between the extremes this middle must be found, which is the true efficient
cause, not only extrinsic but also intrinsic, of all natural things.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
I would like to see you distinguish how you understand it as
extrinsic cause and how as intrinsic.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
I call it extrinsic cause because, as efficient, it is not part of the
composites and produced things. It is intrinsic cause insofar as it does not
operate around matter and outside matter, but as was said a little earlier.
Hence it is extrinsic cause through its being distinct from the substance and
essence of the effects, and because its being is not like that of generable and
corruptible things, although it turns around those things; it is intrinsic cause
as to the act of its operation.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
It seems to me that you have spoken enough about the efficient
cause. Now I would like to understand what that is which you wish to be the
formal cause joined to the efficient. Is it perhaps the ideal reason? For every
agent that operates according to intellectual rule does not seek to effect
except according to some intention; and this is not without apprehension of
some thing; and this is nothing other than the form of the thing that is to be
produced. And therefore this intellect, which has the faculty of producing all
species and driving them with such beautiful architecture from the potency of
matter into act, must pre-have them all according to a certain formal reason,
without which the agent could not proceed to its handiwork, just as it is not
possible for the statuary to execute diverse statues without having
forethought diverse forms beforehand.

[[speaker: Teofilo]]
You understand it excellently, because I wish two kinds of forms to be
considered: one which is cause, not indeed efficient, but that through which the
efficient effects; the other is principle, which is stirred up from matter by
the efficient.

[[speaker: Dicsono Arelio]]
The scope, and the final cause that the efficient proposes to
itself, is the perfection of the universe, which is that in diverse parts of
matter all forms have actual existence; in this end the intellect delights and
pleases itself so much that it never tires in stirring up all sorts of forms
from matter, as Empedocles also seems to wish.
