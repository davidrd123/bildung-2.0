# La Rochefoucauld, Maximes

**Triggered by:** Escolios §10, §25, §84, §86, §92, §96

## Why This Encounter

Gómez Dávila never names La Rochefoucauld in the first 108 aphorisms, but his presence is constant. The moralist strand of the Escolios — observations about vanity, self-deception, envy, the mechanisms of pride — is a direct continuation of the *Maximes* (1665). Following the thread back.

## Selected Maximes (French + Attempt)

### Maxime 19

```
Nous avons tous assez de force pour supporter les maux d'autrui.
```

> We all have enough strength to bear the misfortunes of others.

**Connection:** The same acid observation about human sympathy that runs through §56 (socialism as the philosophy of other people's guilt) and §92 (man prefers to excuse himself with another's guilt).

---

### Maxime 138

```
On aime mieux dire du mal de soi-même que de n'en point parler.
```

> We would rather speak ill of ourselves than not speak of ourselves at all.

**Connection:** The vanity-beneath-humility that §87 diagnoses (applying the Beatitudes to yourself proves a disqualifying pride). La Rochefoucauld saw it first: even self-criticism is a form of self-display.

---

### Maxime 218

```
L'hypocrisie est un hommage que le vice rend à la vertu.
```

> Hypocrisy is the tribute that vice pays to virtue.

**Connection:** §88 (after discrediting virtue, this century discredited vice). La Rochefoucauld assumed hypocrisy was permanent — vice would always need to *pretend* to be virtue. Gómez Dávila's observation is that modernity destroyed even this: vice no longer bothers to pay the tribute.

---

### Maxime 298

```
La reconnaissance de la plupart des hommes n'est qu'une secrète envie
de recevoir de plus grands bienfaits.
```

> The gratitude of most men is nothing but a secret desire to receive greater favors.

**Connection:** The mechanism behind §96 (man forgives those who have what he wants, not what he once wanted). La Rochefoucauld's move: every apparently generous sentiment contains a hidden self-interest. Gómez Dávila inherits this but adds a temporal dimension — the *tense* of desire matters.

---

### Maxime 504

```
Le premier mouvement de joie que nous avons du bonheur de nos amis
ne vient ni de la bonté de notre naturel, ni de l'amitié que nous avons pour eux;
c'est un effet de l'amour-propre qui nous flatte de l'espérance d'être heureux à notre tour,
ou de retirer quelque utilité de leur bonne fortune.
```

> The first impulse of joy we feel at a friend's happiness comes neither from the goodness of our nature nor from the friendship we bear him; it is an effect of self-love, which flatters us with the hope of being happy in our turn, or of deriving some advantage from his good fortune.

**Connection:** §10 (we mistake sharing a virtue's defects for sharing the virtue). La Rochefoucauld's longer, more analytical style shows what Gómez Dávila compresses. The *Maximes* explain the mechanism; the *Escolios* state the conclusion and leave you to find the mechanism.

## What French Does Here

La Rochefoucauld's sentences have a *balance* that is native to French classical prose — the parallel clauses, the *ni... ni...*, the final pivot where the real motive is exposed. This balance is a formal enactment of the moral analysis: everything is weighed, measured, and found wanting. English can reproduce the structure but not the *elegance* — in French, the devastating insight and the beautiful sentence are the same thing. In English they tend to separate (you can be witty or you can be precise, but combining them sounds arch).

Gómez Dávila learned this lesson and applied it in Spanish: the aphorism must be *beautiful* as a sentence or it fails as a moral observation. The form carries the authority. An ugly truth stated in an ugly sentence has less force than a beautiful lie.
