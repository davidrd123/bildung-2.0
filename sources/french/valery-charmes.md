## Source

From "Le Sylphe" in *Charmes* (1922). The full poem is short — a sylph (air spirit) speaks, describing its own fleeting, barely-perceptible existence.

## Notes

- *Aux meilleurs esprits / Que d'erreurs promises!* — "To the finest minds, what promised errors!" The sylph *promises* errors — its gift to intelligence is misdirection. The best minds are the most susceptible because they pursue the glimpse.
- *Ni vu ni connu* — "neither seen nor known." French idiom: unnoticed, undetected. The sylph is invisible.
- *Le temps d'un sein nu / Entre deux chemises* — "the time of a bare breast between two shifts." The duration of the glimpse: the instant when a woman changes her shirt and a breast is visible. That's how long truth (or the sylph, or beauty) is available. Not wrong — just unbelievably brief.
- *chemises* — shifts, undershirts. Not "shirts" in the modern sense. The intimate, domestic garment.

## Why Gómez Dávila Chose This Epigraph

The Valéry stanza is about the *form* of the Escolios: truth as a flash between two concealments. Each aphorism is a bare breast between two shirts — a moment of exposure so brief it can barely be registered. The "promised errors" are what the reader gets instead: the mind chases the glimpse and grabs the wrong thing. Only the finest minds are even susceptible to the chase, and even they are mostly wrong.

This is also a defense of brevity. The aphorism doesn't explain because truth doesn't linger. It flashes and is gone. If you saw it, you saw it. If you didn't, no amount of elaboration will reproduce the instant.

## What French Does Here

The rhyme scheme (esprits/promises, connu/nu, chemises as closing) gives the stanza a lightness that the content doesn't warrant. French verse can be simultaneously playful and devastating in a way that English light verse rarely manages — English light verse tends toward pure comedy (Byron, Ogden Nash), while French *vers léger* can carry metaphysical weight without losing its airiness. The *ni vu ni connu* is an idiom — colloquial, almost shrugging — placed next to an image of extraordinary erotic and philosophical precision. That tonal mixture is very hard to reproduce.
