## What VII.165 Is About

Book VII of the *Lives* covers the Stoics. Section 165 is near the end of the life of Chrysippus. The most likely quotation, given Gómez Dávila's purpose, is about the *form* of Chrysippus's writing — Diogenes Laertius reports that Chrysippus was so prolific and so dense that his works were full of other people's quotations, sometimes to the point that an entire book was made up of borrowed passages.

The passage often cited:

```greek
ἀρτύοντα μὲν δυνάμεως, δὲ μεστά
```

This doesn't quite match the OCR garble, but the general area is right. **Needs verification against the PDF scan (pp. 9-10 of the original edition).**

## Why Gómez Dávila Chose This Epigraph

Whatever the exact text, the placement alongside Shakespeare ("A hand, a foot, a leg, a head / Stood for the whole to be imagined") and Valéry (the flash of a bare breast between two shifts) establishes the thematic frame: **the fragment standing for the whole**. If the Diogenes Laertius passage is about Chrysippus's method of composing from borrowed fragments, it's a mirror of the *Escolios* method: scholia (marginal notes) to an implicit text, composed from the accumulated reading of a lifetime.

## What Greek Would Do Here

Even without the restored text, the *presence* of Greek in the epigraphs is significant. Gómez Dávila's six epigraphs cycle through French (Flaubert), Spanish (Cervantes), Greek (Diogenes Laertius), English (Shakespeare), French again (Valéry), German (Nietzsche), and Latin (Petrarch). Six languages for one book. The implicit claim: this work belongs to no single national tradition. The tradition is the conversation across languages, and the *Escolios* are the latest voice in it.

Greek is the originating language of that conversation — philosophy begins in Greek. Placing a Greek epigraph among the others is a genealogical claim: this book descends from the Greeks, through Latin, into the modern vernaculars.
