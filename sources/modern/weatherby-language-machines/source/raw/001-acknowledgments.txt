Acknowledgments

This is a book that belongs to the Digital Theory Lab, without which I
would never have strung together the words and concepts that follow in
anything like this way. Lisa Gitelman, Cliff Siskin, Alex Galloway, Joe
Lemelin, David Bering-Porter, Ryan Healey, Claire Song, Sam Kellogg,
Emma Rae Bruml Norton, and Alijan Ozkiral make up its New York con-
tingent; Beatrice Fazi, Seb Franklin, Mercedes Bunz, Richard Muller,
Audrey Borowski, and many others have joined from abroad virtually
or in person. I always say that the Lab is a space for collaborative cre-
ation of knowledge, and this book is my first attempt to demonstrate
how deep that runs. The words are mine, but they are the tip of an
iceberg that is made up of thinking together. A few members of the Lab
have read as I have written, and challenged and encouraged me along
the way. Tyler Shoemaker has been absurdly generous with his vast
technical expertise and wit. Arne Sander dedicated great enthusiasm
to every scale of the manuscript in preparation, going far beyond the
call. Jeffrey K irkwood knows what I will write before I write it—may
we always argue over the semio-in the technics. Without Matthew
Handelman and Sarah Pourciau, neither the ideas nor the act of writing
would have been possible—there is no way to name what I owe you both.
Maya: mind is embedded in common life—thank you for ours. Without
my family, all of it, nothing like what I do would be thinkable. When I
wrote my book on German Romanticism, I often thought that Novalis
and Schlegel’s notion of a “sym-philosophy,” the joining of minds in the
critical enterprise, was sentimental. But now I am the sentimental one,
because the overwhelming intellectual friendship of all these people
exceeds anything I could have asked for or deserved. This book is for all
of you.
