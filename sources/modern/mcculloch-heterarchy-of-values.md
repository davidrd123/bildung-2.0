# McCulloch: A Heterarchy of Values

**Source**: Warren S. McCulloch, "A Heterarchy of Values Determined by the Topology of Nervous Nets," *Bulletin of Mathematical Biophysics* 7 (1945), pp. 89-93. This encounter covers the article itself; the appended letter in the PDF is noted but not used as core evidence.
**Status**: open
**Triggered by**: the April 3 Cassirer/Uexkuell/Bogdanov discussion around hierarchy vs. heterarchy; the formal-systems gap identified in the Erkenntnisproblem reading notes; the question whether cross-domain order must be scalar

## Overview

Four pages. McCulloch is not offering a general philosophy of value, still less a theory of knowledge. He is making one bounded formal claim: if values can be placed on a single ordered scale, the corresponding nervous net maps on a plane; if preference is circular, the anomaly is not merely irrational confusion but evidence of a different topology. The smallest interesting case already exceeds one scale.

What the paper establishes securely:

- hierarchy of values means values are "magnitudes of some one kind"
- circular preference forces a transverse loop in the net, a `diadrome`
- the simplest surface for that net is a torus, not a plane
- the anomaly shows "consistency of a higher order," not mere defect

What it does **not** yet establish:

- a general critique of Cassirer
- a theory of symbolic constitution
- an Anders-style critique of technological optimization
- an identity between McCulloch's torus and von Foerster's torus

---

## Passage 1: Hierarchy Requires a Single Scale

### Formal

McCulloch's starting claim is exact: to assert a hierarchy of values is to assert that values are "magnitudes of some one kind." A hierarchy is not just a plurality of ends. It is a transitive ordering in which dominance runs cleanly from higher to lower, and the corresponding net can be mapped without crossovers on a plane.

### Prose

This matters because it turns a philosophical assumption into a structural criterion. If values really are scalar, then the nervous system that enacts the choice can be organized as a planar ordering of closed purposive circuits. If that fails empirically, the problem is not first of all moral or psychological. It is topological.

### Diagrammatic

```text
Hierarchy / plane

A > B > C

closed purposive circuits:
  A inhibits B
  B inhibits C
  A inhibits C

No crossover required.
One ordered scale suffices.
```

### Project pressure

This is the paper's cleanest contribution to the project. It gives a formal test for when an ordering is genuinely scalar instead of merely treated as though it were. That pressure is relevant to any project note that starts speaking too quickly of "higher" and "lower" orders as though one ladder were already given.

## Passage 2: Circular Preference and the Diadrome

### Formal

McCulloch then takes the simplest anomaly: `A > B`, `B > C`, `C > A`. The net now contains a loop formed by the heterodromic connections themselves, transverse to the purposive circuits. He names this transverse loop a `diadrome`. The simplest surface on which the net maps without a crossover is no longer a plane but a torus.

### Prose

The important move is that circularity is relocated from the level of "bad judgment" to the level of structure. The anomaly is not a failed hierarchy. It is evidence that the system never belonged to one scale in the first place. The nervous net has a richer order than the theory of value assumed.

### Diagrammatic

```text
Heterarchy / torus

A > B
B > C
C > A

The dromes remain closed purposive circuits.
But the heterodromic links form an additional transverse loop:

  A -> B -> C -> A

That extra loop cannot be flattened into one scale without a crossover.
```

### Project pressure

Bogdanov is the nearest bridge here. McCulloch is not yet giving a general organizational science, but he is showing how a cross-domain structural invariant might look: not a common content, but a recurrent topology. This is exactly the kind of move the project needs if it is going to compare domains without flattening them into one substance or one ladder.

---

## Passage 3: Higher-Order Consistency

### Formal

McCulloch's conclusion is the line that matters most: circular preferences show "consistency of a higher order." The organism is not inconsistent in the ordinary sense. It is "too rich to submit to a summum bonum."

### Prose

This is as far as the paper securely goes. It does not say all order is heterarchical. It says that some systems are sufficiently rich that a single highest good, a single scale, or a single rank-order of ends will misdescribe them. Heterarchy is not chaos. It is ordered richness that defeats scalar reduction.

### Project pressure

This is where the paper begins to lean on the live work, but still only as pressure:

- `Cassirer`: it cautions against reading the history of knowledge as though one single scale of adequation were obviously available. It does **not** refute Cassirer's directional history by itself.
- `Uexkuell`: it raises the question whether multiple functional circles can interfere heterarchically rather than nesting cleanly. It does **not** show that they do.
- `von Foerster`: it provides a toroidal anti-reduction image adjacent to recursive closure. It does **not** establish that the two tori are the same formal object.

### What no single register captures

McCulloch gives the project something unusually sharp: a way to treat anti-hierarchy as a formal discovery rather than a romantic preference. But the paper is tiny. It shows that a richer order can exist than scalar value theory admits; it does not yet tell you how such an order is lived, symbolized, historicized, or ethically inhabited.

## Open Questions

### Where should first validation happen?

The best candidate is probably Bogdanov rather than Cassirer. McCulloch's contribution is structural and topological, which aligns more closely with organizational comparison than with Cassirer's historical genealogy of concepts. **Working assessment: Bogdanov is the first serious validation seam; Cassirer and Uexkuell remain transfer tests.**

### Does heterarchy transfer from value-choice to knowledge-history?

Not yet earned. The temptation is obvious: if nervous valuation is not scalar, maybe the history of knowledge is not scalar either. But McCulloch's paper is about interlocked purposive circuits, not epochs, symbolic forms, or conceptual adequation. **Working assessment: real pressure, no license yet.**

### Is McCulloch's torus the same as von Foerster's torus?

Probably not in any strict sense. Both figures mark non-planar order and closure, but McCulloch's torus is a topological surface needed to map interacting circuits without crossovers, whereas von Foerster's torus names recursive closure of cognition operating on its own operators. **Working assessment: structural adjacency only, not identity.**

### Why status remains open

Primary reading is complete enough for a bounded encounter, but no translation tranche has yet reached for McCulloch and changed how a passage was read. Until that happens, this file remains a disciplined reserve of formal pressure rather than a confirmed interpretive instrument.
