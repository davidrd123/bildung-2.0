## Decision

Continue Weyl source-local. Do not promote to `texts/` yet. Do not pause.

Chapter I has earned continuation because it is not merely introductory
mathematical housekeeping. It supplies a compact philosophical machine:
relation over subject-predicate judgment, definition as object-forming act,
proof as constructive action, axiomatics as structural determination, and pure
mathematics as the theory of logical empty forms with possible contentful
interpretations.

Promotion should wait until chapter II, `Zahl und Kontinuum. Das Unendliche`,
has been worked at least through a representative batch. The introduction
promised that the book moves from the formal surface toward the substantive
problematic of the infinite; chapter I establishes the formal apparatus, but
chapter II is where the stated philosophical pressure should become decisive.

## What Chapter I Established

- Judgment is relational before it is grammatical. Weyl's `Leerstelle` breaks
  the subject-copula-predicate default and gives a working syntax for relations.
- Mathematical definition is not just abbreviation. The `schöpferische` or
  `aufbauende` definition creates ideal objects by specifying how they are given
  and how identity is tested.
- Quantification destroys direct finite survey. With `alle` and `es gibt`,
  proof becomes stepwise construction rather than truth-table inspection.
- Axiomatics is a control discipline for bold construction. Because theory is
  not a copy of the given, consistency becomes a central mathematical test.
- Completeness cannot mean mechanical decidability without trivializing
  mathematics. Weyl's viable sense is categoricity: uniqueness up to isomorphism.
- Isomorphism is the epistemological hinge. Science determines structure, not
  the inner essence of objects.
- Pure mathematics becomes a general hypothetical-deductive theory of relations:
  logical empty forms plus possible contentful interpretations.

## Project Relevance

Weyl is useful for the repo because he gives a technically serious bridge
between mathematics, epistemology, and philosophy of natural science without
collapsing one into the other. He is especially relevant to any thread about
structure, formalization, interpretation, and the limits of scientific
knowledge.

The strongest harvested claim so far:

> A science determines its domain only up to isomorphic mapping; it knows
> structure, not the inner essence of objects.

That claim should be watched against later material on space-time, measurement,
theory formation, and the external world.
