## Decision

Weyl now earns `texts/` promotion in principle. Do not pause.

The reason is not that the source-local work is finished. It is that chapter II
confirms the central philosophical pressure promised by chapter I: the infinite
and the continuum force mathematics to choose among objectification, intuition,
and symbolic construction, and none of those choices is merely technical.

Promotion should be minimal and pressure-led. The promoted track should not
attempt a complete edition of the book yet. It should start from the core
chapter-II dossier:

- mathematics as the science of the infinite
- the continuum as the test case for objectification
- set theory as naive realism
- Brouwer as idealist return to intuition/construction
- Hilbert as symbolic presentation of the transcendent
- theory formation as symbolic discipline rather than mystical possession

Source-local work should continue after promotion, especially into the physics
chapters, because chapter II explicitly defers the question of what determines
theoretical formation beyond mere consistency until the discussion of physics.

## What Chapter II Established

- Fractions, negative numbers, and complex numbers are not naive things but
  ideal elements introduced by creative definition to preserve formal laws.
- Natural numbers are safe only while the open possibility of continuation is
  not objectified into a completed aggregate.
- The continuum is the chapter's hard case: atomistic, infinitesimal,
  set-theoretic, intuitionistic, and formalist accounts each expose a different
  failure mode.
- Set theory gives analysis rigor and unanimity but pays by treating possible
  sequences, subsets, and properties as completed domains of objects existing in
  themselves.
- Russell's type-theory pressure shows that unrestricted objectification of
  properties generates vicious circularity.
- Brouwer restores intuitive clarity by refusing the leap into the completed
  infinite, but at the cost of making higher mathematics painfully cumbersome.
- Hilbert secures the old analysis by turning mathematics and logic into a
  formal sign game; what is secured is consistency, not contentful truth.
- Metamathematics still depends on finite, contentful reasoning. Even formalism
  does not eliminate intuition; it relocates it to the proof of consistency.
- The final section classifies the foundational options epistemologically:
  set-theoretic foundation is naive realism, Brouwer is idealism, and axiomatic
  formalism is symbolic presentation of the transcendent.
- The chapter's closing formula is `Wissenschaft vom Unendlichen`: mathematics
  makes the finite-infinite tension fruitful for knowledge of reality.

## Project Relevance

Chapter II is highly relevant to the repo's core work on structure, symbol,
interpretation, and whole knowing. It gives a technically serious account of
why formal systems cannot be dismissed as empty manipulation, but also cannot be
allowed to masquerade as direct possession of truth.

The strongest harvested claim:

> The theoretical need for totality is real, but it can be satisfied only in
> symbol; the transcendent must not be mistaken for something directly seen.

That claim connects directly to current repo threads around formalization,
semiotics, scientific models, and the difference between lived/intuitive
knowledge and theoretical construction.

This would preserve the source-local provenance while giving the repo a stable
place to use Weyl as more than a reading note.
