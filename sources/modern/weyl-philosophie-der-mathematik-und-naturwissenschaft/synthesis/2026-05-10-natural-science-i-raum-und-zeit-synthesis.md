## Decision

Natural science part I should modify the promoted Weyl dossier, but not yet by
creating a standalone promoted natural-science object.

Chapter II established symbolic access to totality. Chapter III established
objectivity through invariance, transformation groups, and geometrical
structure. Sections 16-18 show what this becomes once geometry enters physics:
symbolic construction earns reality-contact only through physical coupling,
possible experimental return, and its coordination with lived givenness.

The promoted chapter-II dossier therefore remains the right starting core, but
it now needs a stronger pressure note: symbolic construction is not only a
discipline for handling the infinite. In natural science it is also the only
way to build an objective world-picture from subjective-absolute givenness
without pretending that the world-picture is itself directly intuited.

## What Sections 16-18 Established

- Section 16 physicalizes geometry. Space-time structure is not a ready
  container; it is read from clocks, rigid bodies, light signals, inertial
  motion, field equations, and possible experimental interventions.
- The metric/inertial structure becomes a `Führungsfeld`, a guiding or metric
  field that acts on matter and receives reaction from matter. Geometry and
  physics meet through field-reality, not through pure formal analogy.
- Special relativity replaces old simultaneity structure with light-cone
  causal structure. Active future and passive past are not merely formal
  regions; they articulate the acting and suffering subject's possible
  relation to world-occurrence.
- General relativity shifts the metric from fixed background to mutable
  physical field. The Lorentz group and local frame structure revise but do
  not erase the invariance criterion from chapter III.
- Section 17 gives the epistemological payoff: immediate lived givenness is
  subjective-absolute, while the objective world-picture is coordinate-relative
  and symbolic. The objective is not less real because it is relative; it is
  objective by invariant convertibility across coordinate choices.
- Objective theory is constrained by an anti-surplus rule. A theoretical
  addition that can make no possible difference in givenness is not merely
  unobserved; it is epistemologically idle.
- The subject/object split is not solved by eliminating the subject. The
  coordinate system is the objective analogue of the I: indispensable for
  representation, never absolute in itself.
- Section 18 returns to the constitution of space from the side of givenness:
  visual field, eye movement, binocular depth, body-I, centered space, and
  homogeneous intersubjective space.
- `Anschauungsraum` has local clarity but cannot dictate physical geometry.
  Weyl's tangent-plane analogy prevents any simple return to Kantian Euclidean
  intuition.
- Physical geometry is an ideal network. Its individual determinations need
  not each be directly exhibited in givenness; the network as a whole must
  touch lived reality at possible experimental contact points and "fit" there.
- General relativity does not abolish the `a priori`; it moves the boundary.
  Aprioric metric nature, topology, and dimensionality stand against the
  matter-dependent quantitative course of the metric field.
- The old twofold `a priori / a posteriori` opposition becomes a threefold
  gradation: aprioric structure, law-bound empirical structure, and contingent
  factual course.

## Modification To The Promoted Weyl Dossier

The existing promoted pressure should now be stated as:

> Symbolic construction gives access to what cannot be directly intuited, but
> in natural science it earns objectivity only through invariance, physical
> coupling, and possible return to subjective-absolute givenness.

This modifies the chapter-II thread in three ways:

- Hilbert-style symbolism is no longer enough by itself. Physics adds the
  demand that symbolic structure be coupled to possible measurement and
  perception.
- Invariance is not merely formal. It mediates between arbitrary symbolic
  coordinates and objective relations capable of return to experience.
- Intuition is not discarded. It is localized: immediate givenness, centered
  space, and intuitive space remain necessary contact-points, but they cannot
  legislate global physical structure.

## Relation To Chapter III

Chapter III's geometry campaign asked how objectivity arises from
transformation groups and invariant structure. Natural science part I answers:
objectivity becomes physical when invariant structure is operationally tied to
fields, clocks, rods, light, and possible perception.

This is not a reduction of physics to geometry. Weyl keeps a strict difference
between mathematical possibility and physical actuality. Formalizing
mathematics opens possible structures; physical theory must explain why actual
world-structure realizes this one rather than another.

The decisive example is dimensionality. Mathematics makes `n`-dimensional
geometries available. Physics must ask why actual space-time has its observed
signature and dimensionality: causal structure, wave propagation, gauge
invariance, and rotation-group structure become candidate reasons.
