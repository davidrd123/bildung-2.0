## Decision

Natural science part II should modify the promoted Weyl dossier, but still as
pressure on the existing promoted object rather than as a final natural-science
dossier.

Sections 19-21 show how symbolic construction becomes responsible to reality.
Measurement supplies exact symbolic access; concept-formation explains how the
objects, traits, and hidden elements to be measured are won; theory-formation
shows that the whole system, not isolated propositions, is the unit tested by
experience.

The central addition to the promoted Weyl object is therefore methodological:
symbolic construction is not free formal invention. In physics it must be
experiment-coupled, concept-generating, anti-surplus, internally concordant,
and historically correctable as a whole.

## What Sections 19-21 Established

- Section 19 turns measurement into a symbolic operation requiring equality,
  order, additivity, reproducible units, and in hard cases implicit laws of
  nature. Measurement is already theory-laden without becoming arbitrary.
- Exactness does not come from sensory sharpness. It comes from mathematical
  structure, measurement-foundations, and the reduction of observations to
  space-time coincidences.
- Section 20 shows that physical concepts are made, not merely named.
  Scientific concepts arise through analysis of nature, experimental reaction,
  hidden traits, and lawful decomposition and recomposition.
- Mass, momentum, force, field, charge, and spectrum are not passively read
  from intuition. They are constructive concepts disciplined by experiment and
  by their role in laws.
- Functional concept-formation reconnects physics to the mathematical
  chapters. Modern physical concepts generate possible cases in open
  determination fields rather than classifying actual objects by subtracting
  traits.
- Section 21 makes the theory-system the unit of verification. A single law
  torn from the theory cannot be checked in isolation; only the whole nexus of
  concepts, geometry, laws, and correction procedures meets experiment.
- The Kantian sharp opposition between a priori and a posteriori becomes a
  scale of firmness. Principles are the most resilient, simple, evident, and
  far-reaching laws within an evolving theoretical system.
- Simplicity is not bare economy. Nature must educate us toward inner
  simplicity through historical correction, widening experience, and
  mathematically prepared function-classes.
- The principle of sufficient reason, the continuity principle, and the
  analogy principle are legitimate guides only when disciplined by experience.
  Leibniz supplies powerful tools and also the warning against overestimating
  them as sources of factual truth.
- Theory aims at prediction, but the trust that induction and theory will
  continue to reach the world cannot itself be justified without circularity.
  Weyl names the basic stance `Weltvertrauen` and `Selbstvertrauen`.

## Methodological Shape

Part II has a three-stage architecture.

First, measurement translates reality into exact symbolic determinations. This
requires operations and foundations that exceed direct perception, but it must
return to possible observable coincidences.

Second, concept-formation determines what is to be measured. It isolates
simple recurring elements, posits hidden theoretical traits, and uses
experimental reactions to make those traits determinate. The concepts are
constructive but law-bound.

Third, theory-formation binds measurements and concepts into a corrigible
whole. The system must be concordant, non-dogmatic, simple in a historically
educated sense, and open to correction by experience. The whole theory, not an
isolated sentence, earns empirical standing.

## Relation To Natural Science I

Natural science part I established that the objective world-picture is
symbolic, invariant, physically coupled, and tied back to subjective-absolute
givenness. Part II supplies the working method by which that world-picture is
built.

The methodological sections make the geometry-to-physics bridge stricter.
Geometry participates in empirical theory, but it is not checked as an
isolated formal object. It belongs to the theoretical whole that measurement,
concept-formation, and correction put under experimental pressure.

The metric field example is especially important. It shows that not every
theoretical element can be screened off as an external error source. Some
elements belong to the very structure through which the experiment becomes
interpretable.

## Modification To The Promoted Weyl Dossier

The promoted dossier should now carry this pressure note:

> Weyl's symbolic construction becomes objective in physics only when it is
> operationally measurable, concept-generating, anti-surplus, and corrigible as
> a whole theory under experience.

This modifies the earlier promoted thread in four ways:

- symbolic access to totality must be joined to measurement-foundations and
  possible space-time coincidences
- formal invariance must be joined to physical concept-formation through
  experiment and reaction
- the a priori is no longer a fixed Kantian table but a graded and revisable
  hierarchy of resilient principles
- theory is justified not by direct intuition of its components but by
  concordant predictive success of the whole construction

## Open Questions For Part III

Part II leaves the next question sharply posed: what kind of world-picture is
produced by this method?

The next section should be read for how Weyl treats substance and causality,
because section 21 explicitly names them as the usable Kantian core. The main
risk is to promote "symbolic construction" too early as if Weyl had already
settled the metaphysics. Part III still has to say what the constructed
physical world is allowed to be.

## Translation State

Sections 19-21 are working encounter translations, not publication
translations. Every active page in the range was visually checked against page
images, and the cleaned German was corrected where OCR damaged quotes,
notation, references, or mathematical symbols.

Remaining risks:

- the exact historical references to Galilei, Huyghens, Mach, and Maxwell are
  source-local and should not be over-read as modern physics claims
- `Einstimmigkeit` should not be flattened into formal consistency; in the
  empirical context it includes agreement with possible perception and the
  whole theoretical system
- `Einfachheit` should not be treated as a simple preference for fewer terms;
  Weyl's simplicity is historically educated by widened experience
- the Kant criticism is not anti-Kantian dismissal; Weyl preserves Kant's
  problem while rejecting Kant's claim to final completion
