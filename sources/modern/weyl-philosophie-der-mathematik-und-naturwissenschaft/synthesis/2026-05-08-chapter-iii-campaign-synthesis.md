## Decision

Chapter III should not be promoted as a separate `texts/` dossier yet. It
should, however, modify the existing chapter-II dossier.

Chapter II established the central Weylian pressure: mathematics needs
symbolic access to totality, but totality must not be mistaken for an object
directly given in intuition. Chapter III shows what happens when that pressure
is put to work in geometry. The relevant standard is no longer only
consistency or constructive clarity. Geometry adds objectivity through
invariance, transformation groups, coordinate frames, and finally physical
fields.

The promoted chapter-II dossier remains correct, but its `Weakening Evidence`
should be sharpened: later Weyl does not abandon symbolic discipline; he embeds
it in a stronger account where theoretical systems earn contact with reality by
their invariance structure and by their role inside physics.

## What Chapter III Established

- Projective and affine geometry show that geometry is not identical with
  Euclidean physical space. Geometry is a structured plurality of possible
  relation systems.
- The color-space discussion matters because geometry applies outside ordinary
  space; intuitive givenness can be geometrically structured wherever the right
  operations and continua appear.
- The relativity problem turns objectivity into invariance under an
  automorphism group. A fact is objective when it survives the relevant
  transformation group.
- Objectification cannot eliminate every residue of situated givenness.
  Coordinates and reference frames remain the symbolic residue of the
  demonstrative act.
- Congruence begins from approximate rigid bodies but becomes a norm by which
  actual bodies are judged. Mathematical idealization is not copied from
  physics; it is abstracted and then returned as a standard.
- Similarity and congruence are clarified by normalizers and invariant
  subgroups. Kant's left/right problem is not dismissed but relocated into
  group-theoretic and combinatorial structure.
- Physics cannot be reduced to geometry. Section 14 makes this explicit through
  the divergence between the physical automorphism group `Δ` and the
  geometrical normalizer `Γ′`.
- Riemann keeps Euclidean geometry in the infinitesimal while making the metric
  field variable. Einstein becomes intelligible only after the metric field is
  treated as a physical magnitude that acts and suffers.
- The local-frame passage introduces a double invariance: arbitrary coordinate
  transformations `Ω` and point-dependent rotations `Δ₀`. The quantities `eᵢᵦ`
  mediate between matter and space.
- Topology gives exact combinatorial purchase on vague actual continua. It is
  the middle discipline between morphology's vague gestalt types and ideal
  geometry's unrealizable exactness.

## Modification To Chapter II

Chapter II's promoted claim was:

> The theoretical need for totality is real, but it can be satisfied only in
> symbol; the transcendent must not be mistaken for something directly seen.

Chapter III does not overturn this. It specifies how symbolic construction
becomes objective in the geometric case:

- The symbolically constructed totality must come with a transformation group.
- Objectivity is tested by invariance, not by mere psychological clarity.
- Coordinates are indispensable but non-absolute; they are the instrument of
  objectification and also the sign of its residue.
- The continuum is not only a foundational problem. It becomes manifold,
  metric field, and topological skeleton depending on the discipline brought to
  bear on it.
- Exact application to reality is never innocent. Geometry requires
  idealization, but topology shows that exact questions can still be asked
  within vague givenness when the alternatives are discrete.

The chapter-II dossier should therefore be read less as a final philosophy of
mathematics and more as the first half of a two-step argument. Chapter II
licenses symbolic access to the infinite; chapter III shows how symbolic
systems become objective by invariance and become physically relevant through
fields and local frames.

## Bridge Into Physics

The bridge into physics is now much sharper.

The strongest bridge is not "geometry applies to nature." Weyl's claim is more
precise: once the metric field is physical, geometry and physics can share the
same differentiable-transformation group. But that sharing depends on a field
that acts on matter and receives reaction from matter. The bridge is therefore
not reduction of physics to geometry; it is the physicalization of geometrical
structure.

This also explains why the next source-local campaign should enter the natural
science part rather than promote chapter III wholesale. The decisive question
now is not whether Weyl has a philosophy of geometry. He does. The live
question is how the physics chapters define the criteria for theoretical
systems once consistency, invariance, and physical coupling all matter.
