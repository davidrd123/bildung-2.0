# Part 030: Appendix F, Physical World, Form, and Development

Appendix F is the closing appendix. It does not simply continue Appendix E's
biology bridge. It widens the frame: physical constants, elementary particles,
crystals, stars, organisms, Darwinism, genetics, origin of life, and teleology
are all placed under one methodological claim. Evolution is not the foundation
of explanation. It is the capstone reached only after morphology, law, and
inner constitution have been worked out.

## Translation / Analytic Digest

Weyl begins by demoting evolution as a first principle. Human beings naturally
ask not only what something is, but how it came to be. Mythology, emanation
schemes, Schelling, Hegel, and Darwinian speculation all show the force of this
question. But science has learned that origin is not the first explanation.
The immanent law and inner constitution of a thing must be known before its
genesis can be reconstructed responsibly.

This demotion is not anti-evolutionary. It is methodological sequencing.
Cosmogony needs gravitation and astrophysics; stellar evolution needs
spectroscopy and atom physics; biology needs morphology, taxonomy, genetics,
chemistry, and physics before phylogeny can be explained. Evolution is the
`Schlußstein`, the capstone, not the ground floor.

Classification is rehabilitated as a serious scientific stage. In biology,
typical differences are often grasped with practical certainty before they can
be fully expressed in ordinary concepts. Comparative morphology determines
homology through form, structure, and function. The Melville whale joke at the
opening is not decorative: it marks the difference between surface analogy and
taxonomic-morphological insight.

Weyl names the primary object of this ordering `morphe`: the pronounced forms,
regularities, sequences, and relatively permanent structures in which science
first finds order. This is why crystals matter. Crystal morphology begins as
classification, but because crystals admit exact symmetry treatment it can be
carried all the way into dynamical explanation.

The physical-constant discussion separates explained constants from remaining
mysteries. Relativity and quantum theory can reduce `c` and `h` to unit-choice
inside the right frameworks. The elementary charge `e`, the fine-structure
factor `α`, the gravitational coupling `ε`, and the relation between `ε` and
the world-particle number `N` remain unexplained. The equality of elementary
particles is partially explained by quantization, but the special numerical
values of charge and mass remain open.

Weyl then contrasts order with persistence. Classical physics can preserve
arbitrary initial charge and mass values by inertia, but elementary particles
need an ordering principle that fixes determinate values and restores them
after disturbance. This is a stronger notion than mere conservation. It is an
alignment or setting into a stable order.

Atoms and molecules are not evolutionary products in the same sense organisms
are. Their possible forms are mathematically predestined by universal laws.
Development enters only through probabilities, temperature, and environmental
conditions that make certain molecular states stable or unstable at particular
epochs. This gives a limited bridge to life: complex gene molecules may become
possible only in a certain phase of Earth's history.

The crystallography section is the formal core of the appendix. A crystal's
visible symmetry group `g` is induced by a hidden atomic movement group `G`.
The translation lattice `L` is generated by independent displacement vectors
`e⃗₁`, `e⃗₂`, `e⃗₃`, and the possible space groups `𝔊` are finite in number.
But a continuous metric manifold remains. Crystal form therefore displays the
basic duality of discrete and continuous, inner fixed structure and variable
outer condition.

That same duality appears in water phases, quantum states, crystals, and
organisms. A feature such as a crystal space group is discrete and
constitutional. A compatible metric is continuous and environmental. A
stationary quantum state is constitutional; a general wave-state ensemble is
not. Temperature is the environmental factor par excellence. Weyl says this
whole problem still needs epistemological clarification.

Living form is where the `nisus formativus` appears most extravagantly. Organic
forms are less rigid than inorganic forms because of colloidal matter, but they
combine elasticity with tenacious self-maintenance. Genetics gives a clear
picture of inner constitution, while environment remains the variable factor.
Appendix F therefore extends Appendix E: life is not outside the
constitution/environment schema, but it intensifies it.

The stellar section completes the physical scale. Stars are organized matter at
the largest level. Spectral lines give a legitimate sense in which one may
speak of the life-stage of a star. Jeans and Lemaître provide speculative
cosmogonies, but Weyl's methodological judgment is clear: the atom-physics
route to stellar constitution and stellar-system evolution is basically right,
even if current cosmology remains provisional.

Organic evolution is then treated with caution. The central biological fact is
not human descent but the deep inner kinship of all living beings: cell
structure, metabolism, chromosome duplication, cell division, meiosis, and
fertilization. The ethical correlate is reverence for life. The metaphysical
lesson is that human special status must be held against biological kinship,
not against it.

The real phylogenetic problem is not that complex organisms exist but that
among the enormous space of possible molecular combinations, rare gene-like
combinations have been found and stabilized. Evolution proceeds by probing
possibilities and using previously conquered positions as bases for further
advance. Weyl admits this is only the faintest pointer toward a solution.

Darwin's theory is then corrected by genetics. Modern genetics replaces
Darwin's continuous hereditary variation with recombination and mutation.
Mendelian meiosis and syngamy generate combinatorial variation; particulate
inheritance prevents the homogenizing collapse that blending inheritance would
produce; mutation and structural mutation create genuinely new possibilities.
Natural selection acts on these systems, but genes seem organized into
genotypic systems whose fitness belongs to the whole system, not isolated gene
parts.

Weyl refuses to equate evolution with progress. A line leading toward viral
parasitic specialization may be real evolution while also being regressive.
Large-scale evolutionary trends, orthogenesis, and secular adaptation remain
unexplained by current genetics. Here the appendix keeps teleological pressure
open without granting teleology an explanatory victory.

The origin of life is the sharpest speculative point. Genetics inclines one to
see life as a lucky hit in a game of creative chances: a special molecular
event occurs once and triggers autocatalytic multiplication. Jordan's
stereochemical argument gives this some force; Oparin's protogene hypothesis
gives a different route through a long prebiotic accumulation of organic
molecules. Weyl treats both as valuable but plainly speculative.

The final pages test the temptation of an evolutionary plan. Willis, Huxley,
Bergson, and Shaw all make the plan-pressure visible. Weyl does not deny that
ideas, images, or `Baupläne` might enter life, especially where thought and
will appear. But he ends with restraint: transcendental creative agents
possessing the nature of ideas may be philosophically interesting, but they do
not currently help solve the concrete problems of biology. We do not know them
as we know our own and others' thoughts through inward interpretation.

Appendix F therefore closes the appendices by disciplining both reduction and
teleology. Physical law is not enough until it has passed through morphology,
constitution, probability, and history. But teleological or vitalist agencies
are not explanations merely because the biological problem is deep. The
project's Weyl rule is: build the immanent structure first; only then ask what
kind of development it makes possible.

## What Appendix F Adds

- It makes evolution methodologically secondary: one explains origin only after
  law and inner constitution are known.
- It elevates morphology and classification as real stages of science, not
  merely immature description.
- It generalizes Appendix E's biology bridge into a world/life schema of
  discrete constitution and continuous environment.
- It makes crystallography the formal model for moving from visible form to
  hidden atomic structure.
- It connects genetics to Darwinism without letting selection explain the
  largest evolutionary directions by itself.
- It keeps origin-of-life and teleology pressures open while denying that
  speculative creative agencies solve concrete biology.
