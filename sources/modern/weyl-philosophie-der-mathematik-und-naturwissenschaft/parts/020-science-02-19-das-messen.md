## Translation

### 19. Measurement

In contrast to Aristotelian philosophy, the view that gained ground in the
modern period is that a connection of knowledge in the actual world can be
found only insofar as qualitative determinations are led back to quantitative
ones. This view became fundamental for natural science. Weyl finds it in
Kepler: as the eye is made for colors and the ear for sounds, so the human
mind is made not for understanding just anything but for understanding
quantities. The measure of our knowledge lies in its approximation to the
"nude quantities." Galilei explicitly states the principle: measure everything
that is measurable, and try to make measurable what is not yet so. His
invention of the thermometer is a brilliant confirmation of the second part of
that postulate.

But what does the process of measuring consist in? Weyl takes inertial mass as
his first example. Following Galilei, we ascribe the same inertial mass to two
bodies when neither overruns the other if we drive them against one another
with equal velocities, even if they stick together on impact. This is a
definition by abstraction. Physically defined equality of mass must be tested
in experience as a relation having the character of equality. It must also be
established experimentally that the relation is independent of the ancillary
circumstances of the defining process, here the velocity of the collision.

With equality, the first requirement of measurement, the relation of greater
and smaller is usually also given without difficulty. Of the two bodies, the
one with greater mass is the one that overruns the other at the same velocity.
Finally, however, a process of addition must be given. In the case of masses,
addition consists simply in putting bodies together. Under certain axioms about
these basic concepts, discussed for example by Helmholtz in "Counting and
Measuring," a measurement scale can be founded on them, characterizing every
value of the magnitude in question by a number. It may be necessary to fix a
particular unit of measure arbitrarily; this introduces a new moment of
relativity, as with lengths and masses. Sometimes, however, a natural unit of
measure exists, as the full rotation does in the domain of angles. Practically,
the unit must be as exactly reproducible as possible everywhere and at all
times.

Another kind of magnitude appears in natural laws as absolute or material
constants. These laws state functional dependencies between additive
magnitudes. The refractive index `n` belongs here; its meaning is clarified by
Snell's law of refraction: the sine of the angle of incidence equals `n` times
the sine of the angle of refraction. The additive magnitudes connected in this
law are the two angles. Helmholtz calls such constants "intensive" magnitudes,
in contrast to additive, "quantitative" magnitudes. Intensive magnitudes are,
in particular, all value-determinations of properties.

Temperature measurement is a good example. Bodies have equal temperature when,
in contact with one another, they produce no change in one another. It is not
self-evident, but a fact to be learned from experience, that if `A` has the
same temperature as `B`, and `B` the same temperature as `C`, then `A` and `C`
also have the same temperature. In the realm of temperatures there is no
addition that would lead to a determinate measurement scale. On the basis of
the experience that bodies of unequal temperature produce changes of length in
one another, people helped themselves by determining temperature through the
length of a normal body brought into contact with the body to be measured.

This fixing of temperature is always reproducible and independent of previous
history, whereas the temperature sense lets us feel a body of physically
constant temperature now as warm and now as cold, depending on the warmth
levels to which our skin was exposed immediately before. Wood and iron of the
same temperature also feel different: if warm, the iron feels warmer; if cold,
the iron feels colder. External thermal conductivity is physically involved in
this. The objective concept of temperature therefore removes itself quite far
from the givens of the heat sense.

The temperature scale depends on the chosen normal body. At least all gases
behave approximately alike, and their behavior can be described with small
errors by a simple law that is then set up as characteristic of an "ideal gas."
But only when the so-called second law of thermodynamics, valid for all
bodies, was derived from this law of the ideal gas with the aid of a general
principle did it become possible to realize the ideal-gas thermometer's scale.
Absolute temperature is then characterized not only by saying that bodies of
equal temperature have the same temperature value `T`, but by saying that for
every infinitely slow cyclic process the integral over `dQ/T` becomes zero.
Here `dQ` is the energetically measured quantity of heat supplied in the
individual infinitely small steps of the cycle, and `T` is the temperature
prevailing at the relevant moment. The quantity of heat is additive; temperature
is therefore an intensive magnitude in Helmholtz's sense. Its definition is an
implicit one, including the validity of certain laws of nature. It leaves only
the unit of measure arbitrary, not the zero point. Rather, `T` is necessarily
always positive; there is an absolute zero point of temperature.

The laws of "mechanical similarity," discussed by Galilei on the second day of
his `Discorsi`, rest on the relativity of determinations of magnitude with
respect to an arbitrarily chosen unit of measure. These laws allow us, for
example, to get information about actual occurrences from small models, just as
one can read off side ratios from a small model of a triangle whose angles are
known. If friction of the medium, water or air, plays a role in a swimming or
flight problem, then in passing to the model one must generally replace the
medium too with one of correspondingly changed viscosity. The physical
similarity theorems have limits. In special relativity only one arbitrary unit
of length remains for space and time, and the speed of light `c` becomes the
absolute velocity norm. The existence of an absolute unit for velocities in
relativity theory is no stranger, Weyl says, than the existence of an absolute
unit of angle in geometry. It is merely a consequence of the metric structure
of the four-dimensional world. Add the gravitational constant, and for all
physical measurement only one unit remains to be chosen arbitrarily, for
example the second as time unit. One can go this far without taking account of
the atomistic structure of matter; atom theory and the absolute natural
constants obtainable from atomic laws are deferred to section 22e and appendix
F.

The theory of measurement includes the question how it is possible to determine
magnitudes much more precisely than their immediate sensory distinguishability
allows. What sense does it have to distinguish two shades of yellow that are
sensibly indistinguishable, such as the yellows of the two neighboring
D-lines in the sodium spectrum? A simple example is the exact determination of
the duration of one pendulum swing. One waits for about 1000 swings and divides
the total time by 1000. Accuracy has thereby risen to a thousandfold of what
could be achieved by observing a single swing. Of course a theoretical
presupposition has been made: that all individual swings have the same
duration. But for the intuitionist who respects the limit of sensory precision
and does not want to exceed it by a thousandfold, this presupposition is
"meaningless" just like the indirectly obtained assertion about the duration of
the individual swing. Nevertheless one can confirm it in a certain way by
establishing that the duration of `m` successive swings relates to that of `n`
swings, where `m` and `n` are large whole numbers, as `m:n`, within the
precision limits of direct observation.

In general, the situation is this. Through the exact laws of the underlying
theory, the magnitude `x` to be determined stands in functional dependencies
with several others. By observing those others, conclusions can be drawn about
the value of `x` that allow `x` to be determined more precisely than by direct
observation. The confirmation of the underlying theories consists in all
indirect methods of determining `x` leading to the same result within the
expected error limits. Otherwise observation compels a modification of the
theory. A fact becomes all the more exactly determined the farther its causal
consequences develop in time. A difference in direction between two projectiles
that is at first not noticeable at all finally leads to the clearest
difference: one hits, the other does not. But every such indirect
determination of value, every positing of a sensibly non-present difference,
is possible only on the basis of theories. Their verification occurs by testing
all their numerical consequences and finding concordance.

The whole indirect methodology of experimental physics belongs here: from the
simplest aids such as the vernier, mirror reflections for measuring small
deflections, rotating mirrors by which one resolves the tone-generating
oscillations of luminous bodies, and the microscope, up to the refinement of
modern experimental arrangements in atomic research. These press forward to
make the individual atom, or alpha particle, isolatedly "visible" through its
effect. Mach gives an interesting overview and an attempt to order the
different methodological principles in the chapter "The Physical Experiment
and Its Leading Motives" in `Knowledge and Error`. This is a main field of
activity for the experimental researcher's power of invention.

This may justify the view that the world is much more determinate than it
appears to the senses, indeed that it is absolutely determinate. But the
absolute determinacy of any piece of the world lived through bodily by me
would be obtainable only if I waited for its further development until the end
of all times, together with the completion of the theoretical physics that
supplies the exact laws. It is therefore a limit-idea, not a given. Leibniz's
thought of pre-established harmony, which he illustrates by two independent
clocks that always go alike not because they exert synchronizing influences on
one another but because they are built exactly alike, contradicts the essence
of the continuum. Hume explains in the `Treatise` that the sharpening of
measurements rests on mutual and ongoing correction, and that the concept of a
correction beyond what we can reach with instruments and artificial means, the
absolutely exact measuring rod, is "a mere fiction," as useless as it is
incomprehensible.

In this connection one nevertheless understands the necessity and usefulness
of exact mathematics. Exact theory forms the framework for approximative
verifications. If, for example, we lay Euclidean geometry down as the theory
of space, then with the theorem that the diagonal of a square stands to its
side as `√2:1`, we are equipped for all future sharpenings of direct and
indirect methods of measurement. It leads us to ever new predictions of
approximative character, or to ever finer criteria for the measuring bodies
that are supposed to satisfy the ideal presuppositions of Euclidean geometry
with whatever precision can be given to length measurement at the time.

Recently, the Danish geometer Hjelmslev has advocated a pure geometry of
approximation with the same arguments as Hume. Hume had observed, among other
things, that the certainty that two straight lines cannot have a segment in
common always presupposes a clearly perceptible inclination of the lines
toward one another, whereas if their angle is very small we lack a model-image
of a straight line accurate enough for us to recognize the truth of the
proposition with certainty. But when Hjelmslev formulates the Pythagorean
theorem by saying that, in a right-angled triangle, the sides can be fixed by
numbers such that the square of the hypotenuse-number becomes equal to the sum
of the squares of the two leg-numbers, the idea already appears of reducing
the space of uncertainty for directly read-off measured numbers through the
functional connection declared inviolable and elevated to an exact law.

If one further remembers that the same segment which appears here as
hypotenuse can function as a component in infinitely many other figures, with
whose remaining parts it is bound by analogous functional relations, then one
arrives exactly at the conception of the sense of exact theory that governs
constructive physics. Hjelmslev, Weyl says, has much too strongly only the
figures drawn on the blackboard in view and forgets too much that geometry is
also supposed to provide an ideal foundation for astronomy and atomic physics.
Constructive science can tolerate Brouwerian intuitionism; Hume-Hjelmslev
sensualism, which in principle wants to make the immediately given into the
sole real while being unable actually to carry this through, is deadly for it.

Measurement, as considered so far, rested on the fact that in many cases
physical magnitudes offer concepts of equality and addition satisfying certain
axioms, which project their values onto a numerical scale. Maxwell says that
in this way all applications of mathematics in science are founded on
relations between the laws of physical magnitudes and those of whole numbers.
As important as this special way of introducing numerical symbols into natural
science may be, Weyl argues that no principally privileged role should be
ascribed to it.

If, in a continuum, one creates the basis for an arithmetical distinction of
the individual places by stretching a subdivision-net over the continuum, a net
that at all stages of sharpening and refinement is in large measure free,
though chosen according to a fixed combinatorial schema, one proceeds
differently and much more freely than in the case of measurement proper.
Moreover, the measurement of many physical state-magnitudes, which are not
scalars but vectors or "tensors," such as the metric field, is possible only
relative to such an arbitrarily introduced coordinate system. This free
introduction of coordinates and measurement based on the addition of equal
elements may be typical of the substrates to which the two methods apply: the
first to the form of the world, the second to its content.

What really matters in measurement is symbolic presentation. Numbers are not
the only usable symbols. A further essential and general trait of measurement
is that, relative to the assumed measurement-foundations, it permits things to
be given in a conceptual, symbolic way. If a piece of the infinite Euclidean
plane is materially realized by a plane metal plate, then at first we can fix
places only within the domain of the plate by applying qualitatively
distinguished, repeatedly recognizable material marks to the plate. But if a
right-angled axis-cross together with a unit segment is engraved into it, we
can not only, "ideally" and on the basis of a theory about the behavior of
rigid measuring rods, spread over it an arbitrarily fine-meshed net of
well-characterized places through coordinate specifications. This indirect
method even lets us overstep the limits of the material basis and set such
"number-marks" out into the empty plane extending beyond the material island.
In this way we use the earth as the measurement-basis for astronomical space.

Finally, measurement shows the effort to lead immediate sensory
determinations, which of course can never be eliminated, back wherever possible
to the surest and most precise among them: the determination of space-time
coincidences. One seeks, for example, to exclude subjective comparison of
colors and brightnesses. Every measurement should ultimately come down to a
mark on one scale, a movable pointer or the like, coinciding with a determinate
mark on a second scale. In an astronomical observation, the reading on the
graduated circle happens in this way, while the setting of the instrument on
the star uses a coincidence modified by the interposition of light: the
covering of star and crosshair.

## Analytic Notes

Section 19 is the methodological payoff of the preceding space-time work.
Measurement is not presented as bare numerical labeling. It requires equality,
comparison, addition, reproducible units, and, in harder cases, implicit
definitions that include laws of nature. Absolute temperature is the key
example: its definition already contains the second law of thermodynamics.

The decisive claim is that indirect measurement outruns sensory
distinguishability only through theory. A sensibly absent difference can be
posited when multiple theory-bound indirect determinations converge within
error limits. This extends section 17's anti-surplus discipline: numerical
claims earn sense through theory-wide concordance, not through direct
intuition alone.

Weyl's rejection of Hume-Hjelmslev sensualism is stronger than a routine
defense of exact mathematics. Exact mathematics is necessary because it forms
the scaffold for approximative verifications and keeps generating sharper
criteria for measuring bodies and experimental arrangements. Approximation
without exact theory cannot support constructive physics.

The close widens measurement beyond numbers. Coordinate systems, tensors, and
symbolic presentations show that measurement is a way of giving things
conceptually relative to measurement-foundations. The final reduction to
space-time coincidence ties the method back to sections 16-18: physical
objectivity must return to possible coincidence events without being reducible
to unaided sensory comparison.

## Adversarial Check

The main risk is treating measurement as an operationalist reduction to
observable procedures. Weyl goes the other way: actual measuring operations
matter, but the precision and objectivity of measurement depend on exact
theory, indirect inference, and convergence across theory-bound methods.

A second risk is reading the Hjelmslev passage as hostility to approximation.
Weyl accepts approximation as unavoidable. What he rejects is sensualism that
tries to make immediate givenness the only reality and therefore cannot
support the exact theoretical framework that makes approximation scientifically
useful.

A third risk is overprivileging number. Weyl explicitly says that numbers are
not the only usable symbols. Measurement belongs to a broader account of
symbolic presentation, especially where coordinates and tensors are required.
