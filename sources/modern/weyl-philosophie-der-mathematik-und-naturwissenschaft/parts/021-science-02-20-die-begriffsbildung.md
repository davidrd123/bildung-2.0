## Translation

### 20. Concept-Formation

In the essay on "the autonomy of thinking in the seventeenth century," found
in the second volume of Dilthey's collected writings, Dilthey describes the
rise of mechanics up to Galilei. "Galilei came. In him, after more than two
millennia of description and contemplation of form in nature, which had now
found a conclusion in Copernicus's world-picture, there followed the study of
a real analysis of nature."

What is decisive for this analysis is the isolation of simple processes within
the complexity of occurrence: the decomposition of the one-time world-process
into simple, continually recurring elements. Bacon had already set up the
formula `dissecare naturam`. Descartes says in the `Discourse on Method` that
only the mathematicians have reached certainty and evidence, because they
began from what is easiest and simplest. The power of natural science rests in
no small measure on its renouncing the attempt to sketch a "system of nature"
in one stroke. Instead, it lowered itself with infinite patience to small
individual questions, but subjected them to exhaustive analysis.

Descartes himself still sinned strongly against that methodological remark of
his own. Part of Galilei's superiority to Descartes in natural science lies in
Galilei's investigations of the laws of falling bodies: here he made serious
use of the abstinence and limitation that "shows the master." Weyl then
distinguishes four stages in the procedure of decomposition into simple
elements, of which the first three still belong to the pre-scientific level.

First, three-dimensional spatial reality is cut into individual, spatially
separate, relatively stable part-systems, each forming an intuitive unity:
bodies or things. Processes in such systems are regarded as independent of one
another so long as progressing analysis does not force corrections. Alongside
this comes a cutting of four-dimensional space-time reality into individual
events, spatially and temporally separated, each internally joined into an
intuitive unity.

Second, an intuitively lived process is understood as having arisen through
the space-time meeting and fusion of several simple phenomena. Each individual
phenomenon would manifest itself in perceptions of a different kind from the
total appearance if one crossed out the others or replaced them with "normal
circumstances." The example is sunset behind a cloud edged with gold.

Third, one grasps suchness, distinguishing the dependent parts of phenomena,
their characteristic traits and marks. On this rests the coordination of like
with like, subordination under concepts, and classification. Classification is
corrected by ever-richer experience and so increasingly separates what is
truly essential from what is unessential, advancing toward an ever more
"natural" formation of classes. A concept is more essential the more
connotations it carries on the testimony of experience: the more traits not
contained in the concept itself are found to be common to the objects falling
under it.

Fourth, one does not stop with elements that can be intuitively picked out.
Instead one takes a series of properties that always occur together as signs
of a hidden something. This leads to hypothetical elements such as atoms,
forces, and the electromagnetic field. But it is not only the already
available properties that are read as manifestations of such elements and
their intensive or quantitative value. One also learns to interpret the
behavior of a system when brought together with others. This is the essence of
"reaction," of experiment.

Finally, one is not afraid to decompose hypothetically even what is intuitively
simple outright: white sunlight into the spectral colors, or the acceleration
experienced by a planet into the partial accelerations separately imparted to
it by the sun and the other planets. With the decomposition, the synthetic
principles must also be uncovered according to which the elements are united
into the whole. The formation of resultants from forces is Weyl's example.

Beginning everywhere with what is simplest, one finds fixed lawful relations
among the elements won in this way, elements that recur in all appearances,
and among their variations of value. These relations are to be investigated
quantitatively and expressed through mathematical functions. The decisive
point is this: the further analysis advances, the finer the detail in which
processes are grasped and the finer the elements into which they are
decomposed, the simpler the basic lawful relations become, not the more
complicated, as one might have expected. They also explain the actual course
more fully and accurately. Only by means of this analysis do the right
concepts emerge, the concepts with whose help objective nature is to be
described. They appear throughout as bound to definite facts and valid laws of
nature.

What compels us in physics to think of the unified white color as a composite?
The causal law: the principle that like under like circumstances calls forth
like reactions. It requires that two colors which appear sensibly as the same
white must contain "hidden" differences if, in passing through the same prism,
they yield different spectra. In principle this is no different from cutting
open two outwardly alike balls that show different inertia and weight, and
then discovering a gray lead core inside one of them.

One will find that the most useful way to describe the manifold hidden in
white light is by specifying its spectrum together with its distribution of
intensity. At first the apparatus used for the reaction, the prism with its
special properties, is still mixed in. By varying the prism in form and
substance, one must first learn to separate the two influences from one
another. In this way one reaches the prism-independent wavelength scale in the
spectrum. Weyl recalls the earlier example of the ponderomotive force
undergone by a test body in a field produced by charged conductors: that too
was a separation procedure of this kind. Polarized light of definite spectral
color and intensity, by contrast, proves to be simple because its behavior in
all reactions is completely determined by the traits just named.

A typical example of physical concept-formation is the Galilean concept of
mass. Weyl had already mentioned the criterion for equality of mass. In that
criterion, the concept of momentum appears as primary relative to mass. Two
bodies moving against one another, both carrying out a uniform translation
according to the law of inertia, have opposite and equal momentum when neither
overruns the other in collision. Two bodies have the same mass, to repeat the
Galilean criterion, when at equal velocities they have equal momenta.

This is therefore a constructive concept in the sense described on page 56.
Here in the sphere of actuality, however, real or at least really possible
experiments replace or stand alongside the purely intellectual manipulations
in the realm of numbers, and the outcome of those experiments is used to
determine the value of traits. A step of great consequence was thereby taken.
Once matter had been stripped of all sensory qualities, it first seemed that
one could ascribe to it only geometrical properties. Descartes is fully
consistent in this respect. But now it becomes clear that one can read other
numerical characteristics of bodies from motion and from the lawful alteration
of motion in reactions. Beyond geometry and kinematics, the sphere of properly
mechanical and physical concepts opens up.

At bottom, the Galilean definition of mass already contains the law of
momentum: "To an isolated body that is in uniform motion there belongs a
definite momentum 𝔍 = m𝔳, that is, a vector pointing in the same direction as
its velocity 𝔳. The sum of the momenta of the individual bodies of an isolated
system before a reaction is equal to the sum of the momenta after the
reaction." By subjecting observed motions to this law, one obtains data for
the numerical determination of the ratios among the masses `m` of the
different bodies before and after the reaction.

Constructive natural science has a general task: to assign to objects such
quantitative traits as depend only on the relevant object, even if they cannot
be directly known from it, so that its behavior under circumstances given by
traits of the same kind is fully determined and predictable on the basis of
the laws of nature. The implicit definition of traits is bound to these laws.
In this way science fulfills the postulate, unfulfilled when sensory
properties are presupposed, that all alterations occurring in bodies have
their ground in the essence and properties of the bodies themselves. Weyl
cites Euler for this formulation. The fact that we do not find the general
principles of natural knowledge, but have to work them out laboriously, was
especially stressed by Poincaré's conventionalism.

Passing to the temporal analysis of the reaction process, one sets down the
force as the measure of the action that a body `k` undergoes from other bodies
`k₁, k₂, ...`: since for an isolated body `k` the momentum `𝔍` is temporally
constant, force is the change of this quantity per unit of time, `d𝔍/dt`.
Newton recognized that force composes additively, according to the
parallelogram law of vector addition, from individual forces exerted on `k` by
each of the bodies `k₁, k₂, ...`. The force which `k₁` exerts on `k` at a
moment depends only on the state of those two bodies, their position and their
velocity at the same instant. This is the real meaning of decomposing the
unitary force into individual partial forces.

On the basis of these facts, Weyl says, one is necessarily led to the view
that the definition "force = derivative of momentum with respect to time" does
not correctly render the essence of force. The real state of affairs is
rather the reverse: force is the expression of an independent potency that
links bodies according to their inner nature and their mutual relation of
position and motion, and that causes the temporal alteration of momentum. The
living-metaphysical representation thus adapts itself to theoretical
construction.

Through the fundamental mechanical law of motion, physics receives the task of
investigating the forces acting between bodies in their dependence on place,
motion, and inner state. The latter will enter into the laws of force through
certain numbers characteristic of the inner state of the reacting bodies, as
charge enters Coulomb's law of electrostatic attraction and repulsion. The
concept of force thus becomes a source of new measurable physical marks of
matter.

If the metaphysical image of nature is modified by the results of theoretical
construction, which are supposed to receive in it an intuitive and living
expression and thereby promote further development, concrete research is also
usually already guided by such a living representation, one that stands in
fortunate harmony with the facts. Galilei reads into motion the dynamic
intensity, impact-force, `impetus`, or momentum. Motion appears to him as
conditioned by the struggle of two tendencies: inertia and the force that
deflects the body from the path prescribed to it by inertial guidance. Mass is
the dynamic coefficient according to which inertia resists the deflecting
force.

With reference precisely to Galilei, Goethe remarks in his history of color
theory: "Everything in science depends on what one calls an `Aperçu`, on a
becoming-aware of what actually lies at the ground of the appearances. And
such a becoming-aware is fruitful into infinity." From the right fundamental
aspect, the right fundamental concepts emerge by way of detailed research.

In his book `Substance-Concept and Function-Concept`, Cassirer tried to show
that the logical form of concept-formation in mathematics and physics in no
way corresponds to the Aristotelian schema. If in plane analytic geometry one
defines the ellipse by its equation, by setting a definite quadratic form of
the coordinates equal to 1, then one obtains an individual ellipse by assigning
determinate values to the still-indeterminate coefficients of the quadratic
form. Those coefficients vary within a domain already staked out in advance:
the continuum of all real numbers.

Weyl does not approve Cassirer's remark that, in this procedure, the more
general concept is the richer. The properties of the individual ellipse depend
not only on the general form of the equation but also on the special values of
the coefficients. But this much is right: here the individual cases are won
from the general by giving determinate values to the "variables" within a play
space that is either completely given or opened to free construction.

In Aristotle, the ascent from the individual object to the concept occurs by
lifting out individual abstract moments from the object, subtracting everything
else, and letting agreement in those moments determine that other objects fall
with the given one under the same concept, into the same class. There one has
in view from the beginning only the actually existing objects, as in
descriptive botany and zoology, and one arranges the formation of classes so
that the concepts carry as many connotations as possible according to the
testimony of experience.

By contrast, in mathematical-physical, "functional concept-formation," one
does not subtract. Instead, individual moments intuited by themselves and
capable of continuous gradations are made variable. In the ellipse example,
these moments are the coefficients of the quadratic form. Here the concept
does not extend over the actual objects, but over all possible objects arising
in this way.

Mach says that the arbitrary refinement, easy survey, and handling of a whole
continuum of cases, of whose completeness we are at the same time convinced,
grounds the advantage of such quantitative setups. But for Weyl it is
essential that the continuum is not a closed aggregate but a determination
field open into infinity. Otherwise one would fall back into the Aristotelian
schema of characteristic marks: "A set of points `[x, y]` is an ellipse if
there are numbers `a, b, c` such that for all and only the points of the set
the equation

```text
ax² + 2bxy + cy² = 1
```

holds." One must therefore generate the individual objects falling under the
functional concept. One may not ask, as if facing a state of affairs existing
in itself, whether a presented object falls under it.

The Platonic schema on page 74 is identical with the division-net of the
one-dimensional continuum. In it Plato assigns every essence its place by
passing always from the general to the particular through dichotomy,
`Diärese`. The Platonic conception of ideas as numbers, which rests on this,
is not as distant from modern mathematical natural science as it might first
seem.

It would have to be modified in two respects. First, some stages and divisions
may indeed be prescribed by the thing itself and exactly executable, but not
all of them, as Plato claims with the image of "jointing the sacrificial
animal to be divided" in the `Phaedrus` and the `Statesman`. Wherever there is
a unified connected continuum, this possibility ceases. Second, the process
advances into infinity, and the individual thing appears only on the horizon
as a limit-idea. What is characteristic of Aristotle is that he reverses this
schema and begins from below, from individual beings.

In particular, the concepts gained through mathematical abstraction according
to the prescription at the end of section 2 are "functional" in nature.

## Notes

- Section 20 gives the missing bridge from measurement to theory: measurement
  supplies exact symbolic access, while concept-formation explains how the
  objects and traits to be measured are themselves won.
- Weyl's natural-science concepts are neither mere names nor passive
  classifications. They are generated by experiment-guided analysis and tied
  to laws through implicit definition.
- The Galilean mass example extends the section-19 measurement discussion:
  inertial mass begins from a collision criterion, but its mature concept is
  bound to the law of momentum.
- The force passage is structurally important because Weyl lets theoretical
  construction and living-metaphysical image correct one another, rather than
  reducing one to the other.
- The Cassirer/Mach close connects section 20 back to the mathematical
  chapters: the real opposition is closed aggregate versus infinity-open
  field of generation.
