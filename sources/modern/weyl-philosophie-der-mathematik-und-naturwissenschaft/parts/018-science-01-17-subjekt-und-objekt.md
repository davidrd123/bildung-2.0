## Translation

### 17. Subject And Object

The doctrine of the subjectivity of sensory qualities has been closely linked
with the construction of natural science since Democritus set up the
principle: "Only in opinion (`νόμῳ, οὐ φύσει`) does the sweet exist, in
opinion the bitter, in opinion the cold, the warm, color"; what is truly real,
by contrast, are unchanging particles of substance, atoms moving in empty
space. According to Plato too (`Theaetetus` 156e), those "qualities, hard,
warm, and whatever else they are called, are nothing in and for themselves,"
but arise through the meeting of a "motion" proceeding from the subject and
one proceeding from the object. The real is pure activity; only in the
"image," in consciousness, hovering in the middle, is there suffering. "White
or red, bitter or sweet, sounding or silent, fragrant or foul-smelling are
names for effects on the sense organs," says Galileo; they may no more be
ascribed to the things themselves than the tickle or pain that contact with
objects can produce. Descartes deals with this in detail in the closing
paragraphs of the `Principia` and in the `Traité de la Lumière`, and Locke
does so in `An Essay Concerning Human Understanding`, book 2, chapter 8,
sections 13-22.

The subjectivity of sensory qualities must be asserted in two respects, one
philosophical and one natural-scientific. First, by its essence such a quality
can be given only in consciousness, in sensation. One sees it either as a
characteristic adhering to sensation or, on finer analysis, not as a real
moment in sensation itself but as an entity belonging to the intentional
object that "images itself forth" in the act of consciousness. It is simply
unintelligible how the quality, detached from consciousness, could be ascribed
to a thing in itself as a property in itself. This is the basic thought of
epistemological idealism. Second, the qualities with which the things of the
external world clothe themselves for me depend, beyond the things themselves,
quite essentially first on the nearer physical circumstances, especially in
the case of color on illumination and on the condition of the medium between
the object and my eye, and second on me, on my psychophysical organization.
Even my organ of sight does not seize objects at their place. What I see is
determined only by the state of the optical field at the point of contact with
my sense-body, the retina. These are facts of natural science that even the
realist must concede. How differently the world would visually appear to us if
our eye were sensitive to other wavelengths, or if the physiological processes
on the retina transformed the infinitely dimensional realm of physically
different compound colors into a three- or four-dimensional manifold rather
than a merely two-dimensional one.

In contrast to the "secondary" qualities, Democritus, Descartes, and Locke
declare the primary qualities, the space-time properties of bodies, size,
shape, and motion, to be objective. "The ideas of primary qualities," Locke
says in the passage cited, "are likenesses; those of secondary qualities are
not." According to Descartes, we may demand no resemblance between real process
and perception, between sound waves and tone, any more than between a thing and
the word that designates it. Yet he holds fast to the objective validity of
the ideas that refer to space, because unlike the qualities we know them
clearly and distinctly; what we know in this way is, according to the principle
of his epistemology, true. To support this principle, however, he must appeal
to the truthfulness of God, who does not wish to deceive us. Once the basic
thought of idealism has flashed up, but one still wants to construct the real
world out of elements lying in consciousness that are accepted as real because
for some reason they seem especially trustworthy, one cannot get around such a
God who guarantees truth.

"He is the bridge ... between lonely, wandering thought certain only of one
thing, self-consciousness, and the external world. The attempt turned out
somewhat naive, but one sees how sharply Cartesius had already measured the
grave of philosophy; strange, however, how he used dear God as a ladder to
climb out. Yet even his contemporaries would not let him over the edge ..."
This is from Georg Büchner's philosophical notes. Hobbes, in `De corpore`,
begins from the fiction of abolishing the universe, similar to Husserl's
`epoché`, in order then to let it arise again step by step through construction
from reason. But he too uses as construction-material the general
representations that form the residue of experience, especially the
representations of space and time. This standpoint corresponds to the physics
of Galileo, Newton, and Huygens, which constructs world-occurrence as
intuitively represented processes of motion in intuitive space. It therefore
requires an absolute Euclidean space as standing medium into which the motions
draw themselves. Galileo's statement is well known: the true book of philosophy
is the book of nature, always lying open before our eyes, but written in
letters other than those of the alphabet; the letters are triangles, squares,
circles, spheres, cones, pyramids, and other geometrical figures.

Leibniz penetrates to a more radical conception: "As regards bodies, I can
prove that not only light, heat, color, and the like, but also motion, figure,
and extension are only appearing qualities." Berkeley and Hume must also be
mentioned. D'Alembert no longer justifies the use of the "residue of
experience" for constructing the objective world, as Descartes did, through
the clarity and distinctness of the ideas, but solely through the success of
the method. According to Kant, space and time are merely forms of our
intuition. Stumpf calls it absurd to represent atoms as spatial bodies without
color-qualities, through whose play of motion the ether vibrations are first
triggered, which by virtue of their wavelength are carriers of color. Just as
a color cannot be represented without spatial extension, neither can a space
without a color-quality clothing it be represented, according to Berkeley and
Hume. Therefore the medium in which physics constructs the external world must
not be intuitive space and intuitive time, but a four-dimensional continuum in
the abstract-arithmetical sense. If for Huygens colors were "in reality" ether
vibrations, they now appear only as mathematical functional courses of periodic
character, where four independent variables occur in the functions as
representatives of the space-time medium referred to coordinates. What finally
remains is thus a symbolic construction, in exactly the sense in which Hilbert
carries it out in mathematics.

The construction, out of what is immediately given to me in intuition, of this
objective world that can now be represented only in symbols proceeds through
different stages. The transition from stage to stage is forced by the fact
that what is present at one stage reveals itself as the appearance of a higher
reality, the reality of the next stage. The typical example is how the spatial
body-shape constitutes itself as something identical in the different
perspectival views. The precondition for this is that the standpoint from
which the individual perspectival image appears is varied, and that the
different standpoints actually occupied give themselves as a section from a
continuum of possibilities that is laid out in us, though infinite. Weyl will
return to this in the next section. The systematic natural-scientific
presentation, however, will go the reverse way: it will set up the symbolic
world purely by itself and then, skipping over all intermediate stages, try to
describe which objective courses of state lead to which immediate givens of
consciousness.

Thus perspective teaches us how to find the optical image from the spatial
shape of a body and the observer's standpoint relative to that body. A physical
example already belonging to the highest stages is the constitution of the
concepts "electric field" and "electric field strength." In the space between
charged conductors we find that a weakly charged "test sphere" at each point
`P` experiences a force `𝔎 = 𝔎(P)` determined in magnitude and direction, and
always the same force `𝔎` when I bring the little sphere back to the same
spatial point `P`. If I use different test bodies, it turns out that the force
depends on the test body, but in such a way that one can carry out a factor
split:

```text
𝔎(P) = e · 𝔈(P)
```

Here the vector factor `𝔈(P)`, the electric field strength, depends only on the
conductors and not on the state of the test sphere, apart from the place `P`.
Conversely, the scalar factor `e`, the charge of the test body, is conditioned
only by that body's state and proves to be the same no matter into what
electric fields one brings it. Here we start from force as the given. But the
facts described lead us to assume an electric field, mathematically described
by the vector-valued place-function `𝔈(P)`, that surrounds the conductors and
is present regardless of whether or not we establish the force it exerts on a
test sphere. The test body is only the means of making the field perceptible
and measurable. The equation `𝔎 = e · 𝔈` now no longer appears as the
definition of `𝔈`, but as a natural law, corrigible under circumstances, that
determines the ponderomotive effect exerted by an electric field `𝔈` on a
point charge `e` brought into it. Since according to Maxwell's theory light is
nothing other than a periodically changing electromagnetic field of very small
period, in the eye we possess a sense organ with whose help we perceive
certain electric fields also otherwise than through their ponderomotive
effects. In systematic presentation one will introduce, without any
explanation, purely "symbolically," an electric field strength `𝔈`; state the
laws it satisfies, for example that the line integral of `𝔈` extended over a
closed spatial curve is zero; and state the laws according to which
ponderomotive forces are connected with it. If we regard the forces as what is
directly demonstrable, then the connection to the given has thereby been
carried out.

One can say that physics first freed itself completely from the space and time
of intuition as construction-means of the objective world in the general
theory of relativity. In the schema it sketches, which also includes all
earlier standpoints as special or limiting cases, Weyl therefore explains the
relation of subject and object through a typical case. Suppose the matter is
the observation of two or more stars. He simplifies the apprehending
consciousness to a point-eye whose worldline is called `B`. The observation
takes place at the moment `O` of its life. The construction must be carried
out in four-dimensional number-space; only to make himself easier to
understand does Weyl draw a geometrical figure. `Σ` are the worldlines of two
stars. On the rearward light cone `𝔎` radiating from `O`, which intersects the
two star-lines `Σ` each in one point, run the worldlines `Λ` of the light
signals arriving in `O` from the stars. Through a certain construction
describable in purely arithmetical terms, the numerical value `ϑ` of the angle
under which the stars appear to the observer in `O` is determined. This
construction is "invariant," that is, it is of such a kind that it leads to the
same numerical value `ϑ` if, after an arbitrary deformation of the whole image,
it is carried out again according to the same prescription on the distorted
image.

Everything is contained in that construction: the dependence of the angle on
the stars themselves, on the metric field spreading out between the stars and
the observer, on the observer's place in the world, which is spatial
perspective, and on the observer's state of motion. Different angular values
result in `O` according to the direction of the line `B` passing through `O`;
this is the velocity-perspective known as aberration. Those angles `ϑ` between
each two stars of a constellation determine the visual shape of the
constellation, which is not to be described objectively but only lived through
in intuition, and which appears under the equally non-objectifiable
presupposition that I am that point-eye. If they agree with those of a second
constellation, both constellations appear in the same shape at the moment `O`;
otherwise they appear in different shapes. The objective world simply is; it
does not happen. Only before the gaze of the consciousness crawling upward in
the worldline of my body does a section of this world "come alive" and pass
before it as a spatial image undergoing temporal change.

In the construction of the angles `ϑ`, an important role is played by the
"splitting" of the world into space and time that is carried out for
consciousness at every moment `O`. Objectively it can be described as follows.
If `e₀, e₁, e₂, e₃` are the components of a vector that gives the direction of
`B` in `O`, then my nearest spatial environment in `O` is spanned by all line
elements `(dx₀, dx₁, dx₂, dx₃)` radiating from `O` that are orthogonal to `e`,
that is, that satisfy the equation

```text
Σ_{i,k=0}^{3} g_{ik} dx_i e_k = 0     with g_{ik} = g_{ik}(O).
```

Thus the objective stock contains all the grounds required to do justice to
subjective appearances: there is no difference in lived experience to which a
difference in the underlying objective stock would not correspond, and indeed
a difference invariant under arbitrary coordinate transformations. But the
body of the I, as a physical thing, of course belongs to this objective stock
as well. What is immediately lived through is subjective and absolute; however
foggy it may be, in precisely this fogginess it is a given thus and not
otherwise. The objective world, by contrast, with which we constantly reckon
in practical life and which natural science seeks to crystallize out purely
through methods that consistently develop the criteria according to which
reality constitutes itself for us, the human beings living along in the natural
attitude, is necessarily relative. It can be represented by something definite,
numbers or other symbols, only after a coordinate system has been carried into
the world arbitrarily. This contrasting pair, subjective-absolute and
objective-relative, seems to Weyl to contain one of the most fundamental
epistemological insights that one can read from natural research. Whoever wants
the absolute must accept subjectivity, I-relatedness; whoever is driven toward
the objective cannot avoid the relativity problem. Weyl notes that this thought
is developed beautifully and vividly in the introduction to Born's book on
relativity theory.

Within natural science, the worldview oppositions of realism and idealism name
methodological principles that do not contradict one another. In natural
science we construct an objective world that must satisfy, for the explanation
of the givens of consciousness, the basic principle already formulated with
Helmholtz: a difference in the perceptions that press themselves upon us is
always grounded in a difference of real conditions. Lambert states a special
case of this in his `Photometria` as an axiom: an appearance is the same
whenever the same eye is affected in the same way. Here natural science
proceeds realistically.

For as long as I remain with what is simply given, more exactly with what is
simply given in the moment, there is obviously no need to undergird it by an
objective world. But even if I add memory and in principle acknowledge its
validity, even if I accept the contents of another's consciousness as data
equally entitled with my own, opening myself to the mystery of intersubjective
understanding and granting it belief, I would not need to proceed as we
actually do. Instead I could seek the "transformations" that mediate between
the image-worlds of different consciousnesses. Such a presentation would be
appropriate to Leibniz's monadology. Instead of constructing, from a given
body, the perspectival image that it presents from a standpoint given relative
to it, and conversely constructing the body from several perspectival
recordings, as photogrammetry does, I could exclude the body and pose the task
directly as follows: if `A`, `B`, and `C` are three consciousnesses bound to a
point-body, specify the lawful geometrical connections in which the three
images stand to one another, images that each of the three persons receives in
its visual field from the same body `K` and from the standpoints of the two
other persons. This procedure would be far less perspicuous, indeed it would
be impracticable because of the incompleteness of each individual
consciousness in comparison with the complete real world. In any case there is
no doubt that in this respect natural science proceeds as the realist attitude
requires.

On the other hand, natural science concedes to idealism that its objective
reality is not given but assigned as a task, and that it cannot be constructed
absolutely but only relative to an arbitrarily assumed coordinate system and
in mere symbols. Above all, the core thought of idealism comes into force in
the reversal of the preceding principle: the objective world-picture must
admit no differences that cannot announce themselves in differences of
perception. A being in principle inaccessible to perception is not conceded.
Leibniz says, on the occasion of the fiction of absolute motion, that motion
is indeed independent of observation, but in no way independent of the
possibility of observation at all. Motion exists only where a change accessible
to observation takes place. If this alteration is not establishable by any
observation, then it is not present either. It is true that many physically
different reds call forth exactly the same sensation of red. But if one sends
all these reds through the same prism, the physical difference also announces
itself in a perceptual difference of the colored light-bundle behind the prism.
Through the prism, the hidden difference is broken open for perception, so to
speak. A difference that can in no way be broken open for perception is a
non-thing. This is a very important methodological principle of theoretical
construction.

The formula for the metric field, the Schwarzschild formula surrounding a
mass, for example the sun, can be interpreted, in the form in which it is
usually given if one reads the coordinates appearing in it as a mapping of
real space onto a Euclidean image-space, as follows. Description I: in truth
Euclidean geometry is valid, but the spherically symmetric gravitational field
around our mass-center `O` acts on rigid bodies in such a way that a measuring
rod in `P` directed radially undergoes a shortening in the ratio

```text
√(1 − 2a/r) : 1
```

where `r` is the distance `OP` and `a` is a constant determined by the mass,
while a measuring rod placed perpendicular to `OP` remains unchanged. Why
should measuring rods that alter their true length through warming not also
react in a similar way to a gravitational field? But if one uses a certain
different coordinate system, one arrives at the following description.
Description II: in truth Euclidean geometry is valid, but the measuring rod at
point `P`, whatever direction it may take, is altered by the gravitational
field in the ratio

```text
(1 + a/(2r))² : 1.
```

Both descriptions express the same factual content. To every possible
coordinate system there corresponds a special prescription for correction that
rescues Euclidean geometry. But one is as good as another. Each introduces
into the verifiable factual content an arbitrary element that has no
consequences controllable through perception and therefore must be eliminated,
and can also be eliminated, by using with Einstein only the physical geometry
defined by the behavior of measuring rods, which of course deviates from the
Euclidean. With suitable formulation, theory I or II can be split into two
parts: Einstein's theory `E` and an addition `Z` that is not connected with
`E` and does not touch reality, and therefore must be rejected. The schematic
figure illustrates a theory with a superfluous component `Z`.

In the Bohr model of the hydrogen atom, in which the periodicity of the emitted
light has nothing to do with the periodic orbital time of the electron around
the atomic nucleus, we feel it to be a disturbing feature, however wonderfully
the model explains the spectrum, that no observation datum corresponds to the
period of the electron's revolution. This feature must be eliminated.
Poincaré once uses, to explain the idea of relativity, the fiction that during
the night, while all consciousness sleeps, the whole world with all bodies
contained in it, including my body, enlarges itself in a definite ratio. After
awakening, neither I nor anyone else would notice the slightest thing about
it. In the face of this occurrence, natural science makes common cause with
the idealist, for what under such circumstances could the assertion that the
world had enlarged still mean at all? Difference may be posited only where the
assumption of sameness comes into collision with the principle that like under
like conditions, especially like objective constitution, position, and motion
of the subject, is perceived alike, and with the causal principle.

Between the real world and the given there is an assignment, a mapping in the
mathematical sense. But on one side there stands the one quantitatively
determined objective world, while on the other side there stand not only the
actually and momentarily given, but the possible perceptions of an I, perhaps
remembered or expected on the basis of definite volitional intentions. In the
assignment there enter, besides the singular objective stock of the world, the
possible objective states of this perceiving I: the worldline of the body, and
so on. Helmholtz sets up the principle "of the empiricist view": the
sensations are signs for our consciousness, and learning to understand their
meaning is left to our intellect. One can agree with Helmholtz in what he
means, and yet also agree with Husserl that the spatial thing I see, despite
all its transcendence, is something perceived, consciously given in its bodily
presence. The sensation-data, in the concrete unity of perception, are ensouled
by "apprehensions," and in this animation exercise the "presenting function,"
or rather, in one with it, make up what we call the "appearance of" color,
shape, and so on. A dog approaching another dog sees and sniffs a "fellow dog,"
an integrated whole that is more than a "bundle of sense-impressions."

Here only one of the stages is described through which the constitution of the
external world takes place. One must not deny that the definite way in which a
bodily thing places itself before me through those animating functions is
directed by an immense quantity of earlier experiences. How else could one
describe them than by saying, with Helmholtz, that we always imagine such
objects as present in the visual field as would have to be present in order,
under the usual normal conditions of use of our eyes, to produce the same
impression. As criteria of reality, however, these stages play no role.
Helmholtz speaks here of "unconscious inferences." That sounds somewhat
questionable, but he expressly emphasizes that they are equal to an inference,
more exactly an inference by analogy, only in their result, although the
underlying psychical acts are probably quite different from those occurring in
conscious inferences and their effect cannot be abolished by better insight.
The sensory impression of a mirror image, of a broken stick projecting into
water, or of a rainbow of course does not deceive. Only the bodily thing that
this impression, according to Husserl, places before me is an error. What is
truly present can always be determined only by taking into account all sensory
signs, which in the examples mentioned soon reveal the prevailing "unusual"
conditions. Imagine that the wavelengths of the light to which our eye is
sensitive were on the order of atomic distances in solid bodies. How difficult
the optical "signs," the Laue X-ray diagrams, would then be for us to
interpret. In the definitive description of the connection between the given
and reality, it is therefore better to ignore all intermediate stages of
constitution.

What meaning, then, does the objective world representable only in symbols
have for the life of the naturally situated human being moving among wholly
intuitive givens? Helmholtz answers: when we have learned to read those
symbols correctly, we are able with their help to arrange our actions so that
they have the desired success, that is, so that the expected new
sense-impressions occur. There is not only no other comparison between
representations and things in reality, on which all schools agree; another
kind of comparison is not thinkable at all and has no sense. A representation
of a single individual body is thus already, in fact, a concept that includes
under itself an infinite number of individual intuitions following one another
in time, all of which can be derived from it. The representation of a single
individual table that I carry within me is correct and exact if from it I can
correctly and exactly derive what sensations I will have when I bring my eye
and my hand into this or that definite position with respect to the table. What
other kind of similarity there could be between such a representation and the
body represented by it, Helmholtz says he cannot comprehend.

In the same sense Leibniz remarks on the Cartesian principles: of sense-things
we can know nothing else, nor do we need to demand anything else of them, than
that they agree among themselves and with indubitable rational grounds, so that
the future can to a certain degree be foreseen from the past. To seek in them
some other truth or reality than what is guaranteed in this is vain; skeptics
may demand nothing else, dogmatists promise nothing else. Or Husserl: to the
essence of a thing-noema belong ideal possibilities of boundlessness in the
progress of concordant intuitions, and indeed along typically and definitely
pre-indicated directions. But in the construction of experiential reality
discordances also arise, forcing "corrections." On this rests the empirical
character of reality-knowledge, which necessarily passes through errors.
Thingly existence is never one demanded as necessary by the data, but in a
certain way always contingent, presumptive reality. That means that the further
course of experience can always compel us to give up what has already been
posited with experiential right. It would be entirely possible for every start
of concordance in the image-course of perceptions to "explode" irretrievably;
then no real world would constitute itself through their adjustment according
to rational principles.

The requirements that follow from Weyl's discussion for a correct theory of
world-occurrence can be formulated roughly as follows. First: concordance. The
definite value to be ascribed to a magnitude occurring in the theory in an
individually determined case is established on the basis of the theoretically
posited connections and the contact with what is perceptually given. Every such
determination must lead to the same result. Thus all determinations of the
electron charge `e`, using the laws set up by physical theories together with
observation, lead to the same value of `e`, within the limits of observational
accuracy. Especially often compared with one another are a relatively direct
observation of the magnitude in question, for example the position of a comet
at a certain moment, and a calculation on the basis of other observations, for
example the place calculated for the desired moment from the observed star
positions on preceding days according to Newtonian theory. The demand for
concordance involves freedom from contradiction, but goes beyond it because it
brings theory into contact with experience. Second: it must in principle
always be possible, on the basis of intuitive givens, to determine the definite
value of a magnitude occurring in the theory in an individually determined
case. This expresses the postulate that theory may contain no components
superfluous for the explanation of appearances.

Hume tried with inexorable consistency to hold fast to the standpoint that the
given is the whole of reality. By making clear how completely this standpoint
fails to explain the existential positionings that play a fundamental role in
life and science, he first displayed the reality problem in its full severity.
In him, reality-constituting reason appears under the name of imagination. With
complete honesty he confesses the incurable split between thought and life into
which he falls. His approach is no less impracticable than the grounding of
arithmetic as the doctrine of number-signs present in concreto. The positivism
of Mach and Avenarius seems to Weyl only a less consistent renewal of Hume's
attempt, for in them theoretical positings, which Hume had strictly avoided,
again play a considerable role. But with that we are already in the midst of
theoretical construction, which springs from the striving to complete the given
into a totality, and we no longer have any reason to use sensory givens as its
building material. Kant's transcendental idealism restored insights already
won by Leibniz. The content of this section can be regarded as an elucidation
of Kant's concept of reality as that which coheres with perception according
to laws. Kant goes beyond Leibniz by transforming the old metaphysical
concepts of being, substance and causality, into methodological principles for
constructing experiential reality.

In the logical part Weyl insisted that existence cannot be predicated of
something exhibited, that the logical symbol `Σx` carries an index `x` that
refers to an empty place. This seems to be contradicted by a judgment such as
"this chair is real." But the assertion of real existence contains either, if
understood idealistically, a prediction about a fullness of possible
concordant intuitions that will appear in response to certain volitional
intentions, or, if understood realistically, the assertion that a thing `x`
exists that stands in a certain metaphysical relation to the given chair
appearance.

There is a fitting analogue in geometry for the problem realism-idealism, and
it is materially connected with that problem insofar as, for the objective
world, the coordinate system takes over the role of the I for the subjective.
As "things in themselves," as in section 12, we regard the vectors in a plane;
as "real subject," a coordinate system consisting of two linearly independent
vectors `e₁, e₂`. A vector `ξ` can be represented uniquely in the form

```text
ξ = ξ₁e₁ + ξ₂e₂,
```

and the number-pair `(ξ₁, ξ₂)` of the components is interpreted as the
"appearance" of the "thing" `ξ` for the "subject" `(e₁, e₂)`. The "given" here
is numbers, and Weyl now assumes that only the number-realm, not the
geometrical space of our intuition, stood open. Even then we can construct the
plane affine vector-geometry within algebra, indeed the development of algebra
itself leads to this with necessity. One defines a vector as any number-pair
`(x₁, x₂)`. Let `x₁, x₂` be called its absolute components. Two number-pairs
`(x₁, x₂)` and `(x₁′, x₂′)` are added, or the first is multiplied by a number
`a`, by forming the pair `(x₁ + x₁′, x₂ + x₂′)` or `(ax₁, ax₂)`. If

```text
e₁ = (e₁₁, e₁₂),     e₂ = (e₂₁, e₂₂)
```

are two vectors for which

```text
ξ₁e₁ + ξ₂e₂     (*)
```

is equal to `(0, 0)` only when the two numbers `ξ₁, ξ₂` vanish, then every
vector `ξ` can be uniquely represented in the form `(*)`. Let `ξ₁, ξ₂` be
called the relative components of `ξ` with respect to the system `(e₁, e₂)`.
There is a special "absolute system":

```text
e₁ = (1, 0),     e₂ = (0, 1),
```

with respect to which the relative components coincide with the absolute
components. Among the possible systems, the "subjects," which occur here only
as arithmetical "appearances," one system, the "I," is thus absolutely
distinguished. Yet in a certain sense one can also do justice, on this
algebraic or "conscientialistic" standpoint, to the equal entitlement of all
subjects. If one marks as objective or real only those operations and relations
that are defined solely by means of the two basic connections that let `ξ + ξ′`
arise from `ξ` and `ξ′`, and `aξ` from `a` and `ξ`, then all systems are
objectively equally entitled. The relation, belonging to "pure consciousness,"
of `ξ` to its two absolute components is not objective. But it is a special
case of the real relation that exists between the "thing" `ξ`, the "subject"
`(e₁, e₂)`, and the "appearance," the components `ξ₁, ξ₂` of `ξ` relative to
`(e₁, e₂)`. In algebra one expresses the "objective" standpoint by studying
only those arithmetical operations and relations that are invariant under
arbitrary linear transformations.

Thing, subject, and appearance here all belong to the same appearing world
pervaded by objective relations. Real subject and object, I, Thou, and external
world arise together, as it were, by carrying the invariant-theoretical
standpoint into the arithmetical appearances, since they are correlated with
one another. At this point the analogy grants Leibniz the right against
Descartes, who through his `cogito, ergo sum` assigned the reality of the I a
principled priority over the reality of the external world. The analogy makes
it readily intelligible how the one sense-giving I of pure consciousness can
appear, in objective attitude, as one individual subject among many like it.

Yet despite the objective equal entitlement of the different subjects, in
truth the absolute subject, I, remains absolutely distinguished. This merely
corresponds to the fact that I find. From the standpoint of knowledge alone,
nothing can be objected against conscientialism; it can be carried through
completely. Nevertheless, the recognition of the Thou is demanded from me not
only in the sense that I submit in thought to an abstract norm, "objectivity,"
but absolutely: for yourself you are once again what I am for myself, not a
being but a consciously-being bearer of the appearing world. We can take this
step in the analogy only if, since by assumption space as an intuitive given
was supposed to be closed to us, we pass from the arithmetical model of affine
vector geometry to the axiomatic description, into which the concepts of
vector and of the two basic connections enter undefined. In the axiomatic
system, the equal entitlement of all coordinates need not first be forced by
abstraction. Rather, an individual vector-pair can be distinguished in it only
by individual indication. The model and source of every such indication,
however, is the word "I." Thus axiomatics again shows itself to be the method
of purified realism, which posits a transcendent being but is content with its
copy in the symbol.

The positing of I, Thou, and external world has no influence on the cognitive
treatment of reality. It is a metaphysical matter, not judgment but an act of
recognition or of faith; Weyl points to Fichte's `The Vocation of Man`. But
this faith is nevertheless the soul of all knowledge. From the
metaphysical-realist standpoint, of course, I-ness remains a riddle. Leibniz
believes he solves the conflict of human freedom and divine predestination by
having God, among the infinitely many possibilities and for sufficient
reasons, choose certain ones, for example the essences of Judas and Peter, for
existence, whose substantial nature determines their whole fate. The solution
may be objectively sufficient, but it shatters before Judas's cry of despair:
why did I have to be Judas? The impossibility of an objective formulation of
this question is evident, and therefore no answer can be given in the form of
objective knowledge. Knowledge cannot bring the light-I into coincidence with
the dark, erring human being thrust out into an individual fate.

The positing of the real external world does not guarantee that this world
will constitute itself in reason out of the appearances through the
knowledge-work that creates concordance. For that, it is still necessary that
it be pervaded by simple elementary laws. The mere positing of the external
world therefore does not actually explain what it was supposed to explain.
Rather, the question of its reality flows inseparably together with the
question of the ground for the lawlike-mathematical harmony of the world. Thus
the final answer lies after all, beyond knowledge, only in God, from whom the
light of consciousness flows forth, its own origin hidden from it, grasping
itself in self-penetration, split and stretched between subject and object,
between sense and being.

## Notes

- Section 17 turns the space-time analysis into an epistemology of science:
  natural science is realist in using real conditions to explain perceptual
  differences, but idealist in refusing unobservable differences in the
  objective world-picture.
- `subjektiv-absolut / objektiv-relativ` is the section's central pair.
  Immediate lived givenness is absolute because it is just this given; the
  objective world is relative because it is symbolically represented only
  through an arbitrary coordinate system.
- The electric-field example shows the construction of an objective entity out
  of observed force effects: the test body makes the field measurable but does
  not define its being.
- General relativity supplies the strongest case: the visual constellation is
  lived only by an `Ich` identified with a point-eye, while the objective
  construction is invariant under coordinate transformations.
- The Schwarzschild and Poincaré examples give Weyl's anti-surplus rule:
  theoretical differences that cannot be broken open into possible perceptual
  differences are not allowed into the objective world-picture.
- The Helmholtz-Husserl bridge is not a collapse into sign theory. Helmholtz's
  sensory signs and Husserl's perceptual constitution are intermediate stages;
  the final scientific account relates symbolic reality to possible givens
  while suppressing those intermediate stages.
- The coordinate-system analogy extends section 13: the coordinate system is
  to the objective world what the I is to subjective givenness. Invariant
  theory lets real subject, object, appearance, I, Thou, and external world
  arise correlatively.
- The theological ending is not ornamental. Weyl says the positing of external
  world does not explain lawful mathematical harmony; that harmony pushes the
  question beyond objective knowledge.

## Adversarial Check

The main risk is to read the section as a simple defense of scientific realism.
It is not. Weyl explicitly gives realism and idealism complementary
methodological roles inside natural science: realism grounds perceptual
differences in real conditions, while idealism forbids objective differences
with no possible perceptual consequences.

The second risk is to mistake `objective-relative` for a relativist weakening
of objectivity. For Weyl, relativity is the price of objectivity: invariant
structure is objective precisely because it survives the arbitrary coordinate
system required to symbolize it.

The third risk is to treat the metaphysical close as a retreat from the
scientific argument. It is better read as the limit of the argument: objective
science can demand concordance and anti-surplus observability, but cannot by
itself explain why the real external world is lawfully and mathematically
harmonious at all.
