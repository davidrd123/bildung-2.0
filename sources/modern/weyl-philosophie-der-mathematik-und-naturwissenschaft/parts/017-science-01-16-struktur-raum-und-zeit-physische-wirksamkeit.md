## Translation

### 16. Structure Of Space And Time In Their Physical Efficacy

The possible space-time positions, or "world-points," form a
four-dimensional continuum. Only space-time coincidence and immediate
space-time neighborhood have a sense that can be directly and clearly exhibited
in intuition. One is already ascribing a definite structure to the
four-dimensional extensive medium of the external world when one believes in a
splitting of the world into an absolute space and an absolute time in the sense
that it would have objective meaning to say of two separate, space-time-limited
events that they happen at the same place at different times, or at the same
time at different places. All simultaneous world-points form a
three-dimensional stratum; all same-place world-points form a one-dimensional
fiber. According to this view, the structure of the world can therefore be
described by saying that it possesses a fibration and a stratification running
transversely to the fibers: through every world-point runs one fiber and one
stratum, and each fiber and stratum intersect in a single world-point. If, for
the sake of graphic representation, we drop one spatial dimension and therefore
deal only with processes on a surface, especially on a plane, represent this by
a horizontal plane E, and set time t vertically to it, then in our intuitive
space we can design an image of the world in which the strata of simultaneous
world-points appear as horizontal planes, while the fibers of same-place
world-points are represented by vertical straight lines.

One further ascribes a metric structure to time and space, assuming that the
equality of time intervals and the congruence of spatial figures have objective
sense. The assertions of Euclidean geometry describe the spatial metric
structure more precisely. If equal time intervals correspond in our graphic
image to equal lengths of the vertical time-axis, then the motion of a body
flying along a straight line with uniform velocity, its graphic timetable, is
represented by a slanted straight line. On this "worldline" lie all and only
those space-time positions in which the body is present in the course of its
history. The worldlines of resting bodies are vertical straight lines. Two
bodies meet when their worldlines intersect in one space-time point.

The separation of structure from the underlying amorphous continuum, the
recognition that space in itself is only the medium of "contact," is already
documented in Aristotle's conception of space. Lobachevsky says: "Contact is
the distinguishing mark of bodies, and to it they owe the name geometrical
bodies, as soon as we hold fast to this property in them, while leaving out of
account all others, whether essential or accidental." Here, of course, the
thought is expressed only for space, not for space-time.

Whatever the inner ground for the structure of the world-medium may be, all
natural laws show that it has the most decisive effect on the course of
physical occurrences. It announces itself in the behavior of rigid bodies and
clocks, in the straight-line uniform motion of a mass-point subject to no
influence, in the straightness of a light ray in empty space used for sighting,
in the propagation of a light, sound, or water wave in concentric spheres or
circles, and so on. The task is to know this structure from these physical
effects. How, one must ask, can I objectively establish same-place-ness or
simultaneity of events, equality of time intervals, and congruence of spatial
regions?

As for the first question, the doctrine of the relativity of motion has always
been opposed to the dogma of absolute space. Aristotle designates "place"
(`τόπος`) as a relation of a body to the bodies in its surroundings. Descartes
defines motion as the "transfer of a part of matter, or of a body, from the
neighborhood of those bodies that immediately touch it and are regarded as at
rest, into the neighborhood of other bodies." Locke discusses the relativity of
place with force. Galileo gives a nice account of it in the example of the
writer who makes notes aboard a moving ship and who therefore, "in truth," that
is, relative to the earth, draws with his pen a long, lightly wavy smooth line
from Venice to Alexandretta. Leibniz, with complete thoroughness and also in a
logical-epistemological respect, defends the relativity of spatial position in
his polemics against Clarke and Newton. There it is aptly illustrated by the
analogy of positions in a genealogical family tree.

Leibniz's argument in the third letter is also important. Under the
presupposition that space were something in itself, and thus more than the mere
order of bodies among one another, no reason could be given why God placed the
bodies at this definite position in space rather than at another, preserving
their distances and mutual positions, or why everything was not arranged in
reverse through an exchange of east and west. But if space is nothing other
than this order and relation itself, and without bodies is nothing but the
possibility of giving them a definite position, then the two states, the
original and its reversal, differ in nothing. Their apparent difference is only
a consequence of our chimerical assumption of the reality of space in itself.
In truth, one would be exactly the same as the other, since they are altogether
indistinguishable, and therefore the question why one state was preferred to
the other is completely inadmissible. Newton, the absolutist, by contrast,
regards motion as proof of the creation of the world from God's free arbitrary
will; otherwise it would be impossible to discover why matter moves in this
direction rather than in some other. Leibniz's theology forbids him to burden
God with decisions made "without sufficient reason."

The reference-body on which we rely, with good reason, almost always in daily
life when we speak of rest and motion is the "firm, well-founded earth." In
practice this choice is by far the most appropriate, and it presses itself on
us as self-evident. Only a fantasy that did not bind itself to sensory
appearance and that constructed freely in space could detach itself from this.
Thus Anaxagoras already designed the shadow-cone of the earth in world-space
and inferred from eclipses and phases the correct spatial arrangement of
earth, sun, moon, and fixed stars; in the "face of the moon" he recognized the
shadow-effect of its mountains. By the same method the Pythagoreans arrived at
the hypothesis of the earth's motion. And Aristotle's return to the geocentric
system arises from a conscious opposition to this Pythagorean-Platonic spirit
of a priori mathematical construction.

At the same time, however, granting the earth, the dwelling-place of the human
race, an absolute prerogative over all other reference-bodies expresses a
definite religious attitude toward the universe. It is the attempt to maintain
the idealist position, according to which I am the absolute center of the world
revealed to me, within the objective-real sphere, where that position finds its
limit through the recognition of the Thou demanded by the I; it is maintained
cosmo-theo-logically by widening the I into humanity. Only for this reason did
Copernicus's book become a turning point in worldview, and it is in this
direction that Bruno drew the consequences enthusiastically and stormily. The
shattering act of redemption by the Son of God, crucifixion and resurrection,
no longer the unique pivot of world history but a quickly completed guest
performance in a small nest, repeating itself from star to star: in this
blasphemy perhaps the religiously troubling character of a doctrine that
displaces the earth from the center of the world appears most sharply. Dilthey
says that in the sentence appearing equally in Kepler, Galileo, and Descartes,
that it is foolish to think that the goal of the universe lies in man, a
complete transformation of the interpretation of the world takes place. These
thinkers were pushed toward an immanent teleology, whose expression is the
harmony and beauty of the universe, and the character of previous Christian
religiosity thereby changes. Goethe says that perhaps no greater demand had
ever been made on humanity: with the acknowledgment of Copernicanism a second
paradise, a world of innocence, poetry and piety, the testimony of the senses,
and the conviction of a poetic-religious faith went up in smoke. No wonder
people did not want to let all this go, and opposed in every way a doctrine
that authorized and summoned anyone who accepted it to a previously unknown,
indeed unimagined, freedom of thought and greatness of attitude.

From the standpoint of the relativity of motion, one cannot dispute the truth
or falsity of the Copernican system. The laws of planetary motion only become
much simpler when one constructs the motion relative to the sun rather than
relative to the earth.

Newton grounds his construction of mechanics in the `Principia` on the ideas
of absolute time, absolute space, and absolute motion. Absolute, true, and
mathematical time flows uniformly in itself and by its nature, without relation
to any external object. Absolute space remains always the same and motionless,
by its nature and without relation to any external object. Absolute motion is
the transfer of a body from one absolute place to another. While it cannot be
denied that, kinematically, there is no graspable difference between the
different possible states of motion of a body, Newton tries, on the basis of
phenomena such as centrifugal forces, to distinguish rest dynamically among
the possible states of motion. The causes by which true and relative motions
differ are the forces that have acted on the bodies to produce the motion. To
recognize the true motions of individual bodies and sharply distinguish them
from apparent ones is very difficult, since the parts of that immovable space
in which bodies truly move cannot be sensibly known. Yet the matter is not
altogether hopeless: the necessary aids arise partly from apparent motions,
which are the differences of true motions, and partly from the forces lying at
the basis of true motions as efficient causes. If, for example, two spheres are
connected by a thread at a given distance from one another and rotated around
their common center of gravity, the tension of the thread reveals the striving
of the spheres to move away from the axis of motion, and from this one can
compute the magnitude of the circular motion. Newton says that the following
treatise will teach in more detail how to infer true motions from their causes,
effects, and apparent differences, and conversely how to derive causes and
effects from true or apparent motions.

Belief in absolute space is theologically impregnated in Newton. In his
`Opticks` he says of God that, in infinite space, as if in his sensory organ,
God sees through all things in their innermost being and grasps them fully in
immediate presence. Newton rests here on the theology of Henry More. For More,
space is the first and valid witness for the truth and necessity of
"immaterial natures"; in its properties he finds again the marks of divine
substance, and space is the link between that substance and individual things.
Newton thus fixes the unique nature of world-structure, namely that it consists
in a fibration, by a metaphysical-a-priori idea. But the course of the fibration
in the actual world is to be determined from its effect on observable real
appearances: that is his natural-scientific program.

Newton does not fully master the task. Dynamically he can distinguish only
uniform translation, the pure inertial motion of a body on which no forces act,
from the other states of motion. Among translations, however, he does not
succeed in singling out rest. This cannot succeed because of the validity of
the so-called special principle of relativity, which the laws of Newtonian
mechanics satisfy and which today can be supported for all natural phenomena
by a whole series of the subtlest experiments: in the cabin of a ship moving
straight ahead with uniform velocity, all processes take place exactly as if
the ship were at rest. Along with any natural process, the process obtained
from it by imposing a common uniform translation on all bodies involved in it
is always also possible. Galileo develops the principle clearly and vividly in
the `Dialogo`. Newton helps himself here by a hypothesis that cannot be
anchored in experience and by a dialectical trick, both of which look quite
strange in the midst of the splendidly rigorous inductive construction of his
world-system in Book III of the `Principia`. The hypothesis says that the
universe has a center and that the center is at rest. The common center of
gravity of the solar system moves, as is correctly inferred from the laws of
mechanics, like that of any body-system withdrawn from external influences,
uniformly in a straight line. Newton then says: if it went forward forever, the
center of the world would not be at rest, without in any way grounding the
identification of the center of gravity of the planetary system with the
assumed resting world-center, unless by the fact that only such a point
constructible from material processes can come into question as a resting
center at all, a point that according to the laws of mechanics is in uniform
translation.

The experiences that prove the dynamical non-equivalence of different states
of motion teach us that the world carries a structure. But the concept of
absolute space obviously does not correctly grasp this inertial structure. The
division does not run between rest and motion, but between uniform translation
and accelerated motion. In view of the graphic representation described above,
we can say: it is exactly the same in the world as in space. Straight lines are
objectively distinguished among all lines, but from the family of all straight
lines the "vertical" ones can be selected only by convention, which rests on
individual exhibition.

How do matters stand with stratification, with the concept of simultaneity?
Belief in its objective meaning originally rests, without doubt, on the fact
that everyone with complete self-evidence places the things he sees into the
moment of his perception. In this way I extend my time over the whole world
that enters my field of vision. Even after this naive view was deprived of its
ground by the discovery of the finite velocity of the propagation of light,
there still remained, apart from the conservatism of a prejudice once adopted,
some reasons for holding fast to that belief. In our graphic representation
the horizontal plane running through a world-point O separates future and past
from O. What does that mean? If from O I shoot bullets in all directions, with
all possible velocities, they reach only world-points that are later than O; I
cannot shoot into the past. Likewise an event occurring in O has influence only
on what happens in later world-points, while in the past "nothing can be
changed anymore." Stratification therefore has a causal sense; it fixes the
world's connection of effects. Leibniz recognized this when he declared that
if, of two elements that are not simultaneous, one includes the ground of the
other, then the former is regarded as preceding and the latter as following.
One simple method for instantaneous transfer of time from one place A to
another B consists in giving a jolt at A to a rigid rod reaching from A to B;
the jolt observed in B is simultaneous with the one produced in A.

But with respect to the causal structure of the world, modern development has
led to an essential correction. In the graphic representation, the world-points
at which a light signal given in O and propagating in all directions with the
same velocity c arrives form the mantle of a straight circular cone with vertex
at O. Its opening angle is 90 degrees if, on the t-axis, we make the segment
representing one second of time just as long as the spatial segment that
represents, in the horizontal plane E, the path traveled by light during one
second. It has compellingly emerged, in Einstein's special theory of
relativity, that it is not the horizontal plane through O, but this mantle of
the "light cone," extended backward, that effects the division of the world
into past and future. No effect can propagate with a speed greater than the
speed of light, not even the jolt given to a rigid rod for the purpose of
transferring time; the velocity of every body necessarily remains below c.
This is an unavoidable consequence of the special principle of relativity and
the "constancy of the speed of light," by which one is to understand the fact
that the light cone issuing from O is independent of the state, especially the
state of motion, of the light source emitting the signal at O, and is therefore
determined by O alone.

If I am in O, then O divides my life-line, the worldline of my body, into two
pieces, past and future; nothing is changed about that. But as far as my
relation to the world is concerned, in the forward cone lie all those
world-points on which my doing and letting-be in O has influence; outside it
lie all the events that are completed behind me and about which nothing can now
be changed. The forward cone is my active future. By contrast, inside the rear
cone are localized all those events that I either bodily lived through or saw
with my own eyes, or of which some report could have reached me; only these
events can possibly have had influence on me. It is the region of my passive
past. The two regions, that of the active future and that of the passive past,
do not border on one another without an intervening region, as they did
according to the old view.

It remains to describe physically how the equality of times and the congruence
of material bodies are established. A clock is a closed material system that
once returns exactly to the same state Z in which it already found itself at an
earlier moment. Under the presupposition of the principle of causality in the
form that the state of a system at one moment uniquely determines its whole
history, the same process, the same cyclic succession of states that leads from
Z to Z, will then repeat itself again and again, and each of these periods has
by definition the same duration. What is measured in this way is called the
proper time of the clock; it is directly usable for all events that occur along
the clock's worldline. Helmholtz says that time-measurement presupposes that
physical processes have been found which, repeating themselves in an
unchangedly identical way and under identical conditions, end again
simultaneously if they began in the same moment, or more correctly in
immediately neighboring space-time points: days, pendulum-strokes, the running
of sand and water clocks, for example. The warrant for assuming unchanged
duration lies only in the circumstance that all different methods of
time-measurement, carefully carried out, always again deliver concordant
results.

Concerning the empirical determination of spatial congruence, Helmholtz says
elsewhere that he calls spatial magnitudes physically equivalent when, under
the same conditions and in the same time intervals, the same physical processes
can exist and take place in them. The process most often used, under suitable
precautions, to determine physically equivalent spatial magnitudes is the
transfer of rigid bodies, such as compasses and measuring rods, from one place
to another. It is a fact that if one and the same definite physical process is
possible in the same way in the two spatial pieces 𝔖 and 𝔖′, then every process
taking place in 𝔖 can also occur in unchanged fashion within 𝔖′. A body is
tested as a rigid body by repeatedly proving unchanged in all its properties
when it is brought back under the same conditions. Helmholtz regards physical
geometry, supported by this physically testable concept of congruence, as an
empirical science, the first and most complete of the natural sciences.
Riemann also speaks of it in his habilitation lecture and points out, in a way
that may become very important for the physics of the future, that the
empirical concepts on which spatial metric determinations are grounded, the
concept of the solid body and that of the light ray, lose their validity in the
infinitesimal. Incidentally, it can be shown that the inertial and causal
structure already co-determines the metric structure of the world, so that
clocks and rigid bodies are no longer required for measurement; light signals
and freely moving mass-points suffice.

If I have referred a three-dimensional continuum in any way to coordinates
x₀, x₁, x₂, then it is thereby mapped onto three-dimensional number-space, the
continuum of number triples. Here, in order to use an expression more familiar
to the reader, I will replace number-space by intuitive space equipped with a
Cartesian coordinate system. To apply the procedure to the four-dimensional
world, one must of course drop one dimension in thought. Two-dimensional
examples are plane geographical maps. Using the Mercator map as basis, I
observe, for instance, that San Francisco, the southern tip of Greenland, and
the North Cape lie in a straight line. But I will then not be surprised that on
an orthographic projection representing the northern hemisphere of the earth
this is by no means the case. Likewise, a definite mapping of the world serves
as the basis for using the usual geometrical-kinematical terms, with the
x₀-axis assigned the meaning of time-axis. Of a body whose worldline appears
in the image as a vertical straight line, that is, as a line on which of the
four coordinates x₀, x₁, x₂, x₃ only x₀ changes, we will say, for example, that
it is at rest.

Only relations that remain independent of the chosen mapping under arbitrary
deformation of the image have objective meaning; such a relation is, for
example, the intersection of two worldlines. If a special mapping, or a special
class of mappings, is to be characterized, this must happen physically on the
basis of actual processes and structure: that is what the "general postulate of
relativity" demands. According to the "special theory of relativity," one can
in particular design such a "map" of the world in which the worldline of every
force-free moving mass-point appears as a straight line, and the light cone
radiating from any arbitrary world-point is represented by a vertical straight
circular cone with opening angle 90 degrees. According to this theory, the
inertial and causal structure, and with them the metric structure, have the
character of something rigid and absolutely fixed once and for all. Among those
"normal mappings" that satisfy the conditions just named, no narrower selection
can be made objectively without individual exhibition.

The discrepancy between the kinematic and dynamical analysis of motion calls
for resolution. Huygens, as we know from his letters, tried to carry through
the standpoint of the equivalence of all states of motion even in dynamics;
among his posthumous papers an attempt in this direction has been preserved.
In our own day Mach undertook the same thing in his `Mechanics`: he would like
to see in the flattening of the earth an effect of its rotation relative to the
fixed stars; the fixed stars are supposed to hold and carry along the plane of
the Foucault pendulum. Leibniz, by contrast, however firmly he opposes
Newton's metaphysics of space and however firmly he holds that space is no
more than "the mere order of bodies among one another," evidently agrees with
Newton's mechanical program of distinguishing true motion from apparent motion
by dynamical criteria. Euler is likewise of the opinion that one must, however
illuminating the principle of the relativity of motion is for reason, sacrifice
it under compulsion to dynamical experiences. From Kant's discussions in the
`Metaphysical Foundations` one can, at a pinch, extract a correct formulation
of the problem, but they contain no clarification.

Moreover, according to the general postulate of relativity, the concept of
relative motion of several bodies with respect to one another, so long as we
cannot rely on any world-structure, hangs in the air just as much as that of
the absolute motion of a single body. For if one imagines the four-dimensional
world as a mass of plasticine traversed by individual nonintersecting but
otherwise completely irregular fibers, the worldlines of material particles,
then one can continuously deform the plasticine so that not just one, but all
fibers become vertical straight lines. Therefore a solution of the problem that
would, in consistent pursuit of the Huygens-Mach tendency, eliminate
world-structure altogether is impossible. If we are therefore compelled to seek
the cause of the dynamical non-equivalence of motions in the inertial structure
of the world, then we clearly recognize what is unsatisfactory in the situation
just discussed: something that produces such powerful effects as inertia, for
example when in a train collision it tears the cars apart in struggle with the
molecular forces of the two oncoming trains, is itself supposed to be only a
rigid, once-for-all fixed geometrical property of the world.

Leibniz vigorously emphasized the dynamic character of inertia, as a tendency
that resists deflecting forces. It is not the same, he says, whether something
merely preserves its state until something enters that changes it, which can
also occur if the subject is entirely indifferent between both states, or
whether, far more significantly, it is not indifferent but has a force and, as
it were, an inclination to preserve its state and so to resist the changing
cause. The solution is therefore given as soon as one resolves to acknowledge
the inertial structure as something real that not only exercises effects on
matter but also suffers effects from it. This is the step Riemann already took
for the metric structure of space. In fact, the inertial structure is so
closely linked with the metric structure, since the metric relations of space
fix the straight lines, that one must necessarily posit the metric field as
capable of change if one wants to free the inertial field from its geometrical
rigidity.

Einstein rediscovered this thought independently of Riemann and supplemented
it with an important insight that first made it physically fruitful: from the
equality of gravitational and inertial mass, which before him had only been
registered as a puzzling fact but not understood, he inferred that gravitation
belongs, in the dualism of inertia and force, on the side of inertia rather
than force. In the phenomena of gravitation, then, the capacity for change and
the dependence on matter of the inertial field, or, as I would rather say, of
the guiding field, announces itself. The splitting of the unitary guiding field
into a homogeneous component satisfying Galileo's law and a much weaker
deviation from it, called gravitation, which lays itself around the individual
stars, is possible only relative to a definite coordinate system, not
absolutely. From this conception there followed, compellingly, the laws that
replace Newton's law of attraction and according to which matter acts on the
inertial field. These consequences have been confirmed in experience.

The guiding field is weakly excited by matter, like a sea surface by the
steamers that traverse it. It passes over into the unexcited state described by
the special theory of relativity when all matter disappears, just as the sea
surface smooths itself into a homogeneous plane when the ships are in harbor.
Although Einstein too flirts with the Machian thought, it is nevertheless
excluded, according to an earlier remark, that the guiding field or the "ether"
could be eliminated from natural occurrence as an independent power. It is not
the stars that guide the plane of oscillation of the Foucault pendulum. Rather,
the agreement of both, of the Foucault pendulum and the stellar compass formed
by the directions of the light rays arriving from the stars at the earthly
place of observation, rests on the ether's immense superiority in its
interaction with matter. The old conception, which separated inertia and
gravitation absolutely from one another, erred only in wanting, figuratively
speaking, to compose the actual position of all water particles in the basin
excited by the ships out of an absolutely distinguished rest position and an
elongation produced by the ships.

That is wrong. If the water comes to rest again in the evening when all ships
are in harbor, then we do indeed have the same "qualitative state" as in the
morning before the ships put out: the unruffled plane surface. But the
"material state" hidden behind it, namely the location of the different water
particles, may have shifted completely. This is not a contradiction of the
principle of sufficient reason, which demands a uniquely determined state of
equilibrium of the basin. If all water particles are of the same kind, then two
states of the basin Z and Z′, which arise from one another by the particles
exchanging their places in any way, are each considered by itself
undifferentiated by anything. Only after one has "laid down a coordinate
system," that is, here, after one has provided the particles with numbers that
introduce artificial differences among them and remain attached to them during
motion, can one speak of the two material states Z and Z′ as such. In truth,
however, what is graspable is not the individual material state Z, the
arrangement, but only the displacement of the material state Z -> Z′, the
permutation. Compare the remarks of Leibniz on the relativity of place given
above.

The group Δ₀ of Euclidean rotations in three-dimensional space has now been
replaced by the so-called Lorentz group. It consists of all homogeneous linear
transformations

```text
zᵢ′ = Σₖ aᵢₖ zₖ     (i, k = 0, 1, 2, 3),
```

that leave invariant the indefinite quadratic form

```text
−z₀² + z₁² + z₂² + z₃².
```

For each of these transformations, the absolute values of the coefficient
`a = a₀₀` and of the determinant `d` from the 3 x 3 coefficients `aᵢₖ`
for `i, k = 1, 2, 3` are greater than or equal to 1. We give the
transformation temporal signature `+` or `-` according as `a >= 1` or
`a <= -1`; likewise, the sign of `d` determines the spatial signature. The
Lorentz transformations with temporal signature `+` form a subgroup of index
2 of the total group, and likewise the transformations with spatial signature
`+`. Their common part is in turn contained as a subgroup of index 2 in each of
them. The transformations with temporal signature `-` exchange past and future;
those with spatial signature `-` exchange left and right. The most fundamental
experiences of our life seem to indicate that Δ₀ is restricted to Lorentz
transformations with temporal signature `+`, while including those with spatial
signature `-`. Physics, however, has found this question rather difficult to
decide. A further signature, the topological one, is attached to the coordinate
transformation; it is determined by the sign of its Jacobian determinant.

With the "general theory of relativity" we draw the result of the historical
development so far of the structure-problem of space and time as follows. The
world is a four-dimensional Riemannian space in which, after fixing a unit of
measure, to every line-element issuing from a point P with coordinates
`x₀, x₁, x₂, x₃` and leading to the infinitely neighboring point
`P′ = (xᵢ + dxᵢ)`, there belongs a measure-number

```text
ds² = Σ_{i,k=0}^{3} gᵢₖ dxᵢ dxₖ     (gₖᵢ = gᵢₖ),
```

which is independent of the arbitrary coordinate system used. The coefficients
`gᵢₖ` depend on the coordinates `x₀, x₁, x₂, x₃` of P, but not on the `dxᵢ`.
The metric fundamental form standing on the right side is not positive
definite, but has one negative dimension; that is, by introducing suitable
coordinates at the point P, it can be brought into the normal form containing
nothing indeterminate:

```text
ds² = −dx₀² + dx₁² + dx₂² + dx₃².
```

Only because of this circumstance does the "light cone in P," on which lie the
line-elements issuing from P that make `ds²` equal to zero, separate a region
of active future from a region of passive past for P. The fundamental form
determines, in a way easy to describe more closely, the behavior of clocks and
measuring rods; it fixes the course of the light cones in their whole extent
and singles out the worldlines of pure inertial motion, followed for example by
the planets, from the totality of all possible worldlines. Its coefficients,
the continuous functions `gᵢₖ(x₀, x₁, x₂, x₃)`, describe relative to the
chosen coordinate system the metric field, or the "state of the ether," which
stands in reciprocal action with matter.

In asking about the whole extent of the world, one must distinguish between
purely topological and metric relations. Bruno felt the transition from the
Aristotelian world-system enclosed in the crystalline sphere and circling
around a center to the indifferent breadth of infinite, uncentered Euclidean
space, everywhere populated by stars and underlying modern natural research,
as a mighty liberation. Nevertheless, Aristotle's space, if the bounding
spherical surface no longer belongs to space, is topologically not different
from the infinite at all, but differs only in its metric relations. Infinite
Euclidean space leads to absurdities if one assumes that the masses are
distributed uniformly throughout the whole universe on the large scale and that
Newton's law of attraction holds. The distant masses would then, although the
gravitational force of a constant mass decreases with the reciprocal square of
distance, so predominate in the total gravitational effect that the total force
exerted on a star would become entirely indeterminate.

It is also possible, however, that space is finite and yet unbounded, namely if
in analogy to the closed two-dimensional spherical surface it were a closed
three-dimensional manifold. According to an attractive interpretation by
A. Speiser, Dante transformed Aristotle's bounded space, which remains in
force for the image perceived by the senses, into closed space in archetypal
being by letting the radii proceeding from the center of the earth, the seat of
Satan, converge toward an antipole, the source-point of divine force. The force
of the personal God must radiate from a center; it cannot embrace the
world-sphere in spatial-supraspatial outpouring like Aristotle's "unmoved first
mover." In connection with his theory of gravitation and the attempt to carry
through the Machian principle in it, Einstein constructs a static universe Uₐ
with a closed three-dimensional space in which matter is uniformly distributed.
The total mass of all stars determines the volume of total space. In contrast
to Dante's space, Einstein's naturally lacks a distinguished pole and antipole;
it is as homogeneous as Euclidean space. Uₐ results as a possible solution of
the laws of gravitation, provided the so-called cosmological term is included
in them, introducing a universal constant `a` with the dimension of a distance
and the order of magnitude of the "world-radius."

If one drops two of its spatial dimensions, one can imagine Uₐ as a vertical
circular-cylinder surface with radius `a` and infinite extension in both
directions. This shows that Uₐ has two separate "rims," one in the infinitely
distant past and the other in the infinitely distant future; in this
topological sense Uₐ stretches from eternity to eternity. With the same
reduction of dimensions, the map of the universe U∞ according to the shared
Euclid-Bruno conception, that is, an empty world whose metric structure is
described by the special theory of relativity, is a vertical plane and
therefore has only one connected rim. In the last analysis, the concepts of
closed and open space amount to this topological difference between Uₐ and U∞:
two rims as against one.

According to Einsteinian cosmology, the metric relations are such that the
light cone issuing from a world-point rolls over itself infinitely often. An
observer would therefore have to see infinitely many images of a star, if the
images were not blurred by thin clouding media in world-space and by
deflections. These images would show the star to him in states between which
each time the time had elapsed that light needs to run all the way around the
world-sphere. The present would be interspersed with ghosts of the long-past.
According to de Sitter, however, the laws of gravitation also make possible a
mass-empty world, extending from eternity to eternity, in which the self-overlap
of the region of the future issuing from a world-point does not occur. The
systematic shift of the spectral lines of the most distant heavenly objects,
the spiral nebulae, toward the red side of the spectrum has been interpreted in
the sense of an expanding universe, for which de Sitter's construction provides
the simplest means. Weyl, Friedmann, Lemaitre, H. P. Robertson, and others are
named here. For `a` one obtains in this way a value of about `10²⁷ cm`.
Moreover, the behavior of every world that satisfies certain natural
homogeneity conditions on the large scale, whether it is empty or contains
mass, follows this model asymptotically when the world-radius becomes
essentially larger than `a` in the expansion process. The demand that, from
every world-point O, two separated world-regions of active future and passive
past should result, separated not merely in the nearest neighborhood of O but
throughout their whole course, makes a world closed not only spatially but also
temporally impossible. In such a world, what is objectively unique would appear
according to the tradition passed down from generation to generation as the
"eternal recurrence" of the same world-course.

## Notes

This section is the promised conversion of chapter III's geometry into
physics. The point is not that physics merely applies an independently fixed
geometry. Weyl explicitly argues that the world-structure must be read from
physical effects, and then that the metric/inertial structure itself must be
real and mutable because it acts on matter and receives action from matter.

The Newton-Leibniz dispute is doing double work. It continues the section-14
left/right and invariance problem, but now under the pressure of physical
motion. Leibniz blocks absolute placement by sufficient reason, while Newton
tries to recover absolute motion through dynamics. Weyl's resolution is not
Machian elimination of structure, but a physicalized structure: the guiding
field.

The light cone reframes simultaneity. The old horizontal simultaneity stratum
is replaced by active future and passive past. The subject-centered language is
not ornamental; it prepares the next section on subject/object by making
space-time causality already speak in the grammar of doing, suffering, memory,
and possible report.

The final cosmology paragraph keeps topology and metric apart. Closed/open
space is not simply a matter of finite/infinite metric size; it becomes a
question of rims, self-overlap, and whether active future and passive past stay
globally separated.

## Adversarial Check

- Do not read Weyl as simply endorsing Newtonian absolute structure. He
  criticizes Newton's absolute space but preserves objective world-structure in
  physicalized form.
- Do not flatten the Mach discussion into anti-structure relativism. Weyl says
  the attempt to eliminate world-structure altogether is impossible.
- Do not translate `Führungsfeld` as ordinary gravitational field. It names the
  unified inertial/guiding field whose deviation appears as gravitation.
- Do not treat simultaneity as merely a coordinate convention. Weyl relocates
  the problem into the causal structure of light cones, active future, and
  passive past.
- Do not collapse topology into metric size in the cosmology passage. The
  finite/unbounded and closed/open distinctions depend on topological
  structure, not only on metric extension.
