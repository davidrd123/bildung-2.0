## Translation

Mathematics has traditionally been characterized as the science of magnitude,
or of space and number. Even when we find this definition in Leibniz, however,
the `mathesis` thus delimited is for him only a subdivision of the `ars
combinatoria`. Today the definition must seem far too narrow, given fields such
as projective geometry and group theory. We therefore do not need to settle more
precisely the concept of the quantitative, and the development of mathematics
itself raises doubts about whether quantity is a well-bounded and important
philosophical category.

Geometry, insofar as it investigates actual space, is no longer counted as pure
mathematics. Like mechanics and physics, it belongs in principle to mathematics'
applications. Under the influence first of the general arithmetic of
hypercomplex numbers, then of axiomatic investigations, set theory, and
logistic, the distinction between mathematics and logic becomes blurred.
Benjamin Peirce's 1870 definition, "Mathematics is the science, which draws
necessary conclusions," belongs to this standpoint, as do the relevant chapters
in Husserl and Russell.

The crisis brought about by the antinomies of set theory, whether one follows
Brouwer's consistent intuitionism or Hilbert's symbolism, lets the distinctive
character of mathematics stand out more clearly again. Brouwer, like Plato, sees
the root of mathematical thinking in two-oneness. The neo-intuitionist account
finds an originary event of mathematical thinking in the falling apart of lived
moments into qualitatively different parts, separated by time and able to reunite
again, and in abstracting this falling-apart from all affective content into the
bare intuition of two-oneness. We saw that, by the repeated becoming of "one
into two," the division schema of the one-dimensional continuum arises. The
whole numbers, in binary notation, arise in the same way. Stenzel makes it
likely that Plato ordered the numbers in this schema, calling the dyad the
great-small because this splitting leads to ever larger numbers, while in the
continuum it leads to ever smaller parts.

For whole numbers, however, the natural sequence is more appropriate, and
Aristotle opposes it to the Platonic conception of number. It too can be made to
arise from two-oneness: begin with something undivided, split off an element,
the `1`, preserving it as a unit, and leave an undivided remainder; then split
the remainder again into an element, `2`, and a further undivided remainder, and
so on. Intuitively, one repeatedly cuts a piece from a ray, or lives through a
piece of time that remains open toward the future. Here it is not every part
that falls under bisection, but always only the last remaining undivided
remainder.

Whatever value one gives this final reduction of mathematical thinking to
two-oneness, from the intuitionistic standpoint complete induction appears as
what saves mathematics from being an enormous tautology and stamps its
assertions with a synthetic, non-analytic character. Complete induction is a
pervasive and decisive feature. Where it initially seems absent, for instance in
elementary and projective geometry, the reason is that a naive use is made of
"there is" and "all" with respect to points. For intuitionism this is
inadmissible. The constructive field of geometry is a continuum and is capable
of strict mathematical treatment only after being overlaid with a division net.

From the formalist standpoint, the transfinite axiom component takes the place
of complete induction and gives mathematics its character. Mathematics here does
not consist at all of evident truths. It is bold theoretical construction, the
extreme opposite of analytic obviousness. On the other hand, the contentful
thinking of metamathematics, which must furnish the proof of consistency,
operates through the proof-sequence with a finite inference from `n` to `n + 1`
and concerns itself with extralogical concrete objects that can be completely
surveyed in all their parts. This is why Hilbert agrees with Kant that
mathematics possesses a content secured independently of all logic and therefore
can never be grounded by logic alone.

Still, with Kant's use of the words analytic and synthetic, a single determinate
equation such as `3 + 2 = 5` would have to be called analytic, since, as Leibniz
explained, it follows logically from the definitions `3 + 1 = 4`,
`4 + 1 = 5`, and `(a + 1) + 1 = a + 2`; it thus "lies in the concepts" of the
numbers 3 and 5 and the operation `+ 2`. Mathematics is unquestionably a priori.
It is not grounded in experience in Mill's sense, as if repeated trials on
number examples gave ever greater truth-weight to an arithmetic theorem such as
`m + n = n + m`.

One conspicuous feature of all mathematics, and one that makes access difficult
for laypeople, is the abundant use of symbols. From the intuitionistic
standpoint this is not an essential mark; symbols, like language, are aids to
memory through fixation and aids to communication. In formalism it is different.
Mathematics consists entirely of symbols that have no meaning to be exhibited in
sensuous or intellectual intuition and that are operated on according to secure
rules. Language, by contrast, remains the always somewhat insecure medium for
communicating procedures and meaningful acts of thought. Speiser's remark that
mathematics freed itself from language first in the geometrical figure and later
in the mathematical formula fits this point.

Kant, in the transcendental doctrine of method, locates the essence of
mathematics in construction: philosophical knowledge is rational knowledge from
concepts, mathematical knowledge from the construction of concepts. His detailed
account of construction no longer satisfies Weyl, but this much remains true:
in proving a mathematical proposition one almost always has to go far beyond
the immediate content of the proposition. A proof carried out according to the
syllogistic rule is not a monotone construction, unlike the production of a
formula in which all construction-parts are preserved in the finished result. It
is a constant alternation of building up and tearing down. Together with the
points listed in § 6, this roughly characterizes construction as opposed to pure
contemplation.

The stages through which foundational research in mathematics has recently
passed correspond to three fundamental epistemological attitudes. The
set-theoretic foundation is the stage of naive realism, unaware of the passage
from the given to the transcendent. Brouwer represents idealism by demanding the
reduction of all truth to what is given in intuition. Finally, in axiomatic
formalism consciousness attempts to "jump over its own shadow," leaving behind
the material of the given and representing the transcendent, but only in symbol.

Western philosophy has held fast to the idealist standpoint in epistemology
from Descartes onward, yet repeatedly sought in metaphysics an access to the
realm of the absolute; even after Kant, who wanted once and for all to bolt the
door, came Fichte, Schelling, and Hegel. There is undeniably alive in us a
theoretical need, unintelligible from the merely phenomenal standpoint, that
presses toward totality. Mathematics shows this with special clarity. But from
mathematics we may also learn that this need can be satisfied only on one
condition: that we be content with the symbol and renounce the mystical error
that the transcendent could ever fall within the circle of light of our
intuitive insight.

Only in mathematics and physics has theoretical construction so far achieved
such firmness that it is compelling for everyone whose mind opens itself to
those sciences. This is the main basis of their philosophical interest. If one
wants a brief slogan for the living center of mathematics, one may say:
mathematics is the science of the infinite. To have made the tension between
finite and infinite fruitful for knowledge of reality is the great achievement
of the Greeks. Weyl has tried to make felt what significance this tension, and
the attempts to overcome it, had and still has for the history of theoretical
knowledge.

## Notes

The chapter does not end by choosing Brouwer or Hilbert. It uses both to recover
mathematics' distinctive character: intuitionism shows why mathematics is not a
mere tautology; formalism shows why mathematics is bold theoretical
construction rather than immediate evident truth.

Weyl's three-stage map is explicit: set-theoretic foundation equals naive
realism, Brouwer equals idealism, and axiomatic formalism equals symbolic
presentation of the transcendent. This is the chapter's strongest promotion
signal, because it condenses the whole technical discussion into a general
epistemological pattern.

The key synthesis term is `Wissenschaft vom Unendlichen`. Weyl has steadily
shown that the infinite is not just a mathematical topic but the pressure point
where construction, intuition, symbol, metaphysics, and physics meet.

The final anti-mystical condition matters: the theoretical need for totality is
real, but it must be satisfied in symbol. The transcendent does not become
something directly seen.

## Adversarial Check

The easy misreading is to treat this final section as a neutral survey of
definitions of mathematics. It is more than that: Weyl is reclassifying the
foundational crisis as an epistemological drama about the given, the
transcendent, symbol, and totality.

The other weak reading is to make Weyl simply anti-metaphysical. He is not. He
explicitly acknowledges a theoretical need that presses toward totality. His
constraint is not positivist rejection but symbolic discipline: totality can be
represented by theoretical construction, not possessed as intuitive sight.
