## Translation

### 23a. Causality And Law

Cause and effect govern both theoretical knowledge and practical action, but
Weyl starts by warning that it is difficult to isolate the parts of causality
that actually enter research. Kant's first-edition formulation says that
everything that happens presupposes something on which it follows according
to a rule, and Kant joins this to the third analogy: coexisting substances
stand in thoroughgoing community or reciprocal interaction.

Hume first subjects the category of causality to serious analysis. Cause and
effect appear as spatially and temporally contiguous, so every action at a
distance requires continuous mediation. The causal chain answers the question
"why?" by interpolating an unbroken sequence, and it runs in the direction
past to future. Hume then rejects necessary connection as empirically empty:
we have only one world, and in that world `B` in fact follows `A`. He replaces
necessity with constant conjunction.

But constant conjunction is still not enough. A fully concrete event happens
only once. Causality therefore requires continuity assumptions and typifying
concepts: similar causes have similar effects, remote bodies and events have
negligible influence, and the world-flow must be decomposed, as in section 20,
into simple recurring quantitative elements. The causal relation does not hold
between absolutely individual events but between classes of events and
isolated lawful elements.

Natural law then replaces the causal relation. If several quantities stand in
a functional relation, the same law can be read in different determining
directions. A function-law is indifferent to the difference between determinant
and determined, whereas causality is not. Galileo's fall laws, Newton's refusal
to invent hypotheses about gravity's cause, d'Alembert and Lagrange's closed
dynamical description, and Mach's attack on the "fetishism" of cause all mark
this turn from cause to law.

Weyl then separates law from shape. Kepler still found rationality in the
geometrical form of the planetary system; morphology and crystallography keep
the idea of form alive. But modern physics treats continuous infinitesimal
local-action laws as original. Typical shapes arise when those laws admit
discrete static or periodic solutions.

The pure field or central-force dream seems to restore the world as one whole
in which everything interweaves with everything else. Its causal form is
Laplacean: the world's state in one moment determines all past and future by
differential laws. Yet Weyl sees a danger here. If arbitrary complications are
allowed, lawfulness becomes a mathematical triviality. The astonishing point
is not that some functional relation can be written down, but that the laws
have simple mathematical structure while the quantitative course of the world
is unimaginably complex.

The causal principle also demands a dense enough web of dependencies. This
raises the practical question: where is the cut between conditions and event?
That cut is simple when cause and effect belong to different existential
spheres, as when real conditions underlie an immanent perception. But in nature
cause and effect lie on the same plane. The usual field-physics cut is a
space-like slice `t = const.` separating past and future, but Weyl treats this
as one special causal form rather than causality itself.

Experiment gives another cut: conditions are the part of the event under the
experimenter's power. Will also matters. Because we experience in ourselves a
determining power, and because we act and suffer inside the stream of nature,
we view nature under the metaphysical aspect of cause and effect. Leibniz is
important here: as the inner understanding of "I" opens the concept of
substance, self-consideration also gives the concepts of cause and effect.

Helmholtz gives Weyl's preferred methodological formulation. The law that the
same happens under the same conditions is not an empirical law; as an inductive
claim it would be as weak as meteorological rules. It is a demand to understand
everything, a norm that we force into the construction of reality. In this
sense Helmholtz's causal principle coincides with Kant's doctrine of the
categories.

The day-night example tests this account. Day and night are regularly
successive, yet not causal. Mill, Hume, and continuity arguments fail to settle
the case cleanly. What finally matters is analogy with intervention: I can make
it bright by lighting a lamp, so I put the sun in parallel with such a light
under my control and imagine that if I extinguished it, darkness would follow.
Mill's inductive rules are therefore only a rough first description of
research method; they do not explain how a unified fact is separated into the
different "instances" to which the rules apply.

Causality also appears in physics as a factual component of theory: causal
structure. Pre-relativistic physics allowed instantaneous propagation. After
relativity, light-speed is the upper bound for all propagation and signal
speeds, so distant effects really begin later than their causes. Kant's third
analogy, the community of simultaneous coexistents, falls away. Subjectively,
the future-opening light cone differs absolutely from the past-facing light
cone, because we travel on our bodily world-line with "shuttered
consciousness" and receive direct perceptual news only from the rear cone.
Field laws themselves, however, do not mark that subjective difference; they
allow both outgoing and incoming spherical waves. If the one-directionality of
time is to have a physical ground, it must be found in the laws by which matter
excites the field. Thermodynamics seems to provide this through entropy, but
atomic theory reduces thermodynamics to statistics and introduces chance.

### 23b. Chance

The judgments that guide action rarely fit classical logic's sharp alternatives
of true and false. Future-directed questions are judged as possible, probable,
unavoidable, risky, hopeful, or fearful before any later reality can confirm
them. Even if one hesitates to call these knowledge and judgment, they have the
structure of judgments and are vital to life.

Probability begins historically with gambling. Pascal, Fermat, Huyghens, and
Jakob Bernoulli first analyze chances, expectation, hope, and conjecture.
Laplace's classical definition gives probability as favorable cases divided
by all possible cases, but it presupposes that the cases are equally possible.
For continuous possibilities this immediately becomes a measure problem: the
probability of finding a particle in a region is additive over subregions just
as volume is. Modern probability therefore belongs naturally with measure
theory.

From Laplace's deterministic standpoint, probability can have only a subjective
sense. It concerns events whose premises we do not fully know. Given
probabilities can be combined mathematically under disjunction, conjunction,
exclusion, and statistical independence, but probability is never absolutely
determined.

Jakob Bernoulli's theorem of large numbers bridges subjective and objective
probability. It supports laws that appear not in individual events but in
averages over many similar events. Bernoulli's urn schema shows how a priori
probability should appear as relative frequency over long series. Yet the
connection to reality still has an irreducible inexactness: very high
probability gives practical certainty, not logical certainty.

Equal probability can sometimes be derived from the procedure itself or from
the principle of sufficient reason applied to symmetry. Cell division and dice
throwing are Weyl's examples. Where observed frequencies match the symmetry
assumption, we are satisfied; where they do not, we look for the hidden
asymmetry, as in a loaded die.

Often probability can be determined only a posteriori from observed
frequencies. The probability of a male birth remains Weyl's example, even with
improved biological knowledge. But Weyl rejects a crude frequency-foundation
that defines probability as an actual or limiting relative frequency over an
infinite sequence of trials. That turns probability into a dogmatic fiction of
an infinite trial-sequence and secretly smuggles a priori probability back in.

If strict causality is retained, statistics should be grounded by reduction to
strict law. But if there is primary probability in atomic single events, as
recent physics suggests, probability or a quantity tied to it must enter the
laws of nature as an original factor. The classical definition then survives
only in the special symmetry cases guided by sufficient reason.

Statistical physics makes this concrete. In a gram of hydrogen there are about
`3 · 10²³` molecules. Statistical reasoning about enormously many molecules
explains why a gas is macroscopically uniform, why coffee and milk mix, and
why spontaneous macroscopic deviations are possible but extraordinarily
unlikely. Most ordinary physical concepts involving atomically structured
matter, such as gas density, are therefore statistical averages with some
degree of indeterminacy, and many familiar laws are statistical regularities
rather than strict laws.

Fluctuation theory matters because individual fluctuations can have observable
cumulative effects. Density fluctuations in air scatter sunlight and make the
sky blue; from the color and intensity of the blue one can calculate Avogadro's
number. This gives strong support to molecular theory.

The right of statistical physics rests on the fact that hidden molecular
events are not directly related to perception. Consciousness does not reproduce
molecular turbulence; it integrates over space and time, producing the apparent
homogeneity and continuity of phenomena. Statistical averages can also show
after-effects of past history, such as hysteresis, dispositions, and memory,
without proving that organic processes cannot be mechanically explained.

The old causal analysis of chance tries to derive probability from hidden
exact laws. Chance appears when small causes have large effects: a tiny
uncontrolled difference in firing time or direction can produce very different
results. Weyl's rotating target example shows how a discontinuous outcome
function can make the final sector almost independent of any reasonably smooth
probability distribution over the trigger-time.

The more rigorous version is the residence-time or ergodic program. For a
point molecule bouncing in a cubical container, the limiting fraction of time
spent in a region equals the region's volume, except for special rationally
related initial velocities. Analogous claims can be made for many molecules.
If probability judgments can be interpreted as residence times, then probability
would be objectively clarified inside a world ruled by exact laws.

Weyl is not satisfied. Such limiting claims depend on rational/irrational
distinctions that fit poorly with physical approximation and continuum
measurement. Uneliminable disturbances do not undermine thermodynamics; they
often hasten equilibrium. The mathematical efforts around the ergodic
hypothesis were long resisted, and by the time proof was achieved as far as one
could reasonably expect, quantum mechanics had stripped the hypothesis of its
former physical meaning.

The result is methodological: statistics now plays as important a role as
strict law and has become increasingly independent. Energy conservation is the
prototype of a strict law; entropy increase is the prototype of a statistical
law. Boltzmann's entropy formula `k · log D` makes entropy a measure of
disorder. Weyl concludes that statistical concepts must be recognized as
genuinely original alongside law-concepts. Quantum mechanics intensifies this:
exact laws may determine probabilities concerning the atom, but observed
space-time events remain determined only with a residual uncertainty factor.

The appendices will update the 1926 situation: primary atomic probability
appears as the square of the absolute value of a complex field quantity `ψ`,
while the secondary statistics of quantum thermodynamics rests on a simple
counting of quantum states.

### 23c. One-Directionality Of Time

Entropy seems to explain the one-directionality of time and the difference
between past and future across physics. But there is a serious difficulty.
The statistical derivation of entropy rests on elementary laws that are
reversible under `t` to `-t`. The same derivation that says a non-equilibrium
state is overwhelmingly likely to be followed after `Δt` by a state nearer
equilibrium also says that it was overwhelmingly likely to be preceded `Δt`
earlier by a state nearer equilibrium. Fluctuations confirm reversibility, not
irreversibility.

Weyl therefore locates time-direction not in elementary laws but in probability
judgments shaped by causal orientation. When two isolated systems come into
contact, we judge it unlikely that they are already in thermal equilibrium at
the moment of contact. When they separate, we judge the opposite. The idea
inside causality, that the earlier is the already-given ground of the later
and not conversely, imprints a privileged time-direction on our probability
judgments.

If a system is noticeably away from equilibrium at time `t`, the deviation
`y` falls roof-like on both sides: toward both `t + Δt` and `t - Δt`.
But a single course of states is made by consistently choosing either the
forward or backward slope. In prediction we choose the forward slope. For the
past, we do not infer that the earth was cooler millions of years ago; we
assume a warmer past from which the present follows under the forward
probability judgment. The "miracle" is charged to the past. Our judgment
proceeds as if the system had been created before our time. Weyl explicitly
notes the theological resonance of "creation" while using it to name a
structural fact in the probability judgment.

The heat equation shows the point sharply. From an arbitrary temperature
distribution at `t = 0`, future distributions are calculable, but in most
cases the solution cannot be traced backward even one step. The creation-act
lies just before the present moment. Reversible fluctuations have only local
significance and tell us nothing about the world in the large.

Classical field theory made a similar time-asymmetry appear through retarded
potentials: the electron's motion determines an outgoing field by retaining
only the solution component corresponding to delayed potential. But this
requires an empty past in which the field begins to radiate only after charges
separate. Bohr's quantum view of atomic emission, where an electron jump
releases an outgoing rather than incoming electromagnetic wave, must be
understood from the same principles as entropy.

Cosmology gives a larger version. De Sitter's hyperboloid and the
future-opening light cones of stellar world-lines suggest a shared influence
region and a common stellar origin. Expanding-world solutions of Einstein's
equations often give the universe a temporally point-like beginning. Lemaître's
radioactive-explosion speculation and Eddington's image of a wall at the
beginning of time express the same pressure: the counter-chance is swept out
of ordinary physics but reappears at the boundary of cosmological history.

Left and right do not pose a comparable problem. All current laws are invariant
under left-right interchange. The asymmetry between positive and negative
electricity remains unexplained, but it is not the same kind of deep temporal
problem.

### 23d. Freedom And Purposiveness

The final subsection turns from causality and chance to human categories:
fate, destiny, chance, fortune and misfortune, free will. Weyl moves quickly
from religious and historical fatalisms to the modern determinism of Hobbes
and the freedom problem in Descartes. Descartes' decisive clarity is that
freedom appears first in theoretical acts of affirmation and denial. When I
judge clearly that `2 + 2 = 4`, the real act of judgment in me is not merely
blind natural causality; something purely spiritual, the truth of the matter,
exercises determining power over the act.

Freedom is therefore not the fact that causes of my action lie inside me as a
natural being, and it is not groundless arbitrary decision. It is that the
realm of being is not closed with respect to its determining grounds. In the
I, where sense and being tie themselves into a knot that knowledge cannot
dissolve, being is open toward sense. If this conviction is not deceptive,
there should be some boundary of objectivity visible within nature and its
science.

Older natural science threatened belief in the independence of the I because
it seemed to leave no such open place. Weyl does not respond by letting living
beings break exact natural laws. Even I can give my body an impulse only by
pushing against other bodies that receive the opposite impulse in sum. The old
"switchman" soul that changes direction while preserving energy is inadequate.
Leibniz's harmony of final causes and efficient causes gives a clearer
classical formulation, while Laplace gives the clearest determinist fantasy:
an intellect knowing all forces and positions would see past and future in one
formula.

Kant's transcendental distinction between sensible and intelligible worlds was
an attempt to solve the conflict of natural causality and freedom, but Weyl
finds it hard to think through. In Laplacean physics it only becomes graspable
if the individual exists from eternity to eternity, like a Leibnizian monad or
through transmigration, so that every `t = const.` cross-section cuts it.

Relativity changes the sharpest antinomy between knowledge and being. Earlier
one could think that the world-state on a three-dimensional slice through `O`
determines the future and is in principle knowable from `O`. In relativity the
known and unknown are separated not by the simultaneity slice through `O` but
by the rear light cone. Field laws do not determine the rest of the world from
that cone. I can know all causal premises of my action only immediately after
the deed, and the present is fully known only by all the consequences it
releases to the end of time.

If freedom is taken as a problem of being, then one demands that the forward
cone from `O` not be lawfully determined by the rest of the world. That would
conflict with pure field physics, but Weyl says that the pure field ideal, like
Laplace's, now lies in ruins. The half-lawful, half-statistical physics of the
present is no longer fit to defend determinism. Quantum physics makes the world
less fettered by "unbreakable laws"; yet the old Laplacean world too had an
"open place" in its arbitrary initial condition.

Living beings require teleological concepts in ordinary understanding. The hand
is for grasping, the eye for seeing, and no theory of life can succeed if it
ignores these simple facts. It is too cheap to say that we merely read purposes
into organisms. The many adaptations, functions, self-preservations, and
life-form continuities must first be recognized before Darwinian causal
explanation can attempt to explain them. Purpose and freedom are two aspects
of the same problem when free purposive action is opposed to necessary lawful
events. Because I know myself as open to a purely spiritual image-world, it is
natural to look for a grain of freedom and idea in all creatures.

Life is marked by order and organization. It seems to resist the fall into heat
death by creating order, but Weyl keeps the thermodynamic account sober:
crystals and organisms produce local order only at the cost of increasing
entropy in the environment. Photosynthesis, protein and nucleoprotein
synthesis, gene and chromosome duplication, heat transfer, and excretion of
lower-organized substances all fit that pattern. The old "vital force" view
had to be abandoned after Wöhler's synthesis of urea, though organic compounds
are much more complex than inorganic ones.

Reversible physical laws are indifferent between causality and finality.
Fermat's least-time principle and Hamilton's principle can look purposive, but
they are mathematically equivalent to differential laws connecting infinitesimal
neighbors. Probability and entropy break this symmetry and express the idea
that the earlier is the determining ground of the later.

Kant treats purposiveness as a regulative principle for judging natural beings:
knowing that the eye serves seeing gives us a research problem about the
lawful arrangements by which it performs that function. Schelling, Driesch, and
holism replace purposiveness with wholeness. Weyl grants the importance of
wholeness in biology and psychology but rejects the inference that organic
processes are in principle beyond theoretical-symbolic construction. Atom
physics itself is already far from a machine model, and each atom is a whole
of definite structure.

Biology is not applied physics or chemistry. It is an independent science with
its own laws, which Weyl, following M. Hartmann, calls laws of specific
complication. But it advances by the same methods of generalizing induction,
comparison, and experiment that govern physics. Planning and wholeness in
biology mark problems rather than solve them. If a vital force were to be given
a theoretical form, it would have to appear not as a spatial factor but as
statistical correlations among elementary processes; so far, statistical
thermodynamic laws hold inside organisms.

The questions of life, spontaneous generation, and the soul-body problem remain
premature while the laws of life are so little known. Weyl does not expect
serious difficulty in subjecting psychophysical reality to theoretical
construction if "soul" means only the aggregate of real psychic events in an
individual. The deeper riddle lies elsewhere: in the double position of the I.
The I is not merely an existent individual performing real psychic acts; it is
also "vision," self-penetrating light, meaning-giving consciousness, knowledge,
image, or whatever one wants to call it. As individual it posits reality; its
vision is open toward reason. This mystery lies beyond the means of natural
science.

Weyl closes by refusing to claim that he has written a history of philosophical
thought-work inside natural science. Exact natural science is, if not the most
important fact, the most distinctive fact of modern culture. Philosophy's task
is to understand that fact in its own character and uniqueness.

## Notes

- Section 23 is not a simple indeterminist victory over determinism. Weyl
  separates at least four layers: causal understanding as methodological norm,
  causal structure as relativistic light-cone order, statistical law as an
  original physical mode, and freedom as the openness of being to sense in the
  I.
- Natural law does not simply equal causality. Function-laws are indifferent
  to determinant/determined direction; causal thinking requires a cut between
  conditions and event, and that cut is partly practical, experimental, and
  tied to agency.
- Statistics is not merely ignorance by the end of the section. Weyl treats it
  as an original concept beside strict law, with quantum probability forcing
  the issue.
- Time-direction is not found in reversible elementary laws. It enters through
  probability judgments governed by the causal orientation that treats the
  earlier as the given ground of the later.
- Biological purposiveness is neither a finished explanation nor a projection
  to be dismissed. It is a problem-setting feature of life that must be
  recognized before causal explanation can work on it.
- Weyl rejects both crude vitalism and crude reduction. Biology is autonomous
  because its laws are laws of specific complication, but it remains continuous
  with the scientific method of theory, comparison, induction, and experiment.
- The final boundary is not ordinary psychophysical interaction. The real
  boundary is the double position of the I as natural individual and
  meaning-giving consciousness.
