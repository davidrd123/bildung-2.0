not a simple substrate. It moves historically through atomistic substance,
field, ether, monad, conservation law, and quantum complementarity.

## Translation

### 22. Matter

The seventeenth and eighteenth centuries are governed by a substantial concept
of matter. Behind changing sensible qualities there is supposed to be a
substantial core, Locke's continuously persisting body, identical with itself
through time. Democritus supplies the grand abstraction: the opposition of
void and full, empty space and matter. Galilei too treats visible variation as
mere rearrangement of parts without new coming-to-be or annihilation. The
decisive trait of this concept is that one and the same place of substance
should in principle be reidentifiable throughout the history of a body-system.
The four-dimensional world-continuum is then resolved into world-lines of
substance-points.

But an entirely homogeneous, qualityless substance cannot by itself permit the
recognition of the same place. Democritus' idea therefore leads necessarily to
atomism and empty space. Density, rarefaction, and condensation are explained
by the mixing ratio of atoms and void. Mass becomes the quantum of matter
occupying covered space. The required space is Euclidean, with rigid metric
structure. The atoms are indivisible, rigid, mutually impenetrable, and
geometrically solid; their sensible hardness is not the point.

Huyghens embodies this geometrical-kinematic stance against Leibniz's more
intuitive-dynamic metaphysics. Atomic solidity cannot mean a finite hardness
like diamond or iron; it must be infinite rigidity. Weyl draws the consequence
with the odd non-contiguous atom figure: if an atom is geometrically rigid, even
its disconnected parts must remain one identical whole. A sphere is preferred
only as the closest simple approximation to the ideal in which no inner part of
the atom remains separately identifiable.

A mechanical-atomistic explanation needs laws of free atomic motion and laws
for what happens when atoms touch. Since Galilei, free motion is inertial
translation, not Democritus' fall downward. The interaction of atoms is
collision, but collision is not yet dynamic; it says only that motion after
contact is lawfully determined by motion before contact. Huyghens' principles
for this are the conservation laws of energy and momentum. Euler's pure
mechanical world-picture lists extension, mobility, inertia, and
impenetrability as the basic properties of matter. Newton's `Opticks` still
speaks of primitive, hard, permanent particles formed by God.

This substance-atomism works well enough for gases, but it breaks down when
the observed masses of chemical atoms are not proportional to their atomic
volumes. The image of one homogeneous substance-dough stamped into rigid atom
cookies is shaken. Chemical relative atomic weights agree instead with the law
of multiple proportions.

With Newton, dynamic concepts enter substance physics. Mass is already a
dynamical coefficient in Galilei's momentum law, even if the older definition
as a quantum of matter persists. Atomic hardness becomes repulsive force and
an attraction law. Atoms become centers of force. Kant constructs matter from
the equilibrium of attractive and repulsive force, but Weyl notes that this
dual decomposition is not well grounded if Kant has only one "mass-density" to
work with.

Newtonian gravitation appears as immediate action at a distance, and this
produces ether theories of gravity. The decisive physical way forward is
Faraday's field concept and Maxwell's discovery that the field propagates from
centers of excitation with light-speed, not instantaneously. A Newtonian force
between two bodies is split into excitation of a field by one body, propagation
of that field, and the suffered change of momentum in the other. The field
carries energy and momentum. A radiating body not only loses energy; it recoils.
Field energy and momentum transform into kinetic energy and material momentum,
and conversely.

The energy and momentum of a field-piece also yield inertial mass. For a piece
containing matter and radiation, total momentum `𝔍` is aligned with the
velocity `𝔳` of its energy center, with `𝔍 = m𝔳`; and mass stands to energy in
the relation `m = E/c²`. A piece of field, such as radiation enclosed in a
massless shell, has inertial mass like an ordinary body. The electron's mass is
therefore at least partly due to its electromagnetic field. This raises the
question whether any substantial core is needed at all.

Pure field theory answers no: an electron is a small region of field with very
high field-strength and concentrated field-energy. It moves like a wave over
water; the electron is not made of the same substance at all times. Its
velocity is a phase-like velocity, the motion of an ideal energy-center
constructed from the field-process. Mie's program sought modified field laws
whose static, spherical solutions would explain charge, mass, and atomic
weights. Weyl, Kaluza, Eddington, and Einstein tried to join the electromagnetic
field with the metric and gravitational field into a unified field law.

Geometry and field theory now join organically. Space is no longer an empty
container over against things. There is no empty space; to say that the field
leaves out a region is absurd. Coordinates and field values are correlative:
the domain of possible field-values is also the domain of variable arguments.
The equation `ℰ = 0` in a region does not mean the electric field is interrupted,
only that it is in a rest-state continuous with its other possible states. The
field cannot be pumped out like a box of substance.

Yet pure field theory remains program and hypothesis. The more cautious result
is a return to a matter-field dualism: matter excites the field; field acts on
matter. Helmholtz states the dual aspect as matter considered apart from its
effects, and force as the capacity of matter to act. F. A. Lange says the
uncomprehended residue of analysis is always matter. Leibniz remains the
classic philosopher of this dynamic world-picture: real motion lies not in mere
change of position but in moving force, in a primitive active power.

For Weyl, the modern ether laws are field equations outside narrow
world-channels corresponding to particles. The local field is determined by the
particle insofar as invariant field traits are concerned. The particle's
orientation, however, is relative to the embedding total field and so undergoes
field-reaction. The active charge of a particle can be defined by the flux of
the electric field through a thin imagined surface around it. Likewise, mass is
grounded not in energy-content alone but in the flux of the gravitational field
through a surrounding surface. The strength of ether theory is its sober refusal
to speculate about the particle's interior.

The history of ether moves through four stages. After Galilei it is a
substantial bearer of light and gravitation. With Faraday and Maxwell it loses
its substantial-physical character and becomes the absolute space-medium of
electromagnetic field states. Special relativity removes the substantial ether
by showing that rest is not an intrinsically privileged state. General
relativity then gives world-structure a physical, force-responsive character
again. Ether becomes a synonym for field: the united electromagnetic and
structural field described, in coordinates, by electromagnetic field components
and Einstein's gravitational potentials `gᵢₖ`.

The metaphysical background is older than physics. Kant's first analogy treats
substance as the persisting object of appearance through change. His example of
weighing smoke already presupposes a quantitative conservation principle, but
does not yet say by what measure matter is to be measured. Lavoisier turns the
indestructibility of matter into chemistry. Where individual substance-places
can no longer be tracked, transferability under a conserved quantitative
measure becomes the criterion of substantiality. Energy itself can be treated
as substance in this sense.

Aristotle's matter, `ὕλη`, is the determinable substrate, opposed to determining
form, `εἶδος`. Matter is possibility of being formed. Substantiality belongs
more to forms than to matter. Modern natural science had to fight this
metaphysics of substantial forms. Leibniz reintroduces it through monads while
still accepting mechanical explanation for special phenomena. His decisive step
beyond Aristotle is to place the monad's essence in law: a persisting law that
contains all future states of the subject is what makes identity of substance.
Weyl's diagnostic question is where a philosophy of nature lets the Heraclitean
flux "arm itself into rigidity": Aristotle in immanent substantial forms, Plato
in transcendent ideas, modern science with Leibniz in law.

Descartes' doctrine that spatial extension is the proper substance of bodies
fits field theory well if substance and accident become the relation of
"this" and "thus": the individual here-now and its state. The field-theoretic
world-description consists of here-thus relations, with coordinates for the
here and state-quantities for the thus. Descartes' physics still uses
corpuscles, but if the continuously space-filling fluid of motion is stripped
of substantial interpretation, the Cartesian idea leads in fact to field
theory.

Two roots of the substance concept remain: the external thing that persists
through changing appearances, and the I that is conscious of its identity
through the flux of experiences. Descartes asks what the wax itself is beneath
all sensory change. Locke gives a more critical account of our idea of
substance in general. Hume rejects it as an error born from confusing identity
with relation and inventing an unknown something to bind related elements.

The conservation laws then become the modern refuge of the old principle of
persistence. In special relativity, energy is the time-component of an invariant
objective structure whose spatial projection is momentum; the conservation laws
of energy and momentum belong together. Weyl gives the relativity formulas for
mass, energy, and momentum, and then contrasts this with the older
phenomenological energy principle, where only differences of energy-levels in
state-changes are considered.

Leibniz's `causa aequat effectum` captures the root of the energy principle by
normalizing mechanical change into the raising of a given weight. Later, a
water calorimeter can replace the raised weight as standard effect. The
experience behind the principle says that a state-change `𝔅` in a system can
be undone by connecting it with a calorimeter and auxiliary bodies, leaving
only a warming or cooling of the calorimeter. The calorimeter measure is
independent of the process and auxiliary bodies used.

Relativity transforms this into energy-levels of states and connects it with
momentum. The energy law is not protected by an a priori decree; Bohr and
Slater had recently even tried a short-lived theory abandoning strict
conservation. The older phenomenological derivation concealed the possibility
of momentum conservation because earth served unnoticed as an effectively
immovable reference body. Shooting a bullet into a mountain appears to lose
momentum, but the earth receives it. Electric charge is another conserved
quantity, but because charge is bound to matter the fixed-body-system
assumption can exclude electrical redistributions.

Field physics extends conservation laws to radiation by introducing energy and
momentum currents across the boundary of a region. Charge, energy, and momentum
are additive over subregions. The mass-factor, however, is not a usable
substance measure, because it is invariant and positive but does not obey the
addition law. Its behavior is like side-lengths in a triangle rather than
additive scalar quantity.

Atomism began as speculation but gained firm empirical support in chemistry
through Dalton's interpretation of multiple proportions. Since atoms of a
chemical element must all be equal, their identity cannot be secured by
distinct internal properties, as Leibniz demanded for monads; it can be secured
only by continuity of motion together with spatial separation. From a
substance-theory standpoint, there is no explanation why only the few discrete
possibilities corresponding to chemical elements are realized. Pure field
theory leaves open the hope that non-linear field equations might have only a
discrete number of regular static spherical solutions.

The discovery of the electron made the modern attack possible. Physics found a
material unit common to chemically different substances and free in cathode
rays. The periodic system already pointed toward a unified structure of
chemical atoms. Planck's quantum of action `h` first appeared in radiation
thermodynamics; Planck had to suppose that an oscillator emits light of
frequency `ν` by a discontinuous jump losing energy `hν`. Bohr applies this to
electrons in individual atoms, gaining rules for calculating atomic energy
levels and explaining spectral series. Sommerfeld sees in quantum-numbered
spectra a modern echo of Pythagorean whole-number harmony.

The electron brings the old dream of the unity of all matter closer, but a
positive building block is also needed. Weyl identifies the proton with the
hydrogen nucleus and notes the sequence of atomic numbers, isotope mixtures,
and the post-1926 proliferation of elementary particles: electrons, positrons,
protons, neutrons, neutrinos, and mesons. The construction of the material
world from one or a few units appearing in vast numbers of identical copies is
one of the most fundamental features of the universe and still demands deeper
interpretation.

While this remains a future task, quantum mechanics has changed the situation.
Heisenberg replaces the flexible correspondence principle with a complete,
simple, consistent formulation for calculating energy levels. But the price is
the abandonment of every space-time image of atomic processes. Bohr says the
problem is not a describable modification of mechanical and electromagnetic
theories, but a deep failure of the space-time images used to describe nature.
Schrödinger independently arrives at mathematically equivalent results through
a wave image; the wave is not itself observed, but observable mechanical
phenomena are projected from it statistically.

The result is that quantum physics cannot retain matter and ether as the
fundamental polarity underlying all appearances. Light is not only ether-wave
but corpuscle; the electron is not only corpuscle but wave. The observation
situation and the instruments determine whether photons and electrons appear as
waves of definite frequency or as particles striking here or there. Bohr names
this fundamental feature complementarity. It replaces, in a certain respect,
the old polarity of matter and force. The problem of matter has reached a new
historical stage.

## Notes

- Section 22 is not a simple physics history. It is Weyl's reconstruction of
  how the metaphysical idea of substance survives only by repeatedly changing
  its technical carrier: atom, force-center, field, ether, law, conserved
  quantity, quantum state.
- Matter is progressively de-substantialized. The substantial core first
  becomes a geometrically rigid atom, then a force-center, then a field-knot,
  then a channel or boundary condition in field law, and finally an entity
  whose wave/corpuscle character depends on the observation situation.
- The field is not merely another thing. It changes the ontology of space:
  there is no empty container over against things; spatial structure and field
  state become mutually implicated.
- Weyl keeps the pure field-theory program open but refuses to promote it to
  settled doctrine. The sober position remains matter-field dualism until
  stronger theory earns more.
- Conservation law is the modern form of substance-pressure, but Weyl also
  weakens it: even energy conservation is not metaphysically immune, and mass
  is not additive enough to be a true substance-quantity.
- Leibniz becomes more central, not less. His monad is not retained as a
  metaphysical object, but his shift of substance-identity into law anticipates
  Weyl's modern reconstruction.
- Quantum complementarity is the decisive endpoint for this section: it
  displaces the old matter/force or matter/ether opposition with a relation
  between phenomenon, instrument, and mutually exclusive descriptive modes.
