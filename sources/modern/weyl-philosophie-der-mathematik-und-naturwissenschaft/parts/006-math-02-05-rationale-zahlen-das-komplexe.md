```text
II. Zahl und Kontinuum. Das Unendliche

5. Rationale Zahlen. Das Komplexe

Beim genetischen Aufbau des mathematischen Zahlenreichs
nimmt man seinen Ausgang von den natürlichen Zahlen der Reihe
1, 2, 3, .... Der erste Schritt, der zu tun ist, ist der Aufstieg von den
natürlichen Zahlen zu den Brüchen. Die Brüche verdanken historisch ihre Entstehung dem Übergang vom Zählen zum Messen.
Dem Messen liegt stets zugrunde ein Größengebiet, wie es z. B.
die Strecken auf einer Geraden bilden. Hier herrscht 1. eine Beziehung der Gleichheit a = b (Kongruenz), die den dafür aufgestellten Axiomen (S. 23) genügt, und 2. eine Operation, die aus
irgend zwei Strecken a, b eine Strecke a + b erzeugt. Aus der
Strecke a entsteht dann z. B. die Strecke 5a, indem man die Summe
a + a + a + a + a mit fünf Summanden a bildet. Damit ist der
Anschluß des Messens an das Zählen vollzogen. Die Erklärung
der Vervielfältigung kann scharf so gefaßt werden:

α) 1a = a;

β) ist n eine natürliche Zahl, so entsteht (n + 1) a aus na gemäß
der Formel

(n + 1)a = (na) + a.

Im Gebiete der Strecken gestattet die Operation der Vervielfältigung eine eindeutige Umkehrung, die Teilung: es existiert
bei gegebener Strecke a und natürlicher Zahl n eine und (im Sinne
der Gleichheit) nur eine Strecke x, für welche nx = a ist; sie wird
mit a/n bezeichnet. Die Operation der Teilung kann man mit der
Vervielfältigung kombinieren. So entsteht z. B. 5a/3, das „5/3-
fache“ von a. Das Bruchzeichen m/n dient als Symbol der zusammengesetzten Operation, so daß zwei Brüche gleich sind, wenn
die beiden durch sie bezeichneten Operationen zum gleichen
Resultat führen, auf welche Strecke a sie auch angewendet werden
mögen. Die Multiplikation der Brüche bedeutet die Hinterein-

Es ist nicht angebracht, für jedes Größengebiet eigene Brüche
```

## Draft

```text
II. Number and Continuum. The Infinite

5. Rational Numbers. The Complex

In the genetic construction of the mathematical realm of numbers, one takes
one's starting point from the natural numbers in the series 1, 2, 3, .... The
first step to be taken is the ascent from the natural numbers to fractions.
Historically, fractions owe their origin to the transition from counting to
measuring. Measuring is always based on a domain of magnitudes, such as the
segments on a line. Here there prevails, first, a relation of equality a = b
(congruence) satisfying the axioms set up for it; and second, an operation that
produces from any two segments a, b a segment a + b. From the segment a there
then arises, for example, the segment 5a, by forming the sum a + a + a + a + a
with five summands a. The connection of measuring to counting has thereby been
made. The explanation of multiplication can be sharply formulated as follows:

α) 1a = a;

β) if n is a natural number, then (n + 1)a arises from na according to the
formula

(n + 1)a = (na) + a.

In the domain of segments, the operation of multiplication permits a unique
inverse operation, division: given a segment a and a natural number n, there
exists one and only one segment x, in the sense of equality, for which nx = a;
it is denoted by a/n. The operation of division can be combined with
multiplication. Thus, for example, 5a/3 arises, the "5/3-fold" of a. The
fraction sign m/n serves as the symbol of the composite operation, so that two
fractions are equal when the two operations designated by them lead to the same
result no matter which segment a they are applied to. The multiplication of
fractions means carrying out one after another the operations characterized by
them. That fractions can be added rests on the fact that the operation expressed
by

mx/n + m*x/n*

in its application to the arbitrary segment x can be represented by a single
fraction.

It is not appropriate to introduce separate fractions for each domain of
magnitudes. Since their laws are independent of the nature of the domain of
magnitudes, it is more expedient to define them purely arithmetically. This is
accomplished simply by applying the above considerations to the domain of the
natural numbers themselves. That in this domain a relation such as

5x = 3y

cannot always be solved for y when x is given plays no role in the development
of the theory. We thus arrive at the following formulation: "Two natural numbers
m, n determine a fraction m/n. That, for any two natural numbers x and y, the
second is the m/n-fold of the first is only another expression for the equation
mx = ny." This is a creative definition in the sense of section 2. Two fractions
m/n and m*/n* are equal if arbitrary numbers x, y that stand to one another in
the relation mx = ny always also satisfy the relation m*x = n*y, and conversely.
The rules for calculating with natural numbers allow this transfinite criterion,
which according to its wording requires trying through all possible numbers x,
y, to be replaced by the finite one:

(K) m · n* = n · m*.

Therefore there is present the special case of definition by abstraction: the
equality of the fractions m/n and m*/n* can be explained directly by (K), once
one has first convinced oneself that this relation is an equivalence. The
introduction of fractions as "ideal elements" can also be motivated, without
regard to applications, from a purely arithmetical standpoint: with a suitable
extension of the number operations to them, the most important arithmetical
axioms remain in force, and in addition the invertibility of multiplication, the
possibility of dividing, is forced, something that occurs only exceptionally in
the arithmetic of the natural numbers.

If one applies the same thought anew in order also to force the invertibility of
addition, one proceeds from fractions to the rational numbers, including 0 and
the negative. To be sure, one must make a serious sacrifice: the possibility of
division cannot be saved for the divisor 0. Here no logical obscurities or
philosophical difficulties appear anywhere. A much more serious matter is the
starting point, the system of natural numbers, and the irrational, the transition
from the rational numbers to the continuum of real numbers. But once this level
has been climbed, the further advance to the complex and hypercomplex numbers
passes by no abysses. To introduce the complex numbers one only has to describe
what gives an individual such number and how one is to operate with them. It is
given by its two components; we can therefore say outright that by a complex
number every real number-pair (α, β) is to be understood. We will not explicitly
set down the rules of operation here. According to them, in the complex domain
(1, 0) = e plays the role of the unit, because its multiplication with any
complex number (α, β) reproduces this number (α, β). And (0, 1) is the imaginary
unit i satisfying the equation i · i = -e. The inner ground for these stipulations
is again that they transfer the validity of the formal laws of calculation to
the complex numbers, the pairs of real numbers. Nothing remains of the mystical
odor in which imaginary magnitudes long stood. From the complex numbers one can
ascend to hypercomplex numbers with 3 or more components. But it could be shown
generally that, however addition and multiplication may be defined in their
domain, the validity of all laws of calculation cannot be maintained. In this
respect the complex numbers mark a natural limit for the extension of the
concept of number. Nevertheless, hypercomplex number systems are also important
in mathematics; thus the four-component quaternions, which among the laws of
calculation fail only to satisfy the commutative law of multiplication, serve as
a suitable aid for mastering the rotations of a rigid body in space.

Instead of genetically constructing the realm of numbers, one can also ground
arithmetic on an axiom system. Genesis then serves merely to reduce its
consistency to the consistency of the axioms valid for the natural numbers. The
arithmetical axioms fall into two groups, the algebraic axioms and the axioms of
magnitude. The algebraic group deals with the operations of addition and
multiplication. It contains the formal laws of calculation, such as a + b = b +
a, requires the existence of a 0 and a 1 with the properties

a + 0 = 0 + a = a,     1 · a = a · 1 = a

and the invertibility of addition and multiplication, with the exception of
division by 0. The axioms of magnitude, which are suspended in the domain of
complex numbers, deal with the relation a > b, "a is greater than b." Compare
the table in Hilbert's Foundations of Geometry.
```

## Notes

- `genetischer Aufbau` is rendered as `genetic construction`. The section
  explicitly contrasts this genesis with a later axiomatic grounding.
- `Brüche` is `fractions`, not `rational numbers`; Weyl reserves `rationale
  Zahlen` for the extension that includes `0` and negative numbers.
- The important move is that a seemingly transfinite equality criterion for
  fractions is reduced to the finite cross-multiplication condition `(K)`.
- Fractions are another case of `ideale Elemente`: introduced to force
  invertibility while preserving the main arithmetical laws.
- Weyl sharply lowers the philosophical stakes of rational and complex numbers:
  the real difficulties are the natural numbers and the irrational/continuum,
  not the later formal extensions.
- `Größenaxiome` is rendered as `axioms of magnitude`; these do not apply in
  the complex domain because there is no preserved order relation `a > b`.
