## Translation

### 21. Theory-Formation

The constructive character of natural science has already become clear from
what precedes. Its individual assertions do not each have a meaning that can be
verified in intuition; truth in natural science forms a system that can be
tested only as a whole. Hobbes develops the thesis that we know with certainty
only in sciences that construct their object from conditions of construction
lying in the knowing subject. For him, reality is not the images of
consciousness but what is contained in them, what makes their construction
possible. This synthetic advance, generating appearance from its ground, is
`scientia` in the strict sense, as opposed to mere `cognitio`. Within knowledge
of nature this takes place to the extent that mathematical derivation is
possible.

Dilthey says that with Copernicus, Kepler, and Galilei, and the accompanying
theory of nature's construction through a priori logical-mathematical elements
of consciousness, there arose the sovereign consciousness of the autonomy of
human intellect and its power over things. In modern physics, however, the
building blocks of construction are no longer elements of consciousness won by
abstraction from actuality, but purely "arithmetical" symbols. Dingler even
defines physics as the field in which the principle of symbolic construction
has been fully carried through. Yet a priori construction is coupled with
experience and with the analysis of experience by experiment. Dilthey's own
formulation is decisive: scientific imagination is disciplined by strict
methods that subject the possibilities lying in mathematical thought to
experience, experiment, and confirmation by facts.

Weyl illustrates this with electromagnetic theory, leaving relativity aside by
setting the propagation speed to infinity. Suppose there are elementary quanta
of matter with fixed masses and charges. The position and velocity of these
electrons at an instant `t` determine the electromagnetic field by certain
laws of generation. By further laws this field carries spatially distributed
momentum and energy, and through momentum flow it exerts definite ponderomotive
forces on the generating electrons. Force then produces acceleration by the
fundamental law of mechanics, while velocity and acceleration determine the
change of position and velocity in the next time-element `dt`. Repeated
continuation from `t` to `t + dt`, followed by integration, yields the whole
motion.

Only this full theoretical nexus, into which geometry also decisively enters,
can be experimentally checked. An isolated law taken out of the theoretical
structure cannot be checked by itself. In this way all parts of physics and
geometry grow together into an insoluble unity. Theory develops only by
continuous correction under the pressure of richer and more precise
experience. Enriques' formula is apt: scientific progress rests on science
itself; it is an extension, not a new creation.

The Kepler-Newton planetary theory initially placed every event silently at
the instant of its perception. Roemer later discovered the finite propagation
speed of light through apparent deviations in the motion of Jupiter's moons
from what the theory demanded. A theory of instantaneous light propagation is
used and then shown false; but its coarse correctness, together with other
empirical premises, reveals its fine inaccuracy and correction. Without first
assuming such coarse correctness, no step forward is possible. This is what
Newton's fourth rule of natural philosophy means: propositions inferred from
phenomena by induction are to be regarded as exactly or very nearly true until
new phenomena either make them more exact or subject them to exceptions.

To ease the theorist's task, the experimenter already tries to arrange the
experiment so that it is as sensitive as possible to one law and as insensitive
as possible to other relevant laws, damping the influence of secondary
circumstances. This is the labor of shielding against "sources of error." But
some elements, such as the metric field, can never be eliminated. When a fact
does not agree with the whole theoretical inventory of science, it finally
falls to the theorist to find where the theory must be modified. There are
hardly general rules for this, or for the weight to assign to facts in a
theoretical interpretation. Here genius enters its rights. General relativity
arose because Einstein recognized the principial nature and special carrying
power of the agreement between gravitational and inertial mass.

Different constructions may be suited to explain the same perceptions. Hobbes
and d'Alembert anticipate modern positivists in this insight into the
"ambiguity of truth." Einstein, in his address to Planck, gives the real
epistemological situation fairly: among the thinkable theoretical
constructions, one proves itself practically superior to all the others. No
one deeply immersed in the subject will deny that the world of perceptions
practically determines the theoretical system, even though no logical path
leads to the principles of the theory.

At each state of the theoretical edifice there is a hierarchy of laws. Laws are
credited with different degrees of firmness. Some are held to with great
tenacity as principles. Euclidean geometry was long treated as almost sacred;
the conservation of energy and momentum have comparable or even higher
firmness. A considerable part of the theoretical system can be maintained
against any experience if modifications are permitted in the rest. In practice
natural science therefore does not operate with a sharp Kantian opposition
between a priori and a posteriori, but with a rich scale of firmness.

A simple form, an instinctively evident character, and decisive importance for
a large and manifold field of facts give laws the rank of principles. Thus the
law of inertia is retained even when finer experience, such as Foucault's
pendulum, appears to contradict motion relative to the earth. The "way out" is
that the law concerns not motion relative to the earth but an "absolute
motion" to be determined from the phenomena. The law of momentum rests on the
evident fact that an initially resting system of bodies cannot set itself into
one-sided progressive translation by its own force. The laughter at
Munchausen pulling himself from the swamp by his own pigtail shows how well
everyone instinctively understands this point.

Other examples are Galilei's almost unnoticed assumption of the rule for
combining velocities, and the energy principle. In the special form that
bodies in a homogeneous gravitational field cannot lift themselves all to a
higher level by their own force, this principle is used by Galilei and Stevin
for the inclined plane and by Huyghens for reducing the compound pendulum to
the mathematical pendulum. Huyghens had already grasped the general idea of
the energy principle. Leibniz reads the energy principle into `causa aequat
effectum` and treats it as a special consequence of the principle of reason
demanded by the "logic of quantity."

A negative experience that proves itself again and again under the most
varied circumstances carries much more weight than an individual confirming
experiment. The energy principle is supported by the failure of all attempts
to build a perpetuum mobile. The basic principles of relativity theory are of
the same kind: the special principle of relativity and the principle of the
constancy of the speed of light. Against scholastic philosophy, Newton says
that nothing is achieved by explaining things through hidden specific
qualities. It would be a great advance to derive two or three general
principles of motion from the phenomena and then show how the properties and
effects of bodies follow from those clear principles, even if the causes of
the principles remained undiscovered.

Simplicity counts as `sigillum veri`, the seal of truth. Kepler says that
nature loves simplicity and unity. Aristotle anticipates this in the maxim that
God and nature do nothing in vain, and in the axiom that it is vain to do
through many what can be done through fewer. Galilei, in describing his path
to the laws of falling bodies, asks why the increases in speed of a falling
stone should not occur in the simplest and most plausible way, since no
increase is simpler than one that is always added in the same way. He then
defines uniformly accelerated motion, develops its consequences without first
looking to experience, and finds that the observable consequences are confirmed
for the "naturally accelerated" motion of falling bodies. Newton's first rule
likewise allows no more causes of natural things than are true and sufficient.

But the point is not to set up the absolutely simplest principles, as Dingler
demands. If that were the standard one would give the world not four
dimensions but one. The whole breadth of present experience must be considered,
and the simplest explanation relative to known appearances must be sought.
Often one explanation is simpler for a narrow domain, while a rival theory
remains simpler as experience broadens. The required simplicity is also not
the obvious simplicity lying on the surface. Nature has to educate us toward
the true inner simplicity.

The problem of simplicity is central for the epistemology of natural science.
One attempt has been to reduce it to probability. If twenty related value
pairs `(x, y)` of a functional relation lie, within expected accuracy, on a
straight line in rectangular coordinates, one suspects the strict natural law
that `y` depends linearly on `x`, both because of the line's simplicity and
because it would be very improbable for the twenty points to lie on a straight
line if the underlying law were different. Interpolation and extrapolation
then yield predictions beyond the observations.

But this analysis is vulnerable. Under all circumstances one can define many
functions `y = f(x)` that fit the twenty data points, including functions very
different from a straight line. For each of them too one could call it highly
improbable that the points would lie on it if it did not contain the true law.
It is essential that the function, or rather the class of functions, be made
available a priori by mathematics because of its mathematical simplicity. The
class may not depend on as many parameters as there are observations to be
satisfied. Linear functions `f(x) = ax + b`, for instance, depend only on the
two parameters `a` and `b`.

A theory is significantly confirmed when it remains in agreement with the
facts it was devised to explain even under sharper observational accuracy and
more numerous observation points. Euclidean geometry offers one case: geodetic
and astronomical precision measurements confirmed it far more accurately than
the experiences that first led to it would have suggested. Similar cases
occur continually in physics. Conversely, the Ptolemaic epicycles are a sure
sign of a wrong path because their number had to be increased whenever
observational accuracy rose. Kepler's three laws were much simpler and more
accurate, but Greek geometry's prior discovery of the ellipse as a transparent
and simple figure had to precede Kepler's astronomical discovery. Newton's
law of attraction, especially as a local-action law, again sets the theoretical
foundations deeper. Kepler's laws return from it when only the sun's attraction
on a planet is considered, and Newton's calculation of perturbations fits the
far higher accuracy achieved since Tycho Brahe. The gravitational law also
proved valid beyond its original field of experience, in the motion of double
stars.

Once experience suggests a hypothesis, its consequences must be developed
deductively, always with an eye to consequences accessible to experimental
test. Huyghens describes this method in the introduction to the `Traité de la
Lumière`: unlike geometry, the principles here are verified through the
conclusions drawn from them. One may still reach a degree of probability that
often yields nothing to strict proof, especially when the consequences drawn
from the principles agree completely with known appearances and when new
appearances predicted from the assumption come out as expected. Huyghens'
wave theory of light proves itself, among other things, in uncovering the law
of double refraction in calcite, too complicated to be found purely
empirically. A theory succeeds when it reduces complicated dependencies among
directly observed magnitudes to simple relations among the theory's
fundamental magnitudes.

Mach says that the essential function of a hypothesis is to lead to new
observations and experiments, by which our supposition is confirmed, refuted,
modified, or, in short, experience is expanded. The sailor who imagines a
distant land from objects driven to shore seeks it; whether he finds it or
finds a different land instead, his experience is expanded. In Galilei,
Huyghens, and Newton, the deductive part still plays a much greater role than
in later physics. Galilei is no less proud of the abundance of theorems won
from a single principle than of the discovery of that principle itself. The
empirical component of physics has steadily increased; the discovery of
electricity brings the first great break-in.

The category of perfection is closely related to simplicity. It plays a major
role in Aristotelian philosophy not only methodologically but as a principle
of explanation. Aristotle's claim that the heavenly bodies are indestructible
and unchangeable depends on their perfect spherical shape. Galilei objects in
the `Dialogo`: if spherical perfection has this status, then a hair's-breadth
deviation from exact spherical shape is as impossible as mountains on the
surface. His sense of continuity resists the idea that, in a nature where no
absolutely exact measurements are possible, one exact value of a continuous
magnitude should give its bearer properties that differ in principle from
arbitrarily neighboring values.

Galilei also notes that a sphere can be inscribed in a tetrahedron, so at most
the remaining corners would be destructible, and more spheres could again be
inscribed in them. This shows that such a property as indestructibility cannot
depend on geometrical shape as such. It can depend only on the boundary
surface across which physical state magnitudes, here density, undergo a jump
and which can become the seat of special surface forces. One sees here the
radical reversal in natural interpretation brought by Galilean thought against
Aristotelian thought.

Equally characteristic is Galilei's exuberant praise of changeability against
crystalline perfection: the plant unfolding into blossom is incomparably more
glorious than the rigid Aristotelian heavenly bodies removed from all
occurrence. Kepler still gives broad space to considerations of perfection.
The "rank of the earth" concerns him, and his conviction of the circle's
perfection makes it hard for him to abandon the circular orbit of Mars when
Brahe's measurements force him. He remains attached to the static at first,
seeking the harmony of the planetary system in the regular solids, and only
slowly fights through to a more dynamic view of world-occurrence.

Even Galilei still succumbs at a strange point in the `Dialogo` to explanation
by geometrical perfection when he bases pure inertial motion on circular
rather than straight-line motion. But overall he has taken the turn more
decisively than Kepler: perfection is no longer sought in fixed shapes and
individual things, but in dynamic connections, in laws of nature that leave
wide room for chance and mere facticity. Perfection is no longer a factual
component of theory, but a heuristic principle and a faith that drives
research. Kepler, Galilei, and Bruno share with the ancient Pythagoreans the
faith in a cosmos ordered according to the highest mathematical laws of
reason, and in divine reason as the origin of the rational in nature and as
kindred to human reason. Over centuries this faith repeatedly found surprising
partial fulfillments in physics, perhaps most beautifully in Maxwell's
electromagnetic theory of the ether. But nature again and again proved
superior to the human spirit and forced it to break premature closure in favor
of a deeper harmony.

According to section 19, two strict demands apply to every theory: first,
concordance, which includes noncontradiction; second, the absence of
superfluous purely dogmatic components without influence on observable
appearances. The principle of sufficient reason must also never be violated.
In simple cases it can function as a symmetry principle for setting up laws.
Archimedes uses it when he begins the lever theory from the proposition that
equal weights at equal lever arms are in equilibrium. The whole configuration,
including the direction of gravity, maps into itself under reflection at the
plane through the support point perpendicular to the horizontal lever arm. If
a mass-force configuration, or a state that uniquely determines further
occurrence, maps into itself under a similarity transformation, then what
happens must also be invariant under that transformation. Thus the balance arm
cannot tip to one side.

The same thought leads to the proposition that equal bodies have equal
inertial mass: if they are driven against one another with opposite equal
velocities, neither overruns the other. If this nevertheless happens for two
visibly equal bodies, we infer an inner difference, perhaps documented in the
worst case only by the difference in mass, but still driving us to seek other
differences in physical behavior. The principle of sufficient reason has often
been used to prove the law of inertia by arguing that the state of a body left
to itself must remain unchanged. But what counts as "state"? Scholasticism
interpreted it as place and so believed that a body comes to rest the moment
all action on it ceases. After Galilei, state means velocity in magnitude and
direction. Only experience can decide which interpretation is right. It must
also instruct us about the decisive, determining circumstances in the earlier
examples. Leibniz's argument in the controversy with Clarke and Newton about
the relativity of motion is a typical application of the principle of
sufficient reason, but Leibniz surely overestimated its reach as a source of
factual truths.

Mach, who fights the a priori and the attempt to turn the instinctive elements
in science into a new mysticism or something infallible, warns that even
instinctive recognitions of great logical force, such as Archimedes' symmetry
principle, can mislead. A magnetic needle lying in the magnetic meridian is
deflected in a definite direction by a current-carrying wire held parallel
above it. But the symmetry principle is satisfied if reflection in the plane
containing current and needle maps the current, but not the magnet, into
itself; instead, the magnet's north and south poles are exchanged. This view
is possible because positive and negative magnetism are inseparable and of the
same essence. We form a theoretical representation of magnetism, caused by
molecular electric circular currents perpendicular to the needle, in which the
surprising facts lose their strangeness and become necessities of essence.

Another guide for the theorist is the continuity principle first stated
generally by Leibniz, grounded in the impossibility of a genuine division of a
unitary continuum. It is scientifically wrong to exclude the zero angle and
the straight angle from the concept of angle, as Euclid does. Rest is not
contradictorily opposed to motion; it is a limiting or special case of motion.
Leibniz says that the law for bodies at rest is in a sense only a special case
of the general rule for moving bodies, the law of equality a case of
inequality, and the law for the straight-lined a subtype of the law for the
curved. He calls formations `homogon` when one can pass into another by
continuous alteration.

With the help of the `lex continui`, Leibniz refutes Descartes's impact laws,
which had to be formulated differently for many different cases. Galilei
derives the law of inertia by letting the inclination of the inclined plane,
for which he knows the laws of fall, decrease to zero; inertial motion is the
limit of falling motion. This origin makes understandable why Galilei seems to
have regarded the classical law of inertia as valid only for motions
perpendicular to the direction of gravity, an opinion that general relativity
can in a certain sense endorse. Mach recommends gradually modifying in thought
the circumstances of a special case, as far as possible, while holding fast to
the view already won. This is the surest path to the simplest understanding
of natural processes. Conversely, mathematics and physics often test an
overall view by considering limiting and special cases where relations can be
surveyed more easily.

Closely related to the continuity principle is the analogy principle, which
Newton formulates in his second rule: as far as possible, like effects must be
assigned the same cause. The analogy principle appears most magnificently in
atom theory. Mechanical laws derived from gross visible bodies and confirmed
most exactly by the planets are transferred to atoms. Facts may later force
corrections, but without this start no atomic research could begin. Even the
new quantum mechanics of atoms, so radically different that it gives up any
spatial image of atomic processes, still connects with the old mechanical laws
in their most transparent form, the Hamiltonian equations.

Lorentz obtained the electromagnetic basic laws of electron theory by taking
the phenomenological Maxwell equations used by electrical engineers and
stripping out all quantities by which the influence of matter appears through
material constants: conduction current, electrical polarization, and
magnetization. From the assumption that the true, microscopic electromagnetic
field satisfies these simplified harmonious laws, and with certain ideas about
the atomic structure of matter, he could rederive the old phenomenological
laws for the observable mean values, the macroscopic field.

Exact laws of nature may not contain material constants. These must instead
be determinable from exact laws on the basis of atomic structure. Because
phenomenological material laws usually fail where the finer structure of
matter matters, atomistic theory must show their limits of validity and supply
the atomistic laws that replace them beyond those limits. Maxwell had made
electrical polarization proportional to field strength. That works for static
and slowly changing fields, even for wireless-telegraphy fields already
oscillating more than a million times per second. But in optical oscillations
we encounter dispersion: the proportionality factor Maxwell treated as
constant, the dielectric constant equal to the square of the refractive index,
depends on frequency by laws tightly linked with the atomic structure of the
refracting medium.

What, finally, is the meaning of theory-formation? Hertz describes the method:
we make inner semblance-images or symbols of external objects, and we make
them such that the thought-necessary consequences of the images are always
again images of the nature-necessary consequences of the objects imaged. Under
skeptical epistemology, nineteenth-century English physics made it fashionable
to look only for images and analogies for limited fact-domains, constructing
mechanical models that reproduced certain features of the appearance but that
could not seriously be taken as "explanations," since people had been freed
from the "delusion" that one had to know a reality determined unambiguously in
itself. The method became strangely unfruitful as soon as it consciously set
out to design only images and models.

For Maxwell, physical analogies offered a way around both the disadvantages of
a purely mathematical theory, whose empirically important consequences are
hard to survey, and the disadvantages of a literal physical hypothesis, which
easily blinds one to facts. By a physical analogy he means a partial
similarity between the laws of one domain of appearances and those of another,
so that each illustrates the other. He mentions the analogy between gravitation
and the stationary heat state of a medium, both governed by Laplace's
potential equation, and contrasts it with the analogy between light and the
oscillations of an elastic medium. The latter reaches much further, but its
importance and fruitfulness should not make us forget that it rests only on a
formal similarity between laws. Stripped of its physical garment, it becomes a
theory of transverse changes of state: a system of truths that adds nothing
hypothetical to the observed facts, but probably loses vividness and
application-fruitfulness.

Mach speaks of a progressive "adaptation of thoughts to facts." He justifies
theories by the economy they bring to grasping and communicating states of
affairs and procedures. Others keep the faith that reason, according to
immanent principles, is at work symbolically constructing and presenting its
correlate, transcendent reality; without this faith, science is dead stuff for
them. But all agree on the final aim: predicting events. What guarantees that
the predictions produced by a theory will be fulfilled because the theory was
formed according to economy or reason-principles? This final fact points
beyond knowing. It is Hume's problem: trust in induction, if it is to be
grounded, can be grounded only by the principle of induction itself. But
world-trust and self-trust are the natural basic stance of all spiritual life,
especially of spiritual life enacted in thetic acts of reason, and they need
no "justification."

In his transcendental logic, Kant tried to discover by systematic procedure
the a priori principles for constructing experiential reality. His work has
the merit of lifting into philosophical consciousness the concept of reality
that had governed natural research since Galilei, freeing it from the
metaphysical ballast still loaded onto it by Leibniz's system, and securing it
against the Humean sensualism that had grown up out of natural science. Yet
natural scientists can hardly regard Kant's attempt as satisfactory. What he
lists is far from sufficient and too bound to the special form of contemporary
physics. On the other hand it contains superfluous components that entered
only through the logical schematism of the "great Chinaman of Königsberg."

The actually usable core is the ideas of substance and causality, to which the
last section of the article is devoted. Alongside the two `Analogies of
Experience` concerning them, Kant sets up a third analogy dealing with
reciprocal action; before them come the `Axioms of Intuition`, whose principle
is that all intuitions are extensive magnitudes, and the `Anticipations of
Perception`, according to which in all appearances the real that is an object
of sensation has intensive magnitude. He follows the first three groups with
the `Postulates of Empirical Thought in General`, concerning possibility,
actuality, and necessity. The Kantian problem, for whose answer some fragments
have been gathered here, remains open for the future, probably as an infinite
task. Kant himself, however, regarded metaphysics precisely as the only
science that could promise itself such completion in a short time, leaving
nothing for posterity.

## Notes

- Section 21 completes the methodology arc by making theory the unit of
  empirical accountability. Individual laws are not checked in isolation; the
  theory-system, including geometry, concepts, laws, and correction rules, is
  what meets experiment.
- Weyl is constructivist about science but not apriorist in the hard Kantian
  sense. Symbolic construction is always coupled with experiment and is
  revised through the coarse-to-fine correction of theory.
- The section weakens the sharp Kantian `a priori / a posteriori` split into
  a graded scale of firmness. Principles are laws with special resilience,
  simplicity, evidentness, and reach.
- Einstein functions as Weyl's decisive modern example: no logical road leads
  to theory-principles, yet the world of perceptions practically determines
  one theoretical system as superior.
- Simplicity is not surface economy. The true simplicity of nature is learned
  historically by correction, and it depends on mathematically prepared
  function-classes rather than on raw probability alone.
- The anti-surplus rule from section 19 returns as a theory criterion: no
  purely dogmatic addition may remain if it makes no possible difference to
  observable appearances.
- The principle of sufficient reason, continuity, and analogy are legitimate
  theory-guides, but they require experiential discipline. Leibniz is both a
  source and a warning.
- Maxwell's physical analogy clarifies Weyl's middle path between opaque
  mathematical formalism and dogmatic physical hypothesis.
- The close reframes Kant: his project remains live, but as an open infinite
  task rather than a completed table of fixed a priori principles.
