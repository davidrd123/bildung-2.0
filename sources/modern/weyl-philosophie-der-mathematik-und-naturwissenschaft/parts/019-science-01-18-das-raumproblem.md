## Translation

### 18. The Space Problem

#### a) Origin Of The Representation Of Space

Only in the nineteenth century did more exact investigations begin into the
psychological origin of the representation of space. The sensory regions that
especially contribute to the constitution of spatial representation are the
impressions of sight and touch; Bain added sensations of movement and muscular
feelings.

The single eye sees qualities spread out in a two-dimensional visual field. It
is two-dimensional because every one-dimensional line running through it cuts
it. The basic physiological fact is that the place at which we localize a
visual impression in this field is governed by the stimulated place on the
retina. There is here a one-to-one continuous "mapping" in the mathematical
sense: the field positions cohere continuously in the same way as the retinal
positions to which they correspond.

J. Müller, author of the law of specific sense energies, says outright that
the retina, in every visual field, sees only itself in its spatial extension
and in its state of affection. Helmholtz's great advance in physiological
optics is that he speaks not of identity but of correspondence. The same point
settles the famous problem of upright vision: in objective space the image on
the retina is rotated 180 degrees relative to the represented object. If on
one side stands "objective" space and on the other my intuitive space, and if
we assume that both carry Euclidean-metric structure, then the most "fidelity"
we can demand is that the correspondence between the objective object and the
image hovering before me in intuition be an isomorphic or similar mapping in
the sense of section 4. That is, all geometrical relations describable in the
metric concepts of objective space should be mirrored in geometrical relations
described by the corresponding metric concepts of intuitive space. But it is
nonsense to ask questions that would make sense only if object and image stood
in the same space.

The visual field does in fact have a metric structure. The resting eye
unquestionably grasps something like shape; here shape appears as a quality of
what is seen and the eye knows how to distinguish shapes from one another. But
this shape is similar neither to the seen object nor to the objective image
arising on the retina. This metric structure reaches a finer formation and
partial correction through eye movements. If by a turn of the gaze I can make
image I transform into image II, then I and II are congruent. For the field of
eye movements, shape is therefore no longer a given quality but a concept
obtained abstractively from the relation of congruence.

Lotze, on the basis of his psychological principle that only qualities of
sensation may count as directly perceivable and distinguishable from the soul,
demands the existence of "local signs": sensations whose qualitative gradations
underlie the different places in the visual field. He tried to understand
these more precisely as tendencies of movement that would bring the relevant
place into the point of sight, and as eye-muscle feelings that would accompany
such a movement. But this turns what is founded into the foundation, since the
resting eye already has, independently of eye movements belonging to the next
higher constitutive stage of space, its continuously extended visual field.
Wundt's attempt to stamp color gradations as local signs is better passed over
in silence. Helmholtz, although he accepted Lotze's thesis, admits that local
signs are `qualitates occultae`.

The thesis creates the problem of how one unitary sensation can split into the
two moments of color and extension. How, for example, do two sensations of the
same red at different retinal places `P` and `Q` possess an intimate kinship
that a red at `P` and a green at `Q` do not possess? If one understands the
point-like character of a simple sensation correctly, one will be little
inclined to posit, again as a continuously graded sensation, what makes red
into something extended. Instead one will acknowledge with Kant and Fichte: I
am originally not merely sensing but intuiting. Only because a continuum of
quality covers a temporal or space-time continuum of extension is something
there for me. Sensations as local signs then become superfluous.

What of visual impressions of rest and motion? If I let my gaze glide from top
to bottom, I have the impression that the objects rest, although their images
are projected on ever different retinal places. But this holds only when the
eye movement is voluntarily produced by the eye's motor apparatus. What
matters here is therefore not the muscle feelings connected with the eye
movement, but the intentions of will. Accordingly there is an original
impression of rest and motion or alteration. An object appears at rest when
its image does not shift on the retina and, at the same time, no eye movements
are intended. Between displacements of the retinal image and will-intentions
aimed at eye movements there is, in detail, a system of compensations plainly
refined by experience. Listing's law simplifies this enormously: for every
point of sight, within small fluctuations, there belongs one unique position
of the eye. The three degrees of freedom of the eyeball are thereby reduced to
two for the will.

The possibility of free turns of the gaze obedient to the will is also the
precondition for certain changes in the visual field being "interpreted" as
motions. The immediate impression of rest must not be invoked as "testimony of
the senses" against physical relativity theory. The objective explanation of
that phenomenon, which can only ever consist in demonstrating an objective
difference where a difference lies in what is intuitively given, uses solely
the idea of relative motion between covering physical beings and the dynamic
concept of motion: will-intentions that, through muscular forces, compel the
eyeball to deviate from its natural motion determined by the inertial field
and by its embedding in the rest of the body. The same holds for the movement
sensations of our body. They do not give notice of "absolute motion"; they are
throughout sensations of acceleration, indicating the body's or a limb's being
torn out of natural inertial motion and the resulting dynamic disturbances.

Optical perception of depth is closely connected with binocular vision, as
Wheatstone showed especially strikingly through the stereoscope. The feelings
of effort in accommodation also matter. The places in the visual field of one
eye and of the other stand in a one-to-one correspondence, such that what is
imaged at corresponding places in both visual fields is seen singly.
Stereoscopic depth vision rests on the difference between the two images: the
same color quality, especially the same contour, does not appear at
corresponding places in the two visual fields. Here a "nativist" theory,
especially represented by Hering, opposes an "empiricist" theory represented
by Helmholtz. The nativist theory burdens sensations with the whole
achievement and writes into the retinal places, besides local signs for
direction, a depth-value entering into sensation. Helmholtz's empiricist theory
regards the optical depth dimension as a constitutive achievement. Only the
latter fits the facts without strain. Yet one must add, in a "nativist" sense,
that the depth dimension is something new and original: with its help, from
the material of the two earlier stages, the two-dimensional pure sense-field
and the field of eye movement, the centered three-dimensional space is
constituted. In this space the body of the I finds its place, even if still as
the specially distinguished place of the center. On the two earlier stages
such a body-I, embedded in the field of extension, is plainly not yet present.
In the "flipping" of the perspective interpretation of a plane figure, the
"ensouling function" that transforms the figure in the visual field into the
appearance of an object struck by a ray of sight in centered space is
especially clear.

On this stage there also enters the linkage with the localization-field of
touch and limb movements. In the relevant visual-psychological experiments one
must constantly return, as a check, to reaching for the seen object. Husserl
emphasizes that these alleged facts and contingencies of spatial intuition,
foreign to "true" or "objective" space, prove, except for slight empirical
particularizations, to be necessities of essence. O. Becker has described the
constitutive stages of spatiality more precisely in Husserl's sense.

By stepping into the far horizon of centered space, and through the connected
displacements, through the feeling of the free possibility of bodily movement
and the corresponding will-intentions, homogeneous space is built upon
centered space. Only here does the body become an object with equal rights
beside other spatial objects. Only here do we gain the possibility of placing
ourselves into another's standpoint. Only this space can be thought as one and
the same for different subjects; it is the precondition for the construction
of the intersubjective world. Determinations of the orientation of objects in
it are therefore capable of intersubjective control and correction.

Against Aristotle's doctrine that space is a common sensible
(`αἰσθητὸν κοινόν`), Berkeley maintained that there are only different sensory
spaces. Stumpf objects by asking whether we are to believe that the duration
of a tactile sensation and that of a visual sensation are heterogeneous
contents. Berkeley is probably right that the pre-spatial localization fields
of the first and second stages are separate for sight and touch, whereas from
the third stage onward the one space alone is in question, penetrating the
sensory data of touch and sight. Space thereby becomes the linking band
between different sensory regions.

Bain's association theory of space tries to grasp space in this function; H.
Poincaré carried it through more sharply. First he separates quality-changes
from movements by saying that movements can be undone through a movement of
the I-body expressed in will-intentions and accompanying movement sensations.
Second, he tries to set up criteria for the covering of two spatial points
reached through different series of movement sensations and will-intentions.
Third, he investigates the "mapping" of the spaces of different sense organs
onto one another: for example, the first and second fingertip, or the visual
sense of the left eye and the tactile sense of the right thumb. This mapping
is usually interpreted as identification. On this view, that touch does not
reach into the distance while sight does means that the places in some other
sense-organ's space belonging to two covering places in the space of a tactile
organ likewise cover one another, while non-covering places in the tactile
field may correspond to covering places in the visual field. J. St. Mill
joined Bain, with the difference that for him the representation of space does
not consist in Bain's sensations and their associations, but arises from them
through a creative synthesis, a "psychic chemistry." All these theories ignore
the unquestionable givens on the lowest constitutive stages that do not have
the character of sensation, such as the beside-one-another in the pure visual
field.

#### b) The Essence Of Space

The interpenetration of the This, the here-now, and the Such is the general
form of consciousness. Only in the indissoluble unity of intuition and
sensation, where continuous extension and continuous quality cover one
another, is there something. Phenomenologically one will not get beyond this.
If, metaphysically with Plato, one lets suffering consciousness arise from the
meeting of a "motion" proceeding from the I and one proceeding from the object,
then it is natural to put quality on account of the object and extension on
account of the I. This is not reversible, because extension is the qualitatively
undifferentiated field of free possibilities, while concrete multiplicity
lies in the qualities. Fichte calls the illuminated, transparent, graspable and
permeable space "the purest image of my knowing": it is not seen but intuited,
and in it my seeing itself is intuited. The light is not outside me but in me;
I myself am the light. But how this unitary power of intuition, ensouling the
data of sensation, penetrates them and makes their achievements serviceable to
itself is, to a large extent, co-conditioned by experience.

The binding of these two moments to one another is the root of Aristotle's
thesis of the impossibility of empty space. Hume interprets it this way in the
`Treatise`, part II, though one must remember that spatial separation, or
better space-time separation, is just as much an immediately establishable
fact as space-time contact. Only through a `μετάβασις εἰς ἄλλο γένος`, a
passing-over into another genus, can this epistemological essential fact be
bent into an assertion about substantial-physical occurrence. One then arrives
at consequences such as Descartes draws, while Hume mocks them: the walls of a
box, if one pumped it empty, would have to touch. Leibniz denies the void
mainly from grounds connected with the perfection of the world and the
principle of sufficient reason. He expresses that binding of space to sensory
qualities by designating it, together with time, as the order of phenomena.
Stumpf objects that, in order to distinguish different orders from one
another, we must everywhere acknowledge a special absolute content with
respect to which the ordering takes place. He therefore claims that space
designates rather the positive absolute content on which the order is founded.
He demands that positional relations between spatial points be founded in a
"position" of the individual points by themselves, and through this logical
principle of the non-independence of relations, probably taken from Brentano,
he blocks his own understanding of the relativity of place.

Since the bare Here is nothing for itself that would distinguish itself from
some other Here, space is `principium individuationis`: it makes possible the
existence of numerically different things that are equal to one another in
their essence and constitution. For this reason Kant opposes space, as form of
intuition, to the matter of appearances, to "what corresponds to sensation."
Here too lies the root of the concept of congruence. Leibniz infers from this
the ideality of space and time, because they violate the principle of the
identity of indiscernibles, which he postulates with Spinoza as necessary in
the realm of substances, specifically as a consequence of the principle of
sufficient reason.

In the double nature of the real it is grounded that we can sketch a
theoretical image of being only against the background of the possible. Above
all, the four-dimensional continuum of space and time is the field of a priori
existing possibilities of coincidences. Leibniz therefore calls abstract space
the order of all places assumed as possible, and adds that it is accordingly
something ideal.

If we state the distance of the sun from the earth in meters, this assertion
gains a meaning verifiable in the given only if a rigid rod, on which the
individual meter sections are marked off by applying a movable rigid unit
measure, is supported on the earth in such a way that its end touches the sun.
The physically cleanest realization of the rigid body is the crystal. For
coordinate specifications to have a sense directly readable from reality, we
would have to imagine the whole world filled with a crystal. If, among the
covering motions of the crystal lattice, translations are distinguished by
their special properties, then the coordinate numbering by triples of numbers
could be carried out on its atoms, through the actual execution of those
covering motions, and could then also be used for naming positions throughout
space.

But that rigid rod between earth and sun is not present, its measurement by a
rigid meter-stick is not actually executed, and neither does that "coordinate
crystal" exist or undergo its covering motions. Indeed they must not be
present, because through their real forces they would influence the course of
world-occurrence. Of the structure treated in section 16, only the possibility
can be asserted of reading it off from occurrences that can be produced by the
free intervention of the experimenter. Geometrical statements are therefore
merely ideal determinations that, taken singly and by themselves, lack a sense
to be exhibited in the given. Only the whole network of ideal determinations
touches lived reality here and there, and at these points of contact it must
"fit." Perhaps this, expressed in the most general terms, is the "geometrical
method."

Galilei says that anyone who wants to treat questions of natural science
without the help of geometry undertakes something impossible. The enemies of
this method are, on one side, the empiricists, for whom the a priori
construction is a thorn in the eye and who imagine they can grasp being in a
single layer, without addition, "purely descriptively": Bacon against Galilei,
Hume against Kant, Mach against Einstein. On the other side stand the
metaphysicians, who out of hatred for freedom, for the infinitely open field
of "geometrical" construction, build up a rigid dialectical world of concepts
as true being: Hegel against Newton. From both sides, Aristotle, against
Archytas and Plato, is the great anti-mathematician.

#### c) A Priori Or A Posteriori?

Belief in the apriority of geometrical knowledge, especially Euclidean
geometry, is deeply rooted in earlier times. Kepler writes that the science of
space is singular and eternal and radiates again from the spirit of God; that
human beings may share in it is one reason why the human being is the image of
God. Leibniz tried to prove geometrical truths as analytic truths. With
geometry in view, Kant poses the problem of the `Critique of Pure Reason`: How
are synthetic judgments `a priori` possible? He believes that his doctrine
that space is pure, non-empirical intuition has answered this question for
geometry. Since that in which sensations alone are ordered and can be placed
into certain forms cannot itself again be sensation, the matter of all
appearance is given only `a posteriori`, while the form of appearance as a
whole must lie ready in the mind `a priori` and therefore be capable of
consideration apart from all sensation. Thus, for Kant, his explanation alone
makes comprehensible the possibility of geometry as a synthetic cognition `a
priori`. The development of non-Euclidean geometry shakes the old certainty.

Proclus had already warned, in his commentary on Euclid, against misusing an
appeal to evidence in connection with the parallel axiom. Gauss writes to
Olbers in 1817 that he is ever more convinced that the necessity of our
geometry cannot be proved, at least not by human understanding and not for
human understanding. Perhaps in another life we may come to other insights
into the essence of space, now inaccessible to us. Until then, geometry would
have to be placed not in the same rank as arithmetic, which stands purely `a
priori`, but in the same rank as mechanics. In 1830 he writes to Bessel that
we must humbly admit that if number is merely a product of our mind, space also
has a reality outside our mind to which we cannot prescribe its laws
completely `a priori`.

Helmholtz shows that the two parts of the Kantian doctrine of space are not so
closely connected that the second follows from the first. The first is that
space is a pure form of intuition; the second is that the science of space,
Euclidean geometry, is valid `a priori`. Helmholtz is prepared to accept the
first as a correct expression of the matter, but from it nothing more can be
inferred than that all things of the external world are necessarily spatially
extended. In agreement with Riemann, he demonstrates the
empirical-physical sense of geometry and appeals to Newton, who declared in
the introduction to the `Principia` that geometry itself is grounded in
mechanical practice and is in fact nothing other than that part of all
mechanics which precisely establishes and grounds the art of measuring.

If, beside the "physical equivalence" of spatial magnitudes, there existed an
equality immediately given in transcendental intuition, then the agreement of
the two concepts would still only be a fact of experience. In a case of
conflict, transcendental equality would be demoted "to the rank of a sensory
illusion, an objectively false appearance." Against the argument that
non-Euclidean geometry has no intuitability, Helmholtz sets up a definition of
the intuitable: what is required is the complete representability of the sense
impressions that the object in question would arouse in us under all thinkable
conditions of observation, according to the known laws of our sense organs,
and through which it would distinguish itself from other similar objects. Here
we can refer back to section 17's description of the relation between the
objective world and its subjective image as grasped by the point-eye moving
along a worldline.

Against the argument that any attempted experimental testing of geometry
always brings in properly physical assertions about the behavior of rigid
bodies and light rays, Weyl says that physical laws no more than geometrical
laws permit, each by itself, a test in experience. The "truth" of a
constructive theory can be tested only as a whole. Under the influence of
modern mathematical-axiomatic investigations one distinguishes between
"mathematical space," whose laws are logical consequences of arbitrarily
accepted axioms, and "physical space," the order-schema of actual things that
enters theoretical world-construction as an integrating component. Some
authors set beside it "intuitive space." With regard to that distinction,
Einstein says: insofar as the propositions of mathematics refer to reality,
they are not certain; and insofar as they are certain, they do not refer to
reality.

Since then, the general philosophical development has proceeded so that Kant's
`a priori` judgments have split in two directions. On one side they become
"essence-laws," which express founding relations among the givens of
consciousness but never claim to involve a statement about facts. This line
culminates in Husserl's phenomenology, where the `a priori` is much richer
than in Kant's system. On the other side they become principles of theoretical
construction, whose positing, on the most extreme view, Poincaré's, rests on
mere convention.

After the mathematical part, Weyl need not go further here into the general
mathematics of continua and of the chief structures they can bear. In physical
space, however, one can oppose `a priori` moments to `a posteriori` moments in
a certain objective sense that does not concern, as Kant's distinction does,
the source and kind of cognition. According to the Riemann-Einstein standpoint,
the absolutely given Euclidean-Pythagorean nature of the metric stands on one
side. This nature does not share in the ineradicable vagueness of whatever
occupies a variable place in a continuous scale. Opposed to it are the mutual
orientation of the metrics at different points and the contingent,
matter-dependent, changeful quantitative course of the metric field, which can
be given only approximately and with the aid of immediate intuitive pointers
to reality. General relativity therefore does not simply deny that something
in the structure of the extensive medium of the external world is `a priori`
in this sense. It shifts the boundary between `a priori` and `a posteriori` to
another place. Strictly speaking, as always in such cases, this opposition
means that an aprioric moment can be lifted out of the whole without
exhausting the whole; there is no purely aposterioric remainder left over once
one "subtracts" the first part from the whole.

Besides and prior to the one nature of the metric field, the `a priori` of the
world includes its once-for-all determined topological connectedness-relations,
especially the dimensional number 4. The quantitative course of the metric
field obeys exact laws of nature, Einstein's gravitational laws, which are
constructed similarly to Maxwell's laws of the electromagnetic field. Within
the `a posteriori` one must therefore distinguish once again between
law-governed natural binding and what appears free, a contingent factuality,
even relative to that binding. A three-membered gradation takes the place of
the two-membered one.

If one recognizes an intuitive space beside physical space and claims that its
metric structure fulfills the Euclidean laws on essential grounds, then this
does not contradict physics so long as one holds fast to the Euclidean
character of the infinitely small neighborhood of a point `O`, where the I is
momentarily located. The angles formed for the point-eye by the directions of
the light rays arriving from individual stars do in fact satisfy the laws of
spherical trigonometry in Euclidean space. But one must then admit that the
relation of intuitive space to physical space becomes all the vaguer the
farther one moves from the I-center. It is comparable to a tangent plane laid
at point `O` against a curved surface, the physical space. In the immediate
neighborhood of `O` the two cover one another, but the farther one moves from
`O`, the more arbitrary the continuation of this covering relation into a
one-to-one correspondence between plane and surface becomes.

Intuitive space therefore need not, as such, have a vague character. It also
does not bridge the discrepancies grounded in two-eyed vision by means of a
wavering compromise, except under especially extreme circumstances or when
attention is directed to visual perceptions as such and the "struggle of the
visual fields" breaks out. Intuitive space is intuitively of undisturbed
clarity, although in the objective construction the situation can be
represented only as a compromise. Statements about intuitive space are just as
little authoritative for physical space as, for example, the statement that
the seen color orange stands "nearer" to yellow than to blue points to a
metric of color-space with exact metric laws. As section 12 showed, color-space
possesses only a projective structure.

For the aprioric elements of space there arises the task of understanding,
from rational grounds, their peculiarity and special position among the more
general possibilities uncovered by formalizing mathematics. There are three
different possibilities for the nature of a four-dimensional Riemannian
manifold, according to whether the metric fundamental form possesses 0, 1, or
2 negative dimensions. If the world corresponded to case 0, no propagation of
effects from a world-point `O` would be possible at all. In case 2, past and
future would be fused into one single world-region. Thus the fact that, for
the nature of the metric field in the actual world, the middle case 1 obtains
can be grounded in the necessity of a causal structure. Such a structure lets
an I, acting and suffering, connect with the world in such a way that past and
future, what is known and what is willed, separate from one another.

Similarly, from `n`-dimensional Euclidean or Riemannian geometry, which arose
through compelling formalization from three-dimensional geometry, one must ask
from what inner grounds actual space realizes precisely the case `n = 3`.
Aristotle gave some answers still wholly belonging to mythical thinking.
Galilei discusses and rejects them at the beginning of his `Dialogue`. The
solution Galilei himself gives is only a clearer formulation of the problem,
not an answer. Weyl thinks the most promising path is that theoretical
physical construction will lead to such grounds. With the help of the wave
equation of light, immediately transferable to `n`-dimensional space, one can
show that only in a space of odd dimensional number, when a candle is
extinguished, does darkness enter around the candle in a circle whose radius
grows with the speed of light. At least one important inner difference
concerning the propagation of effects is thereby established between even and
odd dimensional number.

According to a theory Weyl has proposed, which organically links the electric
field with the metric field, the especially simple and harmonious laws Maxwell
found for the electric field can exist only in a four-dimensional world. This
principle of "gauge invariance" holds for no other dimensional number.

The group-structure of the Euclidean rotation group, which governs the metric
nature of the world even after Riemann-Einstein, is fundamentally different
for the different dimensional numbers. One can infer from this that the
indifference of mathematical and physical laws to dimensional number ceases in
a deeper layer scarcely touched by physics today. The hope for a future
compelling solution of the problem from here is therefore well justified.
Bolzano attempted to make the three-dimensionality of space intelligible from
its function in constituting the external world for consciousness, but his
attempt turned out quite bizarre. A newer approach by O. Becker in the same
direction also remains wholly unsatisfactory.

Weyl indicates another path for understanding the Pythagorean nature of the
metric, expressed in the Euclidean rotation group, precisely from the
separation of `a priori` and `a posteriori`. Only in the case of this group
does the intrinsically contingent quantitative course of the metric field,
under all circumstances and however it may have formed itself within the
framework of its a priori fixed nature, uniquely determine infinitesimal
parallel displacement: rotation-free progress from one point into the world.
The claim involves a deep mathematical theorem belonging to group theory,
whose proof Weyl says he has provided. He believes that this solution of the
space problem plays, within Riemann-Einstein theory, the same role as the
Helmholtz-Lie solution plays for rigid Euclidean space. Perhaps the postulate
of the unique determinacy of "straight-progressing" can also be justified from
the requirements posed by the phenomenological constitution of space. Becker,
however, still wants to ground the meaning of the Euclidean rotation group for
intuitive space on Helmholtz's postulate of free mobility. If, in agreement
with section 15, the transformation group `Δ₀` in three or four dimensions is
regarded as the representation of an abstract group, then more emphasis should
be placed on the distinguishing structural features of that abstract group
than on the special concrete representation of `Δ₀`.

The literature list for the section includes Descartes, Galilei, Hobbes,
Leibniz, Locke, Hume, Kant, Helmholtz, Mach, Study, Schlick, Husserl, Becker,
Weyl's `Mathematische Analyse des Raumproblems`, Carnap's `Der Raum`, and R.
Luneburg's `Mathematical Analysis of Binocular Vision`.

## Analytic Notes

Section 18 completes the arc of sections 16-17 by making the space problem
both psychological and physical. Weyl does not let "space" be either a mere
mental container or a finished physical object. It is constituted upward from
the visual field, bodily motion, centered space, and homogeneous
intersubjective space, while physical geometry is constructed downward as an
ideal network whose terms only gain reality-contact through possible
experiments.

The section sharply separates three spaces:

- `mathematical space`: a formal space whose laws follow from accepted axioms
- `physical space`: the order-schema of actual things inside theoretical
  world-construction
- `Anschauungsraum`: intuitive space, clear in its local givenness but not
  authoritative for global physical geometry

The crucial payoff is that general relativity does not abolish the `a priori`.
It relocates it. The Euclidean-Pythagorean nature of the metric, the
topological connectedness-relations, and dimensional number belong to an
aprioric side of physical space, while the quantitative course of the metric
field is matter-dependent and empirical. Even that empirical side divides
again into law-governed binding and contingent factuality. The resulting
schema is threefold, not a simple Kantian `a priori` / `a posteriori` split.

The phenomenological and mathematical halves are not fused. Weyl repeatedly
warns against deriving physical geometry directly from intuitive space. The
tangent-plane analogy is decisive: intuitive space may be locally clear and
Euclidean around the I-center while physical space is globally curved and only
approximately tied to intuition through measurement and observation.

## Translation Decisions

- `Raumvorstellung` is `spatial representation`
- `Gesichtsfeld` is `visual field`
- `Lokalzeichen` is `local signs`
- `Leib-Ich` is `body-I`
- `zentrierter Raum` is `centered space`
- `homogener Raum` is `homogeneous space`
- `Anschauungsraum` is `intuitive space`
- `Wesensgesetze` is `essence-laws`
- `Maßfeld` remains `metric field`
- `Eichinvarianz` is `gauge invariance`
- `gerade-Fortschreiten` is `straight-progressing`, kept awkward because Weyl
  treats it as a postulate-like technical phrase rather than ordinary
  straight-line motion
- `drehungsfreies Fortschreiten` is `rotation-free progress`
- `apriorisch` / `aposteriorisch` are `aprioric` / `aposterioric` when naming
  the structural moment, and `a priori` / `a posteriori` when preserving the
  classical distinction

## Adversarial Check

The main risk is over-Kantianizing the section. Weyl does not simply defend a
Kantian doctrine of Euclidean intuition after non-Euclidean geometry. He
splits the old Kantian claim: one line goes to Husserlian essence-laws of
givenness, the other to principles of theoretical construction. Physical
space then gets its own objective `a priori` / `a posteriori` articulation.

A second risk is treating the early visual-psychology material as historical
preface. It does more work than that. The sequence from visual field to
centered space to homogeneous intersubjective space is Weyl's source-local
bridge between section 17's subject/object problem and the later claim that
the intuitive space around the I is only tangent-plane-like relative to
physical space.

A third risk is flattening the final dimensionality argument into a curiosity.
It is not an aside: it is Weyl's attempt to show that formalizing mathematics
opens a space of possibilities and then asks why actual physical space occupies
this structurally special case.
