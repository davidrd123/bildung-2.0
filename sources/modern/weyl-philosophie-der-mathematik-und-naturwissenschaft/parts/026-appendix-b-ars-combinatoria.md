## Translation / Analytic Digest

Weyl opens with the modern-scientific emergence of abstract symbolic structures
as the hard core of objectivity behind subjective appearance. Appendix A
treated the structure of mathematics itself. Appendix B treats some of the
simplest structures: aggregates and complexes. Their philosophical importance
comes from their contact with individuation and probability, and their natural
importance comes from genetics, quantum mechanics, the nervous system,
computing machines, and even the new game-theoretic attempt of von Neumann and
Morgenstern to ground economics.

The first distinction is between sameness of kind and numerical identity:
`quale` and `quid`, likeness and identity. Leibniz's principle of the identity
of indiscernibles gives an a priori answer, but modern physics reaches an
empirical answer for elementary particles, especially photons and electrons.
This is why the Rosenkavalier epigraph is not decorative. The identity of the
I through time is the most philosophically charged example of the same problem
that combinatorics meets in stripped symbolic form.

A finite set `S` of `n` elements can be divided into complementary subsets
`S‚āĀ + S‚āā`. If the elements are individually labeled by arbitrary marks `p`,
there are `2‚ĀŅ` individually different subsets. But objectivity cannot depend
on a merely arbitrary labeling. Weyl explicitly names the invariant point as a
relativity principle: only relations and statements unchanged by relabeling
have objective meaning. If the elements are not distinguishable, a subset is
specified only by the number `n‚āĀ` of its elements, so the visible division is
one of `n + 1` possibilities. The binomial coefficients then explain how many
individual divisions correspond to one visible division.

Weyl generalizes this to aggregates whose elements fall into equivalence
classes or states `C‚āĀ ... C‚āĖ`. With labels, there are `k‚ĀŅ` individual states.
Without labels, the effective state is the list of occupation numbers
`n‚āĀ ... n‚āĖ`. The permutation group gives the invariant form of the same
relativity principle: individual states are equivalent if they differ only by a
permutation of labels. A monomic aggregate is the limiting case in which no two
elements belong to the same class, so the classes themselves individuate the
elements and artificial labels are unnecessary.

Genetics gives Weyl the central biological application. The duality of genetic
constitution and environment, nature and nurture, functions like an a priori
organizing concept comparable to inertia/force in mechanics. Genes are modeled
as points; alleles are their discrete states. Syngamy unites two gamete
aggregates into a zygote, while meiosis divides a zygote into complementary
subaggregates. Heredity then has a combinatorial basis: stability arises from
the determinate union of two aggregates, variability from the multiple possible
partitions of one aggregate.

The same formalism clarifies probability. A gamete `S‚Ä≤` appears with a relative
frequency `ő≥(S‚Ä≤)`, and its complementary gamete `S‚Ä≥` must have the same a
priori probability. Syngamy probabilities then multiply. This is not a merely
biological detail: it returns to the root of probability theory, where what
counts as a "different case" depends on what is treated as individuated.

Weyl then turns from aggregate composition to identity through time. An
individualist description gives each element a state `C(p; t)` through time,
but this presupposes a fixed labeling across all moments. A non-individualizing
description gives only the visible counts `n‚āĀ(t) ... n‚āĖ(t)`. In that view, the
world is recreated at each instant; no identity-fetter binds present beings to
later beings. Weyl explicitly associates this stance with the early Islamic
Mutakallimun. Where identity is made, it is made through observable continuity
of state, as when a wave is followed across water or a person is recognized.

Section 2 refines the biological vocabulary. Classes split into genera and
classes; genes correspond to genera, alleles to classes. A genotype is an
individual aggregate, a phenotype is the visible development under given
external conditions, and an effective aggregate records only the counts in
classes or genera. Weyl distinguishes his purely combinatorial notion of
species from the biological species concept, citing Dobzhansky to mark the gap.
Balanced syngamy and meiosis preserve species through generations, and pure
lines separate hereditary constitution from environmental variation.

Section 3 applies the same logic to physics. Classical mechanics treats the
state of a point mass as complete when position and velocity are given. Quantum
mechanics replaces the continuous state manifold with discrete possible
states. Radiation in a mirrored cavity can be described either as a set of
oscillators with occupation numbers or as a photon gas. Photons are individuals
without identity: the effective state is exhausted by the occupation number
`n_őĪ` for each photon state `őĪ`, yielding Bose-Einstein statistics.

Electrons reverse the phenomenological order: their corpuscular character is
obvious first, their wave character only later. Yet quantum theory describes
electron gases by the same occupation-number language, with one decisive
difference. No two electrons can occupy the same complete state. The Pauli
principle explains the periodic system and chemical bonding, and it means that
the electron gas is monomic. Weyl's compressed conclusion is striking: physics
confirms the Mutakallimun in a precise sense, because neither photons nor
electrons possess individuality. The Leibniz-Pauli prohibition holds for
electrons, not for photons.

Section 4 adds structure to aggregates and calls the result a complex. Points
may stand in a neighborhood relation; in genetics, connected components of
these point-complexes are chromosomes. A phenotype depends not only on the
aggregate of genetic points but also on the structure of the complex, which
appears biologically as position effect. Linkage and crossing-over explain why
Mendelian independence is exact for points in different chromosomes but only
approximate inside one chromosome. Morgan's Drosophila maps are treated as
quantitative evidence for this structure.

The same section also opens the point/structure mutation distinction. Point
mutations change a point's class without changing the complex's links.
Structure mutations change the links while leaving point states unchanged.
Rods, rings, centromeres, chromosome doubling, and sex determination all enter
as places where a simple combinatorial schema must be supplemented by biological
irregularity. Weyl keeps the formal schema broad enough to include these
departures rather than pretending genetics is already perfectly sharp.

The closing thermodynamics section connects Appendix B back to section 23 and
forward to Appendix C. Quantum theory simplifies statistical thermodynamics
because complete states are discrete and can be counted. For many systems with
fixed average energy, the overwhelming majority of distributions converge to
the canonical distribution proportional to `e^{-őĪUŠĶĘ}`. The mean of a quantity
is computed by the weighted expression `‚ü®Z‚ü©_őĪ`. The reciprocal `őĪ‚ĀĽ¬Ļ` has the
dimension of energy and functions as statistical temperature.

For a composite system without interaction, the canonical distribution factors:
`wŠĶĘ‚āĖ = wŠĶĘ ¬∑ w‚āĖ‚Ä≤`. This gives thermal equilibrium for the parts, statistical
independence for the combination, and a shared value of the temperature
parameter. Comparing with an ideal gas yields `kT`, where `k` is Boltzmann's
constant. The final principle is the canonical distribution
`w = w‚Āį ¬∑ e^{-U/kT}`. Appendix B therefore supplies the combinatorial
infrastructure behind Weyl's earlier treatment of entropy, probability, and
the one-directionality of time.

## What Appendix B Adds

- It makes the objectivity principle concrete as invariance under arbitrary
  relabeling.
- It joins individuation and probability: the counting of cases depends on what
  has been individuated.
- It bridges mathematics into biology without treating biology as mere applied
  mathematics; Weyl repeatedly notes where genetic concepts are sharp only
  relative to a developing theory.
- It makes quantum statistics philosophically central: photons and electrons
  force a non-classical account of individuality.
- It connects the biological vocabulary of structure and mutation to the
  broader Weyl theme that visible phenomena depend on symbolic-structural
  organization.
- It supplies the combinatorial basis for the entropy/probability discussion in
  section 23 and the quantum-causality discussion coming in Appendix C.
