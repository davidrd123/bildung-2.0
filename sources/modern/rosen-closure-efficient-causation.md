# Rosen: Closure to Efficient Causation

**Source**: Robert Rosen, *Life Itself: A Comprehensive Inquiry into the Nature, Origin, and Fabrication of Life* (1991), ch. 10
**Status**: open
**Triggered by**: the close/free translation split in escolios; the `term:hinzutretende` evidence chain in zeitmauer; the Agüera y Arcas hierarchy (Daisyworld: "something that creates the conditions for its own continued existence is stabler than something that cannot")

## Formal (Rosen's register)

An (M,R)-system consists of three mappings in a closed loop:

- **f : A → B** — metabolism. Transforms inputs from the environment into products.
- **Φ : B → Hom(A,B)** — repair. Uses products to regenerate the metabolic mapping itself.
- **β : Hom(A,B) → Hom(B, Hom(A,B))** — replication. The metabolic mapping regenerates the repair mapping.

The system is **closed to efficient causation**: every component that acts as an efficient cause (an Aristotelian "maker") is itself produced by some other component within the system. No external fabricator is needed. The system produces the conditions of its own production.

A living system is thermodynamically open but organizationally closed: the efficient causes required for its continued activity are regenerated from within the system itself. Rosen argued that this closure is not captured by ordinary sequential mechanisms with externally fixed transition structure. What remains disputed is the stronger claim that such closure cannot be realized or modeled by richer computational architectures. Cárdenas et al. preserve the closure concept while questioning Rosen's logic; Mossio, Longo, and Stewart give a type-free λ-calculus formulation and find no principled obstacle to simulation; Palmer et al. argue Rosen's notion of computability was stricter than standard CS usage.

The organizational insight itself is not controversial: the distinction between living and non-living is organizational, not material. The organization that makes something alive is precisely what disappears under reductive analysis — because analysis proceeds by opening the causal loop, and the loop *is* the life. What is contested is whether that organizational pattern is inherently non-mechanical.

## Prose (close translation)

A living system has a peculiar property: each of its essential functions produces the conditions for another essential function, and the chain loops back with no loose end. Metabolism transforms raw material into products; those products regenerate the metabolic machinery; the metabolic machinery regenerates the replacement process that regenerates the metabolic machinery. In the **minimal relational schema**, cut anywhere and the system loses organizational identity — not merely damaged, but no longer the kind of thing it was. (In full organisms, Mossio and Moreno treat organizational closure as necessary but not sufficient for organismality; there are further features beyond closure alone.)

The deeper claim: in a system with closure to efficient causation, the constraints and catalysts that channel the system's dynamics are themselves continually regenerated by the system. This is not recursion (a function calling itself). It is something stranger: a function that *produces the conditions under which it can continue to function*. Rosen argued this pattern escapes standard sequential mechanisms. Whether richer computational architectures (type-free λ-calculus, reflexive objects in cartesian closed categories) can realize it remains an open and active dispute.

## Poetic (Nietzsche/Jünger register)

The living thing is that which makes the maker that makes it.

Not recursion — not the ouroboros swallowing its own tail, which is one motion endlessly repeated. This: the mouth *produces* the tail *which produces* the body *which produces* the mouth. Each organ is the efficient cause of the next and the final cause of the last. Remove any one and you do not have a damaged organism. You have nothing that was ever alive.

The anatomist opens the body to find the cause of life and finds: chemistry. He is not wrong. Life left when the knife entered. What remained was the parts without the parting.

## Diagrammatic

```
             f (metabolism)
     A ——————————————————→ B
     ↑                           |
     |                           |  Φ (repair)
     |                           |
     β (replication)            ↓
     Hom(B,Hom(A,B)) ←—— Hom(A,B)
```

No node is first. No arrow is more fundamental. The triangle has no root — it is its own root. The rootlessness echoes the Chrein category-theory dependency graph (hub nodes presuppose each other), but the resemblance is structural, not identity. Whether "brain-shape" and "life-shape" are genuinely the same shape or just share rootlessness as a feature is an open question, not an earned claim.

## Mathematical

In the category **Set** (schema-level — not a close rendering of the biochemistry):

Given f : A → B, the replacement map Φ is an element of B → Hom(A,B), i.e., Φ ∈ Hom(B, Hom(A,B)) = Hom(B, Bᴬ).

The organizational-invariance map β is: given f ∈ Hom(A,B), produce Φ ∈ Hom(B, Hom(A,B)). So β ∈ Hom(Hom(A,B), Hom(B, Hom(A,B))) = Hom(Bᴬ, (Bᴬ)ᴮ).

**Caveat on labels**: Rosen's original terms "repair" and "replication" are misleading if read in molecular-biology terms. Later authors (Cárdenas et al., Letelier et al.) prefer "replacement" and "organizational invariance." The efficient cause of replacement is not literally the product B but a related property or function of B — a subtlety that matters when moving from relational schema to biochemical realization.

**Caveat on pointwise equalities**: Presenting closure as β(f) = Φ and Φ(f(a)) = f slides too fast from relational organization to specific equations. These capture the schema but not the biology.

Rosen argued that in an (M,R)-system, functions and their arguments must occupy the same level — Hom(A,B) is both a space of maps *and* a source of arguments for higher-order maps, collapsing the hierarchy of logical types. He concluded that no system with externally specified transition structure can realize this. **This conclusion is disputed.** Mossio, Longo, and Stewart give a type-free λ-calculus formulation using reflexive objects in cartesian closed categories and find no principled obstacle. The formal problem is impredicativity or reflexivity, and type-free formalisms handle it without requiring non-computability. The organizational insight (closure as a pattern) is stable; the anti-computability corollary is not.

### What no single register captures

The *identity* between Rosen's closure and Jünger's waxwing observation (zm:1). The waxwing is seen; the seeing produces the seer; the seer produces the conditions for the next seeing. This is not explicit in any register above. It emerges only when the encounter is *placed beside* the translation work. That placement is the project's actual instrument.

## Anchors

These are transfer heuristics — places where the closure concept illuminates something in the live projects. They are not identities. The concept may apply loosely, metaphorically, or not at all once tested against the source passages.

- `zm:1` — the waxwing passage. Analogy that becomes argument. The observation produces the observer. Possible structural echo of closure, but the parallel needs testing against the actual passage.
- `zm:6` — `der Hinzutretende`. The one who enters and changes what they enter. If the observer is an efficient cause within the system observed, this is closure-adjacent. But Jünger's figure is interpretive, not metabolic — the resemblance may not survive scrutiny.
- `zm:11` — `bildende Kraft` (formative power). The organism's self-shaping capacity. Rosen may formalize what Jünger inherits from Goethe's morphology, but the modern autonomy-theory version (closure of constraints) is closer to Goethe than Rosen's original algebra.
- `esc:2` — "thought progresses between symmetric injustices." The walking *is* the thinking — suggestive of closure in the domain of cognition, but this is loose analogy until tested.
- `exg` (general) — Dick's recursive model-revision (each theory produces the anomaly demanding the next) has the circularity of closure. Whether it genuinely closes (converges, regenerates its own conditions) or merely recurses (repeats without organizational invariance) is an open question.

## Open questions (status: open, updated with critique)

### Does Rosen's proof against machines hold?
Not as a settled theorem. The closure concept is broadly accepted; the non-computability corollary is not. Cárdenas et al. preserve the concept while questioning Rosen's logic. Mossio, Longo, and Stewart produce an explicit λ-calculus treatment and find no principled obstacle to simulation. Palmer and Gatherer-style work reopens the door with richer machine formalisms. Some critics argue Rosen's notion of computability was stricter than standard CS usage, which weakens the anti-computability jump further. **Working assessment: the organizational insight is stable; the anti-machine proof is probably not (85% confidence it doesn't hold as stated).**

### Is the logical-type collapse related to Chrein's hierarchy collapse?
Family resemblance, yes. Identity, probably not. Rosen's formal problem is impredicativity: the same entity must occupy the roles of argument, function, and result. That's exactly why Mossio et al. move to type-free λ-calculus and reflexive objects in cartesian closed categories. If Chrein is about rootless mutual presupposition, the analogy is real. But the nearest precise mathematical neighbor is untyped λ-calculus with fixed-point machinery, not "n-categories collapsing" in the abstract. **Working assessment: structural resemblance, not identity (75% confidence).**

### How does closure relate to Freedman's compression?
Not as "the limit of compression" — different axis. Freedman is about compressibility via nested macros. Rosen is about internal production of efficient causes. But there is a bridge: in a closed organization, the macro is not merely a shorter description of independently specified parts. The macro-organization partly constitutes what those parts are doing. Full unwrapping is not neutral — it can destroy the explanatory object by linearizing something whose reality is cyclical and mutually entailing. **That non-neutrality of unwrapping is the connection worth pushing.**

### Where does closure first appear in the Agüera y Arcas ladder?
The ladder needs splitting into at least three axes:
- **Daisyworld**: ecological feedback and homeostasis. Real closure of a sort, but not yet Rosenian — daisies are not collectively regenerating all their own makers.
- **Autocatalytic sets (RAF theory)**: first stage that is genuinely Rosen-adjacent. Self-sustaining, functionally closed catalytic networks. Weak closure starts here.
- **Autopoiesis / closure of constraints with boundary**: stronger organismal closure. Bare RAF is not enough for cells — you need self-produced boundaries.
- **Semantic closure / indirect encoding**: upgrades closure into open-ended, description-based heredity. Pattee, Rocha, Hofmeyr's (F,A) system tries to give Rosen-like closure biochemical realization with the symbol-function split.

**Compressed: Daisyworld = feedback stability. RAF = first weak closure. Autopoiesis = stronger organismal closure. Semantic closure = open-ended heredity.**

### Is "brain-shape = life-shape" earned?
No. Poetic inflation, not earned structure. The Jünger/Dick/Chrein links are good transfer heuristics but not yet identities. Soften or defer.
