# Whitehead Minimal Primer

This is the least Whitehead needed before starting Stengers. It is deliberately
thin. The point is to reduce disorientation, not to settle the system.

## The Basic Timeline

Whitehead's work is usually approached through three overlapping phases:

1. Cambridge: mathematics, logic, and the collaboration with Bertrand Russell on
   *Principia Mathematica*.
2. London: physics, relativity, philosophy of nature, science, and education.
3. Harvard: speculative metaphysics, especially *Science and the Modern World*,
   *Process and Reality*, *Adventures of Ideas*, and *Modes of Thought*.

Stengers cares about the continuity across these phases. The mathematician and
philosopher of science do not disappear when the metaphysician appears.

## The Main Shift

Whitehead is often read as a process philosopher. That means the primary
philosophical unit is not a self-contained substance that later changes. The
primary unit is an event, occurrence, activity, or becoming.

This shift matters because it changes the old question:

- less: what things exist underneath appearances?
- more: what must experience, nature, and knowledge be like if relations,
  events, and becoming are not secondary?

## Why Science Matters

Whitehead is not anti-science. He is suspicious of philosophical pictures that
pretend to be authorized by science while quietly shrinking experience.

A recurring target is the split between:

- nature as directly experienced: colors, sounds, values, felt relations
- nature as scientific reality: matter, molecules, measurable relations

Whitehead calls this kind of split the bifurcation of nature. Stengers treats it
as a bad inheritance: it makes us choose between experience and science when the
harder task is to construct concepts that can keep both in play.

## Terms You Will Meet

`event`

A unit of happening. In the earlier philosophy of nature, events help Whitehead
think nature as passage and relation rather than as a set of static things.

`object`

Not just an ordinary thing. In the early chapters, Stengers tracks different
kinds of objects: sense-objects, perceptual objects, and scientific objects. The
point is to ask how each gives knowledge a foothold.

`ingression`

The way something makes itself relevant in an event or situation. In the early
chapters, this helps avoid treating objects as either merely mental or simply
located bits of matter.

`cogredience`

A technical term from the philosophy of nature. For a first pass, read it as a
way of thinking the relation between a percipient standpoint and the surrounding
field of nature.

`actual entity` / `actual occasion`

In the later metaphysics, the basic units of reality are occasions of becoming.
They are not inert atoms. They are self-realizing processes that inherit from
the past and achieve a determinate form.

`prehension`

Whitehead's broad term for taking account of, feeling, or incorporating another
reality. It is not limited to conscious perception.

`concrescence`

The becoming-together of an actual occasion: many inherited elements are unified
into one achieved actuality.

`creativity`

The ultimate principle of becoming in Whitehead's metaphysics. Treat this with
care: it is not personal creativity or artistic originality, though Stengers will
make the term pressure ordinary habits of thought.

`eternal object`

A pure potential or form of definiteness that can be realized in actual
occasions. Do not read it too quickly as a simple return to Plato.

`God`

Whitehead's God is not an ordinary supernatural ruler. In Stengers's reading,
God becomes part of a speculative construction concerned with value, order,
possibility, and persuasion. Leave inherited reflexes about theism at the door
for the first pass.

## The First Reading Posture

Read Whitehead through Stengers as a philosopher of constructed obligations.
The question is often:

- What must this concept make us stop saying?
- What distinction does it prevent from becoming too easy?
- What kind of attention does it demand?
- What false problem does it dissolve?

If a term feels strange, do not immediately ask "what does it mean?" Ask first:
"what work is this term being made to do?"
