# 1. The Mathematician and the Sunset

## Before Reading

This chapter begins the philosophy-of-nature arc. Keep one question in view:
what must "nature" mean if the red glow of a sunset belongs to nature just as
fully as the molecules or radiation a scientist may describe?

You do not need the whole mature Whitehead system yet. You need the problem of
the bifurcation of nature.

## What This Chapter Is Doing

Stengers follows Whitehead's starting definition of nature as what we are aware
of in perception. She treats it as a mathematical commitment, not a vague
opening. Once accepted, it forbids a familiar split: experienced qualities on
one side, supposedly real scientific nature on the other.

The chapter's work is to show that this starting point is not naive. It is a
constraint. The concept of nature must include everything perception gives
without letting scientific abstraction claim to be the only real nature.

## Argument Path

1. Whitehead starts with nature as what is given in perception.
2. Stengers slows down the word "awareness" so it does not collapse into
   reflective consciousness.
3. The apparently simple example of seeing a grey stone becomes complicated
   once the situation of saying it is considered.
4. Whitehead's empiricism is not a ban on theory; it is a demand to include all
   that perception commits us to.
5. Scientific entities belong to nature, but they do not explain away the
   experienced world as a mere appearance.
6. The red of a sunset and the scientific description must be in the same
   conceptual boat.
7. The target is not science but a theory that divides nature into two orders.
8. Whitehead's concept of nature becomes a constructive resistance to this
   bifurcation.
9. The chapter closes by preparing the shift from the "what" of awareness to the
   "how" of awareness, where later problems will push beyond this concept.

## Key Terms

- awareness
- perception
- nature
- bifurcation of nature
- primary and secondary qualities
- scientific explanation

## Watch For

The chapter is easy to flatten into "experience is real too." That is too weak.
The stronger point is methodological: if you accept Whitehead's starting point,
then every later concept must be constructed so that it cannot quietly re-create
the split between experienced nature and real nature.

## Common Misreadings

- Thinking Whitehead is denying scientific explanation.
- Thinking Stengers is defending immediate experience against abstraction.
- Treating the sunset example as merely aesthetic.
- Treating bifurcation as a topic rather than as a problem-generating machine.

## After Reading

Ask:

- What does the initial definition of nature forbid?
- Why does Stengers call Whitehead's move mathematical?
- What kind of abstraction is legitimate, and what kind becomes abusive?
- How does this chapter prepare the need for events, objects, and ingression?
