# 7. Interlude: A Pragmatics of Concepts

## Before Reading

This chapter is a checkpoint. It asks what the early concept of nature has
achieved, what it excludes, and where its limits are.

Have these terms nearby:

- concept of nature
- scientific object
- ingression
- cogredience
- bifurcation of nature
- metaphysics

## What This Chapter Is Doing

Stengers explains that Whitehead's concept of nature is not a theory of
everything. It has limits. Those limits are not defects. They define what problem
the concept can answer and which questions would be false or premature within
that frame.

The chapter introduces a pragmatics of concepts: judge a concept by what it
allows, blocks, demands, and risks. The concept of nature is successful if it
gives knowledge a foothold without letting any particular kind of knowledge
claim final authority over nature.

## Argument Path

1. Whitehead warned that the hardest part of the concept of nature is its limit.
2. Some tempting questions, especially about the emergence of consciousness from
   neural mechanisms, are treated as badly framed at this stage.
3. Scientific objects belong to nature, but they presuppose knowledge rather
   than explaining knowledge away.
4. Whitehead is close to phenomenology in some concerns, but Stengers insists he
   is not doing phenomenological reduction.
5. "Ultimate" means ultimate relative to a problem, not ultimate in every
   philosophical sense.
6. Nature is neither fully knowable as a closed system nor unknowable as mute
   reality behind categories.
7. The relevant world is one that demands attention and can be learned from.
8. Experimental science becomes one successful mode of due attention, not the
   general definition of knowledge.
9. Ingression and cogredience are tested as terms that may or may not carry the
   concept of nature far enough.
10. The chapter prepares the later move to metaphysics by showing that the early
    problem has been pushed to its edge.

## Watch For

This chapter is especially useful for the whole book because it teaches how
Stengers reads concepts. A concept is not just a definition. It is a practice of
selection, attention, exclusion, and risk.

## Common Misreadings

- Treating "pragmatics" as casual usefulness.
- Thinking the concept of nature fails because it does not answer metaphysical
  questions.
- Treating science as downgraded because it is only one mode of attention.
- Treating "ultimate" as a single hierarchy instead of problem-relative.

## After Reading

Ask:

- Which questions does the concept of nature forbid, and why?
- What does "due attention" add to the reading of science?
- Why does Stengers distinguish Whitehead from phenomenology here?
- How does this chapter explain the transition toward metaphysics?
