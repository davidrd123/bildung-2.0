## Campanella Pressure

Leaf 0829 names `Ascl. 4` directly:

> Respondet in Ascl. 4. quod Deus creavit sensibilem mundum bonum, omnibus gradibus bonitatis plenissimum: idcircoque amavit. Proptereaque voluit esse etiam, qui mundum a se [factum?][?] miraretur, et celebraret diligenter. Cumque non posset mens esse diligens rerum corporearum mundanarum, tegumento corporeo mundano texit eam tanquam domo.

Campanella is not using this as a loose human-dignity commonplace. He is using it to explain why mind is placed into a bodily world: the human being is the caretaker, contemplator, and celebrant of the sensible world.

## Working Takeaway

Campanella's `Ascl. 4` sentence is a close paraphrase of the Asclepius anthropology:

- sensible world as second divine object of love
- human being made to behold, praise, and care for that world
- mind enclosed in corporeal/worldly covering so it can handle corporeal things
- human nature doubled: eternal/mortal, contemplative/governing
- arts and disciplines complete the world by preserving and ordering its terrestrial part

This directly grounds leaf 0829's move from divine governance to human arts without needing later Renaissance comparison yet.
