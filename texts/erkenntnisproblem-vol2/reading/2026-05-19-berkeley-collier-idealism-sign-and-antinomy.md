# Berkeley/Collier: Idealism, Sign, and Antinomy

Status: bounded chapter note for Book VI, chapter 2. Keep chapter-local until Hume supplies contrast evidence.

## Chapter Finding

Cassirer's Berkeley chapter tests the Locke close by removing the unknowable corporeal thing. The result is not a simple victory for empiricism. Berkeley shows that objectivity cannot begin from an external matter behind perception; distance, thinghood, nature-law, spirit, and truth all have to be reconstructed within consciousness. But his reconstruction finally secures experience by binding it to divine intelligence, while Collier secures idealism by showing that absolute external matter collapses into antinomy.

The chapter's center is the passage from suggested perception to metaphysical idealism. Berkeley first derives externality from perception, habit, and suggestion; then he generalizes perception into law-governed appearance-reality; then Cassirer shows the weakness of this grounding, because association cannot supply concept-function, mathematical exactness, empirical identity, or necessary order. Berkeley's late turn to signs, Plato, and reason is real, but it remains theological and metaphysical rather than critical: the sensible world becomes a divine text, not an a priori lawful field of experience.

Collier makes the negative side sharper. Absolute matter and absolute space are not merely unproved hypotheses. They are self-abolishing concepts: if treated as realities independent of consciousness, they generate the antinomies of extension, motion, space, and divine self-sufficiency. But Collier also shows the limit of this idealism. It can expose contradictions in the common world-concept without explaining positively how the sensible world is possible from the new standpoint.

## Evidence Path

- Part 052: Berkeley begins where Locke left a remainder. The external object is not a presupposed thing but the result of perception, judgment, association, habit, and suggestion.
- Part 053: The vision theory becomes full idealism. `Esse = percipi` means possible perception within a lawful empirical context, not momentary sensation, and nature-law becomes the order of appearances rather than the action of absolute matter.
- Part 054: Cassirer tests Berkeley immanently. Association and representation can explain how contents call one another up, but they cannot ground concept-function, mathematical continuity, empirical identity, motion-law, or objective necessity.
- Part 055: The passive idea-schema breaks under the pressure of spirit, self, notion, will, and causality. Berkeley recovers active mind only by moving beyond the beginning of the system.
- Part 056: Berkeley's final revision transforms experience into divine sign-language. Signs are no longer arbitrary names or sensory images; they fix objective relations for consciousness. But mathematics and mathematical physics remain assigned to a lower sphere, which blocks a critical account of scientific experience.
- Part 057: Collier supplies the parallel proof-form. Quasi-external perception is grounded in the function of seeing, while absolute external matter and absolute space are rejected through proof-burden, antinomy, and the space/God problem. The chapter closes with a negative critique, not a positive construction.

## Boundary

This is not just the Locke problem solved. Locke displaced empirical objectivity into unknowable things; Berkeley and Collier remove those things, but the removal exposes a deeper question: what grounds the lawful connection of appearances once transcendent matter has been denied?

This is not the Leibniz answer repeated. Leibniz's symbol and function theory grounded appearances through relation-preserving expression and harmony. Berkeley uses sign-language, but its final ground is divine will and intelligence; Collier uses antinomy, but does not reconstruct the sensible world positively.

This is not yet Kant's critical idealism. Cassirer explicitly marks the difference: Berkeley's "transcendental" science builds a higher realm above experience, while Kant asks after the conditions that make experience itself law-governed and truth-capable.

## Next Test

Hume should test whether experience can retain order once Berkeley's and Collier's supports are removed. If the uniformity of nature, causal connection, and object-formation can be explained from experience alone, then the idealist critique of matter becomes a stage in empiricism's purification. If not, the Book VI problem becomes sharper: empiricism can dissolve transcendent matter, but then has to explain the lawful connection that made experience readable in the first place.
