# Gassendi: Experience As The Ground-Riddle

This note is bounded to the Gassendi chapter, `parts/007-011`. It should not yet be generalized to Hobbes or later empiricism.

## Local Finding

Cassirer's Gassendi chapter turns empiricism against any simple appeal to sense. Gassendi begins from sense and experience, but his own science and psychology require non-sensory functions: mediated signs, scientific reason, pure space and time, atomistic decomposition, conceptual criteria, and self-consciousness. By the chapter close, experience is no longer the solution to the problem of knowledge. It is the problem to be explained.

## Evidence Trail

- Part 007: perception is not direct intake of the object but a mediated motion-sign from which mind reconstructs the acting cause.
- Part 008: sense is unerring only as report of a real effect; atomism already requires concepts of scientific reason against naive sensory intuition.
- Part 009: pure space and pure time have objective validity without acting on sensory organs, so Gassendi's sensory criterion cannot ground them.
- Part 010: Gassendi's physics consciously changes perceptual data by conceptual criteria, decomposing continuous body and motion into discrete elements.
- Part 011: Gassendi cannot explain the mind's power to form, expand, compare, name, and unify sensory data. Experience becomes the passage from an absolute outside object into an independent `Ich`.

## Chapter Boundary

The chapter's final contrast is not generic empiricism versus rationalism. Cassirer says the "rationalist" Descartes represents pure analysis of consciousness, while Gassendi measures inner experience by a fixed metaphysical standard. The standard label hides the actual opposition.

## Re-entry

At the Hobbes opening, test what changes. If Hobbes turns the same object-sensation-thought relation into a mechanical or logical construction, this note remains Gassendi-local but becomes useful contrast evidence. If Hobbes repeats the same unresolved experience-riddle, then the pattern may become broader than the Gassendi chapter.
