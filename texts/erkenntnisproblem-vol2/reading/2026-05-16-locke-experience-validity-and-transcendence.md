# Locke: Experience, Validity, and Metaphysical Transcendence

Status: bounded chapter note for Book VI, chapter 1. Keep chapter-local until Berkeley supplies contrast evidence.

## Chapter Finding

Cassirer's Locke chapter does not reduce Locke to flat sensualism. Locke's philosophical importance lies in the way his genetic psychology becomes a critique of experience. By asking how knowledge is acquired, Locke repeatedly discovers that experience contains functions and standards that sensation and reflection cannot themselves justify.

The chapter's center is the unresolved passage from experience to objective validity. Locke excludes innate ideas and speculative soul doctrine, begins from sensation and reflection, then finds that infinity, space, time, number, truth, intuition, substance, and existence all require more than passive sources. But because he does not reconnect empirical knowledge with rational form, he displaces the objectivity of experience into unknowable things, substances, and archetypes beyond cognition.

The final diagnosis is precise: Locke fuses two problems that must be separated. The objective certainty of empirical knowledge is not the same problem as the metaphysical transcendence of a corporeal world existing in itself.

## Evidence Path

- Part 046: Locke's originality is separation. The question of the objective value and limits of knowledge is detached from metaphysical and natural-scientific theories of the soul, and the critique of innate ideas clears the field for genetic analysis.
- Part 047: Experience splits into sensation and reflection. Reflection initially mirrors inner acts just as sensation mirrors outer objects, which fixes processes and relations as element-like ideas.
- Part 048: Infinity breaks passive reflection. The infinite is not an observed content but a rule-governed consciousness of possible continuation; Locke's source-pair now needs logical necessity.
- Part 049: Space, time, number, and simple ideas repeat the pressure. Locke admits formative and criterion-like functions, yet closes the section by treating simple ideas as immediate warrants for external things.
- Part 050: The concept of truth shifts the center to universal relations among ideas. Intuition grounds certainty in mathematics and morals, while natural science remains probable experience. Locke is an empiricist from resignation, not from Baconian confidence.
- Part 051: The concept of being completes the pattern. Substance is exposed as an empty bearer and an `idolon fori`, but Locke still needs it as condition of objecthood and as the ideal object of perfect knowledge from causes. Existence begins as a logical relation, then becomes an impossible comparison between ideas and unknowable archetypes.

## Boundary

This is not the Hobbes pattern repeated. Hobbes's self-created principles become absolute powers. Locke's problem is different: psychological source-analysis uncovers logical functions but lacks the critical apparatus to ground their objective use.

This is not the Leibniz answer inverted. Leibniz makes function, symbol, and harmony into the route to objectivity. Locke sees the gap between empirical fact and rational validity, but leaves the bridge unbuilt.

Do not turn this into a full Book VI thesis yet. The chapter closes by pointing directly to Locke's successors: removing the transcendent thing may clarify the empirical method, but it may also unsettle the logical grounds of experience itself.

## Next Test

Berkeley should test the Locke diagnosis directly. If Berkeley can remove the unknowable corporeal thing while preserving objective empirical order, then the Locke remainder becomes a solvable metaphysical obstruction. If Berkeley's removal of the thing also endangers the logical objectivity of experience, then Cassirer's warning at the Locke close becomes the governing problem of the next chapter.
