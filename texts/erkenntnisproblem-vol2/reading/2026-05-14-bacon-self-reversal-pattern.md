# Bacon Self-Reversal Pattern

This note is bounded to Book IV, chapter 1, `Bacon`. It records a local Cassirer move that has now recurred strongly enough to be findable outside the part files: Cassirer repeatedly judges Bacon by Baconian standards.

## Evidence

- Part 003: Bacon's doctrine of forms becomes a `negative Instanz` for the history of the problem of knowledge. Cassirer uses Bacon's own methodological category to show where Bacon's method fails.
- Part 004: Bacon's form-concepts become an `idola fori` problem. The physics falls into the same language-error that Bacon's theory of knowledge criticizes.
- Part 005: Bacon's `philosophia prima` repeats the universal/particular failure that it should have solved. The attempted universal science collapses into rhetorical commonplaces because thing-like qualities have already occupied the place of the universal.
- Part 006: Bacon's own distinction between `fruchtbringende` and `lichtbringende` experiments is turned against Bacon. He reaches for the fruits of experience before the principles of knowledge are won.

## Local Finding

The pattern is not merely that Bacon is inconsistent. Cassirer treats Bacon's contradictions as historically diagnostic: Bacon can formulate the new task of empirical science, but the conceptual means he inherits pull each formulation back into the old substantial and anthropomorphic worldview.

This is why the chapter can praise Bacon's pathos and still end by denying that Bacon reaches exact natural science. The standards that expose the failure are internal to Bacon's own announced program.

The chapter close strengthens the pattern rhetorically as well as conceptually. Cassirer does not merely state that Bacon preferred use to theory; he closes with Bacon's own Atalanta image and turns it against Bacon's doctrine. Bacon's methodological and metaphorical instruments become the means by which the chapter judges him.

## Boundary

Do not yet generalize this into Cassirer's method for the whole volume. The pattern is earned for Bacon because four consecutive tranches show Bacon's terms, distinctions, or ideals being used against Bacon's execution.

The next test is Gassendi and Hobbes. If Cassirer reads them by their own internal methodological standards as explicitly as he reads Bacon, this note can become evidence for a broader empiricism-chapter pattern. If not, it remains a Bacon-chapter trait.

## Weakening Evidence

- Gassendi or Hobbes may be treated mainly by external comparison to Cartesian or mathematical science rather than by self-reversal.
- Later Bacon references may return only as historical contrast without the internal turning of Baconian standards against Baconian doctrine.
- The pattern may be specific to Bacon because his rhetoric is unusually rich in methodological oppositions: idols/ideas, light/fruit, method/forms, dissection/abstraction.
