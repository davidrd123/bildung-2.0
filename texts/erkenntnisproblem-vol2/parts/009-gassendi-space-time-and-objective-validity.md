# Part 009: Gassendi, Space, Time, and Objective Validity

Cassirer turns from the atom concept to its necessary correlate: empty space. Gassendi can formulate the modern physical concept of pure space and pure time with unusual clarity. They are not bodies, not properties of bodies, and not fictions dependent on the understanding; their objectivity lies in the necessity with which they must be thought. But this is exactly what his sensualist epistemology cannot justify. If only what acts on the senses can be known, pure space and pure time should be excluded; if they are admitted, objective validity must extend to concepts that organize experience as a necessary connected whole without themselves being single sensory realities.

```text
Schärfer noch tritt dieser Widerstreit an dem notwendigen Korrelat des Atombegriffs: am Begriff des leeren Raumes hervor. Gassendi hat — wiederum im Anschluß an die italienische Naturphilosophie1) — den modernen physikalischen Begriff des Raumes, der später insbesondere von Newton gebraucht und zum Fundament des eigenen Systems gemacht wurde, in voller Bestimmtheit erfaßt und dargestellt.2) Der reine Raum und die reine Zeit sind, wie er ausführt, wahre Wesenheiten, die, unabhängig von der Existenz denkender Subjekte, an und für sich bestehen; aber sie sind nichtsdestoweniger keine Substanzen, in dem gewöhnlichen Sinne des Wortes. Vielmehr handelt es sich in beiden um eine eigene Form der Wirklichkeit, die schlechthin anzuerkennen ist, wenngleich sie sich nicht unter die üblichen ontologischen Gegensätze, wie unter den Gegensatz von „Substanz“ und „Accidens“, fassen und einreihen läßt. Selbst wenn wir uns alle Dinge aufgehoben dächten, bliebe doch der konstante Raum und der regelmäßige Ablauf der Zeit zurück, die beide somit weder selbst Körper sind, noch den Körpern als abhängige Beschaffenheiten anhaften. Das „Wo“ und „Wann“ bildet eine ursprüngliche selbständige Bestimmung; es bezeichnet einen neuen Gesichtspunkt, der zu den Begriffen des „Dinges“ und seiner „Eigenschaften“, aus denen er in keiner Weise ableitbar ist, hinzutreten muß. Die Objektivität, die dem Räume und der Zeit eignet, liegt in der unbedingten Notwendigkeit, mit der beide gedacht werden müssen: sie bietet dagegen keine Analogie zu derjenigen Form der Wirklichkeit, die den physischen Dingen und den Eigenschaften dieser Dinge zukommt.3) Nur das Peripatetische Vorurteil, daß die Zweiteilung in „Substanzen“ und „Accidentien“ das Sein völlig erschöpfe und ausmesse, schafft hier eine logische Schwierigkeit, die vor der schärferen kritischen Analyse des Inhalts des Raum- und Zeitbegriffs indessen sogleich schwindet. Man erkennt alsdann, daß der reine Raum und die reine Zeit, die den Körpern als unabhängige Bedingungen vorangehen, zwar ein „Nichts“ sein mögen, wenn man an sie den gewohnten Maßstab der körperlich-dinglichen Existenz anlegt: daß sie dagegen nichtsdestoweniger echte und wahrhafte Wesenheiten sind, sofern ohne ihre Annahme das Verständnis der Bewegungserscheinungen und der Aufbau der wissenschaftlichen Physik nicht zu gewinnen ist.4)

Man sieht, daß hier ein wichtiges erkenntniskritisches Problem gestellt ist, das uns fortan durch die gesamte Geschichte der neueren Philosophie begleiten wird: die Prinzipien der mathematischen Naturerkenntnis sprengen das feste Schema der Aristotelischen Kategorienlehre. Aber gerade an dieser Stelle macht sich freilich ein Mangel der logischen Begründung fühlbar; denn Gassendis eigene erkenntnistheoretische Anschauung reicht nicht aus, die neue Wendung zu erklären und zu rechtfertigen. Wenn der Sinn der einzige Zeuge der Realität ist, so bliebe in der Tat die Folgerung, die Gassendi bekämpft, zu Recht bestehen: so könnte es für uns kein anderes Sein, als dasjenige von körperlichen Objekten und ihren Eigenschaften geben. Denn sie allein üben einen bestimmten physischen Einfluß auf die Organe der Sinneswahrnehmung aus, während dem reinen Raum und der reinen Zeit, wie Gassendi ausdrücklich betont, jede Form der Wirksamkeit fern bleibt. Nur das Wirkende aber kann, nach den Voraussetzungen von Gassendis Erkenntnislehre, gewußt und erkannt werden. Wenn dennoch den Begriffen des Raumes und der Zeit eine gleichberechtigte Stellung im System der Erkenntnis gesichert werden soll, so wäre hierzu eine Umbildung und Erweiterung des ursprünglichen Planes erforderlich. Objektive Gültigkeit wäre nicht nur dem Inhalt der Wahrnehmung selbst zuzusprechen, sondern sie käme auch allen denjenigen Begriffen zu, die, ohne selbst auf eine einzelne sinnliche Wirklichkeit zu gehen, dennoch unentbehrlich sind, um die Erfahrung als notwendig verknüpftes Ganze zu denken. In der Tat hat Gassendi, wie Epikur selbst, gelegentlich eine derartige Erweiterung versucht, aber freilich mit ihr zugleich das eigentliche Prinzip seines Sensualismus, wie er es an die Spitze seiner Philosophie stellt und wie er es gegen Descartes zu verteidigen sucht, bereits verlassen. Die Konsequenz dieser Ansicht drängt mit Notwendigkeit zu einem anderen Typus der Begründung hin, in welchem das Wissen von den reinen Relationen von der Auffassung sinnlicher Gegebenheiten streng und methodisch geschieden ist. Diese Begründung hat Gassendi nicht erreicht: aber er hat, indem er die Eigenart des Raum- und Zeitproblems scharf heraushebt, wenigstens im allgemeinen auf ihre Notwendigkeit vorausgewiesen.
```

**Draft**

```text
This conflict emerges still more sharply in the necessary correlate of the atom concept: in the concept of empty space. Gassendi has, again in connection with Italian natural philosophy,1) grasped and presented in full determinacy the modern physical concept of space, which Newton later in particular used and made the foundation of his own system.2) Pure space and pure time, as he explains, are true entities that exist in and for themselves, independently of the existence of thinking subjects; but they are nevertheless not substances in the ordinary sense of the word. Rather, in both cases what is involved is a distinct form of reality that must simply be acknowledged, although it cannot be grasped and classified under the usual ontological oppositions, such as the opposition between "substance" and "accident." Even if we thought all things abolished, constant space and the regular course of time would still remain; thus neither of them is itself a body, nor do they adhere to bodies as dependent properties. The "where" and "when" form an original, independent determination; they designate a new point of view that must be added to the concepts of the "thing" and its "properties," from which it is in no way derivable. The objectivity that belongs to space and time lies in the unconditional necessity with which both must be thought; by contrast, it offers no analogy to that form of reality that belongs to physical things and to the properties of these things.3) Only the Peripatetic prejudice that the twofold division into "substances" and "accidents" completely exhausts and measures being creates a logical difficulty here, which at once vanishes before the sharper critical analysis of the content of the concept of space and time. One then recognizes that pure space and pure time, which precede bodies as independent conditions, may indeed be a "nothing" if one applies to them the customary measure of bodily-thingly existence; nevertheless they are genuine and true entities insofar as, without assuming them, the understanding of the phenomena of motion and the construction of scientific physics cannot be won.4)

One sees that an important problem in the critique of knowledge is posed here, one that will accompany us from now on through the entire history of modern philosophy: the principles of mathematical knowledge of nature burst apart the fixed schema of Aristotelian category doctrine. But precisely at this point a lack of logical grounding is, to be sure, felt; for Gassendi's own epistemological view is not sufficient to explain and justify the new turn. If sense is the sole witness of reality, then the consequence Gassendi opposes would in fact remain justified: for us there could be no other being than that of bodily objects and their properties. For these alone exercise a determinate physical influence on the organs of sense-perception, while pure space and pure time, as Gassendi expressly emphasizes, remain remote from every form of efficacy. But according to the presuppositions of Gassendi's theory of knowledge, only what acts can be known and cognized. If, nevertheless, the concepts of space and time are to be secured an equal standing in the system of knowledge, then a transformation and expansion of the original plan would be required. Objective validity would have to be ascribed not only to the content of perception itself; it would also belong to all those concepts that, without themselves going back to a single sensory reality, are nevertheless indispensable for thinking experience as a necessarily connected whole. In fact Gassendi, like Epicurus himself, occasionally attempted such an expansion, but with it he had already abandoned the proper principle of his sensationalism, as he places it at the head of his philosophy and seeks to defend it against Descartes. The consequence of this view presses with necessity toward another type of grounding, in which knowledge of pure relations is strictly and methodically distinguished from the apprehension of sensory givens. Gassendi did not reach this grounding; but by sharply bringing out the distinctive character of the space and time problem, he at least pointed ahead in general to its necessity.
```

```text
1) Gassendi also stood in close personal relations with representatives of Renaissance natural philosophy: as he reports in his life of Peiresc, he met Campanella at Peiresc's home in Aix. The influence of Campanella's writings appears repeatedly in Gassendi's works, especially in the treatment of the problem of space and time. Compare vol. I, pp. 257 ff.

2) Cassirer cites the question whether the agreement between Newton's doctrine of space and Locke's doctrine of space can be explained by direct historical dependence. He quotes Riehl: "It is of interest to know whether this surprising agreement means more than a borrowing from Newton's principles. The certainty of scientific results is increased when they are obtained independently and by entirely different routes. Newton's work did appear for the first time in 1687, two years before the printing of the Essay was completed; but the Essay itself was already complete at that time, and the extract from it published in 1688 contains everything essential in Locke's doctrine of space and time." Cassirer adds that the connection is most simply explained through mediation by Gassendi, whose doctrine of space and time Newton and Locke alike draw on.

3) `Syntagma Philosophicum`, P. II, Sect. I, Lib. II, Cap. I (Opera I, 162): "Furthermore, since it seems to us that even if there were no bodies, nevertheless constant place and passing time would remain, place and time therefore seem not to depend on bodies, and accordingly not to be corporeal accidents. Nor, however, are they for that reason incorporeal accidents, as if they adhered after the manner of accidents to some incorporeal substance; rather, they are certain incorporeal things of a kind different from those that are usually called substances or accidents. Hence it also follows that being taken in the most general sense is not adequately divided into substance and accident, but place and time must be added to the division as two additional members. ... From this it follows that place and time must be held to be true things or real beings; although they are not something of the sort commonly held to be either substance or accident, nevertheless they really are, and they do not depend on the understanding as chimeras do, since, whether the understanding thinks or does not think, place remains and time runs on." Compare especially vol. I, pp. 260 ff.

4) Ibid., Opera I, 164: "Clearly one falls into these rough places because of that preconception with which the Peripatetic school has imbued us: that every being or every thing is either substance or accident, and whatever is not substance or accident is non-being, no thing, or nothing. But since from what has been deduced, besides substance and accident, place, or space, and time, or duration, are also true beings or true things, it is clear that if either of these is anything, it is Nothing in the Peripatetic sense, but not in the genuine sense. That is, these two are kinds of things wholly distinct from those, and place and time can be substance or accident just as little as substance and accident can be place or time."
```

**Notes**

- What this tranche clarified: the space/time problem is stronger than the atom problem because it forces Gassendi to recognize objective realities that do not act on the senses. Pure space and pure time are not bodily things, properties, substances, or accidents, but they are still necessary for physics.
