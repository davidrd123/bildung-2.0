# Part 18: On the Critics of Tektology (5.4)

`5.4` is the polemical close of volume 1, but it is not merely an appendix of grievances. Bogdanov uses his critics to restate, in adversarial form, what the whole book has been trying to establish: tektology is descriptive rather than exhortative, a universal organizational science requires a compact technical language, and philosophical supplements about `matter` or `spirit` are external to the problems the science is actually trying to solve.

## Why This Batch

- `5.4 ¶1-6` answers V. I. Nevsky on `bare schemata`, new terms, and the charge of `metaphysics`.
- `5.4 ¶7-16` answers the accusation of `idealism` and clarifies again why `complexes`, `elements`, and `activities-resistances` do not commit tektology to a philosophical doctrine.
- `5.4 ¶17-24` answers P. M. Kerzhentsev's reading of the `law of leasts` as a reactionary norm and makes explicit the difference between diagnosing a bottleneck and recommending submission to it.
- `5.4 ¶25-34` treats I. Weinstein's article as mostly unintelligible, but still extracts the real question criticism ought to face: is a universal organizational science possible, and if so, how well are its foundations being laid?

Up to now `Tektology` has called forth very few critical responses. I shall not speak now of two or three more or less objective reviews. Properly polemical literature against it over these eleven years, so far as I know, fits into ten pages if not fewer. The point, however, is not quantity:

one can say much in few words. Therefore I must respond to the examples of this literature known to me.

First, a part of V. I. Nevsky's article `Dialectical Materialism and the Philosophy of Dead Reaction` (in the appendix to the book `Materialism and Empirio-Criticism`, 1920) is devoted to tektology. Three main accusations are advanced there. Here is the first: `What the laws of ingression consist in is probably known only to A. Bogdanov; but from the two parts of his Tektology the reader can fish out nothing except abstract, bare schemata that say nothing` (p. 379).

Should I answer? I think a reader who has even glanced through this book can already judge how flattering V. I. Nevsky's assertion is to him, the reader, that he is incapable of `fishing out` anything except `bare` schemata. Whether the results of my investigation are true or false, the analysis of ways of solving a multitude of problems, practical and cognitive, some of them moreover previously unsolved, may perhaps seem to the reader less `bare` than V. I. Nevsky's assertion.

Further: `Besides these schemata, both books contain a great many new terms, confusing an already obscure exposition of a metaphysical system. A. Bogdanov, who likes to protest against the barbarous terminology of bourgeois science, himself heaps up dozens of new terms. What names he does not have, and from where are they all taken: copulation, conjugation (terms taken from biology), ingression, egression, degression and deingression, system differentiation, and every kind of combination of all these symbols, complexes, and elements` (pp. 379-380).

`A metaphysical system`! In ordinary philosophical usage this means a system operating beyond experience, outside possible verification. In the Hegelian-dialectical usage, it means a system alien to the idea of development, proceeding from the fixed, the unchanging, the absolute. The reader himself can judge the... coolness, or perhaps unfamiliarity with terminology, required to call by that name a work wholly devoted to methods of solving practical and scientific-theoretical tasks.

`A great many terms`... Seven are cited, and that is about half of all those I have in fact introduced. What then, does V. I. Nevsky suppose it would have been possible to create a new science of all-embracing scope, a general methodology of every practice and every theory, without resorting to new terms? How else, then, were one to express new concepts, concepts so new that V. I. Nevsky himself could not comprehend them or `fish out` anything from them? Any special science counts its special terms by the hundreds and thousands. I do not doubt that with the development of tektology many more new terms will be worked out in it. But in return its methods will make it possible to discard thousands of old terms from the different sciences, because its task is to establish what is common beneath the diversity of `special` shells.

Третье главное обвинение - это «идеализм».

Чем оно обосновывается? Тем, что - страшно сказать - в «Тектологии» дело идет о «различных комплексах, составленных из разного рода элементов» (стр. 378, ссылка на «Тектологию»), а у Маха тоже «комплексы» и «элементы»! А Мах по всем декретам - идеалист! Надо ли еще доказывать?

Увы, до сих пор нет и не было такой науки, которая изучаемые явления не разлагала бы на те или иные элементы и не переходила бы затем к сочетаниям этих элементов, т. е. к их комплексам. У химии элементы одни, скажем, атомы простых тел, у биологии - другие, скажем клетки, у социологии - третьи - человеческие существа и т. д.; но все науки свои простейшие объекты называют «элементами»; их сочетания носят много разных названий; в химии - соединения, в биологии - организмы, колонии, виды и проч., в социологии - группы, классы и т. д. Я употребил общий и нейтральный, понятный всякому европейцу термин «комплексы». И Мах тут ни при чем: у него дело идет о специальном разложении опыта на его чувственные элементы и о комплексах, соотносительных этим специальным элементам. Если В. И. Невский знает такую науку, у которой нет своих «элементов» и их «комплексов», пусть он ее назовет, это в высшей степени интересно.

В. И. Невский цитирует из моей статьи в «Социализме науки» положение: «Организующая деятельность всегда направлена к образованию каких-нибудь систем из каких-нибудь частей и элементов» и затем замечает: «Как видим, без этих знаменитых богдановских элементов шагу сделать нельзя» (стр. 381). Я думал, что это только констатация непреложного факта. Но, очевидно, В. И. Невскому известна и такая организующая деятельность, которая обходится без всяких элементов. Это опять-таки страшно интересно, и такое открытие В. И. Невский не имеет права держать под спудом, он должен опубликовать его со всей поспешностью!

Но есть еще другая улика «идеализма».

Элементами, которые организуются, служат, как я указываю, всегда активности-сопротивления. И при этом я не объясняю, что является их «основой» - материя или дух. Дело идет здесь, надо заметить, не о той материи, которую изучают физика и химия и которая есть только явление: дело идет о материи как субстрате явлений, о материи как «вещи в себе». И если в «Тектологии», говоря об элементах-активностях, я не говорю о ней, то ясно, что я под ними подразумеваю «дух» и только скрываю это, чтобы уклониться от ответственности.

Я говорю в своей книге о материи-явлении, той, которую раньше представляли состоящей из твердых кусочков - атомов, а теперь физика разлагает на электрические заряды и их силовые поля, и не говорю о материи - вещи в себе, а также о духе.

Мой читатель, я думаю, уже успел понять, почему это так. Просто для них тут не было и нет места. Наука ограничена научными рамками; а «вещи в себе» - понятия философские. Прав ли я, полагая, что тектология между прочим ликвидирует философию вообще, - это вопрос другой; но ясно, что если тектология есть наука, то она обязана изучать только явления, их связь и закономерность, а прочее предоставить философии. Она даже не опровергает этих философских понятий; но в решении ее задач их некуда поместить.

И я не понимаю, кто или что мешает В. И. Невскому или всякому желающему сказать: «А за элементами-активностями скрывается вещь в себе, которая есть материя и которая, действуя на наши органы чувств, вызывает ощущения, относящиеся к этим активностям», - все, как полагается. Если это для вас что-нибудь прибавляет и вы находите, что так лучше, - пожалуйста. Но тектологии как учения об организационных закономерностях это просто не касается.

Такова критика В. И. Невского.

The third main accusation is `idealism`.

On what is it based? On the fact that, horrors of horrors, in `Tektology` there is talk of `different complexes composed of elements of different kinds` (p. 378, citing `Tektology`), while Mach too has `complexes` and `elements`! And Mach, by all decrees, is an idealist! Is any further proof needed?

Alas, there is not and never has been any science that does not decompose the phenomena it studies into one or another elements and then pass to combinations of those elements, that is, to their complexes. Chemistry has some elements, say atoms of simple bodies; biology has others, say cells; sociology has still others, human beings, and so on. All the sciences call their simplest objects `elements`; their combinations bear many different names: in chemistry, compounds; in biology, organisms, colonies, species, and so forth; in sociology, groups, classes, and so forth. I used the general and neutral term `complexes`, intelligible to every European. And Mach has nothing to do with it. With him the issue is the special decomposition of experience into its sensory elements and the complexes corresponding to those special elements. If V. I. Nevsky knows of a science that has no `elements` and no `complexes` of them, let him name it. That would be supremely interesting.

V. I. Nevsky quotes from my article in `Socialism of Science` the proposition `Organizing activity is always directed toward the formation of some systems out of some parts and elements`, and then remarks: `As we see, without these famous Bogdanovian elements one cannot take a step` (p. 381). I thought this was merely the statement of an inescapable fact. But evidently V. I. Nevsky is also acquainted with an organizing activity that gets along without any elements whatever. That again is tremendously interesting, and V. I. Nevsky has no right to keep such a discovery under a bushel; he must publish it with all haste!

But there is another piece of evidence of `idealism`.

The elements that are organized, as I indicate, are always activities-resistances. And at the same time I do not explain what their `basis` is, matter or spirit. Here, it must be noted, the issue is not that matter which physics and chemistry study and which is only a phenomenon. The issue is matter as the substrate of phenomena, matter as `thing in itself`. And if in `Tektology`, speaking of activity-elements, I say nothing about it, then clearly I must mean `spirit` by them and only conceal it in order to evade responsibility.

In my book I speak of matter-as-phenomenon, the matter once imagined as consisting of hard little pieces, atoms, and now decomposed by physics into electric charges and their force-fields, and I do not speak of matter as thing-in-itself, nor of spirit.

My reader, I think, has already had time to understand why. Quite simply, there was and is no place for them here. Science is limited by scientific bounds, while `things in themselves` are philosophical concepts. Whether I am right in holding that tektology among other things liquidates philosophy altogether is another question. But it is clear that if tektology is a science, then it is obliged to study only phenomena, their connection, and their regularity, and to leave the rest to philosophy. It does not even refute those philosophical concepts; but there is nowhere to put them in solving its problems.

And I do not understand who or what prevents V. I. Nevsky or anyone else from saying: `Behind the activity-elements there hides the thing in itself, which is matter and which, acting on our sense-organs, evokes sensations corresponding to these activities`, everything as it should be. If that adds anything for you and you find it better that way, please do. But tektology, as a doctrine of organizational regularities, is simply not concerned with it.

Such is V. I. Nevsky's criticism.

What then, is that a call to equalize all the factors of the harvest to the least? Fortunately no agronomist understood Liebig in that way, and his law remains one of the foundations of agronomy. Recently it has even received further development, pouring itself into the more exact formulas of Mitscherlich-Baule. If, for example, I indicate that a breakthrough of the front occurs at the sector of "least relative resistance" (least relative to the enemy forces opposed to it), does that mean an army is "called upon" to level its whole front to the weak point?

If I remind people that at times whole factories among us worked only half-time because of a shortage of lamp-glass, is that a "call" to maintain such a state of affairs?

In that very report at the First Conference on Scientific Organization of Labor to which P. M. Kerzhentsev refers, I raised the question of which branches of production should be considered "shock" branches, that is, should stand in the first rank in the work of restoration out of ruin, and I answered thus: the branches relatively most damaged, most lagging, inevitably hold back all the rest and therefore are precisely the ones that must receive the most energetic support. Is that a "call" to level all branches to the backward ones?`

Now I fear I am needlessly delaying the reader's attention. Is it really necessary to refute the understanding of the law of leasts and of the proverb "where it is thin, there it tears" displayed by P. M. Kerzhentsev? Would it not have been quite enough simply to note it?
```

The tactics following from the organizational principles of Bogdanov's theory are the working out of "a unity of cognitive methods that bursts the bounds of specialization, giving a whole, harmoniously ordered organization of social experience"... The tactics of the proletariat for Bogdanov consist in the working out of a "world methodology", which is to "embrace human and world activity"` (p. 92, passim).

Good Lord! Until now I thought I knew what the word `tactics` meant. Evidently I was mistaken. In accordance with all dictionaries I had supposed that tactics means the methods of current struggle: parliamentary tactics, economic-strike tactics, terrorist tactics, military-combat tactics, and so on. I thought that the working out of a world methodology might represent a program, a vast scientific-cultural program. It turns out that this is a tactic, competing with strike tactics, guerrilla tactics, and the like.

It is clear that the divergence between the esteemed author and myself is deeply principled. The reader knows from what principles I proceed. But the esteemed author in his criticism appears to proceed from the principle that popular wisdom clothed in the proverb: `There is elderberry in the garden, and an uncle in Kiev.` Principles are not disputed. I shall refrain as well.

Criticism of tektology is probably needed.

If there is to be such criticism, then before anything else it must resolve the question: is a universal organizational science possible? That is, is it possible scientifically to investigate and establish the regularity of organizational processes?

If not, then why not? Such a conclusion, deeply sad for humanity, since it deprives it of hope of scientifically organizing life as a whole, must be strictly and precisely grounded.

If yes, then of course one may put the question: how well is the laying of the foundations of this science being carried out by me, its initiator?

If badly, criticism must indicate how this work is to be done better.
