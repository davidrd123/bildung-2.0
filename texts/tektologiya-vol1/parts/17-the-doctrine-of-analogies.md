# Part 17: The Doctrine of Analogies (5.3)

`5.3` is a review-essay, but not a loose one. Bogdanov uses Mikhail Petrovich's work on analogies to draw a sharp methodological boundary. Bourgeois science can collect analogies, classify them, and even use them heuristically; but because it does not reach the structural-organizational basis of those analogies, it stops halfway. The section therefore functions as a clarification of what tektology is not, and why analogy alone cannot yet count as full science.

## Why This Batch

- `5.3 ¶2-10` sets the social and methodological frame: tektology versus a merely analogical way of thinking, then introduces Petrovich as the strongest contemporary representative of the latter.
- `5.3 ¶11-16` presents the descriptive machinery of Petrovich's approach: `descriptive elements`, `descriptive systems`, and the split between similarities of course and similarities of role.
- `5.3 ¶17-37` gives the typology of roles and Bogdanov's first criticism: the classifications are useful but unstable and preparatory.
- `5.3 ¶38-45` states the real scientific gain of analogy-research: from mechanisms to expected courses of phenomena, and from courses back to hypothetical mechanisms.
- `5.3 ¶46-63` shows the strongest examples and introduces the notion of the `analogical kernel` of a group of disparate phenomena.
- `5.3 ¶64-73` gives Bogdanov's decisive boundary: analogy becomes science only when it passes into explanatory, testable, practically usable knowledge of structural grounds.

Matters stand worse with political economy and the social sciences generally: there too the author shows a certain education, but only in a European-bourgeois sense; with scientific socialism he is plainly unfamiliar.

```text
As the point of departure of his exposition, the author takes William Thomson's well-known proposition: `To understand a phenomenon is to be in a position to establish its mechanical model.` He explains the aphorism as follows: the great physicist had in mind mainly physical phenomena; by a mechanical model he understood such a mechanical system as would be governed by the same mathematical laws as the phenomenon itself, or at least such a system as could explain the course of the phenomenon, its mechanism, by comparison with more familiar facts of equilibrium or motion.

Such exact quantitative comparison is far from always possible; on the contrary, it succeeds only in a minority of cases. Nevertheless, Petrovich argues, Thomson's idea can with proper interpretation be extended to indefinitely broad circles of phenomena. `There exist`, he says, `ways of arriving at an understanding of phenomena apart from the unconditional necessity of knowing their exact mathematical laws.`

First of all one may establish that `among the infinite variety of natural processes there occur phenomena of different orders that present striking similarities to one another.` Usually this provides material for metaphors, used not only in ordinary language but also in special science. But among the countless similarities of this kind, many are far from so superficial and accidental as to have merely conversational, literary, or subjective significance. Many of them `disclose a real analogy, the existence of common features in the course of diverse phenomena`.

Petrovich groups these common features into two classes: on the one hand, similarities in the very course of disparate processes (`ressemblances d'allure`), on the other, similarities in the role played by the most diverse factors in those processes (`ressemblances des roles`). The systematization and generalization of the first will yield definite `types of the course of phenomena`; of the second, `types of roles`.
```

```text
The author begins with the `schematic description` of phenomena. Those sides, traits, and features of a phenomenon that change in its course he designates as its `descriptive elements` (`elements descriptifs`), and their totality as a `descriptive system`. Each descriptive element is expressed by a definite parameter varying correspondingly, and in this way the whole course of the phenomenon is represented.

Of course, far from all descriptive elements admit of exact measurement or can be expressed strictly quantitatively. But even then, in Petrovich's view, their changes still admit of `qualitative` description. By this somewhat imprecise term he means comparative, indefinitely quantitative characteristics: a feeling grows stronger or weaker; the tempo of the development of a society is greater or smaller; the course of a disease is more or less acute; one event is more significant than another; sensitivity to light is higher in one organism than in another; sensation grows more slowly than the stimulus that evokes it; and so forth. In this way one may `qualitatively` describe the course of changes of any element, and approximately determine even the form of the corresponding curve.

Suppose that for a large number of phenomena the course of changes both of the individual descriptive elements (`images individuelles`) and of their totality for each phenomenon as a whole (`images collectives`) has been sufficiently clarified quantitatively or `qualitatively`. Then, by comparing these `images`, we may find common features in several or many, sometimes quite heterogeneous, phenomena, that is, `ressemblances d'allure`. These will be the analogies serving as the basis for a doctrine of common mechanisms.

Petrovich also attempts to embody similarities of role in a general classification. The chief types he sketches are such as the role of a descriptive element, the role of cause, the role of linkage, the role of obstacle, the role of `terrain`, and more particular subdivisions such as `impulsive` causes, `depressive` causes, counteracting causes, reactive causes, provocatory agents, regulating agents, coordinative agents, and inertia in its different forms.

The types of `linkage` too are further classified: stable, deforming, `chain` (`par enchainement`), one-sided, and so forth.

The weakness of these classifications is unmistakable: instability and indeterminacy of the principles of division, arbitrariness of some groupings, interweaving of others, indistinguishability of still others. But the author himself offers the classification more as approximate and illustrative than as final. It is needed in order to pass over to the question of the mechanisms of phenomena.
```

```text
For Petrovich, to explain a phenomenon and to establish its mechanism are one and the same. The question reduces itself to the totality of the facts and conditions of the phenomenon, to the character of the role of each of its components, and to knowledge of the way in which such a combination of roles necessarily determines the features of the phenomenon.

Knowledge of the mechanism of processes is only rarely complete and exact, for too many factors and conditions participate in the concrete course of any phenomenon. Much more often this knowledge is approximate. One must isolate the basis of the phenomenon, casting aside secondary and complicating moments; in that way an simplified concept of the mechanism is obtained. Then, by reintroducing the factors at first set aside one by one and investigating their influence, one may arrive at an ever more exact picture of the mechanism.

Thus the phenomena of tides depend in reality on the joint action of many factors; nevertheless only the first two give the phenomenon its typical form and suffice to explain what is essential in it. Here Petrovich, probably without himself suspecting it, is speaking of the abstract method.

The comparison of clarified mechanisms often reveals their similarity across the most diverse phenomena, while deeply dissimilar factors and conditions play identical roles in identical combinations. With each type of mechanism there are invariabIy linked definite features in the course of phenomena. Therefore, Petrovich holds, one of the basic tasks of `general phenomenology` consists in this: `given quantitative or qualitative data about the mechanism of a phenomenon, to foresee the features of its course corresponding to them.`

He then makes clear that the reverse connection is by no means obligatory. From a definite feature of the course of a process one still cannot confidently infer a definite mechanism. Yet here too hypothetical conclusions become possible, and these admit of verification by additional data.

In general the author successfully clarifies the scientific-heuristic significance of research into analogies in the features of the course of phenomena. One must know how to abstract from the diversity of these outward forms in order to find behind them the kernel of a scientific analogy.
```

```text
The next chapter sketches the specific forms of mechanisms and courses for certain concrete groups of phenomena. Among the most interesting are cases in which a single mechanism yields practical transfer from one domain to another.

The common features in each such group form its `analogical kernel`, or simply the `kernel of the group`. In relation to that kernel one may speak of complete equality of disparate phenomena. The equation of one may be directly applied to another simply by replacing the descriptive elements of the one with the corresponding `homologous` elements of the other.

Among the many interesting analogies of this kind the author gives one that involuntarily makes us smile: according to certain economic theories there allegedly exists a mathematical analogy between economic phenomena and the thermal phenomena of gases. But Bogdanov clearly regards this sort of economic example as superficial.

By contrast, the most valuable cases are precise ones, such as the analogies worked out by C. A. Bjerknes between hydrodynamic fields and electric or magnetic poles, or the broad analogies that arise from the extension of the Gibbs-Le Chatelier `law of equilibrium` to biological and social processes.
```

```text
At the present level of psychology, less scientific significance can be assigned to analogies between the struggle of motives in consciousness and a regular battle. Here scientific precision is impossible so long as volitional processes have not been analytically reduced to combinations of primary and conditioned reflexes.

From scientific illustrations the author then passes to the countless analogies expressed by comparisons, likenesses, and metaphors in ordinary speech and literature. Such comparisons, he says, may possess not only poetic interest and expressive force but also a positive interest grounded in reality, in the existence of a definite sum of features forming the kernel of a group-analogy. Yet one must remember that every scientific analogy too is born in the inquiring mind at first only in the form of a prescientific comparison, juxtaposition, or conjecture, and only afterward develops into a higher cognitive type.

All philosophy, in essence, reduces itself to systematized conjectures, juxtapositions of this kind, which may tend toward passage into scientific analogies, but are not yet such so long as they cannot be verified in exact practical application.

Here lies the boundary. And the doctrine of analogies can become science only insofar as it passes into the living practice of investigation for creative-labor application.

Petrovich illustrates, from the history of science, the genuinely creative significance of analogies, analogies that led both to the discovery of new facts and to the explanation of old ones. But if until now the application of analogies has been a matter of spontaneous and accidental juxtapositions of what, owing to outward heterogeneity, had not previously been juxtaposed in human thought, now it must become consciously planful. Newtons must not have to wait for apples to fall.

Yet Petrovich, for all that, has not reached the idea of the necessity of a scientific explanation of analogies. It is not enough to collect and analyze them, even to apply and verify them; one must find their grounds.

The vital functions of a human being and of a microbe present immense analogies. But those analogies are not only ascertained and verified; they are explained. By what? By the determinate structure of living protoplasm, which forms the basis of one or another function, and therefore the basis of their analogy itself. If analogies bind together disparate phenomena, that points to a unity of structure in the most diverse objects of being. A scientific theory of analogies thus leads to the posing of the question of universal types and regularities of structure, of the structural unity of the universe. And that posing of the question must be scientific-experiential, not philosophically conjectural. Clarified regularities of structure must make possible the prediction of further analogies, and that prediction is subject to verification.

From partial analogies to universal ones, from universal analogies to the general laws of the world's structure: such is this line of scientific monism, whose initial stages are sketched by the Serbian professor's work. He could not pose the task more broadly because of the bourgeois-scientific upbringing that speaks so clearly in his illustrations from the social sphere. Thought trained on the anarchic character of capitalist social processes cannot be consistently organizational.
```

## Notes

- `5.3` clarifies tektology negatively as much as positively. It shows what a near-relative looks like when it stops short of structural explanation.
- The section's strongest line is the one between analogy as noticed resemblance and analogy as scientifically grounded relation.
- Petrovich's real gain, as Bogdanov presents it, is heuristic: analogy can guide the discovery of mechanisms, the transfer of methods, and the prediction of further features.
- But Bogdanov's final criticism is decisive: analogy by itself remains incomplete until the basis of the analogy is explained in determinate structure.
