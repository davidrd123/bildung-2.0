# Part 22: Tektology Is Not Philosophy (front.07 ¶18-29 + P.S.)

The remainder of `front.07` takes the wartime argument and turns it against a sympathetic misunderstanding. The reviewer agrees that a universal organizational science is needed, but wants to absorb it into philosophy. Bogdanov's answer is unusually sharp and unusually useful: tektology is not philosophy because its unity is practical rather than contemplative, because it demands experiment, because its general schemes are partial scientific instruments rather than all-explaining world-pictures, and because even the problem of qualitative change must be approached through determinate laws of disorganization rather than by demand for a total metaphysical system.

## Why This Batch

- `front.07 ¶18-22` answers the sympathetic reviewer by refusing the name `philosophy` for organizational science.
- `front.07 ¶23-24` distinguishes tektology from both theoretical and practical philosophy by practice, experiment, and moral indifference.
- `front.07 ¶25-27` answers the complaint that tektological schemes do not exhaust all world-processes, and says that qualitative change requires the still-unfinished study of disorganization and crises.
- `front.07 ¶28-29 + P.S.` closes on science as collective labor and then widens the whole argument from world war to world revolution.

Более того, эти эксперименты, как мне приходилось указывать, давно уже делались и делаются. Что представляют опыты Квинке, Бючли, Ледюка, Румблера, Геррера и др. над «искусственными клетками», воспроизведение на неорганических смесях различных функций живой клетки? Эти опыты не относятся к биологии, потому что объект их — неживые тела; но они не относятся также просто к химии и молекулярной физике, потому что имеют целью решение задач не химических или физических, а биологических. Это эксперименты организационные в смысле тектологии: они выясняют общие условия строения живых и неживых тел, выделяют архитектурные элементы жизненных функций в «мертвой» природе. Можно опасаться, что старый термин затемнит новые задачи и ослабит внимание к ним. Тектология не должна стать делом философов-специалистов, среди которых она вряд ли может найти какую-нибудь почву, а делом всех широкообразованных людей научной и практической мысли.

**Draft**

```text
Thus theoretical philosophy is always marked by an `explanatory` tendency, and precisely in the contemplative sense: to `explain` the world, to `explain` cognition, so that things may be `clear`, the enigma disappear, and the need `to understand` be satisfied. For tektology, if it `explains` how the most heterogeneous elements are combined in nature, in labor, and in thought, the issue is one of practical mastery over all possible ways of such combination. It lies wholly in practice; even cognition itself is for it a special case of organizational practice, the coordination of complexes of a special type. Here there is a radical difference of tendency. And with so-called `practical philosophy` tektology has perhaps even less in common: `practical philosophy` ordinarily means principles of moral guidance for people, whereas tektology is as alien to morality as its nearest relative, mathematics, or any of the natural sciences. The relation of the parts of a machine or the linkage of chemical bodies lies for it in one and the same line of investigation with the moral and nonmoral relations of people.

Further, the very idea of exact empirical verification is incompatible with the concept of a philosophical theory. As soon as such verification became possible for one or another group of generalizations, that group immediately separated itself from `philosophy`, turning into a branch of `science`: a constant and highly characteristic opposition. Tektology, however, is directly unthinkable without constant verification in experience. What use would organizational regularities be if they could neither be applied nor tested? Try to imagine a `philosophical experiment`. For tektology, by contrast, the staging of planful experiments is something absolutely necessary to its development. More than that, such experiments, as I have already had occasion to point out, have long been made and are being made. What are the experiments of Quincke, Butschli, Leduc, Rhumbler, Herrera, and others on `artificial cells`, the reproduction on inorganic mixtures of various functions of the living cell? These experiments do not belong to biology, because their object is inanimate bodies; but neither do they belong simply to chemistry and molecular physics, because their aim is to solve not chemical or physical tasks, but biological ones. These are organizational experiments in the tektological sense: they clarify the general conditions of the structure of living and nonliving bodies, isolating the architectural elements of life-functions in `dead` nature. To tektology there should also be assigned the old `cosmogonic` experiment of Plateau, reproducing the rings of Saturn in a mixture of liquids, and the more recent experiments of Mayer, in which the possible equilibria of electrons composing the atom are tested by means of floating magnets, and so forth.

In speaking against the conflation of organizational science with philosophy, I do not have a merely verbal dispute in view, but serious interests of the work itself. One may fear that the old term will obscure the new tasks and weaken attention to them. Tektology must not become the affair of philosopher-specialists, among whom it is hardly likely to find any soil, but the affair of all broadly educated people of scientific and practical thought.
```

I pass to the essential point of disagreement with the critic. He writes:

## Notes

- The anti-philosophy line here is sharper than `2.c` because it is forced out by a sympathetic assimilation rather than by a general methodological contrast. Bogdanov is not only saying that tektology stands above philosophy; he is saying why calling it philosophy would actively misdirect the work.
- The phrase `philosophical experiment` is the decisive wedge. It sounds unnatural because philosophy, as such, does not live by empirical testing; tektology does.
- The argument about universality is equally important. A universal scheme need not explain `everything at once`; it can instead be universally applicable to one determinate side of phenomena, as with the conservation law analogy.
- The answer on qualitative change matters historically. In this 1916 preface Bogdanov still presents a fuller doctrine of crises as future work. That should not be flattened into the architecture of the present printed volume; it is evidence of the earlier growth-line of the project.
- The 1917 postscript radicalizes the practical claim once again. War had already made organizational science necessary; revolution now makes it still deeper and more urgent.
- Several obvious FB2 scars were normalized here: `Русское Богатство`, `да еще`, `К тектологии`, and in the postscript the damaged phrase was restored as `организационных форм`, which the sentence clearly requires.
- No glossary change seems earned here. The gain is conceptual and architectural, not lexical.
