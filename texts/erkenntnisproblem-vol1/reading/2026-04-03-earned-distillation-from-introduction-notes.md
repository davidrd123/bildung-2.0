## Promoted claims

### 1. `Herrschaft` and reification are central, not incidental

The preface's language of `Herrschaft über die Erscheinungen` is not just a rhetorical flourish. It states the constitutive, non-mirroring conception of knowledge in its most forceful form: thought does not copy a ready-made world but gains and secures rule over a phenomenal domain. The second half of the same movement is equally important: the instruments of thought reify into apparently independent powers to which thought then seems subject.

This claim is now stable enough for project use because it is no longer resting only on close reading of the preface. The same pattern keeps returning in different historical forms:

- in the introduction, where conceptual shaping replaces naive reproduction
- in the Cusanus chapter, where passive `similitudo` gives way to active `assimilatio`
- in the skepticism chapter, where fixed object and stable subject are both dissolved

The Marx comparison remains outer context. The reification structure itself is now earned.

### 2. The neoplatonic ancestry is internal to Cassirer's own argument

Neoplatonism is not just an external analogy for Cassirer. The book itself is showing a genealogy in which emanation, symbolic embodiment, force, and development are progressively reworked into constitutive epistemology. The Cusanus tranches made this explicit, and the opening of `Die Naturphilosophie` now confirms that the same ancestry continues into Book II.

What can now be said with confidence:

- Cassirer is tracing the ancestry of the constitutive move, not merely borrowing isolated motifs
- Cusanus is a hinge figure in that transformation
- Book II opens by identifying Neoplatonism as the conceptual synthesis through which Renaissance natural philosophy first thinks nature as a self-transforming whole

What should still remain open:

- whether Cassirer finally escapes the deeper metaphysical commitments he historicizes
- whether Heidegger's later-style pressure on this line is decisive

### 3. Skepticism's missing task is historical criticism

This is no longer just an introduction-level methodological expectation. The end of `Der Skepticismus` states it directly: skepticism is radical toward logical grounds but naive toward transmitted fact, and therefore does not yet reach the concept of historical criticism.

That matters because it binds the introduction's methodological claim to a concrete historical result:

- history is not a decorative supplement to systematic philosophy
- the critique of historical transmission is the next task generated by skepticism's own insufficiency
- Bayle appears not as a random later figure, but as an internally demanded continuation

This is one of the clearest cases where the reading notes and the translation now meet on the same point.

### 4. Cassirer is teaching the project its own method

This has moved beyond analogy. The introduction-context note was right to notice that Cassirer's method maps onto the repo's own working discipline: no ready-made schema, unity created through acts of thought, larger connections earned by sharper knowledge of particulars. After the tranches completed so far, that should now be treated as a project finding rather than a clever comparison.

The claim can now be stated more plainly:

- the text itself teaches deferred structure
- the text itself teaches evidence before framework
- the text itself teaches that synthesis must arise from patient work on particulars rather than from imported schema

This does not make every later synthesis valid. It gives a criterion for which syntheses count as earned.
