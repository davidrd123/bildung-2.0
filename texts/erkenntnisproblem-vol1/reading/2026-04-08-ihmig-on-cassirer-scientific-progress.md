# 2026-04-08 - Ihmig on Cassirer, scientific progress, and axiomatics

## Main gain

Ihmig is useful because he states very clearly what is already easy to lose in current Cassirer work:

- scientific development is not just accumulation
- but neither is it sheer break
- the real unit of change is the transformation of conceptual structure

That gives a disciplined way to read `Erkenntnisproblem` as both historical and epistemological at once.

## Promoted claims

### 1. Cassirer already has a continuity-plus-mutation model

Ihmig starts from the modern problem of scientific change and uses it to sharpen Cassirer:

- Cassirer explicitly rejects a purely cumulative model
- but he also rejects a picture of arbitrary rupture
- he therefore needs both `methodische Kontinuität` and real `Mutation`

That matters because it stabilizes a reading rule for the book:

- later theories do not simply preserve all earlier content
- but neither do they merely replace it from outside
- what persists is a methodological or structural line, not a fixed stock of doctrines

### 2. The real object of historical analysis is the changing `Fachwerk` of concepts

Ihmig's use of Hilbert is the strongest immediate payoff.

The key point is:

- a theory is not best understood as a heap of statements
- it is a structured framework of concepts and relations
- change in science therefore appears first as change in that framework

This is exactly the level at which `Erkenntnisproblem` should be read.

Project use:

- when Cassirer moves from one figure to another, the main question is not only "what doctrine does this thinker hold?"
- the stronger question is: what conceptual framework for lawful knowledge becomes possible here?

### 3. Progress appears as deepening, generalization, and unification

Ihmig's account of Hilbert helps clarify why Cassirer is drawn to axiomatic and invariant-style thinking.

Progress is not just more facts. It can mean:

- deeper foundations
- greater generality
- stronger unification across domains
- the identification of invariants under changing interpretations

This gives a sharper background test for the current Book II work:

- where Bruno approaches universality, is it mathematical, logical, or only ontological?
- where a figure speaks of order, relation, or law, does this actually alter the framework of cognition?

### 4. `Erkenntnisproblem` should be read as a history of constitutive frameworks

Ihmig helps restate Cassirer without flattening him into either neo-Kantian dogma or history-of-ideas survey.

The most useful formulation is:

- Cassirer is tracking the historical transformation of constitutive scientific frameworks
- this is why Renaissance material matters even when it is not yet exact science
- these figures matter by preparing, deforming, or obstructing the conceptual conditions of objectivity

This is especially useful for the live `Naturphilosophie` line, where it is easy to overread ontological grandeur as scientific modernity.

## Immediate checks for Book II

Use Ihmig's extraction as a background test in the current Bruno run:

- is the gain merely that Bruno says `nature` more universally, or does he alter the form of lawful cognition?
- when Cassirer distinguishes Bruno from Galileo, is the real issue ontology versus mathematical law?
- where continuity is claimed, what is actually continuous: vocabulary, problem-pressure, or method?
- where rupture is claimed, what exactly mutates: object-concept, law-concept, or explanatory medium?

## Limit

This note does not replace a direct pass through the whole Ihmig chapter.

It is the current working yield:

- Cassirer should be read through conceptual transformation, not statement accumulation
- continuity and mutation are both necessary
- Hilbert helps make visible the structural level on which Cassirer is actually operating
