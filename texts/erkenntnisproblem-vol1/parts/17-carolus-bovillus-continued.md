Wenn für den `intellectus angelicus` Sein und Wissen zusammenfallen und eine unmittelbare Einheit bilden, so bleiben sie für den `intellectus humanus` dauernd getrennt; wenn jener die Begriffe als die ewigen Urbilder erfaßt, die vor dem Sein der Dinge vorausgehen, so vermag dieser nur ein Nachbild des gegebenen Seins zu gewinnen. „Jedes Objekt ist der Zeit nach früher, als die Erkenntniskraft, die ihm entspricht: die Welt aber, die der Ort aller Dinge ist, ist das natürliche Objekt des menschlichen Verstandes. So wird die Gesamtheit aller Dinge, die sich in ihr befinden, dem Verstand von Natur durch die Sinne bekannt gemacht, dargestellt und entgegengebracht, damit er von ihnen lerne und selbst zu ihnen werde.“3) Der Satz, daß Nichts im Verstande ist, was nicht zuvor in den Sinnen gewesen wäre, gilt somit zweifellos und unbeschränkt unter den Bedingungen unserer Erkenntnis, wenngleich er sich, wie wir sahen, in sein Gegenteil verkehrt, wenn man die absolute Erkenntnisweise der höheren geistigen Substanzen zugrunde legt.4)

In similar fashion, perception too is affected by the impression of everything that has color or tone or taste, yet it does not apprehend all this in its concrete immediate constitution, but insofar as it embodies a determinate universal form (`ἀλλ' οὐχ ᾗ ἕκαστον ἐκεῖνα λέγεται, ἀλλ' ᾗ τοιόνδι κατὰ τὸν λόγον`). Thus, as scholasticism formulates this Aristotelian thought, it is not the things themselves, but their immaterial `species`, that are taken up into thought. Yet the concept of `species` itself is grasped here in a double sense — and this is precisely what enables it to become the mediator between outer and inner world: for it designates both the formal objective content of the thing and the sensible or conceptual image we gain of it and to which all further logical elaboration must attach itself. This general basic intuition is more precisely determined in Bovillus by the fact that he regards and develops it from the standpoint of scholastic realism. From the outset his doctrine draws a strict distinction between the being that belongs to the concept “in itself” and the special form this being possesses in our limited and dependent understanding. “If it is given to the `intellect of angels` to grasp concepts and essences in their pure unmoving being, the human intellect is condemned to behold them through an alien medium and by virtue of a constant becoming. As by nature it is bound to matter, so its thought too can take its point of departure only from sensible impressions and remains dependent on this mediation even in its highest achievements. This conditionedness is the expression of its innate ignorance and of the natural inactivity in which it would have to remain without foreign assistance. That the human understanding could draw knowledge from itself and from its own content is impossible; it is nothing other than a mere potency, which can be completed and fulfilled only by an act coming from without.”1)

If for the `intellectus angelicus` being and knowing coincide and form an immediate unity, they remain permanently separated for the `intellectus humanus`; if the former grasps concepts as the eternal archetypes preceding the being of things, the latter can win only an image of given being. The gradation of creation therefore proceeds from the “angelic understanding” to concrete natural things and from these on to the human mind: `in angelico intellectu sunt omnia ante esse, in seipso in esse, in humano post esse`. Objects accordingly possess in the understanding of angels a pure intellectual being, in their own existence a sensible and natural being, while in human thought they acquire a derived, rational being.2)

“Every object is earlier in time than the faculty of knowledge corresponding to it; but the world, which is the place of all things, is the natural object of human understanding. Thus the totality of all things contained in it is by nature made known, presented, and brought before the understanding through the senses, so that it may learn from them and itself become them.”3) The proposition that nothing is in the understanding that was not previously in the senses therefore holds doubtlessly and without restriction under the conditions of our knowledge, even though, as we saw, it is reversed into its contrary if one takes as one's basis the absolute mode of knowledge of the higher spiritual substances.4)

The task of the doctrine of knowledge is to trace, in its individual phases, the change that the immediate being of the object undergoes through its reception into intellect, to describe the transformation of sensible into “intelligible species.” “The images of things as they arise in the world from objects are not yet intellectual, but sensible, and are at first taken up only into the senses. For everything that exists in the world is a sensible substance; and as the substance is, so too are its species and its natural image. From the sensible substances of the world no other than sensible species proceed and move toward us. Here they are first received into the senses, until the understanding, hidden behind human impressions, transforms them into intellectual species, which it draws forth, gains, and abstracts through purification of perception or through rational conjectures.”5) The “forms” of things therefore change their nature when they enter from the “greater world” into the smaller. The actual, material existence that belongs to them there they must relinquish in order to gain entry into the domain of understanding; the sensible determinacy and concreteness by which they are distinguished they can preserve only “up to the gates of mind.” Once they have crossed the entrance of the soul, they are immediately handed over by the intellect to memory, which preserves them as a fixed possession, though in a new mode of being.6)

Strictly speaking, however, this transformation takes place neither in sense nor in intellect as such, but is a work of imagination, which as a middle term between the two powers partakes of the nature of both.7) The further investigation is directed above all toward distinguishing these various basic psychic powers and delimiting their efficacy against one another. “Understanding” appears here more and more as a mere transit-point, as a solely mediating potency through which outer reality is transferred into “inner sense”: `intellectuales species, per quas homo omnia fit, ortum habent in mundo, transeunt per humanum intellectum, finem et statum in memoria`.8) Memory is in truth the genuine microcosm, taking up and mirroring within itself the whole being of outer things: a conception for which Bovillus believes he may appeal to Plato's `ἀνάμνησις`. **Notes**

- `Species` now becomes the technical mediator between thing and thought, precisely because it carries a double sense: formal content and received image.
- Bovillus radicalizes the hierarchy between `intellectus angelicus` and `intellectus humanus`: the human intellect is a mere potency fulfilled only from without.
- Cassirer treats the scholastic maxim `nihil est in intellectu...` as conditionally true for human cognition, but not for higher spiritual substances.
- The most surprising inversion in this tranche is that memory, not intellect, becomes the real microcosm and storehouse of the world's forms.
- The tranche stops mid-sentence at the printed page `68` boundary, where Cassirer is about to continue the mirror analogy for intellect.
