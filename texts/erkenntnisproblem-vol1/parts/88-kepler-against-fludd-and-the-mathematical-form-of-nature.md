# Part 88: Kepler Against Fludd and the Mathematical Form of Nature

Cassirer now makes Kepler's anti-mystical line fully explicit. Mathematics does not become scientific by symbolizing hidden truths, but by serving strict causal analysis and empirical lawfulness. Against Fludd, Kepler insists that abstract concepts can express reality more faithfully than direct imagistic immediacy, and that the mathematical sciences are the very wings by which astronomy rises to the order of sensible things. This dispute then widens into a deeper historical contrast: not only mysticism, but the whole Aristotelian-scholastic world of substantial forms is at issue. Kepler opposes it with a mathematical form-concept grounded in mediation, degree, and transition rather than bare identity and otherness.

Mathematical and Mystical Conception of Nature.

The answer that Kepler gives to this throws light on the new concept of reality. Every proof and every deductive derivation must proceed in abstract concepts, which nevertheless fully express and mirror the real relations of things, "for what could be more similar to one another than the image to the original?" When Fludd had pointed to principles higher than the mathematical for knowledge of the essence of things, Kepler admits that the "naked inwardness" of substances, without mediation by their relations and properties, is closed to him; among these latter, however, the relations of quantity stand in first place, and for this he can appeal to Aristotle himself. Against the objection that the necessary connection here demanded between mathematics and empirical research amounts to a blurring of the boundaries between the two kinds of inquiry, he adduces - like Galileo later - the Platonic saying that arithmetic and geometry, the abstract fundamental sciences of number and form, are the wings of astronomy by virtue of which alone it is able to rise to cognition of the order of sensible things. In this thought he feels himself to be the called continuer of ancient science and philosophy in opposition to all those who take over from both only some accidental and external determinations while surrendering the actual fundamental motifs. The "Paracelsians" and "alchemists" may be left to wish to describe and grasp nature in sensible images and likenesses: science begins only where consciousness becomes dominant that the eye is blind without the aid of mathematical grounds of proof.

It is, down to the expression itself, the same view and the same logical turn as that advanced by Socrates in the Phaedo... (see above p. 314). The demand to "copy" reality through thought here achieves sharper distinction and delimitation. The mystic too strives after an imaging of the real; but instead of hypotheses subject to the control and jurisdiction of thought, he makes use of hieroglyphs: shapes and signs that, for all their intuitive vividness, remain impenetrable to the judgment of the understanding. Thus there arises an inversion of all logical relations of value, which expresses itself naively in Robert Fludd when he rejects cognition of quantities as a "knowledge of shadow-images" that nowhere penetrates to the actual physical being of things. In this disparagement of the means of cognition of magnitude there lays itself bare the contempt that the theosopher feels for nature and its "lower" empirical reality. Astronomical research, which confines itself to describing the paths of the heavenly bodies and thus the mere accidents of created things, is in his eyes unable to approach the metaphysics of the eternal and imperishable substances. Once again Kepler in his rejoinder here uncovers the deepest difference of the directions of thought. "I grasp reality, as you say, by the tail, but I hold it in my hand; you may still seek to grasp its head, if only it does not happen merely in a dream. I am satisfied with the effects, that is, with the motions of the planets; if, on the other hand, you can find in their causes themselves harmonic relations as transparent as those I have found in the revolutions, then it is only fair that I congratulate you on this discovery and myself on understanding it, as soon as I am once in a position to understand it."

The Ontological and the Mathematical Concept of Form.

The same typical opposition of thought that we earlier encountered in Leonardo da Vinci and Fracastoro thus meets us anew with greater energy and clarity. And this recurrence is not accidental, but points back to a fundamental conflict in the history of thought. It is in fact not only mysticism that Kepler has to fight against here: it is the entire Aristotelian-scholastic worldview of substantial forms that is thereby struck at the same time. Kepler himself sees his difference from Aristotle in the fact that Aristotle, because he strove beyond geometry toward a higher and more general science, took his point of departure from a first formal-logical opposition, that of identity and difference. **Working Commentary**

```text
1) Kepler now states the anti-mystical boundary with full precision: symbols are harmless as play, but worthless for science unless they express real causal connection.

2) Fludd helps Cassirer expose the new reality-concept. Mathematical abstraction is not a betrayal of the real but its most faithful image where relations are concerned.

3) The Phaedo comparison matters because it shows Kepler's position as part of the same long anti-image-thinking line Cassirer opened at the start of the chapter.

4) The deepest target is broader than mysticism. Kepler's mathematical form-concept is now set against the Aristotelian-scholastic world of substantial forms.

5) The geometry of more/less and mediation is the strongest new gain here, because it recasts difference itself in mathematical rather than ontological terms.
```
