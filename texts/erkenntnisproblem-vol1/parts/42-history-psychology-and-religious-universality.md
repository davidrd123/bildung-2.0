**Notes**

- Cassirer now makes the Vives claim explicit: history matters scientifically not because past institutions can be repeated, but because the structure of passions and affects persists beneath changing external forms.
- The first Renaissance history of philosophy therefore appears under a double pressure: universal ordering under `perennis philosophia`, then a freer historical criticism that reopens Democritus against Aristotle's authority.
- The expansion of historical range does not produce historicism; for Cassirer it intensifies the conviction of one human reason working through manifold epochs and forms.
- The religious turn is structurally parallel. Cusa, Ficino, and now Campanella seek the enduring spiritual content beneath the plurality of cults, rites, and dogmatic expressions.
- `perennis philosophia` is now load-bearing enough to watch directly, since Cassirer uses it not as a loose slogan but as a methodological principle for ordering intellectual history.
- The tranche stops at the printed page `168` boundary with `religiösen Ent-`, exactly where Campanella's `religio indita` is about to be carried further into the next step of Cassirer's religious-universalist argument.
