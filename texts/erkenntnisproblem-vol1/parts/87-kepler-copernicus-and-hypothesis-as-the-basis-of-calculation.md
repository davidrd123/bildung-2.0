# Part 87: Kepler, Copernicus, and Hypothesis as the Basis of Calculation

Cassirer now closes the historical contrast around Kepler's hypothesis doctrine. Against both the teleological physicalism that fixes nature in advance and the merely computational tradition that treats astronomical formulas as indifferent to truth, Copernicus and Kepler demand a unified natural system. Osiander's reading of Copernicus as a useful fiction is therefore rejected, but Kepler's alternative is not a return to hidden substances. Hypothesis becomes the rational image of nature that must guide calculation and then prove its fruitfulness in future observation. Only thus can mathematics be separated from mystical number-play and bound to causal analysis of phenomena.

... the point of view of the perfect and the imperfect decides what is physically possible and true. That the heavenly bodies move in perfect circles, that in their substance they are immutable and imperishable, is here established from the outset by teleological considerations about the order of the cosmos and the rank of its elements. Observation and mathematical analysis are bound to this result: they find already before them a general conception of "nature" that sets determinate limits for them from the very start. Over against this view, which in the Middle Ages found expression especially in Averroism, there stands, however, a second methodological conception resting on the author of the Ptolemaic system himself. In developing the epicycle theory, Ptolemy had expressly renounced giving a decision concerning the "physical" reality of the individual motions to which he traces back the astronomical appearances. The calculating astronomer concerns himself solely with these phenomena themselves and their exact determinations of measure, while he has not to inquire into their grounds and thus makes no decision about them. Indeed, these grounds are now added to be altogether inscrutable to the human spirit: for it can know only what accords with its own nature, and is thus imperfect and changeable, whereas insight into the inner and perfect essence of the heavenly spheres is denied to it. Into this dispute the work of Copernicus now intervenes. Thus it is understandable that its meaning and its fundamental intention were at first grasped and judged in two different ways. The first editor of the work, Andreas Osiander, wishes to see in it no more than a new hypothesis, whose "reality" could remain wholly undecided: `neque enim necesse est, hypotheses esse veras, imo ne verisimiles quidem, sed sufficit hoc unum, si calculum observationibus congruentem exhibeant`. In this he follows only the ruling tradition as it extends from Ptolemy and Simplicius, from Thomas Aquinas and Bonaventure to the "Terminalists" of the University of Paris.

Realistic and Idealistic Interpretation of the Astronomical Hypotheses.

Over against this interpretation there now stands the judgment of those men who see in the doctrine of Copernicus more than a mere облегчение of calculation, who have derived from it a new general fundamental conception of the cosmos, a new world-view and life-view. In their name Kepler now resolutely and passionately opposes Osiander's presentation, in which he sees a falsification of the Copernican fundamental thought. The Copernican theory has by no means merely simplified our mathematical description of celestial appearances, but has also transformed from the ground up our conception of the forces governing the physical universe. Indeed, for Kepler it has decisively altered the concept of force itself, insofar as through it the path is pointed out that leads over from the animistic conception of force to the mathematically functional one. What is therefore at issue here is not a mere reshaping of the equations of planetary motion, but a revision of the first grounds of the knowledge of nature itself. A unified theory is sought that does not halt before the opposition of the earthly and the heavenly world, but spans the whole of the cosmos down to the apparently remotest phenomena.

On the other hand, to be sure, this theory - contrary to the older form of astronomical "realism" - does not wish to give us insight into the ultimate substantial causes of motion, but seeks only to represent on all sides the rules according to which its actual course proceeds. The demand for the system of nature remains in force, but its fulfillment is now sought in another place. The essential reproach that Kepler directs against the traditional conception of astronomical hypotheses is that by it the astronomer is degraded to a mere calculator and excluded from the "community of philosophers." An overall sketch of the "nature of things" must precede the setting-up of the calculation, just as, on the other hand, the calculation leads back to it again as to its proper goal. "First we depict in hypotheses the nature of things, then upon this foundation we erect a calculus, in that in deductive proof we derive the motions resulting from the assumed presuppositions." Once again we can make the logical relation here entirely clear to ourselves by the analogy of Kepler's aesthetic fundamental doctrines: just as harmony lies not in the tones themselves but is first created by our spirit, so only the conceptual rule brings unity and connectedness into the chaos of sensations. What is immediately given to us are always only signs and "symptoms," not the grounds of natural processes; these are never to be grasped by direct perception, but only by concepts of reason that we hypothetically set up in order subsequently to prove them by their fruitfulness for future observation.

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) Cassirer now places Kepler inside the full historical dispute over hypothesis, and the point is cleaner than a simple realism/anti-realism split.

2) Osiander's Copernicus is rejected because it treats theory as computational convenience, while Kepler wants a unified natural system spanning heaven and earth.

3) But Kepler also refuses the old realist demand for ultimate substantial causes. Hypothesis is a rational image of nature that guides calculation and is judged by future empirical fruitfulness.

4) This makes the hypothesis/calc relation structurally parallel to harmony/perception: order is not immediately given but intellectually formed and then empirically confirmed.

5) The real break from Renaissance number-mysticism is not mathematics by itself, but mathematics put in service of strict causal analysis.
```
