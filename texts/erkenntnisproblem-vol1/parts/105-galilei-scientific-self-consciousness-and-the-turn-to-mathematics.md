# Part 105: Galilei, Scientific Self-Consciousness and the Turn to Mathematics

Cassirer now closes the Galilei arc and turns it forward into the mathematics section. First, the hierarchy of noble geometric figures collapses once continuity lets curved and straight forms pass into one another under lawful comparison. Second, Cassirer states openly what the whole run has implied: Galileo's science is already a theory of knowledge in act, governed by necessity, relation, and function. Finally, the new mathematics section opens by refusing any one-way derivation of physics from pre-given geometry. Galileo's achievement required a new mathematical language, and physics itself retroactively reshaped mathematics.

```text
... is better suited than the triangle. In the satirical turn that the thought of relativity receives here there is at the same time concealed an important logical view. In ancient mathematics the individual geometrical figures stand over against one another as strictly closed and isolated contents; there is between them no mediation and no transition. Even Kepler, who, although he is one of the founders of the infinitesimal method, on the whole still shares the older conception of geometry, can therefore hold fast to the absolute opposition of straight and curved. In comparing both there is indeed a greater and smaller, and therefore there must also be some concept of equality, but one inaccessible to human science and incapable of being established. For Galileo, by contrast, the rigidity of geometrical concept-formation has dissolved. Just as in his physics he teaches us not to grasp the spatial order and configuration of bodies as something given, but to develop them from advancing relative positings, so in his mechanics the finite path is first represented as the integral of velocity, and thus derived from its element. The solution of the projectile problem then showed how the curved path of the body arises from and is composed out of the straight components and determining elements. From every side, therefore, the abolition of the conceptual separation is prepared, a separation in which Galileo himself sees the cornerstone and foundation of the whole Aristotelian physics. The continuity between the individual geometrical figures, by virtue of which they can be strictly compared with one another and transformed into one another, abolishes the unconditional superiority in value of one over another. "Perfection" now signifies nothing other than thoroughgoing, univocal lawfulness: the expression thus designates the generic character of geometrical cognition as such, not the specific difference of an individual figure that arises within it.

Looking back once more from here, we can now survey how the individual thoughts have developed with clear determinacy out of the basic principle. However little Galileo developed a separate theory of cognition beside and outside his scientific achievements, so clearly there presents itself in these achievements themselves a new unified fundamental conception of the task of cognition, which he carries through and consistently enacts. Member joins itself to member in strict inner consequence. The point of departure was experience, experiment, and observation. But experience itself is here no longer, as in natural philosophy, the mere unrelated accumulation of the material of perception, `experimentorum multorum coacervatio`; it is a strictly articulated whole and a necessary connection. From this point of view of necessity there was determined for Galileo the conception and definition of matter and motion. At the same time it became clear that the character of necessity is grounded in the conditional propositions of mathematics, that it therefore points back not to ultimate and highest schematic generic concepts but to universally valid relations and laws. These relations are at once universal and individual, that is, concretely determinate: just as the general functional equation includes all possible individual values of `x` within itself and determines for them the value of the dependent variable. Thus the new view stands under the rule and guidance of the thought of relation, which finds its clearest expression in the concept of function. Not merely the new material of facts but the changed principled view from which it was won forms the boundary between scholasticism and modernity.

Galileo, according to a saying of Campanella, discovered a new heaven and a new earth; but he was able to do so only on the basis of the new ideal of cognition that he formulated for science. He himself accomplished what he denies to syllogistic doctrine: logic in him became the positive and fruitful tool of the knowledge of nature, the "organon" of discoveries. "I do not esteem the first inventor of the lyre," so he judges, "even if his instrument may still have been unfinished in construction and rough in tone, any less for that; indeed I place him higher than a hundred other artists who later perfected his invention. To advance from insignificant beginnings to great discoveries, to discern in the first childish appearance the germ of wondrous art: this is no achievement of commonplace minds, but requires thoughts and designs of a spiritual power rising above the measure of the human." We may apply these words to Galileo himself; however much the instrument of thought for the knowledge of nature that he created has been sharpened and refined in the history of science, he must forever count as its first and original discoverer.

Up to this point mathematics has confronted us merely in the significance it possesses as presupposition and condition of the modern concept of nature. Its achievement, insofar as the preceding developments could determine it, is exhausted in the relation that Galileo brought to pure and classical expression in well-known words. The book of philosophy is that of nature, which lies constantly before our eyes, yet only few are able to decipher and read it, since it is composed and written in letters different from those of our alphabet, in triangles and squares, in circles and spheres, in cones and pyramids. Characteristic as these propositions are, however, the whole of Galileo's achievement and the proper advance that it signifies are designated by them only inadequately. The language of nature, as it is determined here, is that of ancient synthetic geometry. Its grammar is given as fixed possession in Euclid's Elements and, in further elaboration, in Apollonius' doctrine of conic sections. It may suffice to understand even Kepler's laws of planetary orbits; for the laws of falling bodies and the dynamic fundamental conception built upon them it does not suffice. Galileo's task was not confined to approaching the interpretation of appearances with ready-made scientific tools; what was at issue was to discover for the new view of nature a new language in the first place, to determine its characters and to grasp its syntactic structure in firm rules. In the relation of mathematics and physics there now emerges so close a reciprocity that the logical rank-order of the two sometimes appears thereby inverted. It is not merely the mathematical concepts that, in autonomous immanent development, advance to the beginnings of mechanics; it is just as much the system of physical basic concepts that retroactively determines the shape of mathematics. The new goal that is set to inquiry creates the new means out of itself. **Working Commentary**
```

```text
1) The anti-hierarchy of figures is not ornamental; it finishes the break with aesthetic teleology at the level of geometry itself.

2) Cassirer's retrospective summary is one of the strongest local confirmations that the whole Galilei sequence is really about a new concept of knowledge.

3) `Relationsgedanke` and `Funktion` are now explicitly named as the governing form of the new science.

4) The Lyra passage matters because Galilei's method is being interpreted as invention of an instrument, not only collection of results.

5) The mathematics opening corrects a too-simple reading of the famous `book of nature` formula: physics does not merely apply ready-made mathematics, but participates in remaking it.
```
