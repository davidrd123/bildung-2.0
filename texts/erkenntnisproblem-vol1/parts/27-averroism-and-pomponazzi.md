From this chief defect of the Averroist view one can explain the significance that the opposing conception, which attaches itself to the commentary of Alexander of Aphrodisias, gains everywhere with the beginning of the modern age.1) While the dispute, viewed externally, seems confined to the problem of immortality, denied by the Alexandrists and conceded by the Averroists not to individuals but to the all-embracing active intellect, the deeper motive of the opposition lies in the differing basic conception of knowledge. It is the merit of Pietro Pomponazzi's work on immortality that it restores the problem once again to this its proper field.

Pomponazzi's treatise too begins, to be sure, with purely metaphysical discussions in which the place of the human being within the whole of reality is to be determined. With the first beginnings of self-knowledge we simultaneously feel the conflict of our nature and our origin. Just as our psychic life presents itself as a whole of vegetative, sensitive, and intellectual functions, none of which can be reduced to the others, so too there seem to be three different subjects to which we must distribute these manifold modes of activity. The question of immortality thus no longer admits of a fixed and univocal answer, but depends on the objective connection we assume among these conceptually separated basic natures within us. Are we to hold, with Averroes, that the sensible powers are different in individual beings, whereas the thinking power in them is numerically one, and thus split the soul into two heterogeneous halves, to one of which every particularity, to the other every universality is foreign? But with such an answer the true problem of individuality would not be solved; for from the outset it is displaced onto a false site. What makes the human being this particular individual being, what distinguishes Socrates from Plato according to his proper essence, is no external difference graspable and tangible by sense, but the difference in the intellectual itself. Thus `understanding` confronts us not as a general, blurred generic being, but on the contrary as the fundamental principle of specification.1) The numerical separation of the `intelligences` stands fast as a datum of experience: to deny it would mean substituting for reason and unprejudiced observation a metaphysical fantasy more arbitrary and bizarre than any artist has ever invented.1)

But the acknowledgment of the particularity of thinking-power at the same time includes the consequence that its relation to the body to which it belongs can no longer be understood as a purely external bond. Every individual soul is at the same time the soul of its body and expresses it in all the determinacy of its organic nature and organic changes. It is tied to it not merely by an external bond, like a team of oxen to the ploughshare, but is durably and necessarily related to it in all its essential activities. A body counts as `ensouled` for us only insofar as all the processes taking place within it condition one another reciprocally by subordinating themselves to the one goal of self-preservation and self-perfecting of the living being: but this goal can be attained nowhere other than in the body and through it. Thus already the mere thought of a possible separation of the soul stands in contradiction to its original concept and to the function assigned to it by the basic view of the Aristotelian system. **Notes**

- Cassirer now turns the Averroist problem inside out: the real issue is not immortality by itself, but the conception of knowledge that makes an impersonal active intellect seem necessary.
- Pomponazzi’s intervention is to relocate the dispute onto the terrain of individuality and cognition. Individuality is not an external sensible difference added to thought, but rooted in the intellectual itself.
- The soul-body relation is correspondingly tightened. If each soul is the soul of its own body in a constitutive way, then the doctrine of separability becomes philosophically unintelligible even if it remains theologically mandatory.
- The key philosophical gain for Cassirer is Pomponazzi’s appeal to the unity of consciousness: sensation, reflection, and abstraction belong to one and the same `I`, not to two accidentally conjoined substances.
- The `triangle in the quadrilateral` analogy restates the whole issue methodologically: the distinctions are articulations within one structure, not separations of independent things.
- The tranche stops mid-word where Cassirer is about to push the `phantasma` thesis further and close the anti-dualist argument.
