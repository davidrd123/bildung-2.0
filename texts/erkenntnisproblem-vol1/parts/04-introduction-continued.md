Es ist der charakteristische Grundzug der neueren Metaphysik, daß sie kraft ihres eigenen immanenten Fortgangs immer deutlicher zu diesen Fragen hinstrebt. Allgemein soll uns die Geschichte des Erkenntnisproblems nicht sowohl einen Teil der Geschichte der Philosophie bedeuten — denn bei der inneren sachlichen Wechselbedingtheit aller Glieder des philosophischen Systems bliebe jede solche Abtrennung eine willkürliche Schranke —, als sie vielmehr das Gesamtgebiet unter einem bestimmten Gesichtspunkt und einer bestimmten Beleuchtung darstellen und damit gleichsam in einem Querschnitt den Inhalt der neueren Philosophie zur Anschauung bringen soll.

Die analytische Aufgabe, die dem modernen Denken gestellt war, findet ihren logischen Abschluß im System Kants. Hier erst wird der letzte endgültige Schritt getan, indem das Erkennen völlig auf sich selbst gestellt und nichts mehr, im Gebiete des Seins wie des Bewußtseins, seiner eigenen Gesetzlichkeit vorangesetzt wird. Aber indem Kant diese Wendung vollzieht, bringt er damit nicht sowohl die früheren Gedankenreihen zur Vollendung, als er vielmehr zum Schöpfer neuer Probleme wird, die bis unmittelbar in unsere philosophische Gegenwart hineinreichen und daher nicht mehr in geschichtlicher, sondern nur in systematischer Untersuchung behandelt und beurteilt werden können.1) Das System Kants ist uns nicht sowohl das Ende, als ein dauernd neuer und fruchtbarer Anfang der Kritik der Erkenntnis. Aber indem wir unsere historische Betrachtung bis zu ihm hinführen, suchen wir damit zugleich ein Mittel zu seinem sachlichen Verständnis zu gewinnen. Wenn in diesem Zusammenhang das kritische System die Wurzel seiner Kraft besitzt, so enthüllt sich in ihm doch zugleich auch seine notwendige innere Bindung. Je deutlicher wir zu unterscheiden vermögen, in welchen begrifflichen Formulierungen der Vernunftkritik die wissenschaftliche Kultur der Zeit zum Ausdruck und zum Bewußtsein ihrer selbst gelangt, um so klarer werden sich uns die allgemein gültigen Züge der Methodik Kants aus den Besonderheiten der Ausführung herausheben. Eben indem wir an dem Grundgedanken der Methode festhalten, suchen wir damit für die spezielle Ableitung und Begründung der Prinzipien freies Feld zu erhalten. Die „transscendentale Kritik“ bliebe zur Unfruchtbarkeit verurteilt, wenn es ihr versagt wäre, dem Fortschritt der wissenschaftlichen Grundbegriffe selbsttätig zu folgen und ihn in ihren speziellen Ergebnissen und Definitionen zum Ausdruck zu bringen. Je vielseitiger und beweglicher sie sich in dieser Hinsicht erhält, um so reiner wird sich die Universalität und die systematische Einheit ihrer Fragestellung erweisen.

Hier freilich stehen wir an einem Punkt, an dem noch heute die Absicht Kants, wie die der modernen Vertreter der kritischen Methode, am häufigsten und beharrlichsten mißverstanden wird. Immer wieder erhebt sich der Vorwurf, daß die transscendentale Kritik, indem sie von dem Faktum der Newtonischen Wissenschaft ausgeht, damit den geschichtlichen Prozeß gleichsam zum Stehen bringe und eine einzelne Phase der „Erfahrung“ zum allgemeinen Maßstab ihres Gehalts und inneren Wertes mache. Die „Festlegung der Forschung an einen geschichtlichen Zustand bestimmter Einzeldisziplinen“ übt zugleich — wie man einwendet — eine hemmende Tendenz aus: eine Festigung der Vernunft durch ihre Arbeit kann nicht erfolgen, ohne daß sie zugleich „eine Befestigung an ihrer Arbeit und damit ein Hindernis des Fortschritts zu neuer Arbeit wäre“.2) Wäre indes diese Folgerung richtig, so würden wir uns damit zugleich jedes sicheren Halts philosophischer Beurteilung beraubt sehen. Solange die Vernunft in sich selbst noch nicht ihre Festigkeit und ihre Selbstgewißheit gefunden hat, bleibt ihr auch die Geschichte nur ein wirres und widerspruchsvolles Chaos. Es bedarf bestimmter sachlicher Prinzipien der Beurteilung, es bedarf fester Gesichtspunkte der Auswahl und Formung, damit die historischen Erscheinungen, die für sich allein stumm sind, zu einer lebendigen und sinnvollen Einheit werden. Introduction

The genesis of these concepts cannot, to be sure, be presented unless we constantly keep in view their function within mathematical physics; but neither can all the individual phases of their becoming be made intelligible from this alone. Thus, especially in the concepts of space and time, we see how, at their first appearance in modern philosophy, they are still wholly entangled in metaphysical presuppositions. And this connection of both with the doctrine of God, which first meets us in Italian natural philosophy, remains dominant all the way down to Newton. Even Kant, as we shall see, still has before him in his transcendental critique of space and time a determinate historical formulation and articulation of these concepts, conditioned equally by the interest in the scientific grounding of mechanics and by general metaphysical questions. If, then, we cannot detach the object of our investigation from its metaphysical background, we may nevertheless linger over metaphysical problems only insofar as we see in them the covering and the symptom of questions concerning the relation of knowledge to its “object.” It is the characteristic basic trait of modern metaphysics that, by virtue of its own immanent advance, it presses ever more clearly toward these questions. In general, the history of the problem of knowledge should signify for us not so much a part of the history of philosophy, for given the inner material interdependence of all the members of the philosophical system every such separation would remain an arbitrary limit, but rather a presentation of the whole field under a determinate point of view and illumination, one that brings before us, as it were in cross-section, the content of modern philosophy.

The analytic task set for modern thought finds its logical completion in Kant's system. Only here is the final decisive step taken, in that knowing is placed wholly upon itself and nothing any longer, in the domain of being no less than of consciousness, is set prior to its own lawfulness. Yet in carrying out this turn Kant does not so much bring the earlier lines of thought to completion as he becomes the creator of new problems, ones that extend right into our own philosophical present and therefore can no longer be treated and judged historically, but only systematically.1) For us, Kant's system is not so much the end as a continually new and fruitful beginning of the critique of knowledge. But by leading our historical consideration up to him, we seek at the same time to gain a means for the substantive understanding of his thought. Far more closely than the previous accounts of the development of critical philosophy allow one to recognize, it is in its genesis interwoven and made kin with the science of the eighteenth century. Everywhere the general theory here looks to the determinate concrete problematic situation created by the methodological struggles between Leibniz and Newton and by their after-echo in the most significant investigators of the age, such as Euler and d'Alembert.

If in this context the critical system possesses the root of its strength, then its necessary inner bond is at the same time revealed in it as well. The more clearly we are able to distinguish in which conceptual formulations of the critique of reason the scientific culture of the age comes to expression and to self-consciousness, the more clearly the universally valid features of Kant's method will stand out for us from the particularities of its execution. Precisely by holding fast to the fundamental thought of the method, we seek thereby to preserve free scope for the specific derivation and grounding of the principles. “Transcendental critique” would be condemned to unfruitfulness if it were denied the power to follow the advance of scientific basic concepts by its own activity and to bring that advance to expression in its specific results and definitions. The more many-sided and mobile it remains in this respect, the more purely the universality and systematic unity of its problematic will prove itself.

Here, to be sure, we stand at a point where Kant's intention, like that of the modern representatives of the critical method, is still today most frequently and obstinately misunderstood. Again and again the objection is raised that transcendental critique, by proceeding from the fact of Newtonian science, thereby in a sense brings the historical process to a halt and makes one single phase of “experience” the general measure of its content and inner worth. The “fixing of inquiry to a historical state of determinate individual disciplines” at the same time exerts, as it is objected, a hampering tendency: a strengthening of reason through its work cannot occur without becoming at the same time “a fastening to its work and thereby an obstacle to the progress toward new work.”2) But if this inference were correct, we would thereby see ourselves deprived of every secure foothold for philosophical judgment. For it is vain, once one has denied us orientation by the content of rational science, to refer us instead to the history of spiritual culture as the proper reality. So long as reason has not yet found within itself its firmness and its self-certainty, history too remains for it only a confused and contradictory chaos. There is need of determinate objective principles of judgment, there is need of fixed points of view for selection and formation, in order that historical appearances, which by themselves are mute, may become a living and meaningful unity. If anywhere, it becomes clear in intellectual history that its content and coherence are not given, but must first be created by us on the basis of the individual facts: it is only what we make out of it by means of conceptual syntheses.

But where should we seek the substantive ground of these syntheses themselves if we had to surrender our foothold in science and in its present condition? It is certainly true that we find in it only a relative point of support, and that we must therefore keep the categories under which we view the historical process themselves changeable and capable of transformation; but this kind of relativity designates not the limit, but the very life of knowledge. The substantive analysis of the state of rational sciences and the tracing of their gradual becoming now reciprocally illuminate and condition one another. One does not become at home in the historical “working world” of culture unless one has first been filled with a substantive interest in the principles and problems of contemporary research.

The task set to philosophy in each individual phase of its development therefore consists ever anew in bringing out the general logical functions of knowledge as such from a concrete historical complex of determinate scientific concepts and principles. This complex may change, and has changed since Newton; yet the question remains whether in the new content now emerging those most general relations to which critical analysis alone directed its gaze may not again present themselves under another shape and covering. The very concept of the history of science already contains within itself that thought of the preservation of a general logical structure throughout the succession of particular systems of concepts. For in truth, if the earlier content of thought were not linked to the preceding by some identity, there would be nothing that entitled us to combine the scattered logical fragments we would then have before us into a sequence of events.
