Sofern die Ideen als absolutes Sein jenseits der Erscheinungswelt gedacht und gedeutet werden, werden sie von Cusanus ausdrücklich zurückgewiesen; hier tritt er der Aristotelischen Kritik in ihrem ganzen Umfang und in allen ihren Folgerungen bei. Das ist der tiefere Grund und Zweck jener Gegensätzlichkeit, die uns überall in der Sinnenwelt entgegentritt: daß sie die Fähigkeit der Unterscheidung in uns anregt und unterstützt und damit das Denken zum Bewußtsein seiner eigentümlichen Grundkraft erhebt.1) Denn dem Sinn als solchen wohnt keine Kraft der Bestimmung und Unterscheidung bei; vielmehr ist es die Vernunft, die in dem sinnlichen Material erst die festen Abgrenzungen und Sonderungen vollzieht: „ratio sensu ut instrumento ad discernenda sensibilia utitur, sed ipsa est, quae in sensu sensibile discernit“.2) So ist selbst das empirische Weltbild, in dem uns die Gegenstände bereits als einzelne gesondert entgegentreten, schon ein Produkt aus dem Zusammenwirken von Wahrnehmung und Begriff. Durch diesen Gegensatz gegen den mittelalterlichen Realismus aber, dem die Idee an und für sich etwas Existierendes und Absolutes ist, werden wir zu Folgerungen hingeführt, die den Erkenntniswert des Denkens zunächst herabzusetzen scheinen. Wenn unser diskursives Denken die Sichtung und Deutung der sinnlichen Eindrücke zur Aufgabe hat, so ist es klar, daß es nicht auf die Wesenheit der Dinge, sondern nur auf deren „Abbilder“ sich richtet und über diese an keinem Punkte hinauszugelangen vermag. Das System der Erkenntnis löst sich in einen Inbegriff und eine Ordnung von Zeichen auf; die absolute Welt der Objekte bleibt ihm unzugänglich. Wenn die Gegenstände im göttlichen Geiste nach ihrer präzisen und eigentümlichen Wahrheit enthalten sind, so faßt der unsrige nicht ihr Sein, sondern nur mittelbar ihre „Ähnlichkeit“; wenn das Denken Gottes zugleich ein Erschaffen ist, so geschieht unsere Begriffsbildung dadurch, daß wir uns selber den vorhandenen Objekten anpassen und uns nach ihnen umgestalten. Der Begriff der Seele selbst wird unter diesem Gesichtspunkt bestimmt: sie ist die Kraft, die sich allen Dingen anzugliedern vermag (`quae se omnibus rebus potest conformare`).3) Wie dem göttlichen Geiste die `vis entificativa`, so kommt unserem Intellekt die `vis assimilativa` als Merkmal und Grundzug zu;4) wie jenem die ursprüngliche seinspendende Kraft innewohnt, so besitzt dieser die lichtspendende Kraft, die uns das Ganze der Sinnenbilder erst erhellt.5)

Dennoch ist es ein bloßer Abglanz des Wirklichen, der uns durch alle menschliche Forschung zuletzt gegeben werden kann. Durch die Erschaffung der gedanklichen Zeichensprache, in die wir das Sein zu fassen versuchen, hüllt sich das Wesen der Dinge für uns nur in immer dichtere Schleier. Von der `similitudo` schreitet er zur `assimilatio` fort: von der Behauptung einer in den Dingen vorhandenen Ähnlichkeit, die die Grundlage ihrer Zusammenfassung und gattungsmäßigen Bezeichnung abgibt, geht er zur Darlegung des Prozesses über, vermöge dessen der Geist einen harmonischen Zusammenhang zwischen den Objekten und sich selber erst herstellen und erschaffen muß. It would be mistaken to characterize the philosophical Renaissance of the Quattrocento simply as a discovery of Platonic philosophy, for the relation to it had nowhere been broken off even in the Christian Middle Ages. In Augustine's teaching especially, although it arose purely from the needs and demands of faith, the basic motives of Platonic epistemology nevertheless remained clearly and transparently preserved. What is characteristic, however, is the altered point of view under which the doctrine of ideas now appears. Insofar as ideas are conceived and interpreted as absolute being beyond the world of appearances, they are expressly rejected by Cusa; here he joins the Aristotelian critique in its full extent and in all its consequences. And he no less disputes the false apriorism of “innate concepts”: what is inborn in the soul is not individual contents of knowledge, but only the entitlement to acquire them.1) Thus he does not connect himself to the metaphysical elaboration of the “idea,” but goes directly back to the profound methodological discussions of the `Republic` on the relation of sensibility and thought. Perception is not unqualified conflict, but rather the awakener and “paraclete” of the pure concept, which it calls forth in order to overcome and settle the indeterminacy lying within it.2)

That is the deeper ground and purpose of the opposition that confronts us everywhere in the sensible world: it stimulates and supports in us the capacity for distinction and thereby raises thought to consciousness of its characteristic basic power.3) For sense as such contains no power of determination and distinction; rather, it is reason that first carries out the firm delimitations and differentiations within sensible material: `ratio sensu ut instrumento ad discernenda sensibilia utitur, sed ipsa est, quae in sensu sensibile discernit`.4) Thus even the empirical world-picture, in which objects already confront us as separate individuals, is a product of the cooperation of perception and concept. The achievement of the idea is made effective within the sphere of experience itself. But by means of this opposition to medieval realism, for which the idea is in and for itself something existing and absolute, we are led to consequences that at first seem to diminish the cognitive worth of thought. If our discursive thinking has the task of sorting and interpreting sensible impressions, then it is clear that it directs itself not to the essence of things but only to their “images,” and at no point is able to get beyond these. The system of knowledge dissolves into an aggregate and order of signs; the absolute world of objects remains inaccessible to it. If objects are contained in the divine mind according to their precise and proper truth, our own mind grasps not their being but only indirectly their “likeness”;5) if God's thinking is at the same time a creating, our concept-formation occurs through our adjusting ourselves to the given objects and reshaping ourselves after them. The concept of the soul itself is determined under this point of view: it is the power that can conform itself to all things (`quae se omnibus rebus potest conformare`).6) Just as the divine mind possesses `vis entificativa`, so our intellect has `vis assimilativa` as its characteristic and basic trait;7) just as the former contains the original being-bestowing power, so the latter possesses the light-bestowing power that first illumines for us the whole of sensible images.8)

And yet it is only a mere reflected gleam of the real that can ultimately be given to us by all human inquiry. Through the creation of the conceptual sign-language in which we try to grasp being, the essence of things only wraps itself for us in ever denser veils. If, then, the truth of thought consists in nothing other than passively mirroring the relations of the real, we see ourselves, the further the work of the concept advances, the more removed from this final goal and enclosed within our own world of representations. At this point, however, where the oppositions attain their full sharpness, Cusa's dialectical power once again proves itself. The norm of truth itself now undergoes an inner transformation. The concept of likeness, which Cusa takes over as an inheritance from scholastic epistemology, becomes for him, through gradual reinterpretation and deepening, the conceptual handle and vehicle of his own basic view. From `similitudo` he advances to `assimilatio`: from the claim of a likeness present in things, which forms the basis of their grouping and generic designation, he moves to the exposition of the process by which mind must first establish and create a harmonious connection between the objects and itself. The I now cognizes objects no longer by adapting itself to them and copying them, but rather by conversely apprehending and understanding them according to the likeness of its own essence. We understand external things only insofar as we are able to rediscover in them the categories of our own thinking. All “measuring” of objects arises in the last instance from the one drive of mind to attain the measure of itself and of its powers.9) Because it bears within itself the point, the unity, the now, because it thus possesses the true ground out of which line, number, and time are constructed, the intellect can assimilate itself to all these contents and know them in this assimilation.10) It is thus called an image and analogue of the world in the sense that within it, as concentrated unity, lies the content of all that meets us in sensible appearance in the world of things. **Notes**

- This tranche carries the shift from passive `likeness` to active `assimilation` to the point where the mathematical turn becomes explicit in the next tranche.
- Cassirer is now clearly reading Cusa as a precursor not only to Copernicus but to the epistemic posture of Kepler and Galileo.
- The key move is that mind measures objects in order to arrive at the measure of itself.
- The final sentence remains open at the printed page `36` boundary and continues directly in `Part 10`.
