# Part 98: Galilei, Ancient Atomism and the Subjectivity of Sensible Qualities

Cassirer now spells out the deeper ancestry of Galilei's physics. The real kinship with Democritus lies not in atomism as a local doctrine, but in the logical demand to secure an enduring identity within phenomena. At the same time the Platonic demand for knowledge of what remains the same is carried straight onto physical nature by way of geometry. From that methodological delimitation the doctrine of sensible qualities follows directly: what belongs objectively to body is only what can be held in quantitative-spatiotemporal determination, while color, tone, taste, and smell become mere names grounded only in the sentient organism.

**English (draft)**

```text
It belongs in this sense itself entirely to scientific reason, to which it subjects a new field of objects and problems. Identity and immutability are the essential objective marks that we are accustomed to ascribe to matter as such: these very marks, however, at the same time contain the guarantee that a perfect knowledge of it, comparable to the mathematical, is to be attained.

At this point the exact connection can be shown that links Galileo with ancient speculation. Galileo's conceptual affinity with Democritus has rightly been emphasized; but it proves itself not so much in his atomism, which, however interesting and rich in problems it is, remains in the totality of the scientific system only an outer work, as rather in the logical foundations of his physics. Ancient materialism grew historically not directly out of physical observation, but out of dialectical problems and requirements. It is the Eleatic opposition of the one and the many, of thought and sense-perception, that in it was at once to be sharpened and resolved. The demand of pure concept, the claim of strict unchangeable identity, had been fixed: now it was necessary, if a science of phenomena was to be possible, to determine and interpret appearances in such a way that within them themselves something eternal and immutable was displayed and stood out. It is the same task, though grasped with incomparably greater penetration and determinacy, with which Galileo's thought begins.

For him too the general Platonic ideal of comprehension stands firm: science is possible only of what maintains itself in enduring unity. But if this thought for Plato found its full vindication solely in mathematics, the demand is now directed immediately and more strictly onto physical objects. This continuation, however, becomes at once understandable when one considers the way in which Galileo advanced to his concept of nature. In it there is not outwardly linked into unity a multiplicity of arbitrarily heaped-up facts and observations, but rather a strict delimitation and determination of experiential material according to criteria of geometry has been carried out. To nature, in the genuine scientific sense of the word, belong only the true and necessary things, which cannot possibly behave otherwise. In questions of jurisprudence or statesmanship or in every other science that deals with changeable and arbitrary enactments, it may be acceptable to surrender oneself to merely probable arguments. Physics, by contrast, which deals with a fixed and abiding object not alterable by human arbitrariness, may build itself only upon univocal and compelling grounds of proof. Physical induction as Galileo understands it is therefore from the beginning assigned the goal of preparing and clearing the way for the mathematically demonstrative derivation of appearances.

So long as this derivation has not been attained, so long have we no guarantee that we are moving in the domain of true being and not in a fabricated world of fables. The Aristotelian proposition that one must not seek and demand in natural things proofs of mathematical strictness is therefore inwardly incomprehensible to Galileo: he knows and says that with it the proper problem of his research would become a chimera. Only by way of the middle concept of geometrical connection does he arrive at the thought of the strict causal conditionedness of appearances, and only this conditionedness is what he designates by the name of nature.

Only the first conceptual beginnings of Galileo's problem-setting have hitherto met us in the foregoing reflections: but they too already contain within themselves as germ fundamental empirical results and consequences. Thus, for example, the doctrine of the subjectivity of sensible qualities is immediately contained and posited in the preceding conceptual developments; it is not attained as a derived result, but the original delimitation and definition of the field of research itself is that from which it flows. This doctrine is therefore not supported by Galileo in the first instance on determinate individual physiological facts; rather, those facts, insofar as they are drawn in, are expressly used and designated only as examples for the preceding general methodological considerations. The sensible marks of color and tone, which prove infinitely variable according to the constitution of the receiving organ, cannot belong to the domain of true being, which is to be thought as a complex of eternal and necessary constitutions and marks. It is a borrowed and fabricated reality that belongs to them, a reality that under the sharp and thoroughgoing analysis of thought must dissolve into nothing. Thus Galileo too takes over this proposition entirely in the sense in which it had been used and laid out by Democritus.

Matter or bodily substance cannot be conceived without at the same time thinking in it the marks of limitation, spatial shape, and magnitude, and further, insofar as it is to be grasped in individual determinacy, without regarding it as determined according to its spatial and temporal position as well as its state of motion. All these points of view, which can be summed up under the basic categories of number, time, and space, thus necessarily belong to its concept, from which they cannot be detached by any effort of subjective imagination. Whether it is red or white, bitter or sweet, sounding or mute, fragrant or foul-smelling, is, by contrast, without relevance for the determination of its essence; all these constitutions designate merely changing states, not conditions to which the conceptual execution of the concept is bound. Understanding and imagination (`il discorso o l'immaginazione`) are by themselves never able to lead to this second species of marks; only direct sensible perception is able to assure us of them. But with this it is proved that those qualities are to be excluded from the objective image of reality, that they are no more than mere names and have their subsistence nowhere other than in the sensing body. One need only think living beings and their organs abolished: and the world of sensible properties would at the same time be annihilated. To make oneself aware of the radical sharpness...
```

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) Cassirer's Democritus line is precise: the real kinship is logical, not simply atomistic.

2) The Platonism matters just as much. What was for Plato secured only in mathematics is now directed straight onto physical objects.

3) Nature is being constituted here by geometric criteria. That is why demonstrative derivation, not probability, becomes the aim of induction.

4) The doctrine of sensible qualities is method-driven, not primarily physiological. It follows from the object-delimitation already made.

5) Body is now thinkable only through quantitative-spatiotemporal determination; qualitative predicates survive merely as names in the sentient organism.
```
