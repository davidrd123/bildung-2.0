# Part 86: Kepler's Hypothesis and the Physical Grounding of Astronomy

Cassirer now completes the Kepler optics turn and makes its methodological payoff explicit. Empirical knowledge does not merely receive terms from sensation; it extracts mathematically graspable relations from the given and can do so only by first shaping being into a form kin to the intellect. That is why the mathematical hypothesis is not a convenient computational device, but the opening condition of scientific problem-formation. Kepler's distinction between astronomical and physical hypothesis therefore does not reintroduce occult forces beyond mathematics. It demands that astronomical formulas be grounded in universal mathematically-physical principles, so that cosmography becomes a member of cosmophysics.

Und scheint es nicht, als sollten neben und außerhalb der mathematischen Gesetzlichkeit der Bewegung wiederum verborgene Qualitäten und innere Kräfte in ihr anerkannt werden, die sich der Rechnung entziehen? Dieser Verdacht schwindet indes, sobald man die Grundtendenz der Keplerschen Unterscheidung näher erwägt und ihrer Ausführung im einzelnen nachgeht. **English (gloss)**

```text
... the logical character of relations as such is not affected by the origin of their terms. Knowledge of relations is always pure intellectual knowledge, no matter where the terms come from. This now appears as the task of empirical cognition itself: to extract from the given the pure mathematically graspable basic relations. Thought cannot seize arbitrary contents directly; it must first create a handle that renders the material accessible to intellect. We understand being only after casting it into a form kindred to our own mind. This is why the mathematical hypothesis matters so much for Kepler. It is not a technical convenience but the initial move by which the scientific problem can first be rightly posed. Patrizzi's appeal to pure observation is therefore already a misunderstanding: unless mathematical lawfulness is presupposed from the start, inquiry degenerates into miracles and mystical fictions.

To understand Kepler's theory of hypothesis one has to recall the earlier astronomical methodology. Ancient astronomy could treat hypotheses as geometrical devices for saving the appearances without asking after their empirical truth. Even the early modern attempts at reconciling geometrical and physical viewpoints did not overcome the split between describing phenomena and causally explaining them. Kepler is the first to unite the two while still distinguishing them logically. The physical complement he asks for is not a metaphysical reality behind appearances, but the lawful physical ground-relations of empirical being itself.

For a hypothesis to be true, it is not enough to compress astronomical appearances into a short formula. It must also fit our insight into the conditions of concrete natural events generally. Astronomy therefore can be grounded only together with physics; individual facts must be checked against a system of mathematically-physical principles. In this sense mechanics must provide the foundation of the true world-system, and Kepler already grasps in principle what Galileo later fulfills: cosmography becomes a part of cosmophysics.
```
