# Part 86: Kepler's Hypothesis and the Physical Grounding of Astronomy

Cassirer now completes the Kepler optics turn and makes its methodological payoff explicit. Empirical knowledge does not merely receive terms from sensation; it extracts mathematically graspable relations from the given and can do so only by first shaping being into a form kin to the intellect. That is why the mathematical hypothesis is not a convenient computational device, but the opening condition of scientific problem-formation. Kepler's distinction between astronomical and physical hypothesis therefore does not reintroduce occult forces beyond mathematics. It demands that astronomical formulas be grounded in universal mathematically-physical principles, so that cosmography becomes a member of cosmophysics.

... tions as such is not impaired thereby. Knowledge of relations always constitutes pure intellectual knowledge, no matter where the "terms" of the relation may come from. It is precisely this that now appears as the task of empirical cognition in general: that it, as it were, brings out from what is given itself the pure fundamental relations that can be grasped mathematically. Thought cannot directly seize just any content; it must first create for itself the handle that makes the material intelligible and accessible to the understanding. We understand being only after we have first cast it into a form essentially akin to that of our own spirit. The nature of the human intellect entails that everything it is to comprehend perfectly must either itself be magnitude or be mediated to it by magnitudes. Only in this general connection can the full significance be assessed that the mathematical hypothesis possesses for Kepler. For him it is not a technical aid that happens to arise in the course of research, but the beginning and point of departure by virtue of which we first clear our way to the correct posing of the scientific problem. When Patrizzi had expressed doubts as to whether the actual paths of the planets run in manifold disorderly curves or whether they are in fact wholly determinate and uniform lines that merely appear confused and irregular to imperfect sensible apprehension, Kepler already sees in this question a fundamental misunderstanding of the tasks of "philosophical" astronomy. We must not demand to learn from observation that the celestial appearances obey a strict mathematical lawfulness; rather, this is the presupposition under which our research can begin at all. Whoever doubts and misses this first demand of reason must, as with Patrizzi, have his path lead through nothing but wonders. By apparently following pure perception and seeking to keep all anticipations of thought away from it, he in truth only becomes all the more firmly entangled in arbitrary fictions and mystical assumptions.

The Concept of Hypothesis.

To understand and appreciate Kepler's theory of "hypothesis" in detail, one must call to mind the state of astronomical methodology that he found before him. From Plato comes the demand placed upon astronomers to determine those strictly ordered and uniform motions under whose presupposition the appearances in the heavens could be reproduced and "saved" (`διασώζειν τὰ περὶ τὰς κινήσεις ὁμαλῶς φαινόμενα`). Ancient astronomy sought to solve this task by representing the complicated paths of the stars as resultants of simple circular motions, at first thinking the planets as still attached to material spheres that through their revolution were supposed to carry along the body adhering to them. This latter assumption, however, could no longer do justice even in antiquity to the multiplicity of appearances. The number of fixed spheres interlocking with one another accumulated more and more - it is given by Eudoxus and Callippus as 25, by Aristotle already as 49 - without thereby achieving an exact description of the phenomena. The Alexandrian astronomers therefore already renounced any closer physical explanation of celestial appearances and were content with the geometrical elaboration of the epicycle theory, according to which the planets execute a circular motion around a center that is itself again carried around the earth on a larger "deferent" circle. The "truth" of this theory, the possibility of demonstrating the individual components into which it decomposes the motions of the stars in empirical efficacy and actuality, was not asked after: enough that a convenient methodological artifice and an aid to calculation had thereby been found by means of which the place of a planet at a determinate time could be determined with sufficient approximation.

At the beginning of the modern age an attempted reconciliation between geometrical and physical modes of consideration had then been made by Peurbach and Regiomontanus, which, although it seemed to offer a unified intuitive way of representing the matter, did not in principle overcome the defects of either view. The demand for mere "description" of the phenomena and the question of their causal "explanation" still thus stood unmediated over against one another: Kepler is the first who, while knowing how to separate both tasks sharply in logic, grasps them as one by his scientific achievement. To be sure, it might at first appear as an internal objective difficulty that he too opposes the "physical" hypothesis to the "astronomical" one, which serves only the purposes of calculation. For - one may ask in the sense of his own fundamental thoughts - is not in the functional equation in which calculation reaches its completion and goal the full being of planetary motion also exhausted in the scientific sense? And does it not seem as though, beside and outside the mathematical lawfulness of motion, hidden qualities and inner forces were once again to be acknowledged in it that withdraw from calculation? This suspicion, however, vanishes as soon as one considers more closely the basic tendency of Kepler's distinction and follows its execution in detail. The supplement demanded for mathematics remains itself entirely within the domain of phenomena: the goal is not to comprehend appearances, instead of merely calculating them, in their absolute metaphysical essence, but to lead them back to laws in which not merely the geometrical, but the physical basic relations of empirical being present themselves.

The Astronomical and the Physical Hypothesis.

For a hypothesis to be "true," it is not enough that it express the astronomical appearances alone, which after all form only a limited excerpt from our total experience, in a short formula: it must at the same time reproduce them in a way corresponding to our insight into the conditions of all concrete natural occurrence in general. The grounding of astronomy can be accomplished only in connection with the scientific grounding of physics. The truth of a determinate assumption is therefore not attested solely by the immediate confirmation it finds in individual sensible facts, but requires examination and control through a system of mathematically-physical fundamental principles. Only by being ordered into this universal connection is an appearance truly authenticated and "saved." Scientific mechanics - this is the simple thought that everywhere hovers clearly here in view - must provide the foundation of the true world-system. If this demand is only truly fulfilled in Galileo, it is already grasped by Kepler with principled sharpness: with him, for the first time, cosmography - in the thought of universal gravitation, which he is the first to grasp - has become an individual member of cosmophysics. (See below p. 362 ff.)

The historical opposition that from ancient times had existed between the mathematical and the physical hypothesis accordingly receives in Kepler a new turn. A reconciliation between the two is sought; but this reconciliation is now attempted in a different direction and with new conceptual means. Throughout the entire Middle Ages the dispute between the two fundamental methodological views that stood opposed in the theory of the scientific concept had remained fixed at one and the same point. On the one side stood the demand of the "physicists," according to which the forces governing the universe are to be determined from the nature of the things themselves, that is, in the Aristotelian sense from their purposive determination.
