# Part 90: Kepler, Force as Function, and the Arithmetic of Forces

Cassirer now gives the Kepler force-line its methodological center. Cause no longer means an inner driver hidden behind motion, but a system of mathematically determinable conditions. That move excludes supersensible interventions from physics, expels Aristotelian teleology on the same ground, and roots force in number, statics, and lawful derivability rather than in desire, entelechy, or living animation. Kepler cannot yet produce the full arithmetic of basic forces, but Cassirer now treats him as having clearly sketched the ideal of modern dynamics before the empirical gravity discussion begins.

More exact analysis then teaches us to distinguish three groups of these elements: the measure of the force belonging to a planet at a determinate part of its orbit is given by the magnitude of the radius vectors for the individual points of the orbit, by the length of the arc traversed, and by the time in which the stretch of path under consideration is covered. What is sought is not the inner driving cause of motion; rather, the concept of cause itself dissolves into a complex of mathematical conditions. We shall see how this pregnant formulation of the task determined step by step the development of Kepler's special theory of gravitation. For the moment, however, we must linger over the general speculative consequences that are already latent in the fundamental thought. The intervention of supersensible, immaterial factors in the natural process is now definitively excluded: for only in the domain of bodily extension can fixed determinations of measure, and thus the new exact concept of causality, find application and realization. Wherever spirit is supposed to act upon matter, there must - since it cannot master and command the material by a "mere gesture" - at the same time be pointed out material organs and mediations of its activity; but thereby we again become entangled in the lawfulness of magnitude of "nature," which, as a unified and unambiguous connection, renders every other sort of ground of explanation superfluous. If we imagine this total connection loosened at a single point, if we allow even the assistance of an external instance, then a geometrical "uncertainty" is thereby admitted, one that conflicts with the true divine constitution of the universe, according to which each element must be derivable from the others in demonstrative fashion.

Concept of Force and Concept of Function.

With this fundamental view purposive explanation, in its old Aristotelian sense, is at the same time banished from physics. Aristotle's physics builds itself on the basic opposition between heavy and light bodies: while the former strive toward the center of the world as their "natural place," the latter are inhabited by the opposite impulse toward absolute upward motion toward the periphery. A relation such as is here assumed between mover and moved contradicts the new fundamental insight into the logical and mathematical "homogeneity" that must exist between cause and effect: on the one side a mere point, on the other a three-dimensional body; here a structure that, like the assumed center of the world, owes its existence merely to our subjective imagination, there a physical mass with all its real determinations. The new concept of force is rooted, instead of being grounded in the vague analogy to sensible desire, in the pure law of knowledge of number: Kepler himself states that in place of Aristotle's heavenly theology and metaphysics he sets a heavenly philosophy and physics that at the same time contains within itself a new arithmetic of forces.

For the historical problematic it is characteristic here that, in order to prove the possibility of this new science, he appeals above all to the example of statics: the laws of the lever are for him the model by which he elucidates the law of the diminution of the efficacy of force with increasing distance from the center. In fact statics, in the firm scientific condition it had received through Archimedes and, in the modern period, just recently through Stevin, offered the only secure point of attachment for the new thought, which nevertheless reaches far beyond its bounds in significance and fruitfulness. It is the ideal of modern dynamics that Kepler has here sketched and prefigured. He himself was unable to fulfill the demand he set: his laws give the outline of the pure geometrical constitution of the universe without advancing to the arithmetic of the fundamental forces out of which it is formed. But what earlier became clear to us in Kepler's relation to Galileo finds here, when we consider his relation to Newton, completion and confirmation: the methodological thoughts that became scientific deed in Newton are already raised in Kepler, through the energetic pursuit of his logical fundamental principles, to clarity and determinacy. -

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) The decisive shift here is that cause becomes function. Cassirer no longer treats force as a hidden entity behind motion, but as a mathematically articulable dependency among orbital magnitudes.

2) This is why external spiritual agency is excluded. The issue is not a flat materialism; it is that any extra-worldly intervention would destroy the demonstrative derivability demanded by exact science.

3) The banishment of Aristotelian teleology follows from the same rule. Cause and effect must be homogeneous enough to enter one mathematical order.

4) The `Arithmetik der Kräfte` line matters because it roots force in number and statics rather than in entelechy or desire. Kepler still falls short of the full science he is aiming at, but the target is now unmistakable.

5) Newton appears here not as an abrupt replacement but as the later execution of a methodological demand Kepler has already made explicit.
```

**Notes**

- `Kraftbegriff und Funktionsbegriff` is the clearest local formula yet for Cassirer's recasting of cause within exact science.
