# Part 64: Campanella, Judgment-Act, Reason, and Mathematics

Campanella can no longer keep cognition at the level of passive affection alone: perception itself now includes judgment, reason becomes a real power rather than an `ens rationis`, and mathematics exposes the remaining limit of a theory that still cannot grant pure relations their own standing.

The Platonism Campanella confesses in these sentences still wholly bears the features of the medieval view; yet it is interesting to observe how even in this disguise it once again leads toward the logical sense of the doctrine of ideas. Outer things are only the occasional causes of knowledge, while its true causal ground is rather to be sought in the mind's own cognitive power. Objects neither make the human being wise nor determine his willing and acting; they designate only a direction and a "specification" that the power of thought and will in us undergoes2). Perception itself is to be thought not merely as a suffering but at the same time as a doing, since it includes a logical operation and an act of judgment that comes to our consciousness only because of the speed with which it takes place. The receptive apprehension of a content cannot be separated from its "discursive" consideration3).

With this recognition of judgment as a moment and constituent of perception itself, Campanella returns to a thought that had already pressed itself upon Renaissance logicians such as Zabarella and Fracastoro from various sides4) and that gains ever greater significance in the course of development. If he nevertheless seeks to save the unity of the foundation by subsuming this activity of understanding itself under the generic name of "sensation," the weakness of this expedient comes fully to light: for how could the intellect still be called an "imperfect sense" if it is at the same time acknowledged as the condition of every determinate and internally complete act of perception? The path Campanella has now traversed can be followed most clearly in his determination of the concept of "reason." If nominalism rejects universal concepts as fictions, as `entia rationis`, this judgment, as is now said, does not strike reason itself, whose operations and powers are rather to count fully as realities and as basic acts of knowing. The basic acts of knowing are not mere fabrications and fictions of abstraction, but genuine effective potencies: `ratio non est Ens rationis`5). In this epigrammatic sharpening the opposition of two styles of thinking that lie densely beside one another in Campanella's own doctrine is sharply and aptly designated. Nowhere in the construction of his doctrine can he dispense with the rational conditions and factors whose acknowledgment is nevertheless denied him by the beginnings of his theory of knowledge. His system would have to appear as a mere eclectic mixture, if we were not rather permitted to see in it the counteraction and struggle of two motives that found their true reconciliation only in the foundation of exact science, from which Campanella still stands far removed.

The boundary of Campanella's philosophy therefore appears nowhere more clearly than in his assessment of mathematics, which here too is the surest reflex and gauge of the theory of knowledge. The unconditional criterion-value of perception is preserved even at this point: he praises it as the decisive advantage of his doctrine that it succeeded in giving mathematics a strictly sensualist grounding that Aristotle had failed to provide. Yet the highest achievement of science, reproducing reality and its relations, remains denied to mathematics as to logic. Neither can count as cognition, but only as fragments of knowledge and handmaiden service, since they possess no distinctive "subject" of their own but borrow it only mediately from other branches of knowledge. What value could belong to a system of relations that finally leads back to empty identities, to a doctrine of mere signs and their combinations that nowhere reaches the true physical causes? As surely as every true cognition demands reduction to grounds, and as surely as the grounds of real events lie in real effects and forces, so surely is the physical mode of presentation and proof superior to the geometrical. The epicycles, the eccentric motions, the turning of the earth, and similar "ideal formations" that the mathematician devises do not acquaint us with the genuine astronomical causes and with the actual constitution of the cosmos: here and everywhere mathematics must content itself with remaining the "handmaid of physics." When the geometer abstracts from the accidental and particular properties of bodies in order to turn only to their most general quantitative determinations, this shows not his methodical art but his methodical weakness: "for he is wise who considers all things"6). Once again it is that ideal of absolute exhaustion of the scope of cognition that devalues the content of mathematics.

Kepler, who knows how to distinguish sharply and clearly the domain of physics from that of mathematics, the mere auxiliary assumption of measurement and calculation from the "true cause," nevertheless remains in his basic view of the relation between the two sciences the opposite of Campanella at every point. At this point one recognizes that it was not only an external but also a methodical limit that finally left Campanella dwelling within the astronomical worldview of the Middle Ages7).

Yet even on this problem the same general movement and development sets in that we have been able to follow in Campanella's metaphysics as a whole. Even if the concepts of geometry are nowhere realized in the empirical world surrounding us, we could not even mentally draft them if some kind of being did not nevertheless correspond to them. Our conceptual constructing demands a real substrate on which it rests. Such a basis, however, is pure absolute space, which exists as an original existence prior to the whole world of bodies that can arise only within it and under its presupposition. **Working Commentary**

```text
1) Perception no longer remains mere `Leiden`: Campanella now says it includes a logical operation and `Urteilsakt`.

2) That move makes the system's internal tension explicit. Understanding is still subsumed under `Empfindung`, but it is already being asked to function as the condition of determinate perception.

3) `Ratio non est Ens rationis` is the sharpest local formula of the shift: rational acts are treated as real powers, not as empty abstractions.

4) Mathematics then becomes the decisive stress test. Pure relations, signs, and identities still look empty to Campanella so long as they lack a real subject and causal grip on nature.

5) The partial recovery is metaphysical rather than critical: geometry is granted standing again only by being tied to an absolute real substrate, `reiner absoluter Raum`.
```

**Notes**

- Campanella now explicitly breaks with a purely passive model of perception. Judging is said to occur within perception itself, even if too quickly to be separately noticed.
- This pushes him back toward the Renaissance logical line already visible in Zabarella and Fracastoro, but he still tries to save unity by calling the intellect an `unvollkommener Sinn`.
- `Ratio non est Ens rationis` names the contradiction cleanly: rational operations are indispensable and real, even though the system's opening premises had no room to grant them independent standing.
- That same contradiction sharpens in the mathematics discussion. Relations and signs without a real subject or physical cause still count only as auxiliary fragments, not as full cognition.
- Cassirer treats this as a genuine methodological limit, not just a bad opinion about astronomy: it helps explain why Campanella remains bound to the medieval cosmological frame.
- Geometry is then partially rescued only by ontologizing its basis. Absolute space becomes the real substrate that can underwrite ideal constructions.
- The tranche stops mid-sentence on printed page `255`, with the new interpenetration of ideal and real just beginning to be spelled out.
