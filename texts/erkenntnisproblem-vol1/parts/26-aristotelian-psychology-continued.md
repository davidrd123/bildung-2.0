Within Aristotelian psychology the opposition is reflected in the double position that `understanding`, to which the cognition of universal principles is assigned, retains over against the other powers of the soul. Whereas as `passive intellect`, as `nous pathetikos`, it has only to receive and gather together the material that sense and imagination offer it, as active intellect it is supposed to be free from this conditionedness: whereas according to the continuous sequence of the psychological powers thought can exercise itself only upon the `phantasma`, upon the representational image, there now appears an `other kind` of psychic activity that apprehends intelligible and universal objects purely and without admixture. Independent of the sensible, active thinking-power is supposed to possess a being of its own and sufficient unto itself. The natural conditions that regulate the arising and course of organic life lose their force with respect to this part of the soul. Just as intellect enters `from outside` (`thyrathen`) into the individual body in finished and completed form, so too it is supposed to outlast the existence of the body and to persist beyond its bounds. It is the eternal and `divine` principle that indeed binds itself to matter in common existence, but that is not touched or determined in its essence by matter.

Here we recognize the deeper objective motives out of which the Peripatetic doctrine of active intellect arose. Metaphysical psychology is meant to restore and secure once again for thought that spontaneous efficacy which seems denied it by the genetic point of view of the theory of knowledge. **Notes**

- Cassirer now makes the system-level contradiction explicit: Aristotle's developmental psychology binds thought to sensation and representation, while his concept of `episteme` still demands universality that cannot be grounded in the individual thing.
- The active/passive intellect split is the local expression of that contradiction. The `passive intellect` remains continuous with sense and `phantasma`, while the `active intellect` is forced into a separate and self-sufficient status.
- Cassirer’s criticism is not that Aristotle simply retained Platonism, but that he reintroduced a stronger dualism at the level of soul precisely where he had rejected Plato’s separation at the level of Ideas.
- Averroism radicalizes the move: the active intellect becomes one impersonal thinking power in which individuals participate, so that cognition gains dignity by exceeding individuality rather than articulating it.
- The Malebranche comparison is introduced only to be limited. Cassirer’s point is historical and structural: Averroism cannot be understood from within a modern primacy of knowing, because it still begins from a primacy of being.
- The tranche stops mid-word exactly where Cassirer is about to name that reversal more directly.
