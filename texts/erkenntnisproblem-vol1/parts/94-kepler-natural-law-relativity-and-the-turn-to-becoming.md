# Part 94: Kepler, Natural Law, Relativity, and the Turn to Becoming

Cassirer now gives the Kepler law line its broadest reach. Fabricius still wants constancy in circular form, but Kepler relocates constancy into the principles of force and function. Law no longer means fixed figure; it means a rule binding variation itself. That shift then widens into the unity of nature, the exclusion of celestial exceptions, and the first terminologically explicit use of `Naturgesetz`. Finally the cause-problem itself is recast: modern physics no longer seeks the metaphysical ground of being, but lawful explanation of becoming.

... borator and who, like him, directed his research above all to the law of the motion of Mars, shows palpably and clearly that Kepler's discovery could succeed only because he was no longer bound by the logical fetters that the former could not strip off. Fabricius objects that the rule of planetary motion has not been found so long as the curve in which we represent it entails a variable distance from the sun and thus a variable velocity: even if experience should seem favorable to the hypothesis of the ellipse, one should therefore not desist until this "irregularity" has been shown to be mere appearance and reduced to constant circular motions. For Kepler, however, as he replies sharply and pregnantly, the constancy sought no longer lies in the shape of the path, but in the principles of his mechanics and physics: constant is the action of the attracting force, constant the "magnetic" power of the sun, although according to its nature it is due for each point of the orbit a different numerical value. The univocal law of function, which "ties together as in one bond" the complex of the infinitely possible variations, binds and determines the path of the planets more surely than the fictitious heavenly circles could ever have done. What is truly given to us are solely the varying distances; we may not abolish and displace this fundamental phenomenon by auxiliary hypotheses, but must recognize and express it as unity in its multiplicity itself. The striving for the "uniformity" of nature remains in force: but we no longer seek it in fixed geometrical figures, but in that original "arithmetic of forces," no longer in the result, but in the conceptual components. In this sense Kepler adduces against Fabricius the Platonic saying of unity and plurality: how could there be a true unity that did not contain plurality within itself and hold it as its origin? The concept of change reveals to us the being and life of nature; to banish nonuniformity from the appearances as well would mean to abolish physics as a science.

Yet the new fundamental thought unfolds to full determinacy only in its apparent dialectical reversal: for just as modern cognition here had to grasp and justify nonuniformity logically, so at another point it must contest and reject it. Old physics rests on the intuition of the opposition between the earthly and heavenly sphere, on the conflict between the immutability of the heavenly bodies and the sublunary world as the stage of change and transience. Over against this dualism of two physical worlds there now constitutes itself the unity of the new concept of nature. The thought of law in its new formulation indeed demands plurality, but on the other hand excludes "exception" from itself: it is one and the same constitution of the universe that confronts us uniformly in each of its points. We may therefore proceed from a given single appearance - for example from the phenomenon of terrestrial gravity - and still be certain of possessing and holding fast in it an example of universally valid cosmic relations.

At bottom this view is only another expression of the thought of thoroughgoing relativity, as it had gradually come to recognition and implementation through the new astronomical world-system. Just as we are here instructed that no point of space belongs any absolutely distinguishing peculiarity and special position, that it is therefore in itself indifferent from which place we wish to set out in order to sketch and construct the total lawfulness of the universe, so too in the domain of physics no fixed and unconditional boundary line can anywhere exist; so too here every part must in the same way represent and present the fundamental rule of the whole. Ptolemy had explained in the `Almagest`, the basic work of older astronomy, that we must not seek in earthly appearances the decision about what in the heavens is to count as simple and natural: for where there is a diametrical opposition of objects and substances, there the same standard of judgment cannot be applied. Over against this view Kepler emphasizes that the "examples" for the principle of celestial motion lie everywhere directly before our eyes in everyday and familiar phenomena. It means overstraining the difference that arises from the peculiarity of the objects, it means uprooting the force of science and of astronomical hypothesis, if one believes that a principle that for us, for the judgment of reason and geometry, is "simple" and fundamental could lose this validity and significance when measured by the cosmic phenomena. Thus we see how in the concept of natural law, which is here for the first time used in terminological determinacy to designate Kepler's three fundamental rules, the initial geometrical point of view widens: by the fact that the moment of change is taken up here, while that of universal and necessary validity is nevertheless firmly maintained, the "idea" is thereby for the first time discovered and made fruitful for the domain of concrete empirical reality. More and more clearly the guidelines of Kepler's thought point forward to a goal that he himself no longer reached; more and more urgently they demand their unified gathering together in the logic of Galilean physics.

The concept of cause also now participates in the new development that the concept of law has undergone. The `Mysterium Cosmographicum` still quite entirely sets itself the task of unraveling the causes of the "being" and the resting constitution of the universe: the number, order, and magnitude of the heavenly bodies are to be led back to their true origin. It is not to suffice empirically to grasp all these relations; rather, an "a priori" ground is demanded that makes them derivable from a "metaphysical" principle. Modern physics has recognized the insolubility of this demand; it has rejected Kepler's point of departure and way of posing the question. The domain it marks out for its research is not being but becoming; it demands explanation and insight from grounds not for the total stock of the universe, but only for each change that occurs within it. **Working Commentary**

```text
1) Fabricius helps clarify that the real break is not ellipse versus circle by itself, but law in the principles versus constancy in the visible figure.

2) The force/function line now matures into a law concept: variation does not negate unity, but is the very medium through which unity is expressed.

3) The new unity of nature is built on the same relativity principle already earned astronomically. No privileged place, no privileged physical region, no celestial exception.

4) Cassirer is especially explicit here that `Naturgesetz` becomes terminologically stable only once change and necessity are thought together.

5) The cause-problem is now being turned away from ontology toward process. That is one of the clearest signs yet that the Kepler line is leaning toward Galileian physics.
```

**Notes**

- `Die Konstanz der Naturgesetze` is not a retreat to simple sameness; it is constancy through lawful variation.
- The line from terrestrial gravity to cosmic law is one of the strongest local statements of unified nature in the whole Kepler run.
- `Der Grund des Seins und das Gesetz des Werdens` opens a real transition surface into the Galileo section.
