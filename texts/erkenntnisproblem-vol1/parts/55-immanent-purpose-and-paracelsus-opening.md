A new expression of this basic view is found in the discussion and critique of the Aristotelian concept of purpose. However much Aristotle's view of nature demands that the "forms" of things be grasped as the immanent ends toward which they strive, this insight is nevertheless called into question again by the way his metaphysics is completed. For here the "unmoved mover" appears as the ultimate ground and the final end of all that happens, leading an independent existence outside the world and untouched by its being and becoming. The end of development thus no longer lies in the self-realization of the inner essence of things, but in a transcendent being: the concept of the world has lost its self-sufficiency to the concept of God. It is to this inner conflict of the system that Telesio's polemic and reform attach themselves. If all transformation of beings does not come to completion within themselves, if an external goal points out their way and direction, then from this point of view the worth and independence of individual beings vanish. The forms, insofar as they are meant to signify individual principles of growth and development, become idle fictions: all their efficacy dissolves into that of the one all-encompassing primordial being. If the natural things themselves ultimately possess no moving force and no impulse to change, if all their seeming activity is therefore in truth mere passivity, then we lack every means of distinguishing them from one another and asserting them as separate substances`1).

Only the immanence of purpose can restore to being both its independence and its fullness and articulated multiplicity. Each genus is created for its own sake and possesses within itself the center of its existence and efficacy, although all are connected with one another in a steady sequence of stages and point toward one another. From metals to plants, from these to the lower animals and the fishes and birds, higher still to the mammals and human beings, there leads a steadily advancing bodily organization, to which we must think correspondingly assigned an ever richer unfolding in the grades of consciousness`2).` The concept of the world-organism forbids thinking any individual species simply as a means for the ends of a higher one: it demands that in every being, however limited, we find the total law fully embodied. In place of the external consideration of purpose steps the recognition of the thoroughgoing harmony in the structure and development of all living beings. Thus from the transformation of individual plant species into one another we can infer the same process in the whole domain of the organic; thus certain ontogenetic processes, such as the development of the caterpillar into the butterfly, permit us an insight into the principled possibility of the transformation of animal species. Such thoughts, as are found for example in Vanini`1),` still appear, to be sure, disconnected and intermixed with adventurous analogies and speculations; yet they nevertheless point back to a comprehensive spiritual movement. Compared with the medieval view, the concept of nature acquires a deeper meaning. Man is created not immediately as an image of the Godhead, but after the pattern of the world, thus as an "image of the image," as Agrippa von Nettesheim says; only indirectly, therefore, only through the mediation of the whole organic reality, can he come to know himself and grasp his relation to the "absolute"`2).` Already the late scholastic period had pointed toward this thought in Raimund of Sabunde, and it was then worked out and carried further in the school of Nicholas of Cusa: now it grows in Paracelsus's system of nature into central significance.

Paracelsus's basic view of the reciprocal correspondence of microcosm and macrocosm presupposes a new relation between the spiritual and the natural. The isolated consideration of man must necessarily lead into darkness and error; all the light we can gain about his nature shines back solely from the knowledge of the unified life of nature as a whole. **Notes**

- Patrizzi's quotation now closes one line of critique by reducing Aristotelian form to the concrete qualities and operations that actually explain a body's being.
- Telesio's attack on the `unbewegte Beweger` gives the teleological reversal its sharper form: only an immanent end can preserve plurality, independence, and articulated richness within nature.
- The `Weltorganismus` concept here becomes explicitly anti-instrumental: no species is merely a means for a higher one, because each limited being embodies the total law in its own way.
- The Agrippa/Paracelsus line shifts self-knowledge away from direct introspection: man is an `image of the image`, knowable only through the mediation of the whole organic reality.
