# Part 95: From Kepler to Galilei: Experience and the Fight Against Syllogism

The transition to Galilei is now explicit. Kepler's late self-correction clears away a whole class of false ontological questions about why the universe has this orientation rather than another; the world is not a selectable specimen among possibilities but the one lawful whole given in experience. From there Cassirer turns to the Kepler-Galilei correspondence. What matters first is not a finished theory, but a shared scientific character: trust in observation, immediate recognition of method in style, and a common front against the school logic that would rather dispute the planets away than look through the telescope.

3. Galilei.

In der Geschichte des modernen Geistes gehört der Briefwechsel zwischen Kepler und Galilei zu den anziehendsten und charakteristischsten literarischen Zeugnissen. Die Kraft des neuen wissenschaftlichen Bewußtseins und die sittliche Rückwirkung, die aus ihm floß, stellt sich hier an einem vollendeten Beispiel dar. Es bleibt für immer denkwürdig, wie die beiden Begründer der mathematischen Naturwissenschaft trotz aller äußeren Einwirkungen und Intriguen, die sie zu entzweien trachten, sich alsbald in dem gleichen sachlichen Ziele und dem gleichen philosophischen Eros zusammenfinden. Der Briefwechsel knüpft an Galileis Entdeckung des Fernrohrs und an die neuen Himmelsbeobachtungen an, die sich unmittelbar an sie anschlossen. Wir sehen, wie diese Beobachtungen, in denen Galilei selbst die letzte empirische Bestätigung für die Wahrheit des neuen Weltsystems sieht, von allen Seiten und mit allen Mitteln bekämpft werden, wie selbst der Zweifel an der subjektiven Wahrhaftigkeit Galileis sich hervorwagt. Nicht nur die Gegner der Copernikanischen Lehre, auch ihre festesten und frühesten Anhänger, wie Keplers Tübinger Lehrer Maestlin, sind in diesem Verwerfungsurteil einig. An Kepler selbst drängt sich immer von neuem die Zumutung heran, sich bestimmt gegen die neuen Ergebnisse zu erklären; ja ein früherer Schüler von ihm, Martin Horky, glaubt sich durch eine Schmähschrift, die er gegen Galilei richtet, seinen Beifall verdienen zu können.

Sogleich indes bricht Kepler jede Gemeinschaft mit ihm ab: nichts könne ihm schmerzlicher sein - schreibt er in seinem ersten Briefe - als das Lob eines Mannes, der in seiner Beurteilung Galileis sein Unvermögen, wahre geistige Größe zu schätzen, so offen bekundet habe. Und es bleibt nicht bei dieser persönlichen Annäherung: sondern es drängt ihn dazu, öffentlich für den Charakter Galileis und für die Wahrheit seiner Beobachtungen Zeugnis abzulegen. Er setzt sofort sein ganzes wissenschaftliches Ansehen für dieses Ziel ein: noch ehe er das neue Instrument mit eigenen Augen geprüft hat, ist er - der als der Begründer der modernen Optik die theoretischen Grundlagen der Entdeckung sogleich durchschaut - von seinem Werte überzeugt und sucht ihn gegen die "grämlichen Krittler des Neuen", denen alles unerhört heißt, was über die Enge des Aristotelischen Systems hinausliegt, zu verteidigen. In der schlichten Sachlichkeit und Wahrhaftigkeit, die den Grundzug seines persönlichen und wissenschaftlichen Wesens bildet, gesteht er sogleich eigene, ältere Irrtümer ein, die nunmehr durch die neuen Erfahrungen berichtigt seien. Ein Zweifel an diesen aber kann ihm nicht aufkommen: schon der Stil Galileis bürgt ihm für ihre Sicherheit. Der Stil Galileis: das ist der Ausdruck und Reflex der methodischen Denkart, die Kepler als die seinige wiedererkennt.

Diese Gemeinsamkeit bewährt sich zunächst in negativer Richtung in der Stellung, die Kepler sowohl wie Galilei zum herrschenden Schulsystem einnehmen. Es ist literarisch interessant zu verfolgen, wie beide sich gegenseitig die Waffen schmieden und darreichen, mit denen sie die Überlieferung bekämpfen: wie hier ein bezeichnendes Argument, das der Eine prägt, von dem Andern aufgegriffen und weitergeführt wird, wie dort eine epigrammatische Wendung des Einen noch nach Jahren in den Schriften des Andern fortklingt und nachwirkt. In solchen Zusammenhängen enthüllt sich uns gleichsam die Stilgeschichte der neueren Wissenschaft. Einig sind beide vor allem in dem Streit gegen die Syllogistik und ihren Geltungsanspruch. "Ich danke Dir - schreibt Galilei an Kepler - daß Du, wie es von der Schärfe und dem Freimut Deines Geistes nicht anders zu erwarten war, schon nach dem ersten kurzen Einblick, den Du in meine Forschungen genommen, zuerst, ja fast als Einziger, meinen Behauptungen vollen Glauben beigemessen hast. Was aber wirst Du zu den ersten Philosophen unserer hiesigen Hochschule sagen, die trotz tausendfacher Aufforderungen in eiserner Hartnäckigkeit sich dagegen sträubten, jemals die Planeten oder den Mond oder das Fernglas selbst zu betrachten und die somit ihr Auge mit Gewalt gegen das Licht der Wahrheit verschlossen? ... ```text
... sees how he too took a positive part in this process, which seems directly opposed to his beginnings. A whole series of problems that the `Mysterium Cosmographicum` had posed are removed with clear consciousness by the elucidations to the work, separated from it by a period of twenty-five years. "I am not the first," it says there, "whom the useless question has tormented why the zodiac has been placed in a determinate location of space, since infinitely many other positions could have been assigned to it. A similar problem is found in Aristotle: why do the planets move in one determinate direction rather than the opposite? ... to which he himself gives the answer that nature always chooses the best among the possible cases, and that it is better and more excellent for the heavenly bodies to move forward than retrograde. A foolish answer; for before motion, before the bodies existed, there was no difference of sense and direction, there was no forward and backward." Nor does the appeal to analogies of the universe with a living being whose structure is prescribed to it for reasons of organic preservation lead any further here: in the structure of the human being, for example, the question would have to repeat itself anew why his limbs have received their determinate actual arrangement and not the reversed arrangement that they possess in the mirror image. One is immediately reminded here of the related problems discussed in the correspondence between Leibniz and Clarke, and indeed still persisting as far as Kant's essay on the "Difference of Regions in Space." But the answer Kepler gives testifies to the principled insight he has now attained: it is idle to think the world as one among various "possibilities" and to apply to it the point of view of choice, since it is given to us in its totality not as an exemplar of a generic concept, but only once in unambiguous empirical determinacy and lawfulness: `comparatio locum non habet mundorum, ubi unus solus est`. Thus Kepler at last found against false ontological ways of questioning the apt defense, because in the last ground it is the unity of experience and its principles that he is defending.

3. Galilei.

In the history of the modern spirit, the correspondence between Kepler and Galileo belongs among the most attractive and characteristic literary testimonies. The power of the new scientific consciousness and the ethical repercussion flowing from it presents itself here in a perfected example. It remains memorable forever how the two founders of mathematical natural science, despite all the outward influences and intrigues that sought to set them at odds, at once find one another in the same objective goal and the same philosophical eros. The correspondence ties itself to Galileo's discovery of the telescope and to the new heavenly observations that immediately followed upon it. We see how these observations, in which Galileo himself sees the final empirical confirmation of the truth of the new world-system, are attacked from all sides and by all means, how even doubt in Galileo's subjective truthfulness ventures forth. Not only the opponents of the Copernican doctrine, but also its firmest and earliest adherents, such as Kepler's Tuebingen teacher Maestlin, are united in this verdict of rejection. Kepler himself is again and again pressed with the demand to declare himself definitively against the new results; indeed, a former pupil of his, Martin Horky, believes he can earn his approval by a lampoon directed against Galileo.

Kepler at once, however, breaks off every association with him: nothing, he writes in his first letter, could be more painful to him than the praise of a man who in his judgment of Galileo had so openly manifested his incapacity to esteem true spiritual greatness. And it does not remain with this personal approach: rather, it impels him publicly to bear witness for Galileo's character and for the truth of his observations. He immediately stakes his whole scientific reputation on this aim: even before he has examined the new instrument with his own eyes, he - as founder of modern optics, who at once sees through the theoretical foundations of the discovery - is convinced of its value and seeks to defend it against the "peevish carpers at the new," to whom everything is unheard of that extends beyond the narrowness of the Aristotelian system. In the simple objectivity and truthfulness that forms the basic trait of his personal and scientific being, he at once confesses older errors of his own, which are now corrected by the new experiences. But doubt of these cannot arise for him: Galileo's style already guarantees him their certainty. Galileo's style: that is the expression and reflection of the methodological cast of mind that Kepler recognizes as his own.

The struggle against syllogistics.

This commonality proves itself at first in a negative direction in the position that both Kepler and Galileo take toward the reigning school-system. It is literarily interesting to follow how both forge and hand one another the weapons with which they fight tradition: how here a characteristic argument coined by the one is taken up and carried further by the other, how there an epigrammatic turn of the one still sounds on and has its effect years later in the writings of the other. In such connections there reveals itself to us, as it were, the history of style of modern science. Both are united above all in the struggle against syllogistics and its claim to validity. "I thank you," Galileo writes to Kepler, "that you, as was only to be expected from the sharpness and frankness of your spirit, already after the first brief glimpse you had taken of my researches, were the first, indeed almost the only one, to give full credence to my assertions. But what will you say to the first philosophers of our local university, who despite a thousand invitations obstinately refused ever to look at the planets or the moon or the telescope itself, and who thus by force closed their eyes to the light of truth? ... This sort of people believes that philosophy is a book, like the `Aeneid` or the `Odyssey`: and that truth is to be investigated not in the world or in nature, but through the comparison of texts. How you would laugh if you could hear how the most esteemed philosopher of our university labored to dispute the new planets away from heaven and tear them loose, as though by magical formulas, through logical arguments." How faithful and typical this description is, of that we are instructed step by step in Kepler's discussions with the Aristotelians of the age. It is especially characteristic when Chiaramonti, in a dispute over the nature of comets, thinks he can dismiss Kepler with the reproach that he has confused the `methodus arguendi` with the `methodus respondendi`, that he has applied the procedure of mathematics to topical logic, and so forth. From the historical problematic illuminated by these examples one understands how logic must more and more be felt as the true opposition and the proper hindrance of empirical research.
