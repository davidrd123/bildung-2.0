Auch die Auffassung der `sittlichen` Natur des Menschen gewinnt jetzt eine neue Freiheit. Nicht ein jenseitiges Sein und eine jenseitige Vorherbestimmung, sondern der empirische Charakter des Menschen und die sittlichen Einflüsse, die auf ihn einwirken, sind es, die sein Wollen und sein Tun bedingen. Nicht am Himmel, sondern in sich selbst muß der Einzelne den Grund seines Geschickes lesen: die Seele ist des Menschen Dämon. Every particular event is a sign and a representation of the total law; all members of the universe thus stand in an original harmonious accord and point to one another. The full unfolding of this basic view is given in magic, with which astrology is everywhere closely twinned. Here the symbol, and above all the word, counts as the source of a natural effect that intervenes directly in things and determines them accordingly. No less, the numbers and geometrical figures in which the measures and harmonies of the cosmos are mirrored are at the same time endowed with inner forces thanks to which we are able not only to cognize objects, but at the same time to subject them to our will. In this fusion and indifference of causes and symbols lies the proper character of the magical as of the astrological basic view. And it is here above all that Pico's writing now intervenes decisively. Heaven, it declares, can signify and foreshow only what it itself actively brings forth. A sharp and clear distinction is drawn between the `signs` nature itself presents to us and those that spring only from human arbitrariness. Genuine natural signs belong to the corporeal world and fall under its laws: they are either themselves the causes of the event to which they point, or its effects. And everywhere that this immediate connection is excluded, there still exists at least a mediated causal linkage insofar as sign and signified then both stem from one and the same common cause and are mutually determined in their relation through reference back to it. If someone thinks he must add here the case that the relevant effect is indeed not brought forth by the event we are considering, but is nonetheless necessarily and inseparably connected with it, this is a grave deception: for this connection and concord can be established in no other way than by one process acting upon the other, or undergoing an action from it, or finally by both being rooted in a common cause that calls them simultaneously and together into existence.1)

In place of the arbitrary inference by analogy, which transfers a relation it finds in some part of reality immediately to other remote elements of being, there now steps the strict demand to comprehend the course of events according to their univocal and continuous causal nexus. With this thought, however, Pico grows far beyond the view of nature characteristic of the Quattrocento that is expressed in all his other writings. Here he truly becomes a guide and teacher of the age that follows, so much so that Kepler in his critique of astrology appeals to him as a predecessor.2) The heavenly bodies possess no `obscure qualities` by virtue of which they would be able to bring about secret effects in the earthly world; rather, all such gifts and capacities flow from the inner principles and forms of bodies themselves. All influence the upper spheres exercise upon us manifests itself not in supersensible operations, but in the natural powers of light and warmth.3) Astrology, by contrast, measures the efficacy of the planets not according to the real relations of their spatial distance, but according to their position in the various `signs` and `houses` of heaven; it thereby makes an arbitrary fiction, created only for the purpose of convenient division, into the condition and standard of actual natural events. The concepts introduced by mathematicians as necessary means and methods of measurement are misused for the prediction of the future as though they were themselves objects of nature and endowed with real forces.1)

The conception of the `moral` nature of man now likewise gains a new freedom. It is not a transcendent being and a transcendent predestination, but the empirical character of man and the moral influences acting upon him that condition his willing and doing. The individual must read the ground of his fate not in heaven, but in himself: the soul is man's daemon. Thus a great thinker like Aristotle owes his achievements and his talent not to the star under which he was born, but to his own genius, which he received immediately from God: `sortitus erat non astrum melius, sed ingenium melius; nec ingenium ab astro, si quidem incorporale, sed a Deo sicut corpus a patre, non a coelo`.2) Yet the more strictly the thought of compulsion by alien and external powers is rejected, the clearer the light now falls upon the psychological, indeed even bodily causes that limit our action. The thought of freedom, as it is understood here, is therefore not the opposite, but the correlate of the thought of empirical causation. Its meaning and tendency come out more determinately in Pico's oration on the `Dignity of Man`. Here we possess the positive supplement and fulfillment of the thoughts that have so far confronted us in polemical form and turn. To designate the proper precedence of man, it is, as is here stated, not enough to see in him the connecting link that joins high and low, the sensible and the intelligible world, and thus makes him the mediator and interpreter of the cosmos. For however distinguished and central a significance may here seem to be ascribed to him, it still falls short of his proper worth so long as one sees it only as given from without, not as chosen and acquired by him himself. The unique value of the individual is rooted in the fact that he is not, like other things, bound to a single fixed place in the cosmos, but determines his own position in the universe and assigns to himself the standpoint of his contemplation. `It is given into his own hand what kind of existence and life he will belong to. "Into the middle of the world," thus the Creator speaks to Adam, "have I placed thee, so that thou mayest the more easily look around thee and behold all that is therein. I created thee as a being neither heavenly nor earthly, neither mortal nor immortal, so that thou thyself as thine own free shaper and vanquisher mayest give to thyself and stamp upon thyself thy form. Thou canst degenerate into the beast or in self-active resolve be reborn into the divine. The animals bring with them from the womb what they are to have; the higher spirits are from the beginning, or soon thereafter, what they shall remain for eternity. Thou alone hast a development, a growth according to free will; thou hast in thee the seeds of a life of every kind."`1)

**Notes**

- Cassirer now names the decisive move in Pico's anti-astrology critique: the replacement of analogical transfer by the demand for a strict causal nexus.
- The attack on astrology is therefore not only anti-superstitious. It is also a methodological purification of the concept of sign: a sign must be either cause, effect, or jointly rooted with what it signifies in a common cause.
- This is why Cassirer can make Pico a precursor for Kepler. The issue is not merely rejection of hidden astral powers, but the refusal to treat mathematical or classificatory devices as if they were natural forces.
- Cassirer's wording is slightly narrower than the later scholastic tag `qualitates occultae`: he writes `dunklen Qualitäten`, so the draft keeps the source adjective with `obscure qualities` while preserving the allusion.
- The moral payoff matters just as much as the physical one. Freedom is not posed against causality, but as its correlate: once fate is no longer read in the stars, empirical character and moral formation become the real conditions of action.
- Cassirer then ties this directly to the `Oration on the Dignity of Man`: human worth lies not in a fixed cosmological middle position, but in self-determination of one's standpoint and form of life.
- `[visual candidate]` Pico's causal test for genuine signs is diagrammable as a three-part filter: sign as cause, sign as effect, or sign and signified as joint effects of a common cause.
- The tranche stops mid-sentence exactly where Cassirer is about to generalize this new fusion of stricter natural causality and the value of moral personality across the Renaissance as a whole.
