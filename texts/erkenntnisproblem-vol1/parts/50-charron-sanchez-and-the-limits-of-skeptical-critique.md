Bodin gehört demselben Kreise, wie Montaigne und Charron an; auch bei ihm sind es die französischen Religionskriege, die den politischen Hintergrund bilden. Montaigne ist es, der als einer der Ersten sein literarisches Verdienst anerkennt und der ihn von der `Schar der Skribenten der Zeit` ausdrücklich und energisch unterscheidet (Essais II, 32). Auch hier sehen wir somit, wie die Skepsis sich der religiösen Gesamtbewegung der Zeit innerlich einordnet. Goethe hat in Montaignes Essais, in der öffentlichen Beichte, die hier der Einzelne vor aller Welt ablegt, eine Hindeutung auf den Protestantismus gesehen: ein Zusammenhang, der von Montaigne selbst unmittelbar bestätigt wird. `En faveur des huguenots, qui accusent nostre confession auriculaire et privée, je me confesse en public, religieusement et purement` (III, 5). Nicht auf der Macht der Heilsmittel, sondern einzig auf der Kraft und Reinheit der inneren Gesinnung muß das echte religiöse Vertrauen ruhen: wo immer es anders ist, da wird die Gottheit in einen Dämon verwandelt, den man durch magische Zaubermittel zu zwingen vermeint.

Stehen hier die Probleme der Geisteswissenschaft im Mittelpunkt der Betrachtung, so ist bei Sanchez, dessen Werk `Quod nihil scitur` unabhängig von Montaigne entstanden ist, die Skepsis wiederum an den Problemen der Naturerkenntnis entstanden und auf sie bezogen. Der Zweifel, so unbedingt er auftritt, trifft hier dennoch in erster Linie die bestimmte Form der Schulwissenschaft, der Sanchez sich gegenüber sieht. Die Syllogistik ist es, gegen die sich seine Angriffe in erster Linie richten; von ihren Quaestionen und Distinktionen, durch die uns nur Namen und Namen der Namen gegeben werden, will er zur Erforschung der Dinge und ihrer Ursachen zurückleiten. Und wie hier der Dialektik gegenüber auf die Wahrnehmung und Beobachtung zurückgegangen wird, so wird allgemein daran festgehalten, daß die echte Gewißheit, sofern es eine solche gibt, dem `diskursiven` Denken entzogen und auf einen Akt des unmittelbaren, intuitiven Schauens gegründet sein muß. Aber freilich vermag auch diese Rückwendung auf das Selbstbewußtsein uns keinen festen und dauernden Halt zu gewähren: denn wenn das Selbst alle anderen Inhalte an Gewißheit überragt, so ist es ihnen doch, was die Bestimmtheit der Anschauung betrifft, unterlegen. Bodin belongs to the same circle as Montaigne and Charron; with him too the French wars of religion form the political background. It is Montaigne who, as one of the first, recognizes his literary merit and expressly and energetically distinguishes him from the `crowd of scribblers of the age` (Essais II, 32). As for Charron, there are likewise definite traces that he at least knew and used his work `On the State`. Deeper and more significant, however, is the connection in their fundamental religious outlook: what Charron sets forth as a general demand had already been expounded by Bodin in detail and directly placed before the eyes, as he let the individual positive religions speak through representatives of definite personal character, with a wealth of learned knowledge and penetrating dialectical art.

Here too, then, we see how skepticism inwardly orders itself into the total religious movement of the age. Goethe saw in Montaigne's `Essais`, in the public confession that the individual here lays before the whole world, an intimation of Protestantism: a connection that is directly confirmed by Montaigne himself. `In favor of the Huguenots, who accuse our auricular and private confession, I confess myself in public, religiously and purely` (III, 5). This relation is drawn even tighter in the well-known chapter of the `Essais` on prayer, when the religious worthlessness of every external ceremony is emphasized there and the moral validity of prayer is made dependent solely on the transformation and `reformation` of the inner self that represents itself within it. Genuine religious trust must rest not upon the power of the means of salvation, but solely upon the force and purity of inward disposition: wherever it is otherwise, the deity is transformed into a demon that one imagines one can compel by magical charms.

Doubt, however unconditionally it appears, nevertheless strikes here in the first instance the determinate form of school-science that Sanchez has before him. It is syllogistics against which his attacks are primarily directed; from its quaestiones and distinctiones, through which we are given only names and names of names, he wants to lead back to the investigation of things and their causes. And just as one here returns, over against dialectic, to perception and observation, so it is generally maintained that genuine certainty, if there is such a thing, must be withdrawn from `discursive` thought and grounded on an act of immediate, intuitive seeing. In such inward self-apprehension we first recognize the self and its operations. Yet even this return upon self-consciousness cannot provide us with any firm and lasting support; for if the self surpasses all other contents in certainty, it is nevertheless inferior to them with respect to determinacy of intuition. Here too we remain caught in a twilight: since no images and species of the inner processes are given to us, we can indeed establish their being, but can scarcely designate, much less comprehend, their essence and peculiar character.1) Thus we see how the proposition of the self-certainty of the I is here crossed by the other view according to which all cognition is supposed to be conditioned by outer signs and images detaching themselves from objects. **Notes**

- Charron's first full development makes the ethical autonomy thesis explicit: divine law, nature, and reason are drawn together under moral self-legislation, while confessional belonging is denied any authority to determine worth.
- Cassirer then binds skepticism back into the religious movement of the age rather than placing it simply outside religion. Bodin and Protestantism are the key mediations here.
- The Sanchez turn matters because skepticism shifts back from religion and the human sciences to nature-knowledge, anti-syllogistic method, and the demand for intuitive certainty.
- Yet Sanchez also exposes a new limit. The appeal to self-certainty cannot stabilize knowledge as long as inner life lacks the determinacy granted to outer images and signs.
- The magnetism example is a precise historical hinge: skepticism registers the instability of explanation just before Gilbert turns the same field into a site of exact mathematical research.
- La Mothe le Vayer opens as a later terminal case in which skeptical anthropology accumulates material but lacks a principle of selection and criticism.
- The tranche stops at the printed page `199` boundary, with Cassirer breaking mid-critique of La Mothe le Vayer's evidentiary method.
