In these expositions of Campanella, the very problem that later condensed into the metaphysical opposition of occasionalism and pre-established harmony is already precisely marked out 1). In general, the concept of force here begins to assume that more determinate and sharper form in which it is later set by Leibniz against the `bare faculty` of the scholastics. An inner ambiguity clings to the Aristotelian concept of `potency` from its very origin. In conceiving the concept of absolute matter, the concept of a matter without any determination of form whatever, Aristotle thereby transformed a pure boundary concept of thought into a metaphysical determination of essence. A thought springing merely from a logical negation becomes a real factor of becoming and of natural events. Scholasticism attempts in vain to find a reconciliation in this conflict: it can do no more than alternate between the two points of view designated by it. Potency therefore appears to it now as the possible in the sense of a merely abstract relational concept, now as a self-subsistent something of its own that of itself strives toward realization. The dispute of the Alexandrists and Averroists had centered essentially on this question: is matter bare `possibility`, is it merely

the passive ground of change, awaiting all shaping from the forms that approach it, or does it possess an efficacy of its own, independent and self-subsistent? Jean de Jandun, a famous Aristotelian of the fifteenth century, had in this question already decided for the latter, contrary to the Averroist basic view he generally upholds: potency, as he expressly emphasizes, signifies no mere relation that our thought lays into matter, but is present and effective in it as a real something 1). To this development, which had taken place within the Aristotelian school itself, Renaissance natural philosophy now everywhere attaches itself. Already in Cardano it is emphasized that matter is to be grasped not merely as something indeterminate, but as a mode of determinacy of its own, and that a thoroughly positive `reality` therefore belongs to it 2). Telesio and Patrizzi sharpen this thought by seeking a new exposition of the relation of form and matter that remains outside the traditional conceptual oppositions. The way in which things are contained in their `seed` constitutes, as is explained here, a fundamental objective basic relation, one that the customary distinction between act and potency conceals rather than clarifies. **Notes**

- Campanella's force-concept is now explicitly read forward into the later opposition of occasionalism and pre-established harmony.
- The critique of `Potenz` turns on a sharp claim: Aristotle converted a boundary concept of thought into a metaphysical essence, and scholasticism then oscillated within that confusion.
- Cardano, Telesio, and Patrizzi are grouped here not by doctrine but by a common reversal: matter, seed, and power are treated as positive actuality rather than inert possibility.
- Patrizzi's attack on `Substanz` extends the same movement. Once concrete qualities and operations do the real explanatory work, Aristotelian form threatens to collapse into a bundle of `accidents`.
