Yet the basic thought of Pomponazzi's work attains full sharpness and clarity only in the ethical conclusions toward which it leads. Moral reason alone forms the true prerogative and distinguishing peculiarity of the human being, whereas speculative understanding, like the drive toward technical-practical activity, constitutes a trait that we share with the other intelligences of higher or lower kind. With the abolition of immortality, however, moral life itself seems to lose its support and center; with the removal of the transcendent goal, all purposiveness seems uprooted altogether. Even the philosophical Renaissance of Platonism in fact still stood under the spell of this thought. If the soul were mortal, as Ficino's `Platonic Theology` had argued from the very beginning, then there would be no creature more unhappy than the human being, and the value of our empirical existence would be annihilated. For Pomponazzi's ethical basic intuition, by contrast, it stands firm that genuine ethics must prove itself in teaching us to find the purpose of life within life itself. The idea of the unlimited duration of the individual is replaced by the idea of the steady progress and higher formation of humanity. In this sense Pomponazzi's doctrine is the genuine fruit of the humanist view of world and history. The whole human race is to be compared to a single individual in which all parts and organs subordinate themselves to the one purpose of preserving and advancing the whole. Just as here the common goal sets definite circumscribed limits to the growth of the individual members, so it is the moral vocation of humanity that must measure and limit the striving and demands of individuals.1) In this restraint to the empirical limits of our existence we gain a higher ideality and a new outlook upon the infinity given to us in the real life of history itself. Only now do moral laws find their firm support, in that they are recognized not as alien and external commands that must be enforced by hope and fear, but as autonomous statutes and demands springing from our own essence. For the first time in modern ethics, the thought of the autonomy of the moral appears here in full determinacy. Only on the lowest level are reward and punishment indispensable as motives of action, whereas for mature moral insight every action receives its value purely from itself and thus carries its reward and punishment within it: `poena namque accidentalis est poena damni, essentialis vero poena culpae, at poena culpae longe deterior est poena damni`.1)

**Notes**

- Cassirer now completes the logical side of Pomponazzi: the universal is temporally inseparable from the individual but logically prior to it, and knowledge proceeds by a return from concept back into the case.
- The real payoff is ethical. Pomponazzi replaces immortality as the ground of value with progress, human vocation, and the self-grounding authority of moral law within finite life.
- Cassirer names the point with unusual directness: this is the first full appearance in modern ethics of the autonomy of the moral.
- The mortality thesis is therefore not mere negation. It becomes a new valuation of empirical being and a defense of the human as end in itself.
- Pomponazzi’s logical project is then restated in Cassirer’s own vocabulary: preserve the universality of thought, but ground it in relation to empirical material rather than in flight from experience.
- The tranche ends mid-name exactly where Cassirer is about to compare Ficino and Pomponazzi within later modern philosophy.
