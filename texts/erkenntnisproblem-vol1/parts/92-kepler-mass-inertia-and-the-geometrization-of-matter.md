# Part 92: Kepler, Mass, Inertia, and the Geometrization of Matter

Cassirer now pulls the correlate concept into view. Once force is subordinated to function and mathematical law, matter cannot remain a vague residue; it has to be reconstructed through mass, inertia, and quantitative resistance. Kepler still struggles for the vocabulary, but the shift is decisive: matter and force cease to oppose one another as metaphysical principles and become two sides of one mathematical causality. This is also why geometry moves into first methodological place. Nature is no longer a debased stuff awaiting external divine shaping, but a field whose own essence tends toward geometric lawfulness.

Only in this thought of relativity is animism finally uprooted. The purely "inner" forces of metaphysics disappear: no force any longer belongs to a single "subject" in and for itself, but in its definition it already contains the necessary relation to a second, "external" element. The concept of relation, now the predominant point of view, recognizes "forces" only insofar as they attest themselves in a pure mathematical proportion. The question and interest therefore turn from the concept of force to the law of force: for the first time the task is here posed of finding the numerical rule according to which gravity changes with distance from the center. The thought that the magnitude of attraction is inversely proportional to the square of the distance is here expressly stated and taken into consideration; it is finally rejected only because according to Kepler's way of representing the matter gravity does not propagate like light, equally in all directions of space, but only within the plane of the planetary orbits, so that its decrease, it seems, must occur in simple proportion to the distance. Thus he did not in the end attain Newton's fundamental law; nevertheless one may already characterize the mere consideration of the grounds why the effect of the sun on the planets is not, as Kepler himself objects, inversely proportional to the square of the distance, as the most brilliant stimulation and the first historical emergence of the fundamental thought of the law of gravitation. He actually placed under discussion the proposition that bodies act proportionally to their mass and inversely proportionally to the square of their distance; and that he decided for another law of action was by far not so hindering for the development of the thought of gravitation as the whole course of thought itself was furthering, especially since the clearer formulation of mechanical concepts was bound of itself to lead to the overcoming of the defects that still adhered to Kepler's ideas. If the history of physics passes this judgment, then the history of the problem of knowledge must point to the fact that the result of Kepler's empirical research was attained in the exact, straight-line continuation of his philosophical fundamental principle. -

The Concept of Mass.

In that the concept of force subordinates itself to the concept of function and thereby to the fundamental law of mathematics, its correlate concept is of necessity also drawn into the same logical process of development. The concept of matter here receives for the first time in the modern age its scientific shape and firmness. The path to it leads through the concept of mass, and thereby indirectly once again through the concept of magnitude. In the law of attraction that Kepler formulates, the "masses" of the planets first meet us as pure numerical values, as factors that interpenetrate with the counter-factor of the attracting force and determine themselves to a univocal quantitative final result. If the heavenly bodies had no "natural inertia," if there did not belong to them "a kind of weight," then no force would be needed to move them from their place and the slightest external cause would suffice to give them an infinite velocity. But now, since we see that the revolution of the planets occurs in firmly determinate times, that with some it is slower, with others faster, we are necessarily driven to introduce the moment of the "resistance" of matter, by virtue of which that difference can be brought to exact representation and calculation. It is characteristic of the novelty of the thought how Kepler here is still everywhere struggling with the expression, how in particular he seeks at one time to elucidate the concept of mass by the analogy to "weight," while on the other hand trying to maintain a determinate distinction between both moments. The expression "inertia" too develops only gradually and step by step from a sensible image into the determinacy of a fixed mathematical principle; into a conceptual mark that enables us to distinguish bodies, by virtue of their different reaction to one and the same cause of motion, and to maintain them within the total system as individually delimited unities.

It is extraordinarily instructive to follow how the new thought forms itself out of an old metaphysical opposition. Kepler at first still sets the concept of "matter" over against pure "form" in the Aristotelian sense - under form understanding every moving principle, thus at the earliest stage of consideration the guiding intelligences of the planets. In that he now discovers a factor directed against and resisting the impulse to motion, this necessarily falls to the domain of matter; whereby matter is still very much thought as a distinct metaphysical quality and "nature": as an impediment that pure form has to overcome and master.

The "Inertia" of Matter.

Now, however, an entirely new development sets in: the causes producing motion themselves become, as we saw, from spiritual principles into "forces," thus into organs and members of "corporeal nature." In place of the separation of matter and force there now enters their necessary correspondence and belonging-together; both represent only the different sides of the unified mathematical causality. The concept of matter, just like that of force, serves to make possible the application of geometry: `ubi materia, ibi geometria`. We understand here, from a new connection, the changed judgment and valuation that the corporeal world, and thus "nature" in Kepler's sense, experiences. Against Patrizzi Kepler raises the reproach that he, like all who snatch after abstract forms and essences, despises matter, "next to God the sole and highest object," with excessive self-assurance, and therefore necessarily entangles himself in sophisms. The relation of God and nature has changed; God no longer enters nature from without as into a foreign and unworthy stuff, but nature itself is that which, by virtue of its own essence, strives toward the divine, because toward geometrical lawfulness.

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) The reciprocity line now becomes explicitly law-seeking. What matters is no longer simply that forces relate, but that their variation be numerically stated.

2) Cassirer is careful with Kepler's failure on the inverse-square law. The failure matters less than the fact that the right problem has finally been posed.

3) The correlate of force is now fully earned: matter appears scientifically only through mass, inertia, and quantitative resistance.

4) The old metaphysical opposition of matter and form does not just disappear. It is transformed from within until matter and force become corresponding sides of one mathematical causality.

5) `ubi materia, ibi geometria` is the strongest local formula in the tranche. It explains at once the revaluation of nature, the new status of matter, and the coming priority of geometry in method.
```

**Notes**

- `Der Massenbegriff` is one of the clearest places where Cassirer shows a concept of objecthood being rebuilt through mathematical law rather than inherited from ontology.
- The Patrizzi return matters because it confirms that contempt for matter and contempt for empirical nature still belong to the same anti-scientific line.
