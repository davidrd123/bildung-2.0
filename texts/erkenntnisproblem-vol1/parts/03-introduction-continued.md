Vielmehr gestaltet sich der Begriff des „Ich“ sowohl wie der des Gegenstandes erst an dem Fortschritt der wissenschaftlichen Erfahrung und unterliegt mit ihm den gleichen inneren Wandlungen. Nicht nur die Inhalte wechseln ihre Stelle, so daß, was zuvor der objektiven Sphäre angehörte, in die subjektive hinüberrückt, sondern zugleich verschiebt sich die Bedeutung und Funktion der beiden Grundelemente. Die großen wissenschaftlichen Epochen übernehmen nicht das fertige Schema der Entgegensetzung, um es nur mit mannigfachem und wechselndem Gehalt zu erfüllen, sondern sie erschaffen selbst erst begrifflich die beiden Gegenglieder. Die Aristotelische Auffassung der Erkenntnis unterscheidet sich von der modernen nicht nur in der Art der Abhängigkeit, die sie zwischen „Natur“ und „Geist“ annimmt, sondern in dem Kern und Grundsinn dieser Begriffe selbst. Dies also ist eine der ersten und charakteristischsten philosophischen Leistungen jeder Epoche, daß sie sich das Problem des Wechselverhältnisses von Sein und Denken aufs neue formuliert und damit der Erkenntnis erst ihren Rang und ihre spezifische Stellung anweist. Wiederum aber erweitert sich mit dieser Erwägung das Material, auf das die geschichtliche Betrachtung und Untersuchung sich zu richten hat. Es sind keineswegs allein die abgeschlossenen philosophischen Systeme, es sind die mannigfachen Versuche und Ansätze der Forschung, wie der gesamten geistigen Kultur, in denen diese allmähliche Umgestaltung des Ichbegriffs, wie des Objektbegriffs sich vollzieht. So dürfen wir große geistige Bewegungen — wie etwa den italienischen Humanismus oder die französische Skepsis des 16. Jahrhunderts — auch dann in unsere Forschung einbeziehen, wenn ihr direkter Ertrag für die systematische Philosophie gering ist.

Es muß der Versuch gewagt werden, aus der intellektuellen Gesamtbewegung eines Zeitalters sein herrschendes und treibendes Erkenntnisideal zu rekonstruieren. Zu dieser Fassung der Aufgabe nötigt noch ein anderes Moment. Es besagt wenig, wenn wir hören, daß zu einem bestimmten Zeitpunkt auf eine „empiristische“ Periode der Philosophie eine „rationalistische“ gefolgt sei und daß beide etwa ihren Ausgleich in einer dritten „kritischen“ Richtung gefunden hätten. Als „Empiristen“ treten uns sogleich in den Anfängen der neueren Philosophie Bacon, wie Leonardo da Vinci, Galilei wie Paracelsus und Campanella entgegen. Die Fassung unserer Aufgabe dagegen setzt ein engeres, spezifisches Verhältnis zwischen beiden Gedankenkreisen voraus: sie sind uns gleich selbständige und gleich unentbehrliche Symptome ein und desselben intellektuellen Fortschritts. Die Gesamtentwicklung müßte sprunghaft und lückenhaft erscheinen, wenn wir uns der Betrachtung dieses wichtigsten Mittelgliedes begeben wollten.

Denn erst in ihm und kraft des Zusammenhanges mit ihm erhält der philosophische Gedanke selbst seine wahrhafte innere Kontinuität. Daß es Erkenntnis als strengen und eindeutigen logischen Begriff gibt: dies wird erst hier vollständig bewiesen. Auch die übrigen Gebiete der geistigen Tätigkeit, auch das Recht und die Sprache, auch Kunst und Religion enthalten einen bestimmten Beitrag zum allgemeinen Problem der Erkenntnis. Aber wie sie sich diesem Problem von sehr verschiedenen Seiten her nähern, so bleibt es zunächst dahingestellt, ob und wie weit sie mit ihm einen wahrhaft einheitlichen Sinn verbinden. Eine Reihe charakteristischer Stellungnahmen zum „Ich“ und zur „Wirklichkeit“ tritt uns auch hier entgegen, aber ob in dieser Fülle der Motive eine gemeinsame Grundtendenz sich aussondern und festhalten läßt, bleibt häufig fraglich. Der Wechsel der Meinungen gestaltet sich erst hier zu einem klaren und sinnvollen Zusammenhang um, in welchem sich nunmehr auch die Philosophie ihres eigenen Begriffs und ihrer theoretischen Aufgaben erst vollständig bewußt wird. Introduction

The process of knowledge does not advance in such a way that mind, as a finished being, would merely have to take possession of the outer reality standing over against it, itself likewise complete in itself; that it would appropriate it piece by piece and draw it over to itself. Rather, the concept of the “I,” no less than that of the object, takes shape only in the advance of scientific experience and undergoes with it the same inner transformations. Not only do the contents change places, so that what previously belonged to the objective sphere shifts over into the subjective, but the meaning and function of the two basic elements are likewise displaced. The great scientific epochs do not take over the finished schema of opposition merely in order to fill it with manifold and changing content; rather, they are the ones that first conceptually create the two opposed terms themselves. The Aristotelian conception of knowledge differs from the modern one not only in the kind of dependence it assumes between “nature” and “mind,” but in the very core and fundamental sense of these concepts themselves. This, then, is one of the first and most characteristic philosophical achievements of every epoch: that it formulates anew for itself the problem of the reciprocal relation of being and thought, and thereby first assigns to knowledge its rank and its specific position. In this delimitation of the task, even more than in the particular results, lies the originality of every productive age. Yet this reflection again broadens the material toward which historical consideration and investigation must direct themselves. It is by no means only the completed philosophical systems; rather, it is the manifold attempts and initiatives of inquiry, as of intellectual culture as a whole, in which this gradual transformation of the concept of the I and of the concept of the object is accomplished. All tendencies directed toward creating a new methodology of the empirical sciences, or toward grounding the human sciences anew in a deepened concept of self-consciousness, now belong indirectly to our problem. Thus we may include great intellectual movements, such as Italian humanism or the French skepticism of the sixteenth century, within our inquiry even where their direct yield for systematic philosophy is slight.

We must venture the attempt to reconstruct, out of the total intellectual movement of an age, its dominant and driving ideal of knowledge. Another consideration compels this formulation of the task. It means little to hear that at a given point an “empiricist” period of philosophy was followed by a “rationalist” one, and that the two perhaps found their reconciliation in a third, “critical” tendency. At the very beginnings of modern philosophy Bacon and Leonardo da Vinci, Galileo and Paracelsus and Campanella, all immediately confront us as “empiricists.” And yet the concept of “experience” for which all these thinkers stand is only an apparent unity, behind which lie concealed some of the gravest principled oppositions known to the development of the problem of knowledge. What “experience” truly means for each of them can be brought out only by substantive analysis of the totality of his scientific and philosophical achievement: the sense of the concept is disclosed to us not merely in its avowal, but in its enactment across the different fields of problems. The relation between philosophy and science is grasped and described only externally so long as one speaks merely of a reciprocal “influence” that the two exert upon one another. Such efficacy is not the prerogative of one single domain, but applies in the same sense to all the contents and directions of culture. The framing of our task, by contrast, presupposes a closer, more specific relation between the two circles of thought: for us they are equally self-subsistent and equally indispensable symptoms of one and the same intellectual advance. For what the modern concept of knowledge signifies, Galileo and Kepler, Newton and Euler are just as important and fully valid witnesses as Descartes or Leibniz. The total development would have to appear abrupt and full of gaps if we were to dispense with consideration of this most important mediating link.

For only in it, and by virtue of its connection with it, does philosophical thought itself acquire its true inner continuity. That there is knowledge as a strict and unambiguous logical concept: this is first fully demonstrated here. The other domains of intellectual activity as well, law and language, art and religion, also contain a definite contribution to the general problem of knowledge. But however they approach this problem from very different sides, it remains for the moment an open question whether and how far they connect with it a genuinely unitary sense. A series of characteristic positions with respect to the “I” and to “reality” confronts us here too, but whether in this abundance of motives a common basic tendency can be isolated and held fast often remains doubtful. It is only in exact science, in its course steady despite all fluctuations, that the unity of the concept of knowledge, which everywhere else remained a mere demand, receives its true fulfillment and vindication. Only here is the change of opinions transformed into a clear and meaningful nexus, within which philosophy too now first becomes fully conscious of its own concept and of its theoretical tasks. Yet although the contribution made by mathematics and natural science to the progress of the problem of knowledge lies openly before us, it is harder to determine and clearly delimit the general influence exerted from the side of the human sciences. For at the beginning of the modern age the human sciences do not yet confront us as an independent whole that has already found its fixed support within itself. Their content is, as it were, melted down into the dominant system of metaphysics, which is determined equally by the Aristotelian tradition and by church doctrine. Only slowly do the individual moments of thought that are held together in this system as under a dogmatic compulsion emerge into independent, freer motions. It takes the profound intellectual struggles of the Renaissance to recover step by step, in their distinctiveness, the manifold and heterogeneous problems that remain indiscriminately fused together in the medieval world-picture.

In place of the admirable consistency with which, in ancient philosophy, each new phase grows out of the preceding one according to inner logical laws, there enters here a movement complicated in many ways and conditioned by manifold considerations, a movement that only gradually closes around a fixed center. If, therefore, at this stage we want to grasp the problem of knowledge in its concrete historical shape, we must not detach it from the relations and connections it enters into with interests of a different kind. We must not anticipate and place at the head the strict delimitation of its significance, the insight into its special position and its fundamental value, which are the final result of the modern age's labor of thought. How close, in particular, the connection with ethical and religious ideas is can soon be made clear if one recalls the role both play in the development of the modern concept of self-consciousness. Here thinkers such as Pascal must above all engage our historical interest, thinkers in whom two different inward stances toward the problem of knowledge, in whom the new scientific method and the religious fundamental mood of the Middle Ages, meet and conflict. The individual struggle carried out in them is at the same time the expression of a deeper general transformation in the way of thinking. In general, wherever within the consciousness of an epoch metaphysical interests still possess decisive and central significance, we must take our first standpoint and point of departure within those interests themselves; and this consideration holds not only for the field as a whole, but likewise for its individual parts and members. The basic concepts of scientific knowledge, the concepts of force and cause, of substance and matter, all have a long and much-branched metaphysical prehistory extending far beyond the beginnings of the modern age. ...
