# Part 97: Galilei, Pure and Applied Mathematics and the Concept of Matter

Cassirer now answers the concept/reality problem in Galileo's own register. Mathematical figures have no second, lower-grade existence beyond the truth of their definitions; if the relevant conditions are fulfilled anywhere, the consequences hold with the same necessity for ideal and real cases alike. Physics therefore aims not to lower mathematics to approximation, but to carry its certainty into ever wider empirical domains by analysis, hypothetical construction, deduction, and then comparison with observation. This leads directly to the new concept of matter: no longer the resistant opposite of form, but the homogeneous, quantitatively determinable correlate of mathematical necessity.

Somit ist es freilich möglich, daß einem bestimmten Begriff der mathematischen Theorie kein konkreter Anwendungsfall entspricht: sind aber die Bedingungen, die diese Theorie vorschreibt, an irgendeiner Stelle wahrhaft erfüllt, so gelten die Folgerungen aus ihnen mit derselben Stringenz und Notwendigkeit, gleichviel ob es sich um ideale oder reale, um rein gedachte oder um physisch vorhandene Gegenstände handelt. Die Sicherheit der Schlußfolgerungen, die die reine Theorie entwickelt hat, wird durch das spezielle Anwendungsgebiet nicht eingeschränkt: sie besteht, wenn überhaupt, so für jeden beliebigen Kreis von Objekten, innerhalb dessen die betreffenden Beziehungen sich aufweisen lassen. Es gibt nur eine Form der mathematischen Gewißheit, die vorhanden oder nicht vorhanden sein kann, die aber nicht das eine Mal unbedingt, das andere Mal nur ungefähr und mit gewissen Abschwächungen gilt. Diese Form erleidet keine Veränderung, gleichviel, in welchem Stoffe, d. h. in welchem Umkreis physikalischer Probleme und Objekte sie sich darstellt. Und somit ergibt sich jetzt eine neue Fassung der allgemeinen wissenschaftlichen Aufgabe. Das Abstrakte und das Konkrete, die "Theorie" und das "Phänomen" stimmen freilich auf keiner Stufe unserer wissenschaftlichen Erfahrung jemals vollkommen überein. Aber der Grund hierfür ist nicht in irgendeiner ontologischen Differenz ihrer beiden "Naturen" zu suchen, so daß hier ein sachlich unaufheblicher Widerstreit vorläge: vielmehr ist es gerade die fortschreitende Aufhebung dieses Gegensatzes, die das Ziel und den eigentlichen Inhalt aller Erkenntnis ausmacht. Soweit der Gegensatz vorhanden ist, bezeichnet er daher immer nur den Ansatz zu neuen Problemen und Forschungen.

```text
Die mangelnde Übereinstimmung ist in diesem Falle "weder durch das Abstrakte, noch durch das Konkrete, weder durch die Geometrie, noch durch die Physik verschuldet; sie fällt allein dem Rechner zur Last, der die Rechnung nicht richtig anzustellen weiß". Denn Aufgabe der Rechnung ist es, den Zusammenhang, wo er nicht unmittelbar deutlich vorliegt, durch Einfügung von Mittelgliedern ersichtlich zu machen. Erst die gedankliche Zerlegung, die wir an ihm vornehmen, schafft die Handhaben für seine quantitative Erkenntnis. Diese Zerlegung und damit die Verwandlung des empirischen Inhalts in ein System von Größen und Zahlen ist niemals abgeschlossen; aber, soweit sie dringt, teilt sie auch dem physikalischen Wissen den gleichen Charakter vollkommener Gewißheit mit, der der reinen Mathematik eignet. Das Ziel der Physik besteht eben darin, die Begriffe, die ihr die Mathematik liefert, in aller Strenge festzuhalten und sie dennoch zugleich für immer weitere Gebiete von Einzeltatsachen fruchtbar zu machen. Mit meisterhafter Klarheit hat Galilei dieses Doppelverhältnis entwickelt und erläutert. Der Begriff der gleichförmigen Beschleunigung, von dem er ausgeht, ist ihm zunächst nichts anderes als eine "hypothetische Voraussetzung", die nicht unmittelbar auf die "Tatsachen" der Natur bezogen und an ihnen gemessen werden darf, sondern zuvor der Zerlegung und Entfaltung in ihre einzelnen mathematischen Eigentümlichkeiten und Folgerungen bedarf.

Erst nachdem dieser deduktive Teil der Aufgabe abgeschlossen ist, und nachdem er zu festen, zahlenmäßigen Beziehungen hingeleitet hat, ist für die Vergleichung des reinen Gesetzes mit dem Beobachtungsinhalt der Boden bereitet, ist das Maß gewonnen, mit dem wir jetzt an die Mannigfaltigkeit des Wahrnehmungsstoffes herantreten können. "Zeigt die Erfahrung nunmehr, daß solche Eigenschaften, wie wir sie abgeleitet, im freien Fall der Naturkörper ihre Bestätigung finden, so können wir ohne Gefahr des Irrtums behaupten, daß die konkrete Fallbewegung mit derjenigen, die wir definiert und vorausgesetzt haben, identisch sei. Ist dies nicht der Fall, so verlieren doch unsere Beweise, da sie einzig und allein für unsere Voraussetzung gelten wollten, nichts von ihrer Kraft und Schlüssigkeit, - so wenig es den Sätzen des Archimedes über die Spirale Abbruch tut, daß sich in der Natur kein Körper findet, dem eine spiralförmige Bewegung zukommt." Die Schroffheit, in der hier die Trennung des Begriffsgehalts von der Beobachtung vertreten wird, erklärt sich aus der polemischen Absicht dieser Sätze. Man begreift indes, daß es in erster Linie nicht auf die Loslösung der Begriffe, sondern auf ihre innigere Durchdringung mit dem Wahrnehmungsstoffe abgesehen ist: eine Durchdringung, die jedoch nur dann erreicht werden kann, wenn die Begriffe rein aus sich selbst und unabhängig zu immer spezielleren Folgerungen fortgeführt worden sind. Bis in die astronomische Einzelforschung hinein hat Galilei diesen Grundgedanken seiner Wissenschaft festgehalten: wie Kepler verlangt er auch hier, daß der konkreten Beobachtung durch eine Frage des Denkens vorgearbeitet und die Richtung gewiesen werde.
```

**English (draft)**

```text
... flowing from their concepts; it is idle and misleading to oppose to this being of pure definition a different, concrete form of existence. That a given empirical structure "is" a determinate figure can mean nothing else than that it fulfills all the demands and relations summarized in the concept of this mathematical shape. Science consists of a system of pure conditions whose validity is independent of the question whether in our world of perception there are subjects to which the presupposed conditions apply. One can deny the existence of such subjects without thereby touching in the least the conception of the character and cognitive value of pure relations. Thus it is of course possible that to a determinate concept of mathematical theory no concrete case of application corresponds; but if the conditions prescribed by this theory are truly fulfilled anywhere, then the conclusions from them hold with the same stringency and necessity, no matter whether it is a matter of ideal or real, purely thought or physically present objects. The certainty of the conclusions that pure theory has developed is not restricted by the special field of application: it holds, if it holds at all, for every arbitrary circle of objects within which the relevant relations can be shown. There is only one form of mathematical certainty, which can either be present or absent, but does not hold one time unconditionally and another time only approximately and with certain diminutions. This form undergoes no change, no matter in what material, that is, in what circle of physical problems and objects, it presents itself. And thus there now results a new formulation of the general scientific task. The abstract and the concrete, the "theory" and the "phenomenon," indeed never completely coincide at any stage of our scientific experience. But the ground of this is not to be sought in any ontological difference of their two "natures," as though there were here an objectively unabolishable conflict: rather, it is precisely the progressive abolition of this opposition that constitutes the goal and the proper content of all cognition. Insofar as the opposition is present, it therefore always designates only the point of departure for new problems and researches.

This lack of agreement is in this case "caused neither by the abstract nor by the concrete, neither by geometry nor by physics; it falls solely to the calculator, who does not know how to carry out the calculation correctly." For the task of calculation is to make the connection evident where it does not lie immediately clearly before us by inserting intermediate links. The complex phenomenon is of course not directly graspable in its totality for the mathematical concept; for the manifold moments that condition it are nowhere sharply delimited against one another in the first sensible apprehension and thus cannot each for itself be determined by number and measure. Only the conceptual decomposition that we undertake upon it creates the handles for its quantitative cognition. This decomposition, and with it the transformation of empirical content into a system of magnitudes and numbers, is never complete; but insofar as it penetrates, it imparts to physical knowledge the same character of perfect certainty that belongs to pure mathematics. The goal of physics consists precisely in holding fast in all strictness the concepts that mathematics supplies it and yet at the same time making them fruitful for ever wider domains of individual facts. Galileo developed and elucidated this double relation with masterly clarity. The concept of uniformly accelerated motion, from which he starts, is for him at first nothing other than a "hypothetical presupposition" that may not be directly related to the "facts" of nature and measured against them, but first requires decomposition and unfolding into its individual mathematical properties and consequences.

Only after this deductive part of the task has been concluded, and after it has led to fixed numerical relations, is the ground prepared for the comparison of the pure law with the content of observation, is the measure won with which we can now approach the multiplicity of perceptual material. "If experience now shows that such properties as we have derived find their confirmation in the free fall of natural bodies, then we can without danger of error assert that the concrete falling motion is identical with that which we have defined and presupposed. If this is not the case, our proofs nevertheless lose nothing of their force and conclusiveness, since they wished to hold solely and only for our presupposition - just as little as it detracts from Archimedes' propositions about the spiral that in nature no body is found to which a spiral motion belongs." The severity with which the separation of conceptual content from observation is defended here is explained by the polemical intention of these statements. One understands, however, that it is in the first place not the separation of concepts that is intended, but their more intimate interpenetration with the material of perception: an interpenetration that can, however, be attained only when the concepts have been carried forward purely out of themselves and independently to ever more special consequences. Galileo held fast to this fundamental thought of his science even down into individual astronomical research: like Kepler, he too demands here that concrete observation be prepared and given direction by a question of thought.

Step by step there now unfolds the complex of hypothetical positings that are to lead us in steady progress toward the concrete being of things. "Sensible and physical matter" had hitherto counted as the hindrance that stands opposed to the imprinting of pure mathematics in empirical reality. It thus appears as a distinct metaphysical potency that works against pure thought, as a compulsion to which concept must adapt itself. It is the old Aristotelian opposition: all cognition is created and guaranteed according to its content and origin through the universality of "form," while matter in itself designates the absolutely unknowable. The resolution of this conflict can, according to Galileo's general principles, be attempted only in one direction: matter, here hypostatized into a proper independent being, must once again be ordered into the continuous nexus of principles and developed out of it. It may no longer designate merely the limit up to which pure concept is able to penetrate, but must become an integrating component of the conceptual system itself. We saw how Kepler already wrestled with this thought and how in it he sought to carry out the delimitation of his doctrine from natural philosophy. The development toward which he strove now completes itself clearly and effortlessly. For the "form" Galileo has before his eyes as model is not ontological form, but merely mathematical form. Its mastery of the object is therefore guaranteed and secured everywhere that this object itself bears the character of perfect geometrical determinability. This demand, however, is completely fulfilled by the concept of matter as the new physics grasps it: for it designates the wholly homogeneous stuff in which all qualitative differences, as sensible sensation offers them, are aufgehoben and only those moments retained that are subject to quantitative comparison and measurement. The concept of matter thus forms no longer, as hitherto, the opposition, but rather the correlate to the concept of conceptual necessity. It becomes the indispensable middle term in the process by virtue of which we translate the manifold of perception into the form of the physical concept.
```

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) Galileo's reply to Simplicio is not that reality imitates mathematics badly. It is that the opposition between mathematical truth and physical reality has been posed falsely from the start.

2) Applied mathematics does not dilute certainty. It extends one and the same certainty wherever the requisite conditions are actually fulfilled.

3) The theory/phenomenon gap is reinterpreted as a problem for analysis and calculation, not as an ontological gulf.

4) Galileo's hypothetical method is now fully explicit: define, deduce, then compare. The hypothesis is neither a fiction nor a mere summary of data.

5) The concept of matter is transformed on exactly this basis. Matter becomes the homogeneous, measurable correlate to mathematical form rather than its resistant other.
```
