# Part 91: Gilbert's Magnetism and Kepler's Theory of Gravitation

Cassirer now makes the gravity line concrete by way of Gilbert. Gilbert's break consists in refusing to derive motion from privileged places, directions, or vague substantial affinities; attraction must be rooted in bodies themselves and treated as a general natural cause. Kepler deepens this in two ways: attraction becomes a force between cosmic masses rather than only between a body and its parts, and magnetism helps force the transition from psychic animation to mathematically law-governed nature. Gravity is then redefined as reciprocal interaction, not one-sided tendency, and the real question shifts from "what force is" to the law according to which it varies.

The Theory of Magnetism. Gilbert.

In their place there enters with Gilbert, first, the empirical theory of magnetic and "electric" force and the cognition of terrestrial magnetism. And the investigation of this problem immediately gains for him as well cosmic significance, insofar as through it a new general concept of attraction is grounded. Whereas weight had hitherto been explained by the tendency toward the fixed, absolutely resting center of the earth, Gilbert shows that no point in the universe is due any physical precedence or any physical special position. To a mere determination of position, which has no priority over all other possible placements, no determinate forces and activities can be ascribed. "It is not place that works and creates in the nature of things, that decides about the rest or motion of bodies. For it is in itself neither a being nor an efficient cause; rather, the bodies first determine their reciprocal position and location by virtue of the forces inherent in them. Place is a nothing, it does not exist and exerts no force; rather, all natural power is contained and grounded in the bodies themselves." Thus the effect of gravity too is not bound to a single point of space, but proceeds equally from all material masses: without it, which binds and holds together not only earthly matter but also the sun and all the other heavenly bodies, the structure and order of the universe would dissolve. With this ordering into the domain of the general causes of nature, gravitation is at the same time prescribed a fixed determination of magnitude and a given sphere of efficacy: heavy bodies do not strive toward the center by virtue of their "inner" nature, but by virtue of an external effect that weakens with distance and at sufficiently great distance becomes wholly imperceptible. In detail Gilbert conceives this effect, analogously to the phenomena of magnetism and electricity, as mediated by a subtle fluid that streams out from the center and lays itself around it in concentric spheres of ever lesser density.

There are two points in which Kepler transformed and deepened Gilbert's theory, which he otherwise takes over in all essential main features; and both point back to a common origin. First, the attraction that in Gilbert existed only between the total mass of a determinate heavenly body and its individual parts is expanded in its efficacy and significance by being recognized as a fundamental force effective beyond the sphere of individual bodies, between the different cosmic masses. Kepler has here removed a barrier that had still remained throughout even for the boldest and most decided adherents of the new astronomical worldview: even for Giordano Bruno gravity still signifies merely the pull of the individual parts of mass toward the whole from which they have detached themselves, without influencing the position and reciprocal relation of the heavenly bodies among themselves. By applying his new concept of gravitation especially to the relation of the earth to the moon, Kepler thereby arrives at an explanation of ebb and flow with which he anticipates the fundamental thought of Newton's theory of this phenomenon. To this extension in the empirical efficacy of the concept there corresponds at the same time an inner change of content: for even in Gilbert, however much he rejects dark qualities and affinities, psychic potencies were not in principle excluded from the explanation of nature. The order and constancy of planetary motion are still traced back in him to the animation of the individual heavenly bodies, just as for him too the constancy of the magnetic poles is a testimony to a psychic principle that guides the earth in its revolution.

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) Gilbert matters here less as a detached figure than as the historical medium through which gravity is stripped of privileged place and substantial affinity.

2) The anti-ontological line stays active. "Up" and "down" are no longer allowed to harden into real opposing powers.

3) Kepler's first deepening is cosmological. Attraction now binds distinct masses and begins to reorganize the whole structure of the heavens.

4) The second deepening is methodological. Magnetism does not preserve animism; it pushes Kepler out of it, because acting body and medium are both fixed quanta inside one mathematical order.

5) Reciprocity is the key gain at the end of the tranche. Once gravity is defined as interaction, the dominant question shifts from force-substance to force-law.
```
