# Part 62: Campanella, Skepticism, and the Failure of Abstraction

Campanella's skeptical counter-image turns out to be structural rather than ornamental: once cognition is treated as abbreviated sensation, abstraction looks like loss, and only the still-unreached logic of relations would have answered the problem.

Das Werk beginnt mit einer ausgebildeten Theorie des Skepticismus, die zwar nirgends als eigene, endgültige Lehre behauptet wird, die sich jedoch in der Ausführlichkeit und Genauigkeit, mit der sie dargestellt wird, als ein wichtiges bedingendes Moment der logischen Gesamtentwicklung erweist. In der Tat ist, näher betrachtet, die Skepsis das natürliche Korrelat und die notwendige Kehrseite des Erkenntnisideals, das bisher gezeichnet wurde. Wir erfassen die Außendinge nur in den Eigenbewegungen unseres Geistes: welches Mittel aber vermöchte uns zu lehren, die zufällige und die wesentliche Bewegung, die fremde Zutat und das wahrhafte Sein des Gegenstandes zu scheiden? Dem Kreise der Subjektivität ist nirgends zu entrinnen, „jeder besitzt eine eigene Philosophie, je nachdem er in verschiedener Weise von den Dingen sinnlich bestimmt wird“. Man glaube nicht, durch irgendein anderes, höheres Mittel des Bewußtseins sich über diese individuelle Beschränkung erheben zu können: sind sie doch alle, wie wir sahen, in die Bedingtheit des „Sinnes“ einbezogen und verstrickt. Der abstrakte Allgemeinbegriff vor allem, dem man die Einsicht in das „Wesen“ der Dinge zuzutrauen pflegt, ist hierzu seinem Ursprung und seiner Ableitung nach unvermögend. Wer nur die „Wesenheit“ des Menschen, wer also nur die allgemeinen Züge und Merkmale kennt, die allen Individuen der Gattung gleichmäßig zukommen, dem ist damit der Einblick in die Varietäten und Besonderungen, die erst das wirkliche Sein von Diesem oder Jenem ausmachen, verschlossen. Hier enthüllt sich uns eine innere Antinomie, zu der alles menschliche Erkennen uns notwendig immer von neuem zurückführt. „Erkennen“ heißt für uns eine unendliche Mannigfaltigkeit von Einzeleindrücken und Einzelfällen in eine abgekürzte Formel zusammenfassen: wer aber versichert uns, daß bei dieser notwendigen Arbeit der „Abstraktion“ nicht gerade die wesentlichen Momente des Falles übersehen und geopfert werden? Wir glauben, indem wir von den besonderen Merkmalen absehen, zu höherer Allgemeinheit emporzusteigen, während wir dadurch nur die scharfen und deutlichen Umrisse des Einzelnen immer mehr aus dem Auge verlieren, also statt der Weite nur Verschwommenheit des Bildes zurückbehalten2).

In der Tat verbirgt der herkömmliche logische Gegensatz zwischen dem Inhalt und dem Umfang der Begriffe eine innere Schwierigkeit und ein Problem, das von Campanellas bisherigen Voraussetzungen aus nicht zu lösen ist. Wenn dem Erkennen ernstlich die Aufgabe gestellt wird, die konkret vorhandenen Dinge direkt und einzeln nachzubilden, so werden die Gattungsbegriffe selbst zu starren dingartigen Gebilden; zu Kopien der Objekte, die diese indes nicht in ihrer ursprünglichen Frische, sondern oberflächlich und abgebläßt wiedergeben. Jeder neue Schritt und jede neue Leistung des Denkens wird so zu einer immer weiteren Abkehr vom ursprünglichen Wesen der Wirklichkeit. Erst die neue Form der Logik, die zugleich mit der modernen Wissenschaft entstanden ist, hat diesen Einwand und diese Schwierigkeit wahrhaft aufzuklären und zu beseitigen vermocht. Denn die echte Allgemeinheit, die sie fordert und verbürgt, eignet nicht den Gattungsdingen, sondern den fundamentalen Urteilen und Beziehungen, die den Prozeß der Erfahrung beherrschen und leiten. Von Gesetzen und Relationen wird ausgegangen, denen, wie den reinen mathematischen Gebilden, kein einzelnes empirisches Abbild unmittelbar entspricht. Ihm ist die Induktion, die auch der Grund der universellen Axiome und Prinzipien sein will, nichts anderes als eine bloße Sammlung und Häufung der Einzelbeobachtungen: „experimentorum multorum coacervatio“. To such a process are traced back all the relations and categories of pure science, the concepts of space and time, of activity and suffering, of form and matter. For to ground and derive a content means to lead it, insofar as it is in and for itself insufficient and uncertain, back to another, securer origin. Where the need for "rational" deduction still exists, that is proof that the highest goal of evidence has not yet been reached, that a final support, which can lie only in an immediate perception, is still lacking. Reason, which extracts the common features from a group of objects, cannot for precisely that reason equal sensation in determinacy, since sensation discloses to us the full concretion of the object: it is an "imperfect sense" that grasps the object not in its own nature but according to its generic similarity with other things of the same kind1).

All these discussions, however, possess from the very beginning in Campanella's metaphysics a peculiar counter-image that corrects and supplements them. The work begins with a developed theory of skepticism, which is nowhere asserted as its own final doctrine, yet by the fullness and exactness with which it is presented proves itself to be an important conditioning moment of the whole logical development. In fact, on closer view skepticism is the natural correlate and necessary reverse side of the ideal of knowledge sketched thus far. We grasp outer things only in the self-movements of our mind: but what means could teach us to separate the accidental and the essential motion, the foreign addition and the true being of the object? There is nowhere any escape from the circle of subjectivity; "each possesses a philosophy of his own, according as he is sensibly determined by things in a different way." Let no one think to rise above this individual limitation through any other, higher means of consciousness; for, as we have seen, they are all entangled in and bound to the conditionedness of "sense." Above all the abstract universal concept, to which one likes to entrust insight into the "essence" of things, is powerless for this purpose in view of its origin and derivation. Whoever knows only the "essence" of the human being, that is, only the general features and marks common to all individuals of the species alike, is thereby denied insight into the varieties and particularities that first make up the actual being of this or that one. Here an inner antinomy reveals itself, to which all human knowing necessarily leads us back again and again. For us, "to know" means to compress an infinite multiplicity of individual impressions and individual cases into an abbreviated formula; but who assures us that in this necessary labor of "abstraction" precisely the essential moments of the case are not overlooked and sacrificed? We believe that by disregarding the particular marks we rise to higher generality, whereas in doing so we only lose more and more sight of the sharp and clear outlines of the individual, retaining not breadth but only blurredness of the image2).

The traditional logical opposition between the content and extension of concepts does in fact conceal an inner difficulty and a problem that cannot be solved from Campanella's premises. If cognition is seriously assigned the task of reproducing concrete existing things directly and individually, then the generic concepts themselves become rigid thing-like formations, copies of the objects that give them back not in their original freshness but in superficial and faded form. Each new step and each new achievement of thought becomes thus a further departure from the original essence of reality. Only the new form of logic that arose together with modern science was able truly to clarify and remove this objection and difficulty. For the genuine universality it demands and secures belongs not to genus-things, but to the fundamental judgments and relations that govern and guide the process of experience. It proceeds from laws and relations, to which, just as to pure mathematical formations, no individual empirical image directly corresponds. And when the demand for more concrete determination arises, it can be satisfied only by discovering a new relation that supplements and interpenetrates the previous one. Here the particular case under consideration always appears only as the intersection where the lines of several laws meet; here the universal always appears as the means and the ever-fruitful motive that teaches us to discover the individual. Only on this path do we truly understand cognition as a progressive act of determination, whereas under the image of "abstraction" it appears instead as growing indeterminacy3). Campanella closed this solution off from himself because he starts from another concept of experience than that of the new science. For him induction, which would also ground universal axioms and principles, is nothing but a mere collection and piling-up of individual observations: `experimentorum multorum coacervatio`. With this single phrase he separates himself from Galileo's ideal of experience. Like Galileo, he loves to repeat that philosophy is written solely in the book of nature lying open before our eyes; but the letters by which we are to decipher it are for him not "lines, triangles, and circles," but the subjective qualities and perceptions of the individual senses. From this standpoint the skeptical objections he himself raises cannot in fact be fundamentally overcome: they lie at the ground of his concept of cognition and are already preformed within it. But precisely because of that they are more than a merely rhetorical accessory of his metaphysics, as one might at first take them to be; they contain at the same time a self-reflection that leads onward to new questions.

For now not only the object but even the subject of cognition itself is threatened with the loss of its truth and essence. In the act of cognition the I becomes estranged from itself: it loses its own being in order to exchange it for a foreign one. To "grasp" things means to be seized by them and to sink into them. Thus there is no longer any boundary separating knowledge from madness: `scire est alienari, alienari est ...`

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) Campanella explicitly lowers reason beneath sensation: thinking is only a distant and confused form of sensing, while sense needs no proof because it serves as proof.

2) The skeptical core is that abstraction may achieve generality only by sacrificing the essential singularity of the case.

3) Cassirer's own answer is stated directly here: genuine universality belongs to laws and relations, and concretion comes from further determination, not from genus-copies.
```

**Notes**

- Campanella's skepticism is not decorative. Cassirer treats it as the natural reverse side of the same epistemic ideal that has been governing the chapter.
- Once cognition is defined as abbreviation of singular impressions, abstraction appears as blur and loss rather than as a gain in determination.
- The escape route is stated explicitly in Cassirer's own terms: the new logic of relations treats universality as law-governed structure, not as a pale generic copy of things.
- Campanella cannot take that route because his `experience` remains aggregate observation, `experimentorum multorum coacervatio`, rather than Galilean construction and measurement.
- The pressure now turns back on the subject itself: if knowing means being transformed into the thing, then alienation and madness stand structurally close to knowledge.
