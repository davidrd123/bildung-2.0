# Part 85: Kepler, Geometric Archetypes, and the Construction of Perception

Cassirer now shows how Kepler's relation-model actually transforms perception. Sensory species and causal affection still remain in the outer vocabulary, but the decisive work is no longer done there. Harmony is judged by inner archetypes of order, Platonic ideas are retained only as relations for thought rather than hypostatized things, geometry is privileged over bare number, and even the eye is explained from the mind's immanent grasp of spatial form. Kepler's optics therefore becomes a theory of constructed spatial order: distances and positions are not simply given, but achieved through intellectual acts working on sensory indications.

```text
... caught in the Aristotelian definition of light as the "energy of the transparent" and in the scholastic conception of the opposition of light to the absolute darkness of matter. An exact physical way of representing the matter has not been attained: light remains an immaterial essence to which we can ascribe local motion only in a transferred sense, since in truth it spreads and continues timelessly. Analogously, every sense-perception in general is held to be conditioned by "effluxes" from the objects and therefore designates merely a receptive and suffering state of the sensing organ. If, however, the material of perception, and thus the first basis for every comparison of sensible representations, must be given from outside, the positing of relations itself that we perceive between different contents is nevertheless not to be made intelligible from these material presuppositions. What the senses offer us remains a confused chaotic mass of relationless impressions: unity and connectedness first arise in apprehension and judgment through the intellect, which transfers these predicates from itself to the objects. Let no one object that the soul in the act of comparison only finds the proportion between the contents, not creates it. Spirit would not be able to apprehend and recognize the harmonious relations as such if it did not compare the given contents with the "archetype" of order and consonance that it bears within itself. The "proportion" may at most already be given along with the contents that come from outside, although even this holds only in an improper sense: that it is, however, designated as harmonious and distinguished from other disharmonious relations always includes an act of judgment and valuation that is possible only because we relate the actual impression to constant ideal measures within ourselves.

Relation to the Platonic Theory of Ideas.

It is Ficino's version and grounding of Platonism that is evidently still at work here. Since Kepler thinks the "archetypes" of the beautiful and the true from the beginning in the form of relations, not in the form of things, every temptation is lacking here to hypostatize them as absolute objects outside all knowledge. They "exist" only for a spirit that thinks them; they are objects of a divine or human consciousness, although they are not dependent on this or that particular subject and its accidental peculiarities. To strip them of every relation to thought as such would mean to rob them of all significance and all content of which they are logically capable. Aristotle's critique of the Platonic theory of ideas is therefore unable to unsettle Kepler: indeed, in close connection with Proclus, he analyzes it in detail and seeks to refute it. This critique, as he aptly shows, misses the actual target insofar as it heedlessly passes over the peculiarity of pure concepts of relation.

Characteristic of the direction of his mathematical interest and of his mathematical genius is the fact that he proceeds not from number but from space, not from magnitude in general but from geometrical shape. He expressly surrenders the abstract universal concept of number to nominalistic criticism: the relations between mere numbers have in themselves and in isolation no cognitive value, but receive their significance only from the counted things, from the astronomical configurations and motions in whose representation they are employed. As regards the continuous magnitude of geometry, by contrast, it is emphasized again and again that we recognize and rediscover it in physical objects only because we possess its ideal prototype in our own thought. It is not the perception of the eye that brings forth in us the cognition of geometrical forms; rather, conversely, the eye itself and its peculiar function can be understood and made into an object of knowledge only on the basis of these very forms. Even if no sensible eye had ever been bestowed upon it, spirit would therefore, out of itself and according to pure geometrical laws, invent the eye and postulate it as an instrument for the cognition of external reality. "For the knowledge of quantities, innate in the soul, determines what kind of eye there must be: the structure of the eye is thus ordered according to the nature of spirit, not the reverse."

At this point, however, it becomes clear at the same time how closely Kepler's idealism is bound up with his concept of scientific experience - indeed, how experience itself is what progressively wins ever new tasks for itself out of this idealism. For here we stand before the general fundamental motif from which Kepler's optics developed. The philosophical intention of optics is directed toward proving that the perception of spatial reality, the apprehension of the magnitudes and distances of objects, is not something given to us from the beginning, but something first to be won on the basis of determinate intellectual processes. The final spatial articulation and arrangement of positions is a work of the understanding, which admittedly takes its point of departure from the indications of sensation. Here, as one sees, the new correlation between sensibility and thought, already sought before Kepler by Nicholas of Cusa and by Leonardo da Vinci, gains a sharper determination. We may acknowledge sensible contents as genuine beginnings of knowledge because the indication of the mathematical is already given in them as well, and thus determinate relations of thought are prefigured in them. Sensation already contains, although hidden and unclear, the pure intellectual harmonies.
```

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) Kepler still speaks in inherited optical language, but Cassirer's point is that the decisive work has already shifted away from physical species toward intellectual relation.

2) The crucial move is evaluative: proportion only becomes harmony by reference to an inner archetype of order carried by spirit itself.

3) This is why Kepler's Platonism avoids hypostasis. Ideas are relations for thought, not objects floating outside cognition.

4) Geometry now outranks bare number, and the eye is explained from the mind's prior grasp of geometric form rather than the reverse.

5) Kepler's optics therefore becomes a construction-theory of spatial perception: distances and positions are achievements of intellect working from sensory signs.
```
