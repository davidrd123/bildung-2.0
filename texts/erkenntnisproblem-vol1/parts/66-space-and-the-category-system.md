# Part 66: Space and the Category System

Patrizzi's space-concept is where the scientific concept of space first breaks openly with the scholastic categories: space becomes a `Mittleres` beyond substance and accident, body and spirit, but the attempted metaphysical reconciliation immediately turns it into primordial physical ground.

Wenn diese Sätze einen Fortschritt in der Unterscheidung der reinen Ordnungsprinzipien von dem Wahrnehmungsstoff, der sich in ihnen darstellt, enthalten, so sehen wir doch, daß die Erkenntnistheorie des Telesio selbst diese Sonderung nicht tiefer zu begründen vermag: der „Sinn“, auf dessen unmittelbares Zeugnis wir auch hier verwiesen werden, bietet kein Mittel, uns des „reinen Raumes“ wie der reinen Zeit zu versichern. (Vgl. ob. S. 237 f.) In eine neue Phase tritt das Problem im System des Patrizzi ein, in welchem es auf veränderte logische und metaphysische Vorbedingungen trifft. Zwar ist es auch hier zunächst die einfache Wahrnehmung, die die Wahrheit der räumlichen Beziehung und die Existenz des Leeren unmittelbar in sich enthalten und verbürgen soll: „denn wird nicht der Abstand zwischen Erde und Himmel ebenso direkt wie diese Objekte selbst vom Gesichtssinn erfaßt?“ Wer diese Grundtatsache der Empfindung leugnet, für den ist alle dialektische Untersuchung verloren: „cernentibus enim et mente praeditis nostra scribimus“. Alle üblichen Ordnungen und Einteilungen versagen für die Charakteristik dieses eigentümlichen sinnlichen Datums. Was ist jener Raum, der vor der Welt voranging, der sie umfaßt und jenseit ihrer eine eigene Erstreckung und Ausbreitung besitzt? Ist er die bloße Fähigkeit (`aptitudo`), körperliche Inhalte aufzunehmen? Oder kommt ihm eine Art dinglicher Wesenheit zu: ist er somit als Substanz oder als Accidens, als körperlich oder unkörperlich zu denken? Keine dieser Bestimmungen trifft zu; denn sie alle sind nur Mittel, die Dinge in der Welt zu bezeichnen, können daher nicht zur Charakteristik des Raumes dienen, der keinem Gegenstande, er sei materiell oder immateriell, als Merkmal oder Beschaffenheit anhängt. Ein neuer Gesichtspunkt der Betrachtung ist daher zu fordern: eine philosophische Beurteilung, die sich nicht auf die herkömmlichen logischen Kategorien beschränkt und einengt. Wir können etwa dem Raum „Größe“ zusprechen: aber nur insofern, als wir ihn damit nicht der Kategorie der Quantität unterordnen, sondern als deren Quell und Ursprung bezeichnen wollen. Wir können ihn eine Substanz im wahrsten und tiefsten Sinne dieses Begriffes nennen, da er, ohne sich selbst auf ein anderes Sein zu stützen, der ganzen Körperwelt ihren Halt und ihre Unterlage gibt: — aber auch hier müssen wir uns gegenwärtig halten, daß die bekannten Unterscheidungen, die wir in der Kategorie der Substanz mitzudenken pflegen, für ihn ihre Gültigkeit verlieren. Denn er ist weder ein einzelnes Ding, das sich aus Materie und Form zusammensetzt, noch auch ein Gattungsbegriff, da er keine Unterarten in sich faßt, von denen er als allgemeine Bestimmung prädiziert werden könnte. Er ist weder materiell noch immateriell, da ihm auf der einen Seite die Fähigkeit des Widerstandes, also die Grundeigenschaft der körperlichen Naturen abgeht, auf der anderen Seite dagegen das Merkmal der Ausdehnung zukommt, das ihn von allem geistigen Sein unterscheidet. So erkennen wir in ihm überall ein „Mittleres“ zwischen Gegensätzen, die wir gewohnt sind, als ausschließend und contradiktorisch zu betrachten: „corpus incorporeum est et non corpus corporeum“2).

In diesem Oxymoron vollendet sich die Charakteristik, in der zum erstenmal der Raumbegriff der Naturwissenschaft seine Selbständigkeit gegenüber dem scholastischen Begriffs- und Kategoriensystem gewinnt. ...conceptual essence1). If these sentences contain an advance in distinguishing the pure principles of order from the stuff of perception displayed in them, we nonetheless see that Telesio's own theory of knowledge cannot ground this distinction any more deeply: the "sense," to whose immediate testimony we are referred here as well, offers no means of assuring us of "pure space" any more than of pure time. A new phase of the problem appears in Patrizzi's system, where it encounters altered logical and metaphysical presuppositions. Here too it is at first simple perception that is supposed immediately to contain and guarantee the truth of spatial relation and the existence of the void: "for is not the distance between earth and heaven grasped by sight just as directly as these objects themselves?" Whoever denies this basic fact of sensation, for him all dialectical inquiry is lost: `cernentibus enim et mente praeditis nostra scribimus`. And yet this naive certainty must immediately be shaken as soon as one tries to fix the essence of space in determinate concepts and assign it to one of the classes by which we are accustomed to divide all being. All the usual orders and divisions fail for the characterization of this peculiar sensible datum. What is that space that preceded the world, encompasses it, and possesses an extension and spread of its own beyond it? Is it the mere capacity (`aptitudo`) to receive bodily contents? Or does some kind of thing-like essence belong to it: is it therefore to be thought as substance or as accident, as bodily or as incorporeal? None of these determinations fits, for they are all only means of designating things in the world and can therefore not serve to characterize space, which inheres in no object, material or immaterial, as a mark or property. A new point of view is therefore required: a philosophical assessment that does not confine and constrict itself to the inherited logical categories. We can, for example, ascribe "magnitude" to space, but only insofar as we do not subordinate it thereby to the category of quantity, but wish to designate it as quantity's source and origin. We can call it a substance in the truest and deepest sense of the concept, since, without itself resting on another being, it gives the whole world of bodies its support and substrate. Yet here too we must keep in mind that the familiar distinctions we ordinarily think together with the category of substance lose their validity in its case. For space is neither an individual thing composed of matter and form, nor a genus-concept, since it contains no sub-species of which it could be predicated as a universal determination. It is neither material nor immaterial, since on the one hand it lacks the capacity of resistance, the basic property of bodily natures, while on the other hand it possesses extension, which distinguishes it from all spiritual being. Thus we recognize in it everywhere a "middle" between opposites that we are accustomed to regard as excluding and contradictory: `corpus incorporeum est et non corpus corporeum`2).

In this oxymoron the characterization is completed in which, for the first time, the scientific concept of space wins its independence over against the scholastic system of concepts and categories. The questions raised here will meet us once again, in complete agreement, at a high point of mathematical natural science, in the works of Leonhard Euler. They have been compared with Kant's problem-setting in the Inaugural Dissertation, from which they are nonetheless separated in principle by the metaphysical nexus to which they belong. Yet even here a steady historical mediation between the extremes can be shown, insofar as Patrizzi's doctrine continues to operate on the one hand in Gassendi's foundation of physics, and on the other in the speculative spiritualism of Henry More, both of which in turn became models for the Newtonian doctrine of space.

The logical analysis has demonstrated the complete difference in essence between space and body: now it becomes necessary, from the standpoint of metaphysics, to seek a reconciliation of this opposition and its cancellation within a higher unity. Once again the concept of development is given this task: the two moments are to be understood as two opposite phases of one and the same process of becoming, in which primordial being unfolds successively and determines itself toward concrete actuality. Space must here necessarily take first place: it is, after all, that whose positing first makes all other contents possible and whose cancellation would also annihilate all the other elements. And just as it is the presupposition of all material things, so too all physical determinations whatever, especially all qualities such as heat and cold, light and darkness, that penetrate bodies or adhere to their surfaces, are to be thought as dependent on it. All these qualitative marks are not indeed as such to be regarded as spatial quanta, but they participate mediately, through their relation to bodies, in the nature of space and in the possibility of fixed quantitative determination3). Among the basic qualities themselves there further reigns a firm natural rank-order: the first stage of physical fulfillment is light, which permeates space on all sides and out of which "warmth" and "fluidity" further develop as progressive degrees of condensation. Every empirical body partakes in determinate measure of these four elements (`spatium`, `lumen`, `calor`, `fluor`)4). **Working Commentary**

```text
1) Telesio's move toward independent space and time still cannot be epistemically grounded from `Sinn` alone; that limitation is stated plainly at the opening.

2) Patrizzi's real novelty is not merely that he posits space, but that space breaks the inherited category grid. It is neither substance nor accident, neither material nor immaterial, and appears as a `Mittleres`.

3) `Corpus incorporeum est et non corpus corporeum` is the sharpest local formula of the break. Cassirer treats this oxymoron as the point where the scientific space-concept first becomes autonomous against scholastic logic.

4) The metaphysical repair immediately carries a cost: space is then made the first phase of becoming and the ground of physical qualities, so the logical condition is converted into primordial stuff.
```

**Notes**

- The tranche opens by saying directly that Telesio's own theory of knowledge cannot justify pure space and pure time, even if his physics points toward them.
- Patrizzi then shifts the issue: the problem is no longer only whether space is perceived, but how it can be thought when every standard category misfires.
- Cassirer's strongest local claim is that the space-concept of natural science first becomes self-standing here, precisely by refusing the scholastic alternatives.
- The historical line is explicit: Patrizzi's problem will recur at Euler and is historically mediated forward through Gassendi and Henry More toward Newton.
- The metaphysical reconciliation is also the new danger. Space is made prior in the process of becoming and begins to determine light, warmth, and fluidity.
- That is already the limit of the whole construction: space ceases to be a logical condition and hardens into physical ground.
- The tranche stops mid-sentence on printed page `263`, with the consequences for the concept of absolute extension still unfolding.
