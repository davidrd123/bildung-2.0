Es ist somit aus einem innerlichen sachlichen Zusammenhang zu erklären, daß sich der Kampf um das Weltsystem alsbald zu der allgemeinen Frage nach den Grenzen der Religion und Wissenschaft, des Glaubens und der Vernunft erweitert. Wenn man die Akten des Galileischen Prozesses studiert, so staunt man über die Klarheit und Schärfe, in der diese Fragestellung von beiden Seiten erfaßt und festgehalten wird. Lange zuvor hatte Galilei selbst in seinem bekannten Brief an die Großherzogin Mutter Christine von Lothringen die scharfe Scheidelinie zwischen der theoretischen und praktischen Bedeutung der Religion gezogen, die er in seiner Verteidigung durchgehend einhält. Diese Rückwirkung der neuen Weltansicht auf das Selbstbewußtsein des Individuums stellt sich am deutlichsten in der Persönlichkeit und den Werken Giordano Brunos dar.

Es ist nicht, wie zumeist angenommen wird, der allgemeine Begriff der Natur, der Bruno zum Vorgänger Keplers und Galileis stempelt. It is thus to be explained from an inner objective connection that the struggle over the world-system at once expands into the general question of the limits of religion and science, of faith and reason. When one studies the records of Galileo's trial, one is struck by the clarity and sharpness with which both sides grasped and held fast to this way of posing the problem. The original starting-point recedes more and more before the new, deeper, and more comprehensive issue. Long before this, Galileo himself in his well-known letter to the Grand Duchess Mother Christine of Lorraine had drawn the sharp line between the theoretical and practical significance of religion, a line he maintains throughout his defense. If nature and Holy Scripture are here both equally acknowledged as witnesses of divine revelation, there can nevertheless be no doubt about the rank-order between these two basic sources. The secure experiences of the senses and the demonstrative conclusions that we build upon them must count for us as the first and indubitable basis, from which alone we may then attempt the interpretation of the biblical books and the determination of their true and contradiction-free meaning. The thought of a "double truth" is clearly and securely set aside: it is impossible that the same God who endowed us with senses, understanding, and insight, and in them gave us the means for the clear apprehension of reality, should at the same time have wished to instruct us about the being of natural things in another way through immediate revelation in Scripture. All statements and judgments about the existence of objects are to be won solely through the testimony of scientific experience and in the regulated prescribed course of its method. In the necessity disclosed to us here, one reads a firm and immovable criterion that can be restricted or surpassed by no other instance. If Scripture expresses the will and essence of divinity in a form adapted to the understanding and capacity of the people, and thus capable of changing and varied interpretation, then before nature itself as the unique and unchanging self-testimony of divinity every doubt and ambiguity disappears. The mathematical characters in which it is written exclude every arbitrary construal, every reinterpretation according to the accidental wishes and ends of the individual. "Why in the knowledge of the universe and its parts should we begin rather with the investigation of God's words than with that of his works? Or is perhaps the work less noble and excellent than the word?" All truth of facts falls under the jurisdiction of the scientific concept, while the task of religion and its documents is placed solely in the mediation of moral "truths of salvation." Only with this delimitation are the different directions and capacities of consciousness brought into inward agreement, and the indissoluble unity and necessity of the intellect itself secured. For Galileo, the assertion of the Copernican world-system becomes equivalent to the self-assertion of reason.

This repercussion of the new worldview upon the self-consciousness of the individual appears most clearly in the personality and works of Giordano Bruno.

It is not, as is mostly assumed, the general concept of nature that marks Bruno as a precursor of Kepler and Galileo. However important his thoughts are for the reform of cosmology, they still by no means amount, in comparison with the circle of natural philosophy from which he emerges, to a wholly changed direction of investigation and research. The modern concept of mathematical causality remains entirely foreign to Bruno. All efficacy among the individual members of the universe counts for him as mediated through the working of a common psychic principle in which they all equally partake. The concept of the world-soul is the necessary correlate to the thought of causal connection. We can understand the dependence among spatially separated elements of being only by positing an original unified common spirit that permeates the universe and that, according to the special conditions of organization, comes to expression now more clearly, now more confusedly, in the various genera and individuals. This "inner sense," by virtue of which every limited finite being partakes in the infinite life of the whole while at the same time striving to assert itself in its individuality, belongs not only to higher organisms but to every individual being as such: "not without a form of sense or consciousness, unnoticed though it is by superficial philosophy, do water-drops themselves gather into the spherical shape most fitting to their self-preservation." Sympathy and antipathy, striving and counter-striving therefore form the basic type of all natural events. No body, insofar as it is considered as mere matter, is able as such to act upon another body; rather every genuine activity must have its origin in the form, in the inner "quality" of the elements, and thus ultimately in the soul. The possibility of cognition too rests on this connection: on the continuity and community linking the individual subject, as a part of the universe, with all its other members. The general world-picture that opens before us here is, as one sees, not in principle different from that of Fracastoro, indeed not even from that of Agrippa of Nettesheim. Even within astronomy Bruno holds fast to the Peripatetic view of the ensoulment of the heavenly bodies: he once again clothes the doctrine of the "blessed movers" of the stars with all the charm that artistic imagination can lend this image.

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) Galilei's Copernican defense now becomes a full delimitation of domains: truth of fact belongs to scientific method, while religion is restricted to moral and salvific truth.

2) Cassirer states the strong version clearly: Copernicanism is not just a hypothesis about the heavens, but the self-assertion of reason.

3) Bruno's first presentation is intentionally deflationary. Cassirer refuses to make him modern simply because he radicalizes cosmology.

4) The limiting residue is clear: world-soul, sympathy, inner sense, and ensouled heavens still carry Bruno's causality and cognition.

5) The real difference is therefore being relocated from doctrinal content to `Begründung` and epistemic foundation. That is the live edge of the tranche.
```

**Notes**

- The Galilei section removes `doppelte Wahrheit` and subordinates scriptural interpretation to secure experience and demonstrative inference wherever facts are at issue.
- Nature, because written in mathematical characters, becomes the uniquely unambiguous self-testimony of God.
- This is why Cassirer can say that the Copernican system becomes equivalent to the self-assertion of reason.
- Bruno then arrives under a warning sign: not yet mathematical causality, but world-soul and universal psychic mediation.
- Cassirer explicitly places Bruno much closer than expected to Fracastoro and Agrippa at the level of world-picture.
- The open question is now sharper than before: if Bruno is still so entangled with older Naturphilosophie in content, where exactly does the break in foundation begin?
