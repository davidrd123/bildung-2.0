Der Neuplatonismus bezeichnet den allgemeinen Charakter der Lehre Ficins, aber er erschöpft nicht ihren gesamten Gehalt und ihre geschichtliche Bedeutung. Wenn man bisher in der Darstellung von Ficins Platonismus einzig bei diesem Zuge verweilte, so hat man darüber gerade die kräftigsten und fruchtbarsten Keime, die er für die Philosophie und Wissenschaft der Zukunft enthielt, übersehen. Ficins Hauptwerk: die „Theologia Platonica de immortalitate animorum“ ist, äußerlich betrachtet, freilich nichts anderes als ein Kompendium von verschiedenen Unsterblichkeitsbeweisen, die hier so ausführlich und vollständig wie an keiner anderen Stelle der Geschichte der Philosophie dargestellt und erörtert werden. Schon die geschichtlichen Anfänge des Unsterblichkeitsproblems aber müssen uns darüber belehren, daß die Wege und Schicksale dieser Frage mit den Grundfragen der Theorie des Erkennens aufs engste verknüpft und verschwistert sind. Der `Phaedon` enthält zugleich die eingehendste und umfassendste logische Grundlegung der Ideenlehre, die Platon gegeben hat. Hier zuerst wird das „reine Denken“ in seiner Selbständigkeit und Kraft erkannt und von allen anderen psychologischen Instanzen geschieden. Der Gedanke der Unsterblichkeit wird zum Vehikel für die Entdeckung der Ursprünglichkeit der Denkfunktion und für ihre scharfe Abgrenzung gegen die unmittelbare sinnliche Empfindung und Wahrnehmung.

Die moderne Auffassung ist, wie wir sehen werden, schon von den Zeiten der Renaissance an darauf gerichtet, diesen geschichtlichen Zusammenhang zwischen metaphysischer und erkenntnistheoretischer Fragestellung zu lockern. Dennoch behauptet er sich bis weit über die Anfänge der neueren Philosophie hinaus und noch bei Descartes läßt sich seine Kraft und Wirksamkeit beobachten. Es begreift sich hieraus, daß Ficins Lehre auch dort, wo sie einzig ihr metaphysisches Hauptziel zu verfolgen scheint, indirekt dennoch in die Geschichte des Erkenntnisproblems eingreift. Vor allem bleibt es ihr Verdienst, daß sie zuerst die Platonische Lehre von der `Wiedererinnerung` der Folgezeit rein und vollständig überliefert und daß sie damit der modernen Entwicklung des Bewußtseinsbegriffs einen festen historischen Mittelpunkt geschaffen hat. Um die Unsterblichkeit des Geistes zu erweisen, nimmt Ficin seinen Ausgang vor allem von der Unendlichkeit seiner Funktion. Jeder echte Begriff, den wir bilden, enthält eine unbegrenzte Anzahl einzelner Exemplare unter sich; jeder Akt des Denkens besitzt und betätigt die Wunderkraft, eine unendliche Mannigfaltigkeit ins Eins zu fassen und eine einfachste Einheit wiederum in die Unendlichkeit aufgehen zu lassen. Und wie sollte der Geist nicht seiner Kraft und Wesenheit nach unumschränkt sein, da er es ist, der die Unendlichkeit selbst entdeckt und sie nach ihrer Art und Beschaffenheit definiert? Alle Erkenntnis bedeutet eine Ausgleichung und Anpassung des erkennenden Subjekts an die Objekte, die sich ihm gegenüberstellen (`cognitio per quandam mentis cum rebus aequationem perficitur`); das Unendliche könnte somit von uns nicht als Inhalt gedacht und erfaßt werden, wenn es nicht in der eigenen Natur des Geistes enthalten und angelegt wäre. Das Maß darf, sofern es adaequat und erschöpfend sein soll, an Kraft und Umfang nirgends hinter dem Gemessenen zurückbleiben: so muß der Geist selbst schrankenlos sein, um den stetigen Wandel der Zeit und der Bewegung seinen unwandelbaren Begriffen unterwerfen und die Unendlichkeit umfassen und messen zu können.1)

Es ist somit keine Erklärung des Erkenntnisprozesses, wenn man ein äußeres jenseitiges Sein in den Geist hinüberwandern läßt: denn das Denken begreift in Wahrheit nur das, was mit ihm von derselben Natur ist und was es aus seinem eigenen Grunde hervorbringt. In this assertion of the unique cosmic position and significance of the human soul lies the deepest ground of the effect the Platonic Academy exercised upon the general philosophical and artistic culture of the age; the thoughts Ficino utters here resound in Pico della Mirandola's `Oration on the Dignity of Man` and, with the highest force and penetration, in Michelangelo's sonnets. Yet however much we still find ourselves here within the spell of Plotinus and his aesthetic fundamental doctrines, a new interest already shines through at this point, one that points toward an altered line of questioning. Neoplatonism designates the general character of Ficino's doctrine, but it does not exhaust its whole content and historical significance. If earlier accounts of Ficino's Platonism lingered only over this trait, they thereby overlooked precisely the strongest and most fruitful seeds it contained for the philosophy and science of the future. Ficino's chief work, the `Theologia Platonica de immortalitate animorum`, is admittedly, viewed outwardly, nothing other than a compendium of various proofs of immortality, presented and discussed here as fully and exhaustively as anywhere else in the history of philosophy. Yet the historical beginnings of the problem of immortality themselves must teach us that the paths and fortunes of this question are most intimately linked and twinned with the fundamental questions of the theory of knowledge. The `Phaedo` at the same time contains the most detailed and comprehensive logical grounding of the doctrine of Ideas that Plato ever gave. Here for the first time `pure thought` is recognized in its self-subsistence and power and separated from all other psychological faculties. The thought of immortality becomes the vehicle for discovering the originality of the thinking-function and for sharply distinguishing it from immediate sensible sensation and perception.

As we shall see, the modern view is directed from the times of the Renaissance onward toward loosening this historical connection between metaphysical and epistemological questioning. Yet it asserts itself far beyond the beginnings of modern philosophy, and even in Descartes one can still observe its force and efficacy. It is therefore understandable that Ficino's doctrine, even where it seems to pursue only its metaphysical main aim, nonetheless indirectly intervenes in the history of the problem of knowledge. Above all, its merit remains that it first transmitted Plato's doctrine of `recollection` purely and fully to later ages, and that by doing so it created a firm historical center for the modern development of the concept of consciousness. In order to prove the immortality of spirit, Ficino takes his point of departure above all from the infinity of its function. Every genuine concept that we form contains beneath itself an unlimited number of individual instances; every act of thought possesses and exercises the wondrous power of gathering an infinite multiplicity into one and, conversely, of letting the simplest unity unfold again into infinity. And how should spirit not be unrestricted in power and essence, when it is precisely what discovers infinity itself and defines it according to its kind and constitution? All knowledge signifies an equalization and adaptation of the knowing subject to the objects that stand over against it (`cognitio per quandam mentis cum rebus aequationem perficitur`); the infinite therefore could not be thought and grasped by us as content if it were not contained and grounded in the very nature of spirit. Measure, if it is to be adequate and exhaustive, may nowhere lag behind what is measured in power and extent: thus spirit itself must be without limit if it is to subject the constant change of time and motion to its unchanging concepts and to comprehend and measure infinity.1)

In general, the demand for a thoroughgoing correspondence and `proportion` that must reign between the object and the function of knowledge now becomes the guiding thought of Ficino's doctrine. Intellect and the `intelligible` object do not stand over against one another as foreign and external; they are of the same origin and coincide in their highest completion. `Ipsum intelligibile propria est intellectus perfectio unde intellectus in actu et intelligibile in actu sunt unum`.2) (Compare above, pp. 38 and 56.) It is therefore no explanation of the process of knowledge to let an outer transcendent being migrate over into spirit: for thought in truth grasps only what is of the same nature as itself and what it brings forth from its own ground. This holds not only for the higher spiritual activities, but already for simple sense-perception: even there consciousness is not determined solely by the bodies outside it, but gives itself its own form. `Just as a living body changes, reproduces, nourishes, and increases through seeds lying within itself, so spirit and inner sense judge all things by means of innate forms that are stimulated to activity from without.` The content of consciousness is therefore not so much an image of the outer object as an imprint of our own spiritual power: just as one and the same object appears differently to us according as this or that psychic faculty, according as sense, imagination, or reason, contemplates and forms it. `Judgment follows the form and nature of the one judging, not of the object judged.`

Even the `images` of individual things that imagination sketches are not immediately pressed and `burned into` spirit by the things themselves: all the more, then, must we recognize in pure intellectual concepts not the copies of an external reality, but the products of the power of understanding. It is vain to try to derive the content of these concepts from sensible perceptions and images: for how could the sensible `phantasma` produce something freer and more comprehensive than itself? The corporeal world forms an unrelated multiplicity of particular and restricted individual objects; considered in themselves, these can never create a pure spiritual content that would reproduce and represent the common nature shared by them all. And what is denied to the elements in their separateness, their sum can never accomplish either. For even their gathering into an aggregate always gives us only scattered members without determinate lawful order and connection. `Just as nothing truly simple arises from an accumulation of stones, but only a mere heap, so the multitude of individual things may indeed yield a confused togetherness of images, but not the one and simple concept.` With clarity and decisiveness, Ficino refutes the sensualist theory of `abstraction`. If we were dependent on gaining the universal by running through individual cases, we would have to recognize in it from the very beginning a false and illusory demand. **Notes**

- Cassirer now states the point explicitly: Ficino's immortality doctrine matters for the history of knowledge because the `Phaedo` binds the problem of immortality to the discovery of pure thought's autonomy.
- Ficino's transmission of Plato's doctrine of `Wiedererinnerung` becomes, for Cassirer, a historical center for the modern development of the concept of consciousness.
- The infinity argument is decisive: mind can think and measure the infinite only because infinity is native to its own function, not imported from outside.
- The governing principle is now `correspondence` or `proportion` between object and cognitive function, which pushes Ficino toward a strongly productive account of knowledge.
- The anti-abstraction argument is the tranche's payoff so far: concepts are not copied from images or abstracted from a heap of particulars, but produced from the mind's own lawful power.
- The tranche ends mid-sentence exactly where Cassirer is about to say what positive model must replace abstraction.
