# Part 93: Kepler, Geometry, Scientific Possibility, and the Law of the Nonuniform

Cassirer now turns the Kepler line into an explicit methodology of law. Euclid is defended against Ramist objections because an element is not raw material but a form of connection. Geometry therefore remains the model of deduction, and even the dignity of pure number depends on its relation to spatial form. The seven-gon case sharpens the point: algebra can define a magnitude without thereby granting it scientific existence. Scientific possibility requires describability. At the same time, Kepler's empirical work forces a loosening of his older fusion of law and shape. The decisive step is the ellipse: with it, the lawfulness of the nonuniform first becomes scientifically real.

... and against modern objections once again strengthens the authority of Euclid. Petrus Ramus had raised against Euclid the reproach that he denies the true methodological order by placing a complex of heterogeneous definitions at the head, instead of first creating for each individual domain and each individual problem, only when it has been reached in the progress of conceptual development, the special logical substructure. "For when nature wishes to create the forest, it does not first bring forth the roots of all trees, and the architect, when building a city, does not first lay the foundation of all buildings." Against such a superficial consideration and judgment Kepler once again brings into the field his deeper insight into the essence of scientific "hypothesis." This is the basic error of his opponents, that they are unable to grasp the true sense of the "elements": that under them they understand only a manifold relationless quantity of concepts and theorems, applicable to all kinds of magnitudes and serviceable for their scientific treatment. In this view the architect of the edifice of geometry is degraded to a mere day laborer, who has to procure the material and at most work it up. For Euclid, however, the proper significance of the element lies not in the matter but in the form: it is not the `stoicheion`, but the `stoicheiosis`, the mode and necessity of connection, that holds him fast. Geometry thus remains for Kepler the model and guiding measure for every kind of conceptual deduction. The rank-order established by Kepler's first work, the `Mysterium Cosmographicum`, remains normative for his further scientific creation. The task set there, to lead back the structure of the universe to the shape of the five regular bodies, gradually deepened and widened; yet the thought always remains dominant that in the geometrical images and forms engraved and given to spirit the "archetype" of the external world is contained.

All dignity that we may ascribe to pure number, too, stems in the end and originally from geometry; abstract number, if it gave up connection with shape, would sink down to a mere generic concept, in the language of scholastic terminism to a `conceptus secundae intentionis`. The attempt of the "Pythagoreans and Platonists" to build up the world of things in its substantial content out of the relations and properties of pure numbers is therefore expressly rejected; it is only conditionally and with essential restrictions correct when Kepler's doctrine has been characterized as "empirical Pythagoreanism." Precisely this distinguishes Kepler from Descartes, that in his whole way of thinking and investigating he is still wholly rooted in the synthetic geometry of the ancients, whereas in Descartes the problem of space, though likewise central, is already grasped in a methodological transformation by which it appears only as a special case and example of the universal concept of magnitude. -

Kepler himself characterized with full clarity the fundamental difference of the ways of considering the matter in his work on the "Harmony of the World" in the discussion of the regular plane polygons. If we set ourselves the task, it is there explained, of dividing a given circle into seven equal parts by an inscribed regular polygon, we soon find that it is insoluble, that there is no geometrical procedure by which the side of the required polygon could be represented. Here we stand immediately before a demand that geometrical construction refuses, although according to its purely conceptual character it does not recognizably differ from other solvable tasks, such as the representation of the regular square and pentagon. Algebraic analysis, to be sure (`doctrina analytica ab Arabe Gebri denominata Algebra, Italico vocabulo cossa`), is able fully to define the required side and to express it in an equation; it may therefore regard it as a fixed magnitude different from all others. But with this kind of determinacy nothing is yet said about its "existence": rather, the requirement remains here that only those contents are "possible" that can prove themselves through intuition. Kepler connects with this an admonition to the "metaphysicians" to improve the old ontological proposition that nothing can have determinations and characteristics if it is non-being; for here, after all, there lies before us a content to which no being belongs in and for itself, but which is nevertheless bounded by certain conditions and to which determinate properties can at least hypothetically be ascribed, insofar as one can say that, if a regular heptagon were inscribed in the circle, its side would have this or that constitution. The genuine scientific being, the `essentia scientificalis`, of an element can in the last sense of course be secured only by geometrical grounding and "description": `scientiae possibilitatem praecedit descriptionis possibilitas`. Even an infinite understanding would possess no "idea," because no inner intuition, of the required polygonal side.

If Apelt has characterized it as a basic trait of the Aristotelian-scholastic concept of "substantial form" that in it two heterogeneous things, "the form of intellectual synthesis and the form of figured synthesis, i.e. law and shape," are united with one another, then this critique also strikes Kepler's original view: the "idea" is in him too at first still wholly interwoven with the "shape" and absorbed into its conditionedness. Only the gradual and steady course of Kepler's empirical research leads to loosening this initial logical connection and dependence. Kepler himself repeatedly reported with what inner difficulties he had to struggle before deciding upon the thought of sacrificing the absolute geometrical "perfection" of the planetary paths, which, as he assumed, could consist only in their strictly circular form. Even in Copernicus we encounter at this point a naively teleological derivation: the heavenly bodies must perform their revolution in circles in order in the act of their motion itself to express their "being," that is, their geometrical form and limitation as spherical figures, and to represent it in the most fitting way. **Working Commentary**

```text
1) Euclid matters here because the element is understood formally, as connection, not as a warehouse of conceptual material.

2) Kepler's geometry-first stance is now explicit enough to distinguish him sharply from both Ramist method-talk and Cartesian generalization of magnitude.

3) The heptagon example is one of the clearest local places where Cassirer separates algebraic definability from scientific existence.

4) The phrase `scientiae possibilitatem praecedit descriptionis possibilitas` is method-bearing. Scientific possibility is tied to describability, not to mere logical consistency.

5) The ellipse is the decisive empirical blow. What observation forces is not only a new orbit-shape, but a new legitimacy for lawful nonuniformity.
```
