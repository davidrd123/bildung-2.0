# Part 73: Bruno, Two Realms, Mathematics Missing, and Analysis

Cassirer now gives Bruno his strongest near-modern moment: sensible and intelligible are treated as distinct domains with distinct conditions of knowledge, and cognition is redirected toward relations and analysis rather than mere genus-abstraction. But the whole construction still fails to become exact science because Bruno cannot justify the correlation of sense and thought without the missing middle term of mathematics.

Imagination proves its middle and double position above all at the concept of the infinite as the ground and expression of metaphysical being: for if sense everywhere presses toward fixed limitation, toward restriction of the world-picture through a closed perceptual horizon, imagination drives beyond every such arbitrarily assumed end and thereby shows itself akin to the intellect, whose character and essence consist in the infinity of its operations. If a point of passage and mediation has thus been found here, the further development of Bruno's concept of nature presses more and more toward recognizing and presenting the two basic moments in relative independence. The last major metaphysical work `De triplici minimo et mensura` shows the completion of this development. The attempt to reinterpret the data of perception from conceptual points of view is here sharply rejected. Each of the two domains possesses its own right and is subject to its own criteria of judgment drawn from itself. Sense is not deceived, since, rightly understood and interpreted, it nowhere claims more than relative truth for itself; its statements therefore never intend validity for the objects as such, but only for their relation to the sensing subject. Just as only sight can judge colors and only hearing tones, so our empirical powers of cognition, despite the limits set to them, are the only fully valid means of making us at home in the world of immediate experience. It would be foolish to want to measure sensible things by a standard created and fitted only for eternal and unchangeable being. There are two wholly different conditions of cognition prevailing in the two cases: "it is stupid reasoning to want to reduce sensibles to the same condition of cognition in which rational and intelligible things are discerned." "The objects of sensation are true, not according to some abstract and universal standard, but according to the homogeneous, particular, and proper measure belonging to them, a measure itself to be taken as mutable and variable. To want to give a universal determination and definition of sensible contents is therefore no different than if one wished to judge the intelligible from the standpoint of sensibility."

The separation of the faculties thus now includes at the same time the recognition of their distinctiveness and of the proper function belonging to them. It almost seems that a standpoint has thereby been reached such as is marked by Kant's habilitation dissertation `De mundi sensibilis atque intelligibilis forma et principiis`: the sensible and intelligible form two strictly separated realms of being, to be grasped and judged according to different principles of cognition. Only for Giordano Bruno there is no way and no means to work out within the world of appearance itself universally valid propositions and relations; here the individual constitution of each individual subject therefore remains the sole guideline. The possibility of an exact and necessary science of phenomena, toward which Bruno's tendency actually aims, is not secured; for although the two powers of cognition are now each appreciated according to their characteristic performance, their cooperation upon the same content of consciousness is not intelligible on the previous presuppositions. The correlation of thought and sensibility, of reason and experience, that Galileo brings to scientific presentation, Bruno cannot definitively ground from the standpoint of his theory of knowledge: what he achieves is merely to anticipate it in his aesthetic intuition of unity. The characteristic feature of Bruno's doctrine, and the explanatory ground for all the contradictions always found in his determination of the relation between immanence and transcendence, is that he did not bring to pure execution in his doctrine of the concept the thought of immanence demanded by his view of nature. His metaphysics demands the unity of all psychic capacities, indeed ultimately their absolute indifference: it is one and the same consciousness that permeates all forms of nature and expresses itself on the lower levels as dull and confused sensation, on the higher and highest as pure activity of reason. But the psychological postulate here set cannot be justified by Bruno's theory of knowledge: we shall be able to follow in detail how, through its renunciation of the genuine middle term of mathematics, it must necessarily fail to reconcile intuition and thought.

Nevertheless, even here one can at least indicate the beginnings pointing toward such an end. If the peculiarity of sensibility is found in its boundless relativity, thinking and scientific comprehension now appear referred to the determination and proof of relations. The point is not to ascend by canceling determinate marks to ever higher but also ever poorer genera, and thus evaporate the particular into general logical abstractions. The way of thought is rather that of analysis: an at first confusedly grasped whole must be broken down into its materially determinate basic moments, and these individual members then recombined into a unity in which they are clearly grasped and presented according to their reciprocal relation to one another. **Working Commentary**

```text
1) Cassirer now grants Bruno the strongest possible separation short of modern science: sensible and intelligible have different conditions of cognition and must not be judged by the same standard.

2) The decisive failure is equally explicit: Bruno cannot produce universally valid relations within the phenomenal realm because he lacks the genuine middle term of mathematics.

3) That is why Galilei becomes the contrast term. Bruno can aesthetically anticipate the correlation of Vernunft and Erfahrung, but cannot ground it scientifically.

4) The positive remainder is important: once sensibility is understood as relative, cognition is redirected toward relations and analysis rather than genus-abstraction.

5) `Analyse` is the term to watch now. Cassirer is explicitly moving toward a relation-centered model of knowing even while saying Bruno cannot fully secure it.
```

**Notes**

- Imagination still mediates at the opening, but the real turn of the tranche is the strong two-domain distinction.
- Cassirer explicitly compares the achieved standpoint to Kant's `De mundi sensibilis atque intelligibilis forma et principiis`.
- The comparison is immediately limited: Bruno still has no route to exact, necessary science of phenomena.
- The reason given is sharp and likely durable for this whole Bruno run: no mathematics, therefore no true reconciliation of intuition and thought.
- The final positive move is substantial: scientific understanding is no longer described as genus-climbing but as analysis of a whole into determinate moments and their relations.
- The tranche ends mid-sentence with that analytic function still unfolding.
