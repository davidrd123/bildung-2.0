**Notes**

- Cassirer now finishes Pomponazzi’s anti-dualist line at the anthropological level: a second, transcendent human being would be the illegitimate price of any doubled intellect-function.
- The crucial distinction is not between body and no body, but between needing the body as `object` and needing it as `subject`. That lets Pomponazzi preserve dependence on sensible representation without collapsing intellect into organ-bound sensation.
- The human intellect is therefore genuinely medial: between animal soul and abstract intelligence, materially conditioned in existence but not materially grounded in its function.
- Cassirer’s formulation here is exact and important: the soul’s `immateriality` can be preserved as a functional-logical claim without implying a separately subsisting postmortem entity.
- The argument is now turning from psychology into logic. The soul/body correlation reappears as the relation of universal and particular, concept and sensation.
- The tranche ends mid-sentence exactly where Cassirer is about to develop that logical correlation more fully.
