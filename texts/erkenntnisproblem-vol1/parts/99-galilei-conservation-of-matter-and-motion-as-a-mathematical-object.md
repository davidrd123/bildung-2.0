# Part 99: Galilei, Conservation of Matter and Motion as a Mathematical Object

Cassirer now carries the Galilean reversal through. Early modern thought had first reduced scholastic concepts to names in the name of sense; Galileo's own analysis turns the result back on perception itself. Whatever cannot be redeemed in pure quantitative determination counts as mere name. From that recast concept of matter follows the conservation thesis: absolute generation and destruction become scientifically unthinkable. The same line then admits movement itself as a fully legitimate mathematical object and rejects every claim that physics needs a special method opposed to mathematics.

**English (draft)**

```text
... to become fully conscious of this consequence, put ourselves back at the starting-point of the investigation. We saw how modern science began by referring back, as against a physical world-picture rooted in ontological oppositions and distinctions, to the primary source of sensible experience; how by this means it reduced what had hitherto counted as a fixed and exhaustive system of concepts to a mere collection of names. Sensible sensation itself, however, the more sharply and clearly the task contained in it was grasped, led back to the demand for mathematical analysis, in which the concept now found a new being and a new embodiment. As this new point of view arises and progressively deepens, a complete reversal at the same time takes shape within the earlier opposition: for now, as one sees, it is the individual perception which, insofar as it cannot be reduced to a pure quantitative determination and authenticated in it, counts as an arbitrary name. The genuine object of nature is first won when, in the change and variation of our perceptions themselves, we learn to hold fast the necessary and universally valid rules. It is especially characteristic that Galileo, for the scientific constitution of matter, expressly renounces not only color and tone but also sensations of touch and resistance, so that weight, however essential and indispensable it is as an empirical property, still does not enter into the concept of body for him. Indeed it seems as though by the reduction to magnitude and shape even the physical point of view of mass were to be eliminated, as will later be the case in Descartes. This restriction is understandable, however, from the logical interest that predominates at this point: the reality of body is to be determined from mathematics alone, which here still essentially coincides with geometry. Galileo's physical concept of body gradually developed in the same measure in which mathematics in him accomplished the transition from its ancient shape to the modern form of analysis.

The advance that is here achieved on the way toward determining the concrete content proves itself in a consequence that follows directly from the premises thus far: with the concept of matter that Galileo lays down, the thought of the conservation of substance is at the same time given. In thinking away from the real object of nature the change and mutability belonging to the subjective contents of perception, we have thereby fixed this object as a persisting identical unity. An absolute arising and passing-away would involve a direct contradiction to that concept of the world which understanding discovers and projects out of itself. A genuine substantial transformation, in which a material is so reshaped that it must count as wholly annihilated, is an unperformable thought. It is characteristic that in the Dialogues on the Two Chief World Systems Galileo's Aristotelian opponent appeals, in order to refute this proposition, to immediate sensible appearance: do we not daily see herbs, plants, and animals arise and perish before our eyes, do we not see opposites continually contend with one another, how the earth changes into water, water into air, and this again condenses into clouds, rain, and storm? To deny such manifest facts, he says, would be to abolish the principles of science itself and thereby the possibility of every proof. For an empiricism of this kind Galileo, in accordance with the new concept of experience that develops in him, has no understanding and no tolerance. Not everything that rests on the alleged testimony of immediate observation counts for him as a fact in the scientific sense. Only systematic connection and agreement with the totality of phenomena decides the value of an individual fact; but in order to test this agreement we must hold the special case together with general systematic principles. Through relation to such criteria, for example to the law of the conservation of matter, a process that presents itself to perception as absolute creation and annihilation becomes for judgment a mere relative displacement of parts within the homogeneous totality of substance. We know from Galileo's own testimony that the direction of thought described here was also maintained in the discovery of the fundamental fact of his science, the discovery of the laws of falling bodies.

In general it shows itself that the concept of motion, the more it moves into the focal point of interest and the more clearly it stands out as the proper type of physical reality, undergoes an analogous development to the one we were able to trace in the concept of substance. Here too the partition between abstract and concrete consideration, between theory and application, is first abolished: motion is for Galileo a just as fully valid and legitimate mathematical concept as the triangle or the pyramid. Nothing foreign, nothing external enters with it into the circle of pure mathematical objects. Through Galileo's writings there runs the struggle against those who demand for the peculiarity of physical objects a special physical method opposed to the mathematical one, or differing from it in any essential feature whatever. To deny the immediate applicability of geometrical consequences to empirical changes is just as ridiculous as if one wished to assert that the laws of arithmetic fail when counting up a concrete quantum. The specifically physical method of which the Peripatetics boast, the derivation of appearances from qualitative oppositions, in fact puts in the place of scientific determinateness an infinite ambiguity of possible grounds of explanation. On the example of hydrostatics Galileo sets forth that, whereas he lays down a simply universal law and progressively derives from it all the particularity of the individual case, his opponents are able to support their explanatory principle only by ever more complicated distinctions and restrictions: the connection between the universal and the particular, which comes to light in the mathematical equation with immediate certainty, can here be maintained only by ever new logical distinctions, by the contrast of what holds in itself and per accidens, in the proper or improper sense, absolutely or relatively. **Working Commentary**
```

```text
1) The strongest turn here is the reversal of the old nominalism charge: immediate perception itself becomes `Name` unless it can be grounded quantitatively.

2) Weight and even tactile resistance are withheld from the strict body-concept at this stage. The leading interest is still logical and geometrical.

3) Conservation is not added empirically from outside. It follows directly from the homogeneous, enduring concept of matter.

4) Experience is redefined: not every observation counts as fact, only what can be held in systematic agreement with the whole of phenomena.

5) Motion is now admitted without remainder into mathematics, and the demand for a separate `physische Methode` is treated as a regression into qualitative ambiguity.
```
