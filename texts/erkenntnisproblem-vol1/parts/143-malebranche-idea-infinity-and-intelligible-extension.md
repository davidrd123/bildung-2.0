# Part 143: Malebranche, Idea, Infinity, and Intelligible Extension

Cassirer now gives Malebranche's actual counter to the Berkeley danger. `Perception` is not enough, because perception yields only momentary states; `Idee` names an object-side rule that exceeds the flux of consciousness. Mathematics supplies the decisive model, especially through generality and infinity, and from there the section arrives at its full metaphysical claim: the source of intelligible certainty is not our own mental state but `intelligible extension`, the Augustinian light in which objects become knowable.

**English (gloss)**

```text
Malebranche's answer begins by separating psychology from logic. Perceptions are states of the subject; ideas are what thought is actually about. Mathematical deduction shows the difference best, because the validity of the proof depends on the object-side structure of the square or triangle, not on the changing inner condition of the mind that entertains it.

That distinction deepens through infinity. A true concept is not an inductive pile of examples but a rule that prefigures an unlimited field of possible cases. Circle, hyperbola, asymptote, space: each case shows that the universal precedes the instances rather than being composed out of them.

Cassirer's point is then stronger than a merely formal theory of concepts. The stability and universality of mathematical knowledge force Malebranche toward a class of objects that exceeds the momentary contents of consciousness. `Idea` is not just a cleaner perception; it points beyond subjective states altogether.

That is why the section ends at `intelligible extension`. The source of certainty is no longer the finite subject but a luminous common ground in which objects are given. Cassirer names the move directly: this is the transition from Descartes to Augustine, now reconstructed through modern epistemology. The tranche ends just as the full consequence for all knowledge is about to be stated.
```
