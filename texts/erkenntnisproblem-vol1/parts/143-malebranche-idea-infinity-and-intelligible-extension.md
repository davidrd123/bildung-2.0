# Part 143: Malebranche, Idea, Infinity, and Intelligible Extension

Cassirer now gives Malebranche's actual counter to the Berkeley danger. `Perception` is not enough, because perception yields only momentary states; `Idee` names an object-side rule that exceeds the flux of consciousness. Mathematics supplies the decisive model, especially through generality and infinity, and from there the section arrives at its full metaphysical claim: the source of intelligible certainty is not our own mental state but `intelligible extension`, the Augustinian light in which objects become knowable.

Noch deutlicher tritt dies hervor, wenn wir erwägen, daß die mathematischen Begriffe jederzeit ein Allgemeines bedeuten, das sich nicht in einer begrenzten Anzahl von Beispielen erschöpft, sondern schlechterdings eine unendliche Mannigfaltigkeit von Fällen in sich schließt, während unsere Perzeption uns stets nur einen vereinzelten momentanen Zustand des Bewußtseins erschließt. Allgemein ist es die Beziehung zwischen dem einen und allumfassenden Raum und seinen einzelnen Teilen und Gestaltungen, die für das Problem das vorbildliche Beispiel gibt. Die Idee der Einen Ausdehnung ist nicht das Produkt aus dem Zusammenfließen der besonderen Figuren; sie ist die allgemeine Bedingung, die die Bildung und Abgrenzung des Einzelnen erst ermöglicht. **English (draft)**

```text
... makes possible this fact itself, is the first necessary presupposition that we must postulate for the concept of cognition. Before we enter upon the consequences that for Malebranche are immediately contained and enclosed in this beginning, we must once more make clear to ourselves the necessity and right of the way the question is posed. Psychology and logic, the doctrine of the subjective course of thought and its causal laws, and the consideration of the fundamental relations that obtain between the objects of thought, now step clearly apart. Once again it is mathematics that points out the way here and confirms the fundamental philosophical distinction. If I grasp the idea of a square and derive from it in gapless necessary sequence all the properties of such a figure, then the object toward which I look in my thinking and which guides all my individual steps is undoubtedly not the image in my mind by virtue of which I as an individual subject represent the square to myself. For this image, according to its particular constitution, in no way enters into the deduction in which we bring the conceptual properties of the square to clarity for ourselves. `Between our perceptions and our ideas there therefore exists the same difference as between us as knowing subjects and that which is known by us.`

This comes out still more clearly when we consider that mathematical concepts always signify a universal that is not exhausted in a limited number of examples, but absolutely includes in itself an infinite multiplicity of cases, whereas our perception always discloses to us only an isolated momentary state of consciousness. If we were restricted to the material that the various states of our consciousness offer us, then every concept would be nothing other than a heap of individual Vorstellungen. The idea of the circle would mean nothing other than the confused total image that arises in us out of repeated perceptions of actual circles. But in that case it would be robbed of its proper core and of its scientific content. For it is the privilege of the mathematical concept that it is not gathered up inductively, but that in it we possess an original rule by virtue of which we delimit in advance future experience and the totality of cases. The individual examples that we run through serve only as a psychological point of support and aid in assuring ourselves of this totality; the encompassing generic unity denotes not the sum, but the presupposition of the particular cases. The fundamental principle of the infinite is the clearest and sharpest protest against the customary psychological theory of concept-formation.

The relation that subsists between the hyperbola and its asymptote I cannot make clear to myself by following both lines in their individual parts and comparing with one another the manifold `perceptions` that I thus gain; only the comprehensive `idea`, the unitary mathematical formula of the hyperbola, is able to instruct me about it. In general it is the relation between the one all-embracing space and its individual parts and configurations that furnishes the exemplary case for the problem. The idea of the one extension is not the product that arises from the confluence of the particular figures; it is the general condition that first makes possible the formation and delimitation of the individual. Thus we stand here before a remarkable result: pure cognition itself, as it lies indubitably before us in the basic disciplines of mathematics, geometry, and analysis, leads us to a class of objects that point clearly beyond the individual contents and states of consciousness.

We can explain this state of affairs no otherwise than by the presupposition that the source of these cognitions does not lie in ourselves, but that it is a transcendent spiritual essence that communicates itself to us in them and transfers its own certainty and clarity to us. `When I think of the figures of geometry, I work on a ground-plan that is not my own: cela ne vient point de la modalite qui nous est propre et particuliere, c'est un eclat de la substance lumineuse de notre maitre commun.` With this the last decisive step has been taken, the transition from Descartes to Augustine accomplished. But Augustine's fundamental thought receives here, where it meets the principles of modern epistemology, all-embracing significance. What holds for the eternal and necessary truths holds thereby also for the particular cognitions whose model and condition they are. If for us there are no objects other than intelligible ones, and if everything intelligible contains something persistent and enduring that can be grasped by our changeable I only, not created by it, then we understand the proposition `that we behold all things in God` as a necessary consequence.
```

**Working Commentary**

```text
1) The idea/perception distinction is now doing the real work of rescuing universality from the flow of consciousness.

2) Cassirer is using mathematics here not as ornament but as proof that objectivity cannot be reduced to inner states.

3) Infinity is the sharpest anti-psychologist weapon in the section, because it blocks the view that concepts are accumulated examples.

4) `Intelligible extension` is the metaphysical price of securing scientific constancy once bodies and naive objecthood have been abandoned.

5) The next tranche should continue directly, because the final sentence is about to generalize this result from eternal truths to all objectual knowledge.
```
