## II. The Reform of Aristotelian Psychology

**German (corrected)**

```text
II.

Die Reform der Aristotelischen Psychologie.

Es wäre geschichtlich einseitig und ungerecht, wenn man das entscheidende und positive Ergebnis der Renaissancephilosophie lediglich in der Bekämpfung des Aristotelismus erblickte. Das neue Verständnis der antiken Kultur, das jetzt gewonnen wird, kommt auch der Erfassung der echten Peripatetischen Lehre zugute. Der Scholastik werden nun die Grundgedanken ihres eigenen Meisters in der genaueren und reineren Fassung, in der die philologische Kritik sie wiederhergestellt hat, entgegengehalten: auf den originalen Denker Aristoteles beruft man sich, um Aristoteles als Schulhaupt zu stürzen. Leonardo Bruni, der erste Übersetzer der wichtigsten Platonischen Dialoge, überträgt auch die Aristotelische Politik und die Nikomachische Ethik, und in dem Maße, als er sich in diese Werke vertieft, glaubt er mehr und mehr zu erkennen, daß die Form, in welcher man sie bisher allein kannte, die Schuld trägt, wenn sie nicht ihre volle geschichtliche Wirkung auszuüben vermochten. Könnte Aristoteles wieder unter uns erscheinen: er würde erzürnt seine eigenen Schriften in der barbarischen Gestalt, die ihnen das scholastische Latein gegeben, verleugnen und von sich weisen. Und doch schließen diese Schriften in ihrer ursprünglichen Fassung alle Vorbedingungen für die wahrhafte humanistische Erziehung und für die Bildung zum Redner in sich.1) Auch Bessarion — nach dem Worte Ficins einer der Ersten in der großen „Platonischen Familie“ —, fertigt nichtsdestoweniger mit Eifer und Sorgfalt eine neue Übersetzung der Aristotelischen Metaphysik an, deren Manuskript er Nikolaus Cusanus zum Geschenk überläßt.2) In den Kreisen der Florentinischen Akademie ist es sodann Giovanni Pico della Mirandola, der die Versöhnung von Platon und Aristoteles als das eigentliche Endziel der Philosophie betrachtet und verkündet.3) Nicht die Befangenheit in der geschichtlichen Überlieferung spricht sich in solchen Versuchen aus, sondern die freiere, dogmatisch nicht beengte Auffassung, die man von der Peripatetischen Lehre selbst, ihrem Gehalt und ihren Entstehungsbedingungen gewonnen hat. Von den Einzelsätzen des Systems, die zuvor als unveräußerlicher und unverrückbarer Bestand galten, wird auf die gedanklichen Motive zurückgegangen; der feste geschlossene Bau des Ganzen beginnt sich zu lockern, und an seine Stelle tritt die dialektische Bewegung und die Wiederherstellung des Denkprozesses, in dem die einzelnen Grundsätze erreicht wurden.

Schon der Gegensatz der Auslegungen, der Streit zwischen „Alexandristen“ und „Averroisten“, dient dieser selbständigen und freieren Form der Aneignung. Für die neue Richtung, die damit eingeschlagen wird, aber ist es vor allem bezeichnend, daß die Grundfragen der Aristotelischen Psychologie und Erkenntnislehre es sind, die jetzt in den Mittelpunkt der Betrachtung rücken. Das Mittelalter wird, abgesehen von den logischen Streitigkeiten, vor allem durch die Aristotelische Metaphysik und Physik beherrscht und bestimmt: der Aufbau des Kosmos und sein Zusammenhang mit dem „unbewegten Beweger“ ist es, der sein Interesse fesselt. Alle diese Fragen treten jetzt völlig zurück: einzig und allein der Begriff und das Problem der Seele entscheidet nun über die Parteistellung innerhalb der Peripatetischen Philosophie. Eine Erzählung aus dieser Zeit berichtet, wie Portius, als er seine Lehrtätigkeit in Pisa mit einer Vorlesung über die „Meteorologica“ des Aristoteles zu beginnen gedachte, durch stürmische Zurufe seiner Zuhörer, „Sprich uns von der Seele“ unterbrochen wurde.1) In diesem allgemeinen Interesse aber bekundet sich, wenngleich zunächst noch nicht bewußt und bestimmt, zugleich die gedankliche Richtung auf ein neues Ziel: die dialektische Zergliederung des Aristotelischen Seelenbegriffs wird zu einem Faktor in der Entstehung des modernen Bewußtseinsbegriffs. Die Psychologie des Aristoteles wird in ihren wesentlichen Hauptzügen durch die Voraussetzungen seiner Metaphysik und Erkenntnislehre bestimmt. The abstract universal concept alone would fill it, while the beauty and multiplicity of particular shapes and forms would be withdrawn from it forever. Yet here lies for the human being the sense and significance of his empirical existence: “it is in the colored reflection that we have life.” A modern fundamental feeling speaks here in the concepts and forms of the inherited astronomical worldview. The earth is not a low and contemptible dwelling place; it is the middle choir of the divine temple and the firm foundation about which all the heavenly spheres revolve as around their pivot. The motion and mutability of earthly being is no inner defect; rather, in it we possess the necessary counter-image through which we first learn to feel and enjoy rest and peace in God. “Perhaps God himself has ordained that divine joys fall to spirits of higher rank of themselves, while lower spirits must attain them with effort; that the former partake of blessedness from birth, while the latter must first acquire it in life. Thus he has prevented the higher spirits from exalting themselves and the lower from despising themselves, since the former receive blessedness from without, whereas the latter are the creators of their blessedness.”1) The imperfection of the individual itself thus becomes a testimony to its eternal vocation.
```

```text
Yet despite all these characteristic and important beginnings, Ficino did not succeed in mastering and resolving the thought of `transcendence`. In the system as a whole, this thought still remains in the end the dominant ideal. Dionysius the Areopagite becomes the proclaimer and guarantor of genuine Platonic philosophy because he taught us to seek the divine light not through activity of the understanding, but by the power of affect and will, as exalted above all being and knowing. “Fly beyond not only sensible things but also intelligible objects; leave the domain of understanding and raise yourself, through love for the one highest good, to the good itself, which lies beyond all being, all life, and all understanding.”1) The relativity that only a moment ago seemed to be understood as a necessity of human knowing now appears here once again as its limit. In this conflict a deeper opposition of thought reveals itself, one that runs through the whole philosophy of the Renaissance and that will meet us again in many forms.

II.

The Reform of Aristotelian Psychology.

It would be historically one-sided and unjust if one were to see the decisive and positive result of Renaissance philosophy solely in the struggle against Aristotelianism. The new understanding of ancient culture now being won also benefits the apprehension of genuine Peripatetic doctrine. The basic thoughts of their own master, in the more exact and purer form in which philological criticism has restored them, are now set against scholasticism: one appeals to the original thinker Aristotle in order to overthrow Aristotle as school-head. Leonardo Bruni, the first translator of the most important Platonic dialogues, also translates the Aristotelian `Politics` and the `Nicomachean Ethics`; and the more he immerses himself in these works, the more he comes to believe that the form in which they had hitherto alone been known bears the blame for their inability to exercise their full historical effect. If Aristotle could appear again among us, he would angrily disown and reject his own writings in the barbarous shape that scholastic Latin has given them. And yet these writings, in their original form, contain all the preconditions for a genuinely humanist education and for the formation of the orator.1) Bessarion too, one of the first in the great “Platonic family,” as Ficino calls him, nonetheless prepares with zeal and care a new translation of Aristotle's `Metaphysics`, whose manuscript he presents to Nicholas of Cusa as a gift.2) Then in the circles of the Florentine Academy it is Giovanni Pico della Mirandola who regards and proclaims the reconciliation of Plato and Aristotle as the true end-goal of philosophy.3) What speaks in such attempts is not captivity to historical tradition, but the freer, dogmatically unconstrained conception that had been gained of Peripatetic doctrine itself, of its content and the conditions of its emergence. From the individual propositions of the system, previously treated as an inalienable and immovable stock, one now goes back to the motives of thought; the firm closed structure of the whole begins to loosen, and in its place there enters dialectical movement and the restoration of the process of thinking through which the individual principles were reached.

Even the opposition of interpretations, the dispute between “Alexandrists” and “Averroists,” serves this more independent and freer mode of appropriation. For the new direction thereby struck out, it is above all characteristic that it is the basic questions of Aristotelian psychology and theory of knowledge that now move to the center of attention. The Middle Ages, apart from the logical disputes, had been ruled and determined above all by Aristotelian metaphysics and physics: it is the structure of the cosmos and its connection with the “unmoved mover” that holds its interest fast. All these questions now recede completely; from here on the concept and problem of the soul alone decides one's party-position within Peripatetic philosophy. An anecdote from this time reports how Portius, when he intended to begin his teaching activity at Pisa with a lecture on Aristotle's `Meteorologica`, was interrupted by the stormy shouts of his hearers: “Speak to us of the soul.”1) Yet in this general interest there simultaneously manifests itself, though at first not yet consciously and distinctly, the direction of thought toward a new goal: the dialectical dissection of the Aristotelian concept of soul becomes a factor in the emergence of the modern concept of consciousness. Aristotle's psychology is determined in its essential main features by the presuppositions of his metaphysics and theory of knowledge. Just as true being is sought in the individual thing, so perception, which immediately discloses this concrete existence, counts as the original witness of every certainty. **Notes**
```

- Cassirer's final balance of Ficino is now explicit: the intelligible raises the value of empirical being, yet the unresolved ideal of transcendence still reasserts itself and blocks a full immanent resolution.
- The earth and embodied life are positively revalued here; empirical imperfection becomes not a disgrace but the condition under which finite beings actively win blessedness.
- The tension in Ficino is named in Cassirer's own terms: relativity first appeared as a necessity of knowledge, then returns as its limit.
- Section `II` opens by insisting that Renaissance philosophy does not merely defeat Aristotelianism; it recovers Aristotle against scholastic Aristotle through philology, translation, and renewed attention to the original problems.
- The decisive shift in focus is from Aristotelian cosmology to Aristotelian psychology: the problem of the soul, not the structure of the cosmos, becomes the hinge for the emergence of the modern concept of consciousness.
- The tranche ends at the point where Cassirer is about to derive higher thought from Aristotle's account of perception and individual substance.
