# Part 140: Malebranche, Force, Relativism, and the Concept of Relation

Malebranche now pushes the phenomenal method against its next residue: force. Cassirer's point is not skeptical flattening. Force is dissolved into law, and law into quantitative relation. From there the section turns through `Relativismus`, but it does so in two distinct registers: first as a merely psychological or biological limitation of perception, then more deeply as the objective method-concept that leads from universal geometry to truth itself as `reale Beziehung`.

In der Tat hat Malebranche, hat somit der geschmähte `Rationalismus` Hume die Waffen geliefert, mit denen er die populäre Ansicht der Ursächlichkeit bekämpft. In diesem Sinne hat Malebranche selbst beständig an der Entdeckung des einheitlichen quantitativen Grundgesetzes gearbeitet, aus dem alle besonderen Bewegungsvorgänge mit deduktiver Strenge ableitbar werden sollten; innerhalb der cartesianischen Schule ist er der Erste, der den Übergang von den Stoßregeln Descartes' zum Leibnizischen Satz der Erhaltung der lebendigen Kraft vollzogen hat.

```text
Allgemein weist der Relativismus, der einen Grundzug von Malebranches Erkenntnislehre ausmacht, eine doppelte Beziehung auf, ohne doch seiner eigentlichen Absicht nach schwankend und zweideutig zu werden. Er knüpft zunächst an psychologische Erwägungen und Tatsachen an: die Relativität der Wahrnehmung ist es, durch die die bloß relative Geltung aller reinen Erkenntnisse beleuchtet und erwiesen werden soll. Einen absoluten Maßstab besitzen wir so wenig für das Sein der Körper wie für ihre Veränderung. Der Zustand unserer Organe wie die äußeren psychologischen Nebenumstände der Wahrnehmung entscheiden darüber, welche Größe wir einer bestimmten Raumstrecke oder einer gegebenen Zeitdauer zuschreiben. Denken wir uns, daß der Gesamtverlauf unserer Vorstellungen plötzlich beschleunigt oder verlangsamt würde, so müßte sich damit unser Urteil über alles äußere Geschehen und die Geschwindigkeit seines Ablaufs notwendig ändern. So scheint alle gedankliche Entscheidung über die Wirklichkeit in den Umkreis der bloßen Empfindung hineingezogen und der gleichen Bedingtheit wie diese unterworfen. Unsere Sinne, die uns lediglich zur Erhaltung des Lebens, nicht zur Erkenntnis der Wahrheit gegeben sind, erschließen uns nirgends das einzelne unabhängige Sein eines Dinges, sondern lehren uns nur seine Reaktion und seine Unterscheidung gegen andere kennen, da beides hinreicht, um unser praktisches Verhalten gegenüber der Mannigfaltigkeit der Objekte zu leiten. **English (draft)**
```

```text
... what one calls the force or power of created beings. If one believes to possess such an idea, if not in outer, yet in `inner` experience, this too rests on self-deception that immediately vanishes before deeper analysis. Between the act of will that I perceive in myself and a material alteration of the outer world there exists no necessary, indeed no comprehensible and intelligible connection. Only the prejudice of a superficial consideration can feign to us a logical connection in a process in which in truth there is only a mere succession and coexistence of appearances. We may explore the whole field of consciousness, which for us necessarily constitutes the only rule of judgment: nowhere do we find in it a capacity that mirrors to us an inner causal relation between two members of being. Wherever experience, as in the impact of bodies, seems to place before our eyes the transition of a force from one body to another, what is really present is only a constant lawful relation between processes of motion, a rule by which the distribution of velocities over different parts of space is determined. The laws, not the bodies, are those to which all efficacy in the genuine logical sense goes back: `parce que ces lois sont efficaces, elles agissent, et les corps ne peuvent agir.`

From this last conclusion Malebranche only draws the consequence of a basic view that was introduced and necessarily demanded by modern mathematical natural science. We were able to follow in particular in Kepler how the concept of force of natural philosophy and of naive representation dissolves, with growing insight into the principles of its own inquiry, into the concept of function. For Galileo this transformation already forms the secure presupposition from which he combats the substantial forms of school philosophy. It is at this point that Malebranche's critique now intervenes; and it is this connection that separates it from Hume's critique, with which it has been compared. In fact Malebranche, thus the much-maligned `rationalism`, supplied Hume with the weapons with which he combated the popular view of causality. But whereas with Hume exact mechanics itself is included in the judgment that turns against the anthropomorphism of naive intuition, the strict boundary line and opposition between the two domains remain here. We `comprehend`, if not the inward mode of the transition, still the regularity of the relation between cause and effect, insofar as we can translate it into the language of clear and distinct ideas of magnitude and fix it within this language. In this sense Malebranche himself worked constantly at the discovery of the unitary quantitative basic law from which all particular motions ought to be derivable with deductive strictness; within the Cartesian school he is the first to accomplish the transition from Descartes's rules of impact to Leibniz's principle of the conservation of living force.

In general the relativism that constitutes a fundamental trait of Malebranche's theory of knowledge points to a double relation, without thereby becoming wavering and equivocal in its proper intention. It first ties itself to psychological considerations and facts: it is the relativity of perception by which the merely relative validity of all pure cognitions is to be illuminated and proved. It lies in the direction of this way of posing the problem when Malebranche repeatedly points back to Bacon, whose doctrine of idols in fact furnished the basic schema for the psychological analyses of the first part of the `Recherche`. The data that our senses supply to us make us acquainted not with the real being of bodies, but only with the relation in which they stand to one another and to our own body. We possess as little an absolute measure for the being of bodies as for their change. The state of our organs as well as the external psychological accessory circumstances of perception decide what magnitude we ascribe to a determinate stretch of space or a given duration of time. If we think the total course of our representations suddenly accelerated or retarded, our judgment concerning all outer events and the speed of their course would necessarily change with it. Thus relativity appears at first as a mark characterizing the limited world of our sensation; it is a biological, not a logical motive from which it is here derived and grounded.

But we are led to a deeper apprehension as soon as Malebranche turns to the presentation of his doctrine of method. Now it is the fundamental content of Descartes's `Rules` that becomes alive in him once more and is grasped and carried forward by him more securely and determinately than by any other pupil or successor. He too proceeds from the demand for a universal science of which all special cognitions are to be only individual applications; he too refers the sciences of nature in particular, insofar as they attain exact results like mechanics and astronomy, back to `universal geometry` as their sufficient and necessary condition. All `speculative truths` have to do with nothing other than the relations of things and further with the `relations of relations`. Their whole content and object must therefore find in the relations between lines its exact symbolic designation and its exhaustive objective presentation. This connection is now no longer supported by a psychological analysis of our faculties and powers; it follows with objective necessity from the concept of truth itself. `Truth` means and designates nothing other than a `real relation` that occurs between two ideas, a relation that lets us know their identity or difference. **Working Commentary**
```

```text
1) The attack on force is stronger than a skeptical denial of causality. Cassirer is relocating intelligibility from efficacious things to lawful relations.

2) The Hume comparison matters mainly as a boundary-marker. Malebranche gives Hume an anti-anthropomorphic critique of cause, but not a warrant to erase mathematical physics.

3) The bridge back to Kepler and Galilei is now explicit: function and law, not hidden power, carry the scientific line.

4) `Relativismus` would be easy to misread if taken only psychologically. Cassirer explicitly deepens it into a method-concept through universal geometry and truth as relation.

5) The next tranche should continue directly, because the line ends just where `Wahrheit` as `reale Beziehung` is about to be developed more concretely.
```
