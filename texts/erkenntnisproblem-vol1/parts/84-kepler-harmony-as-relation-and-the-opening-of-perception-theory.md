# Part 84: Kepler, Harmony as Relation, and the Opening of Perception Theory

Cassirer now deepens the Kepler line from exact induction into a genuinely logical account of relation. Harmony is no longer treated as a property of things, but as a pure relation constituted through the mind's unifying act. The older world-soul language is still present, but it is being thinned into functional dependence rather than mythic force. At the same time the aesthetic motive is not left behind: musical pleasure becomes the middle between sense and intellect, and the spirit's apparent passivity in beauty is reinterpreted as its own lawful activity. From there Cassirer opens the next decisive step, a general theory of perception.

Zunächst wird die reine Harmonie selbst von ihrem sinnlichen Ausdruck geschieden: denn ein und dasselbe Zahlverhältnis kann sich an sehr verschiedenem Material, bald im Gebiet der Töne, bald in dem der Gestalten und Bewegungen darstellen. Die Harmonie ist somit nicht der Ausdruck für den Bestand eines Dinges, sondern für den Bestand einer reinen Relation. Immer entschiedener wird diese notwendige Korrelation von Kepler bezeichnet und hervorgehoben. Was eine geometrische Proportion ist und welche Bedeutung ihr zukommt, läßt sich nicht einsehen, ohne eine Tätigkeit des Geistes als ihre notwendige Bedingung anzunehmen.

Der Begriff des Bewußtseins selbst ist freilich hierbei noch nicht völlig eindeutig bestimmt: denn neben seinem rein logischen und psychologischen Sinn besitzt er, in den Anfängen von Keplers Spekulation, noch eine mythisch-poetische Nebenbedeutung. Die Ordnung des Alls dient hier zugleich als Zeugnis für seine durchgängige Beseelung: das einheitliche Leben, das den Kosmos durchdringt, stellt erst den Zusammenhang und die Abhängigkeit zwischen seinen Teilen her. Wir erinnern uns indes, daß schon innerhalb der Naturphilosophie der Begriff der Weltseele eine Vorbereitung für den Gedanken der durchgängigen und immanenten kausalen Wechselwirkung der Dinge bildet. Jetzt wird dieser logische Charakter des Begriffs gefestigt und vertieft: denn nicht mehr als eine wirkende Kraft, die nach der Analogie des Triebes und der Muskelbewegung gedacht wäre, sondern als reine funktionale Beziehung zwischen den Gliedern des Universums wird die Seele beschrieben. ... created according to the pattern of geometrical proportion, so that the self may find in it continually new tasks and new material for its restless self-activity. Between the human intellect and the human senses, on the one hand, and the articulation of the heavenly bodies and their movements, on the other, there exists, as the commentary on the motions of Mars explains more fully, an inner connection and "fitness": both are laid out for one another and measurable through one another. To investigate the essence and the origin of this connection and set it forth in detail is the task undertaken by the work on the harmony of the world. Here it is no longer mere examples of harmonious relations that are spread out before us in rich abundance; rather, the question now directs itself consciously and clearly to the general concept that appears in them all. In particular, the discussions of the fourth book on the essence of sensible and intellectual harmonies are characteristic in this respect. No longer the mathematician or the physicist, but the philosopher now speaks to us, seeking in penetrating logical distinctions to expound the nature of his fundamental concept.

The Sensible and the Intellectual Harmony.

At the outset pure harmony itself is separated from its sensible expression: for one and the same numerical ratio can present itself in very different material, now in the domain of tones, now in that of shapes and motions. Sensible harmony is built up from four moments, all of which are necessary for its subsistence. It requires first of all some perceptible objects that belong to the same genus and are in some way determined as magnitudes; it requires further the reception of the objects into consciousness and a comparative activity of the soul, which in turn, if it is to reach its end, presupposes a suitable relation between the elements compared. Only the conjunction of these determinations yields the unified fundamental essence of harmony. That sensible things by themselves do not suffice to grasp this essence is easy to see. A mere aggregate of individual tones does not in and of itself constitute a melody; rather, melody first arises when we think a determinate order impressed upon the tones, an order grounded in their reciprocal relations. Harmony is therefore not the expression of the subsistence of a thing, but of the subsistence of a pure relation. It is no new concrete object standing alongside the individual elements; rather, it owes its being solely to the spiritual acts by virtue of which we shape a manifold into unity in the thoroughgoing relation of its parts. In this sense it is and remains a pure "thing of thought": if one thinks away the unifying function of soul and thought, no place remains for it. Kepler marks and emphasizes this necessary correlation ever more decisively. What a geometrical proportion is, and what significance belongs to it, cannot be understood without assuming an activity of spirit as its necessary condition.

The concept of consciousness itself, to be sure, is here not yet determined in a wholly unambiguous way: for besides its purely logical and psychological sense, it still possesses, in the beginnings of Kepler's speculation, a mythic-poetic secondary meaning. The order of the universe here serves at the same time as testimony to its thoroughgoing ensoulment: the unified life that pervades the cosmos first establishes the connection and dependence between its parts. We recall, however, that already within natural philosophy the concept of the world-soul formed a preparation for the thought of the thoroughgoing and immanent causal interaction of things. Now this logical character of the concept is consolidated and deepened: for the soul is described no longer as an effective force conceived on the analogy of impulse and muscular motion, but as a pure functional relation between the members of the universe. It is not the powers of the deity, not the creation of the universe, that we have to unravel: that would mean, as Kepler expressly emphasizes over against the theological mode of consideration, posing an insoluble problem. What we are able to comprehend is only the conceptual constitution of the whole, whose basic features we find prefigured within ourselves in the concept of magnitude.

But with this mathematical motif the aesthetic motif is inseparably linked. As in Plato it is the idea of the beautiful that mediates and guides the ascent from the sensible to the intelligible, so for Kepler the pleasure aroused in us by musical consonance is a "middle term" between mere sensual enjoyment and the higher intellectual satisfaction that spirit experiences in the consciousness of its own creation. The same order that we apprehend in tones through a dim innate disposition attains complete form and clarity in the progress of research: it is one and the same basic drive of consciousness that stirs in artistic enjoyment and in astronomical research. For in joy at rhythm and consonance spirit is only apparently passive: in truth it unfolds here too an independent movement and activity. The elevation and stimulation of its being that it feels is at bottom its own work, to which, however, it is led not by conscious intention, but by a natural "instinct."

The Theory of Perception.

This conception of the aesthetic impression became of fundamental significance for Kepler's overall philosophical view: for here already lie the general motives and seeds for the theory of sensible perception as such. Outwardly, to be sure, Kepler's doctrine at this point still appears as a faithful image of the traditional view: knowledge of things is mediated by sensible "species" that penetrate into the mind. **Working Commentary**

```text
1) Cassirer now gives the strongest logical definition of harmony so far: it is not a thing but a pure relation constituted through acts of unification.

2) This lets Kepler's older world-soul language be retained but transformed. The soul no longer has to mean a quasi-physical force; it becomes a functional relation within the whole.

3) The aesthetic motive is therefore not decorative. Musical pleasure is a real epistemic middle term between sensation and intellectual production.

4) Kepler's apparent passivity before beauty is reinterpreted as the spirit's own lawful activity, only not yet fully conscious of itself.

5) This is why Cassirer can now move from harmony into a general theory of perception: the same structure that made harmony possible is already the germ of sensory cognition.
```

**Notes**

- `Die sinnliche und die intellektuelle Harmonie` is the key hinge for the relation claim.
- The world-soul motif should be read here as a transitional carrier toward functional relation, not as a regression.
- The opening of `Die Theorie der Wahrnehmung` is still transitional; the next tranche should show how far Kepler escapes traditional species-talk.
