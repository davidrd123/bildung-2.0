The history of religion is completed when, through all the differences of the particular cults, it has once more found its way back to the idea of the One God; and humanity approaches this idea to the extent that it realizes and establishes within itself the demands of social unity. Only then is the true `theocracy` attained, through which each individual will is at once bound and clarified within itself concerning the goal of its striving. If therefore in Ficino Plato is called, with a phrase of Numenius, the `Attic Moses`, and Christ and Socrates are set side by side, then comparisons of this sort already become a standing formula among the later thinkers.1) There is, as Mutianus Rufus writes, only One God and One Goddess; but there are many forms and many names: Jupiter, Sol, Apollo, Moses, Christ, Luna, Ceres, Proserpina, Tellus, Mary, a connection that one must, as he adds, shroud in silence like the Eleusinian mysteries and hide behind fables and riddles.2) How this pure and universal theism3) steps out of its restriction to the sphere of speculation and intervenes directly in the fundamental questions of ethics is shown especially by the example of Erasmus.

The Augustinian concept of the City of God, which expressly excluded the great pagans, is now burst open: they too belong to the genuine and true `community of saints`, even if they may be absent from our `registers`. To the greatness of ancient ways of thinking and disposition is opposed the life-conduct of Christians, which in most cases is exhausted in ceremonies, in incantations and magic formulas, in the keeping of fasts and in external ecclesiastical works.1) In this comparison, belonging to the `Convivium religiosum`, the humanist tendency passes directly over into the basic thoughts of the Reformation. These form the culmination, but also the limit, of the religious movement of the Renaissance. Within Protestantism itself it is above all the doctrine of Sebastian Franck in which the epoch's total philosophy of religion once again comes to expression. The identification of the divine `Word` with the `natural light` is brought to completion in him: `what Plato, Seneca, Cicero and all enlightened pagans have called the light of nature and of reason, theology designates as the Word, as the Son of God and as the invisible Christ. He was present in Seneca and Cicero just as much as in Paul. Accordingly he understands by Christ (Logos) the immanence of moral-religious ideas in God and their working and communication to human beings.`2)

Thus the total religious development of the age, which we have been able to sketch here only in scattered indications, flows back once more into the thought of the `Logos`. In this multiform and fruitful concept the whole result of the Renaissance's labor of thought can now be summed up. Dialectic no less than psychology, contemplation of nature no less than the human sciences, have all led us back to one and the same central question, one designated in the language of theology and Neoplatonism by the concept of the `Logos`, in the language of modern philosophy by the concept of `consciousness`. Burckhardt has shown and demonstrated on every side how within the Italian Renaissance `men and humanity were first completely recognized in their deeper essence`. `This one result of the Renaissance alone may fill us with eternal gratitude. The logical concept of humanity had always been possessed; but it knew the thing itself.` For the history of the problem of knowledge it is of the highest interest to observe how the new objective elements of culture now streaming in from every side, and above all the transformation of the basic aesthetic and ethical outlook, in turn lead to a recreation of the logical and theoretical concept of self-consciousness. Long before this problem emerges in independent abstract formulation, it works as a latent driving force in the individual spiritual movements; and we shall soon encounter it again, in new form and inflection, in the French Renaissance of the sixteenth century.

**Notes**

- Cassirer now states the teleology of Renaissance religion explicitly: the plurality of cults is historically justified only insofar as it leads back toward the idea of the one God through realized social unity.
- Erasmus and Sebastian Franck carry the argument out of speculation and into ethics and reform. Universal religion becomes a criticism of ceremonial Christianity and a defense of the immanent moral content of faith.
- The section's final synthesis matters for the whole book: `Logos` is the name under which dialectic, psychology, nature-study, and the human sciences are gathered back into a single problem.
- Cassirer then gives the philosophical payoff in modern terms: the real product of the Renaissance is not merely richer content, but a recreated logical and theoretical concept of `self-consciousness`.
- `[visual candidate]` The final convergence is diagrammable: dialectic, psychology, nature, and history/culture all flowing into `Logos` / `consciousness` as the central Renaissance problem-form.
- Section `IV` closes here. The next clean move is the chapter opening on `Der Skepticismus`, where Cassirer explicitly turns the Cusan `docta ignorantia` into a general principle of modernity.
