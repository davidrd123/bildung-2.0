# Part 101: Galilei, From Substance to Function and Phenomenal Knowledge

Cassirer now states the philosophical stake of the Galilean turn with unusual directness. Fracastoro still explains order through qualitative kinship, sympathy, and the self-preservation of elemental beings; even motion remains teleological and thing-centered. Galileo overturns this by beginning not from the kinds of beings but from a general lawfulness of operation. The old opposition between empiricism and rationalism therefore recedes. The real issue becomes whether thought starts from things or from relations, from substance or from function. Galileo's own answer is then formulated in the sunspot letters: the inner essence of substances is inaccessible, but empirical `affezioni` can be known and can ground deeper science.

Alle Bewegung wird durchaus in diesem qualitativ-teleologischen Sinne gefaßt und gedeutet: einer der führenden Aristoteliker der Zeit, Cesare Cremonini, formuliert noch einmal in voller Allgemeinheit das Axiom, daß Bewegung nicht möglich sei ohne ein strebendes Subjekt und ein Ziel, dem es sich in seinem Streben zuwendet. Der absolute Charakter des Subjekts entscheidet über die Beziehungen, in die es eingeht: die Dinge werden geordnet und in Klassen eingeteilt, um aus dieser Einteilung das Prinzip für die Unterscheidung der Bewegungen zu gewinnen. Bei Galilei erst wird der alte Satz: „operari sequitur esse“, den die Scholastik der naiven dinglichen Weltansicht entlehnt, zu nichte. Er beginnt mit einer allgemeinen Gesetzlichkeit des Wirkens, die unabhängig von aller Besonderheit der empirischen Objekte allgemeine und notwendige Geltung beansprucht; erst unter ihrer Voraussetzung werden die Arten und Gattungen des Seins unterscheidbar.

In der Geschichte des Erkenntnisproblems ist nunmehr ein neuer fundamentaler Gegensatz zur Klärung und entschiedenen Herausarbeitung gelangt. Der alte Widerstreit des „Empirismus“ und „Rationalismus“ tritt zurück; er wird gegenüber dem System Galileis, das durchaus auf der Durchdringung und Wechselbeziehung von Erfahrung und Vernunft beruht, unbestimmt und unfruchtbar. Ein anderes, tieferes Problem ist jetzt gestellt: die Frage ist, ob mit den Dingen oder den Beziehungen, ob mit dem Dasein oder mit den Formen der Verknüpfung zu beginnen ist. Gegenüber der substantiellen Weltansicht erhebt sich eine Auffassung, die auf dem Grunde des Funktionsbegriffs erwachsen ist. An dieser Stelle wird es besonders deutlich, daß die Geschichte der neueren Philosophie außerhalb des Zusammenhangs mit der exakten Wissenschaft nicht zu begreifen und nicht zu entwickeln ist. Der dialektische Widerstreit, der hier entstanden ist, wird die treibende Grundkraft der künftigen Systeme: die Cartesische, wie die Leibnizische Erkenntnislehre bilden nur bestimmte Einzelphasen in jenem allgemeinen Fortschritt von der Substanz zur Funktion.

Bei Galilei nun ist der neue Gesichtspunkt nicht nur in der Art und Richtung seiner Forschung implicit enthalten, sondern auch mit überraschender Präcision und Klarheit formuliert. „Entweder - so heißt es in seinen ‚Briefen über die Sonnenflecke‘ - wir suchen auf dem Wege der Spekulation in das wahre und innerliche Wesen der natürlichen Substanzen einzudringen, oder wir begnügen uns mit der Erkenntnis einiger ihrer empirischen Merkmale (affezioni). Den ersteren Versuch halte ich für ein Bemühen, daß bei den nächsten irdischen wie bei den entferntesten himmlischen Substanzen gleich eitel und vergeblich ist. Wir kennen so wenig die Substanz der Erde, wie die des Mondes, so wenig die unserer irdischen Atmosphäre, wie die der Sonnenflecke. Denn ich sehe nicht, daß wir im Verständnis der nahen Substanzen irgendeinen anderen Vorteil besitzen, als die Fülle der Einzelbestimmungen, die aber alle (ihrem metaphysischen Wesen nach) gleich unbekannt bleiben. ... qualitative affinity and qualitative opposition that enter into the explanation of spatial order. The position and relative placement of the individual elements corresponds to the degree of their inward belonging-together. From the drive of self-preservation implanted in every natural body there follows first the exclusion of empty space. All elements strive toward mutual contact, since only in it can they preserve and secure their continuance; for the vacuum, as the marked logical contrary of the concept of body, is at the same time a constant threat to their physical existence. The same general point of view explains further the determinate local articulation: each element occupies "by its nature" that place in which it is best isolated and protected from alien influences and conflicting properties. Thus fire has its place between the concave vault above the lunar sphere and the outer boundary of the earthly atmosphere: here it borders on one side the region of light, to which it is akin by the quality of "dryness," and on the other the region of air, with which it shares the quality of warmth. Analogously, air receives the position between fire and water that is most fitting to it, since it stands in community with the one through the property of warmth and with the other through that of moisture. In general, wherever we find a being-together of materials or the dissolution of such togetherness, this must be referred back to purely inward relations of "sympathy" and "antipathy": if, for example, we see air in a tube displaced by the water penetrating it, then this process, as is expressly stressed, is not to be conceived and explained in a purely mechanical way, but rests on the disharmony that exists between the nature of the two elements. "Just as the parts of a living being display manifold correspondences and relations among themselves, just as they demand a determinate arrangement and articulation, so too the parts of the universe, which is no less to be thought as a living organism, mutually determine their position; were this not the case, the universe itself would not be in its proper constitution." All motion is comprehended and interpreted throughout in this qualitative-teleological sense: one of the leading Aristotelians of the time, Cesare Cremonini, once more formulates in full generality the axiom that motion is not possible without a striving subject and an end toward which it turns in its striving. The absolute character of the subject decides the relations into which it enters: things are ordered and divided into classes in order to win from this division the principle for distinguishing motions. Only with Galileo is the old proposition "operari sequitur esse," which scholasticism borrowed from the naive thing-centered view of the world, annulled. He begins with a general lawfulness of operation that claims general and necessary validity independently of all particularity of empirical objects; only under its presupposition are the kinds and genera of being distinguishable.

In the history of the problem of knowledge a new fundamental opposition has now attained clarification and decided elaboration. The old conflict of "empiricism" and "rationalism" recedes; over against Galileo's system, which rests throughout on the interpenetration and reciprocal relation of experience and reason, it becomes indeterminate and unfruitful. Another, deeper problem is now posed: the question is whether one is to begin with things or with relations, with existence or with the forms of connection. Over against the substantial worldview there rises a conception that has grown upon the ground of the concept of function. At this point it becomes especially clear that the history of modern philosophy cannot be grasped or developed outside the connection with exact science. The dialectical conflict that has arisen here becomes the driving basic force of the future systems: Cartesian as well as Leibnizian epistemology form only determinate individual phases within that general advance from substance to function.

In Galileo, moreover, this new point of view is not only implicitly contained in the kind and direction of his inquiry, but is also formulated with surprising precision and clarity. "Either," so it says in his Letters on Sunspots, "we seek by way of speculation to penetrate into the true and inner essence of natural substances, or we content ourselves with knowledge of some of their empirical marks (`affezioni`). The former attempt I hold to be an endeavor equally vain and futile in the case of the nearest earthly as of the most distant heavenly substances. We know the substance of the earth no more than that of the moon, the substance of our terrestrial atmosphere no more than that of sunspots. For I do not see that in the understanding of nearby substances we possess any advantage other than the abundance of individual determinations, which all remain equally unknown in their metaphysical essence. If I ask, for example, after the substance of clouds, the answer will be that they consist of a moist vapor; if I then wish to know what this vapor is, I may perhaps be told that it is water rarified by the power of heat. **Working Commentary**

```text
1) Fracastoro matters here because he shows how far a serious natural investigator can still remain bound to qualitative and organismic explanation.

2) `operari sequitur esse` is one of the clearest local formulas for what Galilei overturns.

3) Cassirer now explicitly replaces the empiricism/rationalism contrast with the deeper problem of things versus relations, substance versus function.

4) The Galileo quote is not anti-knowledge. It narrows knowledge to `affezioni` precisely in order to make science stricter.

5) Nearness loses epistemic privilege here; law and phenomenal determination outrun the supposedly immediate access of touch.
```
