Der Begriff des Logos bildet nunmehr sowohl die Verknüpfung, wie die Grenzscheide der Zeitalter.

Bei Georgios Gemistos Plethon, dem ersten entschiedenen Verkünder der Platonischen Lehre, ist dies letztere Moment, ist es der Gegensatz gegen das überlieferte theologische System, der deutlich in den Vordergrund tritt. Wenn er Aristoteles bekämpft, so nimmt er seine Naturlehre ausdrücklich aus. Nur seine Metaphysik und Theologie ist es, die er treffen will, — in der er vielmehr die kirchliche Scholastik seiner Zeit zu treffen gedenkt. Nicht um einen Kampf abstrakter philosophischer Lehren handelt es sich hier, sondern um den Kulturgegensatz zwischen Hellenismus und christlichem Mittelalter. Wie Aristoteles die griechische Sprache nicht mehr in ihrer ganzen Reinheit und Fülle widerspiegelt, so erscheint bei ihm — wie ihm von Plethon vorgeworfen wird — auch das antike Lebensideal bereits verkümmert und gebrochen. Die sittliche Erneuerung, die Plethon für den Staat und die Kirche seiner Zeit verlangt, und die im Mittelpunkt all seiner philosophischen Bestrebungen steht, muß sich von der Aristotelischen Autorität befreien, um zu den echten Quellen der selbständigen und humanen Ethik zurückzukehren. Den asketisch-mönchischen Geboten tritt jetzt eine weltliche „Tugendlehre“, dem Dualismus des Jenseitsglaubens tritt der antike Glaube an eine fortdauernde Wanderung und Umgestaltung der Seele entgegen. Es sind die Götter Griechenlands, die zum Kampf gegen das mittelalterliche Ideal aufgerufen werden und die vor allem der politischen Regeneration, für die Plethon in seiner Schrift über die Gesetze eintritt, dienen sollen. Eine neue Religion, die weder christlich noch muhamedanisch sein, sondern alle bisherigen geschichtlichen Formen in einer höheren Einheit aufheben will, soll jetzt entstehen. Die Ideenlehre wandelt sich in eine polytheistische Götterlehre. Die mannigfachen Kräfte, die Plethon zum Aufbau seiner Naturansicht braucht, werden zu persönlichen Einzelwesen hypostasiert und mit besonderen Götternamen belegt.1)

In der Spekulation über die hierarchische Stellung und Rangordnung dieser Kräfte, die vor allem auf Proklus zurückgeht, werden sodann Philosophie und Mythos so unlöslich mit einander verknüpft und vermischt, daß jede Trennung zwischen beiden, jede Heraushebung eines gesonderten, abstrakten Gedankengehalts vergeblich wäre. So interessant daher Plethons Lehre vom Standpunkt der allgemeinen Kulturgeschichte ist, so wenig bedeutet sie für das innerliche, logische Verständnis des Platonismus. In truth, what is now at issue is not yet the appropriation of Plato in his original and genuine form: before this could happen, a preparatory step first had to be taken, and those elements of Platonism that had entered into Christian doctrine and fused with it had to be disentangled and grasped in their own worth. From this point of view the philosophical aims and movements of the Quattrocento can be understood and summed up in a unified way. If Marsilius Ficino, in full subjective sincerity, sees his task in uniting and reconciling Plato's doctrine with revealed religion, this occurs because he is able to behold religion itself only in the light of Platonism, because he can see in it nothing other than the `doctrine of the Logos`. Christianity was able, in the first centuries of its development, to shape and condense itself into a theoretical system only by taking up into itself this basic doctrine of Greek speculation. But in so doing ancient philosophy and science were thereby mediately acknowledged within it, even if they were used and tolerated only as means for interpreting the doctrine of revelation. The first step of the modern age consists in removing this barrier: in restoring the doctrine of the “Logos” according to its full sense and content, instead of using it merely as an instrument of theology. We have already been able to trace this striving and this turn in Nicholas of Cusa (see above, pp. 53ff.). In this connection the otherwise so ambiguous and shimmering relation of the thinkers of this epoch to the Church becomes intelligible. While outwardly they still seem to stand in full substantive agreement with doctrine, in truth they seize upon within it only that moment that stems from philosophy and from Hellenism. The concept of the Logos now forms both the link and the dividing line of the ages.

In Georgios Gemistos Plethon, the first decided herald of Platonic doctrine, it is this latter moment, the opposition to the inherited theological system, that clearly steps into the foreground. When he combats Aristotle, he expressly exempts Aristotle's natural philosophy. It is only his metaphysics and theology that he wishes to strike, or rather the ecclesiastical scholasticism of his own time as mirrored in them. What is at stake here is not a struggle of abstract philosophical doctrines, but the cultural opposition between Hellenism and the Christian Middle Ages. Just as Aristotle no longer reflects the Greek language in its full purity and plenitude, so too in him, as Plethon reproaches him, the ancient ideal of life already appears stunted and broken. The ethical renewal that Plethon demands for the state and the church of his time, and that stands at the center of all his philosophical efforts, must free itself from Aristotelian authority in order to return to the genuine sources of independent and humane ethics. A worldly “doctrine of virtue” now confronts ascetic-monastic precepts; the ancient belief in the enduring migration and transformation of the soul confronts the dualism of belief in the beyond. It is the gods of Greece that are called up for battle against the medieval ideal and that are to serve above all the political regeneration for which Plethon argues in his treatise on the laws. A new religion is now to arise that is neither Christian nor Mohammedan, but will sublate all previous historical forms in a higher unity. The doctrine of Ideas is transformed into a polytheistic doctrine of gods. The manifold powers Plethon requires for the construction of his view of nature are hypostasized into personal individual beings and furnished with special divine names.1)

In the speculation on the hierarchical place and rank-order of these powers, which goes back above all to Proclus, philosophy and myth are then so indissolubly linked and mixed together that any separation between them, any extraction of a distinct abstract content of thought, would be futile. However interesting Plethon's doctrine may therefore be from the standpoint of general cultural history, it means very little for an inward logical understanding of Platonism. The question of fate, the problem whether nature acts according to conscious intention, is treated in detail between Plethon and his next pupil Bessarion in letters and writings; but the actual content of the doctrine of Ideas soon recedes behind investigations of this kind.2) Only occasionally is this “highest dark and difficult problem” still touched upon, not in order to pursue its dialectical grounding, but rather its metaphysical consequences.3) Under these circumstances even the customary comparison between Plato and Aristotle remains without principled yield: among humanists such as George of Trebizond it drifts wholly into the domain of personal accusations. And even where a more exact knowledge of the two systems exists, it is not used to examine them according to their own objective content: rather, the agreement of both doctrines with church doctrine, which according to the party-position of the judge is now asserted and now denied, remains the actually decisive criterion.

To be sure, there exists between Plethon and Marsilius Ficino, both in personality and in the basic intention of their doctrine, a thoroughgoing and characteristic difference. **Notes**

- Cassirer treats the Quattrocento as a preparatory disentangling of Christianized Platonism rather than a direct recovery of Plato's true problem.
- `Logos-Lehre` becomes the mediating key: it both explains Ficino's reconciliation project and marks the line along which philosophy silently extracts itself from theology.
- Plethon matters culturally and ethically more than logically; his Platonism becomes anti-scholastic civil religion and polytheistic myth rather than a renewal of the doctrine of Ideas in its strict sense.
- Ficino broadens the archive but also dulls the reforming edge: Platonism becomes a universal harmonizing center in which even its own historical transformations are flattened into one continuous revelation.
