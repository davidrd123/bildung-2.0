Die Geschichtsphilosophie der Renaissance stellt sich uns zunächst in fast mythischem Gewande, unter dem Bilde einer Uroffenbarung dar, die dem Menschengeschlecht von Anfang an mitgegeben ist und die ihm fortan als dauernder Besitz im Wechsel aller Lebens- und Lehrformen verbleibt. Schon Georgios Gemistos Plethon beruft sich auf jene stetige Kette der Überlieferung, deren einzelne Glieder durch die Namen des Zoroaster und des Merkurios Trismegistos, durch Pythagoras und Platon bezeichnet werden. Die echten Grundlagen der Philosophie sind von allem Anfang an vorhanden gewesen; sie können nur vorübergehend verdunkelt, niemals aber aus der Geschichte der Menschheit ausgetilgt werden. In naiver Form spricht sich der gleiche Gedanke bei Plethons Schüler Bessarion aus, bei dem er mit christlich-theologischen Vorstellungsweisen verschmilzt. Hier wird geradezu ein innerlicher, sachlicher Zusammenhang zwischen der Mosaischen Schöpfungsgeschichte und der Theologie Homers angenommen, der durch direkten Einfluß oder durch natürliche Eingebung zu erklären sei.1) Festere Fügung nimmt die Grundidee sodann, nachdem sie innerhalb der Platonischen Akademie, bei Ficin und Pico, in mannigfacher Weise abgewandelt worden, bei Reuchlin an, bei dem sie zum höchsten Leitgedanken aller Philosophie überhaupt erhoben wird. Alle Forschung kann nunmehr nur noch das eine Ziel haben, in den gemeinsamen Strom der einheitlichen Überlieferung, die durch den Namen der `Cabbala` bezeichnet wird, einzumünden. Der historische Gesichtskreis weitet sich, indem neben der antiken Literatur die hebräischen Quellen herbeigezogen und mit ihr zu einem universalgeschichtlichen Gesamtbild vereint werden. deeper investigation of objective nature leads man back to insight into the true essence of his I, just as, conversely, deeper knowledge of the I opens to him new domains of objective reality. In a typical way the two phases and directions of this process of thought are described and summarized in Carolus Bovillus's work `On Wisdom`. True wisdom, as is there declared, is in its genuine and all-embracing meaning nothing other than the imprinting of the model and ideal of humanity that we carry within us. It is our own uncorrupted essence that we preserve in its image. The entire task of cognition is comprised in this: to penetrate from the first and unfinished feeling of humanity, laid in each of us, to conscious understanding of its concept, to transform the `primus homo` into the `secundus homo`. We give ourselves to natural objects only in order to discover and extract from them all the features akin in essence to our own being; the investigation of the `great world` is meant only to reflect back to us ever more purely the image of the microcosm. Thus man is beginning and end of all knowledge and, as it were, the `palinode` of the world. The original natural unity of his being must be left and broken through; through the free activity of thought it must be transformed into a duality so that from this there may again grow the conscious cognition of the unity of his essence. This elevation of our own nature to reflective self-knowledge forms the highest and final goal of all conceptual labor. `So all wisdom consists in a multiplication and differentiation, a fecundity and radiation of the I: in a duality of man born from an original unity. For the first, sensible man, who bears everything he possesses as a loan from nature, is a unity, yet at the same time the source and beginning of all human fecundity. By conscious art he must refashion and form himself into a duality; he must work out from himself the image of the human species, which is the fruit and end of all wisdom. Thus he wins back twofold, through the abundant yield of art and of labor upon himself, the gifts he has received from nature, and becomes a double-man: qui a natura homo tantum erat, artis fenore et uberrimo proventu reduplicatus homo vocatur et homohomo.`1) These words, which one could choose as a motto to designate the general character of the epoch, contain, as if in concentrated form, the basic thought of humanism: history and contemplation of nature are for it only means of advancing, through self-conscious spiritual energy, to an intensification of human essence and human worth.

* * *

The philosophy of history of the Renaissance first presents itself to us in almost mythic garb, under the image of a primordial revelation given to the human race from the beginning and henceforth remaining to it as an enduring possession amid all changes of forms of life and teaching. Already Georgios Gemistos Plethon appeals to that continuous chain of transmission whose individual links are designated by the names of Zoroaster and Mercurius Trismegistus, of Pythagoras and Plato. The genuine foundations of philosophy have been present from the very beginning; they can only be temporarily obscured, never eradicated from the history of humanity. In naive form the same thought is expressed in Plethon's pupil Bessarion, in whom it fuses with Christian-theological ways of representation. Here an inner objective connection is directly assumed between the Mosaic story of creation and the theology of Homer, to be explained either by direct influence or by natural inspiration.1) The basic idea then receives firmer articulation, after being variously transformed within the Platonic Academy in Ficino and Pico, in Reuchlin, for whom it is raised to the highest guiding thought of philosophy as such. All inquiry can henceforth have only the one goal of flowing into the common stream of the unified tradition designated by the name of `Cabbala`. The historical horizon widens as Hebrew sources are brought in alongside ancient literature and united with it into a universal-historical total picture. For all their acuteness and comprehensive spiritual giftedness, the Greeks would not have been able to attain the highest, had not Pythagoras received the first seeds of true philosophy from the Orient. `Thus he too, who first received these seeds, may with full right be called a Cabbalist, although he first exchanged the unknown name of Cabbala for the Greek surname of philosophy.`2) The whole history of spirit is accordingly thought as a single continuous tradition, as the explanation and interpretation of an abiding and given foundational text. In this foundational text Christianity too is supposed to possess its own firmest support: `nulla est scientia quae nos magis certificat de divinitate Christi quam Magia et Cabala`.1)

Against this view, however, another way of looking soon rises, at first still proceeding alongside it without firm separation, but gradually awakening to ever clearer independent consciousness. According to this view, the content and theme of human history consists not in a fixed externally derived revelation, but in the unified human reason that unfolds successively in manifold forms and stages. Already in Plethon it is stated that the criterion deciding which teachings we are to think as belonging to the whole of authentic tradition is to be sought in ourselves alone. And more and more the double meaning comes to prevail that history gains in the Renaissance as a whole, in that it is thought not merely as the depiction of a one-time state of affairs, but at the same time as the veil and presentation of an enduring content. Political historiography already lets this basic trait stand out clearly: for the greatest, such as Machiavelli, the manifold historical destinies of nations are only, as it were, a variable and fleeting garb behind which there shines through, clearly recognizable, the always identical empirical basic essence of man. `History`, in its proper scientific sense, according to which it aims at causal understanding of events, is therefore nothing other than applied psychology. **Notes**

- Cassirer uses Bovillus to compress the humanist program into one formula: knowing nature and knowing the self are reciprocal movements, and both exist to intensify the human being into a reflective `second man`.
- The key payoff is strong: history and nature are not separate ends, but instruments of human self-potentiation. That is the humanist center Cassirer now makes explicit.
- The philosophy of history first appears in a mythic form as primordial revelation and continuous transmission. Plethon, Bessarion, and Reuchlin all read intellectual history as commentary on a standing original text.
- `Cabbala` becomes the emblem of that universal transmission model, where Greek and Hebrew sources are unified and Christianity seeks its deepest historical warrant.
- Cassirer then immediately sets a second model beside it: history as the successive unfolding of one human reason. That is the more modern line, and it already appears in Plethon before becoming clearer in political historiography and pedagogy.

- `[visual candidate]` The paired history models are also diagrammable: primordial revelation and transmission on one side, successive unfolding of human reason on the other.
