This sense and this force of skepticism finally emerge, more clearly than in the domain of theoretical knowledge, at the principles of morality. At first, to be sure, the disappearance of the unconditional standard here seems to uproot and annihilate the ethical question itself. Just as sensation reveals to us not the being of the external object, but only the nature of our own I in outward reflection, so the value that seems to adhere to things is in truth no objective property of the things themselves, but the reflex of the judging subject. In itself nothing is either good or evil: only our `representation` confers this character upon it: `we call value in things not what they bring, but what we bring to them` (II, 40). But with that the concept of the good is given over to boundless indeterminacy and ambiguity: for nowhere does the contradiction and incompatibility of individuals and peoples stand out more sharply than here. There is no custom, however extreme and fantastical, that is not sanctified by the law of some nation; no acknowledged moral content that does not change into its opposite with the change of times or places. Local and political boundaries become boundaries for the concept of morality: `what kind of goodness is it that, held in esteem and validity yesterday, is so no longer tomorrow and becomes a crime when one crosses a river? what kind of truth, bounded by these mountains and turned into a lie beyond them?` (II, 12). `The first and universal grounds of moral prescriptions are hard to grasp and foam away in the hands of our teachers; or else they do not even dare to touch them, but throw themselves from the start into the sanctuary of custom; there they swell up and celebrate a cheap triumph` (I, 22). Thus we find ourselves delivered over to external opinion and the given convention as our only guides: `the authority of laws does not come from their being just, but from their being laws; this alone is the mystical ground of their authority; they have no other.`

And yet in this consequence, in which Montaigne's skeptical concept of doctrine completes itself, there lies at the same time the reversal of his philosophical view as a whole. It is the problem of morality at which an inner turn of thought is accomplished. For skepticism from the start carries within itself here, as in antiquity, a determinate and positive ethical guiding thought. Its final end is `ataraxia`: through turning away from all absolute ends, mind is to attain within itself a fixed point of equilibrium and repose that is no longer touched by the change of outward things. What all striving after knowledge could not accomplish, self-conscious and voluntary renunciation accomplishes. By stripping all particular authoritative norms of their mystical origin, doubt protects the individual, who may in practice still submit to them, from inwardly surrendering himself to them unconditionally. It is skepticism that preserves the individual from having the moral standard imposed on him from outside, that assures him, against all arbitrary moral conventions, of the freedom of his judgment in thought. Montaigne's critique thus, as becomes ever clearer, is directed not so much against the `good` as against the relative and mutable `goods`. Thus from the very beginning, and immediately alongside the skeptical guiding theses, the basic motives of Stoic ethics emerge as determinative in the `Essais`. Among the classical authors whose quotations are strewn in colorful abundance through the work, Seneca stands in the first place. Just as Montaigne presents his character and style in a characteristic literary portrait, so his writings, together with those of Plutarch, form the inexhaustible source from which, in his own words, he `draws unceasingly like the Danaids.` (I, 25; II, 10; II, 32.) Hegel's general remark that skepticism and Stoicism necessarily refer to one another and condition one another reciprocally finds in Montaigne its characteristic historical confirmation. `Skeptical self-consciousness experiences, in the flux of all that seeks to establish itself for it, its own freedom as given and preserved by itself; it is to itself this ataraxia of self-thinking, the unchangeable and true certainty of itself.`1) If in the theoretical sphere the critical dissolution of the absolute object did not lead to an attempt at a scientific theory of appearances, and if here too the concept of the I offered no secure footing, in the moral sphere the annihilation of external norms immediately contains the summons to rebuild from one's own interior the law that thereby seems destroyed. That value does not adhere to things, but that it is we who bring it to them: this `subjectivism` is not the refutation, but the beginning and the positive presupposition of the possibility of ethics.

Now the `laws of nature` of morality stand opposed as criterion to arbitrary usages and ordinances. `We have abandoned nature, which guided us so surely and happily, and want it to take instruction from us, and yet our knowledge must constantly return to it and to the traces of its instruction in order to find in it the pattern of constancy, innocence, and repose. There thus exists an identical common fundamental law that is concealed and distorted from us only by the sophistications of our reason. If, from the standpoint of theoretical knowledge, nature dissolved for us into a chaos of lawless impressions, it signifies for the moral problem the source and guarantee of law. Skepticism becomes the means that leads back to this genuine ground. By annihilating within itself and canceling against each other the falsifying accretions of `reason`, it thereby restores the rule of nature in its originality and purity. Every external and transcendent grounding of morality is therefore superfluous and harmful: only that action is truly valuable which is not determined by external prescriptions but proceeds from its own inner norm. Morality must therefore learn above all to do without the support of `religion`, which binds its commands to fear and hope. `I love it such that laws and religions do not make it, but perfect and authorize it; such that it feels itself able to sustain itself without aid, born in us from its own roots, from the seed of universal reason imprinted in every man not denatured` (III, 12). **Notes**

- The skepticism chapter now pivots from epistemology into ethics without abandoning its central structure. The dissolution of objective value first appears as moral indeterminacy, then turns into the condition for inward norm-formation.
- Cassirer's Montaigne is not moving toward moral nihilism. The skeptical destruction of external standards becomes the path toward `ataraxia`, Stoic self-possession, and the reconstruction of law from within.
- The decisive reversal is that `Subjektivismus` becomes positive: value is not found in things but brought to them, and this becomes the beginning rather than the refutation of ethics.
- Nature re-enters under a different sign than in the epistemological argument. There it dissolved into lawless impressions; here it returns as the source and guarantee of moral law.
- The von Foerster pressure that was decisive in Part `45` does not govern this tranche in the same direct way. The chapter has turned from organ-conditioned mediation to Stoic and moral reconstruction.
- The tranche stops at the printed page `183` boundary, with Cassirer about to continue from `Und wenngleich die Theorie der Wissenschaft`.
