# Section 25: Resurrection and Form (§581-597, pp. 107-108)

What returns here is rarely what the present counts as current: old age, dead literary blocks, exact phrasing, translated poetry, and forms of life that fashion has already written off.

---

## §581
> El que no se anticipa a la vejez no prolonga su juventud,
> sino corrompe hasta sus recuerdos.

**Close:** He who does not anticipate old age does not prolong his youth, but corrupts even his memories.

**Free:** Whoever refuses to anticipate old age does not preserve youth; he corrupts even the memory of it.

`tags: old-age, youth, memory`

---

## §582
> En nuestra vida intelectual, como en la historia, ciertos
> bloques literarios —géneros, épocas, lenguas— que se habían
> vuelto ilegibles, e insípidos, resurgen de pronto remozados y
> frescos. Todo cadáver intelectual puede resucitar súbitamente
> con vitalidad portentosa.

**Close:** In our intellectual life, as in history, certain literary blocks —genres, epochs, languages— that had become illegible, and insipid, suddenly resurge renewed and fresh. Every intellectual corpse can suddenly resurrect with portentous vitality.

**Free:** In our intellectual life, as in history, whole literary blocks —genres, periods, languages— that had become unreadable and insipid can suddenly rise again, renewed and fresh. Any intellectual corpse may unexpectedly resurrect with startling vitality.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the clearest statements in the volume that apparent death is not final judgment. What loses vigency may return with force once the reader and the historical air around him change.

`tags: resurrection, literature, vigency`

## §583
> Mientras no convierten la igualdad en dogma, nos
> podemos tratar como iguales.

**Close:** So long as they do not turn equality into dogma, we can treat each other as equals.

**Free:** So long as equality is not made into a dogma, we can perfectly well treat one another as equals.

`tags: equality, dogma, relation`

## §584
> No esperemos nada de la novela mientras el buen novelista
> no se resuelva a escribir, como en el siglo pasado, "malas"
> novelas.

**Close:** Let us expect nothing from the novel so long as the good novelist does not resolve to write, as in the last century, "bad" novels.

**Free:** We should expect nothing from the novel until the good novelist resolves, as in the last century, to write "bad" novels again.

`tags: novel, novelist, bad-novels`

## §585
> La menos probatoria de las pruebas, en filosofía, es la
> que pretende confirmar las tesis de un sistema con tesis de
> otro.

**Close:** The least probative of proofs, in philosophy, is the one that pretends to confirm the theses of one system by means of the theses of another.

**Free:** In philosophy, the least convincing kind of proof is the one that tries to confirm one system's theses by borrowing the theses of another.

`tags: philosophy, proof, system`

## §586
> El hombre inteligente no tiene ideas confusas sino
> cuando abriga, sin saberlo, inconfesables propósitos.

**Close:** The intelligent man has confused ideas only when he harbors, without knowing it, unconfessable purposes.

**Free:** An intelligent man has confused ideas only when he is secretly harboring purposes he cannot confess.

`tags: intelligence, confusion, motive`

## §587
> Aun cuando la erudición hoy florezca, las ciencias
> humanas están estrangulando la historia. Psicología,
> sociología, economía, etc, construyen esquemas intemporales
> que restauran, subrepticiamente, al hombre abstracto del
> dieciocho.

**Close:** Even though erudition flourishes today, the human sciences are strangling history. Psychology, sociology, economics, etc., construct atemporal schemas that surreptitiously restore the abstract man of the eighteenth century.

**Free:** However much erudition may flourish today, the human sciences are strangling history. Psychology, sociology, economics, and the rest build timeless schemes that quietly restore the eighteenth century's abstract man.

**Exegesis:**
This is a direct accusation against explanatory frameworks that look more concrete than they are. The cost of their elegance is the return of an abstract human type that history had been supposed to dislodge.

`tags: human-sciences, history, abstraction`

## §591
> El mundo debe servir de tema, pero no de norma. Así
> como un espectáculo vulgar se pliega, sobre el lienzo, a
> imperativos estéticos.

**Close:** The world must serve as theme, but not as norm. Just as a vulgar spectacle bends itself, upon the canvas, to aesthetic imperatives.

**Free:** The world should supply the theme, but never the norm, just as a vulgar spectacle, once placed on canvas, submits to aesthetic imperatives.

`tags: world, norm, aesthetics`

## §592
> La civilización no consiste en la suma de objetos que la
> componen, como tampoco el hombre en la suma de órganos que
> lo constituyen.

**Close:** Civilization does not consist in the sum of the objects that compose it, just as man does not consist in the sum of the organs that constitute him.

**Free:** Civilization no more consists in the sum of its objects than a man consists in the sum of his organs.

`tags: civilization, object, organism`

---

## §593
> El verso tiende a mentir espontáneamente. Pero es sólo
> en verso que el alma logra a veces confesarse.

**Close:** Verse tends spontaneously to lie. But it is only in verse that the soul sometimes manages to confess itself.

**Free:** Verse tends naturally toward lying, yet only in verse does the soul sometimes manage to confess itself.

**Exegesis:**
The aphorism treats poetry as dangerous and necessary at once. The same medium that tempts one toward falsification can also become the only medium adequate to confession.

`tags: verse, soul, confession`

---

## §594
> La capacidad de leer poemas traducidos es signo
> inequívoco de insensibilidad a la poesía.

**Close:** The capacity to read translated poems is an unequivocal sign of insensibility to poetry.

**Free:** Being able to read poetry in translation without loss is a sure sign of insensitivity to poetry.

`tags: poetry, translation, insensibility`

## §595
> No añoro una naturaleza virgen, una naturaleza sin la
> huella campesina que la ennoblece y sin el palacio que corona
> la colina. Sino una naturaleza a salvo de industrialismos
> plebeyos y de manipuleos irreverentes.

**Close:** I do not long for a virgin nature, a nature without the peasant trace that ennobles it and without the palace that crowns the hill. But rather for a nature safe from plebeian industrialisms and irreverent manipulations.

**Free:** I do not long for virgin nature stripped of the peasant mark that ennobles it or of the palace crowning the hill, but for a nature kept safe from plebeian industrialism and irreverent tampering.

`tags: nature, peasant, industrialism`

## §596
> Ni desconozcamos los méritos del autor subalterno, ni

**Close:** Let us neither fail to recognize the merits of the subordinate author, nor lose time in reading him.

**Free:** Let us not deny the subordinate author's merits, but neither should we waste our time reading him.

`tags: author, merit, subaltern`

## §597

**Close:** Few readers suspect the cunning a sentence needs in order to seem naive.

**Free:** Few readers suspect how much cunning a sentence requires in order to appear naive.

`tags: sentence, naivete, cunning`
