# Section 40: Progress and Ugliness (§881-900, pp. 156-159)

The key pressure here is against terrestrial destiny: progress, posterity, and public virtue are all measured against ends they cannot satisfy.

---

## §881
> El cristianismo nunca enseñó que la historia tuviera finalidad, sino fin.

**Close:** Christianity never taught that history had purpose, but end.

**Free:** Christianity never taught that history had a goal, but an end.

**Exegesis:**
One word does the work. "Finality" would make history purposive from within; "end" marks cessation, judgment, closure.

`tags: Christianity, history, end`

---

## §882
> La desventura del moderno no es tener que vivir una vida mediocre, sino creer que podría vivir una que no lo fuera.

**Close:** The misfortune of the modern is not to have to live a mediocre life, but to believe that he could live one that was not.

**Free:** The modern man's misery lies not in having to live a mediocre life, but in believing he might have lived a non-mediocre one.

`tags: modernity, mediocrity, illusion`

---

## §883
> La democracia es el régimen político donde el ciudadano confía los intereses públicos a quienes no confiaría jamás sus intereses privados.

**Close:** Democracy is the political regime where the citizen entrusts public interests to those to whom he would never entrust his private interests.

**Free:** Democracy is the regime in which the citizen entrusts public interests to people he would never trust with his private affairs.

`tags: democracy, citizen, trust`

---

## §884
> La inmoralidad máxima consiste en cualquier contribución al progreso.

**Close:** The maximum immorality consists in any contribution to progress.

**Free:** The supreme immorality lies in any contribution to progress.

`tags: progress, immorality, contribution`

---

## §885
> Para no ser crápula hoy se requiere un alma casi tan vigorosa como en otros siglos para ser santo.

**Close:** In order not to be a debauchee today one requires an almost as vigorous soul as in other centuries to be a saint.

**Free:** Today it takes almost as vigorous a soul merely not to be debased as it once took to become a saint.

`tags: soul, debasement, sanctity`

---

## §886
> Toda obra de arte nos habla de Dios. Diga lo que diga.

**Close:** Every work of art speaks to us of God. Whatever it may say.

**Free:** Every work of art speaks to us of God, whatever it may happen to say.

**Exegesis:**
The claim is not about overt religious content. Art says God even when its declared content says something else.

`tags: art, God, expression`

---

## §887
> El mundo felizmente es inexplicable. ¡Qué sería un mundo explicable por el hombre!

**Close:** Happily the world is inexplicable. What would a world explicable by man be!

**Free:** Happily the world is inexplicable. What would a world be that man could fully explain!

`tags: world, inexplicable, explanation`

---

## §888
> La ventura del hombre dependerá finalmente de que recobre la capacidad de conjugar el verbo: renunciar.

**Close:** Man's good fortune will depend finally on his recovering the capacity to conjugate the verb: to renounce.

**Free:** Man's fate will finally depend on recovering the ability to conjugate one verb: renounce.

`tags: renunciation, fortune, capacity`

## §889
> La única empresa del hombre -digna de respeto sería la de levantar monasterios sobre todas las colinas.

**Close:** The only enterprise of man worthy of respect would be that of raising monasteries on all the hills.

**Free:** The only human enterprise worthy of respect would be to raise monasteries on every hill.

`tags: monastery, enterprise, respect`

## §890
> La literatura es la más sutil, y quizá la única exacta de las filosofías.

**Close:** Literature is the most subtle, and perhaps the only exact, of the philosophies.

**Free:** Literature is the subtlest, and perhaps the only exact, form of philosophy.

`tags: literature, philosophy, exactness`

---

## §891
> Noble es la persona capaz de no hacer todo lo que podría.

**Close:** Noble is the person capable of not doing everything he could.

**Free:** Noble is the person capable of not doing everything he has the power to do.

`tags: nobility, restraint, capacity`

---

## §892
> Las civilizaciones son obra de los que asignan al hombre un fin transmundano. Su destrucción es obra de quienes le fijan un destino terrestre.

**Close:** Civilizations are the work of those who assign to man a transmundane end. Their destruction is the work of those who fix for him a terrestrial destiny.

**Free:** Civilizations are built by those who assign man an end beyond the world. They are destroyed by those who assign him a destiny entirely within it.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the clearest transmundane/terrestrial contrasts in the volume. Civilizations live by an end beyond the world; they perish by being locked into worldly destiny.

`tags: civilization, transmundane, destiny`

---

## §893
> Dialogar con quienes no comparten nuestros postulados no es más que una manera tonta de matar el tiempo.

**Close:** To dialogue with those who do not share our postulates is nothing more than a stupid way of killing time.

**Free:** Dialoguing with those who do not share our premises is only a foolish way to kill time.

`tags: dialogue, premises, futility`

---

## §894
> La difusión de la cultura tuvo por efecto capacitar al tonto a parlotear de lo que ignora.

**Close:** The diffusion of culture had the effect of capacitating the fool to chatter about what he ignores.

**Free:** The spread of culture has mainly enabled fools to chatter about what they do not know.

`tags: culture, fool, chatter`

---

## §895
> Las tradiciones sociales son el substituto de la fuerza. Violencia estatal o violencia popular es la alternativa de las sociedades en "progreso constante".

**Close:** Social traditions are the substitute for force. State violence or popular violence is the alternative of societies in "constant progress."

**Free:** Social traditions are what stand in for force. State violence or popular violence is the alternative facing societies in "constant progress."

**Exegesis:**
The claim is not antiquarian. Tradition matters because without it only naked force remains, whether exercised from above or below.

`tags: tradition, force, violence`

---

## §896
> Bien común, voluntad general, necesidad histórica, son los nombres con que el adulón de turno bautiza los caprichos de la fuerza.

**Close:** Common good, general will, historical necessity are the names with which the flatterer of the hour baptizes the caprices of force.

**Free:** Common good, general will, historical necessity: these are the names the flatterer of the day gives to force's whims.

`tags: force, common-good, necessity`

---

## §897
> La historia social no puede contentarse con inventariar sin juzgar. La existencia de estructuras presupone juicios de valor.

**Close:** Social history cannot be content with inventorying without judging. The existence of structures presupposes value judgments.

**Free:** Social history cannot be satisfied merely to inventory without judging. The very existence of structures presupposes judgments of value.

`tags: social-history, judgment, structure`

---

## §898
> Como criterio de lo mejor, el hombre moderno no conoce sino la posterioridad.

**Close:** As criterion of the better, modern man knows nothing but posteriority.

**Free:** As a criterion of what is better, modern man knows only what comes later.

`tags: modernity, criterion, posteriority`

---

## §899
> Para descubrir al tonto no hay mejor reactivo que la palabra: medieval. Inmediatamente ve rojo.

**Close:** To discover the fool there is no better reagent than the word: medieval. Immediately he sees red.

**Free:** There is no better reagent for discovering the fool than the word "medieval": he immediately sees red.

`tags: medieval, fool, reagent`

---

## §900
> El hombre se acostumbra a la fealdad absoluta y al mal puro, con facilidad, que espanta. Un infierno sin suplicios se convierte fácilmente en lugar algo tórrido de veraneo.

**Close:** Man grows accustomed to absolute ugliness and pure evil with an ease that terrifies. A hell without torments easily becomes a somewhat torrid vacation place.

**Free:** Man gets used to absolute ugliness and pure evil with frightening ease. A hell without explicit tortures easily becomes a rather hot vacation spot.

**Exegesis:**
The terror lies in adaptation. Pure evil need not present itself dramatically in order to become livable.

`tags: ugliness, evil, adaptation`
