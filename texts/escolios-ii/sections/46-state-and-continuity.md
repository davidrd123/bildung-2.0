# Section 46: State and Continuity (§1001-1020, pp. 175-178)

Here the pressure falls on substitution: state for society, ideology for nature, hermeneutics for theology, and self-assertion for seriousness.

---

## §1001
> El socialista asevera que sus opiniones son conclusión de un raciocinio, simplemente porque ignora que son prole de las secretas bodas de observaciones inexactas con postulados clandestinos.

**Close:** The socialist asserts that his opinions are the conclusion of a reasoning, simply because he ignores that they are the offspring of the secret weddings of inexact observations with clandestine postulates.

**Free:** The socialist calls his views the result of reasoning simply because he cannot see that they are the offspring of bad observations and hidden postulates.

`tags: socialism, postulates, reasoning`

---

## §1002
> Ninguna clase social ha explotado más descaradamente a las otras que la que hoy se llama a sí misma "estado".

**Close:** No social class has exploited the others more shamelessly than the one that today calls itself "state."

**Free:** No social class has exploited the others more brazenly than the class that now calls itself "the state."

`tags: state, class, exploitation`

## §1003
> Arte, literatura, filosofía, son cosas demasiado importantes para que hablemos de ellas con énfasis.

**Close:** Art, literature, philosophy, are things too important for us to speak of them with emphasis.

**Free:** Art, literature, and philosophy matter too much to be discussed in an inflated tone.

`tags: art, philosophy, tone`

## §1004
> No es justo reprochar su mal gusto a los escritores de este siglo, donde la noción misma de gusto pereció.

**Close:** It is not fair to reproach the writers of this century for their bad taste, where the very notion of taste perished.

**Free:** It is unfair to reproach the writers of this century for bad taste when the very idea of taste has died.

`tags: taste, writers, century`

**Close:** Denying that a "human nature" exists is an ideological ruse of the optimist for defending himself against history.

**Free:** Denying human nature is the optimist's ideological trick for defending himself against history.

## §1006
> La evidencia nueva no es más perfecta que la evidencia vieja. Es meramente una nueva evidencia.

**Close:** New evidence is not more perfect than old evidence. It is merely a new evidence.

**Free:** New evidence is not superior to old evidence. It is only new.

## §1007
> Si el hombre llegare a fabricar un hombre, el enigma del hombre no habrá sido descifrado sino entenebrecido.

**Close:** If man should come to fabricate a man, the enigma of man will not have been deciphered but darkened.

**Free:** If man ever manufactures a man, the human enigma will not have been solved but made darker.

`tags: man, fabrication, enigma`

## §1008
> Del activismo puro desatado por la borrachera revolucionaria, el hombre despierta, una mañana lívida, entre vómitos y sangre.

**Close:** From pure activism unleashed by revolutionary drunkenness, man awakens, one livid morning, amid vomit and blood.

**Free:** After the pure activism unleashed by revolutionary drunkenness, man wakes one pallid morning amid vomit and blood.

`tags: activism, revolution, aftermath`

## §1009
> El que lucha contra el envejecimiento envejece meramente sin madurar. Si creemos en Dios no debemos decir: Creo en Dios, sino: Dios cree en mí.

**Close:** He who fights against aging merely ages without maturing. If we believe in God we must not say: I believe in God, but rather: God believes in me.

**Free:** Whoever fights aging merely grows old without ripening. If we believe in God, we should not say "I believe in God" but "God believes in me."

**Exegesis:**
The second sentence shifts belief from possession to answerability. Faith is no longer primarily my act toward God, but God's prior wager on me.

`tags: aging, faith, maturity`

## §1010
> Compadezcamos al igualitario. Qué infortunio ignorar que hay rangos y rangos por encima de nuestra mediocridad. A veces dudamos de la sinceridad del que nos adula, pero nunca del acierto de sus adulaciones.

**Close:** Let us pity the egalitarian. What misfortune to ignore that there are ranks and ranks above our mediocrity. At times we doubt the sincerity of him who flatters us, but never the correctness of his flatteries.

**Free:** Pity the egalitarian. What a misfortune not to know that there are ranks above our mediocrity, and ranks above those. We may doubt the flatterer's sincerity, but never the accuracy of the compliment.

`tags: equality, rank, flattery`

## §1011
> Dos condiciones son necesarias para que una aristocracia nazca: que las leyes no lo impidan y que no lo faciliten.

**Close:** Two conditions are necessary for an aristocracy to be born: that the laws not impede it and that they not facilitate it.

**Free:** Two conditions are needed for an aristocracy to arise: the laws must neither block it nor manufacture it.

`tags: aristocracy, law, birth`

## §1012
> La memoria de una civilización está en la continuidad de sus instituciones. La revolución que la interrumpe, destruyéndolas, no le quita a la sociedad un caparazón quitinoso que la paraliza, sino meramente la compele a volver a empezar.

**Close:** The memory of a civilization is in the continuity of its institutions. The revolution that interrupts it, by destroying them, does not take from society a chitinous shell that paralyzes it, but merely compels it to begin again.

**Free:** A civilization's memory lies in the continuity of its institutions. The revolution that destroys them does not strip society of a paralyzing shell; it simply forces society to start over.

**Exegesis:**
Institutional continuity is not treated here as dead casing but as the medium of memory itself. Revolution does not liberate a living core from a husk; it wipes out accumulated form.

`tags: civilization, institutions, revolution`

## §1013
> El combate intelectual no se gana levantando barricadas, sino dejando cortésmente el campo libre, para que las tonterías del adversario se rompan solas las narices.

**Close:** Intellectual combat is not won by raising barricades, but by courteously leaving the field free, so that the adversary's stupidities break their noses by themselves.

**Free:** Intellectual combat is not won by throwing up barricades but by politely leaving the field open until the adversary's stupidities break their own noses.

`tags: intellect, polemic, stupidity`

## §1014
> "Renunciar al mundo" deja de ser hazaña, para volverse tentación, a medida que el Progreso progresa.

**Close:** "Renouncing the world" ceases to be a feat, to become a temptation, as Progress progresses.

**Free:** As Progress advances, "renouncing the world" ceases to be a feat and becomes a temptation.

`tags: progress, renunciation, world`

## §1015
> Nadie debe tomarse en serio. Sólo esperar resultar tal.

**Close:** No one should take himself seriously. He should only hope to turn out such.

**Free:** No one should take himself seriously; one can only hope to prove serious in the end.

**Exegesis:**
Seriousness is not a stance one may assume. It is a result one may at best deserve.

`tags: seriousness, humility, result`

## §1016
> La teología protestante cae fácilmente en la tentación de convertirse en una hermenéutica histórica, la teología católica en la de volverse una hermenéutica jurídica.

**Close:** Protestant theology easily falls into the temptation of becoming a historical hermeneutics; Catholic theology into that of turning itself into a juridical hermeneutics.

**Free:** Protestant theology easily degenerates into historical hermeneutics, Catholic theology into juridical hermeneutics.

`tags: theology, protestantism, catholicism`

## §1017
> Los especialistas no pueden comunicarse mutuamente las particularidades que verdaderamente saben, sino las generalidades que erróneamente creen saber.

**Close:** Specialists cannot communicate to one another the particularities that they truly know, but rather the generalities that they mistakenly believe they know.

**Free:** Specialists cannot really share the particulars they genuinely know; what they exchange are the generalities they mistakenly think they know.

`tags: specialists, particulars, generalities`
