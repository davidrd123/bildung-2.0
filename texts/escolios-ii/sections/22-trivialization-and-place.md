# Section 22: Trivialization and Place (§527-545, pp. 98-100)

This stretch keeps showing how quickly things are debased once their proper place, style, or hierarchy is lost.

---

## §527
> Dios es la realidad que el deista pierde y que el
> panteista recupera.

**Close:** God is the reality that the deist loses and the pantheist recovers.

**Free:** God is the reality the deist loses and the pantheist, however confusedly, recovers.

`tags: God, deism, pantheism`

---

## §528
> No existe objeto que una interpretación proterva no
> pueda colocar dentro de un contexto que lo envilezca.

**Close:** There exists no object that a perverse interpretation cannot place within a context that debases it.

**Free:** There is no object that a malicious interpretation cannot set in a context that degrades it.

`tags: interpretation, context, debasement`

## §529
> Volver trivial toda cosa está al alcance de cualquiera.

**Close:** To make anything trivial is within anyone's reach.

**Free:** Anyone can trivialize anything.

`tags: triviality, ease, degradation`

## §530
> A finales del siglo pasado sólo hubo un "arte sin
> estilo", en la segunda mitad de éste sólo hay un estilo sin arte.

**Close:** At the end of the last century there was only an "art without style"; in the second half of this one there is only a style without art.

**Free:** At the end of the last century one could still find art without style; in this century's second half one finds only style without art.

`tags: art, style, century`

## §531
> Las extravagancias del arte moderno están
> enseñándonos a apreciar debidamente las insipideces del
> arte clásico.

**Close:** The extravagances of modern art are teaching us to appreciate properly the insipidities of classical art.

**Free:** The extravagances of modern art are teaching us to value, at their proper worth, the insipidities of classical art.

`tags: modern-art, classical-art, extravagance`

---

## §532
> Las burocracias no suceden casualmente a las
> revoluciones. Las revoluciones son los partos sangrientos de
> las burocracias.

**Close:** Bureaucracies do not succeed revolutions accidentally. Revolutions are the bloody labors of bureaucracies.

**Free:** Bureaucracies do not merely come after revolutions by chance; revolutions are the bloody birth-labors of bureaucracies.

**Exegesis:**
The aphorism reverses a common chronology. Bureaucracy is not the later corruption of revolutionary energy; the revolution itself is already the birth process of the apparatus that will emerge from it.

`tags: bureaucracy, revolution, birth`

## §533
> Los argumentos que ruedan por el mundo son de tal

**Close:** The arguments that roll through the world are of such a kind that the common man can only hit the mark by mistake.

**Free:** The arguments that circulate through the world are so bad that ordinary man can only be right by accident.

`tags: argument, common-man, error`

## §534
> Un universo que cruzó el Macedonio es, por lo menos,
> capaz de arcángeles.

**Close:** A universe crossed by the Macedonian is, at the very least, capable of archangels.

**Free:** A universe once crossed by Alexander is, at the very least, capable of archangels.

`tags: universe, Alexander, archangels`

## §535
> Los que no se dejan conmover por retóricas nobles no
> son ponderados, prácticos, ecuánimes, sino viles.

**Close:** Those who do not let themselves be moved by noble rhetorics are not measured, practical, or equitable, but vile.

**Free:** Those who cannot be moved by noble rhetoric are not sober or practical or even-handed; they are vile.

`tags: rhetoric, nobility, vileness`

---

## §536
> Las más nobles cosas de la tierra quizá no existan, sino
> en las palabras que las evocan. Pero basta que allí estén,
> para que sean.

**Close:** The noblest things on earth perhaps exist only in the words that evoke them. But it is enough that they be there for them to be.

**Free:** The noblest things on earth may exist nowhere except in the words that evoke them. But if they are there, that already suffices for them to be.

**Exegesis:**
This line does not treat language as a poor substitute for reality. It grants evocative language a thinner but still real mode of being: enough to keep the noble from vanishing altogether.

`tags: language, nobility, evocation`

---

## §537
> El escritor debe exhibir sin epítetos las vilezas que
> muestra, para que el lector que no se indigna
> espontáneamente automáticamente se condene.

**Close:** The writer must exhibit, without epithets, the vilenesses he shows, so that the reader who does not become indignant spontaneously may automatically condemn himself.

**Free:** The writer should show vileness without moralizing epithets, so that any reader who is not spontaneously indignant condemns himself.

`tags: writer, vileness, indignation`

---

## §538
> La historia es el arte de dar a los términos generales los
> tintes distintos que tienen en cada época.

**Close:** History is the art of giving to general terms the distinct tints they have in each age.

**Free:** History is the art of restoring to general terms the different shades they take on in each age.

**Exegesis:**
This is a concise counterpart to the earlier attack on hyperchronism. Historical intelligence does not abolish general terms; it recolors them until they become answerable to the age in which they were actually used.

`tags: history, terms, tint`

---

## §539
> Los dioses no habitan sino las comarcas, los aposentos,
> las almas, donde la historia, la humilde historia, acumula
> piadosamente sus trastos. La maldición de las obras
> modernas es que no puedan fundarse sino sobre el suelo
> limpio. Sobre la roca estéril.

**Close:** The gods inhabit only the regions, the rooms, the souls where history, humble history, piously accumulates its old things. The curse of modern works is that they can be founded only upon clean ground. Upon sterile rock.

**Free:** The gods dwell only in those regions, rooms, and souls where humble history has piously accumulated its clutter. The curse of modern works is that they can be built only on clean ground, on sterile rock.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the clearest anti-cleanliness aphorisms in the volume. Sacred habitation requires accumulation, residue, inherited furniture; the modern demand for pure ground leaves only sterility.

`tags: gods, history, residue`

---

## §540
> Las insolencias del adolescente no son más que patadas
> del asno que se acomoda al establo. Mientras que la
> insolencia del adulto que arroja bruscamente de sus hombros
> los años de paciencia que lo encorvan es un espectáculo
> admirable.

**Close:** The insolences of the adolescent are nothing more than kicks of the donkey settling itself into the stable. Whereas the insolence of the adult who brusquely throws from his shoulders the years of patience that bend him is an admirable spectacle.

**Free:** Adolescent insolence is only the kicking of the donkey settling into the stable, whereas the insolence of the adult who suddenly throws off the years of patience that have bent his shoulders is an admirable sight.

`tags: adolescence, insolence, adulthood`

## §541
> Obligaciones o placeres, objetos o personas: basta
> moverlos del sitio subordinado que a cada cual corresponde,
> para convertirlos en nada.

**Close:** Obligations or pleasures, objects or persons: it suffices to move them from the subordinate place that belongs to each in order to turn them into nothing.

**Free:** Obligations or pleasures, objects or persons: move any of them out of the subordinate place proper to it, and you turn it into nothing.

`tags: order, subordination, nothingness`

## §542
> Pedirle a una cosa lo que no estuvo destinada a dar es la
> manera eficaz de volver irrisorio lo que da.

**Close:** To ask of a thing what it was not destined to give is the effective way of making ridiculous what it does give.

**Free:** The surest way to make a thing's true gift look ridiculous is to demand from it what it was never meant to give.

`tags: destination, gift, ridicule`

## §543
> Todo inconforme sabe, en el fondo del alma, que el sitio
> que su vanidad rechaza es el sitio mismo que su naturaleza le
> fijó.

**Close:** Every dissatisfied man knows, in the depth of his soul, that the place his vanity rejects is the very place his nature assigned him.

**Free:** Every discontented person knows, deep down, that the place his vanity rejects is the very place his nature fixed for him.

`tags: vanity, place, nature`

## §544
> Hay menos ambiciosos en el mundo que individuos que
> hoy se creen obligados moralmente a serlo.

**Close:** There are fewer ambitious men in the world than individuals who today believe themselves morally obliged to be so.

**Free:** There are fewer truly ambitious people in the world than people who now think ambition is a moral duty.

`tags: ambition, duty, morality`

---

## §545
> Cuando las jerarquías desaparecen, no es tanto el caos,
> como la insulsez, lo que predomina.

**Close:** When hierarchies disappear, it is not so much chaos as insipidity that predominates.

**Free:** When hierarchies vanish, what predominates is not chaos so much as insipidity.

**Exegesis:**
The claim is sharper than the familiar conservative fear of disorder. What rank protects against is not only chaos, but flatness: a world where nothing rises enough above anything else to acquire savor.

`tags: hierarchy, insipidity, order`
