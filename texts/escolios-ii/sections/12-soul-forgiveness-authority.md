# Section 12: Soul, Forgiveness, Authority (§241-270, pp. 48-54)

This run keeps returning to the things modern innocence cannot bear: sin, hierarchy, forgiveness, and authority that is recognized rather than manufactured.

---

## §241
> La mayoría de los hombres mueren sin que les haya
> nacido alma.

**Close:** The majority of men die without a soul having been born to them.

**Free:** Most people die before a soul has ever come to birth in them.

`tags: soul, birth, death`

---

## §242
> La opinión de la posteridad sobre nuestras lenguas es
> imprevisible, pero las ideas modernas parecerán pensadas
> en latín corrompido. La sintaxis intelectual del moderno
> recuerda la sintaxis gramatical de Fredegario.

**Close:** Posterity's opinion of our languages is unpredictable, but modern ideas will seem to have been thought in corrupted Latin. The modern's intellectual syntax recalls the grammatical syntax of Fredegar.

**Free:** Posterity may judge our languages however it will, but modern ideas are likely to sound as though they had been thought in debased Latin. The modern intellect writes like Fredegar.

`tags: language, posterity, modernity`

---

## §243
> Las verdades no son herramientas para la explotación del
> planeta, sino estandartes bajo los cuales creemos que vale
> la pena morir. El historiador se suicida, cuando transforma
> la interpretación correcta de un acontecimiento en clave de
> la historia.

**Close:** Truths are not tools for the exploitation of the planet, but standards under which we believe it worth while to die. The historian commits suicide when he transforms the correct interpretation of an event into a key to history.

**Free:** Truths are not tools for managing the world but banners worth dying under. Historians destroy themselves when they turn one sound reading of an event into the master key to all history.

**Exegesis:**
The second sentence sharpens the first. Truth is not validated by explanatory reach. The moment one interpretation becomes a universal key, the historian trades fidelity for domination.

`tags: truth, history, interpretation`

## §247
> La Iglesia pudo bautizar a la sociedad medieval porque
> era sociedad de pecadores, pero su porvenir no es
> halagüeño en la sociedad moderna donde todos se creen
> inocentes.

**Close:** The Church was able to baptize medieval society because it was a society of sinners, but its future is not promising in modern society where everyone believes himself innocent.

**Free:** The Church could baptize medieval society because it knew itself sinful. Its prospects are worse in a society where everyone imagines himself innocent.

**Exegesis:**
`§246-248` belong together. Christianity can shape a civilization only where guilt is still intelligible. The real obstacle is not sin but innocence as ideology.

`tags: Church, innocence, modernity`

## §251
> El método dialéctico parece inventado para eludir la
> obligación de colocar cada cosa en su sitio impuesta por el
> método jerárquico.

**Close:** The dialectical method seems invented to evade the obligation of placing each thing in its site imposed by the hierarchical method.

**Free:** The dialectical method can look like an invention for evading the hierarchical demand to put each thing in its proper place.

**Exegesis:**

## §253
> El hombre no puede ni condenarse a sí mismo, ni
> absolverse. El hombre no es más que una capacidad de ser
> perdonado.

**Close:** Man can neither condemn himself nor absolve himself. Man is no more than a capacity to be forgiven.

**Free:** Man cannot sentence or acquit himself. He is, at bottom, a capacity for forgiveness received.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the volume's starkest anti-autonomous statements. The self is not sufficient even for its own final judgment. It remains open on a side it cannot close.

`tags: forgiveness, judgment, grace`

## §256
> Aun cuando la necesidad rigiera el curso de la historia,
> aun cuando supiéramos su derrotero en cada instante, las
> normas intemporales nos conminarían muchas veces a
> caminar a redopelo de los hechos y a elegir deliberadamente
> el desastre.

**Close:** Even if necessity governed the course of history, even if we knew its course at each instant, timeless norms would many times enjoin us to walk against the grain of the facts and to choose disaster deliberately.

**Free:** Even if history were ruled by necessity and we knew its path at every moment, timeless norms would still often command us to go against the facts and deliberately choose disaster.

**Exegesis:**
This is the clearest refusal in the cluster of fact as moral verdict. Even perfect knowledge of historical process would not abolish obligation.

`tags: norms, history, disaster`

## §257
> Imaginación, fantasía, humorismo, son rumbos
> divergentes de la metáfora. Imaginación, cuando la
> identificación metafórica asciende hacia un estrato superior
> del ser. Fantasía, cuando los términos de la metáfora
> pertenecen al mismo estrato. Humorismo, cuando la
> identificación desciende hacia estratos inferiores.

**Close:** Imagination, fantasy, humor, are divergent directions of metaphor. Imagination, when the metaphorical identification ascends toward a higher stratum of being. Fantasy, when the terms of the metaphor belong to the same stratum. Humor, when the identification descends toward lower strata.

**Free:** Imagination, fantasy, and humor are three directions metaphor can take: upward into a higher level of being, sideways within the same level, or downward into lower ones.

`tags: metaphor, imagination, humor`

## §258
> Habiendo previamente resuelto que las formas religiosas
> no son más que etapas de un progreso, la filosofía de la
> religión, desde Lessing, limita la religión auténtica al
> respeto que se tenga por la dirección atribuida a ese
> supuesto progreso. A esta solución desabrida se opone el
> catolicismo, que integra tanto el rito mágico como la
> contemplación mística, tanto el comportamiento ético
> como el raciocinio teológico. El catolicismo es la
> estructuración jerárquica de la historia de las religiones.

**Close:** Having previously resolved that religious forms are no more than stages of a progress, the philosophy of religion, since Lessing, limits authentic religion to the respect one has for the direction attributed to that supposed progress. To this insipid solution Catholicism opposes itself, integrating magical rite as much as mystical contemplation, ethical behavior as much as theological reasoning. Catholicism is the hierarchical structuring of the history of religions.

**Free:** Once philosophy of religion assumes that religions are merely stages of progress, "authentic religion" gets reduced to reverence for that supposed direction of progress. Catholicism opposes that flatness by holding rite, mysticism, ethics, and theology together in a hierarchy; it is a way of ordering the history of religions instead of leveling it.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the volume's strongest hierarchical formulations. Catholicism is not here one religion among others plus a moral addendum. It is presented as an ordering power that refuses reduction to a single register.

`tags: Catholicism, hierarchy, religion`

## §262
> También las virtudes son intraducibles. Unas no hablan
> sino latín. Otras sólo saben inglés. Hay exquisitas en
> francés que se depravan en otros idiomas. Algunas
> necesitan el alemán para expresar su escrupulosa seriedad.
> Varias sólo en español no parecen meros ademanes
> plebeyos. Del griego, en fin, nadie ha podido traducir una
> sola virtud.

**Close:** Virtues too are untranslatable. Some speak only Latin. Others know only English. There are exquisite ones in French that deprave themselves in other languages. Some need German to express their scrupulous seriousness. Several only in Spanish do not seem mere plebeian gestures. From Greek, in the end, no one has been able to translate a single virtue.

**Free:** Virtues too resist translation. Some live only in Latin, others only in English; some are exquisite in French and turn coarse elsewhere; some need German for their scrupulous gravity; several only in Spanish avoid sounding merely plebeian. As for Greek, no one has ever managed to translate even one virtue out of it.

`tags: virtue, translation, language`

## §268
> Mi cristianismo es menos ético que ontológico: me vivo
> menos como pecador que como creatura.

**Close:** My Christianity is less ethical than ontological: I live myself less as sinner than as creature.

**Free:** My Christianity is less ethical than ontological: I experience myself less as sinner than as creature.

## §269
> Autoridad es la característica propia a lo que nos
> subyuga, como la poesía de Homero o el genio de Platón.
> Autoridad no es lo que logra mandar, sino lo que no es
> concebible que se le desobedezca sin demencia.

**Close:** Authority is the characteristic proper to that which subjugates us, like the poetry of Homer or the genius of Plato. Authority is not what manages to command, but what it is not conceivable to disobey without madness.

**Free:** Authority belongs to what subdues us by its excellence, like Homeric poetry or Plato's genius. Authority is not whatever succeeds in commanding, but whatever cannot be disobeyed without derangement.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the best definitions in the volume. Authority is not primarily coercive or administrative. It is that form of superiority whose claim is recognized before it is enforced.

`tags: authority, excellence, obedience`

## §270
> Nada auténtico se puede autenticar con argumentos.
> Sólo podemos razonar nuestra adhesión a personas, o
> nuestra admiración por obras de arte, cuando la persona es
> medio de fines egoístas y la obra pretexto ideológico.

**Close:** Nothing authentic can be authenticated with arguments. We can only reason our adhesion to persons, or our admiration for works of art, when the person is a means to selfish ends and the work an ideological pretext.

**Free:** Nothing authentic can be proven authentic by argument. Once our attachment to a person or admiration for a work becomes fully arguable, the person has become an instrument or the work an ideological excuse.

`tags: authenticity, argument, art`
