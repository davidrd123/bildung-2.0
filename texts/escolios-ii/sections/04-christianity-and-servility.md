# Section 04: Christianity and Servility (§81–100, pp. 20–23)

This batch turns from historiography to civilizational allegiance. Christianity, aristocracy, literature, colonialism, and democracy are all judged by whether they preserve vertical form or dissolve into adaptation, service, and middle-class flattening.

## §81
> El determinista se impacienta con sus adversarios, como
> si tuvieran razón de decirse libres.
> Los deterministas son gente muy irritable.

**Close:** The determinist grows impatient with his adversaries, as if they had reason to call themselves free. Determinists are very irritable people.

**Free:** Determinists are touchy, as though their anger itself betrayed a suspicion that freedom might be real.

`tags: determinism, freedom, irritability`

## §82
> A fuerza de adaptarse a la "mentalidad moderna", el
> cristianismo se volvió una doctrina que no es difícil acatar,
> ni es interesante hacerlo.

**Close:** By dint of adapting itself to the "modern mentality," Christianity has turned into a doctrine that is not difficult to obey, nor is it interesting to do so.

**Free:** After so much adaptation to the modern mentality, Christianity has become both easy to follow and not worth following.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the central religious judgments of the early volume. Adaptation does not merely weaken Christianity; it renders it boring. The faith loses not just rigor but attraction when it trims itself to what modernity already wants.

`tags: Christianity, modernity, adaptation`

## §83
> Quien intenta evadirse de su raza pretende saltar fuera de
> su sombra.
> Pero ya que la auténtica índole de una raza es siempre
> misterio, su espíritu no sirve de pauta consciente a nuestros
> actos.
> El individuo es fiel a su sangre, cuando es leal a su más
> íntima vocación.

**Close:** He who tries to evade his race seeks to leap outside his shadow. But since the authentic nature of a race is always mystery, its spirit does not serve as a conscious guideline to our acts. The individual is faithful to his blood when he is loyal to his most intimate vocation.

**Free:** One cannot simply step outside inherited belonging; but because that belonging is mysterious, not programmatic, fidelity to it appears as fidelity to one's deepest vocation rather than as racial self-theory.

**Notes:**
- However uncomfortable the vocabulary is, the second and third sentences matter: Gómez Dávila blocks any easy reduction of *raza* to explicit biological program by calling its reality mysterious and indirect.

`tags: race, vocation, inheritance`

## §86
> El cristianismo de una sociedad es directamente
> proporcional al número de abadías que funda.

**Close:** The Christianity of a society is directly proportional to the number of abbeys it founds.

**Free:** A society's Christianity can be measured by whether it founds abbeys.

**Exegesis:**
This is not statistical wit. Abbeys matter because they are durable, liturgical, communal forms. Christianity becomes visible socially not through opinion but through the institutions capable of embodying prayer, hierarchy, leisure, and continuity.

`tags: Christianity, abbeys, society`

## §88
> El cristianismo no es doctrina para clase media.
> Ni para clase media económica. Ni para clase media
> intelectual.
> Carece, pues, de porvenir.

**Close:** Christianity is not a doctrine for the middle class. Nor for the economic middle class. Nor for the intellectual middle class. It therefore lacks a future.

**Free:** Christianity is not a religion for the middle. Not socially, not economically, not intellectually. Which is why, under modern conditions, it has no future.

**Exegesis:**
The logic is brutal and exact. If modern society is increasingly middle-class in every sense, then Christianity's lack of fit with that social psychology becomes a forecast. The sentence belongs with §82: adaptation may buy survival, but only by ceasing to be interestingly Christian.

`tags: Christianity, middle-class, future`

## §89
> Recelosos de la implantación de estructuras feudales en
> América, los Reyes Católicos, tanto como Habsburgos y
> Borbones, hicieron abortar todo embrión de feudalismo,
> logrando así que sólo tres factores tejieran la historia del este
> continente: la pusilanimidad del burócrata, la codicia del
> tendero, y la anarquía del mestizo.

**Close:** Distrustful of the implantation of feudal structures in America, the Catholic Monarchs, as much as Habsburgs and Bourbons, caused every embryo of feudalism to abort, thereby achieving that only three factors should weave the history of this continent: the pusillanimity of the bureaucrat, the greed of the shopkeeper, and the anarchy of the mestizo.

**Free:** Because the Spanish crown blocked feudal structures in America, the continent was left, in his harsh summary, to be shaped by three forces: timid bureaucracy, shopkeeping greed, and mestizo anarchy.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the batch's most compressed political diagnoses. Whether or not the genealogy persuades in full, the form of the judgment matters: the absence of a mediating feudal order leaves only administration, commerce, and disordered mass life. Gómez Dávila consistently reads political failure as a failure of vertical form.

`tags: America, feudalism, bureaucracy`

## §90
> A Homero, poeta de la aristocracia jónica, y a Dante,
> poeta del ordo medieval, hay que agregar a Shakespeare,
> "poeta del feudalismo" (según Morley).
> La reacción no anda mal de poetas.

**Close:** To Homer, poet of Ionian aristocracy, and to Dante, poet of the medieval ordo, one must add Shakespeare, "poet of feudalism" (according to Morley). Reaction is not poorly off for poets.

**Free:** Alongside Homer and Dante, Shakespeare too belongs to the poets of ordered hierarchy; reaction, he says, is not short of great poets.

`tags: poetry, reaction, feudalism`

## §92
> La literatura resucitará cuando renuncien a "cambiar el
> mundo".

**Close:** Literature will rise again when they renounce "changing the world."

**Free:** Literature will come back to life when writers give up trying to change the world.

**Exegesis:**
This is not quietism. It is a defense of literary integrity against moral-political conscription. Literature dies when it becomes instrument first and art second. The sentence pairs naturally with §94.

`tags: literature, activism, resurrection`

## §94
> Hoy los textos más herméticos, más experimentales,
> más gratuitos, así como las actitudes religiosas más místicas
> o más ascéticas, entrañan la ambición de "colaborar" y
> "servir".
> La gloria literaria, o celeste, es premio hoy reservado al
> servilismo social.

**Close:** Today the most hermetic, most experimental, most gratuitous texts, as well as the most mystical or most ascetic religious attitudes, entail the ambition to "collaborate" and "serve." Literary glory, or heavenly glory, is today a prize reserved for social servilism.

**Free:** Even the most obscure art and the most ascetic religion now smuggle in the ambition to collaborate and serve. Literary and heavenly glory alike are awarded to social servility.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the section's strongest claims. Even apparent refusals of utility are contaminated by the need to be socially useful. The problem is not service in a Christian sense, but the modern compulsion to justify every act by public function.

`tags: literature, mysticism, servility`

## §96
> Aquellos cuya gratitud por el beneficio recibido se
> convierte en devoción a la persona que lo otorga, en lugar de
> degenerar en el odio acostumbrado que todo benefactor
> despierta, son aristócratas.
> Aun cuando caminen en harapos.

**Close:** Those whose gratitude for the benefit received turns into devotion to the person who grants it, instead of degenerating into the customary hatred every benefactor awakens, are aristocrats. Even if they walk in rags.

**Free:** Aristocrats are the rare people whose gratitude deepens into devotion to the benefactor instead of curdling into resentment, even if they wear rags.

**Exegesis:**
Aristocracy here is not class location but a form of soul. The decisive marker is the ability to receive a gift without converting dependence into hatred. That is one of Gómez Dávila's deepest recurrent tests.

`tags: aristocracy, gratitude, benefactor`

## §99
> El fervor del culto que el demócrata rinde a la humanidad
> sólo es comparable a la frialdad con que irrespeta al
> individuo.
> El reaccionario desdeña al hombre, sin encontrar
> individuo que desprecie.

**Close:** The fervor of the cult the democrat renders to humanity is comparable only to the coldness with which he disrespects the individual. The reactionary disdains man, without finding an individual he despises.

**Free:** The democrat worships humanity while treating actual individuals coldly. The reactionary distrusts Man in the abstract without needing to despise concrete persons.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of Gómez Dávila's cleanest reversals. Abstract humanitarianism and concrete contempt go together; reactionary contempt, by contrast, is aimed at the universalized idol of "man." Whatever one makes of the argument, the structure is central to his anthropology: abstraction deforms charity.

`tags: democracy, humanity, individual`
