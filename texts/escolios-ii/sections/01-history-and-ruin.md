# Section 01: History and Ruin (§1–27, pp. 5–10)

Volume II opens in a colder register than volume I. Gómez Dávila does not begin by telling us how to read him; he assumes the form is already understood and turns immediately to history, judgment, decay, and the thinning Christian residue of the modern world.

Spanish comes from the volume II PDF extraction in [source/aphorisms.yaml](/home/davidrd/Projects/Learning/SelfStructure/bildung-2.0/texts/escolios-ii/source/aphorisms.yaml). The Italian witness in [source/aphorisms.it.yaml](/home/davidrd/Projects/Learning/SelfStructure/bildung-2.0/texts/escolios-ii/source/aphorisms.it.yaml) was used only to confirm a few obvious line-break recoveries.

The method here follows the later `Escolios I` discipline: every entry gets close and free renderings; exegesis appears only where it adds real pressure.

## §1
> Nuestra atención a las letras contemporáneas decrece con los años, no porque
> los sentidos se emboten, sino porque hemos visto varias literaturas
> contemporáneas sucederse las unas a las otras, dejando meramente un puñado
> de aciertos problemáticos entre un acervo colosal de basura.

**Close:** Our attention to contemporary letters decreases with the years, not because the senses grow dull, but because we have seen several contemporary literatures succeed one another, leaving only a handful of problematic successes amid a colossal store of trash.

**Free:** We care less and less about contemporary literature not because age dulls us, but because we have watched too many "contemporary literatures" come and go, each leaving behind a few dubious achievements in a mountain of junk.

**Exegesis:**
The opening is already retrospective and civilizational. "Contemporary literature" is not denied because the past is sacred, but because enough "contemporaries" have passed by to expose the category as a marketing haze. The first sentence of volume II begins with historical attrition.

`tags: literature, contemporary, judgment`

## §2
> La objetividad del historiador no consiste en abstenerse
> de dictar juicios de valor, sino en sentenciar con acierto.

**Close:** The objectivity of the historian does not consist in abstaining from value judgments, but in judging rightly.

**Free:** A historian is objective not when he avoids value judgments, but when he gets them right.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the governing sentences of the whole opening cluster. Gómez Dávila rejects the neutral historian before the section has properly begun. History is not made objective by bracketing value; it becomes objective only through disciplined value judgment. The alternative is not objectivity but timidity dressed as method.

`tags: history, objectivity, judgment`

## §3
> La obra de arte no es artefacto preparado para ejercitar
> nuestra facultad de análisis, sino aparato para incitarnos a
> disparar juicios de valor.

**Close:** The work of art is not an artifact prepared to exercise our faculty of analysis, but an apparatus for inciting us to fire off value judgments.

**Free:** A work of art is not built to give analysis a workout. It is a device for provoking judgment.

**Notes:**
- The PDF has `in- citarnos`; normalized here to `incitarnos`.
- *disparar juicios de valor* is more aggressive than "make value judgments." The art object provokes a shot, not a seminar.

`tags: art, judgment, criticism`

## §4
> Admitir que cada época escriba una distinta historia del
> pasado no es confesar que todas las interpretaciones se
> valgan.
> Ciertas épocas tienen vocación para la historia, mientras
> que otras carecen de talento historiográfico.
> Toda visión de la historia es peculiar a una época, pero el
> genio historiográfico de algunas transmite a su visión un
> valor historiográfico intemporal, análogo a la
> intemporalidad del valor estético.
> La torpeza historiográfica de otras épocas, en cambio,
> aventura interpretaciones que documentan meramente sobre
> la época que las fabrica.

**Close:** To admit that each epoch writes a different history of the past is not to confess that all interpretations are equally valid. Certain epochs have a vocation for history, while others lack historiographical talent. Every vision of history is peculiar to an epoch, but the historiographical genius of some transmits to its vision an intemporal historiographical value, analogous to the intemporality of aesthetic value. The historiographical clumsiness of other epochs, by contrast, ventures interpretations that merely document the epoch that fabricates them.

**Free:** Admitting that every age rewrites the past does not mean every reading is equally good. Some ages are born for history; others are historiographically tone-deaf. Every historical vision belongs to its time, but the best epochs achieve a lasting truth in history, much as great art does. The worst merely tell us about themselves.

**Exegesis:**
This is a much subtler historicism than the progressive version he despises. Gómez Dávila concedes that every historiography is epochal, but he refuses the slide from situatedness to equivalence. Some periods see better. Their historical visions are dated in origin without being merely dated in value. The analogy to aesthetic judgment is decisive: history, like art, can be temporally situated and still normatively ranked.

`tags: history, historiography, value`

## §5
> Si un vacío físico no lo convence de la desaparición de
> una cosa,, el hombre se obstina en verla presente mientras
> persista su espectro.
> En el mundo contemporáneo las cosas yerguen aún su
> apariencia intacta, pero el siglo succiona implacablemente
> su savia. La espesa pulpa de las cosas, de momento en
> momento, se corrompe, se pudre, se disipa.
> El hombre se instala ciegamente en la terrestre solidez de
> su destino, cuando la substancia del mundo fluye hoy por
> una secreta herida hacia la nada.

**Close:** If a physical void does not convince him of a thing's disappearance, man stubbornly sees it as present while its specter persists. In the contemporary world things still rear up in their intact appearance, but the century implacably sucks out their sap. The thick pulp of things, from moment to moment, corrupts, rots, dissipates. Man installs himself blindly in the earthly solidity of his destiny when the substance of the world today flows through a secret wound toward nothingness.

**Free:** Unless something leaves a literal hole behind it, man goes on acting as if it were still there. Modern things still stand upright in outward appearance, but the century drains their sap. Their thick substance is already rotting away. Meanwhile man settles himself into the world's seeming solidity while its substance leaks through a hidden wound into nothing.

**Notes:**
- The double comma after *cosa* is retained in the source file as witness noise; ignored here.
- *savia* / *pulpa* make the world vegetal and fleshy at once. The modern loss is not merely conceptual but vital.

**Exegesis:**
This is the first great ruin-image of the volume. Institutions, forms, words, maybe even Christianity itself, still stand in place, but as husks. The century's damage is metabolic before it is visible. The hidden wound matters because it explains why mere physical persistence proves nothing. A form may survive after its life has already drained away.

`tags: modernity, decay, ontology`

## §8
> Nominalismo y realismo no son corrientes filosóficas
> paralelas o sucesivas, sino períodos históricos.
> En ciertas épocas la realidad se pulveriza en individuos.
> En otros tiempos los universales son los protagonistas de la
> historia.

**Close:** Nominalism and realism are not parallel or successive philosophical currents, but historical periods. In certain epochs reality pulverizes into individuals. In other times universals are the protagonists of history.

**Free:** Nominalism and realism are not just rival doctrines. They are historical climates. In some ages reality breaks down into individuals; in others universals take center stage.

**Exegesis:**
This is more than a claim about medieval philosophy. It is a claim about the texture of whole epochs. What looks like a conceptual dispute between schools is, at a deeper level, a shift in what reality itself foregrounds. Volume II keeps returning to this historical thickening and thinning of reality.

`tags: nominalism, realism, history`

## §9
> El alma no florece sino en el período biológico que
> corresponde a la edad con que nace.
> Quienes nacen para ser jóvenes se vuelven grotescos al
> envejecer. Quienes nacen para ser viejos son grotescos
> durante su agria juventud.

**Close:** The soul flowers only in the biological period corresponding to the age with which it is born. Those born to be young become grotesque in old age. Those born to be old are grotesque during their acrid youth.

**Free:** The soul flowers only in the biological season proper to the age it was born for. Some are born to be young and become grotesque when they age; others are born old and look grotesque in their bitter youth.

`tags: soul, age, destiny`

## §10
> El que meramente se resigna a su suerte se siente
> frustrado por un destino sin sentido.
> El que humildemente la acepta sabe que tan sólo no
> entiende el significado de la divina decisión que lo
> concierne.

**Close:** He who merely resigns himself to his lot feels frustrated by a destiny without meaning. He who humbly accepts it knows only that he does not understand the meaning of the divine decision that concerns him.

**Free:** Mere resignation feels trapped in a meaningless fate. Humble acceptance knows only that it does not yet understand the divine decision that has fallen to it.

**Notes:**
- The PDF breaks *humildemente* across a line; normalized here.

**Exegesis:**
The opposition is not between rebellion and passivity but between two kinds of submission. Resignation yields to meaninglessness; acceptance yields to hidden meaning. The difference is theological, not psychological. One bows to absurdity, the other to obscurity.

`tags: destiny, resignation, acceptance`

## §15
> Resulta más fácil, en nuestro tiempo, encontrar
> comportamientos cristianos que almas cristianas.
> Habiendo expulsado su cristianismo hacia la zona
> periférica de la persona, el cristiano actual aún le somete
> parcialmente su conducta, pero no le subordina sus
> actitudes radicales.
> Ya escasean esos seres indeleblemente cristianos, en
> cuyos ojos enturbiados por el orgullo, la lujuria o la
> blasfemia, nuestros ojos adivinan una mirada secretamente
> fraternal.

**Close:** It is easier, in our time, to find Christian behaviors than Christian souls. Having expelled his Christianity toward the peripheral zone of the person, the contemporary Christian still partially subjects his conduct to it, but does not subordinate his radical attitudes to it. Those indelibly Christian beings are now scarce, in whose eyes clouded by pride, lust, or blasphemy our eyes divine a secretly fraternal gaze.

**Free:** These days it is easier to find Christian conduct than Christian souls. The modern Christian has pushed Christianity out to the edge of the self: it still governs some behavior, but no longer his deepest attitudes. Those indelibly Christian beings are rare now, the ones whose eyes, however clouded by pride, lust, or blasphemy, still betray a secretly fraternal gaze.

**Notes:**
- The PDF line break around *seres indeleblemente cristianos* has been normalized.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the most important Christian diagnostics in the opening run. What has survived is behavior, not soul-form. Christianity continues as moral residue and social manner long after it has ceased to govern the radical center of the person. Gómez Dávila is not lamenting hypocrisy here; he is describing a historical thinning in which the form remains visible while the inner law has retreated.

`tags: Christianity, soul, conduct`

## §17
> En las Geisteswissenschaften las matemáticas "servent à
> cacher les défauts de l'esprit".

**Close:** In the Geisteswissenschaften, mathematics "serve to hide the defects of the mind."

**Free:** In the human sciences, mathematics often serve only to hide defects of mind.

**Notes:**
- The French phrase comes from La Rochefoucauld: *La gravité est un mystère du corps inventé pour cacher les défauts de l'esprit.*

**Exegesis:**
The target is not mathematics but camouflage. Quantification in the human sciences becomes suspect when it is used as borrowed prestige rather than as necessity. The sentence is short because the accusation is not technical but moral: exactitude can be adopted as costume.

`tags: human-sciences, mathematics, camouflage`

## §19
> Quien aprende a decir "Si, pero..." o "No, pero... " se
> franquea las puertas de la historia.

**Close:** He who learns to say "Yes, but..." or "No, but..." passes through the gates of history.

**Free:** The man who learns to say "Yes, but..." and "No, but..." wins passage through history's gates.

**Exegesis:**
Qualification is not indecision here; it is historical adulthood. The person who can place an affirmation under condition and a negation under reservation has entered a world in which forces are mixed, eras are thick, and judgments must survive contact with complication. It is the historical version of §2 from volume I: thought advances between opposing injustices.

`tags: history, qualification, judgment`

## §20
> El progresista olvida que el pecado frustra todo ideal
> que añore, el conservador olvida que corrompe toda realidad
> que defienda.

**Close:** The progressive forgets that sin frustrates every ideal he longs for; the conservative forgets that it corrupts every reality he defends.

**Free:** The progressive forgets that sin wrecks every ideal he desires; the conservative forgets that it corrupts every reality he wants to preserve.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the fairest political-religious sentences in the volume. Gómez Dávila is not distributing blame equally for rhetorical effect; he is naming the distinct theological blindness proper to each side. The progressive overestimates ideals; the conservative overestimates inheritances. Sin cuts through both dream and institution.

`tags: progressivism, conservatism, sin`

## §21
> La deducción filosófica es el arte de transformar, una
> observación exacta, pero limitada, en un sistema
> comprehensivo, pero falso.

**Close:** Philosophical deduction is the art of transforming an exact but limited observation into a comprehensive but false system.

**Free:** Philosophical deduction is the trick of turning one accurate but limited observation into a total system that is false.

**Exegesis:**
This is a precise statement of Gómez Dávila's anti-systematic method. Systems do not usually begin in stupidity; they begin in truth. The corruption comes in the extension, not the first insight. A sound local observation is forced to bear universal weight and collapses under the burden.

`tags: deduction, system, observation`

## §22
> Visitar un museo o leer a un clásico son, para las

> éticos.

**Close:** Visiting a museum or reading a classic are, for contemporary multitudes, simple ethical behaviors.

**Free:** For contemporary masses, going to a museum or reading a classic has become little more than moral good behavior.

**Exegesis:**
Culture here has been moralized into decorum. Museums and classics no longer work as adventures, shocks, or teachers; they function as badges of proper conduct. The old book has already begun to become a document.

## §24
> La crítica "estéril" logra a veces esas conversiones del
> alma que modifican substancialmente los problemas.
> La crítica "constructiva" sólo multiplica catástrofes.

**Close:** "Sterile" criticism sometimes achieves those conversions of the soul that substantially modify problems. "Constructive" criticism only multiplies catastrophes.

**Free:** So-called sterile criticism sometimes produces the conversions of soul that actually change the problem. "Constructive" criticism only multiplies disasters.

**Exegesis:**
The attack on solutionism becomes explicit. Criticism that merely refuses, judges, negates, or wounds may be called sterile by activists, but it can alter the person who is carrying the problem and therefore alter the problem itself. Constructive criticism, by contrast, often rushes to apparatus, policy, or program and therefore leaves the soul untouched while multiplying consequences.

`tags: criticism, conversion, catastrophe`

## §25
> La crucifixión, según el cristiano de hoy, fue un
> lamentable error judicial.
> La facultad de percibir la misteriosa necesidad de lo atroz
> pereció con la escena griega y los altares cristianos.

**Close:** The crucifixion, according to the Christian of today, was a lamentable judicial error. The faculty for perceiving the mysterious necessity of the atrocious perished with the Greek stage and the Christian altars.

**Free:** For today's Christian, the crucifixion was just a regrettable miscarriage of justice. The power to perceive the mysterious necessity of atrocity died with Greek tragedy and Christian altars.

**Exegesis:**
He is not glorifying atrocity. He is accusing modern Christianity of moral domestication so complete that it can no longer understand its own central image. Once the cross becomes merely a legal scandal, Christianity has been translated into humanitarian sentiment and stripped of tragedy, sacrifice, and dread.

`tags: crucifixion, atrocity, tragedy`

## §26
> Si las supersticiones desaparecieran, la teología liquidaría
> rápidamente la religión.

**Close:** If superstitions disappeared, theology would rapidly liquidate religion.

**Free:** If superstition ever vanished, theology would finish religion off in short order.

**Exegesis:**
The barb lands because it treats superstition as religion's vulgar but protective crust. Theology imagines itself as purifier, but Gómez Dávila suspects that once the embarrassing popular forms are stripped away, theology's analytic zeal would dissolve what it claims to preserve. The sentence belongs with §17: prestige-methods often hide defects of mind.

`tags: superstition, theology, religion`

## §27
> Para aligerar la nave cristiana, que zozobra en aguas
> modernas, la teología liberal se desembarazó ayer de la
> divinidad de Cristo, la teología radical se desembaraza hoy
> de la existencia de Dios.
> El intelectual de izquierda no ataca con intrepidez y
> arrogancia sino las ideas que cree muertas.

**Close:** To lighten the Christian ship, foundering in modern waters, liberal theology yesterday rid itself of Christ's divinity, and radical theology today rids itself of God's existence. The left-wing intellectual attacks with intrepidity and arrogance only the ideas he believes dead.

**Free:** To lighten the Christian ship as it founders in modern waters, liberal theology threw overboard Christ's divinity yesterday, and radical theology throws overboard God's existence today. The left intellectual is brave and arrogant only against ideas he takes to be already dead.

**Notes:**
- The current Spanish and Italian witnesses both preserve these two movements together. They may be one aphorism or two adjacent aphorisms fused by transmission. Kept together for now.

**Exegesis:**
Even if the witness has fused two neighboring units, the pressure is the same. Modern theological adaptation works by sacrificial unloading: first Christ's divinity, then God himself. The left intellectual's courage is parasitic on that prior weakening. It is easy to attack what one has already decided no longer lives.

`tags: theology, modernity, left-intellectual`
