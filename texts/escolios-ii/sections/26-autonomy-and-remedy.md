# Section 26: Autonomy and Remedy (§598-620, pp. 109-113)

This stretch keeps contrasting modern autonomy and its false remedies with older forms of discipline, style, forgiveness, and adult meditation.

---

## §598
> El escritor que no ha torturado sus frases tortura al
> lector.

**Close:** The writer who has not tortured his sentences tortures the reader.

**Free:** Any writer who has not tormented his own sentences ends by tormenting the reader.

`tags: writer, sentence, reader`

## §599
> La civilización tiene por requisito ineludible que los
> mediocres imitemos sin vergüenza alguna. Que sólo unos
> pocos osen innovar.

**Close:** Civilization has as ineluctable requirement that we mediocrities imitate without any shame at all. That only a few dare to innovate.

**Free:** Civilization requires that we mediocrities imitate without shame, and that only a few dare to innovate.

`tags: civilization, imitation, innovation`

## §600
> El aroma de los huertos monacales y el rumor de los
> bosques que rodean el monasterio solitario circulan en el latín
> adusto del alto medievo. Como las piedras románicas, arte de
> campesinos nobles en sus fortalezas monásticas.

**Close:** The aroma of monastic orchards and the murmur of the forests surrounding the solitary monastery circulate in the austere Latin of the high Middle Ages. Like the Romanesque stones, art of noble peasants in their monastic fortresses.

**Free:** The austere Latin of the high Middle Ages carries within it the scent of monastic orchards and the murmur of forests around the solitary monastery, like Romanesque stones: the art of noble peasants in their monastic strongholds.

**Exegesis:**
This is not picturesque medievalism. Language, architecture, and way of life are treated as sharing one atmosphere, one civilizational density that can still be felt in the style itself.

`tags: Latin, monasticism, Romanesque`

## §601
> La pululación de escritores mediocres en ciertas épocas
> intriga al que olvida que una pululación semejante existe en
> todas, que meramente no la vemos donde la aparición
> inexplicable de un gran escritor la oculta.

**Close:** The swarming of mediocre writers in certain epochs intrigues the one who forgets that a similar swarming exists in all, that we simply do not see it where the inexplicable appearance of a great writer hides it.

**Free:** The swarm of mediocre writers in some periods troubles only those who forget that every period has one; we just fail to notice it wherever the inexplicable appearance of a great writer hides it.

`tags: writers, mediocrity, epoch`

**Free:** Man shuts his eyes to the real problems just as the commentator shuts his eyes to the text's real difficulties.

`tags: commentary, problems, text`

## §606
> No es a abrazar toda verdad a lo que el hombre debe
> alistarse, sino a morir con sus dioses. Adaptarse envilece.

**Close:** It is not to embrace every truth that man ought to enlist himself, but to die with his gods. To adapt oneself degrades.

**Free:** Man should not prepare himself to embrace every new truth, but to die with his gods. Adaptation degrades.

**Exegesis:**
The line is not anti-truth in the shallow sense. It is anti-opportunism: against a pliability that abandons the gods simply because the age has changed its demands.

`tags: truth, gods, adaptation`

**Free:** When dialogue has become the last resort, the situation is already past remedy.

`tags: dialogue, remedy, last-resort`

## §608
> El cristianismo no inventó la noción de pecado, sino la
> de perdón.

**Close:** Christianity did not invent the notion of sin, but that of forgiveness.

**Free:** Christianity did not invent sin; it invented forgiveness.

**Exegesis:**
The aphorism is concise enough to overread easily. Its force lies in where it places Christianity's novelty: not in diagnosis of fault, but in the possibility of pardon.

`tags: Christianity, sin, forgiveness`

**Close:** The texts of our contemporaries entangle and demoralize the philologist because he supposes them written with the same meticulous care with which he reads them.

**Free:** Contemporary texts bewilder and demoralize the philologist because he assumes they were written with the same meticulous care he brings to reading them.

`tags: philology, contemporaries, care`

## §611
> Basta que un sistema parezca descifrar el enigma de la
> existencia, para saber que sus soluciones son falsas.

**Close:** It suffices that a system seem to decipher the enigma of existence for us to know that its solutions are false.

**Free:** The moment a system seems to decode the enigma of existence, we can already know that its solutions are false.

**Exegesis:**
The point is not anti-intellectualism. It is that existence is falsified the moment the enigma appears solved by system alone.

`tags: system, enigma, false-solution`

## §618
> El moderno ya no se atreve a predicar que el individuo
> nazca como página blanca. Demasiados descalabros le
> enseñaron que somos los herederos agobiados de nuestra
> familia, nuestra raza, nuestra sangre. La sangre no es líquido
> inocente, sino viscosa pasta histórica.

**Close:** The modern no longer dares preach that the individual is born as a blank page. Too many crashes taught him that we are the burdened heirs of our family, our race, our blood. Blood is not innocent liquid, but viscous historical paste.

**Free:** The modern no longer dares preach that the individual is born a blank page. Too many wrecks have taught him that we are burdened heirs of family, race, and blood. Blood is not innocent liquid, but viscous historical paste.

`tags: modernity, inheritance, blood`

## §619
> El demócrata se pasma cuando se entera de la insólita
> coalición que lo amenaza, cuando descubre que el clasicismo
> de Sófocles se alía, para condenarlo, con el romanticismo de
> Kierkegaard. Cuando ve pactar, para esa empresa, la pompa
> episcopal de Bossuet con el ateísmo dionisíaco de Nietzsche.

**Close:** The democrat is astonished when he learns of the unusual coalition that threatens him, when he discovers that the classicism of Sophocles allies itself, to condemn him, with the romanticism of Kierkegaard. When he sees the episcopal pomp of Bossuet make pact, for that enterprise, with the Dionysian atheism of Nietzsche.

**Free:** The democrat is stunned when he discovers the strange coalition arrayed against him: Sophoclean classicism allied with Kierkegaardian romanticism, Bossuet's episcopal pomp making common cause with Nietzsche's Dionysian atheism.

**Exegesis:**
The line matters because it shows the anti-democratic case as structurally convergent rather than school-bound. Gómez Dávila is interested in coalitions of rank and seriousness, not in family resemblance within one camp.

`tags: democracy, coalition, Nietzsche`
