# Section 14: Grace and the Legitimate (§301-330, pp. 59-64)

This stretch keeps asking what can still count as legitimate once progress begins inventing both its morals and its revolutions from scratch.

---

## §301
> La necesidad de la gracia procede menos de nuestra
> incapacidad para cumplir la ley que de la esterilidad de su
> cumplimiento. No es de la impotencia de la voluntad, sino
> del fracaso de sus obras, de donde surge la urgencia de la
> gracia.

**Close:** The need for grace proceeds less from our incapacity to fulfill the law than from the sterility of its fulfillment. It is not from the impotence of the will, but from the failure of its works, that the urgency of grace arises.

**Free:** Grace becomes necessary less because we cannot keep the law than because even keeping it proves barren. Its urgency comes not from weak will but from the failure of our works.

**Exegesis:**
This is a sharp theological correction. Grace is needed not merely as emergency assistance for moral weakness, but because the law fulfilled still does not give life.

`tags: grace, law, works`

## §305
> El reaccionario aboga por la libertad del esclavo, con el
> fin de limitar la libertad del amo. El reaccionario es menos
> amigo de la libertad que enemigo del absolutismo.

**Close:** The reactionary pleads for the freedom of the slave, with the end of limiting the freedom of the master. The reactionary is less a friend of freedom than an enemy of absolutism.

**Free:** The reactionary argues for the slave's freedom in order to limit the master's. He is less a friend of freedom than an enemy of the absolute.

**Exegesis:**
This matters because it refuses the lazy identification of reaction with simple domination. The measure here is not maximal liberty but the refusal of absolutes.

`tags: reactionary, freedom, absolutism`

## §306
> El Progreso se reduce finalmente a robarle al hombre lo
> que lo ennoblece, para poder venderle barato lo que lo
> envilece.

**Close:** Progress is finally reduced to stealing from man what ennobles him in order to be able to sell him cheaply what debases him.

**Free:** Progress ends by taking from man what ennobles him so it can sell back cheaply what degrades him.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the volume's cleanest anti-progress formulas because it turns the whole process into exchange: expropriate nobility, retail degradation.

`tags: progress, nobility, debasement`

## §307
> Para complacer al determinista, digamos que no hay acto
> sin causa. Para molestarlo, que no hay causa sin acto.

**Close:** To please the determinist, let us say that there is no act without cause. To annoy him, that there is no cause without act.

**Free:** To satisfy the determinist, say there is no act without a cause. To irritate him, add that there is no cause without an act.

`tags: determinism, cause, act`

## §308
> Bajo el soplo del progresismo contemporáneo, el
> historiador se ha esfumado, dejando el pasado nuevamente
> en manos del simple erudito. La erudición moderna —más
> refinada que la del infolio barroco, pero viciada por
> categorías hermenéuticas igualmente anacrónicas— archivó
> la historia, ese sutil y frágil invento de unos reaccionarios
> decimonónicos.

**Close:** Under the breath of contemporary progressivism, the historian has evaporated, leaving the past once again in the hands of the simple erudite. Modern erudition - more refined than that of the baroque folio, but vitiated by equally anachronistic hermeneutical categories - archived history, that subtle and fragile invention of certain nineteenth-century reactionaries.

**Free:** Under contemporary progressivism the historian has disappeared and the past has fallen back into the hands of the mere scholar. Modern erudition may be more refined than baroque folio-learning, but it has filed away history itself, that subtle and fragile invention of some nineteenth-century reactionaries.

**Exegesis:**
The point is not anti-erudition. It is that scholarship without historical judgment turns the past back into archive.

`tags: history, erudition, progressivism`

## §315
> Afirmar que las épocas están todas a igual distancia de
> Dios, no es enseñar que todas se salvan, sino que todas
> pueden salvarse. Ranke no resbala un rasero sobre la
> historia, sino condena la aberración progresista.

**Close:** To affirm that the epochs are all at equal distance from God is not to teach that all are saved, but that all can be saved. Ranke does not slide a measuring rod over history, but rather condemns the progressive aberration.

**Free:** To say that all epochs stand at equal distance from God is not to say that all are saved, only that all are saveable. Ranke is not leveling history; he is rejecting the progressive delusion.

**Exegesis:**
This is a direct rebuttal to the standard progressive misreading. Equality before transcendence is not equivalence in value.

`tags: Ranke, history, salvation`

## §326
> Los que protestan si afirmamos que el bien se convierte
> en mal, o el mal en bien, si Dios lo quiere, que el mismo
> acto podría ser, por lo tanto, malo o bueno alternativamente,
> no entienden que el ser voluntad de Dios cambia la esencia,
> no meramente la nomenclatura, de las cosas.

**Close:** Those who protest if we affirm that the good becomes evil, or evil good, if God wills it, that the same act could therefore be bad or good alternatively, do not understand that being the will of God changes the essence, not merely the nomenclature, of things.

**Free:** Those scandalized by the claim that the same act could become good or evil according to God's will do not understand that divine willing changes the essence of things, not just their label.

**Exegesis:**
The point is not arbitrariness but metaphysical dependence. If the source of being is different, the act is not merely renamed but altered at the level of what it is.

`tags: God, essence, good-and-evil`

## §328
> Período histórico es el lapso durante el cual predomina
> una determinada definición de lo legítimo. Revolución es el
> tránsito de una definición a otra.

**Close:** A historical period is the span during which a determined definition of the legitimate predominates. Revolution is the transit from one definition to another.

**Free:** A historical period is the span during which one definition of the legitimate prevails. Revolution is the passage from one definition to the next.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the volume's best compressed definitions because it relocates history around legitimacy rather than around production, rights, or institutions alone.

`tags: legitimacy, period, revolution`

## §330
> Siendo las cosas que no ennoblece la vejez tan raras
> como los hombres que la vejez ennoblece, el mundo
> moderno destruye las cosas viejas y prolonga la senectud

**Close:** Since the things that old age does not ennoble are as rare as the men whom old age ennobles, the modern world destroys old things and prolongs the senescence of man.

**Free:** Since things not ennobled by age are as rare as the men ennobled by it, the modern world destroys old things while prolonging human senescence.

`tags: old-age, modernity, decay`
