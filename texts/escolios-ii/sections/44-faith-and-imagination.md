# Section 44: Faith and Imagination (§961-980, pp. 169-171)

This pair keeps theology, imagination, and public life under the same pressure: what is real is not what is most available to technique, publicity, or definition.

---

## §961
> No sé si el diablo castigue, en otro mundo, a la sociedad irreligiosa. Pero veo que aquí pronto la castiga la estética.

**Close:** I do not know whether the devil punishes, in another world, irreligious society. But I see that here aesthetics soon punishes it.

**Free:** I do not know whether the devil punishes irreligious society in another world, but I can see that here aesthetics soon does.

`tags: devil, society, aesthetics`

## §964
> No basta imaginar algo para que exista, pero sólo existe lo que imaginamos.

**Close:** It is not enough to imagine something for it to exist, but only what we imagine exists.

**Free:** Imagining something is not enough to make it exist, yet only what we imagine truly exists for us.

**Exegesis:**
The aphorism is paradoxical on purpose. Imagination does not create reality ex nihilo, but without imagination nothing attains lived presence.

`tags: imagination, existence, paradox`

## §977
> Buscar el Sitz im Leben de un texto no tiene por objeto relativizarlo, sino definir mejor lo que no tiene de relativo.

**Close:** To seek the *Sitz im Leben* of a text does not have as object relativizing it, but defining better what in it is not relative.

**Free:** Seeking a text's *Sitz im Leben* is not meant to relativize it, but to define more precisely what in it is not relative.

**Exegesis:**
This is a strong methodological correction. Historical placement is not for dissolving truth, but for clarifying what in a text survives its setting.

`tags: text, Sitz-im-Leben, relativity`

## §980
> Hay ciencias que se pueden enseñar y otras que sólo podemos aprender. Ciencias naturales, ciencias humanas.

**Close:** There are sciences that can be taught and others that we can only learn. Natural sciences, human sciences.

**Free:** Some sciences can be taught; others can only be learned. The natural sciences belong to the first group, the human sciences to the second.

**Exegesis:**
The closing contrast is concise but pointed. Human sciences require more than transmission of procedure; they demand formation of judgment.

`tags: science, teaching, learning`
