# Section 21: Hyperchronism and Fealty (§511-526, pp. 95-97)

These pages keep dragging explanation back toward concrete causes, concrete loyalties, and the partial vision from which any real judgment must start.

---

## §511
> El progreso de falsificación del universo culmina con la
> fotografía que despoja al objeto de sus universales para
> reducirlo a una abstracción nominalista.

**Close:** The progress of the falsification of the universe culminates with photography, which strips the object of its universals in order to reduce it to a nominalist abstraction.

**Free:** The falsification of the universe reaches a climax in photography, which strips the object of its universals and reduces it to a nominalist abstraction.

`tags: photography, nominalism, universals`

**Free:** Men are so naturally vile that only a few deserve the honor of not having their vileness forgiven.

## §513
> El historiador no debe jamás olvidar que los rasgos
> esenciales del hombre nunca son causa suficiente del
> acontecimiento concreto. Desconocer que el hecho
> irrepetible tiene causa irrepetible constituye el pecado de
> hipercronismo.

**Close:** The historian must never forget that the essential traits of man are never sufficient cause of the concrete event. To fail to recognize that the unrepeatable fact has an unrepeatable cause constitutes the sin of hyperchronism.

**Free:** The historian must never forget that "human nature" never suffices to explain a concrete event. The sin of hyperchronism begins when one refuses to see that an unrepeatable fact has an unrepeatable cause.

**Exegesis:**
This is a direct prohibition against explaining history by anthropological remainder alone. General traits matter, but once they are made causally sufficient, the event itself disappears.

`tags: history, causality, hyperchronism`

## §518
> El feudalismo se fundó sobre sentimientos nobles:
> lealtad, protección, servicio. Los demás sistemas políticos
> se fundan sobre sentimientos viles: egoísmo, codicia,
> envidia, cobardía.

**Close:** Feudalism was founded upon noble sentiments: loyalty, protection, service. The other political systems are founded upon vile sentiments: egoism, greed, envy, cowardice.

**Free:** Feudalism rested on noble sentiments such as loyalty, protection, and service; the other political systems rest on vile ones such as egoism, greed, envy, and cowardice.

**Exegesis:**
This is not a sociological thesis but a rank judgment. The point is not that feudal orders were pure, but that their declared and animating sentiments were qualitatively different from those modern politics ordinarily mobilizes.

`tags: feudalism, loyalty, politics`

**Free:** Let us not allow the practical urgency of classification to make us think we understand a concrete act simply because we have identified the class it belongs to.

`tags: classification, act, understanding`

## §520
> La relación entre el cristianismo y Cristo es el prototipo
> de la relación feudal. Señor que da la vida por sus fieles.
> Vasallos fieles al señor hasta el martirio. El cristianismo es
> un vasallaje místico.

**Close:** The relation between Christianity and Christ is the prototype of the feudal relation. Lord who gives his life for his faithful. Vassals faithful to the lord unto martyrdom. Christianity is a mystical vassalage.

**Free:** The relation between Christianity and Christ is, for Gómez Dávila, the prototype of the feudal bond: a lord who gives his life for his faithful, and vassals faithful to their lord unto martyrdom. Christianity is a mystical vassalage.

**Exegesis:**
The line matters because it does not use feudal language merely as metaphorical ornament. It treats fealty, protection, and martyrdom as a real structure of relation rather than as an anachronistic analogy.

`tags: Christianity, Christ, fealty`

## §523
> Mientras más radicalmente comparta los prejuicios de su
> tiempo, más fácil le es al historiador creerse dueño de
> criterios objetivos para juzgar la historia. La moda es el
> único absoluto que nadie suele disputar.

**Close:** The more radically he shares the prejudices of his time, the easier it is for the historian to believe himself master of objective criteria for judging history. Fashion is the only absolute that nobody usually disputes.

**Free:** The more completely a historian shares the prejudices of his time, the easier it becomes for him to imagine he possesses objective criteria for judging history. Fashion is the one absolute almost no one thinks to challenge.

`tags: historian, prejudice, fashion`

## §524
> El acto de despojar de sus bienes a un individuo se llama
> robo, cuando otro individuo lo despoja. Y justicia social,
> cuando una colectividad entera lo roba.

**Close:** The act of depriving an individual of his goods is called robbery when another individual deprives him of them. And social justice when an entire collectivity robs him.

**Free:** When one individual strips another of his goods, the act is called robbery. When an entire collectivity does it, it is called social justice.

`tags: robbery, social-justice, collectivity`

## §525
> La imparcialidad del historiador lúcido está en
> mantenerse siempre consciente de su parcialidad.

**Close:** The impartiality of the lucid historian lies in always remaining conscious of his partiality.

**Free:** The lucid historian's impartiality consists in never forgetting his own partiality.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the cleanest statements in the range of what disciplined judgment looks like. Objectivity is not produced by pretending to have no angle of vision, but by remaining consciously answerable to the one you actually have.

`tags: historian, impartiality, lucidity`

**Free:** Biographers of writers usually eliminate the person so that they can busy themselves with his insignificant life.
