# Section 15: Belief, Style, and Homage (§331-360, pp. 65-70)

This stretch keeps testing whether rank can still be perceived once modern habits of explanation, democracy, and public speech have flattened it.

## §331
> Cuando el historiador descubre que en un santo cristiano
> se escondió un dios pagano, todos dejan de creer en el santo.
> Yo comienzo a creer en el dios.

**Close:** When the historian discovers that a pagan god was hidden in a Christian saint, everyone stops believing in the saint. I begin to believe in the god.

**Free:** Once the historian uncovers a pagan god beneath a Christian saint, everyone else stops believing in the saint. I start believing in the god.

`tags: history, paganism, belief`

## §332
> Historia, crítica, filosofía.
> El método que intento practicar consiste en un proceso
> trifásico.
> Acto intelectual unitivo
> que integra a una interpretación histórica,
> en la cual va englobado un juicio crítico,
> las condiciones de posibilidad
> tanto de la crítica
> como de la historia.
> Acto que envuelve, así:
> la historicidad de lo real,
> su relieve axiológico,
> sus condiciones epistemológicas.

**Close:** History, criticism, philosophy. The method I try to practice consists in a three-phase process. A unitive intellectual act that integrates into a historical interpretation, in which a critical judgment is enclosed, the conditions of possibility of both criticism and history. An act that thus envelops the historicity of the real, its axiological relief, and its epistemological conditions.

**Free:** History, criticism, philosophy: the method he is after is a threefold but unifying act. It reads historically, judges critically within that reading, and then reflects on the conditions that make both the judgment and the history possible.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the clearest explicit method statements in the volume. The point is not to line up three separate disciplines but to hold them inside one act, so that history, value, and knowledge are not treated as detachable layers.

`tags: method, history, criticism`

## §340
> La democracia no confía el poder a quien no le hace el
> homenaje de sacrificarle la conciencia y el gusto.

**Close:** Democracy does not entrust power to one who does not render it the homage of sacrificing to it conscience and taste.

**Free:** Democracy gives power only to those who pay it homage by sacrificing conscience and taste.

**Exegesis:**
The key word is *homenaje*. What democracy wants here is not mere obedience but tribute: a public proof that conscience and taste have been subordinated to the regime's demands.

`tags: democracy, power, homage`

## §342
> La fe en Dios no resuelve los problemas, pero los vuelve
> irrisorios. La serenidad del creyente no es presunción de
> ciencia, sino plenitud de confianza.

**Close:** Faith in God does not resolve problems, but it renders them derisory. The serenity of the believer is not presumption of knowledge, but fullness of trust.

**Free:** Faith in God does not solve problems, but it makes them lose their ultimacy. The believer's serenity comes not from knowing everything, but from the fullness of trust.

**Exegesis:**
This is a useful correction to apologetic habits. Faith is not presented as a superior problem-solving device, but as a changed proportion between the problem and the one who bears it.

`tags: faith, trust, problems`

## §344
> Los que presumen poseer la fórmula de la felicidad
> universal acaban estrangulando al prójimo recalcitrante. El
> despotismo no es tanto fruto de la libido dominandi como
> del dogmatismo de la caridad.

**Close:** Those who presume to possess the formula of universal happiness end by strangling the recalcitrant neighbor. Despotism is not so much the fruit of the *libido dominandi* as of the dogmatism of charity.

**Free:** Those convinced they hold the formula for universal happiness end up choking the neighbor who resists. Despotism grows not only from the lust to dominate, but from charity hardened into dogma.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the sharpest lines in the batch because it shifts the source of tyranny away from obvious cruelty and toward moral certainty on behalf of others.

`tags: despotism, charity, universal-happiness`

## §350
> Un anacronismo larvado trivializa la obra del historiador
> incapaz de transformar en categorías interpretativas de cada
> época la estructura de sus hechos.

**Close:** A larvated anachronism trivializes the work of the historian incapable of transforming the structure of his facts into the interpretive categories of each epoch.

**Free:** Hidden anachronism trivializes the historian's work whenever he cannot translate the structure of his facts into the interpretive categories proper to each age.

**Exegesis:**
The warning is not only against obvious presentism. It is against anachronism that has gone underground inside the historian's own categories.

`tags: history, anachronism, categories`

## §358
> Ver preferencialmente la positividad de lo existente es la
> predisposición mental indispensable al historiador. Los
> trenos son permisibles en historia, excomulgar no lo es.

**Close:** Preferentially seeing the positivity of what exists is the mental predisposition indispensable to the historian. Dirges are permissible in history; excommunicating is not.

**Free:** The historian needs a prior disposition to see, first of all, the positive reality of what exists. Lament may be allowed in history; excommunication is not.

**Exegesis:**
This does not forbid grief or moral pain. It forbids treating the past as something to anathematize before one has learned to see what it positively was.

`tags: history, positivity, excommunication`

**Free:** The great historian is less the one who can see faults in what he loves than the one who can acknowledge virtues in what he hates.

## §360
> Los viejos despotismos se limitaban a encerrar al hombre
> en la vida privada, los de nuevo cuño prefieren que no tenga
> sino vida pública. Para domesticar al hombre basta politizar
> todos sus gestos.

**Close:** The old despotisms limited themselves to shutting man up in private life; the new kind prefer that he have nothing but public life. To domesticate man it suffices to politicize all his gestures.

**Free:** Older despotisms were content to confine man to private life; the newer kind prefer that he have only public life. To domesticate him, it is enough to politicize every gesture.

**Exegesis:**
The target is not political action as such, but total political occupancy. Once every gesture becomes public and political, domestication is complete.

`tags: despotism, public-life, politics`
