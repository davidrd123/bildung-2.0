# Section 24: Providence and Tension (§558-580, pp. 103-106)

These pages keep refusing flat explanations: history runs below events, providence changes with standpoint, truth is a tension, and the ethical life is shaped less by proof than by form.

---

**Close:** The *Aufklärung*, among the French romantics, finally overcomes romanticism.

**Free:** In the French Romantics, the Enlightenment finally defeats romanticism.

`tags: Aufklärung, Romanticism, France`

## §559
> La verdadera historia fluye bajo los hechos. Tanto
> supra-estructuras como infra-estructuras son expresiones del
> clima del alma. Todo lo que acontece resulta de alteraciones en
> la substancia misma del hombre.

**Close:** True history flows beneath the facts. Both superstructures and infrastructures are expressions of the climate of the soul. Everything that happens results from alterations in the very substance of man.

**Free:** Real history runs under the facts. Superstructures and infrastructures alike express the climate of the soul; what happens outwardly issues from changes in man's very substance.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the strongest statements in the volume that history is not exhausted by event-surface. The line does not deny structures; it demotes them beneath a deeper alteration in the human substance that gives them form.

`tags: history, soul, structure`

## §560
> La noción de providencia no puede hacer parte de filosofía
> alguna. Pero toda filosofía que no hunda en ella secretamente
> sus raíces resulta grotesca.

**Close:** The notion of providence cannot form part of any philosophy. But every philosophy that does not secretly sink its roots into it turns out grotesque.

**Free:** Providence cannot appear as one item inside a philosophy, yet any philosophy that does not secretly root itself there becomes grotesque.

**Exegesis:**
The aphorism sets providence at once outside and beneath philosophy: not a thesis among theses, but a hidden root without which the whole theoretical edifice becomes absurd.

`tags: providence, philosophy, roots`

## §562
> La tolerancia consiste en una firme decisión de permitir
> que insulten todo lo que pretendemos querer y respetar,
> siempre que no amenacen nuestras comodidades materiales.
> El hombre moderno, liberal, demócrata, progresista, siempre
> que no le pisen los callos, tolera que le empuerquen el alma.

**Close:** Tolerance consists in a firm decision to allow them to insult everything we claim to love and respect, so long as they do not threaten our material comforts. Modern man, liberal, democrat, progressive, so long as they do not step on his toes, tolerates having his soul dirtied.

**Free:** Tolerance means a firm willingness to let everything we claim to love and respect be insulted, provided our material comforts remain untouched. Modern liberal-democratic-progressive man, so long as his toes are not stepped on, tolerates the fouling of his soul.

`tags: tolerance, comfort, soul`

## §563
> Decir que la libertad consiste en cosa distinta de hacer lo
> que queremos es mentira. Que convenga, por otra parte,
> limitar la libertad es cosa evidente. Pero el engaño comienza
> cuando pretenden identificarla con las limitaciones que le
> imponen.

**Close:** To say that freedom consists in something other than doing what we want is a lie. That it may be advisable, on the other hand, to limit freedom is evident. But the deception begins when they pretend to identify it with the limitations imposed on it.

**Free:** It is a lie to say that freedom means anything other than doing what we want. That freedom often needs to be limited is obvious enough; the deceit begins when those limits are made to count as freedom itself.

**Exegesis:**
The point is not libertarian absolutism. The point is conceptual honesty: limits may be necessary, but the necessity of limits does not entitle one to rename them freedom.

`tags: freedom, limits, deception`

## §568
> La verdad, para el cristiano, es tensión entre ciertas
> proposiciones contrarias. La teología no tiene la función de
> resolver el conflicto, sino de mostrar su necesidad.

**Close:** Truth, for the Christian, is tension between certain contrary propositions. Theology does not have the function of resolving the conflict, but of showing its necessity.

**Free:** For the Christian, truth lives in the tension between certain contrary propositions. Theology exists not to dissolve that conflict, but to show why it is necessary.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the clearest statements in the run that doctrinal truth is not simple harmonization. Theological work is custodial rather than eliminative: it keeps the tension from being flattened into a false peace.

`tags: truth, theology, tension`

## §572
> La razón, en teología, sólo tiene la función de demoler
> ídolos.

**Close:** Reason, in theology, has only the function of demolishing idols.

**Free:** In theology reason has only one task: to demolish idols.

**Exegesis:**
Reason is not made sovereign here, but neither is it expelled. Its proper theological role is iconoclastic: it clears away false gods rather than generating the real one.

`tags: reason, theology, idols`

> escritor que supo hablarnos de ese país o de ese tiempo.

**Close:** When the longing for other places, for other centuries, awakens in us, it is not really in this or that time, in this or that country, where we desire to live, but in the very phrases of the writer who knew how to speak to us of that country or of that time.

**Free:** When we begin to long for other places and other centuries, what we truly want is not to live in some particular country or era, but inside the very phrases of the writer who knew how to speak to us of them.

`tags: longing, writer, phrases`

## §576
> Mientras el historiador no admita que la causa de la
> existencia, o de la inexistencia, de determinados hechos en
> determinadas épocas es la presencia, o la ausencia, de
> individuos capaces, o incapaces, de producirlos, una creciente
> pululación de teorías seguirá atribuyendo la paternidad de los
> hechos a causas incapaces de engendrarlos.

**Close:** So long as the historian does not admit that the cause of the existence, or of the nonexistence, of certain facts in certain epochs is the presence, or the absence, of individuals capable, or incapable, of producing them, a growing swarming of theories will continue attributing the parentage of facts to causes incapable of engendering them.

**Free:** Until the historian admits that the existence or nonexistence of certain facts in certain periods depends on the presence or absence of individuals capable of producing them, theories will keep multiplying that assign parentage to causes unable to beget what they are invoked to explain.

**Exegesis:**
This extends the earlier attack on hyperchronism. The protest is specifically against theories that invoke vast causes where only actual capable persons can explain why something did or did not come into being.

`tags: historian, causality, individuals`

## §580
> La ética debe ser la estética de la conducta.

**Close:** Ethics ought to be the aesthetics of conduct.

**Free:** Ethics should be the aesthetics of conduct.

**Exegesis:**
The aphorism does not collapse ethics into mere appearance. It suggests instead that conduct should acquire the formal rightness, proportion, and style one expects from aesthetic judgment.
