# Section 13: Love, Method, and Homage (§271-300, pp. 54-59)

Love, hierarchy, and method keep being tested against the same enemy here: the flattening that makes everything equally available and therefore equally cheap.

---

## §271
> El hombre no ama la realidad empírica del ser amado,
> sino su idea concreta. Ni suma de accidentes, ni mero
> participante de un ideal genérico, el ser amado es la idea
> concreta de su perfección individual. Todo ser es una
> estatua trunca, a la que sólo el amor devuelve sus curvas
> mutiladas.

**Close:** Man does not love the empirical reality of the beloved being, but its concrete idea. Neither a sum of accidents, nor a mere participant in a generic ideal, the beloved being is the concrete idea of its individual perfection. Every being is a truncated statue, to which only love restores its mutilated curves.

**Free:** We do not love the beloved as an empirical bundle, but as the concrete idea of a singular perfection. Every being is a broken statue; only love restores the missing curves.

**Exegesis:**
The sentence refuses both empiricism and abstraction. Love does not erase the individual into an ideal type, but sees the individual as more fully itself than bare fact can show.

`tags: love, individuality, perfection`

## §280
> En las democracias, donde el igualitarismo impide que la
> admiración sane la herida que la superioridad ajena saja en
> nuestras almas, la envidia prolifera. La envidia es el
> innoble sustituto democrático del homenaje.

**Close:** In democracies, where egalitarianism prevents admiration from healing the wound that another's superiority gashes in our souls, envy proliferates. Envy is the ignoble democratic substitute for homage.

**Free:** In democracies, egalitarianism forbids admiration from healing the wound caused by another's superiority, so envy multiplies. Envy is democracy's ignoble substitute for homage.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the cleanest explanations in the volume of why envy and equality belong together. The real loss is not privilege but the capacity to turn superiority into homage instead of grievance.

`tags: democracy, envy, admiration`

## §281
> La literatura no usurpó las funciones religiosas que,
> desde hace rato, ejerce. Al colocar la religión al servicio
> del hombre, fue preciso que alguien se pusiese al servicio
> de Dios.

**Close:** Literature did not usurp the religious functions that, for some time now, it has exercised. By placing religion at the service of man, it became necessary that someone place himself at the service of God.

**Free:** Literature did not steal the religious role it now plays. Once religion was put at man's service, it became necessary for someone to put himself back at God's.

**Exegesis:**
This is stronger than the claim that literature inherited religious prestige. It suggests that literature became religious by default once religion itself accepted a merely human function.

`tags: literature, religion, service`

**Close:** Just as in the nineteenth century there existed two parallel paintings, one official and the other authentic, so the thought of the century flows in parallel channels. Reactionaries are the impressionists of this century.

**Free:** Just as the nineteenth century had two parallel kinds of painting, one official and the other real, so this century has two parallel streams of thought. Reactionaries are its impressionists.

## §292
> Lógica, dialéctica, paradoja, jerarquía. Método lógico,
> que el principio de identidad regula. Método dialéctico que
> guía el principio de contradicción. Método paradójico,
> obediente al principio de coincidencia de los contrarios.
> Método jerárquico, que aplica el principio de ordenación.
> El método jerárquico no identifica los términos, ni los
> absorbe, ni los equilibra, sino los ordena.

**Close:** Logic, dialectic, paradox, hierarchy. Logical method, which the principle of identity regulates. Dialectical method, which the principle of contradiction guides. Paradoxical method, obedient to the principle of the coincidence of contraries. Hierarchical method, which applies the principle of ordering. The hierarchical method does not identify the terms, nor absorb them, nor balance them, but orders them.

**Free:** Logic, dialectic, paradox, hierarchy: four methods, each governed by a different principle. The distinctive thing about hierarchy is that it neither identifies opposites, nor absorbs them, nor merely balances them. It orders them.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the volume's methodological centerpieces. Hierarchy is not compromise, synthesis, or equilibrium. It is a way of keeping differences real while refusing to leave them flat.

`tags: method, hierarchy, paradox`

## §293
> La interpretación histórica reclama categorías éticas. El
> historiador que las elude empobrece su objeto. Ayer pareció
> necesario eliminarlas, porque se confundían con prejuicios
> congénitos al universitario liberal, progresista, demócrata.
> Pero es desmesura suprimir la ética para evitar meramente
> la virtuosa indignación del progresista con la "inmoralidad
> del pasado".

**Close:** Historical interpretation claims ethical categories. The historian who evades them impoverishes his object. Yesterday it seemed necessary to eliminate them because they were confused with prejudices congenital to the liberal, progressive, democratic university man. But it is excess to suppress ethics merely to avoid the progressive's virtuous indignation at the "immorality of the past."

**Free:** Historical interpretation needs ethical categories; the historian who avoids them thins out his object. It once seemed necessary to banish them because they arrived mixed with liberal-progressive prejudices, but it is an overreaction to abolish ethics merely to escape progressive moralizing about the past.

**Exegesis:**
He is not asking for a return to moralistic history. He is saying that value-neutral history produces its own distortion when it refuses to judge at all.

`tags: history, ethics, judgment`

## §300
> Hubo un catolicismo dórico: el de las iglesias románicas
> y las órdenes militares. Un catolicismo benedictino y
> feudal. Hubo también un catolicismo jónico: el de las
> catedrales góticas y las sumas escolásticas. Un catolicismo
> de cogulla mendicante y de lirios reales. Hubo, en fin, un
> catolicismo corintio: el de los templos barrocos y la
> contra-reforma tridentina. Un catolicismo de sotanas
> rurales y de pompas romanas.

**Close:** There was a Doric Catholicism: that of Romanesque churches and military orders. A Benedictine and feudal Catholicism. There was also an Ionic Catholicism: that of Gothic cathedrals and scholastic summas. A Catholicism of mendicant cowls and royal lilies. There was, finally, a Corinthian Catholicism: that of baroque temples and the Tridentine Counter-Reformation. A Catholicism of rural cassocks and Roman pomps.

**Free:** There has been a Doric Catholicism, Romanesque and military; an Ionic Catholicism, Gothic and scholastic; and finally a Corinthian Catholicism, baroque and Tridentine. Catholicism is not styleless; it has worn distinct historical forms without ceasing to be itself.

**Exegesis:**
The typology is not decorative. It argues that historical form need not dissolve identity. A tradition can be internally plural without becoming formless.

`tags: Catholicism, style, history`
