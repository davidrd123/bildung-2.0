# Section 34: Inheritance and Prison (§761-780, pp. 136-139)

These pages move between transcendence and confinement without making either easy: inheritance nourishes, imagination inhabits, science inventories, and modern confidence turns out to be one more form of imprisonment.

---

## §761
> No trato de envenenar las fuentes.
> Sino de mostrar que están envenenadas.

**Close:** I do not try to poison the springs.
But rather to show that they are poisoned.

**Free:** I am not trying to poison the springs, only to show that they have already been poisoned.

`tags: source, diagnosis, corruption`

---

## §762
> Inmanente es, en el fondo, lo que podemos definir.
> Trascendente, lo que podemos describir tan sólo.

**Close:** Immanent is, at bottom, what we can define.
Transcendent, what we can only describe.

**Free:** In the end, the immanent is what we can define; the transcendent, what we can only describe.

**Exegesis:**
The distinction is compressed but sharp. Definition belongs to what can be bounded; transcendence remains available only through descriptive approach.

`tags: immanence, transcendence, definition`

## §767
> El que se niega a ser heredero muere de inanición intelectual.

**Close:** He who refuses to be an heir dies of intellectual inanition.

**Free:** Whoever refuses to be an heir dies of intellectual starvation.

**Exegesis:**
Inheritance is not optional ornament here. It is nourishment without which intellect starves while pretending to autonomy.

`tags: inheritance, intellect, starvation`

## §773
> El historiador tiene por función y por deber destruir las generalizaciones del sociólogo.

**Close:** The historian has as his function and duty the destruction of the sociologist's generalizations.

**Free:** The historian's function and duty is to destroy the sociologist's generalizations.

**Exegesis:**
The line is polemical, but its point is disciplinary. History answers generalization by reintroducing the concrete and the irreducible.

## §775
> La filosofía, infortunadamente, consiste en un discurso, que si no es coherente no es nada, sobre un universo, que no es nada si es coherente.

**Close:** Philosophy, unfortunately, consists in a discourse that, if it is not coherent, is nothing, about a universe that is nothing if it is coherent.

**Free:** Philosophy unfortunately consists in a discourse that is nothing if incoherent, about a universe that is nothing if coherent.

**Exegesis:**
The aphorism pins philosophy between two incompatible demands. Thought requires coherence, while the real may cease to be real precisely when fully coherent.

`tags: philosophy, coherence, universe`

## §779
> La ciencia no puede hacer más que el inventario de nuestra prisión.

**Close:** Science can do no more than the inventory of our prison.

**Free:** Science can do no more than take inventory of our prison.

**Exegesis:**
This is not ignorance worship. It is a rank judgment: science can measure the prison's contents without thereby showing a way out.

`tags: science, prison, inventory`

## §780
> La naturaleza acabó de morir en este siglo.
> Tan sólo en el arte de siglos pretéritos descubrimos, asombrados, que la naturaleza no es simple experimento de física explotado por organismos diligentes.

**Close:** Nature has finished dying in this century.
Only in the art of past centuries do we discover, astonished, that nature is not a simple physics experiment exploited by diligent organisms.

**Free:** Nature has finally died in this century.
Only in the art of earlier centuries do we discover, astonished, that nature is not just a physics experiment being exploited by industrious organisms.

**Exegesis:**
The second sentence matters as much as the first. Nature is recovered not by theory but by retrospectively encountering older works in which it had not yet been reduced to use, process, and exploitation.

`tags: nature, art, death`
