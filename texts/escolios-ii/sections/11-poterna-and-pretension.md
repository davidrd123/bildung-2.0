# Section 11: Poterna and Pretension (§221-240, pp. 45-48)

Several of the volume's central pressures meet here at once: aesthetic passage into the sacred, distrust of systems, and the need to repair perception rather than simply repeat refutations.

---

## §221
> Debajo de los dogmas sobre los cuales los naturalismos
> se asientan, la imaginación acumula calladamente su
> dinamita.

**Close:** Beneath the dogmas on which naturalisms rest, imagination quietly accumulates its dynamite.

**Free:** Beneath every naturalist creed, imagination is quietly stockpiling explosives.

`tags: naturalism, imagination, dogma`

---

## §222
> Toca hoy oponerse a toda censura, para que la carroña
> moderna se pudra con mayor rapidez.

**Close:** Today one must oppose every censorship, so that modern carrion may rot with greater rapidity.

**Free:** The task now is to oppose censorship, so that modern carrion can rot faster in the open.

`tags: censorship, decay, modernity`

---

## §223
> Para pasar del mundo profano al sagrado, la poterna de
> la experiencia estética se ofrece a los que intimida la
> puerta de la conversión religiosa.

**Close:** To pass from the profane world to the sacred, the postern of aesthetic experience offers itself to those whom the door of religious conversion intimidates.

**Free:** For those frightened by conversion, aesthetic experience offers a side gate from the profane world into the sacred.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the strongest early volume-II formulations of aesthetic seriousness. Art is not identical with conversion, but it can open a real passage toward what conversion names more directly. The image matters: not a substitute temple, but a postern.

`tags: aesthetics, sacred, conversion`

---

## §224
> La zona del universo patente a la conciencia se ha
> encogido tanto que ya no percibimos sino la sombra de la
> materia.

**Close:** The zone of the universe patent to consciousness has shrunk so much that we no longer perceive anything but the shadow of matter.

**Free:** The portion of the universe still open to consciousness has narrowed so far that we see little more than matter's shadow.

`tags: consciousness, matter, reduction`

---

## §225
> Los que no queremos pertenecer a este siglo de envidia
> tenemos que cercenarle diariamente siete cabezas a la
> envidia de nuestro corazón.

**Close:** Those of us who do not want to belong to this century of envy have to cut seven heads off the envy of our heart every day.

**Free:** Whoever does not want to belong to this envious century has to decapitate the heart's envy every day, and not just once.

`tags: envy, century, asceticism`

---

## §226
> Como todo lo que hoy se construye se pasa
> automáticamente al enemigo, esperemos antes de construir
> que el tiempo traiga materiales que no traicionan.

**Close:** Since everything built today passes automatically to the enemy, let us wait before building until time brings materials that do not betray.

**Free:** Since whatever gets built now is automatically handed over to the enemy, better to wait for materials time will not betray.

**Exegesis:**
This is not quietism. It is a warning about premature construction under hostile conditions. Institutions, slogans, even restored forms can become enemy property if the material itself is false.

`tags: building, betrayal, patience`

---

## §227
> El que es fiel a las pautas de un sistema llama
> presuntuoso al que opina libremente en cada caso. Como si
> optar por un sistema total no fuese más presuntuoso que
> osar juicios limitados.

**Close:** He who is faithful to the guidelines of a system calls presumptuous the one who opines freely in each case. As if opting for a total system were not more presumptuous than daring limited judgments.

**Free:** The system-man calls case-by-case judgment presumptuous, as though committing oneself to a total system were not the greater presumption.

`tags: system, judgment, presumption`

---

## §228
> El historiador suele creer universales los criterios de
> verosimilitud de su tiempo.

**Close:** The historian usually believes the criteria of verisimilitude of his own time to be universal.

**Free:** Historians often mistake their age's standards of plausibility for universal ones.

`tags: history, verisimilitude, presentism`

---

## §229
> Los apóstoles de la "necesidad histórica" olvidan que es
> obra de los que no se sometieron a ella.

**Close:** The apostles of "historical necessity" forget that it is the work of those who did not submit to it.

**Free:** The preachers of historical necessity forget that history gets made by people who refused to bow to it.

`tags: history, necessity, agency`

---

## §230
> Para vivir no es necesario ocupar un trono. Basta
> sabernos legítimos pretendientes a una corona. Pero es
> imposible vivir si sospechamos que nuestras pretensiones
> son ilegítimas.

**Close:** To live it is not necessary to occupy a throne. It is enough to know ourselves legitimate pretenders to a crown. But it is impossible to live if we suspect that our pretensions are illegitimate.

**Free:** One need not sit on a throne to live; it is enough to know one has a rightful claim. Life becomes impossible only when one suspects the claim itself is false.

**Exegesis:**
The point is interior legitimacy, not social success. One can live without possession, but not without some conviction that one's desire answers to a real order rather than to vanity.

`tags: legitimacy, rank, inwardness`

---

## §231
> Toda solución parece trivial a quien ignora el problema.

**Close:** Every solution seems trivial to whoever ignores the problem.

**Free:** Any solution looks trivial to someone who has never really seen the problem.

`tags: problem, solution, triviality`

---

## §232
> Expresar ideas es fácil, pero es casi imposible
> comunicar el contexto que las hace inteligibles. Quien no
> comparta nuestras experiencias se engaña creyendo
> entendernos.

**Close:** Expressing ideas is easy, but it is almost impossible to communicate the context that makes them intelligible. Whoever does not share our experiences deceives himself in believing that he understands us.

**Free:** Ideas are easy to state; what is nearly impossible to transmit is the lived context that lets them make sense. Without that shared ground, understanding is mostly self-deception.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the volume's cleanest statements about why aphoristic writing needs more from a reader than semantic agreement. The sentence can be grasped quickly; the context that makes it live cannot be shipped intact.

`tags: context, understanding, experience`

---

## §233
> Frente a tanto intelectual soso, a tanto artista sin
> talento, a tanto revolucionario estereotipado, un burgués
> sin pretensiones parece una estatua griega.

**Close:** Before so many insipid intellectuals, so many artists without talent, so many stereotyped revolutionaries, an unpretentious bourgeois seems a Greek statue.

**Free:** Set against so many bland intellectuals, talentless artists, and prefab revolutionaries, a plain unpretentious bourgeois can look like a Greek statue.

`tags: bourgeois, modern-types, contrast`

---

## §234
> Quien se expresa brevemente, sin el aparato usual de
> referencias y de citas, aspira a que sus solos ademanes lo
> acrediten.

**Close:** Whoever expresses himself briefly, without the usual apparatus of references and citations, aspires to have his gestures alone accredit him.

**Free:** Whoever writes briefly without the standard machinery of citation is asking to be judged on bearing alone.

`tags: brevity, citation, style`

---

## §235
> Desconfiemos de quienes necesitan certificados de
> origen para probar su nobleza.

**Close:** Let us distrust those who need certificates of origin to prove their nobility.

**Free:** Distrust anyone who needs paperwork to certify nobility.

`tags: nobility, pedigree, distrust`

---

## §236
> No es la riqueza lo que escandaliza al pobre, sino el
> enriquecimiento.

**Close:** It is not wealth that scandalizes the poor, but enrichment.

**Free:** The poor are scandalized less by wealth than by the sight of someone getting rich.

`tags: wealth, poor, resentment`

---

## §237
> El pueblo se ha burlado siempre de los nuevos ricos y,
> aunque las confisque, respeta las fortunas rancias.

**Close:** The people have always mocked the newly rich and, even though it confiscates them, respects old fortunes.

**Free:** People always mock the nouveau riche and, even when they confiscate them, still respect old fortunes.

`tags: wealth, old-fortunes, people`

---

## §238
> Este mundo empobrecido es producto de una visión
> deformada por prejuicios ya abrogados. No debemos
> ocuparnos en repetir refutaciones sino en inventar ritos
> propicios a la catarsis de los sentidos.

**Close:** This impoverished world is the product of a vision deformed by prejudices already abrogated. We must not busy ourselves repeating refutations but inventing rites propitious to the catharsis of the senses.

**Free:** This impoverished world comes from perception warped by prejudices already officially repealed. The task now is not to repeat the refutations but to invent rites that cleanse the senses.

**Exegesis:**
The old arguments have already won on paper. What remains is damaged perception. That is why the remedy must be ritual rather than merely polemical: something has to retrain the senses, not just the thesis.

`tags: rites, perception, catharsis`

---

## §239
> Ni el hombre posee instintos religiosos o artísticos, ni
> filosofía o ciencia dependen de facultades inherentes a la
> naturaleza humana. Ninguna de estas actividades es
> elemento constitutivo de la condición del hombre. Todas
> son accidentes de su historia. Dios, la belleza, la verdad,
> pueden morir, porque no son meras proyecciones innatas de
> la naturaleza humana. Sólo son inmortales en el hombre los
> apetitos que comparte con las bestias.

**Close:** Man possesses neither religious nor artistic instincts, nor do philosophy or science depend on faculties inherent to human nature. None of these activities is a constitutive element of the condition of man. All are accidents of his history. God, beauty, truth, can die, because they are not mere innate projections of human nature. Only the appetites he shares with the beasts are immortal in man.

**Free:** Religion, art, philosophy, and science are not built-in human constants. They are historical achievements, and because they are achievements they can die. What persists by nature are only the appetites we share with animals.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the volume's hardest anti-consolations. Culture and transcendence are not guaranteed by human nature. They survive, if they survive, as historical victories that can be lost.

`tags: history, beauty, truth`

---

## §240
> Toda teoría que pretenda calificar de engaño lo que nos
> conmovió noblemente, algún día, es falsa.

**Close:** Every theory that pretends to qualify as deception what moved us nobly one day is false.

**Free:** Any theory that explains away what once moved us nobly as mere illusion is false.

`tags: theory, nobility, illusion`
