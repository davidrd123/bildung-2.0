# Section 52: Reticence and Adult Intelligence (§1121-1140, pp. 194-196)

These pages work less by system than by discipline: reticence, cultural measure, strong reading, and the adult intelligence that refuses paradise-management.

---

## §1121
> El irrespeto mutuo convierte pronto la amistad o el amor entre almas plebeyas en mero contrato bilateral de grosería.

**Close:** Mutual disrespect soon converts friendship or love between plebeian souls into a mere bilateral contract of grossness.

**Free:** Mutual disrespect quickly turns friendship or love between plebeian souls into a mere bilateral contract of vulgarity.

`tags: friendship, love, disrespect`

---

## §1122
> La total libertad de expresión no compensa la falta de talento.

**Close:** Total freedom of expression does not compensate for the lack of talent.

**Free:** Unlimited freedom of expression does not make up for the absence of talent.

`tags: expression, freedom, talent`

## §1123
> El impacto de un texto es proporcional a la astucia de sus reticencias.

**Close:** The impact of a text is proportional to the astuteness of its reticences.

**Free:** A text's impact is proportional to the cunning of what it withholds.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the clearest local statements of Gómez Dávila's own practice. Force belongs not to exhaustive statement but to intelligent reserve.

`tags: text, reticence, impact`

## §1125
> Mal gusto es la nota común a las crispaturas que el escritor de los últimos dos siglos, por talento que tenga, repetidamente nos causa, y que el escritor de siglos anteriores, por mediocre que sea, no nos causa jamás.

**Close:** Bad taste is the common note of the irritations that the writer of the last two centuries, however talented he may be, repeatedly causes us, and that the writer of earlier centuries, however mediocre he may be, never causes us.

**Free:** Bad taste is the common note in those spasms of irritation that writers of the last two centuries keep causing us, however talented they are, and that earlier writers, however mediocre, never cause.

`tags: taste, writers, centuries`

## §1127
> Alma culta es aquella donde el estruendo de los vivos no ahoga la música de los muertos.

**Close:** Cultivated soul is that one in which the din of the living does not drown the music of the dead.

**Free:** A cultivated soul is one in which the noise of the living does not drown out the music of the dead.

**Exegesis:**
Culture is auditory discipline here: making room for the dead without theatrical antiquarianism.

`tags: soul, dead, culture`

## §1128
> Para calcular la elevación de nuestro pináculo cultural, comparemos: con la ingravidez de un texto ático, o con la gravedad de un párrafo latino, o con el cromatismo de una secuencia medieval, o con la limpidez de una página dieciochista, la argamasa verbal del escritor contemporáneo.

**Close:** To calculate the elevation of our cultural pinnacle, let us compare: with the weightlessness of an Attic text, or with the gravity of a Latin paragraph, or with the chromaticism of a medieval sequence, or with the limpidity of an eighteenth-century page, the verbal mortar of the contemporary writer.

**Free:** If we want to measure the height of our cultural summit, let us compare the contemporary writer's verbal mortar with the weightlessness of an Attic text, the gravity of a Latin paragraph, the chromatic richness of a medieval sequence, or the limpidity of an eighteenth-century page.

`tags: culture, style, comparison`

## §1135
> Si existiesen leyes de la historia, su descubrimiento las abrogaría.

**Close:** If laws of history existed, their discovery would abrogate them.

**Free:** If there were laws of history, discovering them would repeal them.

**Exegesis:**
Historical life refuses the passivity that a law-governed object would require. Discovery would alter the field discovered.

## §1137
> La voluntad no es motor, sino freno. La conciencia no puede querer sino lo que quiere, pero puede vedarse lo que quiere. La conciencia elige entre tendencias existentes la que ha de prevalecer mediante la voluntad que subyuga las que le son adversas. La tendencia así elegida crece y se expande en expresión positiva de la voluntad.

**Close:** The will is not motor, but brake. Consciousness cannot will except what it wills, but it can forbid itself what it wills. Consciousness chooses among existing tendencies the one that is to prevail by means of the will that subdues those adverse to it. The tendency thus chosen grows and expands in positive expression of the will.

**Free:** The will is not an engine but a brake. Consciousness cannot generate desire out of nothing, but it can forbid what it wants. It chooses, among existing tendencies, which one shall prevail by using the will to subdue its rivals; the chosen tendency then grows into the will's positive expression.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the most explicit anthropological claims in the stretch. Will does not create ex nihilo; it governs, restrains, and selects.

`tags: will, consciousness, tendency`

## §1139
> La religión carece de utilidad social. Si los dioses no existen, la religión para nada sirve.

**Close:** Religion lacks social utility. If the gods do not exist, religion serves for nothing.

**Free:** Religion has no social utility. If the gods do not exist, religion is good for nothing.

**Exegesis:**
This does not attack religion. It strips away every secondary justification and insists that religion stands or falls with reality, not usefulness.

`tags: religion, utility, gods`
