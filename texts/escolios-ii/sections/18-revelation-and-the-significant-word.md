# Section 18: Revelation and the Significant Word (§421-450, pp. 80-84)

This batch keeps asking what survives the present's self-certainty: orthodoxy, revelation, monastic witness, and the power of language to recover more than opinion.

---

## §421
> Tratemos, al envejecer, de asumir actitudes que nuestra
> adolescencia hubiese aprobado y de tener ideas que no
> hubiese entendido.

**Close:** Let us try, as we age, to assume attitudes our adolescence would have approved and to have ideas it would not have understood.

**Free:** As we grow older, let us try to take on attitudes our adolescence would have approved and to hold ideas it could not yet have understood.

`tags: aging, adolescence, ideas`

---

## §422
> La verdad, ni es histórica, ni está fuera de la historia.

**Close:** Truth is neither historical nor outside history.

**Free:** Truth is neither merely historical nor external to history.

`tags: truth, history, paradox`

## §423
> El escritor actual no intenta dar en el blanco asestando
> una sola palabra, sino arrojando un chorro de libros.

**Close:** The current writer does not try to hit the target by directing a single word, but by throwing a jet of books.

**Free:** The contemporary writer no longer tries to hit the mark with one exact word, but by spraying books at it.

`tags: writer, excess, precision`

## §424
> Nada más frecuente que sentirnos dueños de varias

**Close:** Nothing is more frequent than feeling ourselves owners of several ideas because we only seize inadequate expressions of the same one.

**Free:** Nothing is more common than thinking we possess several ideas when we have only caught several inadequate expressions of the same one.

`tags: ideas, expression, multiplicity`

**Free:** What makes every present irritating is its conviction that it is right simply because it is the present.

**Free:** An outmoded orthodoxy remains loyal only in yesterday's critic, while its obedient adherent runs after the new orthodoxy.

`tags: orthodoxy, criticism, fashion`

## §435
> La poesía agoniza cuando el poeta recela que el

**Close:** Poetry agonizes when the poet suspects that the universe is scientifically explicable.

**Free:** Poetry begins to die when the poet suspects the universe can be fully explained scientifically.

`tags: poetry, science, explanation`

## §436
> La historia del cristianismo revela al cristiano qué
> presencia Cristo ha querido tener en la historia. Pretender
> borrar esa historia, para retornar al solo Cristo evangélico,
> no es gesto de devoción sino de orgullo.

**Close:** The history of Christianity reveals to the Christian what presence Christ has wanted to have in history. To pretend to erase that history, in order to return to the evangelical Christ alone, is not a gesture of devotion but of pride.

**Free:** The history of Christianity shows Christians what kind of presence Christ has wanted in history. To erase that history in the name of returning to the bare gospel Christ is not devotion but pride.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the clearest anti-primitivist lines in the volume. History is not treated as later corruption to be stripped away, but as part of the form Christ chose to take.

`tags: Christianity, history, pride`

---

## §437
> Revelación es el valor que le sobreviene de pronto a un
> hecho psicológico.

**Close:** Revelation is the value that suddenly comes over a psychological fact.

**Free:** Revelation is the value that suddenly overtakes a merely psychological fact.

**Exegesis:**
This is a major definition. Revelation does not deny the psychological fact; it is what arrives upon it and changes its order of significance.

`tags: revelation, psychology, value`

---

## §438
> Como los hechos nada enseñan, el tonto se mueve entre
> ellos sin adquirir la experiencia que tremola. Experiencia es
> lo que queda al hombre inteligente de los inventos fallidos
> de su inteligencia.

**Close:** Since facts teach nothing, the fool moves among them without acquiring the experience that trembles there. Experience is what remains to the intelligent man from the failed inventions of his intelligence.

**Free:** Facts teach nothing by themselves, so the fool moves among them without acquiring the experience latent within them. Experience is what remains to an intelligent person after the failed inventions of his own intelligence.

`tags: facts, experience, intelligence`

**Free:** In every revolutionary process, successive reactionary groups appear according to how susceptible different people are to disgust before murder.

## §441
> Discriminar estéticamente entre dos idiomas es tan
> absurdo como es de sensato discriminar entre los grados de
> destreza estética de sus parlantes respectivos. No hay
> idiomas torpes, sino escritores torpes.

**Close:** To discriminate aesthetically between two languages is as absurd as it is sensible to discriminate between the degrees of aesthetic skill of their respective speakers. There are no clumsy languages, only clumsy writers.

**Free:** It is absurd to rank languages aesthetically, though it is perfectly sensible to rank the aesthetic skill of those who speak and write them. There are no clumsy languages, only clumsy writers.

`tags: language, aesthetics, writers`

**Close:** The only thing, finally, that keeps us from being ashamed to be men is that there were monks.

**Free:** In the end, what keeps us from being ashamed of being human is that there were monks.

`tags: monks, humanity, shame`

## §446
> La literatura es el arte de devolver al vocablo
> significativo la función expresiva del grito.

**Close:** Literature is the art of restoring to the significant word the expressive function of the cry.

**Free:** Literature is the art of giving the meaningful word back the expressive force of a cry.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the best definitions in the range because it makes literature answerable both to meaning and to urgency. It is not ornament added to speech, but recovered pressure within it.

`tags: literature, word, cry`

**Free:** Time reshapes the terrain of our convictions. History often consists in problems that interest intelligent people without being, strictly speaking, intelligent people's problems.

`tags: time, conviction, history`

## §448
> Los pensadores contemporáneos difieren entre sí como
> los hoteles internacionales, cuya estructura uniforme se
> adorna superficialmente con motivos indígenas. Cuando, en
> verdad, sólo es interesante el localismo mental que se
> expresa en léxico cosmopolita.

**Close:** Contemporary thinkers differ among themselves as international hotels do, whose uniform structure is superficially adorned with indigenous motifs. When, in truth, only the mental localism that expresses itself in cosmopolitan lexicon is interesting.

**Free:** Contemporary thinkers differ the way international hotels differ: the same uniform structure decorated superficially with local motifs. What is genuinely interesting is mental localism expressed through cosmopolitan language.

`tags: thinkers, cosmopolitanism, localism`
