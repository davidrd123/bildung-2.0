# Section 19: Judgment and Two Faiths (§451-480, pp. 84-90)

Here judgment keeps reappearing where modern habits would rather substitute process, sociology, optimism, or technique.

---

## §451
> Gracias a la descripción fenomenológica, la historia de las
> religiones se libertó del esquema evolucionista que la convertía
> en ascenso hacia los prejuicios del historiador de turno.

**Close:** Thanks to phenomenological description, the history of religions freed itself from the evolutionist schema that turned it into an ascent toward the prejudices of the historian of the day.

**Free:** Phenomenological description released the history of religions from the evolutionist ladder that had been turning it into a climb toward whatever prejudices the current historian happened to hold.

**Exegesis:**
This is a methodological claim, not just a historiographical one. The gain is not neutrality, but freedom from a false narrative of ascent that smuggles the observer's own assumptions in as history's destination.

`tags: phenomenology, religion, historiography`

---

## §452
> El individualismo religioso olvida al prójimo, el
> comunitarismo olvida a Dios. Siempre es más grave error el
> segundo.

**Close:** Religious individualism forgets the neighbor; communitarianism forgets God. The second error is always graver.

**Free:** Religious individualism forgets the neighbor, but communitarianism forgets God, and that second forgetfulness is always the worse one.

`tags: individualism, communitarianism, God`

## §453
> El suicidio más acostumbrado en nuestro tiempo consiste en
> pegarse un balazo en el alma.

**Close:** The most customary suicide in our time consists in shooting oneself in the soul.

**Free:** The most common suicide in our time is not bodily but spiritual: a gunshot to the soul.

`tags: soul, suicide, modernity`

## §454
> La historia pierde su color y su relieve si el historiador no
> arriesga juicios de valor. El optimismo es gesto de enfermo
> asustado.

**Close:** History loses its color and relief if the historian does not risk judgments of value. Optimism is the gesture of a frightened sick man.

**Free:** History goes flat when the historian refuses value judgments. Optimism is the reflex of a sick man trying to calm his fear.

**Exegesis:**
The two sentences belong together even without needing a large theory around them. Refusal of judgment and compulsory optimism are treated as the same evasive move: both spare the mind from having to see what is actually there.

`tags: history, judgment, optimism`

## §455
> Lo que finalmente importa no es que un pueblo ejecute
> una política eficaz en la tierra, sino que sea un gesto
> admirable en la historia. Y al que socarronamente nos
> pregunte, ¿qué queda del que pierde?, preguntemos con
> ironía ¿qué queda del que gana?

**Close:** What finally matters is not that a people carry out an efficacious policy on earth, but that it be an admirable gesture in history. And to the one who sarcastically asks us, what remains of the one who loses?, let us ask ironically, what remains of the one who wins?

**Free:** In the end what matters is not whether a people runs an effective policy on earth, but whether it becomes an admirable gesture in history. And when someone sneers, what is left of the loser?, the proper reply is: what is left of the winner?

`tags: history, politics, defeat`

## §462
> El viejo libro inteligente no se torna nunca obsoleto,
> porque el nuevo libro inteligente sólo vuelve explicitas ideas
> que implícitamente englobaba el libro viejo. La inteligencia
> es paisaje cuya iluminación varía, pero cuyo relieve no cambia.

**Close:** The old intelligent book never becomes obsolete, because the new intelligent book only renders explicit ideas that the old book implicitly encompassed. Intelligence is a landscape whose lighting varies, but whose relief does not change.

**Free:** An old intelligent book never really becomes obsolete, because a new intelligent book merely brings into the open ideas the older one already held implicitly. Intelligence is a landscape whose light changes while its contours remain.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the clearest defenses here of rereading rather than replacement. New intelligence illuminates; it does not abolish the underlying relief.

`tags: books, intelligence, rereading`

## §469
> La complejidad de una técnica exige, hasta un
> determinado punto, una complejidad creciente de la mente;
> pero, a partir de ese punto, la complejidad creciente de la
> técnica favorece una decreciente complejidad de la mente.
> El aparato infinitamente complejo lo maneja mejor una
> suma infinita de unidades infinitamente simples.

**Close:** The complexity of a technique demands, up to a certain point, a growing complexity of mind; but, from that point onward, the growing complexity of technique favors a decreasing complexity of mind. The infinitely complex apparatus is best handled by an infinite sum of infinitely simple units.

**Free:** Technique requires more complex minds only up to a point; beyond that point, increasing technical complexity rewards mental simplification. An infinitely complex apparatus is most easily run by an infinite sum of infinitely simple units.

`tags: technique, mind, simplification`

## §474
> Nunca hubo conflicto entre razón y fe, sino entre dos fes.

**Close:** There has never been conflict between reason and faith, but between two faiths.

**Free:** The conflict was never between reason and faith, but between rival faiths.

**Exegesis:**
The line matters because it refuses the standard battlefield altogether. The argument is not that reason disappears, but that reason is always already serving one ultimate confidence or another.

`tags: reason, faith, conflict`

## §475
> Credo ut inteligam. Traduzcamos así: creó para volverme
> inteligente.

**Close:** *Credo ut intelligam.* Let us translate it thus: he created to make me intelligent.

**Free:** *I believe so that I may understand.* Let us translate it this way instead: he created me in order to make me intelligent.

**Exegesis:**
The aphorism shifts the formula from method to creaturely dependence. Understanding is not merely what belief permits; it is what creation itself was ordered toward.

`tags: belief, intelligence, creation`
