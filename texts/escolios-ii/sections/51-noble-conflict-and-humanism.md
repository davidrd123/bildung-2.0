# Section 51: Noble Conflict and Humanism (§1101-1120, pp. 191-194)

The line here is severe but not merely punitive: petty conflict must be exchanged for noble conflict, and bad benevolence is treated as one of the real social dangers.

---

## §1101
> Quien reconcilia a un individuo consigo mismo, lo rebaja.

**Close:** He who reconciles an individual with himself lowers him.

**Free:** Whoever reconciles a man with himself diminishes him.

`tags: self, reconciliation, lowering`

---

## §1102
> El alma enferma no se sana suprimiendo sus conflictos mezquinos, sino arrojándose entre conflictos nobles.

**Close:** The sick soul is not healed by suppressing its petty conflicts, but by throwing itself among noble conflicts.

**Free:** A diseased soul is not healed by silencing its petty conflicts, but by hurling itself into noble ones.

**Exegesis:**
The cure is not inner peace as such. It is elevation of the field of struggle.

`tags: soul, conflict, nobility`

---

## §1103
> Aún sabiendo que todo perece, debemos construir en granito nuestras moradas de una noche.

**Close:** Even knowing that everything perishes, we must build in granite our dwellings of one night.

**Free:** Even knowing that everything perishes, we should build our one-night dwellings in granite.

`tags: mortality, building, permanence`

## §1104
> No es traduciéndolo en otro idioma como desciframos el mito cristiano. Cuando nos parece intraducible es cuando lo hemos descifrado.

**Close:** It is not by translating it into another language that we decipher the Christian myth. It is when it seems untranslatable to us that we have deciphered it.

**Free:** We do not decipher the Christian myth by rendering it into another language. We decipher it when it begins to seem untranslatable.

**Exegesis:**
This is one of the volume's strongest statements about understanding. True decipherment does not flatten opacity; it discovers why the thing resists substitution.

`tags: myth, translation, decipherment`

---

## §1105
> Sólo el hombre inteligente puede no ser egoísta sin que peligre la tranquilidad del vecino.

**Close:** Only the intelligent man can be unselfish without the neighbor's tranquility being endangered.

**Free:** Only an intelligent man can afford not to be selfish without endangering his neighbor's peace.

`tags: intelligence, egoism, neighbor`

## §1106
> El egoísmo del imbécil es la salvaguardia de sus prójimos.

**Close:** The imbecile's selfishness is the safeguard of his neighbors.

**Free:** The fool's selfishness is what protects the people around him.

`tags: selfishness, fool, safeguard`

## §1107
> La ira de los imbéciles es menos temible que su filantropía.

**Close:** The anger of imbeciles is less fearsome than their philanthropy.

**Free:** A fool's anger is less frightening than his philanthropy.

`tags: fools, philanthropy, danger`

---

## §1108
> El imbécil benévolo, confiado en su recta intención, se autoriza atentados contra el hombre, más atroces aún que los que su intención torcida concede al malvado.

**Close:** The benevolent imbecile, trusting in his upright intention, authorizes himself outrages against man more atrocious still than those his crooked intention grants to the wicked man.

**Free:** The benevolent fool, trusting his good intentions, licenses outrages against man even worse than those a wicked man permits himself out of bad intention.

**Exegesis:**
Good intentions are treated here not as mitigation but as accelerant. Benevolence without intelligence becomes invasive violence.

`tags: benevolence, intention, atrocity`

## §1109
> Hay que predicarles egoísmo a los tontos. Para economizar muertos.

**Close:** One must preach selfishness to fools. To economize on corpses.

**Free:** Fools should be preached selfishness, simply to save lives.

`tags: fools, selfishness, death`

## §1110
> El egoísta posiblemente no sepa lo que le conviene, pero no actúa, por lo menos, como si supiera lo que conviene a los demás.

**Close:** The egoist possibly does not know what suits him, but he does not act, at least, as if he knew what suits others.

**Free:** The egoist may not know what is good for him, but at least he does not behave as if he knew what is good for everyone else.

`tags: egoism, knowledge, others`

## §1111
> Hoy, curiosamente, no se llama humanismo lo que enseñaría un humanista florentino, sino lo que enseñaba el averroísta paduano.

**Close:** Today, curiously, humanism is not called what a Florentine humanist would teach, but what the Paduan Averroist taught.

**Free:** Oddly enough, what now gets called humanism is not what a Florentine humanist would have taught, but what a Paduan Averroist did teach.

`tags: humanism, Florence, Averroism`

## §1112
> Con sus manos democráticas Lamennais estranguló el alma de Sainte-Beuve. El primer trofeo del catolicismo progresista fue la incredulidad del hombre más inteligente de su siglo.

**Close:** With his democratic hands Lamennais strangled the soul of Sainte-Beuve. The first trophy of progressive Catholicism was the incredulity of the most intelligent man of his century.

**Free:** With democratic hands Lamennais strangled Sainte-Beuve's soul. The first trophy of progressive Catholicism was the unbelief of the most intelligent man of his century.

> Hoy pululan vicios que son virtudes pervertidas por mentes tontas.

## §1115
> Que el cristianismo haya nacido como conspiración de proletarios sólo puede creerlo quien siente que la riqueza colmaría su corazón.

**Close:** That Christianity was born as a conspiracy of proletarians can be believed only by him who feels that wealth would fill his heart.

**Free:** Only someone who feels that wealth would satisfy his own heart can believe Christianity began as a conspiracy of proletarians.

`tags: Christianity, proletariat, wealth`

## §1116
> Si nuestras descripciones evitan la pathetic fallacy, estamos falsificando deliberadamente nuestra visión. La supuesta "objetividad" buscada es un producto más "subjetivo" que la introyección espontánea.

**Close:** If our descriptions avoid the pathetic fallacy, we are deliberately falsifying our vision. The supposed "objectivity" sought is a product more "subjective" than spontaneous introjection.

**Free:** If our descriptions avoid the pathetic fallacy altogether, we are deliberately falsifying our vision. The objectivity we seek that way is more subjective than spontaneous projection.

**Exegesis:**
The point is not a defense of sentimentality. It is a warning that scrubbed description may distort the world more violently than acknowledged participation does.

`tags: objectivity, description, pathetic-fallacy`

**Free:** Learning how to read eighteenth-century poetry is part of the passport into civilization.

## §1118

**Close:** Historical causality is graspable only post eventum, since the will obeys laws but chooses the one it fulfills.

**Free:** Historical causality can be grasped only after the fact, because the will obeys laws yet chooses which one it will fulfill.

`tags: history, causality, will`
