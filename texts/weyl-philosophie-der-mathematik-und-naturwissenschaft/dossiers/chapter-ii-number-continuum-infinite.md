# Chapter II - Number, Continuum, The Infinite

Status: promoted working dossier

Primary anchor: `weyl:math-02`

Source-local authority:
`sources/modern/weyl-philosophie-der-mathematik-und-naturwissenschaft/`

## Claim

Chapter II earns promotion because it converts a technical foundations story
into a general epistemological pattern: mathematics needs the infinite, but the
infinite cannot simply be given as an object. Weyl's chapter tests three ways of
handling that pressure.

- Set theory treats possible sequences, subsets, and properties as completed
  object-domains.
- Brouwerian intuitionism refuses the completed infinite and returns truth to
  construction, choice sequence, and intuition.
- Hilbertian formalism recovers classical mathematics by treating transfinite
  moves as rule-governed symbolic play whose first demand is consistency.

Weyl does not simply choose one of the three. The promoted result is sharper:
the need for totality is real, but must be satisfied symbolically. The
transcendent is represented; it is not directly seen.

## Anchor Ladder

`weyl:math-02:5` - Rational and complex numbers are relatively low-risk ideal
extensions. They show the pattern of creative definition: new ideal elements
are introduced to preserve formal laws.

`weyl:math-02:6` - The natural-number sequence is more dangerous because
continuation is open. Counting depends on temporal ordering, signs, and the
constructive possibility of always going on.

`weyl:math-02:7` - The continuum is the hard case. Atomism and infinitesimals
fail as foundations; limit theory works mathematically, but the intuitive
continuum still resists treatment as a rigid being made of completed parts.

`weyl:math-02:8` - Set theory gives analysis rigor by admitting the actual
infinite. Its philosophical cost is objectification: possible sequences,
subsets, and properties become closed aggregates of objects existing in
themselves.

`weyl:math-02:9` - Brouwer restores contact with construction. Existential
claims over the infinite require construction; the continuum is understood
through part-whole relation and becoming choice sequences rather than element
and set.

`weyl:math-02:10` - Hilbert's symbolic mathematics is not a return to evident
truth. It is a consistency rescue: formal signs are manipulated by rules, and
classical mathematics is licensed if contradiction can be excluded.

`weyl:math-02:11` - Weyl classifies the foundations positions
epistemologically: set theory as naive realism, Brouwer as idealism, Hilbertian
formalism as symbolic presentation of the transcendent. The chapter closes by
calling mathematics the science of the infinite.

## Why It Matters Here

This dossier gives the repo a disciplined way to use formalization without
collapsing into either anti-formalist suspicion or formalist triumphalism.
Weyl's chapter says that symbolic systems can be real knowledge instruments
without being direct possession of truth.

That matters for current repo pressure around:

- translation and commentary as non-identical but lawful transformations
- semiotics and signs that do not merely point at ready-made objects
- scientific models as theoretical formations rather than passive mirrors
- the relation between intuition, construction, and formal discipline

## Chapter III Pressure

The source-local chapter-III campaign strengthens rather than weakens this
dossier. Geometry shows how symbolic construction becomes objective in a more
specific way: through invariance under transformation groups, coordinate frames
whose arbitrariness is controlled, and physical fields that connect formal
structure back to matter.

The chapter-II claim should therefore be read with one added constraint:
symbolic access to totality is not enough by itself. In geometry and physics,
the symbolic system must also define the transformations under which its claims
remain invariant. Chapter III adds the bridge from symbolic discipline to
objectivity by invariance and then to physics through the metric field and
local-frame quantities `eᵢᵦ`.

## Rival Reading

A competent rival reading could say that the chapter is mainly a dated survey
of early twentieth-century foundational schools, and that its value should stay
inside history of mathematics rather than becoming a project-side philosophical
anchor.

That reading is not frivolous. The technical terrain changed after Weyl:
formal logic, model theory, proof theory, set theory, category theory, and
constructive mathematics all developed far beyond the 8th edition's frame.

The reason the dossier still earns promotion is that the chapter's reusable
claim is not a technical settlement. It is the epistemological pattern Weyl
extracts from the crisis: objectification, intuition, and formal symbolism are
different stances toward the passage from the given to the transcendent.

## Weakening Evidence

This dossier should be weakened or revised if later source-local work shows
that:

- Weyl abandons the symbolic-discipline position in the physics chapters.
- The physics chapters make consistency merely secondary and replace it with a
  different criterion for theoretical systems.
- Later passages show `Wissenschaft vom Unendlichen` to be rhetorical rather
  than structurally load-bearing.
- Cross-project reuse turns the thread into a generic "symbols are important"
  claim rather than the stricter Weylian claim about totality, transcendence,
  and discipline.
