# Section 09: The Sacred and the Dull (§211–250, pp. 48–57)

The aphorisms accelerate into a fusillade. Forty aphorisms in ten pages — the highest density yet. The moralist, the theologian, and the literary critic fire in rapid alternation. Several clusters emerge: theology stated as paradox (§230, §241, §242, §243, §244), the nature of writing (§225, §248), the defense of tradition against modernity (§235, §247), and the Bildung sequence (§234, §238, §239). Interspersed: some of his funniest one-liners.

---

## §214

> El moderno nunca se siente tan personal como cuando hace lo
> mismo que todos.

**Close:** The modern man never feels so personal as when he does the same thing as everyone else.

**Free:** Modern people never feel so individual as when they're doing exactly what everyone else does.

**Notes:**
- The paradox of consumer individualism in one sentence. "Express yourself" = buy the same things as everyone. "Be unique" = follow the same trends.
- *personal* — personal, individual, distinctive. The feeling of distinctiveness without the reality.

**Exegesis:**
Against the modern cult of self-expression. The more "personal" the choice (this brand, this aesthetic, this lifestyle), the more conformist it actually is — because the options were all pre-manufactured. Genuine individuality (§27, §32) is invisible to the person who possesses it. What feels personal is usually the market's latest offering. §5 (limiting the audience limits the capitulations) is the antidote: real individuality is won by withdrawal, not by choice.

`tags: modernity, conformity, individuality, irony`

## §219

> De los modernos sucedáneos de la religión probablemente el
> menos abyecto es el vicio.

**Close:** Of the modern substitutes for religion, probably the least abject is vice.

**Free:** Among modern replacements for religion, vice is probably the least degrading.

**Notes:**
- *sucedáneos* — substitutes, surrogates. Ersatz.
- The other substitutes (implied): politics, career, therapy, wellness, art-as-spirituality. Vice is less abject because it at least acknowledges *need* — the body screaming for something the soul has lost. The political zealot or the wellness consumer pretends no loss occurred.

**Exegesis:**
A ranking of modern apostasies by honesty. The drug addict knows he's looking for God in the wrong place. The activist thinks she's found a better God. The careerist doesn't know she's looking at all. Vice retains the structure of religious longing (need → search → temporary fulfillment → insufficiency → renewed need) while the "respectable" substitutes flatten the structure into secular satisfaction. Connects to §88 (vice becoming suburban parks — but here vice is *defended* against its even worse competitors).

`tags: religion, vice, modernity, substitutes`

## §221

> El que ignora que dos adjetivos contrarios califican simultáneamente
> todo objeto no debe hablar de nada.

**Close:** He who does not know that two contrary adjectives simultaneously qualify every object should not speak about anything.

**Free:** If you don't know that two opposite adjectives apply to everything at once, you shouldn't open your mouth.

**Notes:**
- A methodological command. Everything is simultaneously X and not-X: beautiful and ugly, true and false, good and bad — depending on level, angle, context. This is §2 (thought between symmetrical injustices), §41 (ambivalence of feelings), §145 (contrary theses complete each other), §179 (intelligence rests in tension) — all compressed into a *prohibition*: if you can't hold both adjectives at once, be silent.

`tags: contradiction, perception, method`

## §225

> Meditar es traducir un instante de lucidez en el idioma de
> una época y en el léxico de un gremio.

**Close:** To meditate is to translate an instant of lucidity into the idiom of an epoch and the lexicon of a guild.

**Free:** Meditation is translating a flash of clarity into the language of your time and the vocabulary of your trade.

**Notes:**
- *instante de lucidez* — "instant of lucidity." The raw moment of seeing. Pre-verbal, pre-conceptual.
- *idioma de una época... léxico de un gremio* — "idiom of an epoch... lexicon of a guild." Two layers of translation: first into the historical language available to you, then into the specialized vocabulary of your discipline.
- The close version preserves the Latin-derived architecture. The free version demystifies but loses the formality of *meditar*.

**Exegesis:**
The most important aphorism for the translation project's method. *All* intellectual work is translation — from the moment of lucidity (which has no language) into the double constraint of historical epoch and professional vocabulary. Gómez Dávila's *Escolios* translate his instants of lucidity into 20th-century Spanish and the lexicon of the Catholic reactionary moralist. Our translation translates them again — into 21st-century English and the lexicon of the bildung-2.0 project. Each translation is a further degradation of the original lucidity *and* a further specification that makes it accessible. Barrett's constructed-categories thesis applies: the target language doesn't just *express* the insight; it *shapes* it. Every translation is both a loss and a new perception.

`tags: meditation, translation, lucidity, epoch`

## §230

> Lo que no es religioso no es interesante.

**Close:** What is not religious is not interesting.

**Free:** If it isn't religious, it isn't interesting.

**Notes:**
- Seven words. Absolute. No qualification.
- *religioso* — religious. Not "spiritual," not "transcendent" — *religious*. The institutional, liturgical, doctrinal weight of the word is deliberate.
- Connects to §208 (the world is sacramental or dull). Same claim, different register. §208 is aesthetic (sacramental). §230 is intellectual (interesting).

**Exegesis:**
The most provocative aphorism since §45 (every end other than God dishonors us). On its face, absurd — mathematics is interesting, sex is interesting, cooking is interesting, none of these are "religious." But Gómez Dávila would say: they're interesting precisely insofar as they *are* religious — insofar as they open onto something that exceeds them. Mathematics is interesting when it reveals an order that didn't have to exist. Sex is interesting when it discloses the irreducible otherness of another person. Cooking is interesting when it becomes communion. Strip the religious dimension and each becomes merely technical: a problem to solve, a need to satisfy, a task to complete. Interesting and dull.

`tags: religion, interest, the-sacred`

## §233

> El hastío no es fruto de la posesión prolongada, sino del
> contacto fugaz con mil objetos.

**Close:** Boredom is not the fruit of prolonged possession, but of fleeting contact with a thousand objects.

**Free:** Boredom doesn't come from having the same thing too long — it comes from touching a thousand things briefly.

**Notes:**
- Against the consumer logic that boredom is caused by monotony. The opposite: boredom is caused by *variety without depth*. Scrolling. Channel-surfing. The infinite menu.
- *posesión prolongada* — "prolonged possession." The deep relationship with one thing. This is what *cures* boredom.

**Exegesis:**
The most prescient aphorism for the digital age, written in 1977. The infinite scroll is the perfection of "fleeting contact with a thousand objects." Attention deficit is not a pathology but the predictable result of a culture that offers everything briefly and nothing deeply. §209 (the sensual perfection of the instant) is the antidote — but the instant requires *presence*, not variety. You can't have the instant if you're already swiping to the next one.

`tags: boredom, possession, contact, modernity`

## §234

> La cultura no llenará jamás el ocio del trabajador, porque
> sólo es el trabajo del ocioso.

**Close:** Culture will never fill the leisure of the worker, because it is only the work of the leisured.

**Free:** Culture can never fill a worker's free time — it's only the occupation of those who are already free.

**Notes:**
- *ocio del trabajador* vs. *trabajo del ocioso* — the chiasmus is perfect: the worker's leisure / the leisured one's work. Culture is produced *by* leisure and requires leisure to be received.
- The democratization of culture fails because culture isn't a *content* you can distribute; it's a *practice* that requires a certain relationship to time.

**Exegesis:**
The Bildung aphorism, sequel to §161 (the book doesn't educate the one who reads it to be educated) and §59 (the cult of labor as a slave's philosophy). Culture — Bildung — requires *otium*: not empty free time but the positive condition of being free *for* contemplation. The worker's "leisure" is recovery from labor, not *otium*. Stuffing culture into that recovery time produces the consumer of culture (museums on Sunday, audiobooks during commutes) but not the *formed* person. Formation requires being a certain kind of person before the book arrives. Connects to the Schuon thread: tradition is living practice, not content delivery.

`tags: culture, leisure, work, Bildung`

## §235

> Rechazar todo lo que el mundo actual predica sería presuntuoso,
> si desde los hexámetros de Homero hasta los últimos versos de
> Yeats toda la literatura de Occidente no predicara lo contrario.

**Close:** To reject everything the present world preaches would be presumptuous, if from the hexameters of Homer to the last verses of Yeats all the literature of the West did not preach the contrary.

**Free:** Rejecting everything the modern world preaches would be arrogant — if the entire literature of the West, from Homer's hexameters to Yeats's last verses, didn't preach the opposite.

**Notes:**
- The most concrete defense of his reactionary position. It's not personal eccentricity — it's the *entire Western literary tradition* against the modern consensus.
- *hexámetros de Homero... últimos versos de Yeats* — the span is deliberate. From the beginning of Western literature to its last great traditionalist poet. Everything in between (Sophocles, Virgil, Dante, Shakespeare, Goethe, Baudelaire) agrees with Gómez Dávila, not with the modern world.

**Exegesis:**
A one-sentence justification for the entire bildung-2.0 project. The project's bet is the same as Gómez Dávila's: that the Western literary-philosophical tradition, taken as a whole, is a more reliable guide to reality than the modern consensus. Homer, Jünger, Dick — each in his own way is "preaching the contrary" to what the current world assumes. The "ancient letters" (§181) are the cure for the "modern itch" precisely because they represent an accumulated dissent so massive that no single modern individual needs to be "arrogant" to join it.

`tags: tradition, modernity, literature, rejection`

## §236

> La ortodoxia es la tensión entre dos herejías.

**Close:** Orthodoxy is the tension between two heresies.

**Free:** Orthodoxy is the tension between two heresies.

**Notes:**
- Untranslatable because untouchable. Both versions are identical.
- The theological version of §179 (intelligence rests in the tension of contraries). Orthodoxy is not a *position* but a *balance* — the point of equilibrium between opposing errors. Move toward either pole and you fall into heresy.

**Exegesis:**
Chesterton said something similar ("Orthodoxy is a balance, not a compromise"), but Gómez Dávila's version is more precise. A compromise *splits the difference*; a tension *holds both poles*. The orthodox position on Christ (fully human, fully divine) is not a compromise between Arianism and Docetism but the *active tension* between them. This applies beyond theology: the orthodoxy of any vital tradition is the balance between its extremes, held in place by the *tension*, not by the resolution.

`tags: orthodoxy, heresy, tension`

## §238

> Educar al hombre es impedirle la "libre expresión de su personalidad".

**Close:** To educate man is to prevent him from "freely expressing his personality."

**Free:** Education means stopping people from "freely expressing their personality."

**Notes:**
- The scare quotes on "libre expresión de su personalidad" are the argument. The phrase is a slogan; education is the antidote to it.
- Connects to §200 (the individual becomes a person only through a rigid norm freely met). "Free expression" without prior formation produces not a person but a mass man who mistakes his impulses for his personality.

**Exegesis:**
The anti-Rousseau aphorism. Rousseau: the child is born free and education corrupts. Gómez Dávila: the child is born raw material and education *forms*. "Personality" that hasn't been shaped by encounter with a norm that exceeds it isn't personality — it's appetite in costume. This is *Bildung* in its strictest sense: formation through discipline, not through liberation. The bildung-2.0 project itself enacts this: you don't "freely express" your response to Gómez Dávila; you sit with his sentence until it forms you. The discipline is the freedom.

`tags: education, personality, Bildung, formation`

## §240

> Toda afirmación que no roe una secreta llaga es una mera impertinencia.

**Close:** Every affirmation that does not gnaw at a secret wound is a mere impertinence.

**Free:** Any claim that doesn't bite a hidden wound is just an impertinence.

**Notes:**
- *roe* — gnaws, nibbles. The truth must *gnaw* — it must work on something raw and private.
- *secreta llaga* — "secret wound." Every person carries one. The serious statement addresses it. Everything else is chatter.

**Exegesis:**
The criterion of seriousness. A philosophical statement is serious only if it touches the reader's wound — the wound they carry privately, the one they don't discuss. Most philosophy is "mere impertinence" because it addresses problems the reader doesn't actually have. The *Escolios* work because they gnaw: they touch pride, self-deception, spiritual poverty, the loss of meaning, the fear of death. If they didn't touch your wound, they'd be mere cleverness.

`tags: truth, wound, seriousness`

## §241

> Dios es la condición trascendental de la absurdidad del universo.

**Close:** God is the transcendental condition of the absurdity of the universe.

**Free:** God is the transcendental condition for the universe being absurd.

**Notes:**
- Kantian language (*condición trascendental* — transcendental condition, i.e. that which makes experience of X possible) applied to Camus's territory (the absurd).
- The argument: without God, the universe isn't absurd — it's just *nothing*. For it to be *absurd* (to have the quality of seeming like it should make sense but not making sense), there must be *something* in relation to which it fails. That something is God. The absurd presupposes the meaningful.

**Exegesis:**
The deepest aphorism since §113 (everything is trivial without a metaphysical adventure). Camus said the universe is absurd. Gómez Dávila says: *if* the universe is absurd, *then* God exists — because absurdity is a relational property that requires a standard of meaning to measure against. A truly godless universe wouldn't be absurd. It would be *nothing at all* — not even boring (§208). Absurdity is a *theological* experience: the sense that things *should* make sense, which is itself evidence of a standard of sense they're failing to meet.

`tags: God, absurdity, transcendence`

## §242

> Dios es la substancia de lo que amamos.

**Close:** God is the substance of what we love.

**Free:** God is what we love, underneath everything we love.

**Notes:**
- Seven words. The most Augustinian aphorism yet.
- *substancia* — substance, in the philosophical sense: that which underlies and supports the qualities. Not the *object* of what we love but the *ground*.
- The close version is perfect. The free version unpacks but loses the compression.

**Exegesis:**
When you love a person, a landscape, a piece of music — what you actually love, underneath the particular, is God. Not instead of the particular (that would be Platonism) but *in* the particular (that would be sacramentalism). The beloved is real, irreducible, particular (§21, §27, §32) — and the substance of that irreducible particularity is God. This is §208 (the world is sacramental) stated ontologically: the sacrament works because God is the substance of what the sign signifies.

`tags: God, love, substance`

## §244

> La sabiduría se reduce a no enseñarle a Dios cómo se deben
> hacer las cosas.

**Close:** Wisdom reduces to not teaching God how things ought to be done.

**Free:** Wisdom comes down to not telling God how to run things.

**Notes:**
- *se reduce a* — "reduces to," "comes down to." The entire content of wisdom in one negative command.
- The free version's colloquial "how to run things" captures the absurdity of the pretension better than the close version's formal register.

**Exegesis:**
Connects to §15 (maturity begins when we stop feeling responsible for the world) and §45 (every end other than God dishonors us). The wise person has *one* rule: don't presume to know better than God. This isn't fatalism — it's the acknowledgment that our understanding of how things ought to be is always partial, always contaminated by our own desires, and always less comprehensive than the divine vantage point we can sense but not inhabit (§145: only God knows how contrary theses complete each other).

`tags: wisdom, God, humility`

## §245

> Algo divino aflora en el momento que precede el triunfo y en
> el que sigue al fracaso.

**Close:** Something divine surfaces in the moment that precedes triumph and in the moment that follows failure.

**Free:** Something divine shows itself in the instant before victory and the instant after defeat.

**Notes:**
- *aflora* — surfaces, comes to light, outcrops (geological). The divine is *below* and *breaks through* at specific moments.
- Two moments: *before* triumph (when the outcome is uncertain, when everything is at stake) and *after* failure (when the pretensions have been stripped away). Not *during* triumph (that's pride) and not *during* failure (that's despair).
- The asymmetry is crucial: the divine is in the *approach* to success and the *aftermath* of defeat. Both are moments of nakedness — before the ego inflates or after it has been punctured.

**Exegesis:**
A precise phenomenology of the sacred in human experience. The moment before winning the race, the moment after losing the beloved — these are when the carapace of the self cracks and something from beyond shows through. This is why both the warrior-about-to-fight and the saint-who-has-lost are closer to God than the comfortably successful. Connects to §31 (intelligent narration of defeat as the vanquished's subtle victory) and §210 (the only patent of nobility is defeat).

`tags: divinity, triumph, failure, time`

## §247

> La literatura toda es contemporánea para el lector que sabe leer.

**Close:** All literature is contemporary for the reader who knows how to read.

**Free:** For the reader who can read, all literature is contemporary.

**Notes:**
- *sabe leer* — "knows how to read." Not literacy but *competence* — the trained attention that makes Homer as present as this morning's newspaper.
- This is the §162 (taste for the obsolete) principle extended: it's not that old literature is surprisingly good; it's that *there's no such thing as old literature* for the formed reader. Temporal distance is an illusion of bad reading.

**Exegesis:**
The motto of the entire project. If all literature is contemporary for the reader who knows how to read, then translating Gómez Dávila (1977), Jünger (1959), and navigating Dick (1974-82) are not acts of historical recovery but acts of *present encounter*. The texts are alive now. The project doesn't rescue them from the past; it meets them where they already are — which is here.

`tags: literature, reading, contemporaneity`

## §249

> Tan repetidas veces han enterrado a la metafísica que hay
> que juzgarla inmortal.

**Close:** Metaphysics has been buried so many times that one must judge it immortal.

**Free:** They've buried metaphysics so often by now we should assume it's immortal.

**Notes:**
- Positivism buried it. Kant buried it. Heidegger buried it. Wittgenstein buried it. Carnap buried it. Rorty buried it. Cognitive science buried it. And yet.
- The humor is the argument: anything that survives that many funerals isn't dead. It's just unpopular.

## §250

> Un gran amor es una sensualidad bien ordenada.

**Close:** A great love is a well-ordered sensuality.

**Free:** Great love is sensuality in good order.

**Notes:**
- *sensualidad bien ordenada* — not *purified* sensuality, not *sublimated* sensuality — *well-ordered* sensuality. The senses are not suppressed but *arranged*. The lover doesn't transcend desire; he gives it architecture.
- Connects to §44 (a voluptuous presence communicating its splendor), §209 (the sensual perfection of the instant), and now this: the *ordering* of the sensual, not its elimination, is what produces love.

**Exegesis:**
The closing aphorism of this section returns to the body — after a stretch of intense theology (§241, §242, §244), he grounds everything in sensuality. This is anti-Manichean at the deepest level: love is not the soul escaping the body but the body finding its form. A kiss placed well. A sentence shaped right. The *Escolios* themselves as well-ordered sensuality: each one a sensual object (it has sound, rhythm, weight — cf. §33) given its proper form by syntax (§140). The book is an act of love in this exact sense.

`tags: love, sensuality, order`
