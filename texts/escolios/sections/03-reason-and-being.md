# Section 03: Reason and Being (§36–46, pp. 16–17)

The metaphysical core. Having laid out his epistemology (how we know) and his individualism (what matters), Gómez Dávila now turns to the deep architecture: what is truth, what is reason, what is being itself. This is his densest stretch so far — several aphorisms here require slow digestion.

---

## §36

> Los errores son proletarios insurrectos. Verdades refractarias
> a su oficio y su rango.
> Una proposición es falsa cuando obstruye proposiciones más veraces.

**Close:** Errors are insurgent proletarians. Truths that are refractory to their office and their rank. A proposition is false when it obstructs more truthful propositions.

**Free:** Errors are truths in revolt — refusing their proper station and rank. A proposition is false when it blocks more truthful ones.

**Notes:**
- *proletarios insurrectos* — "insurgent proletarians." Errors aren't the *opposite* of truth — they're truths that have overstepped their place. A partial truth that claims to be the whole truth is an error.
- *refractarias a su oficio y su rango* — "refractory to their office and rank." OCR had "Iractarias" — corrected to *refractarias* (stubborn, resistant). The feudal metaphor: truths have a proper rank; errors are truths that refuse theirs.
- The second sentence gives the mechanism: falsehood is *obstruction*, not opposition. A proposition is false not because it contradicts reality but because it blocks a fuller view.

**Exegesis:**
A hierarchical theory of truth. Not relativism (all truths equal) and not absolutism (one truth, everything else false). Truths have *degrees* and *stations*. The error isn't in the proposition itself but in its pretension to a rank above its own. "All men are created equal" is true at one rank; treated as a metaphysical absolute, it obstructs deeper truths about human difference. This is Gómez Dávila's version of Aquinas's analogia entis applied to epistemology.

`tags: truth, error, hierarchy`

## §37

> Las verdades no están en la circunferencia de un círculo
> cuyo centro es el hombre.
> Las verdades se levantan en parajes fragosos que el hombre
> recorre siguiendo los meandros de una senda sinuosa que
> las revela, las oculta, finalmente las ostenta o las esconde.

**Close:** Truths do not lie on the circumference of a circle whose center is man. Truths rise up in rugged places that man traverses following the meanders of a sinuous path that reveals them, hides them, finally displays them or conceals them.

**Free:** Truths don't sit on the rim of a circle centered on man. They stand in rough terrain that we cross by following a winding trail — a path that reveals them, hides them, at last flaunts them or buries them.

**Notes:**
- *parajes fragosos* — "rugged/craggy places." Landscape as epistemology. Truth is a mountain trail, not a geometry problem.
- *las revela, las oculta, finalmente las ostenta o las esconde* — the fourfold verb sequence (reveals, hides, displays, conceals) enacts the path it describes. The rhythm in Spanish is incantatory.
- The close version preserves "meanders of a sinuous path" — elegant but heavy in English. The free version flattens to "winding trail" — loses the Latinate music but reads better.

**Exegesis:**
Against the Enlightenment model where man stands at the center and radiates reason outward in all directions equally. Truth isn't equidistant from us in every direction — it's scattered across rough terrain, sometimes visible, sometimes not, depending on where you stand on the path. This is anti-systematic: you can't survey truth from above, only encounter it along the way. A walking epistemology.

`tags: truth, epistemology, anti-anthropocentrism`

## §38

> Sin actos intencionales los hechos no son ni necesarios, ni casuales.
> Sin intenciones, el mundo es bloque de sucesiones brutas.
> La intención funda la casualidad cuando las cosas la sorprenden
> y la causalidad cuando no la desconciertan.
> La razón establece funciones, la intención necesidad y contingencia.

**Close:** Without intentional acts, facts are neither necessary nor contingent. Without intentions, the world is a block of brute successions. Intention founds contingency when things surprise it and causality when they do not disconcert it. Reason establishes functions; intention establishes necessity and contingency.

**Free:** Without intentional acts, nothing is necessary or accidental. Without intentions, the world is just a slab of brute sequence. Intention creates chance when things surprise it, and causality when they don't. Reason finds functions; intention finds necessity and contingency.

**Notes:**
- His most phenomenological aphorism yet. Echoes Husserl and Brentano: intentionality (directedness of consciousness) is what structures raw experience into meaningful categories.
- *bloque de sucesiones brutas* — "block of brute successions." Without a mind directed at the world, there's no difference between the necessary and the accidental — just one thing after another.
- *casualidad / causalidad* — chance / causality. The near-homonymy in Spanish (one letter apart) is part of the argument: they're born from the same source (intention) and differentiated only by surprise.

**Exegesis:**
The densest aphorism so far. He's arguing that the categories we use to understand the world (necessary/contingent, cause/effect) aren't features of raw reality — they're products of intentional consciousness encountering reality. Not idealism (the world isn't mind-dependent) but something like critical realism: the world is real, but its *structure* only appears to a mind that engages it. Reason does the sorting; intention does the *founding*.

`tags: intention, causality, phenomenology`

## §39

> El ser rezuma por todos los poros del mundo.

**Close:** Being oozes through every pore of the world.

**Free:** Being seeps from every pore of the world.

**Notes:**
- *rezuma* — oozes, seeps, exudes. A visceral, almost disgusting word for a metaphysical claim. Being isn't hidden behind the world or above it — it *sweats* through it.
- Seven words. After the density of §38, this lands like a deep breath.
- Close version: "oozes" (more grotesque, truer to *rezuma*). Free version: "seeps" (gentler, less vivid).

**Exegesis:**
Pure metaphysical realism, stated with the body's vocabulary. Against every form of dualism that puts being elsewhere — in a Platonic heaven, in a Kantian noumenon, in a future utopia. Being is *here*, in the pores, in the sweat of things. The most Thomistic aphorism yet: *esse* (being/existence) is not a distant abstraction but the most intimate actuality of every thing.

`tags: being, metaphysics`

## §40

> La razón es un acto del espíritu que analiza un acto espiritual previo.
> La razón no engendra, sino educa lo engendrado.

**Close:** Reason is an act of the spirit that analyzes a prior spiritual act. Reason does not engender, but educates what has been engendered.

**Free:** Reason is the mind analyzing its own prior act. Reason doesn't create — it raises what has already been born.

**Notes:**
- *engendra... educa lo engendrado* — begets... educates the begotten. The parental metaphor: something else gives birth to understanding; reason is the tutor, not the parent.
- *acto espiritual previo* — "prior spiritual act." Intuition? Perception? Faith? He doesn't say. Reason always arrives *second*.
- The free version compresses heavily. "Raises what has already been born" captures the parenting metaphor but loses the formal precision of "educates the engendered."

**Exegesis:**
Reason is secondary. This is anti-Cartesian at the root: *cogito* doesn't come first. Something pre-rational — an encounter, an intuition, an act of attention — comes first. Reason then works on that material. This explains why Gómez Dávila trusts the aphorism over the treatise: the aphorism captures the *prior act*; the treatise is reason's post-hoc education of it.

`tags: reason, spirit, method`

## §41

> Al corregir la natural ambivalencia de los sentimientos,
> la razón los corrompe y mutila el universo.
> Quien suprime las secretas connivencias entre sus amores
> y sus odios, se vuelve un fanático que camina entre esquemas.

**Close:** By correcting the natural ambivalence of feelings, reason corrupts them and mutilates the universe. Whoever suppresses the secret collusions between his loves and his hatreds becomes a fanatic who walks among schemas.

**Free:** When reason corrects the natural ambivalence of feelings, it corrupts them — and mutilates the universe. Suppress the secret alliances between your loves and your hatreds, and you become a fanatic walking through abstractions.

**Notes:**
- *connivencias* — collusions, connivances. Loves and hatreds are secretly in league with each other. They know something reason doesn't.
- *esquemas* — schemas, diagrams, outlines. The fanatic's world is a blueprint, not a landscape.
- This is the emotional companion to §2 (thought progresses between symmetrical injustices). There, thought needed opposing errors; here, feeling needs opposing passions.

**Exegesis:**
Against emotional hygiene, against therapeutic culture, against every attempt to "resolve" ambivalence. Your love for something and your hatred of it are not contradictions to be resolved but a stereoscopic vision that sees depth. Flatten the ambivalence and you get ideology — a flat world of schemas where everything is either loved or hated, never both. The fanatic is the person who has successfully purified his feelings.

`tags: reason, feeling, fanaticism`

## §42

> En la coherencia de ciertos sistemas, una visión se articula;
> otras resultan de la mera inercia de una idea.

**Close:** In the coherence of certain systems, a vision articulates itself; others result from the mere inertia of an idea.

**Free:** Some philosophical systems are coherent because a vision holds them together. Others are coherent only from the inertia of a single idea.

**Notes:**
- Two kinds of systematic thought: the genuine (where a living vision organizes the parts) and the mechanical (where one idea just keeps rolling).
- *inercia* — inertia. A physics metaphor: an idea in motion stays in motion, producing "consequences" that are really just momentum.
- The free version makes the implicit contrast explicit. The close version preserves the compression.

**Exegesis:**
His criterion for distinguishing real philosophy from scholastic machinery. Hegel: inertia of the dialectic. Aquinas: a living vision. Marx: inertia of class struggle. Nietzsche: a living vision. The test isn't coherence (both pass) but what *kind* of coherence — organic or mechanical.

`tags: philosophy, systems, vision`

## §43

> La ética nace de la conjunción de circunstancias empíricas
> con la norma trascendente que las circunstancias revelan.

**Close:** Ethics is born from the conjunction of empirical circumstances with the transcendent norm that the circumstances reveal.

**Free:** Ethics arises where empirical circumstances meet the transcendent norm those circumstances reveal.

**Notes:**
- Neither pure deontology (norm from above) nor pure consequentialism (norm from below). The norm is *transcendent* but only *visible* through circumstances.
- *revelan* — reveal. The circumstances don't create the norm; they *disclose* it. The norm was always there.

**Exegesis:**
His most concise statement of natural law theory. The moral law exists independently of circumstances, but you can't access it except *through* circumstances. This is why casuistry (case-by-case moral reasoning) isn't the enemy of principle but its necessary mode of expression. Ethics without cases is empty; cases without ethics are blind. A Thomistic epistemology applied to morals.

`tags: ethics, transcendence, empiricism`

## §44

> Una presencia voluptuosa comunica su esplendor sensual a toda cosa.

**Close:** A voluptuous presence communicates its sensual splendor to every thing.

**Free:** A voluptuous presence lends its sensual radiance to everything around it.

**Notes:**
- *voluptuosa... esplendor sensual* — he's not being coy. This is about beauty's capacity to transform perception. In the presence of the beautiful, everything becomes more vivid.
- One sentence, dropped between ethics (§43) and theology (§45). The sensual as a bridge between moral law and God.

**Exegesis:**
A strange aphorism to find between a statement on ethics and a statement on God. But for Gómez Dávila, beauty is the mediator — the sensual isn't the enemy of the sacred but its ambassador. The voluptuous presence (a person? a landscape? a work of art?) doesn't just please; it *reveals*, the way circumstances reveal the norm in §43. Beauty is epistemological.

`tags: beauty, sensuality, presence`

## §45

> Todo fin diferente de Dios nos deshonra.

**Close:** Every end other than God dishonors us.

**Free:** Any goal that isn't God degrades us.

**Notes:**
- Six words. Absolute. No qualification, no "perhaps."
- *deshonra* — dishonors, disgraces. Not "disappoints" or "fails" — *dishonors*. The claim is about dignity, not satisfaction.
- Close version: "dishonors" (preserves the honor/shame register). Free version: "degrades" (stronger, more physical).

**Exegesis:**
The theological absolute that anchors everything else. Liberty (§24) is a means — this is the end. The empty mirrors of humanism (§71, upcoming) reflect nothing because they lack this. After the sensual beauty of §44, this lands like a bell: beauty points somewhere, and what it points to is God. Not an argument but a declaration — the reactionary's first and last conviction.

`tags: God, purpose, honor`

## §46

> La inteligencia no aspira a liberarse, sino a someterse.
> La verdad es el resplandor de la necesidad.

**Close:** Intelligence does not aspire to free itself, but to submit. Truth is the splendor of necessity.

**Free:** The mind doesn't want freedom — it wants to submit. Truth is necessity's radiance.

**Notes:**
- *someterse* — to submit, to subject oneself. Against the Enlightenment narrative of reason as liberation. Intelligence *wants* to be bound — by truth, by reality, by what is.
- *resplandor de la necesidad* — "splendor/radiance of necessity." When you see that something *must* be so, the recognition glows. That glow is truth.
- This pairs with §6 (the "dry joy" of grasping meaning). Both describe truth as an experience that is simultaneously binding and beautiful.

**Exegesis:**
The most counter-Enlightenment sentence in the Escolios so far. Freedom of thought is not the goal; *capture* by truth is the goal. The mind that frees itself from all constraint is not liberated but orphaned. Truth isn't what we choose but what seizes us. "The splendor of necessity" — one of his most luminous phrases — suggests that the deepest beauty is in seeing that things could not be otherwise.

`tags: intelligence, truth, submission`
