# Section 04: Politics and the Reactionary (§47–72, pp. 18–22)

Gómez Dávila's most provocative register. He turns from metaphysics to the political arena — democracy, revolution, bourgeoisie, Marx, socialism — and every sentence is designed to detonate. But the provocation has a structure: he's not defending any existing regime but diagnosing what all modern political arrangements get wrong.

---

## §47

> Sólo la libertad limita las abusivas intervenciones de la ignorancia.
> La política es la ciencia de las estructuras sociales adecuadas
> a la convivencia de seres ignorantes.

**Close:** Only freedom limits the abusive interventions of ignorance. Politics is the science of social structures adequate for the coexistence of ignorant beings.

**Free:** Only liberty checks the overreach of ignorance. Politics is the science of arranging society so that ignorant beings can live together.

**Notes:**
- *seres ignorantes* — "ignorant beings." Not *stupid* — *ignorant*. We are all ignorant about most things. Political structures must account for this.
- The argument: if no one knows enough to run things, freedom is the safeguard. Central planning presumes knowledge no one has. A Hayekian insight in a pre-Hayek idiom.

**Exegesis:**
His most "liberal" (in the classical sense) moment. Freedom isn't a value here — it's an engineering solution to the problem of universal ignorance. This connects to §24 (freedom is a means): the end it serves is protecting us from each other's confident stupidity. Also connects to §26 (epistemology humbles science) — political humility follows from epistemic humility.

`tags: liberty, politics, ignorance`

---

## §48

> Una "sociedad ideal" sería el cementerio de la grandeza humana.

**Close:** An "ideal society" would be the cemetery of human greatness.

**Free:** A perfect society would be where human greatness goes to die.

**Notes:**
- *cementerio* — cemetery. Not "end" or "death" — a place where the dead are *kept*. Greatness wouldn't disappear; it would be entombed, preserved as a monument to what is no longer permitted.
- The scare quotes around "sociedad ideal" are part of the argument.
- Free version restructures for punch; close version preserves the metaphor's specificity.

**Exegesis:**
Utopia eliminates the conditions for greatness: struggle, inequality, risk, failure. If everyone is comfortable, no one needs to be heroic. If everything is arranged, nothing needs to be created. This is Nietzsche's "last man" in one sentence — and Gómez Dávila's deepest objection to progressivism: not that it fails, but that its success would be catastrophic.

`tags: utopia, greatness, politics`

## §49

> Después de toda revolución el revolucionario enseña que
> la revolución verdadera será la revolución de mañana.
> El revolucionario explica que un miserable traicionó
> la revolución de ayer.

**Close:** After every revolution the revolutionary teaches that the true revolution will be tomorrow's revolution. The revolutionary explains that a wretch betrayed yesterday's revolution.

**Free:** After every revolution, the revolutionary announces that the *real* revolution is still coming — tomorrow. And that some scoundrel betrayed yesterday's.

**Notes:**
- The structure is a perpetual motion machine: revolution → betrayal → next revolution → betrayal → ... The cycle never ends because the promise is unfulfillable.
- *miserable* — wretch, scoundrel. Always a personal betrayal, never a structural failure.
- Free version adds italics on *real* to capture the emphasis implicit in *verdadera*.

**Exegesis:**
The Trotskyist pattern: "permanent revolution" as a confession that revolution never delivers. Stalin betrayed Lenin, Thermidor betrayed Robespierre, the bureaucrats betrayed Mao. The explanation is always personal treachery, never the possibility that the project itself is incoherent. Gómez Dávila sees this as theological: revolution is a secular eschatology, and its perpetual deferral is the proof that it borrowed its structure from religion without its content.

`tags: revolution, politics, illusion`

## §50

> Si Sócrates realmente nada sabe, ¿por qué no acepta
> meramente lo que su interlocutor propone?
> ¿Espera, tal vez, que la verdad nazca de la congruencia de caprichos?
> ¿Creerá Sócrates, quizá, que el "bien" consiste en lo que
> los votantes unánimemente aprueban?, ¿será Sócrates demócrata?
> ¡No!
> Como todo reaccionario, Sócrates sabe que en una democracia
> no es lícito enseñar. El demócrata necesita creer que inventa
> lo que le sugieren.

**Close:** If Socrates truly knows nothing, why doesn't he merely accept what his interlocutor proposes? Does he perhaps expect truth to be born from the congruence of whims? Does Socrates perhaps believe that the "good" consists of what voters unanimously approve? — is Socrates a democrat? No! Like every reactionary, Socrates knows that in a democracy it is not permissible to teach. The democrat needs to believe he invents what is suggested to him.

**Free:** If Socrates really knows nothing, why not just agree with whoever's talking? Does he expect truth to emerge from matching whims? Does he think the "good" is whatever the voters unanimously approve? Is Socrates a democrat? No! Like every reactionary, Socrates knows you can't teach in a democracy. The democrat must believe he invented what you whispered to him.

**Notes:**
- The longest aphorism so far — almost a miniature essay. The rhetorical questions build toward the thunderclap: *¡No!*
- *como todo reaccionario* — "like every reactionary." Gómez Dávila claims Socrates for the reaction. The Socratic method isn't democratic dialogue; it's aristocratic pedagogy disguised as inquiry.
- *inventa lo que le sugieren* — "invents what is suggested to him." The deepest insight: democratic man's vanity requires that he believe every idea is his own. You can only teach him by making him think he's teaching himself.

**Exegesis:**
His most extended political aphorism and perhaps his most audacious claim: Socrates as reactionary. The argument: if Socrates genuinely knew nothing, he'd be a relativist who accepts whatever anyone says. But he doesn't — he keeps pushing toward truth, which means he *does* know something, or at least knows the direction. This makes him an aristocrat of the intellect operating in democratic disguise. The Socratic method is a concession to democratic vanity: you can't *tell* the democrat anything; you have to make him "discover" it.

`tags: Socrates, democracy, teaching, reactionary`

## §51

> Los parlamentos democráticos no son recintos donde se discute,
> sino donde el absolutismo popular registra sus edictos.

**Close:** Democratic parliaments are not chambers where matters are discussed, but where popular absolutism registers its edicts.

**Free:** Democratic parliaments aren't places for debate — they're where popular absolutism files its decrees.

**Notes:**
- *absolutismo popular* — "popular absolutism." The democratization of absolute power. The mob-king.
- *registra sus edictos* — "registers its edicts." Not even *issues* — *registers*. The decisions are already made; parliament is the notary.

**Exegesis:**
Carl Schmitt meets Tocqueville. The democratic parliament's function is not deliberation but ratification. Public opinion has already decided; the parliament stamps it. This is darker than mere cynicism — it's a structural claim about mass democracy: sovereignty doesn't divide when you expand the franchise; it just changes who's absolute.

`tags: democracy, parliament, absolutism`

## §52

> El burgués entrega el poder para salvar el dinero; después
> entrega el dinero para salvar el pellejo; y finalmente lo ahorcan.

**Close:** The bourgeois surrenders power to save his money; then surrenders his money to save his skin; and finally they hang him.

**Free:** The bourgeois gives up power to keep his money, then gives up his money to save his neck — and in the end they hang him anyway.

**Notes:**
- *pellejo* — skin, hide. Colloquial: "save one's skin." Cruder than *vida* (life) — it reduces the bourgeois to an animal hiding in its skin.
- Three stages of surrender, three semicolons, one inevitable conclusion. The rhythm is a countdown.
- Both versions are close — the Spanish is already almost English in its bluntness.

**Exegesis:**
A miniature philosophy of history as bourgeois capitulation. The sequence: political power → economic power → physical survival → death. Each concession is "reasonable" in the moment and fatal in the aggregate. This is Gómez Dávila's portrait of the West's relationship to totalitarianism: always willing to trade the next thing for survival, never understanding that the endpoint is the same.

`tags: bourgeoisie, politics, history`

---

## §53

> Burguesía es todo conjunto de individuos inconformes con lo
> que tienen y satisfechos de lo que son.

**Close:** The bourgeoisie is any group of individuals dissatisfied with what they have and satisfied with what they are.

**Free:** Bourgeois: dissatisfied with what they have, satisfied with what they are.

**Notes:**
- A *definition*, not a description. And not economic: *todo conjunto de individuos* — "any group." You can be bourgeois in a palace or a commune.
- The chiasmus (have/are) is the argument: the bourgeois wants more *stuff* but no more *soul*. Material ambition, spiritual complacency.
- The free version strips to the chiasmus alone. Punchier but loses *todo conjunto* — the universality of the definition.

**Exegesis:**
This is why Marxists are bourgeois (§54, next): they want to redistribute wealth but never question their own spiritual poverty. The aristocrat is the opposite: satisfied with what he has (or indifferent to it), dissatisfied with what he is. The reactionary project is essentially *anti-bourgeois* in this sense — not anti-wealth but anti-complacency.

`tags: bourgeoisie, self-satisfaction`

## §54

> Los marxistas definen económicamente a la burguesía para
> ocultarnos que pertenecen a ella.

**Close:** Marxists define the bourgeoisie economically in order to hide from us that they belong to it.

**Free:** Marxists define the bourgeoisie in economic terms so we won't notice they're members.

**Notes:**
- The jab lands because of §53: if bourgeoisie is spiritual complacency rather than economic class, then the Marxist — materially ambitious, spiritually smug — is the paradigmatic bourgeois.
- *ocultarnos* — "to hide from us." Not "to hide from themselves" — they know.

**Exegesis:**
A devastating one-two with §53. First redefine bourgeoisie spiritually, then point out that the accusers fit the definition. The Marxist critique of the bourgeoisie is itself a bourgeois operation — it keeps the argument on economic terrain (where the Marxist can claim distance) and away from spiritual terrain (where the resemblance is obvious).

`tags: marxism, bourgeoisie, hypocrisy`

## §55

> Sólo sus enemigos saben estimar la grandeza del comunismo.
> La tesis marxista difama tanto los motivos del comunista
> como los de su adversario.

**Close:** Only its enemies know how to appreciate the greatness of communism. The Marxist thesis defames the motives of the communist as much as those of his adversary.

**Free:** Only its enemies can appreciate communism's greatness. Marxist theory slanders the communist's motives no less than his opponent's.

**Notes:**
- *estimar la grandeza* — "appreciate the greatness." He's not being ironic. Gómez Dávila genuinely respects the communist's commitment — it's the theory he attacks.
- The second sentence: Marxism reduces all motivation to economic interest, which means the communist's own idealism is also "really" just class interest. The theory insults its own adherents.

**Exegesis:**
One of his most generous political aphorisms. The communist before victory (§57) is admirable because he sacrifices for a vision. But Marxist *theory* — which says all action is determined by material conditions — can't account for that sacrifice without reducing it to self-interest. The enemy (Gómez Dávila) can honor the communist's idealism precisely because the enemy's philosophy *allows for* idealism. Marxism can't.

`tags: communism, marxism, greatness`

## §56

> Quienes disculpan su abyección pretendiéndose "víctimas de
> las circunstancias" son socialistas doctrinarios.
> El socialismo es la filosofía de la culpabilidad ajena.

**Close:** Those who excuse their abjection by claiming to be "victims of circumstances" are doctrinaire socialists. Socialism is the philosophy of other people's guilt.

**Free:** Anyone who excuses their own wretchedness by calling themselves a "victim of circumstances" is a doctrinaire socialist. Socialism is the philosophy of someone else's fault.

**Notes:**
- *abyección* — abjection, degradation. Not misfortune — moral degradation. The claim isn't that suffering is one's own fault but that *moral failure* is.
- *culpabilidad ajena* — "other people's guilt/culpability." Four words that summarize his view of the entire socialist tradition.
- The scare quotes around "víctimas de las circunstancias" are doing heavy work.

**Exegesis:**
The philosophical core of his anti-socialism. Socialism, for Gómez Dávila, isn't primarily an economic program — it's a *moral psychology*: the refusal to accept personal responsibility by externalizing blame to "the system," "the oppressors," "the circumstances." This is linked to §13 (collective enterprises as moral evasion) and anticipates modern critiques of victimhood culture — though from a much harsher vantage point.

`tags: socialism, responsibility, blame`

## §57

> El militante comunista antes de su victoria merece el mayor respeto.
> Después no es más que un burgués atareado.

**Close:** The communist militant before his victory deserves the greatest respect. Afterward he is no more than a busy bourgeois.

**Free:** The communist militant before victory deserves the highest respect. After victory, he's just a busy bourgeois.

**Notes:**
- *burgués atareado* — "busy bourgeois." The devastating final image: the revolutionary who wins becomes a bureaucrat. All the fire, all the sacrifice, and the result is... meetings.
- The pairing with §55: the enemy appreciates the communist *more* than Marxism does, and this is why — the theory can't explain the pre-victory heroism, and victory destroys it.

**Exegesis:**
The arc of every revolution: idealism → victory → bureaucracy. Lenin in the Finland Station, Lenin chairing committees. Che in the Sierra Maestra, Che running the national bank. The transformation is inevitable because revolution promises transcendence but can only deliver administration. The "busy bourgeois" is the saddest figure in Gómez Dávila's political bestiary.

`tags: communism, bourgeoisie, irony`

## §58

> Quienes defienden lo existente luchan por algo concreto:
> un privilegio, una estructura social, un bien encarnado;
> en contra, quien batalla por un programa abstracto puede
> creer que defiende lo universal.
> El izquierdista se cree generoso porque sus metas son borrosas.

**Close:** Those who defend what exists fight for something concrete: a privilege, a social structure, an embodied good. On the other hand, whoever fights for an abstract program can believe he defends the universal. The leftist thinks himself generous because his goals are blurry.

**Free:** Defenders of the existing order fight for something concrete — a privilege, a social structure, a tangible good. But those who fight for an abstraction can believe they're defending the universal. The leftist feels generous because his aims are vague.

**Notes:**
- *bien encarnado* — "embodied/incarnate good." Not just "good" — *incarnate* good. The theological language (incarnation) applied to conservative politics: the real good is always particular, local, embodied.
- *borrosas* — blurry, smudged. OCR had "DOrrosas" — corrected. The leftist's generosity is optical: you can project anything onto a blur.

**Exegesis:**
The conservative defends *this* community, *this* institution, *this* privilege — and knows he's partial. The progressive defends "justice," "equality," "the people" — and believes he's universal. But the universality is an illusion produced by vagueness. The vaguer the goal, the more virtuous it feels. This is perhaps Gómez Dávila's most precise diagnosis of left-right asymmetry: the right knows it's selfish; the left doesn't know its generosity is an abstraction.

`tags: left-right, politics, abstraction, concreteness`

## §59

> Este siglo de pedagogía proletaria predica la dignidad del
> trabajo, como un esclavo que calumnia el ocio inteligente y voluptuoso.

**Close:** This century of proletarian pedagogy preaches the dignity of labor, like a slave who slanders intelligent and voluptuous leisure.

**Free:** This century of proletarian education preaches the dignity of work — like a slave who bad-mouths intelligent, sensuous idleness.

**Notes:**
- *ocio inteligente y voluptuoso* — "intelligent and voluptuous leisure." The aristocratic ideal: *otium* (leisure) as the condition for thought and pleasure. Not laziness — *cultivation*.
- *calumnia* — slanders, defames. The slave doesn't merely prefer work; he *attacks* leisure. Resentment, not preference.

**Exegesis:**
Against the modern cult of productivity. Both capitalism and communism agree that work is dignifying — Gómez Dávila says both are slaves' philosophies. The aristocratic tradition (Aristotle, the Roman *otium*, the medieval contemplative life) valued leisure as the precondition for everything worthwhile: philosophy, art, love, prayer. The "dignity of labor" is what slaves tell themselves to cope.

`tags: work, leisure, modernity`

## §60

> Las superestructuras cambian, cuando las "fuerzas de producción"
> varían; pero como cada configuración de fuerzas encierra una
> pluralidad virtual de estructuras, la que se realiza no depende
> de motivos económicos.

**Close:** Superstructures change when the "forces of production" vary; but since each configuration of forces contains a virtual plurality of structures, the one that is realized does not depend on economic motives.

**Free:** Superstructures change when productive forces change — granted. But since any given set of forces is compatible with multiple structures, which one actually emerges isn't determined by economics.

**Notes:**
- He *concedes* the Marxist premise (economic base shapes superstructure) then dismantles it from within. The concession is strategic.
- *pluralidad virtual de estructuras* — "virtual plurality of structures." For any economic base, many cultural/political arrangements are possible. Economics narrows the field but doesn't select the winner.
- The free version makes the dialectical structure explicit ("granted... but").

**Exegesis:**
The most analytically rigorous aphorism in this political sequence. He's doing what few anti-Marxists bother to do: granting the strongest version of the thesis and showing it's still insufficient. Even if economic forces *constrain* superstructures, they don't *determine* them — which means the superstructure requires a non-economic explanation. Culture, religion, accident, personality — the things Marxism dismisses as epiphenomenal — turn out to be the decisive factors.

`tags: marxism, economics, determinism`

## §61

> El amor al pueblo es vocación de aristócrata. El demócrata
> no lo ama sino en período electoral.

**Close:** Love of the people is the vocation of the aristocrat. The democrat loves them only during election season.

**Free:** Loving the people is the aristocrat's calling. The democrat only loves them at election time.

**Notes:**
- *vocación* — vocation, calling. Not hobby, not pose — *calling*. Noblesse oblige as spiritual duty.
- The democrat's love is periodic, instrumental, transactional. The aristocrat's is permanent because it's structural — the people are his responsibility, not his constituency.

**Exegesis:**
The most Tocquevillian aphorism (echoing §69's direct invocation). The old aristocracy's paternalism, whatever its flaws, involved permanent obligation. Democratic politicians cycle through love and indifference with the electoral calendar. This doesn't defend aristocratic *rule* — it diagnoses democratic *love* as structurally insincere.

`tags: aristocracy, democracy, love, the-people`

## §62

> A medida que el estado crece el individuo disminuye.

**Close:** As the state grows, the individual diminishes.

**Free:** The bigger the state, the smaller the person.

**Notes:**
- Eight words. Zero-sum: state and individual share a fixed quantity of sovereignty.
- The free version's "person" (vs. "individual") is warmer but less precise — Gómez Dávila means *individuo* in the philosophical sense.

**Exegesis:**
The simplest statement of his political philosophy. Not an argument but an axiom — the foundation from which everything else follows. Connects to §13 (collective enterprise as prison) and §47 (politics as management of ignorance). The state isn't evil; it's a gravitational force that contracts what it touches.

`tags: state, individuality, politics`

## §63

> No logrando realizar lo que anhela, el "progreso" bautiza
> anhelo lo que realiza.

**Close:** Not managing to realize what it desires, "progress" baptizes as desire what it does realize.

**Free:** Unable to achieve what it wants, "progress" rebrands what it achieves as what it wanted.

**Notes:**
- *bautiza* — baptizes. A mock-sacramental word: progress performs a naming ceremony on its own accidents.
- The scare quotes on "progreso" are structural — they appear whenever he uses the word.
- The free version uses "rebrands" — anachronistic but perfectly captures the operation.

**Exegesis:**
The mechanism of progressive ideology: retroactive desire. We didn't get what we promised, so we'll redefine the promise to match what we got. The suburb wasn't the dream, but once built, the dream is retroactively adjusted. This is the secular equivalent of failed prophecy: when the messiah doesn't come, you reinterpret the prophecy.

`tags: progress, illusion, modernity`

## §64

> La técnica no cumple los viejos sueños del hombre, sino
> los remeda con sorna.

**Close:** Technology does not fulfill man's old dreams, but mimics them with mockery.

**Free:** Technology doesn't fulfill humanity's ancient dreams — it parodies them with a sneer.

**Notes:**
- *remeda con sorna* — "mimics with mockery/scorn." *Remedar*: to imitate, to ape. *Sorna*: sarcasm, mockery. Technology is a cruel mimic.
- The old dream: to fly (we get economy class). To communicate across distances (we get social media). To conquer death (we get life support machines).

**Exegesis:**
Each technological "fulfillment" is a travesty of the original vision. Icarus dreamed of flight as transcendence; we got airport security lines. The alchemist dreamed of transformation; we got plastics. The dreams were spiritual; the fulfillments are mechanical. Technology gives you the *form* of the dream drained of its *content* — which is worse than not fulfilling it at all.

`tags: technology, progress, irony`

## §65

> Como el triunfo de cualquier virtud mutila a otras, todo
> "progreso" acarrea un regreso simétrico.

**Close:** As the triumph of any virtue mutilates others, every "progress" brings a symmetrical regression.

**Free:** Since the triumph of any one virtue maims the rest, every "progress" drags a matching regression behind it.

**Notes:**
- *mutila* — mutilates. Virtues don't merely crowd each other out; they *wound* each other. Justice and mercy, courage and prudence, equality and excellence.
- *regreso simétrico* — "symmetrical regression." The regression isn't random; it's *shaped* by the progress that causes it. The mirror image.

**Exegesis:**
A law of moral conservation — perhaps the most Gómez Dávilean idea in the book. Every advance has a shadow of equal magnitude in another dimension. Industrial progress, environmental regression. Sexual liberation, relational fragmentation. Informational access, attentional collapse. The scare quotes on "progress" tell you he thinks the symmetry is *exact*, which makes net progress impossible.

`tags: progress, virtue, symmetry`

## §66

> El pueblo no se rebela nunca contra el despotismo sino
> contra la mala alimentación.

**Close:** The people never rebel against despotism but against bad food.

**Free:** The people never revolt against tyranny — only against hunger.

**Notes:**
- *mala alimentación* — bad food/nourishment. Not "hunger" exactly — *bad* food. The distinction matters: revolution comes from deterioration of conditions, not from the abstract injustice of the conditions.
- Close version keeps "bad food" (literal, earthy). Free version uses "hunger" (more dramatic, less precise).

**Exegesis:**
A materialist observation from an anti-materialist. He's not endorsing the reductive view — he's stating a sociological fact that undermines revolutionary ideology. Revolutions claim to be about freedom, justice, dignity. They're actually about bread prices. The French Revolution followed bad harvests; the Russian followed wartime shortages. The ideological packaging comes after.

`tags: the-people, revolution, materialism`

## §67

> Cuando se deje de luchar por la posesión de la propiedad
> privada se luchará por el usufructo de la propiedad colectiva.

**Close:** When the struggle for the possession of private property ceases, the struggle for the usufruct of collective property will begin.

**Free:** Once the fight over owning private property ends, the fight over who *uses* the collective property begins.

**Notes:**
- *usufructo* — usufruct: the legal right to use and enjoy the fruits of something you don't own. A technical legal term.
- The insight: abolishing private property doesn't abolish the struggle — it just shifts it from ownership to access. Soviet apartments, party privileges, dacha assignments.

**Exegesis:**
The shortest refutation of the socialist premise that private property is the source of social conflict. Conflict doesn't arise from property but from scarcity and desire, which persist under any ownership regime. The communist *nomenklatura* fought over apartments, cars, and vacation spots exactly as fiercely as capitalists fight over profits — just with different vocabulary.

`tags: property, politics, conflict`

## §68

> La movilidad social ocasiona la lucha de clases.
> El enemigo de las clases altas no es el inferior carente de
> toda posibilidad de ascenso, sino el que no logra ascender
> cuando otros ascienden.

**Close:** Social mobility occasions class struggle. The enemy of the upper classes is not the inferior lacking all possibility of ascent, but the one who fails to ascend when others do.

**Free:** Social mobility *causes* class struggle. The enemy of the upper class isn't the person with no chance of rising — it's the one who fails to rise while watching others succeed.

**Notes:**
- An inversion of the standard narrative: class struggle doesn't come from immobility but from *mobility*. The caste system is stable; the meritocracy is explosive.
- *el que no logra ascender cuando otros ascienden* — relative deprivation, not absolute. Tocqueville's key insight about the French Revolution: conditions were *improving* when it erupted.

**Exegesis:**
Again Tocqueville (the "patron saint" of this political section). The most dangerous moment for a social order is not when it oppresses but when it *partially liberates*. The serf who can never rise doesn't resent the lord. The small merchant who watches his neighbor become rich while he stays small — that's the revolutionary. This explains why revolutions happen in modernizing societies, not in static ones.

`tags: class, social-mobility, resentment`

## §69

> Nada más noble que el aristócrata liberal — como Tocqueville —
> para quien la libertad de todos es el privilegio que compete
> defender a la clase dirigente.

**Close:** Nothing nobler than the liberal aristocrat — like Tocqueville — for whom the liberty of all is the privilege it falls to the ruling class to defend.

**Free:** Nothing nobler than the liberal aristocrat — a Tocqueville — who sees universal liberty as the privilege the ruling class is obliged to defend.

**Notes:**
- The only proper name (besides Socrates) to appear in this entire stretch. Tocqueville is Gómez Dávila's political hero.
- *compete defender* — "it falls to... to defend" / "is obliged to defend." Not charity but *obligation*. The privilege of the ruling class is the *duty* to defend everyone's freedom.
- *privilegio* used deliberately: defending freedom is itself an aristocratic privilege.

**Exegesis:**
His political ideal, stated once and clearly. Not restoration of the ancien régime but a Tocquevillian aristocratic liberalism: the best defend the freedom of all, not because equality demands it but because noblesse obliges it. The ruling class's *purpose* is to be the guardian of liberty. When it fails this, it forfeits its legitimacy. A politics of duty, not rights.

`tags: aristocracy, liberalism, Tocqueville, liberty`

## §70

> Cierta manera desdeñosa de hablar del pueblo denuncia
> al plebeyo disfrazado.

**Close:** A certain disdainful way of speaking of the people denounces the plebeian in disguise.

**Free:** A certain contemptuous way of talking about the people betrays the commoner in costume.

**Notes:**
- *plebeyo disfrazado* — "disguised plebeian." Callback to §3 (ideas change disguise). Now it's people changing disguise.
- The real aristocrat doesn't sneer at the people (§61: he loves them). The one who sneers is performing an aristocracy he doesn't possess.
- *denuncia* — denounces, exposes, betrays. The contempt is self-revealing.

**Exegesis:**
A companion to §61 and a self-corrective. Having spent twenty aphorisms being harsh about democracy and the masses, he now turns the scalpel on *his own side*: the reactionary who sneers at the people is not an aristocrat but a fraud. True aristocracy is paternal, not contemptuous. This is Gómez Dávila policing his own tribe.

`tags: the-people, aristocracy, self-deception`

## §71

> Si el hombre es el único fin del hombre, una reciprocidad
> inane nace de ese principio como el mutuo reflejarse de
> dos espejos vacíos.

**Close:** If man is the sole end of man, an inane reciprocity is born of that principle, like the mutual reflection of two empty mirrors.

**Free:** If man is man's only purpose, what follows is a hollow reciprocity — like two empty mirrors reflecting each other.

**Notes:**
- *reciprocidad inane* — "inane/empty reciprocity." Humanism without God: I serve you, you serve me, but *for what*? The mirrors reflect nothing because there's nothing in either one to reflect.
- *dos espejos vacíos* — "two empty mirrors." One of his most haunting images. Infinite reflection of nothing. The secular humanist hall of mirrors.

**Exegesis:**
The theological indictment of secular humanism, directly echoing §45 (every end other than God dishonors us). If the answer to "what is man for?" is "other men," the answer regresses infinitely. What are *those* men for? Other men. And those? An infinite corridor of empty mirrors. Only a transcendent end (God) breaks the regress and gives the reflection content. This is not an argument for God's existence but for the *necessity* of the idea of God as the condition of human meaning.

## §72

> Ni cristianismo, ni paganismo, enseñan éticas altruístas.
> Tanto la moral cristiana, como la moral pagana, son
> individualismos éticos, que imponen deberes sociales tan
> sólo como medios de nuestra perfección terrestre o de
> nuestra salvación enigmática.

**Close:** Neither Christianity nor paganism teaches altruistic ethics. Both Christian morality and pagan morality are ethical individualisms that impose social duties solely as means of our earthly perfection or our enigmatic salvation.

**Free:** Neither Christianity nor paganism teaches altruism. Both are ethical individualisms: they impose social duties only as means to our earthly perfection or our mysterious salvation.

**Notes:**
- *individualismos éticos* — "ethical individualisms." A startling classification: both the Christian and the Stoic/Aristotelian traditions are fundamentally about *your own* perfection. Love your neighbor — but as a means to *your* salvation. Practice virtue — but for *your* excellence.
- *salvación enigmática* — "enigmatic salvation." Not "certain" — *enigmatic*. Even the Christian goal is wrapped in mystery.
- *altruístas* — altruistic. He means the modern, Comtean sense: sacrifice of self as an end in itself. Neither tradition teaches this.

**Exegesis:**
A bracing reframing. Modern altruism (selflessness as the highest good) is actually foreign to both classical *and* Christian ethics. Aristotle's virtues perfect the individual; Christian charity saves the individual. Social duties exist in both, but instrumentally — they serve the person's *telos*. This doesn't make them selfish; it makes them *teleological*. The modern cult of altruism, by contrast, has no telos — it's sacrifice without a destination, which is why it so easily becomes resentful.
