# Section 07: Depth and Limit (§147–170, pp. 34–38)

The aphorisms contract into their sharpest compression yet. Many are single sentences of devastating precision. The themes spiral tighter: depth, pleasure, taste, vulgarity, wisdom, impurity. Gómez Dávila is writing at the edge of silence — each aphorism seems to be testing how little can be said and still mean everything.

---

## §147

> En la sociedad medieval la sociedad es el estado; en la
> sociedad burguesa estado y sociedad se enfrentan; en la
> sociedad comunista el estado es la sociedad.

**Close:** In medieval society, society is the state; in bourgeois society, state and society confront each other; in communist society, the state is the society.

**Free:** Medieval: society *is* the state. Bourgeois: society *versus* the state. Communist: the state *is* the society.

**Notes:**
- A political typology in three clauses. The chiasmus of the first and third (*society is the state* / *the state is the society*) makes them look identical — but they're mirror images. In the medieval case, the social fabric *generates* political authority organically. In the communist case, political authority *absorbs* the social fabric by force.
- The bourgeois middle term (*se enfrentan* — confront each other) is the only one with tension. It's the only arrangement where liberty is possible (connecting to §23).

**Exegesis:**
A compressed philosophy of political history. The medieval integration is organic but unfree (no separation means no space for dissent). The bourgeois separation creates freedom but also permanent conflict. The communist reunification is total but coerced — the state swallows society. Gómez Dávila's preference is for the bourgeois arrangement, despite its tensions, because only there does the gap between state and society create the space in which the individual can breathe.

## §148

> El azar regirá siempre la historia, porque no es posible
> organizar el estado de manera que no importe quien mande.

**Close:** Chance will always govern history, because it is not possible to organize the state so that it doesn't matter who rules.

**Free:** Chance will always rule history, because you can't design a state where it doesn't matter who's in charge.

**Notes:**
- *azar* — chance, randomness. Not fate — *accident*. History is ungovernable because individuals (who arrive by chance) make irreplaceable differences.
- Against all constitutionalism that claims to be person-proof. The American founders tried; every presidency proves the point.

**Exegesis:**
The irreducibility of the individual (§27, §32) applied to political theory. No system of laws, institutions, or checks can make the *person* in power irrelevant. A good person in a bad system will do some good; a bad person in a good system will do damage. This is why Gómez Dávila distrusts institutional solutions and trusts aristocratic formation: what matters is not the structure but the *quality of the person* who inhabits it.

`tags: chance, history, state, politics`

## §149

> El que se alimenta sólo de ideas generales desfallece.

**Close:** He who feeds only on general ideas faints.

**Free:** A diet of nothing but general ideas leaves you faint.

**Notes:**
- *desfallece* — faints, swoons, weakens. Not "fails" — *physically collapses*. The metaphor is nutritional: general ideas are not food. They're air.
- Connects to §33 (ideas as having color, smell, weight — the *bagasse* aphorism). Living ideas are sensory; general ideas are the dried husk.

**Exegesis:**
Against ideology, against systems, against "big ideas" as a substitute for concrete encounter. The mind that feeds only on abstractions — justice, equality, freedom, progress — starves. It needs *particular* truths: this poem, this person, this place, this meal. Gómez Dávila's own aphorisms are the antidote to what they diagnose: each one is a particular, a *specific* thought with texture, not a general principle.

`tags: ideas, concreteness, nourishment`

## §150

> La profundidad no está en lo que se dice, sino en el nivel
> desde el cual se diga.

**Close:** Depth is not in what is said, but in the level from which it is said.

**Free:** Depth isn't in what you say — it's in the level you say it from.

**Notes:**
- *nivel* — level, stratum. A vertical metaphor: depth is a *position*, not a content. Two people can say the same words; one is deep, one is shallow. The difference is where in themselves the words come from.
- This echoes §146 (intelligent and stupid books say the same thing but have different authors). The author's *depth* is what transforms identical content into different meanings.

**Exegesis:**
A theory of philosophical quality. Depth is not achieved by saying complicated things — it's achieved by being the kind of person whose simplest statements reverberate. "God is love" is deep from Augustine, banal from a greeting card. The words are the same. The level is different. This also describes the *Escolios*: many of the aphorisms, paraphrased, are commonplaces. Their depth comes from the level at which Gómez Dávila inhabits them.

`tags: depth, writing, method`

## §152

> Comenzamos eligiendo porque admiramos y terminamos
> admirando porque elegimos.

**Close:** We begin by choosing because we admire and end by admiring because we choose.

**Free:** First we choose because we admire. In the end we admire because we chose.

**Notes:**
- The chiasmus (admire→choose becomes choose→admire) traces a psychological arc: youthful love is responsive to quality; mature loyalty is responsive to commitment. The first is aesthetic; the second is an act of will.
- Neither is wrong. But the second risks self-deception: "I admire this because I chose it" can mask investment bias.

**Exegesis:**
A description of how preferences calcify into convictions. The young reader discovers Gómez Dávila and is seized by admiration — the choosing follows. The old reader has been reading him for decades and now admires partly because of the investment. This is true of religions, political allegiances, marriages, friendships. The question is whether the original admiration was genuine enough to survive the shift — whether the loyalty is still rooted in something real or has become circular.

`tags: admiration, choice, self-deception`

## §154

> La mayor astucia del mal es su mudanza en dios doméstico
> y discreto, cuya hogareña presencia reconforta.

**Close:** The greatest cunning of evil is its transformation into a domestic and discreet god, whose homely presence comforts.

**Free:** Evil's greatest trick is becoming a quiet household god whose cozy presence puts you at ease.

**Notes:**
- *dios doméstico* — "domestic god." A Roman *lar*, a household deity. Evil doesn't arrive as a demon; it arrives as a comfort.
- *hogareña* — homely, domestic, of the hearth. *Reconforta* — comforts, reassures. Evil as hot cocoa.
- OCR had *ma]* for *mal* — corrected.
- The free version's "Evil's greatest trick" consciously echoes Baudelaire: "La plus belle ruse du diable est de vous persuader qu'il n'existe pas." Both are about evil's self-concealment, but Gómez Dávila's version is subtler: evil doesn't convince you it doesn't exist — it convinces you it's *cozy*.

**Exegesis:**
Not dramatic evil (which is recognizable) but comfortable evil — the kind that moves into your house, sits by the fire, and becomes part of the furniture. Consumer comfort, therapeutic culture, the conviction that you deserve to feel good. The devil you don't fight because he brings you slippers.

`tags: evil, domesticity, deception`

## §156

> La discrepancia entre una preferencia y un juicio es la
> matriz de la moral y de la estética.

**Close:** The discrepancy between a preference and a judgment is the matrix of morality and aesthetics.

**Free:** The gap between what you prefer and what you judge to be right is where morality and aesthetics begin.

**Notes:**
- *preferencia* — what I like, what I'm drawn to. *Juicio* — what I judge to be good, beautiful, true. They don't always coincide. The space between them is where moral and aesthetic life happens.
- *matriz* — matrix, womb, origin. Not just "source" — the *generative structure* from which both disciplines are born.
- Pairs with §155 (moral autonomy is reproving what our sensibility tolerates).

**Exegesis:**
If preference and judgment always coincided, there would be no need for morality or aesthetics — you'd simply do what you wanted and it would always be right. The fact that they diverge (I prefer the easy thing; I judge the hard thing better) is what creates the entire field of moral and aesthetic struggle. This is against all forms of emotivism ("the good is what you feel is good") and against pure subjectivism in aesthetics ("beauty is what you like"). The *discrepancy* is the engine. Without it, ethics and aesthetics are just autobiography.

`tags: morality, aesthetics, judgment, preference`

## §158

> La vulgaridad consiste en pretender ser lo que no somos.

**Close:** Vulgarity consists in pretending to be what we are not.

**Free:** Vulgarity is pretending to be what you're not.

**Notes:**
- Eight words. A definition that redefines vulgarity. Not crudeness, not ignorance, not poverty — *pretension*. The vulgar person is the one performing a self they haven't earned.
- Connects to §70 (the disdainful way of speaking about the people betrays the disguised plebeian) and §53 (bourgeoisie as satisfied with what they are).

**Exegesis:**
By this definition, vulgarity is available at every social level. The nouveau riche is vulgar; so is the professor who performs an erudition he doesn't possess; so is the revolutionary who performs a solidarity he doesn't feel. Conversely, the genuinely crude person who doesn't pretend otherwise is *not* vulgar. Authenticity, not refinement, is the antidote. Gómez Dávila — who was genuinely aristocratic in temperament — could afford this definition. For the rest of us, it's a question: what am I pretending to be?

`tags: vulgarity, authenticity, pretension`

## §161

> El libro no educa a quien lo lee con el fin de educarse.

**Close:** The book does not educate the one who reads it with the aim of educating himself.

**Free:** A book doesn't educate anyone who reads it in order to be educated.

**Notes:**
- The paradox of instrumental reading. Reading *for* education defeats education. The book educates only when the reader surrenders to it without purpose — when reading is an end in itself.
- *con el fin de educarse* — "with the aim of educating himself." The self-improvement reader, the curriculum-checker, the person reading "the great books" to become cultured.

**Exegesis:**
The *Bildung* aphorism. This is directly relevant to the entire bildung-2.0 project. Bildung (formation) happens as a *side effect* of genuine encounter, not as a goal of strategic reading. The moment you read Gómez Dávila *in order to become formed*, you block the formation. You must read him because you can't stop — because the text has seized you — and the formation happens while you're not looking. This connects to §110 (revelation authenticates what's already known): the book doesn't *add* something; it *activates* something already present. But only if you're not trying.

`tags: education, Bildung, reading`

## §162

> Quien no gusta de lo obsoleto no puede saber si tiene
> gustos auténticos.

**Close:** He who does not enjoy the obsolete cannot know whether he has authentic tastes.

**Free:** If you don't enjoy what's obsolete, you can't know whether your taste is real.

**Notes:**
- *lo obsoleto* — the obsolete. Not "the old" — specifically what has gone out of fashion, what the present rejects.
- The test: if you only like what's current, your taste might be nothing but conformity. Only liking something the present has discarded proves your taste is *yours* and not the era's.

**Exegesis:**
A criterion for intellectual independence. The person who loves only what's fashionable — this year's novels, this decade's philosophy, this century's art — is not exercising taste but *obeying* it. The reactionary who loves Petrarch, or Jünger, or Gómez Dávila himself (all "obsolete") is at least proving that his preferences aren't dictated by the zeitgeist. This is §5 (limiting the audience limits capitulations) applied to aesthetics.

`tags: taste, obsolescence, authenticity`

## §163

> El placer es el relámpago irrisorio del contacto entre
> el deseo y la nostalgia.

**Close:** Pleasure is the derisory lightning flash of the contact between desire and nostalgia.

**Free:** Pleasure is the mocking flash of lightning where desire meets nostalgia.

**Notes:**
- *relámpago irrisorio* — "derisory/mocking lightning flash." Pleasure is brief (a flash), contemptible (derisory), and violent (lightning). It *mocks* by its brevity.
- *deseo y nostalgia* — desire (forward-looking) and nostalgia (backward-looking). Pleasure is the instant where the two cross. You want something and you remember wanting it; the intersection is a spark.
- This is the temporal theory from §143 (the momentary beauty of the instant) applied to pleasure specifically, and it's darker.

**Exegesis:**
A sentence that encodes an entire philosophy of time. Desire reaches forward; nostalgia reaches backward; pleasure is the infinitesimal present where they meet and discharge. But the flash is *irrisorio* — it mocks us, because the meeting lasts no longer than lightning. The present doesn't exist except as the collision of two absences (what we want, what we miss). Hedonism is the attempt to prolong lightning.

`tags: pleasure, desire, nostalgia, time`

## §164

> Para las circunstancias conmovedoras sólo sirven lugares
> comunes. Una canción imbécil expresa mejor un gran dolor
> que un noble verso.
> La inteligencia es actividad de seres impasibles.

**Close:** For moving circumstances only commonplaces serve. A stupid song expresses a great pain better than a noble verse. Intelligence is the activity of impassive beings.

**Free:** Only clichés work for genuinely moving occasions. A dumb song says grief better than a fine poem. Intelligence belongs to those who are unmoved.

**Notes:**
- *lugares comunes* — commonplaces, clichés. OCR had *lusares* — corrected.
- *canción imbécil* — "stupid/idiotic song." The pop ballad at the funeral. The folk tune that makes you cry. Intelligence can't reach what cliché touches directly.
- The third sentence is the depth charge: *La inteligencia es actividad de seres impasibles.* Intelligence is for the unmoved. If you're genuinely suffering, intelligence fails you.

**Exegesis:**
Against his own method. The aphorism acknowledges the limit of the aphorist's art: when real grief arrives, the *Escolios* are useless. You reach for a cliché because the cliché was *made* for this — it's a container shaped by millions of prior griefs. The intelligent sentence is too particular, too precise, too *his* — it doesn't fit your pain. This is the most honest aphorism a writer of intelligent sentences can write.

`tags: emotion, intelligence, cliché, grief`

## §167

> La sabiduría no consiste en moderarse por horror al exceso,
> sino por amor al límite.

**Close:** Wisdom does not consist in moderating oneself out of horror of excess, but out of love of the limit.

**Free:** Wisdom isn't restraint from fear of excess — it's restraint from love of the limit.

**Notes:**
- *horror al exceso* vs. *amor al límite* — two motivations for the same behavior (moderation), but utterly different. The first is fear; the second is love. The first is reactive; the second is positive.
- *límite* — limit, boundary, form. The Greek *peras*. Love of limit is love of form, of definition, of the bounded and the shaped.

**Exegesis:**
The Apollonian principle stated morally. The wise person isn't afraid of going too far — she *loves* the shape that restraint gives. The difference between the prude and the ascetic: the prude fears the body; the ascetic loves the discipline. The difference between the timid moderate and the wise conservative: the moderate fears change; the conservative loves what change would destroy. This connects to §83 (renunciation looks like impotence to those who can't renounce): the love of limit is invisible to those who see only prohibition.

`tags: wisdom, limit, moderation`

## §169

> La perfección es el escollo de quien no entiende que
> resulta de actos impuros.

**Close:** Perfection is the stumbling block of one who does not understand that it results from impure acts.

**Free:** Perfection defeats anyone who doesn't understand it comes from impure acts.

**Notes:**
- *escollo* — reef, stumbling block. The same word as §27 (the individual as the reef of philosophies of history) and the near-pun with *escolio* (the book's title). Third appearance of this word.
- *actos impuros* — "impure acts." Compromises, false starts, revisions, failures, influences. Perfection is distilled from mess.

**Exegesis:**
Against perfectionism. The person who waits for purity before beginning never begins. Every finished work — the *Escolios* included — is the product of impure acts: of reading that misunderstood, of first drafts that failed, of influences that were only half-digested. The *escollo/escolio* pun is surely deliberate: perfection is a reef (escollo) because the scholia (escolios) that approach it are themselves impure acts. The implicit text — the perfect whole — can never be written; only the impure fragments that gesture toward it.

`tags: perfection, impurity, art`

## §170

> No es cierto que las cosas valgan porque la vida importe.
> Al contrario, la vida importa porque las cosas valen.

**Close:** It is not true that things have value because life matters. On the contrary, life matters because things have value.

**Free:** Things don't matter because life is important. Life is important because things matter.

**Notes:**
- The inversion is the entire philosophy. The standard modern view: life is the ultimate value, and things derive value from serving life. Gómez Dávila: no — life is *instrumental*. Life is the means by which values (beauty, truth, goodness) are perceived and served.
- The free version strips to the bare inversion. Both versions are nearly identical in force.

**Exegesis:**
The most anti-modern sentence in the book. Modernity says: life first, values second (hence bioethics, quality of life, self-preservation as supreme good). Gómez Dávila says: values first, life second (hence martyrdom, self-sacrifice, the willingness to die for what matters). This doesn't mean life is cheap — it means life is *dignified* by what it serves, not valuable in itself. A life that serves nothing is not valuable just because it's alive. A life that serves beauty, truth, or God matters enormously — *because of what it serves*. The most direct statement yet of his anti-humanism: man is not the measure; the things man perceives are.

`tags: value, life, meaning, inversion`
